Summary
The paper tackles domain adaptation for SMT when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available (as few as ~100 sentence pairs). It frames data selection as a supervised sentence-level domain classification problem and proposes using CNN-based text classifiers. Two variants are explored: (i) a supervised “one-hot” CNN trained on small labeled in-/out-of-domain samples; and (ii) a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with word2vec embeddings trained on large unlabeled data. The classifier scores sentences on both source and target sides; top-N bilingual pairs (by sum of scores) are selected to train a phrase-based SMT system. Across four language directions and social-media test domains, the semi-supervised CNN selects 2.5–10% of the training data yet outperforms the full-data baseline and standard LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM). Notably, it yields +3.1 BLEU for zh→en (sms) with ~3% of data and remains effective with only 100 in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective semi-supervised CNN-based sentence selector for SMT domain adaptation under extreme scarcity of in-domain data, demonstrating robustness down to ~100 labeled sentences.
2) Empirical evidence across four language directions and social-media domains that CNN-based selection can outperform strong LM-based selection baselines, with statistical significance and substantial data reduction.
3) A practical recipe combining source and target sentence classifiers and showing that summing their scores gives the best downstream MT performance.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus on the “tiny in-domain” regime. The paper convincingly argues and demonstrates that common LM-based selection struggles as the labeled in-domain sample shrinks (<800 sentences), while the semi-supervised CNN remains stable, delivering up to +2.1 BLEU with only 100 sentences. This is a practically important setting.
- Strong, multi-condition empirical results. Experiments span four diverse translation directions with large (9–15M) training corpora and social-media test domains, and include significance testing. The top gains (+3.1 BLEU zh→en sms; +1.4 BLEU ar→en and en→zh) with only 2.5–10% of training data are compelling.
- Sensible classifier design and training protocol. The semi-supervised formulation that fuses one-hot indicators with embeddings trained on unlabeled general data is well motivated for small labeled sets. The choice to classify both source and target sides and select by the sum of scores is practical and empirically validated.
- Practicality and simplicity. The method uses off-the-shelf word2vec and a standard CNN toolkit; it requires no domain metadata and minimal annotation (dev-as-positive, random negatives). This lowers barriers to adoption for industrial pipelines.
- Ablation on label budget. The curve varying labeled positives from 6k to 100 is informative and directly supports the main claim about robustness in the tiny-data regime.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior text classification work. The modeling contribution is modest: applying known CNN/semi-supervised CNN techniques (Johnson & Zhang 2015) to SMT data selection. The paper’s value is mainly empirical; a deeper analysis or new modeling idea would strengthen novelty.
- Outdated MT setting and missing modern baselines. All results are with phrase-based SMT. For current standards, evaluation on NMT (e.g., Transformer) is necessary to establish relevance. Likewise, comparisons to stronger contemporary selection methods (e.g., classifier-based selection with fastText/SVM/character n-grams, sentence embeddings like LASER/LaBSE, contrastive LM perplexity, or instance/phrase weighting approaches) are missing.
- Potential confounds in zh→en sms. The existence of a 120k in-domain subset inside the general pool makes this condition atypically favorable for selection. While the proposed method surpasses “in-domain only” training, it is unclear how much of the gain stems from recovering that hidden subset. A controlled setting without such leakage would clarify generality.
- Incomplete methodological details and analysis. Key implementation specifics are underspecified: exact unlabeled corpora used to train embeddings (size, per-language vs joint), tokenization/segmentation (especially for Chinese, tweets/sms), vocabulary/OOV handling, and runtime/computational costs to score 9–15M sentences. There is little error/qualitative analysis of what the selector prefers, and limited ablation (e.g., effect of using only embeddings or only one-hot, effect of source-only vs target-only).
- Validation protocol and possible overfitting. The dev set is used both as positive training data for the classifier and to tune the number of selected sentences for downstream MT (via “held-out,” but it is not clear if it’s the same dev). If the same dev is reused, there is risk of overfitting selection size to the domain dev. A clean split (train/valid for the classifier, separate dev for MT tuning) should be clarified.

Questions to Authors
- What unlabeled data exactly were used to train word2vec for each language? Were embeddings trained per language or jointly? How sensitive are results to embedding quality/dimension?
- How are noisy tokens handled in social media (emojis, URLs, hashtags, code-switching)? What tokenization/segmentation is used for Chinese?
- Do you train separate CNNs for source and target, or share any parameters? What is the impact of using source-only vs target-only vs sum (please report deltas)?
- What is the runtime and memory footprint to score all sentences for each language direction? How many epochs, and how long to train the CNNs with 6k positives?
- How is the “held-out” set defined for tuning the number of selected sentences? Is it distinct from the dev used as positive examples?
- Can you provide an ablation of semi-supervised components: (i) one-hot only, (ii) embeddings only, (iii) combined (current), and visualization/qualitative examples of selected vs rejected sentences?

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Evaluate on an NMT system and report whether selection gains transfer; even a smaller-scale experiment would strengthen impact.
- Compare to simple, strong discriminative baselines for sentence selection (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on tf-idf/character n-grams; fastText; LASER/LaBSE cosine) to better contextualize the CNN advantage.
- Consider iterative hard-negative mining or curriculum selection to further sharpen the classifier.
- Add analysis of selection precision/recall vs selection size, and category/topic/style breakdowns to understand what the CNN captures beyond LM perplexity.

Overall assessment: Promising empirical results and a practical method for tiny in-domain adaptation, but limited modeling novelty and missing modern baselines/settings. I lean toward rejection in its current form, with encouragement to extend to NMT, strengthen baselines, and deepen analysis.
