- Tackles SMT domain adaptation with extremely small in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) using semi-supervised CNN classifiers to select in-domain-like bilingual sentence pairs.  
- Classifier uses one-hot CNN features plus segment embeddings from word2vec trained on unlabeled text; source- and target-side scores are summed to rank sentence pairs.  
- Selected pairs train phrase-based SMT systems; method outperforms strong LM-based selection baselines and the all-data baseline across four language directions, with up to +3.1 BLEU using 2.5–10% of data.  
- Main contribution: empirical demonstration that semi-supervised CNNs enable reliable data selection under extreme low-resource in-domain conditions (100–800 sentences).  
- Contribution: thorough multi-language/multi-domain evaluation with significance testing and analysis of performance vs. labeled sample size, including stability checks at 100 sentences.  
- Contribution: a practical selection strategy combining source- and target-side CNN scores that empirically outperforms single-side selection.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains and stability with minimal labeled data, convincingly shown across experiments.  
- Strength: Large-scale, broad evaluation (four directions, social-media domains, 9–15M pools) demonstrating practical relevance and statistically significant improvements.  
- Strength: Practical, scalable semi-supervised design using pretrained word2vec embeddings that is easy to reproduce and deploy.  
- Strength: Reasonable design choices (source+target scoring, sensible hyperparameters) and reported toolkit/settings.  
- Strength: Stability analysis (repeated 100-sentence runs) shows low variance, mitigating lucky-sample concerns.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty — method mainly applies existing CNN classification and embeddings to SMT selection, with small modeling innovation.  
- Weakness: Experiments use phrase-based SMT only; no evaluation on modern NMT, raising questions about relevance and transferability.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablations and analyses (source vs target vs sum, embeddings vs random, bow vs seq, CNN vs simpler classifiers, contribution of semi-supervised term).  
- Weakness: Potential confound in zh→en due to a large embedded in-domain subset in the general pool; lack of overlap/recovery analysis clouds generality.  
- Weakness: Reproducibility and methodological gaps — some architecture/training details, negative sampling, selection thresholds, and runtime costs are under-specified.  
- Question: Have you tested the selection strategy for NMT (e.g., Transformer fine-tuning) and does sscnn still beat LM-based selection?  
- Question: For zh→en, what fraction of selected sentences overlap the 120k in-domain subset, and how do baselines compare?  
- Question/Ablation requests: report source-only vs target-only vs sum; with vs without pretrained embeddings; bow vs seq vs both; CNN vs simple linear classifier on same tiny labeled data.  
- Question: How sensitive are BLEU gains to the number of selected sentences — please include selection-size curves on held-out dev for at least one pair.  
- Question: What is the wall-clock time to score 10–15M sentence pairs on both sides; any batching/GPU details?  
- Additional comment: Fix incomplete/broken references and typos in the paper.  
- Additional suggestion: Report selection-quality metrics (AUC/PR) on a labeled held-out set to complement downstream BLEU.
