Summary and main contributions
- Proposes framing MT domain adaptation under tiny in-domain data as a supervised sentence-pair in/out-of-domain classification problem using convolutional neural networks, and enhances it with a semi-supervised variant that injects pre-trained word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora.
- Applies the classifier to score both source and target sentences and selects parallel data by the sum of the two scores, yielding consistent gains across four language directions and social-media test domains with only 2–10% of the full training data; crucially, shows strong performance when only 100 in-domain sentences are available.
- Provides a simple, practical recipe with publicly available toolkits and extensive experiments (9–15M sentence-pair conditions), including significance testing and robustness to tiny labeled seeds.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Recasting data selection as semi-supervised CNN-based text classification tailored to the “tiny in-domain” regime is a fresh direction relative to the prevailing LM-based cross-entropy selection. The method leans on CNN’s ability to capture local, position-invariant domain cues and on embeddings to generalize from scarce labels—well-aligned with the problem’s constraints.
- Strong empirical impact under realistic, large-scale settings: Across zh→en (sms), ar→en (tweets), en→es (Facebook), en→zh (Facebook), the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms the “alldata” baseline and LM-based selectors with far less data. Notable gains include +3.1 BLEU on zh→en using only ~300K selected pairs (~2.5% of 12.2M), and +1.4 BLEU on ar→en with ~700K selected (p < 0.01 in multiple cases). The approach even beats the “in-domain only” subset in zh→en, highlighting its ability to retrieve broader, useful material.
- Sample efficiency in the tiny-label regime: With only 100 in-domain sentences, sscnn yields +2.1 BLEU over the full-data baseline and materially exceeds LM-based selection and one-hot CNN. The reported stability over three random seeds (std ≈ 0.12 BLEU) is compelling evidence that the method is not brittle.
- Bilingual scoring that is simple yet effective: Scoring source and target sides independently and summing improves selection. This bilingual use of signals is practical and helps the method generalize when explicit domain metadata are absent.
- Practicality and clarity: The method uses standard toolkits (conText, word2vec), clear hyperparameters, and a straightforward training protocol (balanced positives/negatives), making it easy for practitioners to adopt in production-scale pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond application: The CNN architecture and semi-supervised use of pre-trained embeddings are established in text classification; the contribution is primarily the application to MT data selection. There is no deeper modeling of bilingual interactions (e.g., joint bilingual CNN), which could further distinguish the work.
- Missing baselines and ablations: Comparisons are confined to LM-based selection. Absent are (i) simple supervised discriminative baselines (logistic regression/SVM on n-grams) trained on the same tiny labels, (ii) strong non-neural selection/weighting like VSM/instance weighting/mixture models under tiny seeds, and (iii) bilingual cross-entropy selection variants tuned on both sides. Ablations on the value of embeddings vs. one-hot regions, bow vs. seq regions, and source vs. target vs. sum are not fully quantified.
- Selection-budget tuning and risk of overfitting: The number of selected sentences N is “empirically determined on held-out,” but details of the held-out split are unclear given the dev set is also used as positive labels. Without clear selection curves (BLEU vs. N) or a principled criterion, there’s a risk of per-method overfitting to validation.
- Limited analysis of efficiency and behavior: No timing/throughput numbers for scoring 9–15M sentences; practitioners would benefit from knowing training/inference costs. Error analysis is absent; it would help to characterize which domains/topics benefit most and failure modes when in-domain evidence is extremely sparse.
- Realism of the zh→en condition: The largest gain comes where an in-domain subset exists within general data; while this showcases retrieval prowess, it partly conflates selection with recovering known in-domain material. The paper does show gains for the other three tasks without such a subset, but smaller; more discussion on these settings would strengthen the generality claim.

Questions to authors
- Can you add a simple discriminative baseline (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM on bag-of-ngrams) trained on the same tiny in-domain labels? This would help isolate whether CNNs (vs. any supervised classifier) are crucial.
- How exactly is N chosen? What is the validation set if the dev set is used as positives? Please provide BLEU vs. N curves to show robustness and avoid selection-budget overfitting.
- Are word embeddings trained per-language separately? Did you try updating embeddings during supervised training, or bilingual embeddings? Any results for a joint bilingual CNN instead of summing independent scores?
- What are the compute costs to classify 9–15M sentences (wall-clock, GPU/CPU)? Any batching/throughput details?
- How sensitive are results to the choice/size of unlabeled data used for word2vec, and to hyperparameters (region size, pooling, number of filters)?
- Do you expect similar benefits for NMT data selection? Any preliminary results?

Overall recommendation
This paper introduces a simple yet bold idea—semi-supervised CNN-based data selection targeted at the tiny in-domain regime—and backs it with convincing large-scale evidence and strong gains, especially when labels are very scarce. Despite some missing baselines and limited architectural novelty, the concept and demonstrated impact warrant acceptance.
