Summary and contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available. It trains a sentence-level domain classifier using a CNN: positives are dev-set sentences (in-domain), negatives are randomly sampled general-domain sentences. A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot inputs with pre-trained word2vec embeddings, following region-embedding ideas, and classifies source and target sides separately; sentence pairs are selected by the sum of the two scores to train the SMT system.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical finding: semi-supervised CNN-based data selection consistently outperforms strong LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) across four language directions and social-media test domains, with substantial gains using only 2.5–10% of training data; notably +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms and +1.4 BLEU on ar→en/en→zh.
2) Methodological framing of domain selection under extreme label scarcity: demonstrates that CNNs with pre-trained embeddings are robust with as few as 100 labeled sentence pairs, outperforming LM-based selection and a supervised one-hot CNN.
3) Practical insights for selection: summing source and target domain scores works best; experimental analysis on sensitivity to the number of labeled samples; significance testing across conditions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results in a realistic, difficult setting. The paper evaluates on four directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) with large general-domain corpora (9–15M pairs) and social-media test domains where in-domain data is scarce. Semi-supervised CNN yields consistent improvements over the “alldata” baseline and over n-gram/RNNLM/combo selection, with statistically significant gains. The zh→en sms case is especially compelling: with ~3% of the data, the method beats both “alldata” and a 120k in-domain-only system.
- Careful experimental design and analyses. The study includes: (a) two CNN variants (one-hot vs semi-supervised); (b) classification on both sides and a combined scoring strategy; (c) a controlled analysis varying labeled data from 6k down to 100 sentences showing stability and superiority of sscnn at low labels; (d) significance tests via bootstrap. Hyperparameters and toolkits are reported, and selection sizes are tuned on held-out data across methods, which supports fairness.
- Clear motivation and plausible explanatory arguments. The rationale for CNNs (local n-gram indicators + max pooling) and for pre-trained embeddings (generalization to unseen but semantically similar words) is well articulated and consistent with the observed robustness at tiny label sizes.
- Practical relevance. The scenario (hundreds of topics; rapid adaptation; social media) is timely and important for practitioners. Showing competitive performance using a tiny labeled set and a relatively small selected subset is valuable for efficiency as well.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The CNN architecture and the use of pre-trained embeddings closely follow prior work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang, 2015). The main novelty is the application to SMT data selection with a specific scoring/selection protocol. This is acceptable if supported by strong empirical evidence (which is present), but should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Missing comparisons to strong simple discriminative baselines. Given the tiny labeled regime, it is important to compare against: (a) TF-IDF/BM25 cosine similarity to in-domain centroid; (b) linear SVM/logistic regression on bag-of-ngrams; (c) averaged word-embedding sentence vectors with cosine similarity or linear classifiers. These are common, competitive, and inexpensive baselines for sentence/domain classification, and could narrow the gap to CNNs.
- Incomplete ablations and reporting. The paper claims summing source+target scores works best, but does not report the performance of using only source or only target, or other combinations. It also does not analyze the impact of region type (bow vs seq) individually, or the effect of fine-tuning vs freezing embeddings. The statement that different negative-sampling strategies yield “very similar” results is not quantified. Runtime and resource costs (vs LM-based selection) are absent.
- Potential data usage ambiguity and overfitting concerns. Positives are taken from the MT development set; in phrase-based SMT, the dev set is also commonly used for tuning. The paper should clarify whether a separate validation set is used for tuning selection size and whether the dev used for classifier training overlaps with SMT tuning, to avoid leakage or overfitting selection choices to the evaluation pipeline.
- Scope limited to phrase-based SMT; no discussion of NMT. While the setup is consistent with prior work, a brief discussion of whether the approach transfers to NMT (e.g., data selection for NMT training) would strengthen the paper’s broader impact.

Questions to authors
- Do you fine-tune the pre-trained word embeddings during supervised CNN training? If yes, what regularization was used to avoid overfitting with tiny labels? If no, did you try fine-tuning and what was the effect?
- How is the selection size N chosen for each method? What held-out set is used for this tuning? Is the dev set used for classifier training also used for SMT tuning, and if so, how do you avoid overfitting?
- Could you report ablations for selection based on source-only, target-only, and their weighted combinations? What is the correlation between the two scores?
- Can you add baselines using TF-IDF/BM25 cosine, averaged word embeddings + cosine, and linear SVM/LogReg on bag-of-ngrams? These would help quantify the specific gain from CNNs.
- What are the training/inference times and computational resources for sscnn vs RNNLM/ngram selection on one language pair?

Overall assessment against reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate; application-focused novelty on top of known CNN/embedding techniques.
- Soundness: Generally sound; clear method and consistent empirical benefits; would benefit from additional baselines and ablations.
- Significance: High practical significance for domain adaptation with tiny labels; results are meaningful across multiple directions/domains.
- Clarity: Good; method and setup are described with sufficient detail, though some ambiguities remain (selection tuning, embedding fine-tuning).
- Empirical evaluation: Strong breadth and statistical testing; missing simple discriminative baselines and runtime analysis.

I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying data usage and adding (or at least discussing) the missing baselines/ablations.
