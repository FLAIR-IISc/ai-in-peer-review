Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to classify sentence pairs as in-domain vs. out-of-domain with very small in-domain supervision, and to select top-scoring parallel sentences for SMT training.
- A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot CNN inputs with pre-trained word embeddings (skip-gram), purportedly improving robustness when the labeled in-domain set is tiny.
- Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) show that CNN-based selection, especially the semi-supervised model, outperforms using all data and Moore–Lewis/RNNLM-based selection, with up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms and +0.7–1.4 on others, selecting only ~2.5–10% of the data.

Strengths
- Clear practical problem: data selection for domain adaptation with extremely small in-domain sets (as low as 100 sentence pairs) is important and under-explored in SMT-era work. The paper targets a realistic social-media setting.
- Strong empirical gains with small supervision: The semi-supervised CNN outperforms n-gram/RNNLM selection in low-resource in-domain regimes (≤800 sentences), with consistent improvements and small variance (std 0.12 BLEU in 3 runs with 100 in-domain sentences).
- Sensible, simple approach: The architecture (region CNN + max-pooling) and the idea of combining one-hot regions with pre-trained embeddings is straightforward and widely applicable; execution seems computationally practical (train on tiny labeled data, score the large pool).
- Multi-language evaluation at scale: Four directions, 9–15M parallel pairs each, and three social media test domains. Compared to common baselines (all data, Moore–Lewis, RNNLM, and their combination) with significance testing.

Weaknesses
- Methodological and reporting gaps limit reproducibility and interpretation.
  - Architecture/training details are incomplete: objective function, optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, number of epochs/early stopping, regularization (dropout/L2), whether pre-trained embeddings are fine-tuned, and how “region embeddings” are constructed for bow vs. seq inputs. Equation (2) implies learning W and V, but it is unclear if ul(x) (embeddings) are updated.
  - Tokenization/segmentation is unspecified, especially for Chinese. Word vs. character, and segmentation tool significantly affect one-hot and embedding inputs and thus selection quality.
  - Selection policy lacks detail: how probabilities are calibrated across source/target models; tie-breaking; how the “sum” of source+target scores is normalized; whether thresholding or fixed-top-N is used; what the “separate validation set” is (distinct from dev?) for choosing N; and whether any overfitting to dev occurs since dev is also positive training data.
- Potential domain leakage confounds the main gain: For zh→en sms, the authors note a 120K in-domain subset within the general pool. The largest reported improvement (+3.1 BLEU) may largely reflect recovery of this subset. There is no analysis quantifying overlap between selected data and the known in-domain subset, nor deduplication checks wrt dev/test. This raises concerns about how representative the gains are for settings without embedded in-domain data.
- Baseline strength and fairness:
  - Moore–Lewis is limited to 3-gram with Witten–Bell smoothing (rationale given, but Kneser–Ney is standard) and no tuning of corpus size schedule; RNNLM setup is under-specified (hidden size, class factorization, noise-contrastive estimation, etc.). No comparisons to competitive instance-weighting or mixture modeling approaches (e.g., Foster et al., Sennrich 2012), or to modern discriminative sentence scorers beyond plain LMs.
  - For SMT, key details are omitted: tuning algorithm (MERT/MIRA/PRO), language model sizes and interpolation specifics (the text ambiguously mentions both 4-gram and Gigaword 5-gram), lexicalized reordering settings, pruning thresholds. Without strong, fully specified baselines, it’s hard to attribute gains solely to selection quality.
- Inconsistent and incomplete experimental reporting:
  - The number of selected sentences N is “empirically determined on held-out” but the held-out set is not specified; per-task/per-method Ns vary but rationale and selection curves are not shown. The claim that source+target score sum is best is not backed by a table/plot.
  - Figure 1 refers to “all five data selection methods,” but six are described (ngram, rnnlm, comblm, ohcnn, sscnn; plus alldata baseline). It is unclear which five are plotted. The figure is only discussed for zh→en; similar curves for other directions are missing.
  - Significance testing: bootstrap details (# samples, paired vs. stratified) are omitted.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues reduce clarity:
  - Frequent typos and inconsistencies: “the later”→“the latter”, “unlabled”, “indomain/out-domain”, “GloV e”, inconsistent hyphenation (“LM based” vs. “LM-based”), capitalization, spacing around footnote markers, inconsistent dataset naming (“facebook” lowercase).
  - References are highly inconsistent/incomplete and contain malformed entries (e.g., “Axelrod et al.,? \Q2011\E”, missing venues/pages, stray punctuation, repeated author lists, missing years, incorrect accents). This does not meet conference standards and complicates verification.
  - Footnotes/URLs: numbering and placement are irregular; some links lack descriptive text; toolkit names are inconsistently formatted (“conText4”).

Questions to authors
- Do you fine-tune the pre-trained word embeddings during supervised CNN training? If yes, with what learning rate and regularization? If not, why?
- How are Chinese sentences tokenized for both one-hot and embedding inputs (which segmenter, vocabulary size, OOV handling)? Same for Arabic?
- How exactly is the “sum of source and target scores” computed and calibrated? Are the two classifiers trained independently and their logits/probabilities directly summed? Any normalization?
- What is the “separate validation set” used to choose N? Is it distinct from the dev set used as positive labels? If not, how do you mitigate overfitting?
- For zh→en, what fraction of the selected sentences overlaps with the known 120K in-domain subset? Are dev/test sentences deduplicated from training?
- Please provide full hyperparameters for RNNLM and CNN training, and the SMT training/tuning pipeline, to enable reproduction. What is the runtime to score 9–15M sentences?

Additional comments
- Please correct typos and standardize terminology (e.g., in-domain/out-of-domain; LM-based; GloVe; latter).
- Clean and complete the bibliography with consistent formatting (authors, year, title, venue, pages).
- Report ablations: one-hot only vs. embeddings only vs. combined; source-only vs. target-only vs. sum; fine-tuned vs. frozen embeddings.
- Consider adding a fairness check by matching selected-data sizes across methods and plotting BLEU vs. data size curves per method.

Recommendation: Reject. While the problem is important and the empirical gains are promising, the paper’s methodological under-specification, potential leakage in the main result, underdeveloped baselines, and extensive presentation issues outweigh the contributions at this stage. A revised submission with tightened experimental rigor and polished presentation could be competitive.
