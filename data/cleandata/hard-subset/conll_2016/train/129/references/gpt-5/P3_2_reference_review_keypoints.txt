- Tackles SMT domain adaptation with very small in-domain parallel data (100–6k) by training sentence-level CNNs to rank and select general-domain sentence pairs.
- Key technical element: semi-supervised CNN combining one-hot region features with word2vec-derived region embeddings; scores source and target separately and sums scores.
- Experiments on 4 language pairs/domains show consistent BLEU gains over using all data and over n-gram/RNNLM selection, with strong improvements for zh→en sms.
- Demonstrates robustness down to 100 labeled in-domain sentences with low variance across samplings.
- Empirical finding: semi-supervised CNN selection substantially outperforms LM-based selection in extreme low-label settings (≤800 sentences).
- Practical method: simple, scalable pipeline using pre-trained embeddings and separate source/target CNNs that selects a small fraction of data with competitive results.
- Low-resource analysis: controlled study showing stability and meaningful gains even with only 100 labeled examples.
- Strength: addresses a realistic, underexplored low-data social-media setting and yields consistent improvements.
- Strength: strong baselines/scope — compares to Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM selection across large bilingual corpora with statistical validation.
- Strength: shows clear benefit of semi-supervised embeddings over one-hot CNNs (~0.5 BLEU average).
- Strength: practical and reproducible setup using public data and standard toolkits.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty; architecture largely follows prior work (Johnson & Zhang 2015b) and task is standard supervised selection.
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to simple discriminative baselines (logistic/SVM on n-grams, TF-IDF/BM25, averaged-embedding cosine) and to PU/domain-adversarial methods.
- Weakness: potential data-leakage and unclear tuning protocol — held-out/dev splits for labeling and hyperparameter/N tuning are not clearly disjoint.
- Weakness: calibration and class-prior mismatch not addressed; no precision/recall or top-K selection quality analysis and no negative-sampling sensitivity study.
- Weakness: insufficient ablations — source vs target vs sum, region size/pooling, embeddings fixed vs fine-tuned, effect of pretraining corpora not analyzed.
- Weakness: missed opportunity to quantify selection precision (e.g., overlap of selected data with known in-domain subsets such as zh→en 120k).
- Weakness: runtime and throughput for scoring millions of sentences with two CNNs are not reported.
- Question: are pre-trained word2vec embeddings fixed or fine-tuned; what corpora were used and how were OOVs/tokenization handled (esp. Chinese)?
- Question: clarify the held-out set used for CNN tuning and whether it is disjoint from dev used for labeling and for tuning N via BLEU.
- Question: how is N chosen per task (tuned by dev BLEU?), how sensitive are results to N, and can selection-size curves be provided?
- Question: for zh→en sms, what fraction of the selected 300k overlaps the known 120k in-domain subset?
- Question: can authors compare to lightweight discriminative baselines (averaged-embedding cosine, linear SVM/logistic on n-grams) trained on the same tiny data?
- Question: what is computational cost (CNN training time, scoring throughput, total selection time) and is GPU required?
- Question: how sensitive are results to negative sampling choices (seed, negatives per positive) and did authors try class-weighting or PU-learning?
- Question: did authors try bilingual/joint models or cross-lingual embeddings (concatenated inputs) instead of summing independent source/target scores?
- Overall: solid empirical contribution with clear practical gains, but limited novelty and missing analyses/comparisons that, if addressed, would strengthen the paper.
