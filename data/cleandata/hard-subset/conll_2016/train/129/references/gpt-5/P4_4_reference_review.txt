Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to classify bilingual sentence pairs as in-domain vs. out-of-domain for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The approach includes a semi-supervised variant that augments one-hot inputs with word embeddings trained on large unlabeled general-domain text. The classifier scores source and target sides separately and selects top-N sentence pairs (by summed scores) to train phrase-based SMT systems. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) show consistent improvements over training on all data and over LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, combined), with the largest gain on zh→en SMS. The paper also studies sensitivity to the amount of in-domain labeled data, reporting competitive performance even with ~100 sentences.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation that is effective with very small in-domain samples, outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines across multiple language pairs.

2) An empirical study at scale (9–15M sentence pairs per direction) demonstrating robustness to extremely small in-domain seeds (down to 100 sentences) and showing stability across resamples.

3) A practical selection protocol that leverages both source and target sentence classification scores (summed) and shows that modest selected subsets (2.5–10% of data) can outperform training on all data.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements with tiny in-domain data: The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) yields up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS with only ~300K selected pairs (≈2.5% of 12.2M), and +0.7 to +1.4 BLEU on three other directions. Gains over all-data and over LM-based selection methods are statistically significant (p<0.05/0.01), addressing a realistic, data-scarce adaptation scenario.

- Robustness to very small labels: Varying the in-domain seed from 6K down to 100 sentences (fixed 300K selected pairs), sscnn maintains superior performance to LM-based methods, with +2.1 BLEU over the all-data baseline at 100 sentences and low variance across resamples (mean 25.03, SD 0.12), indicating stability in low-label regimes.

- Sound semi-supervised design leveraging unlabeled text: Using word2vec embeddings trained on large general-domain corpora to inform segment embeddings plausibly mitigates sparsity and OOV issues; the ablation vs. one-hot CNN (ohcnn) shows consistent additional gains (~0.5 BLEU on average).

- Breadth of evaluation: Four language directions, diverse social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), and large-scale training corpora strengthen external validity. Standard SMT training and significance testing procedures are used.

- Practical efficiency in data usage: Selecting 2.5–10% of data to surpass full-data systems is operationally appealing for faster, cheaper training, especially for multi-domain or multi-topic MT deployments.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparisons to non-LM discriminative baselines: The paper does not compare against simpler text classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with n-grams/char n-grams) or neural alternatives (e.g., CNN without semi-supervision beyond embeddings, shallow MLPs, fastText). Without these, it is difficult to attribute the gains uniquely to the CNN architecture versus to supervised discriminative selection in general.

- Incomplete ablations and architecture clarity: Equation (2) suggests combining one-hot and embedding-based segment representations via an additional convolution layer, but the exact two-stage architecture, training details (epochs, regularization, dropout, optimizer, batch size), and calibration of scores are under-specified. The contribution of source vs. target score vs. their sum is asserted but not quantitatively reported.

- Confound in the largest gain scenario: For zh→en SMS, the all-domain pool contains a 120K in-domain subset; this setting may favor methods that recover that subset. Although noted, this complicates extrapolation of the +3.1 BLEU gain to cases without embedded in-domain data (where gains are more modest, +0.7–1.4 BLEU).

- Selection size determination and calibration: Top-N is “empirically determined on held-out,” but the selection of N, sensitivity to N, and any score normalization across source/target models are not analyzed. This limits guidance for practitioners on how to set N across domains/languages.

- Reproducibility and computational cost: While toolkits are cited, important implementation specifics (tokenization/normalization, vocabulary curation, class balancing, runtime/resources) and training cost comparisons vs. RNNLM selection are not provided, hampering reproducibility and cost-benefit assessment.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: How does your method compare to strong linear classifiers (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with word and character n-grams) for sentence-level domain classification using the same labeled data? Did you test fastText or a shallow CNN without the semi-supervised component?

- Architecture details: Please clarify the exact network: Is there a first convolution over embeddings to produce segment vectors, followed by a second convolution that combines one-hot and embedding segments, as implied by Eq. (2)? What regularization (dropout/L2), optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, and number of epochs were used?

- Source vs. target scores: Could you report results when selecting by source-only, target-only, and sum explicitly across tasks? Are the scores calibrated (e.g., via a held-out threshold) before summation?

- Selection size N: How was N chosen per task, and how sensitive are results to N? Is there a heuristic relating N to the size of the in-domain dev set or to classifier confidence?

- Computational cost: What are training/inference times and resource requirements for sscnn vs. n-gram and RNNLM selection on your datasets? Is the selection stage feasible for frequent re-adaptation?

- Generalization: Beyond the zh→en SMS case with an embedded in-domain subset, do you have experiments varying the amount of embedded in-domain material to assess how performance degrades when the general pool is entirely out-of-domain?

- Integration with SMT: Did you try instance weighting or mixture modeling using the CNN scores instead of hard selection? If so, how do those compare?

Overall assessment
The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful improvements on a practical problem. The primary novelty lies in applying semi-supervised CNNs to SMT data selection under extreme label scarcity, with convincing evidence. Adding comparisons to simpler discriminative baselines and clarifying architectural and selection details would strengthen the case, but the current results already suggest a useful contribution.
