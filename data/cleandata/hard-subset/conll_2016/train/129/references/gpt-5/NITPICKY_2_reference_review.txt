Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier for sentence-level domain relevance scoring to select subsets of general-domain parallel data for SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain data (hundreds of sentences) is available. Combines one-hot features with region embeddings derived from word2vec, scoring source and target sides separately and selecting by sum.
- Presents empirical results on four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook), showing that training on 2.5–10% of the data selected by the CNN outperforms using all data and LM-based selection (Moore–Lewis style n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination).
- Studies the low-label regime, reporting that with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, the semi-supervised CNN still yields sizable BLEU gains (e.g., +2.1 on zh→en sms), with low variance across random samples.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains under low-resource in-domain conditions. On zh→en sms, the method yields +3.1 BLEU over using all data and beats an “in-domain only” subset (25.7 vs. 26.0 BLEU), and on the remaining tasks achieves up to +1.4 BLEU. Results are statistically significant and improvements persist even at 100 labeled sentences.
- Simple, generally applicable pipeline. The approach requires only dev data as positives and random general-domain sentences as negatives; no domain labels, topic tags, or manual features are used. Scoring both source and target and summing is a practical trick that is easy to adopt.
- Sensible motivation for semi-supervision. Using unlabeled general-domain text to learn embeddings to support tiny labeled sets is appropriate and consistent with prior text classification findings; the empirical small-sample study corroborates stability.
- Uses widely available toolkits and reports some hyperparameters (window size, stride, pooling, number of filters, embedding dim), which aids partial reproducibility.
- Significance testing is employed, and selection fractions are kept small, highlighting a compelling compute/data trade-off for large SMT training sets.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The core idea—using a text classifier for domain relevance scoring and selecting top-N sentences—is conceptually close to established data selection/weighting (Moore–Lewis, RNNLM-based selection, mixture/instance weighting). Replacing LM-based scores with CNN-based scores is an incremental methodological change; there is no deeper architectural or theoretical advancement beyond Johnson & Zhang’s region embeddings adapted to this task.
- Experimental protocol lacks critical details and risks data leakage. The dev set is used as positive training data; hyperparameters and, crucially, the number of selected sentences N are said to be “determined … on a separate validation set,” but this validation set is never defined. For the social media tasks, where in-domain data is scarce, what exactly is held out for validation? If test sets influenced N or hyperparameters, results would be optimistic.
- Baseline and fairness issues. (a) It is unclear whether the LM-based baselines implement the bilingual cross-entropy difference on both sides as in Axelrod et al. (2011) and combine source/target appropriately; “comblm” is described as ngram+rnnlm (equal weight) but not whether it’s bilingual. (b) The n-gram LM is limited to 3-grams with Witten–Bell; stronger baselines typically use higher-order Kneser–Ney LMs. (c) Missing simple discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with n-gram features) and data weighting baselines (instance/phrase weighting), which could narrow the gap to CNN and test whether CNN is necessary.
- Insufficient ablation/analysis. No breakdown of source-only vs. target-only selection or calibration/normalization before summing scores. No ablation of region size, pooling, number of filters, or whether embeddings are fixed/fine-tuned. The claim that selecting negatives from a first-round negative pool yields “very similar” results is unquantified. The observed large zh→en gains are partly attributed to an in-domain subset present in the pool; a clearer analysis of how often CNN recovers that subset and results on pools without such subsets is needed.
- Reproducibility and compute cost are under-specified. Exact training corpora composition, tokenization, vocabulary handling (OOVs, casing), and pre-processing are not described. It is unclear whether embeddings were trained per language (likely) and on which corpora specifically. No information on training/inference time or hardware; classifying 9–15M sentence pairs on both sides with CNNs can be nontrivial in practice.
- Technical inaccuracies/unclear modeling. Training is described as “stochastic gradient ascent,” which is atypical for minimizing loss; likely a mistake. The semi-supervised architecture (how “segment embeddings” are produced from word2vec and combined with one-hot inputs; whether embeddings are updated) is vague. It is unclear whether the described two-stage/stacked CNN is actually implemented using the cited toolkit or approximated by concatenation.
- Writing/formatting issues are numerous. Typos (“unlabled,” “GloV e,” “indomain”), spacing (“5- gram”), inconsistent hyphenation, and many broken/incomplete references (e.g., “Axelrod et al.,? \Q2011\E”) detract from professionalism and make verification difficult. Footnote/URL formatting is inconsistent.

Questions to authors
- What exactly is the “separate validation set” used to tune N and CNN hyperparameters? Is it distinct from both the dev set used as positives and the test set? If not, how did you prevent leakage?
- Do your LM-based baselines implement bilingual cross-entropy difference on both source and target with summation (as in Axelrod et al., 2011), for both n-gram and RNNLM variants? If not, please provide results for that stronger bilingual baseline.
- Please specify the exact training corpora for each language (sources, sizes after filtering, tokenization/casing), vocabularies, and whether word2vec was trained per language on the same general-domain data used for SMT training. Were embeddings fixed or fine-tuned?
- How are “segment embeddings” computed from word2vec: directly convolving over sequences of embeddings (single CNN), or a two-stage network producing region embeddings that feed another CNN, per Johnson & Zhang (2015b)? If two-stage, how was this implemented in conText?
- How did you calibrate/normalize source and target scores before summation? Did you try source-only vs target-only selection; can you report their scores?
- What is the runtime and hardware used for training the CNN(s) and scoring 9–15M sentence pairs per language (both sides)? How does it compare to LM-based selection in wall-clock time?
- Did you try stronger n-gram LMs (e.g., 5-gram Kneser–Ney) and/or logistic regression/SVM classifiers with n-gram features? Please provide results or justify omissions.
- For the zh→en task with an in-domain subset in the pool, can you quantify the overlap between your selected data and that subset? How do results look on pools without known in-domain components?

Overall, the empirical results are promising, but the limited novelty, missing/below-par baselines, unclear validation protocol, and numerous presentation issues significantly weaken the submission. Clarifying the protocol and strengthening baselines/ablations are essential.
