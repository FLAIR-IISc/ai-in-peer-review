- Paper tackles SMT domain adaptation with very small in-domain labels (as few as 100 sentences) using a CNN sentence-level domain classifier to select parallel data.

- Proposes two CNN variants: one-hot CNN trained on tiny labeled data and a semi-supervised CNN augmenting one-hot with pretrained word embeddings.

- Scores source and target sentences independently and sums scores to select top-N sentence pairs for phrase-based SMT training.

- Empirically shows consistent BLEU gains across four language directions and social-media domains versus using all data and LM-based selection baselines.

- Main contribution: empirical demonstration that semi-supervised CNN enables effective data selection with tiny in-domain labels, yielding up to +3.1 BLEU with 2.5–10% of data.

- Contribution: defines and analyzes a practical low-resource adaptation setting, showing robustness down to 100 labeled sentences and outperforming LM baselines and one-hot CNN.

- Contribution: presents a simple bilingual selection strategy (sum of source+target scores) that is easy to deploy and reportedly outperforms single-side selection.

- Strength: strong empirical results under realistic constraints, including significant gains (e.g., +3.1 BLEU zh→en sms) and significance testing.

- Strength: focuses on a hard, underexplored “tiny supervision” regime with informative analysis of performance vs. labeled-sample size.

- Strength: language-agnostic, sensible semi-supervised approach using embeddings from unlabeled text and practical bilingual scoring.

- Strength: scalable and practical workflow using standard toolkits, with small selected subsets outperforming training on all data.

- Strength: stability analysis shows low variance when repeating 100-sentence experiments, suggesting robustness.

- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—standard CNN classifier and semi-supervised combination similar to prior work; main novelty is application.

- Weakness: incomplete methodological details and missing ablations (region embedding construction, tokenization, OOV handling, whether embeddings are fixed, selection-size tuning, negative sampling comparisons).

- Weakness: baseline coverage incomplete—no comparison to simple discriminative classifiers (SVM/logistic n-grams), sentence weighting, mixture/adaptation baselines, or other neural classifiers (e.g., BiLSTM).

- Weakness: potential tuning/data leakage concerns—dev data used as classifier positives and unclear held-out sets for selection-size tuning.

- Weakness: missing computational/resource reporting (training/inference time, hardware, memory) and hyperparameter sensitivity for scoring millions of sentences.

- Weakness: limited qualitative analysis of selected data and translation error types beyond BLEU improvements.

- Question: which unlabeled corpora and sizes were used to train word2vec per language, and were embeddings trained per language and kept fixed during CNN training?

- Question: how are “seq-regions” and “bow-regions” constructed from embeddings, and how are one-hot and embedding-based regions combined?

- Question: how were numbers of selected sentences chosen, what held-out set was used, and is it distinct from dev and SMT tuning sets?

- Question: can quantitative results for source-only, target-only, and summed-score selection be provided?

- Question: was a linear n-gram classifier (logistic regression/SVM) trained on the same tiny labels as a baseline, and how did it compare?

- Question: was data weighting (soft selection) tried instead of hard selection, and what were the results?

- Additional comment: fix formatting and incomplete entries in the reference list for camera-ready.

- Additional comment: provide runtime/memory reporting and release selection code/configs to aid reproducibility.

- Additional comment: clarify n-gram LM smoothing choices (Witten–Bell vs. Kneser–Ney) per task.
