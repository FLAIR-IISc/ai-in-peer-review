Summary and contributions
This paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. It trains a CNN-based domain classifier using the in-domain dev set (positive) and randomly sampled general-domain data (negative), then scores each source and target sentence separately and selects top-N bilingual pairs (best by sum of scores) to train phrase-based SMT. To mitigate small labeled data, it uses pre-trained word2vec embeddings and combines them with one-hot inputs in a semi-supervised CNN.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical demonstration that a simple semi-supervised CNN classifier for data selection outperforms strong LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM, combination) across four language directions and social media domains, using only 100–6,000 in-domain sentences. Gains up to +3.1 BLEU with 2.5–10% of data; robustness to as few as 100 in-domain sentences.
2) A practical recipe for tiny-in-domain adaptation: one-hot + pre-trained embeddings, scoring both sides of bitext, and tuning selected-data size, with off-the-shelf tools, showing stability and statistical significance.
3) Evidence that selected small subsets can beat both training on all data and, in one case, an in-domain-only subset, highlighting the utility of CNN pooling and embeddings for domain cues.

Strengths
- Strong, multi-setting empirical results: Four directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) with large training pools (9–15M), social media test domains, and consistent, statistically significant gains. The semi-supervised CNN improves over alldata by +3.1, +1.4, +0.7, and +1.4 BLEU, and over LM-based selection by ~0.5 BLEU on average, while using only a small fraction of data.
- Realistic and challenging scenario: Focus on tiny in-domain labeled sets (down to 100 sentences). The “fixed-300k selection” study shows CNN methods degrade gracefully; sscnn still achieves +2.1 BLEU with 100 labeled sentences, with small variance across resamplings. This is a meaningful and under-explored regime.
- Method simplicity and portability: Uses standard CNN text classification (Johnson & Zhang style) and word2vec trained on available unlabeled data; no reliance on domain metadata. Existing toolkits (conText, word2vec) suffice, making it easy to adopt in practice.
- Sensible bilingual selection design: Scoring both source and target sides and using the sum empirically works best; classifying monolingually avoids alignment/feature engineering and is language-agnostic.
- Ablation vs. one-hot: The comparison with ohcnn isolates the benefit of unlabeled-data embeddings (+0.5 BLEU on average), supporting the “semi-supervised” claim with a clear control.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The CNN architecture and semi-supervised use of pre-trained embeddings closely follow prior text classification work; the main contribution is applying this to SMT data selection and combining source/target scores. As a research contribution, this feels incremental relative to Johnson & Zhang and prior selection work.
- Reproducibility gaps: Important details are missing or ambiguous: whether embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned; size/scope/tokenization of the unlabeled corpus used for word2vec per language; exact preprocessing for CNN and SMT; selection of N (held-out set definition and separation from dev/test); classifier training details (epochs, batch size, class balance), and exact BLEU settings (cased/uncased, tokenization). No code/models released.
- Comparison fairness and tuning budget: Selection size N is tuned per method on held-out data, but the procedure is not fully specified; CNN methods may benefit from more hyperparameter tuning than LM baselines. In the zh→en case, a large in-domain subset exists in the pool, potentially inflating gains; quantifying how much of it is recovered by each method would contextualize the +3.1 BLEU claim.
- Efficiency/scalability not reported: There is no discussion of training/inference time or computational cost to score 9–15M sentences with CNNs versus LM-based selection. Practical deployability could be affected by runtime/memory.
- Missing ablations/analyses: The paper mentions that sum of source+target scores is best but does not show numbers. Sensitivity to region size/stride, pooling, number of negatives, and class imbalance is not explored. No qualitative error analysis or examples of correctly/incorrectly selected sentences to illuminate model behavior.

Questions to authors (to clarify during response)
- Are the pre-trained word2vec embeddings kept fixed or fine-tuned during CNN training? What unlabeled corpora and tokenization were used to train them, and what are their sizes per language?
- How exactly is N (the number of selected sentences) chosen for each method? What is the held-out set, and is it disjoint from the dev set used to train the classifier and from the data used for SMT parameter tuning?
- Please report runtime (training + inference) for the CNN selector and total wall-clock to score the full training pool on one task, and compare to n-gram and RNNLM selection.
- Provide results for source-only vs target-only vs sum-of-scores selection. Are the gains from summing consistent across languages?
- In the zh→en condition with a 120k in-domain subset in the pool, what fraction of those sentences do each of the methods retrieve in the top 300–400k? How does performance correlate with recovery rate?
- Did you try a joint bilingual classifier (e.g., concatenating source/target representations or using cross-lingual embeddings) instead of summing two monolingual scores? If so, how does it compare?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, practical paper addressing a timely and important problem—adapting MT with very limited in-domain data. While the modeling novelty is modest, the empirical evidence across multiple language pairs and the robustness at extremely small labeled data sizes are compelling and useful to the community. With clarifications on reproducibility and runtime, I would lean toward accept.
