Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes using a convolutional neural network classifier to score sentence pairs as in-domain vs. out-of-domain for SMT data selection when only tiny in-domain data are available. Two variants are explored: one-hot CNN (supervised) and a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with word2vec embeddings trained on unlabeled general-domain text.
- Empirical evaluation across four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) shows that selecting a small subset (≈2.5–10% of data) via the semi-supervised CNN yields higher BLEU than training on all data and than n-gram/RNNLM-based selection, with especially strong gains when in-domain seed is very small (down to 100 sentences).
- Practical finding that summing source- and target-side CNN scores for selection is best (though no table provided), and that the method can outperform an “in-domain-only” subset in one condition.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and realistic scenario: adaptation with extremely scarce in-domain data. Demonstrates gains of up to +3.1 BLEU over using all data, and consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and social-media test domains.
- Robustness to tiny in-domain samples is convincingly highlighted via a controlled study on zh→en where the in-domain seed is varied from 6k down to 100 sentences; semi-supervised CNN is consistently stronger and more stable than LM-based selectors in the low-sample regime. The small standard deviation reported for three random 100-sentence samples suggests stability.
- The approach is simple, practical, and scalable: train a binary sentence-level classifier with pre-trained embeddings, score both sides, select top-N. It uses widely available toolkits and data, making the idea accessible to practitioners.
- Empirical scope: large training pools (9–15M sentence pairs), multiple domains, and significance testing via bootstrap resampling are positives.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The model is a straightforward application of existing CNN text classification architectures (esp. Johnson & Zhang 2015b) to data selection. The semi-supervised setup (word2vec embeddings + CNN) and region-conv + max pooling are standard. There is no architectural innovation nor new learning objective tailored to SMT selection (e.g., bilingual coupling, margin-based selection, or domain adaptation theory).
- Experimental design and fairness concerns:
  - The selection budget N varies across methods and is tuned per method; there is no comparison at a fixed budget (e.g., all methods at 300K/700K/1.3M), which makes relative effectiveness harder to assess and may favor some methods.
  - The paper says “empirically determine the number of selected sentences … on a separate validation set,” but the positives are the dev set itself; it is unclear what the “separate” set is. Risk of tuning selection size on the same domain sample used to train the classifier.
  - For zh→en, the presence of a 120K in-domain block inside the general pool confounds conclusions; no analysis of overlap between selected sets and that in-domain block, nor whether methods differ mainly in recovering that block.
- Missing ablations and key details:
  - No ablation to quantify the contribution of embeddings vs. one-hot within the same architecture, seq- vs. bow-regions, window/stride sensitivity, max vs. average pooling, or source-only vs. target-only vs. sum (the latter is asserted but not presented).
  - Training details are underspecified: optimizer, learning rate, batch size, epochs/early stopping, regularization (dropout/weight decay), vocabulary sizes, OOV handling, tokenization/casing/segmentation, and whether embeddings are updated or fixed.
  - RNNLM baseline details (hidden size, classes, BPTT length, smoothing) are omitted; an under-tuned RNNLM can be a weak comparator.
- Incomplete baselines:
  - The Moore–Lewis/dual cross-entropy method is cited, but it is unclear whether both source and target LM scores are combined in the same way as prior work; “comblm” combines model types, not necessarily sides. A strong bilingual cross-entropy baseline should be explicit.
  - No simple discriminative selector baselines (e.g., tf-idf logistic regression/SVM, bag-of-ngrams) which are competitive in low-data regimes and would isolate the value of CNNs vs. classic linear models.
- Reproducibility and presentation quality:
  - Data description is vague for social-media sets (no URLs/IDs, licensing notes, or preprocessing pipelines). Word2vec training corpora per language, window size, min-count, negative sampling vs. hierarchical softmax, and training steps are missing.
  - Numerous formatting and reference issues: many citations have missing years/pages (“? 2015”, “\Q2014\E”), typos (“GloV e”), broken footnotes/URLs, inconsistent notation (e.g., “ul(x) is the embed- ding”), and inconsistent table/figure formatting. This falls below conference standards.
- Methodological caveats:
  - The classifier is trained with balanced classes but applied to a highly imbalanced pool; calibration/thresholding is not discussed. Hard-negative mining is mentioned qualitatively but not quantified.
  - Selection is per-sentence monolingual per side; no bilingual interaction in the model despite using bilingual data. No analysis of selection quality (domain relevance vs. translation adequacy/noise), especially for social media where parallel data can be noisy.

Questions to authors
- What is the “separate validation set” used to tune N? If it is not the dev set used for positive labels, please specify its composition and ensure no leakage.
- Did you train distinct CNNs per language for source/target scoring? For en–zh, what corpora were used to train Chinese embeddings, and were embeddings updated during supervised training?
- Can you report results for fixed selection budgets (e.g., 300K, 700K, 1.3M) across all methods to control for N? Also report source-only, target-only, and source+target selection.
- For zh→en, what fraction of the selected 300–400K overlaps with the 120K in-domain subset? How do methods differ in recovering that subset?
- Please provide full RNNLM and CNN training hyperparameters, preprocessing/tokenization details, and links/IDs for social-media datasets to enable reproduction.

Additional comments
- Consider adding a simple tf-idf logistic regression/SVM selector and a properly implemented bilingual Moore–Lewis baseline.
- Include ablations isolating embeddings, region type, pooling, and negative sampling strategy.
- Please fix the numerous reference and formatting errors; they are distracting and hurt readability.
