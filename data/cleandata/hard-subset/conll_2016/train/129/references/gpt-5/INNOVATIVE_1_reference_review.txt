Summary and Contributions
- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier for domain relevance scoring of sentence pairs, enabling data selection for SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain labeled data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) are available. The model combines one-hot inputs with region embeddings from word2vec trained on large unlabeled corpora.
- Demonstrates consistent gains over strong LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) across four language directions and three social-media domains, with sizable improvements at very small selection budgets (2.5–10% of the data). Notably, shows 3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms with only ~300K selected pairs and robustness down to 100 labeled examples.
- Introduces a practical bilingual scoring heuristic (independent source/target CNNs with sum-of-scores selection) that empirically outperforms using either side alone.

Strengths
- Addresses a genuinely hard and relevant setting: adaptation with extremely scarce in-domain labels. Prior selection methods generally presuppose thousands to hundreds of thousands of in-domain sentences; showing strong, stable improvements with 100–800 sentences is both novel and practically impactful.
- Clear empirical improvements over strong, widely used baselines. The semi-supervised CNN outperforms n-gram and RNNLM selection consistently and significantly on 4 language directions. The zh→en result (3.1 BLEU gain; surpassing the “in-domain-only” 120K subset) is especially compelling.
- Solid breadth of evaluation. Experiments span four typologically diverse directions (zh↔en, ar→en, en→es), and three challenging social-media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook). This breadth increases confidence that the effect is not a narrow artifact.
- Strong low-label regime analysis. The controlled study fixing selected MT data to 300K and varying labeled in-domain size is well designed. The stability at 100 labeled pairs (with small standard deviation across three random samples) speaks to robustness, an important practical property.
- Simple, implementable recipe. Using off-the-shelf word2vec and an existing CNN toolkit, plus a straightforward scoring scheme (max pooling, ReLU, region size 5, sum of source/target scores), makes the approach easy to replicate and deploy in production pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate methodologically. The core technique adapts known semi-supervised CNNs with region embeddings (Johnson & Zhang, 2015b) to a domain classification/data selection setting for SMT. While the application and tiny-label focus are valuable, the model itself is a fairly direct instantiation of prior CNN text classification ideas. A comparison to simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with tf-idf/bow-ngrams, or fastText) is missing and would help establish that CNNs (not just any supervised classifier with embeddings) are necessary.
- Fairness/transparency of selection budget tuning. The number of selected sentences is “empirically determined on held-out,” but the held-out data are not clearly defined (is it the same dev set used for CNN training?). If so, there is a risk of overfitting selection size to the same domain evidence used to train the classifier, possibly favoring the proposed method relative to baselines if their budgets were not tuned identically. Please clarify the tuning protocol and ensure equal treatment across methods.
- Computational cost and scalability not reported. Classifying 9–15M sentence pairs on both source and target sides using CNNs can be expensive. Runtime, hardware, and throughput are not reported, nor is a comparison to LM-based selection cost. Without this, it’s hard to assess practical trade-offs, especially for multi-domain, multi-language deployments.
- Limited ablations and analysis. Beyond ohcnn vs sscnn, there is little insight into the contribution of design choices (region size/stride, max vs average pooling, embedding dimensionality, domain of embedding training data). An ablation on using only embeddings (no one-hot), or only source vs only target scoring, and an error analysis (e.g., what types of sentences are newly captured) would strengthen the case.
- Scope restricted to phrase-based SMT. While reasonable given the setup, it would be valuable to at least discuss or pilot whether the selection transfers to NMT training or LM adaptation on the target side (the latter is listed as future work). Given the generality of the method, even a small NMT experiment would broaden impact.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Budget tuning protocol: What held-out set was used to choose the number of selected sentences? Was the same protocol (and same held-out) used for all baselines? If the dev set was used both to train the CNN and to tune selection size, can you provide results using a separate validation set to rule out overfitting?
- Computational efficiency: What is the end-to-end time to train the CNN(s) and score all sentences (per side), and on what hardware? How does this compare to Moore-Lewis and RNNLM selection in wall-clock time?
- Simpler baselines: How would a linear classifier with bag-of-ngrams or tf-idf, or fastText with pre-trained embeddings, perform in the same setup? This would isolate the benefit of CNN’s convolution/pooling vs generic supervised selection.
- Source vs target scoring: Can you quantify the gains from summing source and target scores vs either alone across languages? Do the source and target classifiers disagree in systematic ways (e.g., noisy domains, morphology)?
- Embeddings and OOV: How are OOVs handled in the CNN? What unlabeled corpora were used to train word2vec per language, and how sensitive are results to embedding dimensionality or training data domain?

Overall, this is a strong, practical contribution with compelling results in a genuinely low-label regime. Despite moderate methodological novelty, the demonstrated effectiveness and robustness across languages/domains make it a valuable addition to the adaptation/data selection literature. I lean positive.
