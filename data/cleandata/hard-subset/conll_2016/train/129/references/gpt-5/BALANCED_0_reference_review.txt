Summary
The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. It trains a binary domain classifier with CNNs to label general-domain sentence pairs as in- or out-of-domain and selects the top-N pairs for SMT training. Two classifiers are explored: a supervised one-hot CNN (bag/sequence regions) and a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on unlabeled general-domain text. Source and target sentences are scored separately; selection is based on the sum of both scores. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en SMS, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M sentence pairs show improvements over an alldata baseline and over LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis 3-gram, RNNLM, and a combination). Notably, the semi-supervised CNN yields the largest gains and remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple semi-supervised CNN classifier trained on very small in-domain sets can select parallel data that consistently outperforms standard LM-based selection for SMT across multiple language pairs and domains, with strong gains (up to +3.1 BLEU) and robustness down to ~100 in-domain sentences.
2) Practical selection recipe: scoring both source and target with CNNs and selecting by summed scores, with evidence that this choice is effective across settings.
3) Sensitivity analysis to in-domain labeled data size, demonstrating stability and advantage of the CNN approach under extremely low-label regimes.

Strengths
- Strong and consistent empirical gains across diverse settings: The semi-supervised CNN outperforms both the alldata baseline and three LM-based selection baselines on all four language directions, with statistically significant improvements (e.g., +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS with 3% of training data; up to +1.4 BLEU elsewhere). This breadth increases confidence the approach is generally useful for low-resource adaptation.

- Robustness to tiny in-domain labels: The analysis that varies labeled in-domain size from 6000 down to 100 sentences is valuable. The semi-supervised CNN maintains a sizeable advantage over LM-based methods in the extreme low-label regime (+2.1 BLEU with only 100 sentences), which is precisely the regime of interest and a clear practical contribution.

- Method is simple, scalable, and uses readily available tools: The approach requires no manual features, leverages standard word2vec pretraining, and uses off-the-shelf CNN toolkits. Training is efficient because the labeled set is tiny; scoring can be parallelized. This makes the technique attractive for practitioners.

- Sensible experimental protocol and reporting: Budgets for selected data are tuned on held-out sets; statistical significance via bootstrap resampling is reported; hyperparameters for CNNs and word embeddings are given. The paper also reports an interesting case where an in-domain subset exists in the pool and shows the CNN surpasses the in-domain-only baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core technical idea is a straightforward application of known CNN text classification architectures (one-hot and semi-supervised with pretrained embeddings) to the well-studied problem of SMT data selection. The “semi-supervised” aspect amounts to standard unsupervised embedding pretraining. The contribution is primarily empirical rather than methodological.

- Incomplete baselines and ablations: 
  • Missing comparisons to strong and simple discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM on bag-of-ngrams/TF-IDF, sentence embedding cosine similarity, or IR-style retrieval). It is unclear whether CNNs are necessary versus these lighter-weight alternatives, especially in the tiny-label regime. 
  • LM baselines seem underpowered: 3-gram with Witten-Bell may be weaker than 5-gram KN; bilingual cross-entropy difference on both sides (Axelrod) with tuned LMs is standard. Tools such as XenC or higher-order LMs could strengthen the baseline. 
  • Missing ablations on key design choices: source-only vs target-only vs sum (claimed but not quantified), effect of omitting pretrained embeddings, architecture choices (bow vs seq), and choice of region/window sizes.

- Potential confounds in SMT setup and selection budgets: 
  • All systems for en-target use large external Gigaword LMs; the extent to which selection helps beyond target LM effects is not disentangled. 
  • Selection budgets differ per method (chosen on held-out), which is reasonable but can bias comparisons if some methods benefit from larger budgets; a budget-swept curve per method would clarify trade-offs. 
  • In zh→en, there is a 120k in-domain subset inside the pool; the paper reports surpassing “indomain-only (120k)” but does not analyze overlap/recall. The observed gains may partially reflect recovering much of this subset.

- Reproducibility and detail gaps: Training details for the CNN classifiers (optimizer, epochs, early stopping, regularization, class weighting for imbalanced scoring of the full pool, runtime) are sparse. The negative sampling strategy is briefly mentioned as comparable across two options but without quantitative evidence. It is also unclear whether target-side classifier training assumes availability of target in-domain text (references) in all realistic settings; a source-only scenario would strengthen the practical case.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide quantitative ablations for selection based on source-only, target-only, and sum-of-scores across the four tasks? How large is the gain from combining both sides?

- How does your method compare to simple, strong baselines such as: (a) logistic regression on bag-of-ngrams/TF-IDF; (b) cosine similarity using averaged word2vec sentence embeddings; (c) a multi-layer perceptron on bag-of-ngrams? These could clarify whether CNN-specific modeling is crucial.

- Can you strengthen the LM-based baselines by using 5-gram KN LMs and bilingual cross-entropy difference (both sides) as in Axelrod et al., and/or XenC? Do your conclusions hold under these settings?

- Please report ablations isolating the contribution of pretrained embeddings: one-hot CNN vs semisupervised CNN vs embeddings-only (e.g., average or simple CNN over embeddings). How sensitive are results to embedding dimension and pretraining corpus size?

- For zh→en, what fraction of the known 120k in-domain subset is recovered by your top-N selection? Does performance drop if you remove those sentences from the pool?

- What are the training and scoring costs (time, GPU/CPU) for CNN vs RNNLM selection on the largest corpus? This would help assess scalability.

- In scenarios where only source-side in-domain text is available (common in practice), how does performance change if you train only the source classifier and select by source scores?

Decision
Weak reject. The paper presents a practical and effective application with convincing empirical gains and a valuable analysis in the tiny-label regime. However, methodological novelty is limited, and key comparisons/ablations are missing, making it hard to attribute the advantage specifically to the proposed semi-supervised CNN over simpler alternatives or stronger LM baselines. Addressing the questions above (particularly stronger baselines and ablations) could move this to a solid accept.
