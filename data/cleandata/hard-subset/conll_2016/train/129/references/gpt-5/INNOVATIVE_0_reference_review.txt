Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles MT domain adaptation when only a tiny in-domain set (as little as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose using a semi-supervised CNN classifier to score sentence pairs for in-domain relevance, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora. Source and target sides are scored independently and combined, and the top-N sentence pairs are used to train an SMT system. Experiments across four language directions and social-media test domains show consistent gains over standard LM-based data selection, with strong improvements even under extremely small labeled data.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A practical, semi-supervised CNN-based data selection framework for SMT that explicitly targets the “tiny in-domain data” regime, combining one-hot features and region embeddings to robustly identify domain-relevant segments.
2) Strong empirical evidence across four language directions and realistic domains (sms, tweets, Facebook) that the method outperforms competitive LM-based selection (n-gram and RNNLM), including stability and gains with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences.
3) A simple but effective bilingual selection strategy (independent source/target scoring and sum) that consistently delivers best performance and selects compact training subsets (2.5–10% of full data).

Strengths
- Focus on a high-impact, under-served regime: The “tiny in-domain” setting (≈100–1,600 sentences) is both realistic and hard. The paper clearly motivates why classic LM-based selection needs substantial in-domain text, and demonstrates that the proposed approach still works when LM-based methods falter or become unstable.
- Consistent, meaningful gains with compact data: Across zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh, sscnn outperforms both full-data baselines and strong LM-based selections, often with only a few percent of the training data. The reported +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms and up to +1.4 BLEU elsewhere are practically significant for SMT.
- Robustness under extreme label scarcity: The analysis varying the size of the in-domain labeled set is compelling: with only 100 sentences, sscnn still yields +2.1 BLEU, and results are stable across multiple random draws. This convincingly supports the core claim.
- Method simplicity and generality: The architecture is straightforward to reproduce (window size, stride, ReLU, pooling specified), uses standard tools (word2vec, conText), and requires minimal domain engineering. The method is conceptually applicable beyond bilingual selection and beyond SMT.
- Bilingual scoring design: Scoring source and target independently and summing is a sound, easy-to-implement idea that yields clear gains, reinforcing that cross-lingual signals help select better parallel subsets.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior CNN text classification: The CNN design and semi-supervised setup closely follow Johnson & Zhang and standard word2vec pretraining. The primary novelty lies in the application to SMT data selection under tiny in-domain data and the bilingual scoring scheme. While I value the bold shift in application focus, the modeling itself is not novel.
- Baselines omit other strong adaptation paradigms: Comparisons are restricted to LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, and a simple combo). Prior SMT adaptation methods (mixture/instance weighting, VSM/distributed VSM, phrase-level weighting, decipherment, or bilingual cross-entropy selection variants) are not included. This leaves open whether sscnn’s advantage persists against these alternatives, especially those that do not require large in-domain sets.
- Potential conflation of dev usage: The dev set is used as positive (in-domain) supervision. It is unclear whether the same dev is also used for MT tuning, or whether an independent held-out set is used to choose N (the number of selected sentences). If dev serves multiple roles, there is a risk of overfitting selection to the tuning domain characteristics. Please clarify the data splits.
- Reporting gaps and ablations: While the paper states that summing source+target scores is best, the supporting numbers are not shown. Similarly, an ablation contrasting one-hot only vs embedding only vs combined (and whether embeddings are fixed vs fine-tuned) would strengthen the causal story. Runtime/efficiency comparisons vs RNNLM selection (training and scoring throughput) are also missing.
- The zh→en in-domain subset confound: The training pool contains a 120K in-domain subset. Although the paper discusses this and shows sscnn surpasses an “in-domain only” baseline, a more diagnostic analysis (e.g., how many of these in-domain sentences are recovered by each method, and performance when excluding them from the pool) would contextualize the large gains.

Questions to Authors
- Data splits: Is the same dev set used both as positive examples for the CNN and for MT tuning/weight optimization? If so, did you perform any safeguards (e.g., separate dev-in for classifier training and dev-tune for MT)? What dataset was used to choose N (selected size)?
- Embedding usage: Are the pre-trained word embeddings kept fixed or fine-tuned during CNN training? If fine-tuned, with what learning rate and regularization? Any impact on performance?
- Bilingual scoring: Please provide results for source-only, target-only, and source+target sum across tasks to substantiate the claim that the sum is best.
- Efficiency: How does sscnn training/scoring time compare to RNNLM selection for the same data volume? What is the approximate wall-clock for selecting 1M sentences?
- Generality: Have you tried using the method for monolingual LM data selection (e.g., Gigaword) as hinted in future work, and does the “tiny in-domain” advantage persist?

Overall recommendation
This paper offers a clear, practically important contribution: a simple, semi-supervised CNN framework that enables effective domain adaptation with extremely scarce in-domain labels. Despite modest modeling novelty and missing comparisons to some adaptation baselines, the empirical gains, robustness under tiny supervision, and multilingual breadth make this a compelling direction. I lean toward acceptance given the significance of the setting and the demonstrated impact.
