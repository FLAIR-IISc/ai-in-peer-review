Summary and claimed contributions
- Problem: Adapt SMT to a new domain with only a tiny in-domain seed (hundreds of sentences). The paper proposes selecting a small, high-quality subset from very large general-domain parallel data using a sentence-level domain classifier.
- Method: A CNN-based sentence classifier trained with very limited labeled in-domain positives and randomly sampled negatives. A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot features with region embeddings derived from pre-trained word2vec, and trains separate classifiers on source and target; selection uses the sum of scores.
- Empirical findings: Across four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook), selecting only 2.5–10% of the training data yields systems that outperform using all data. The semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforms n-gram and RNNLM cross-entropy-difference baselines and a one-hot CNN, with up to +3.1 BLEU (zh→en). Notably, with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, the approach still yields +2.1 BLEU in zh→en.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that semi-supervised CNNs provide robust, strong data selection for SMT domain adaptation with extremely small in-domain seeds, outperforming established LM-based selection.
2) A practical selection pipeline that scores both sides of a parallel sentence and scales to tens of millions of sentence pairs, with consistent gains across four language directions and social-media domains.
3) An empirical analysis of sensitivity to in-domain seed size down to 100 sentences, showing stability and superiority over LM-based selection in the tiny-data regime.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, realistic setting: fast adaptation with only tiny in-domain parallel data, particularly relevant for social-media domains where labeled data are scarce.
- Strong, consistent improvements with substantial data reduction: sscnn beats alldata by +3.1/+1.4/+0.7/+1.4 BLEU in four directions while using only 2.5–10% of data; it also beats the best LM-based baseline in every case.
- Careful and relevant baselines: compares against Moore–Lewis and RNNLM-based cross-entropy difference and their combination, which are the strongest widely used selection baselines in SMT.
- Sensitivity to seed size: the curve on zh→en shows that with 100–800 labeled sentences, LM-based selection degrades/plateaus while sscnn remains robust, indicating the method’s particular value in the very-low-label regime.
- Practicality and reproducibility of tools: uses publicly available conText and word2vec; describes high-level hyperparameters; selection by summing source and target scores is simple and empirically best.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: the semi-supervised CNN closely follows Johnson & Zhang (2015b) applied to domain classification. The contribution is primarily an application to SMT data selection rather than a new model. A stronger case would include comparisons against other discriminative selection/weighting frameworks (e.g., logistic regression with n-gram features, instance weighting, VSM/mixture models), or a bilingual CNN/joint model.
- Incomplete experimental clarity and potential circularity: it is stated that the number of selected sentences N is chosen on a “held-out” set, but only dev and test are available; dev is also used as positive labels for training the classifier and for SMT tuning. If the same dev set serves both roles, this risks bias. The protocol for choosing N needs to be clearly separated from data used to train the classifier and to tune SMT.
- Missing training details that affect reproducibility: no learning rates, epochs, optimizer, regularization (e.g., dropout), early stopping, or whether pre-trained embeddings are frozen or fine-tuned. The construction of “segment embeddings” from word2vec in Eq. (2) is under-specified (are they averaged, convolved, or learned region embeddings?), and the exact architecture when combining one-hot and embedding features is unclear.
- Baseline parity and breadth: for LM baselines, it is unclear if bilingual cross-entropy difference (summing source and target) is used, to match the proposed bilingual scoring. Other competitive sentence embedding baselines (e.g., averaging word2vec with cosine similarity, doc2vec/paragraph vectors) and discriminative weighting approaches (Matsoukas et al., Foster et al., Chen et al.) are not included.
- Confounding data characteristics: the zh→en corpus contains a 120K in-domain sms subset inside the general data; the method’s ability to rediscover it may inflate gains. The paper does not report results when that subset is explicitly removed from the pool, nor similar seed-size curves for other language pairs. More analysis (e.g., examples of selected sentences, domain label distributions) would strengthen claims about generality.
- Efficiency not reported: CNN scoring of tens of millions of sentences can be computationally heavy relative to n-gram selection. Runtime and resource comparisons would help practitioners.

Questions to authors
- What exact data were used to select the number of sentences N? Please clarify whether a separate held-out set (distinct from the dev used as positives and from the SMT tuning set) was used. If not, can you re-run with a clean split to avoid bias?
- Are pre-trained word embeddings updated during supervised CNN training, or kept fixed? How are “segment embeddings” ul(x) produced from word2vec (averaging, a separate convolution layer, or region embedding learning as in Johnson & Zhang 2015b)?
- For source/target scoring, did you train separate monolingual classifiers and sum their posterior scores? Did you also try: (i) using only source or only target scores (report numbers), and/or (ii) a joint bilingual classifier (e.g., concatenating source/target representations)?
- For LM baselines, did you compute cross-entropy difference on both source and target sides and combine scores? If not, please add this stronger baseline. Also, what vocabulary and smoothing choices were used for RNNLM selection?
- Can you provide selection-time and training-time costs (CPU/GPU hours) for sscnn vs ngram/rnnlm, and memory footprints?
- For zh→en, if you remove the 120K sms subset from the candidate pool, what are the gains? Can you plot seed-size curves for another language pair (e.g., ar→en) to demonstrate robustness?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with practical impact and a well-motivated problem. While the modeling novelty is modest, the results are convincing and the tiny-seed setting is valuable. Clarifying the protocol, expanding baselines, and adding efficiency and ablation details would strengthen the work.
