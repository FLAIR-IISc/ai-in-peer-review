Summary
The paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. It frames data selection as sentence-level domain classification and proposes a CNN-based classifier trained on very small in-domain samples (positive) and random general-domain sentences (negative). A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot inputs with word2vec embeddings learned from large unlabeled data. The model scores both source and target sentences; top-N sentence pairs by summed scores are used to train phrase-based SMT systems. Experiments on four language directions (9–15M sentence pairs) and social-media test domains report that semi-supervised CNN selection outperforms using all data and LM-based selection (Moore–Lewis with n-gram and RNNLM).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection under extremely low in-domain supervision, with evidence that it can work with ≤800 and even ~100 in-domain sentences (on one task).
2) Large-scale empirical evaluation across four language directions, with consistent improvements over “use-all” and LM-based baselines, and significance testing.
3) Practical recipe (classify both sides; sum scores) and implementation details that may be of interest for practitioners.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and realistic scenario: adaptation with very scarce in-domain data (100–800 sentences). The demonstrated robustness at the low-data regime (on zh→en) is practically valuable.
- Solid scale and breadth of evaluation: four translation directions, tens of millions of sentences, social-media domains, and consistent improvement over using all data.
- Semi-supervised approach is sensible: leveraging unlabeled general-domain data via word embeddings to compensate for tiny labeled sets is a reasonable and established tactic, here applied to MT data selection.
- Reporting quality: comparisons to n-gram and RNNLM-based selection, significance tests, and some analysis (source vs target vs sum; mention of a 120k in-domain subcorpus case) increase credibility.
- Simplicity and portability: relies on standard toolkits (word2vec, phrase-based SMT), making potential adoption easier.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and methodologically underdeveloped. Using CNNs for sentence-level domain classification with pre-trained embeddings is a straightforward transfer of known text classification techniques; the contribution is largely empirical. The paper lacks ablations to substantiate the specific architectural choices (one-hot vs bow vs seq; with/without embeddings; source-only vs target-only vs sum; window sizes, etc.).
- Potential unfairness/under-optimization of baselines. The Moore–Lewis/Axelrod implementation details are insufficient: it is unclear if bilingual cross-entropy difference with both sides optimally combined and properly tuned was used; “comblm” uses equal weights without tuning; RNNLM settings (e.g., network size, training regimen) are not justified. Stronger non-neural baselines (e.g., tf-idf/VSM or Rocchio selection, mixture/instance weighting, Chen et al. 2013/2014) are cited but not compared.
- Risk of data leakage and unclear validation. The dev set is used as positive (training) data, and the number of selected sentences N is tuned on a “held-out set” that is not defined. If the same domain dev is reused for both classifier training and selection tuning, results may be optimistic. Clarification of data splits is essential.
- Generality of the “tiny data” claim is not established. The 100–800 sentence robustness is demonstrated only for zh→en; similar analyses are missing for the other three directions. In addition, the largest reported gain (3.1 BLEU) occurs in a setting where a large in-domain subset exists within the general pool, confounding the interpretation (simple methods can also exploit such structure, and the gain over an “in-domain only” baseline is only +0.3 BLEU).
- Missing cost/efficiency analysis. Training CNNs and scoring both sides across 9–15M sentence pairs is computationally nontrivial; there is no reporting of runtime, hardware, or complexity relative to LM-based selection. Practical deployability is hard to assess.
- Lack of classifier diagnostics and selection behavior analysis. No classification metrics (precision/recall), no analysis of selected subsets (domain distribution, overlap with known in-domain segments), and limited discussion of negative sampling strategies diminish methodological transparency.
- Presentation issues. References are incomplete/garbled in places, and important experimental details are scattered or omitted, making reproduction difficult.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Did your LM-based baselines combine source- and target-side scores in the bilingual Moore–Lewis/Axelrod manner with tuned combination weights? What were the RNNLM hyperparameters and training regimen? Did you tune “comblm” weights?
- Validation: What exact data were used to tune CNN hyperparameters and select N? Was there a separate validation set disjoint from the dev set used for positive labels? If not, can you provide results with a clean split?
- Generality: Can you report the “tiny in-domain” learning curves (like Figure 1) for the other three language directions?
- Ablations: What is the contribution of embeddings versus one-hot alone? How do source-only, target-only, and sum selection compare across tasks? Any results with bilingual embeddings or joint models?
- Efficiency: What is the end-to-end runtime and compute footprint for sscnn versus n-gram/RNNLM selection on your largest setting?
- Fair comparisons: Did you evaluate simple VSM/tf-idf or SVM/LogReg sentence classifiers for selection (e.g., Chen et al., 2013/2014)? If not, can you add them?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the empirical results are promising, but the methodological novelty is modest and several critical comparisons/controls and clarifications are missing. Strengthening baselines, clarifying validation, adding ablations, and extending the tiny-data analysis beyond one task would substantially improve the paper.
