- Paper tackles SMT domain adaptation with extremely small in-domain samples (as few as 100 pairs) using CNN sentence-level domain classifiers to select high-relevance bilingual data.
- Two classifier variants: supervised one-hot CNN (ohcnn) and semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) that augments inputs with pre-trained embeddings; source and target scored independently and combined at selection time.
- Main contribution 1: sscnn enables effective data selection under extreme label scarcity, outperforming LM-based baselines (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM) across four language directions with up to +3.1 BLEU.
- Main contribution 2: Practical selection recipe combining source+target CNN scores and pre-trained embeddings, validated at scale on 9–15M sentence-pair corpora from heterogeneous sources.
- Main contribution 3: Empirical analysis showing CNN-based selection is stable and superior as labeled data is reduced, including controlled experiments from 6K → 100 labeled examples.
- Strength: Strong empirical performance in low-label regimes with statistically significant BLEU gains, notably +2.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS with only 100 labeled pairs.
- Strength: Clear, reproducible pipeline and well-motivated architectural choices (windowed convolution, max-pooling, pre-trained embeddings).
- Strength: Appropriate baselines (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM) and use of bootstrap significance testing with validation for selection sizes.
- Strength: Cross-lingual experiments across Arabic, Chinese, English, Spanish and large corpora support generality of approach.
- Strength: Informative labeled-sample-size analysis illustrating when CNN selection outperforms LM-based methods and stability across small random samples.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—application of known CNN techniques to selection rather than new modeling innovations.
- Weakness: Missing strong non-neural discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM on tf-idf or n-grams) to isolate CNN-specific benefits.
- Weakness: Potential data leakage/duplication concerns (especially zh→en SMS) not analyzed—deduplication/overlap stats needed.
- Weakness: Incomplete ablations—missing source-only vs target-only, ohcnn vs sscnn controlled comparisons, score-combination methods, embedding choices, negative sampling effects.
- Weakness: Practical cost/fairness info missing—no runtime/compute comparisons, GPU/CPU usage, epochs, or details on tuning budget parity across methods.
- Question 1: Report overlap/near-duplicate rates between dev/test and training data (esp. zh→en SMS) and results after deduplication.
- Question 2: Compare selection using simple linear classifiers (logreg/SVM on tf-idf or char n-grams) trained on the same tiny labeled set.
- Question 3: Provide ablations for source-only, target-only, and sum scoring with selected sizes chosen by validation for at least one language pair.
- Question 4: Clarify tokenization/segmentation for Arabic/Chinese, embedding training details (corpus size, epochs, window, subword), and quantify sscnn vs ohcnn gains under identical hyperparameters.
- Question 5: Report training/inference times for ohcnn/sscnn vs n-gram/RNNLM baselines, hardware used, epochs, and negative set sizes.
- Question 6: Did the authors try instance weighting, adding selected data to full corpus, or other selection strategies and how do those compare?
- Additional comment: Clarify whether classifier dev set was also used for SMT tuning and how validation set for N was constructed to avoid overfitting.
- Additional comment: Consider reporting TER or chrF in addition to BLEU.
- Additional comment: Suggest exploring bilingual/joint classifiers (concatenated source–target or cross-lingual embeddings) as a potentially informative experiment.
- Overall assessment: Solid, practical contribution showing sscnn-based selection works under extreme label scarcity, but stronger baselines and additional analyses are needed to fully validate and justify broader adoption.
