Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available (as few as ~100 sentence pairs). It proposes to train a sentence-level domain classifier using a CNN, with a semi-supervised variant that combines one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled data. The classifier assigns in-domain relevance scores to source and target sentences; the top-N sentence pairs are selected to train a phrase-based SMT system.
- Contributions:
  1) Empirical finding: semi-supervised CNN-based sentence selection substantially outperforms strong LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) across four translation directions/domains, especially in the low in-domain data regime.
  2) Method: a simple two-input CNN classifier for domain relevance that fuses one-hot and pretrained embedding features at the region level, and uses both source and target sentence scores (summed) for selection.
  3) Evidence of practicality at scale: results on large training corpora (9–15M pairs) and challenging social-media test domains, with selection sizes of only 2.5–10% of the data yielding better MT than using all data.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with tiny in-domain data, supported by significance testing. The semi-supervised CNN yields +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS with only 300K selected pairs (~2.5% of 12.2M), and consistent gains of +0.7 to +1.4 BLEU on ar→en, en→es, en→zh. The stability study down to 100 labeled sentences (average BLEU 25.03, stdev 0.12) is compelling evidence that the approach works in the truly low-resource adaptation regime. This directly addresses a realistic and difficult setting.
- Competitive and appropriate baselines. The paper compares to widely used selection methods: Moore-Lewis with 3-gram LMs, RNNLM selection, and their combination, and shows advantages of the CNN approach across tasks. This situates the work within established SMT adaptation literature and demonstrates a clear improvement over state of the art circa the cited work.
- Sensible and well-motivated model design. The use of CNNs for text classification is well grounded. The semi-supervised variant (Equation 2) that fuses one-hot and embedding-derived region features is a straightforward yet effective way to leverage unlabeled data, and aligns with prior work (Johnson & Zhang). The decision to score both source and target sentences and to use the sum is reasonable and supported by empirical observation.
- Scale and practical relevance. Experiments span four language directions with millions of training pairs and social media domains where in-domain data scarcity is realistic. The finding that selecting a small subset can outperform training on all data is practically valuable, with potential compute/storage savings.
- Clarity and reproducibility details. Key hyperparameters are stated (region/stride size, number of filters, nonlinearity, pooling). Off-the-shelf toolkits are used for CNN and word2vec. Statistical testing procedure is reported.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core technique—CNN text classification with pretrained embeddings for semi-supervised learning—is an adaptation of known methods to data selection. The paper’s novelty lies more in the application and empirical study than in new modeling ideas. A stronger methodological contribution (e.g., architecture or loss tailored to domain relevance, or bilingual joint modeling) would strengthen the case.
- Incomplete ablations and analyses. While the paper states that summing source and target scores works best, it does not quantify the comparison to source-only or target-only scoring. Similarly, the contributions of seq- vs bow-regions, or of one-hot vs embeddings, are not disentangled. An ablation isolating each component’s impact would clarify where the gains come from.
- Potential confounds in the zh→en SMS setting. The presence of a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain pool is acknowledged. The paper claims to outperform training on that subset, but does not analyze how many selected sentences come from that subset or whether the CNN is primarily recovering it. A breakdown would help interpret the large gains and the generality of the approach.
- Selection size and tuning protocol risk overfitting to domain. The number of selected sentences is “empirically determined” on held-out data. It is not fully clear whether the same dev set is used both as positive examples for training the classifier and for tuning selection size and SMT tuning, which could lead to optimistic estimates. A cleaner separation of data for classifier training, selection-size tuning, and SMT tuning would strengthen the validity.
- Missing comparisons and efficiency/reporting gaps. There is no comparison to simple discriminative sentence selection baselines (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with bag-of-ngrams or doc2vec/sentence embeddings) that might be strong in the tiny-data setting. Runtime and computational cost of classifying tens of millions of sentences (source and target) with CNN are not reported; practical throughput would be useful for adoption.

Questions to authors
- Data splits: Which sets are used for (a) positive examples to train the CNN, (b) tuning the number of selected sentences, and (c) SMT MERT/tuning? Are these strictly disjoint? If not, can you provide results with a cleaner split?
- Source vs target scoring: What are the BLEU results when selecting by source-only and target-only scores, and how often do they disagree? Any insights by language direction?
- Ablations: What is the performance of ohcnn-only vs embeddings-only vs the combined model, holding selection size fixed? Likewise, seq- vs bow-regions?
- zh→en SMS in-domain subset: What fraction of sscnn-selected sentences comes from the 120K in-domain subset? Do gains persist if those are removed from the pool?
- Efficiency: What is the time to score all sentences for a language pair, and how does it compare to LM-based scoring?

Overall evaluation
This paper addresses an important and practical problem—domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data—and demonstrates clear, significant gains over strong baselines on large-scale SMT tasks. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the empirical contribution is solid and relevant to the community. With added ablations and clarifications on data usage, I would lean toward acceptance.
