Summary and main contributions
- The paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. It proposes using a sentence-level domain classifier based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select a small in-domain-like subset from large general-domain corpora for training phrase-based SMT systems.
- The primary contribution is empirical: a semi-supervised CNN (pretrained word embeddings + one-hot inputs) consistently outperforms strong LM-based data selection methods (Moore–Lewis with n-grams, RNNLM, and their combination) across four translation directions, often using only 2.5–10% of the full data. The biggest gain is +3.1 BLEU (zh→en sms), and the method remains effective with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU).
- A secondary contribution is a practical recipe for scoring source and target sides independently with the CNN and selecting by the sum, which empirically works best. The paper also provides evidence of stability under tiny labeled data and across multiple domains (sms, tweets, Facebook).

Strengths
- Strong, practical empirical gains under realistic constraints. Improvements over training on all data and over established LM-based selection are consistent across four language directions with large training sets (9–15M pairs). The largest gain (+3.1 BLEU with only 300K selected pairs) is meaningful for SMT, and even the smaller gains (+0.7–1.4 BLEU) are competitive given the small selection sizes.
- Focus on a relevant and challenging scenario. Adapting to social media with minimal labeled data is both timely and hard; the paper demonstrates that semi-supervised CNNs are robust in this low-resource-in-domain setting, including the extreme case of 100 labeled pairs.
- Sensible semi-supervised design improves stability. The integration of pretrained word embeddings into the CNN leverages abundant unlabeled general-domain text, which plausibly explains the robustness to tiny in-domain labels; the experiment varying labeled size and the low variance across multiple random 100-sentence samples support this claim.
- Comparisons to strong baselines with significance testing. The baselines implement widely used cross-entropy difference selection with both n-gram and RNNLMs, and results include bootstrap significance markers. Hyperparameters and toolkits (conText, word2vec) are specified, aiding interpretability.
- Practicality and simplicity. The method is conceptually simple (binary sentence classification) and easy to deploy, yet yields substantial computational savings by training on a fraction of the data while improving BLEU.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The semi-supervised CNN architecture (region embeddings from unlabeled data + one-hot regions) follows Johnson & Zhang (2015). The contribution is largely an application to SMT data selection with minor twists (scoring both sides and summing). Ablations beyond one-hot vs semi-supervised are minimal, so it is hard to isolate what aspects are most responsible for gains.
- Potential data leakage/unclear validation protocol. The paper states it uses the dev set as positive labeled examples, yet also claims to choose the number of selected sentences on a “separate validation set.” The data setting section lists only dev and test. If the selection size is tuned on dev used to train the classifier, overfitting is a concern. The exact split (e.g., dev-train/dev-val) is not described.
- Reproducibility gaps and missing implementation details. The SMT pipeline is described only as “standard phrase-based SMT,” without precise details on tokenization/segmentation (especially for Chinese), truecasing, tuning algorithm (MERT/MIRA/PRO), LM training data, vocabulary handling, or exact corpora subsets. No code or selected-sentence lists are released. This limits faithful replication and assessment of confounders.
- Baseline breadth and diagnostic analysis are limited. There is no comparison to simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., tf-idf + logistic regression/SVM) or modern non-convolutional neural classifiers, which would help determine whether convolution is necessary. There is also no random selection control at matched sizes to quantify the net benefit of learned selection. Classifier performance (accuracy/precision-recall) is not reported, and there is no analysis of overlap with known in-domain subsets (for zh→en) or per-length/domain breakdowns.
- Computational considerations are unreported. Scoring 9–15M sentence pairs on both source and target is nontrivial; the paper does not report runtime, memory, or throughput, nor contrast with LM-based selection costs. This matters for practical deployment claims.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How exactly was the “separate validation set” constructed for determining the number of selected sentences? Did you split the dev set into train/validation? Please specify splits per task and confirm no test leakage.
- What precise SMT setup was used: toolkit, tuning algorithm, tokenization/truecasing, Chinese segmentation, social-media normalization, and LM training data for each direction? Any domain-specific preprocessing?
- What are the domain classifier metrics (accuracy/AUC) on held-out validation? How do they correlate with downstream BLEU vs. selected set size?
- For zh→en, where a 120K in-domain subset exists within the general pool, how much of it is recovered by your top selections? Does the CNN go beyond simply rediscovering that subset?
- Did you evaluate simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., tf-idf + logistic regression or linear SVM) and a random selection baseline at matched sizes? If so, how do they compare? If not, can you provide at least one such baseline?
- Are embeddings fixed or fine-tuned during CNN training? How sensitive are results to embedding dimension, training data, or region size/stride/pooling choices?
- What are the computational costs (training time for CNN, scoring throughput) compared to LM-based selection?

Overall assessment of reviewing criteria
- Novelty: Moderate (application-focused; limited model innovation).
- Significance: High practical impact in low in-domain data settings; consistent gains across multiple directions and domains.
- Soundness: Generally sound, but the validation protocol and missing diagnostics should be clarified.
- Clarity: Mostly clear; some typos and reference formatting issues; key methodological and data-split details need elaboration.
- Reproducibility: Partial; toolkits and some hyperparameters given, but important SMT and preprocessing details are missing.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the validation protocol and adding a simple discriminative baseline or random selection control to solidify the empirical claims.
