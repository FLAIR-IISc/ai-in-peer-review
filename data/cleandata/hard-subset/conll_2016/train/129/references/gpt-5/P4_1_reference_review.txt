Summary
The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data (hundreds to a few thousand sentences) is available. It formulates data selection as a sentence-level domain classification problem and proposes CNN-based classifiers: (a) a supervised one-hot CNN using bag-of-words and sequence regions, and (b) a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled general-domain text. Separate classifiers are trained for source and target sides using the dev set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; sentence pairs are ranked by the sum of source and target scores, and the top N are used to train phrase-based SMT. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) with 9–15M sentence-pair pools and social-media test domains show consistent gains over training on all data and over LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). Notably, the semi-supervised CNN achieves up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms with ~3% of the data and remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU).

Contributions (as assessed)
1) An effective semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation under extremely low in-domain supervision, consistently outperforming strong LM-based baselines across four language directions.

2) An empirical demonstration that combining source- and target-side monolingual domain classifiers and using pre-trained word embeddings enables robust selection even with ≤800 positive examples, with stability shown at 100 examples.

3) A large-scale, multi-language evaluation showing that small selected subsets (2.5–10% of the pool) can surpass systems trained on all data and, in one case, even a 120K in-domain subset embedded in the pool.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across multiple language pairs and domains: The semi-supervised CNN yields statistically significant improvements over both the alldata baseline and LM-based selection (up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en, +1.4 on ar→en and en→zh, +0.7 on en→es), while using a small fraction of data. This demonstrates practical utility and scalability of the approach.

- Robustness with tiny in-domain supervision: With only 100 labeled in-domain sentences, the method still improves by +2.1 BLEU (zh→en), and variance across three random 100-sentence samples is low (std 0.12 BLEU). This directly addresses the stated low-resource adaptation setting and surpasses LM-based methods under this constraint.

- Sound classification design leveraging unlabeled data: The semi-supervised CNN architecture integrates one-hot regions with embeddings trained on large unlabeled corpora, aligning with established semi-supervised CNN practices. This plausibly explains effectiveness on sparse, domain-specific indicators and unseen but semantically related words.

- Fair and relevant baselines with significance testing: Comparisons include Moore-Lewis (n-gram), RNNLM-based selection, and their combination, with selection size tuned on held-out data, and bootstrap resampling used for significance. This situates the contribution relative to commonly used SMT data selection methods.

- Practical details for replication of the core idea: The paper specifies CNN architecture choices (region size, stride, pooling, nonlinearity, neurons), toolkits (conText, word2vec), and the selection protocol (separate source/target classifiers, score summation), enabling reimplementation of the main method.

Weaknesses
- Limited baseline coverage for classification-based selection: The study does not compare against strong non-neural classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with bag-of-ngrams, fastText) trained on the same tiny in-domain data and using pre-trained embeddings. Such baselines could clarify whether CNN-specific modeling (convolutions/pooling) is necessary versus simpler classifiers with embeddings.

- Ambiguity around validation and potential overfitting: The dev set is used as positive training data; selection size N is said to be chosen on “held-out data,” but it is unclear what data constitute this held-out set and how leakage between tuning and evaluation is avoided. Clarification is needed to ensure no circularity with the dev/test domain.

- Insufficient ablations on design choices: The paper reports that summing source and target scores works best, but does not quantify the contribution of (a) target-side classification, (b) using embeddings versus one-hot only, (c) bow- vs seq-regions, or (d) negative sampling strategy (random vs “second-round negatives”). Such ablations would strengthen the technical understanding of what drives gains.

- Reproducibility gaps in data and training specifics: Preprocessing/tokenization, exact corpora used for word2vec per language, CNN optimization details (optimizer, learning rate, epochs, regularization/dropout), and selection thresholds are not fully specified. Given the sensitivity of small-sample training, these details matter for reproducibility.

- Confounding factor in zh→en due to embedded in-domain subset: The large gain on zh→en is influenced by a known 120K in-domain subset in the pool. An analysis of overlap between selected data and this subset, and analogous analyses for other language pairs (e.g., which source corpora are being selected), would contextualize the improvements and generality.

Questions to Authors
- What dataset was used as the “held-out” set to tune the number of selected sentences N? If the dev set is used for positive training, how is overfitting avoided in tuning N?

- Can you report ablations on: (i) source-only vs target-only vs source+target scoring; (ii) semi-supervised CNN vs one-hot CNN vs a linear classifier with pre-trained embeddings; (iii) bow- vs seq-regions; (iv) with/without pre-trained embeddings?

- For the zh→en sms task, what proportion of the selected sentences overlaps with the 120K in-domain subset? For other tasks, can you characterize which general-domain subcorpora are preferentially selected?

- What are the training details for the CNNs (optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, epochs, early stopping, regularization)? Was dropout used?

- How are the word embeddings trained for each language (corpus composition, tokenization, vocabulary cutoffs)? Are embeddings updated during CNN training or kept fixed?

- How computationally expensive is the approach at selection time (throughput for classifying 10–15M sentences per side), and how does this compare to LM-based selection?

Overall, the paper is technically sound and clearly written, with substantive empirical evidence that semi-supervised CNN-based selection is effective for SMT adaptation under extreme in-domain scarcity. The main limitations concern missing ablations and comparisons to simpler classifiers, and clarity on validation and data specifics.
