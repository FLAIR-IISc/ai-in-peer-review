Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes using a semi-supervised CNN classifier to perform sentence-level data selection for SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) are available. The CNN combines one-hot region features with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled general-domain data, and scores both source and target sentences, selecting pairs by the sum of scores.
- The approach is evaluated across four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and social-media–like test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook). It consistently outperforms strong LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination), while using only 2.5–10% of training data.
- The work demonstrates notable robustness in the extreme low-resource adaptation regime: with only 100 in-domain sentences, the semi-supervised CNN yields up to +2.1 BLEU, with very low variance.

Strengths
- Novel and timely conceptual shift for data selection under tiny in-domain supervision. Moving from LM perplexity-based selection to a semi-supervised CNN that aggregates local n-gram evidence via convolution and exploits pretrained embeddings is a clear, bold idea. The bilingual scoring (source and target, summed) is simple and effective.
- Strong empirical gains with high practical impact. Across four language directions and realistic social-media domains, the method consistently beats all LM-based baselines. Highlights include +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms with ~3% of the data, and +1.4 BLEU on ar→en and en→zh. The method even surpasses training on a 120K in-domain subset in one condition.
- Particularly compelling in the extreme low-label setting. The analysis varying the number of labeled in-domain sentences shows the semi-supervised CNN clearly dominates when ≤800 sentences are available and remains stable at 100 sentences (+2.1 BLEU, σ≈0.12 over three random samples). This is precisely the challenging regime the paper aims to address and where prior LM-based selection struggles.
- Methodological simplicity and reproducibility. The setup is transparent (word2vec skip-gram embeddings, region size/stride, ReLU, max pooling), tools are publicly available, and significance testing is reported. The approach slots into standard SMT pipelines without architectural changes to MT models.
- Efficiency benefits via compact selected sets. Achieving better performance with 2.5–10% of the data translates into tangible training-time and memory savings, important for large corpora and many domains.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparative baselines beyond LM-based selection. Since the core is a supervised classifier with tiny labeled data plus unlabeled embeddings, comparisons to strong non-neural discriminative baselines (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with TF-IDF or averaged embeddings) and to instance-weighting/mixture-model adaptation would strengthen the evidence that CNN-specific inductive bias, not just supervised classification, drives gains.
- Incomplete ablations of the CNN design. The paper cites combining one-hot and embedding-based regions and states that summing source+target scores works best, but does not present ablations (one-hot only vs embedding-only; source-only vs target-only vs sum; window sizes; pooling choices). Such analysis would clarify which components are crucial, especially in the tiny-data regime.
- Potential data leakage and domain composition concerns. For zh→en sms, the general-domain pool contains a 120K in-domain subset. While this setting is realistic, the method’s strong gains may partly reflect its ability to rediscover that subset. For the other three tasks, it is stated there is no known in-domain component; however, a more explicit verification or a controlled synthetic setup would help isolate the method’s behavior when no in-domain sentences exist in the pool.
- Negative sampling strategy may be too easy. Training with randomly sampled negatives could inflate classifier separability. The paper mentions trying negatives from first-round negatives with similar results, but detailed diagnostics (e.g., ROC, precision at top-K, or “hard negative” sampling) would increase confidence in robustness.
- Scope limited to phrase-based SMT. Given the field’s move to NMT (even if this work predates some of it), a brief exploration or argument for portability to NMT data selection (or at least target-side LM selection, which is listed as future work) would broaden impact.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations: (a) one-hot only vs embedding-only vs combined; (b) source-only vs target-only vs sum; and (c) effect of region size and pooling? Which pieces are most important at 100–800 in-domain sentences?
- How does a strong linear classifier baseline (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with TF-IDF or averaged word embeddings) compare given the same labeled data? This would isolate the benefit of convolution/pooling.
- What are the training/inference costs for the CNN classifier relative to LM-based selection (both CPU/GPU time and memory)? How does this scale across many domains?
- For zh→en sms, what fraction of the 120K in-domain subset is recovered among the top selected sentences? For the other three tasks, can you verify that the selected sentences are not from a hidden in-domain shard?
- Have you tried applying the same selection to NMT training or to target-side LM training (Gigaword), as you suggest in future work? Any preliminary observations?

Overall evaluation
This paper introduces a simple, novel, and practically effective semi-supervised CNN framework for SMT data selection with tiny in-domain seeds, and demonstrates consistent gains across multiple language pairs and domains, with especially compelling results in the extreme low-resource regime. Despite some missing ablations and broader baselines, the conceptual advance and empirical impact are strong. I lean toward acceptance.
