- Problem: SMT domain adaptation with extremely small in-domain seeds (~100 sentence pairs) using CNN-based sentence selection.

- Method: Train CNN classifiers (dev as positives, random general-domain negatives), score source and target independently, sum scores, and select top-scoring sentence pairs for phrase-based SMT.

- Variants compared: Supervised one-hot CNN and semi-supervised CNN with word2vec embeddings versus n-gram, RNNLM, and combined LM-based selection baselines.

- Contribution 1: Semi-supervised CNN selection substantially outperforms strong LM-based baselines when seeds are tiny and remains competitive/better at larger seed sizes across four translation directions.

- Contribution 2: Practical, scalable pipeline that selects a small fraction of data (src+tgt score sum) to train systems that can beat “all data” baselines.

- Contribution 3: Systematic seed-size sensitivity study showing robustness down to 100 labeled sentences with low variance across resamples.

- Strength: Tackles an important, underexplored regime (extreme low-resource domain adaptation), well motivated for social media and topic-specific MT.

- Strength: Strong empirical evaluation at scale (9–15M sentence pools, four language directions, realistic sms/tweet/Facebook test sets) with significant BLEU gains and significance testing.

- Strength: Demonstrated practical robustness—semi-supervised CNN yields notable BLEU improvements even with only 100 in-domain sentences.

- Strength: Modeling choices are sensible—CNNs capture local indicators and embeddings enable semantic generalization; separate src/tgt scoring is a reasonable heuristic.

- Strength: Baselines are competitive and include a useful in-domain subset reference.

- Weakness: Novelty is incremental—adapts known semi-supervised CNN techniques and engineering heuristics without architectural innovation or bilingual signal use.

- Weakness: Potential data leakage and unclear validation protocol—dev used as positives and possibly for selection tuning; “separate validation set” not clearly defined.

- Weakness: Missing ablations and baseline comparisons—no evaluation of simple discriminative classifiers (logistic/SVM/TF-IDF), or quantified ablations for embeddings, region types, and src/ tgt contributions.

- Weakness: Reproducibility issues—confusing semi-supervised CNN description and missing training details (optimizer, learning rate, epochs, regularization, vocab/OOV handling, scoring runtime).

- Weakness: Limited generality—experiments are in phrase-based SMT while NMT is dominant; applicability to NMT/backtranslation not discussed.

- Clarification request 1: Specify exactly which data tune the number of selected sentences and confirm no overlap with CNN training or MT tuning sets.

- Clarification request 2: Confirm whether separate CNNs were trained per language and report source-only, target-only, and sum selection results.

- Clarification request 3: Provide ablation results for embeddings vs none, seq vs bag-of-words regions, and semi-supervised CNN vs strong linear classifiers, especially for 100–800 sentence seeds.

- Clarification request 4: Report computational cost for training and scoring (9–15M sentences), any indexing/approximate search used, and end-to-end runtime per language pair.

- Clarification request 5: Describe negative sampling strategy, sensitivity to class priors/weighting, and threshold calibration for selection.

- Overall: Solid empirical contribution with convincing practical results but limited methodological novelty and some clarity/reproducibility concerns; adding ablations and clearer validation would strengthen the paper.
