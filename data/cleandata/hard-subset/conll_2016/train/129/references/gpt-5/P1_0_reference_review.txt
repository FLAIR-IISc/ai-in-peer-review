Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). It proposes training a sentence-level domain classifier using CNNs to score and select in-domain-like sentence pairs from large general-domain bilingual corpora. The classifier is trained in a semi-supervised fashion: one-hot CNN features from labeled (dev) data are combined with segment embeddings derived from word2vec trained on unlabeled general-domain text.
- The selected sentence pairs (by summing source- and target-side CNN scores) are used to train phrase-based SMT systems. Across four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh; 9–15M training pairs), the approach beats strong LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) and the all-data baseline, achieving up to +3.1 BLEU with only 2.5–10% of the full data; robustness is shown even with 100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN classifier can perform reliable domain-based data selection under extremely low-resource in-domain conditions (100–800 sentences), consistently outperforming LM-based selection and one-hot CNN selection. This is the main contribution: a practical, simple approach with strong empirical gains at scale.
2) A thorough multi-language, multi-domain evaluation with significance testing, including an analysis of performance as a function of the labeled in-domain sample size and a stability check for the 100-sentence regime.
3) A specific selection strategy that combines source- and target-side CNN scores, which appears to yield the best results over source-only or target-only selection (although ablations are limited).

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under very low in-domain data: The paper convincingly shows that semi-supervised CNNs remain accurate and stable with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, yielding up to +2.1 BLEU on zh→en. The comparative figure vs. LM-based methods across varying labeled sizes is informative and supports the central claim.
- Scale and breadth of evaluation: Four language directions, diverse social-media test domains, and large training pools (9–15M pairs) demonstrate robustness and practical relevance. Improvements over strong baselines (RNNLM selection and combined LM selection) are statistically significant and achieved with a small fraction of the data.
- Practical method leveraging unlabeled data: Using pretrained word2vec to supply segment embeddings is an effective, scalable semi-supervised mechanism; it is easy to reproduce and deploy.
- Sensible design choices: Scoring both source and target independently and summing scores empirically helps. Hyperparameters (region size, stride, pooling) are reasonable, and toolkits and settings are named.
- Stability analysis: The repeated 100-sentence experiments with low variance (std 0.12 BLEU) are a useful robustness check, mitigating concerns about lucky sampling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The method is a direct application of known CNN text classification with pre-trained embeddings (Johnson & Zhang; Kim) to domain classification, followed by standard data selection. The modeling contribution over existing semi-supervised CNN frameworks is small; the main novelty is the application to SMT data selection under tiny in-domain conditions.
- Outdated MT setup and missing NMT evaluation: All experiments use phrase-based SMT. Given current practice, demonstrating effectiveness for modern NMT (e.g., fine-tuning with selected data or data weighting) is important for impact and relevance. It is unclear whether the selection gains transfer to NMT, where adaptation behaviors differ.
- Insufficient ablation and analysis: Key components are not isolated. Missing ablations include: source-only vs target-only vs sum (only a statement that sum is best), effect of embeddings vs random initialization, bow vs seq regions, window/pooling variants, and the specific contribution of semi-supervised term (V·u_l(x)) vs one-hot alone on identical training sizes. No analysis of the selected data (overlap with known in-domain pools, lexical/topic characteristics) is provided.
- Potential confound in zh→en: The general-domain pool contains a large in-domain subset (120k sms). The paper reports sscnn beats training on that subset alone, but does not analyze how many of those sentences are recovered by each method. The large gain there may mainly reflect effective retrieval of an embedded in-domain corpus; without a recovery/overlap analysis, the generality of the result is unclear.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility gaps: Some training details are under-specified: exact architecture and combination of segment embeddings and one-hot regions (two-stage “second network” vs single layer), negative sampling protocol (single vs repeated re-sampling), inference thresholding/selection strategy and sensitivity to N, and computational cost of classifying tens of millions of sentences on both sides. References contain formatting issues and some incomplete entries.

Questions to authors
- NMT applicability: Have you tested the same selection strategy for modern NMT (e.g., Transformer) with fine-tuning on selected data? Does sscnn still outperform LM-based selection? Any preliminary results?
- Overlap analysis: For zh→en, what fraction of the selected 300k sentences comes from the 120k in-domain subset? How does this compare across baselines? For tasks without embedded in-domain data, what topical/lexical characteristics do the selected sets exhibit?
- Ablations: Can you report:
  - source-only vs target-only vs sum?
  - sscnn without pretrained embeddings vs with embeddings?
  - bow-only vs seq-only vs both?
  - replacing CNN with a simpler linear classifier (e.g., logistic regression on n-grams) trained on the same tiny labeled data?
- Sensitivity to N: How sensitive are BLEU gains to the number of selected sentences? Please include curves on held-out dev for at least one language pair.
- Efficiency: What is the wall-clock time to score 10–15M sentence pairs on both sides with your CNNs? Any batching or GPU details?

Additional comments
- Please fix incomplete/broken references (e.g., “Glove: Global vectors for,” missing venues/years) and typos.
- Consider reporting selection quality metrics (AUC/PR) on a labeled held-out set to complement downstream BLEU.
