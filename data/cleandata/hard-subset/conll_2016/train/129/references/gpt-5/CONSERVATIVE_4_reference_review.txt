Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a CNN-based sentence-pair classifier for SMT data selection under extreme scarcity of in-domain data (hundreds of sentences). A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot inputs with word2vec embeddings trained on large unlabeled general-domain text. Scores from separately trained source- and target-side classifiers are summed to rank bilingual sentence pairs for selection.
- The method is evaluated on four large-scale SMT settings (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) with social-media test domains. It outperforms training on all data and several LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). The largest reported gain is +3.1 BLEU (zh→en sms) with ~3% of the data; with only ~100 labeled in-domain sentences, the method still yields +2.1 BLEU over the full-data baseline.
- The paper argues CNNs with max-pooling capture local domain indicators and that pretrained embeddings help when labeled in-domain data are tiny.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant regime (very small in-domain supervision). Prior selection methods typically assume thousands to tens of thousands of in-domain sentences; demonstrating consistent improvements with ≤800 labeled sentences is useful to practitioners who cannot afford larger in-domain sets.
- Sensible, strong baselines. The comparisons to Moore-Lewis, RNNLM-based selection, and their combination reflect established practice. Bootstrap significance testing is reported and effects are generally consistent across four directions.
- Scalability demonstration. Applying selection over 9–15M sentence pairs and building competitive phrase-based SMT systems shows the method is at least feasible at realistic scales.
- Simple pipeline integration. Training a discriminative classifier over sentences and selecting top-N is straightforward to adopt within standard SMT workflows without modifying decoders or feature templates.
- Some robustness analysis. The experiment varying the amount of in-domain supervision down to 100 sentences (with repetition and small variance) is informative and aligns with the paper’s motivation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established selection paradigms. Classifier-based sentence selection is a natural extension of prior work; the semi-supervised aspect via pretrained embeddings mirrors well-known text-CNN setups (e.g., Johnson & Zhang). The contribution is largely empirical rather than methodological. The paper does not compare to simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM on bag-of-n-grams or tf-idf cosine ranking) that could test whether CNN-specific capacity/max-pooling, rather than just discriminative training and pretrained embeddings, drives gains.
- Potential data leakage and unclear validation protocol. The in-domain “dev” is used as positive training data for the CNN. The paper then states “the number of selected in-domain sentences is determined by the performance on held-out data,” but it is unclear what this held-out set is and whether it is disjoint from the dev used to train the CNN and from the tuning data used for SMT MERT/MIRA. If dev is reused for multiple roles, improvements may be overestimated. A clear data split diagram is needed.
- Selection-size fairness and reporting. Different methods use different selected sizes (e.g., 300k vs 800k vs 1.6M) optimized per method. This confounds algorithmic quality with budget. For rigorous comparison, curves of BLEU vs. number of selected sentences (for all methods) should be shown for each task. The single plot provided varies labeled in-domain size while fixing selection to 300k for one task; it does not address cross-method fairness.
- Scope of baselines and ablations. Important omissions include: (a) instance/phrase weighting methods (e.g., Foster et al., Matsoukas et al.), which directly address domain relevance; (b) sentence-level similarity retrieval baselines (BM25/tf-idf) that are strong and cheap; (c) ablations isolating the contribution of pretrained embeddings vs. one-hot; (d) using only the source side for both supervision and selection (a realistic scenario when target-domain text is unavailable), as the current approach exploits both source and target sides.
- Insufficient implementation details and multilingual preprocessing specifics. The paper lacks crucial details for reproducibility: tokenization/segmentation (especially Arabic and Chinese), vocabulary handling for one-hot models (size/cutoffs), exact unlabeled corpora and settings used to train word2vec for each language, CNN architecture variants (bow vs seq contributions), scoring calibration and combining source/target scores, and computational cost (training/inference time to score tens of millions of sentences).
- Interpretation of the largest gain is confounded. The +3.1 BLEU on zh→en occurs where an in-domain 120k subset exists within the pool. It is not clear why Moore-Lewis/RNNLM could not recover this subset comparably, or how close CNN-selected data are to that subset. An analysis of overlap and of domain purity would clarify whether the CNN is simply better at rediscovering an embedded in-domain corpus.

Questions to authors
- Data splits: Please detail the exact splits for (i) CNN training positives, (ii) CNN validation for hyperparameters and for selecting N, (iii) SMT tuning (dev for model weights), and (iv) test. Are these disjoint? If not, how did you prevent leakage?
- Fairness: Can you provide BLEU vs. selected-size curves for all methods on all tasks, with identical selection budgets, and report the area under the curve or best-at-fixed-budget results?
- Baselines: How do simple tf-idf/BM25 retrieval and a logistic regression/SVM on bag-of-ngrams compare? These would test whether CNN-specific modeling is necessary.
- Source-only scenario: What is performance when training and applying the classifier on the source side only (no target-side dev, no target-side scoring)?
- Multilingual preprocessing: How were Arabic and Chinese segmented? What were vocabulary sizes and OOV handling for one-hot inputs? What unlabeled corpora and training settings were used for word2vec per language?
- Cost: What are training and inference times (and hardware) for the CNN classification over 9–15M sentences relative to LM-based selection?

Additional comments
- Please include an ablation on using embeddings vs. not, and bow-CNN vs. seq-CNN contributions.
- Report overlap with the discovered 120k in-domain subset in zh→en to support the claim that CNN better identifies domain-relevant content.
- Minor: fix citation formatting issues and provide code/scripts for selection to aid reproducibility.
