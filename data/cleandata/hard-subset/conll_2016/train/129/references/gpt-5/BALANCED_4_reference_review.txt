Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors train a CNN-based sentence-pair classifier using the in-domain dev set as positives and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; to mitigate label scarcity they use semi-supervised CNNs that incorporate word2vec embeddings trained on large unlabeled data. The classifier scores both source and target sides of each candidate sentence pair; scores are summed to rank and select a small in-domain-like subset to train a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments on four language directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M training pairs show improvements over training on all data and over LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM). A robustness study shows gains persist with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical finding: a simple semi-supervised CNN data selector (with pretrained embeddings) consistently outperforms strong LM-based selection baselines across four language pairs/domains, and remains effective with very small labeled in-domain data (down to 100 sentences).
2) Practical recipe for tiny-supervision adaptation: dual-side (source+target) scoring and ranking by summed scores; demonstration that small selected subsets (2.5–10% of data) can beat using all data.
3) Sensitivity analysis to labeled sample size, showing stability and superior performance of semi-supervised CNNs under extreme label scarcity.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across diverse settings: The method yields statistically significant BLEU gains over “alldata” in all four directions, with especially large gains in zh→en sms (+3.1 BLEU with only ~3% of data) and solid gains elsewhere (+0.7 to +1.4). This breadth (four directions, multiple social-media domains) strengthens the claim of generality.
- Tiny-supervision focus with robust performance: The analysis varying positive samples from 6,000 down to 100 sentences is compelling. Semi-supervised CNNs maintain clear advantages over LM-based selection in the low-label regime (e.g., +2.1 BLEU at 100 sentences), addressing a practically important setting.
- Sensible and effective dual-side scoring: Scoring both source and target and summing improves selection quality; this mirrors best practices in LM-based selection and likely contributes to the gains. Although briefly described, this is a useful, pragmatic design.
- Reproducibility and statistical rigor: Key hyperparameters, toolkits (conText, word2vec), and significance testing via bootstrap are reported. Reporting standard deviation for repeated 100-sentence runs is a nice touch, indicating stability.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core idea—use a CNN classifier (with pretrained embeddings) for in-/out-of-domain text classification—is well established in text categorization. The contribution is largely an application/engineering advance to SMT data selection rather than a new model or learning principle. This is not necessarily disqualifying, but novelty is moderate.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline comparisons: Selection set sizes are tuned per method and differ across methods. Without BLEU vs. number-of-selected-sentences curves for each method, it is hard to ensure fairness (some methods might benefit from more or less data). It is also unclear whether the LM-based baselines used both source and target sides and their sum (as in Axelrod et al.); if not, the baselines may be weaker than necessary.
- Missing ablations on key choices: The paper states that sum(src,tgt) performs best but does not show results vs. source-only or target-only selection. Similarly, there is no ablation for one-hot vs. embedding-only CNNs, the impact of region size/stride/pooling, or the negative sampling strategy (random vs. hard negatives). Such ablations would clarify what drives the gains.
- Potential overfitting/tuning on dev: The dev set is used both as positive labels and (apparently) to choose the number of selected sentences (“held-out data” is mentioned but not specified). If the same dev set guides both classifier training and selection-size tuning, this risks optimistic selection of N. A clear separation of train/validation/test for classification and for MT hyperparameters is needed.
- Lack of classifier diagnostics and efficiency analysis: No classifier accuracy/AUC or calibration metrics are reported, making it hard to assess selection quality independently of downstream MT. Also, runtime/cost comparisons vs. LM-based selection (which can be very fast) are missing; practitioners may care about throughput on billion-token corpora.
- Outdated downstream system: All experiments are with phrase-based SMT. Given the field’s shift to NMT, it is unclear whether the gains transfer to neural MT training or fine-tuning regimes. This limits current impact.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Did your n-gram and RNNLM selection follow Axelrod’s cross-entropy difference on both source and target sides with summed scores? If not, please add that variant for a fair comparison.
- Selection-size tuning: What data exactly were used to determine the number of selected sentences per method? Was there a validation set separate from the dev set used as positives? Please clarify the data splits to rule out overfitting.
- Source vs. target ablation: Can you report BLEU for source-only, target-only, and sum for both LM-based and CNN-based selectors?
- Classifier diagnostics: What are the in-/out-of-domain classification accuracy/AUC on a held-out set? How do these relate to downstream BLEU?
- Efficiency: What is the end-to-end cost (training + inference) for CNN selection vs. LM-based selection on your largest setting?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Add BLEU as a function of the number of selected sentences for each method to ensure fair comparison across operating points.
- Include ablations: one-hot only vs. embedding-only CNN; region/stride/pooling sensitivity; random vs. hard negative sampling.
- Report results selecting data for target-side language models (mentioned as future work) and, if possible, a small NMT study to improve contemporaneous relevance.
- Clean up the references (many entries have formatting issues and missing details).

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a practical contribution and convincing results under tiny-supervision constraints, but with limited novelty and some missing analyses that, if addressed, would strengthen the case.
