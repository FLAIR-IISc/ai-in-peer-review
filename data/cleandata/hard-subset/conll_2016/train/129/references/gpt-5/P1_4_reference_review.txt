Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) are available. It proposes to train a sentence-level domain classifier with CNNs to score general-domain sentence pairs for selection. Two variants are explored: a one-hot CNN trained on the small labeled sample, and a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot features with pretrained word embeddings learned on large unlabeled corpora. Source and target sentences are scored independently; the sum of scores is used to select top-N pairs for training a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments across four translation directions (zh↔en, ar→en, en→es) and social-media test domains show consistent BLEU gains over using all data and over established LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis and RNNLM).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN classifier for domain relevance enables effective data selection with tiny in-domain labels, yielding consistent and sometimes large BLEU gains (up to +3.1 BLEU) across multiple language pairs/domains with only 2.5–10% of the original training data.

2) A practical low-resource adaptation setting and analysis: showing robustness of the approach down to 100 labeled sentences, outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines and a one-hot CNN.

3) A simple bilingual selection strategy (scoring source and target separately, combining scores) that is easy to deploy and outperforms single-side selection (claimed qualitatively).

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under realistic constraints. The method consistently improves BLEU over training on all data and over LM-based selection across four directions, including challenging social media domains. The +3.1 BLEU zh→en sms gain with only 300k selected pairs (∼3% of data) is substantial, and the approach remains effective with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU). Significance testing is provided.

- Focus on a genuinely hard and underexplored setting. Most prior selection/weighting work assumes thousands to hundreds of thousands of in-domain sentences; this work targets “tiny” supervision. The analysis of performance vs. labeled-sample size is especially informative and shows clear advantages of the semi-supervised CNN when labels are scarce.

- Sensible, language-agnostic methodology. Using word embeddings learned from large unlabeled text to enrich the classifier is a well-justified semi-supervised strategy, particularly valuable in sparse regimes. Scoring both sides of bilingual data and summing scores is simple and practical, avoiding dependence on domain labels in the large corpus.

- Scalability and practicality. The approach uses standard toolkits (word2vec, ConText CNN, RNNLM) and runs a single classifier per side to score millions of sentences, then trains a standard phrase-based SMT system on the selected subset. The fact that selected subsets (2.5–10%) outperform training on all data is also appealing computationally.

- Stability analysis. Repeating the 100-sentence experiment with different samples yields low variance (std 0.12 BLEU), supporting robustness rather than lucky sampling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. The core model is a standard CNN-based text classifier; the semi-supervised variant—combining one-hot inputs with pre-trained embeddings—is closely related to prior work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2015b). The contribution is primarily an application to SMT data selection, rather than a new method. This is acceptable if the empirical/analytical contributions are strong, but methodological novelty is modest.

- Incomplete methodological details and ablations. Critical details are underspecified: how segment embeddings are constructed from word embeddings (concatenation vs averaging vs region embeddings), tokenization/normalization (especially for social media), OOV handling, whether pretrained embeddings are fixed or updated, vocabulary sizes, and specifics per language. The claim that “sum of source+target scores works best” lacks a quantitative ablation table. Clarifying the held-out data used to choose selection sizes is needed to rule out overfitting. Negative sampling strategies are mentioned but not compared quantitatively.

- Baseline coverage. While Moore–Lewis and RNNLM selection are strong baselines, the study lacks comparisons to simple discriminative sentence classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with n-gram features), which would isolate the value of CNNs vs. standard text classification. It also omits sentence weighting approaches (vs. hard selection) and mixture/adaptation baselines that are competitive in practice, as well as neural (non-CNN) classifiers (e.g., BiLSTM). This makes it harder to attribute gains uniquely to the proposed modeling choice.

- Possible tuning/data leakage concerns. The dev set is used as positive training data for the classifier, and selection sizes are “determined by performance on held-out data,” but it is unclear whether this held-out data is distinct from the dev used to train the classifier and from the SMT development used for tuning. Clarifying data splits is important to ensure no optimistic bias.

- Computational/resource reporting. There is no reporting of training/inference time, hardware, or memory footprint for scoring 9–15M sentence pairs on both sides, nor hyperparameter sensitivity. For practitioners, these details matter for adopting the method at scale.

- Limited qualitative analysis of selected data and translation improvements. Beyond BLEU, the paper does not analyze what types of sentences are selected, domain vocabulary coverage, or error types improved. Such analysis would strengthen understanding and trust in the approach.

Questions to the authors
- What unlabeled corpora and sizes were used to train word2vec for each language? Were embeddings trained separately per language, and were they kept fixed during CNN training?

- How exactly are “seq-regions” and “bow-regions” constructed from embeddings? Are region embeddings concatenations of word embeddings or pooled summaries? How are one-hot and embedding-based regions combined within the network?

- How were the numbers of selected sentences (e.g., 300k/700k/etc.) chosen? What held-out set was used, and is it distinct from the dev set used to train the classifier and from the SMT tuning set?

- Can you provide quantitative results for source-only, target-only, and sum-of-scores selection to substantiate the choice?

- Have you compared against a linear n-gram classifier (logistic regression/SVM) trained on the same tiny in-domain labels? This would clarify how much of the gain comes from CNN vs. using a discriminative classifier at all.

- Did you try data weighting (soft selection) in the SMT training rather than hard selection? If so, how did it compare?

Additional comments

- A brief runtime/memory report and release of selection code/configs would help reproducibility.
