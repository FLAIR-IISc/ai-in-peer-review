- Task: SMT domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data using a semi-supervised CNN + pre-trained word2vec, scoring source and target separately to select top-N parallel pairs.
- Contribution 1: Empirical demo that semi-supervised CNN selection outperforms LM-based selection across four language pairs with 100–6,000 in-domain sentences, up to +3.1 BLEU.
- Contribution 2: Practical recipe: combine one-hot inputs with pre-trained embeddings, score both sides, tune selected-data size, using off-the-shelf tools.
- Contribution 3: Small selected subsets can outperform training on all data and sometimes in-domain-only subsets; CNN pooling and embeddings give useful domain cues.
- Strength: Strong, multi-setting empirical results with statistically significant BLEU gains across large training pools and social-media domains.
- Strength: Realistic focus on tiny labeled regimes (down to 100 sentences) with robust, low-variance performance in resampling.
- Strength: Method simplicity and portability—standard CNN text classifier and word2vec, no domain metadata required.
- Strength: Sensible bilingual selection—monolingual scoring of source and target and summing scores is language-agnostic and effective.
- Strength: Ablation vs one-hot shows embeddings provide consistent gains (~+0.5 BLEU), supporting the semi-supervised claim.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—approach is incremental over prior CNN/embedding work and prior selection methods.
- Weakness: Reproducibility gaps—missing details on embedding fine-tuning, unlabeled corpora/tokenization/sizes, preprocessing, training hyperparameters, and BLEU settings; no code released.
- Weakness: Comparison fairness/tuning budget unclear—selection-size tuning procedure not fully specified and CNN may have had more tuning; zh→en may benefit from recoverable in-domain subset.
- Weakness: Efficiency/scalability not reported—no runtimes or resource costs for scoring 9–15M sentences with CNNs versus LM methods.
- Weakness: Missing ablations/analyses—no reported source-only vs target-only vs sum numbers, sensitivity studies, or qualitative selection examples.
- Question: Are pre-trained word2vec embeddings fixed or fine-tuned during CNN training, and what unlabeled corpora/tokenization/sizes were used per language?
- Question: How is N (selected size) chosen for each method, what held-out set is used, and is it disjoint from classifier dev/test and SMT tuning data?
- Question: Report runtime (training + inference) to score the full pool with the CNN selector and compare to n-gram and RNNLM selection.
- Question: Provide source-only vs target-only vs summed-score results and whether summing gains are consistent across languages.
- Question: In zh→en with a 120k in-domain subset in the pool, what fraction of those sentences does each method retrieve in the top 300–400k, and how does recovery correlate with performance?
- Question: Did you try a joint bilingual classifier (concatenated representations or cross-lingual embeddings) instead of summing monolingual scores, and how did it compare?
- Overall: Solid, practical paper with modest novelty but compelling empirical evidence; recommend accept pending clarifications on reproducibility and runtime.
