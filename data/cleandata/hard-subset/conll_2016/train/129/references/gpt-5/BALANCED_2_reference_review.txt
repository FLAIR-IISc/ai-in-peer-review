Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as little as ~100 sentences) is available. The authors train a sentence-level domain classifier using convolutional neural networks (CNNs): a supervised “one-hot” CNN and a semi-supervised variant that augments one-hot inputs with pre-trained word2vec embeddings. The classifier scores both source and target sides; sentence pairs are ranked (best by the sum of scores) and the top N are used to train a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments on four large-scale settings (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) show consistent gains over training on all data and over strong LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). Gains are largest when in-domain supervision is smallest.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first):
1) A practical, simple, and effective data selection framework for SMT that uses a semi-supervised CNN classifier trained with tiny in-domain data, delivering consistent improvements over robust LM-based selection baselines across four language directions.
2) An empirical characterization of the “tiny in-domain” regime, showing the method’s stability and advantage down to 100 labeled sentences, with significant BLEU gains and low variance across random samples.
3) A bilingual selection strategy (separate source/target classifiers and summed scores) that improves over monolingual-only scoring in practice.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence across diverse settings: The method improves BLEU over “all data” by up to +3.1 and over the best LM-based selection by ~0.4–0.6 BLEU on average, with significance tests. Results span four language directions and social-media target domains, making a convincing case for practical utility.
- Addresses a genuine pain point: The tiny in-domain scenario (hundreds of sentences) is common in emerging or topic-specific domains. Demonstrating robust improvements with as few as 100 labeled sentences (avg. +2.1 BLEU, low std) is impactful for practitioners.
- Sensible, scalable design: The semi-supervised CNN leverages abundant unlabeled data via word embeddings and simple max-pooling to focus on domain-indicative n-grams. Training is on small labeled sets; inference to score millions of sentences is feasible. Hyperparameters and toolkits (conText, word2vec) are documented.
- Methodological clarity and fair baselines: Comparisons include strong, widely used selection baselines (Moore–Lewis, RNNLM, combined), with consistent tuning of the selected set size on held-out data and standard SMT pipelines. The analysis that sum(source, target) works best is intuitively appealing and empirically motivated.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The CNN architecture and semi-supervised setup largely follow prior work on text categorization (e.g., Johnson & Zhang). The novelty is primarily in applying this to SMT data selection and demonstrating benefits in the tiny-data regime. This is valuable but incremental relative to existing classifier-based and LM-based selection paradigms.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics: The paper asserts that sum(source, target) is best but provides no quantitative ablation (source-only vs target-only vs sum). Similarly absent are ablations for bow- vs seq-regions, one-hot vs embeddings, or pooling/region size choices. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to specific design choices or to generalize guidance.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: While LM-based baselines are strong, other competitive selection/weighting baselines are not evaluated (e.g., discriminative sentence/phrase weighting, mixture models, VSM/distributed VSM weighting). Also missing are non-CNN classifier baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with n-gram features) and modern neural sequence models (e.g., LSTM/Transformer encoders) to isolate the benefit of convolution/max-pooling specifically.
- External validity of the largest gain: The largest improvement (+3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms) is partly explained by a large in-domain subset existing inside the general data. While this is a realistic scenario for some corpora, it complicates the interpretation of gains relative to settings without such hidden in-domain pockets. On the other three tasks (no known in-domain component), gains are modest (0.7–1.4 BLEU).
- Reporting gaps and practical details: Several training details are missing (optimizer, learning rate, epochs, regularization/dropout, early stopping criteria). There is no runtime/throughput analysis for scoring ~10–15M sentences (both source and target), nor a discussion of memory/vocabulary constraints (especially for Chinese segmentation and OOV handling in embeddings). Selection size N is tuned, but selection curves (BLEU vs N) are not shown; ensuring identical tuning protocols across baselines should be made explicit.

Questions to authors
- Selection protocol: How was N (number of selected sentence pairs) tuned for each baseline and task? Can you provide BLEU vs N curves to show robustness and prevent cherry-picking?
- Scoring and calibration: How are source/target classifier outputs calibrated before summation? Are they probabilities, logits, or raw scores? Do results change if you normalize by sentence length?
- Training details: What optimizer, learning rate schedule, number of epochs, batch size, and regularization (dropout/L2) were used? Any early stopping on held-out data? What was the vocabulary size and handling of OOVs for embeddings? How was Chinese tokenization performed?
- Ablations: Can you report results for source-only vs target-only vs sum; one-hot vs embeddings-only vs combined; bow- vs seq-regions? This would clarify where the gains originate.
- Computational cost: What is the wall-clock time and hardware footprint to train the classifiers and score 9–15M sentences per language side? How does this compare to LM-based selection at scale?
- Weighting vs hard selection: Did you try using classifier scores as sentence weights in SMT training (vs hard top-N selection)? How does it compare to mixture/instance weighting baselines?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a solid, well-executed application of semi-supervised CNNs to SMT data selection in a highly practical and under-explored regime (tiny in-domain data). The empirical results are strong and of clear interest to the community. However, methodological novelty is limited and several important ablations and baseline comparisons are missing. If strengthened with the requested diagnostics, weighting comparisons, and reporting of computational costs, the paper would make a compelling case. As is, I view it as a useful, pragmatic contribution with moderate novelty and strong empirical value.
