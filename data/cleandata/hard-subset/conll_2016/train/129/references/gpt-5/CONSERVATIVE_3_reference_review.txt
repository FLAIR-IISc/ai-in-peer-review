Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) as sentence-pair domain classifiers to drive data selection for SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain datasets (hundreds to a few thousand sentences) are available. The core idea is to train a supervised “one-hot CNN” or a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from word2vec pretrained on large unlabeled corpora. The classifier scores source and target sides; top-N parallel sentences from the general pool are selected (based on source+target scores) to train a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments on four translation directions (9–15M general-domain sentence pairs) show improvements over using all data and over LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination), with the largest gain (3.1 BLEU) in zh→en SMS where a hidden in-domain subset exists; gains of 0.7–1.4 BLEU are reported on the other tasks. Learning-curve analysis suggests the method is relatively robust even with 100 in-domain sentences.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing CNN-based domain classification for SMT data selection can outperform standard LM-based selection, especially under very small in-domain supervision.
2) A semi-supervised CNN setup that combines one-hot and unsupervised word-embedding features for domain classification, applied to bilingual data selection.
3) A multi-direction evaluation on large-scale SMT settings with significance testing.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant regime (tiny in-domain data). The comparison against n-gram/RNNLM selection under varying amounts of in-domain supervision directly targets a scenario where LM-based selection often struggles. The learning curve down to 100 sentences is particularly informative.
- Broad empirical scope. Four language directions, large training pools, and social-media test domains increase the external validity relative to many adaptation papers with narrower settings.
- Consistent improvements over strong established baselines. The LM-based methods are standard and reasonably tuned; the semi-supervised CNN outperforms them on all tasks with significance, and uses only 2.5–10% of the data, making a compelling efficiency argument for training-time data size.
- Sound experimental hygiene on several fronts. They avoid using any explicit domain labels for the pool, evaluate on test sets separate from the dev used to train the classifier, and apply bootstrap resampling for significance.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing discriminative baselines. Using CNNs (with pre-trained embeddings) for text classification is established; the contribution is mostly applying this to MT data selection. To substantiate that CNNs per se matter, the paper should compare against simpler discriminative selectors trained on the same tiny labeled data: e.g., logistic regression/SVM over n-gram tf-idf or averaged/pretrained embeddings; a shallow MLP; or a linear classifier on region-averaged embeddings. Without these, the incremental value of the proposed CNN architecture over much simpler, cheaper classifiers is unclear.
- Potential confound in zh→en gains due to an in-domain subset inside the pool. The largest reported improvement occurs in a condition where 120K in-domain sentences exist within the general corpus. This favors any selector good at rediscovering that subset. A fairer assessment would remove that in-domain subset from the pool and re-run selection, or at least report results both with and without it to isolate the method’s benefit when no hidden in-domain data exist.
- Insufficient ablations of the CNN architecture and features. The paper mixes seq- and bow-regions, max pooling, ReLU, 500 filters, and combines one-hot with word2vec regions. It does not quantify the contribution of (i) word embeddings vs one-hot alone, (ii) seq vs bow regions, (iii) source vs target vs combined scoring (only a qualitative statement), or (iv) sensitivity to hyperparameters. Given the small in-domain training size, robustness to architectural choices is important.
- Selection budget tuning lacks clarity and may bias comparisons. The number of selected sentences N is “empirically determined on held-out data,” but what is the held-out set and is it shared across methods? Are all methods allowed to tune N to their own optimum (as the table suggests)? If so, what prevents overfitting to the small dev/held-out domain? A plot of BLEU vs N for each method would make the comparison more transparent and reduce concerns about cherry-picking N.
- Stability and variance not fully characterized. The three-run stability check (SD 0.12 BLEU) is reported only for sscnn in one task at 100-sentence supervision. Results should report variance across different random negative samples, across random seeds, and across more tasks and supervision sizes, including for the LM-based and one-hot CNN baselines, to support claims of robustness.

Questions to authors
- Please add comparisons to simpler discriminative selectors trained on the same tiny labeled sets: (a) logistic regression/SVM with tf-idf n-grams; (b) linear/MLP classifiers on averaged pretrained embeddings; (c) cosine similarity between dev centroid and candidate sentences in embedding space. How do these fare relative to ohcnn/sscnn?
- For zh→en, can you re-run selection after removing the known 120K in-domain subset from the pool, and report results? Alternatively, show how many of the selected sentences overlap with that subset for each method.
- How exactly was N (the number of selected sentences) tuned? What held-out data was used and was it identical across methods? Please include BLEU vs N curves for each method on at least one task.
- Provide ablations: sscnn without one-hot (embeddings only), without embeddings (ohcnn), seq-only vs bow-only, and source-only vs target-only vs combined scoring with numbers.
- What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier(s) and running selection compared to LM-based selection (training LMs and scoring cross-entropy difference), measured in wall-clock and hardware?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of word2vec parameters and unlabeled corpus? Did you try GloVe or different vector sizes?
- How many negatives were sampled, and how sensitive are results to the negative sampling strategy? Did you try multiple random negative sets and report variance?
- Did you control for superficial cues (e.g., sentence length, punctuation, URLs, emojis) that might trivially separate social media vs news? Any analysis of the learned filters/most salient n-grams to ensure the classifier isn’t exploiting artifacts?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and shows promising results. However, to meet the bar for acceptance, it needs stronger methodological baselines and ablations to demonstrate that the proposed semi-supervised CNN offers substantial, robust advantages beyond simpler, established discriminative selection methods, and that the large zh→en gain is not driven by a favorable corpus artifact.
