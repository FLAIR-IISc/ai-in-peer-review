Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) are available. The authors train a sentence-level domain classifier using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and use the classifier scores to select a small, high-relevance subset from large general-domain bilingual corpora. They explore two variants: a supervised one-hot CNN (ohcnn) and a semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) that augments one-hot inputs with pre-trained word embeddings trained on unlabeled general-domain text. Source and target sentences are scored independently and combined at selection time.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN domain classifier enables effective data selection for SMT with extremely small in-domain samples, outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis and RNNLM) across four language directions, with up to +3.1 BLEU and robust gains even with 100 labeled sentences.
2) A practical selection recipe combining source- and target-side CNN scores and leveraging pre-trained embeddings, validated at scale on 9–15M sentence-pair corpora from heterogeneous sources (news, web, UN, social media).
3) Empirical analysis of label-scarce regimes, showing stability and consistent superiority of CNN-based selection as labeled data shrinks.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance in low-label regimes: The sscnn consistently outperforms all LM-based baselines across four language directions and diverse social-media test domains, with statistically significant gains. The robustness with only 100 labeled pairs (+2.1 BLEU on zh→en sms) is compelling and practically important for rapid domain bootstrapping.
- Sound methodology and clear operationalization: The pipeline is simple and reproducible—train a binary in-/out-domain sentence classifier using tiny labeled positives and random negatives; score the full corpus; select top-N by sum of source+target scores. The architectural choices (windowed convolution, max-pooling, pre-trained embeddings) are well motivated by text CNN literature.
- Sensible baselines and significance testing: Moore-Lewis (n-gram), RNNLM, and their combination are appropriate, widely-used data selection baselines. Bootstrap resampling significance tests are reported and selection sizes are validated on held-out data.
- Cross-lingual generality and scale: Results span Arabic, Chinese, English, and Spanish, with training corpora of 9–15M sentence pairs. This supports the claim that the approach is broadly applicable rather than language/idiosyncrasy-specific.
- Analysis of labeled-sample size: The controlled experiment fixing selected set size (300K) and varying labeled data (6K → 100) is informative, illustrating where CNN-based selection begins to meaningfully outperform LM-based selection and the stability across random tiny samples.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Using CNNs as sentence-level domain classifiers and leveraging pre-trained embeddings is a direct application of Johnson & Zhang (2015b) to domain selection. The paper’s novelty lies primarily in applying this to SMT selection under tiny in-domain conditions, rather than in new modeling ideas. This is acceptable for a practical paper but should be acknowledged.
- Missing strong non-neural classifier baselines: A simple linear classifier (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM) over tf-idf BOW or n-grams trained on the same tiny labeled data is a natural and strong baseline for domain classification and selection. Prior SMT adaptation leveraged vector-space similarity and discriminative weighting (e.g., Foster et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2013/2014). Without such baselines, it is unclear whether gains stem from representation learning per se or merely from using any discriminative classifier instead of cross-entropy selection.
- Potential data leakage/duplication concerns: For zh→en, an in-domain sms subset (120K) exists within the general training pool, and sscnn outperforms training on that subset alone. However, the paper does not analyze near-duplicates or overlap between the in-domain dev/test and training data. If duplicates exist, selection may benefit from retrieval-like effects. A deduplication analysis and overlap statistics would increase confidence.
- Incomplete ablations: The contribution of each design choice is not disentangled. Missing ablations include: source-only vs target-only scoring; one-hot CNN vs semi-supervised CNN on identical settings; effect of region size/stride; contribution of word embeddings size/type; selection by sum vs max vs product of scores; varying negative sampling ratios. These would clarify what drives the gains.
- Practical considerations and fairness: Runtime/compute comparisons vs n-gram and RNNLM selection are not reported. Since practitioners trade off quality vs cost, reporting training/inference time, GPU/CPU use, and pass counts over the corpus would help. Also, the procedure for tuning selection size N “on a separate validation set” needs more detail to ensure comparable tuning budgets across methods.

Questions to authors
1) Overlap analysis: What is the overlap (exact or near-duplicate) between dev/test and training data, especially for the zh→en sms setting? Did you perform deduplication before selection? Can you report overlap rates and re-run results with deduplication?
2) Discriminative baselines: How does a simple linear classifier (logreg/SVM) on tf-idf BOW or character n-grams, trained on the same tiny labeled set, compare for selection? This would isolate the effect of CNNs vs discriminative classification more generally.
3) Ablations on scoring: Please report performance for source-only, target-only, and sum scoring across at least one language pair, including the selection sizes chosen by validation.
4) Embeddings and tokenization: What tokenization/segmentation was used for Arabic and Chinese? How were embeddings trained (corpus size, epochs, window, subword handling)? Can you quantify the gain of sscnn vs ohcnn with identical hyperparameters across all four tasks?
5) Compute cost: What are training and inference times for ohcnn/sscnn vs ngram/rnnlm baselines (per language pair), and hardware used? How many epochs and how large are the selected negative sets?
6) Selection strategy: Did you try weighting by the CNN domain score (instance weighting) or adding selected data to the full corpus instead of replacement? How do those compare?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether the dev set used to train the classifier is also used for SMT tuning, and how the separate validation set for choosing N is constructed to avoid overfitting.
- Consider reporting TER or chrF alongside BLEU.
- The bilingual nature of the data suggests trying a bilingual/joint classifier (e.g., concatenated source–target with shared convolution or cross-lingual embeddings). Even a small experiment could be insightful.

Overall, this is a solid and practical contribution demonstrating that semi-supervised CNN-based selection is effective under extreme label scarcity. With additional baselines and analyses, the case for broader adoption would be stronger.
