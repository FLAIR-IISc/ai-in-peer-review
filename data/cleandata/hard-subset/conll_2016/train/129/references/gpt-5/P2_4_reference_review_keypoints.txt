- Problem: SMT domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as ~100 sentence pairs).  
- Method: Train a sentence-level CNN domain classifier, semi-supervised by fusing one-hot inputs with region embeddings from word2vec.  
- Selection: Score source and target sentences, sum scores, and select top-N parallel sentence pairs to train phrase-based SMT.  
- Empirical claim: Semi-supervised CNN selection substantially outperforms LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM, combination) across four language directions/domains.  
- Low-data strength: Strong gains in the tiny-data regime (e.g., +3.1 BLEU zh→en SMS with 300K selected pairs) and stability down to 100 labeled sentences.  
- Baselines: Compared against competitive, appropriate baselines (3-gram Moore-Lewis, RNNLM).  
- Model design: Sensible CNN architecture that fuses one-hot and pretrained embedding region features, scoring both source and target.  
- Scale/practicality: Experiments on large corpora (9–15M pairs) and social-media domains show small selections (2.5–10%) can outperform training on all data.  
- Reproducibility: Key hyperparameters and toolkits reported; statistical testing performed.  
- Novelty concern: Limited methodological novelty—adapts known CNN+embeddings approaches to selection rather than proposing a new model.  
- Ablation gap: Missing quantitative ablations isolating source-only vs target-only scoring, one-hot vs embeddings, and seq- vs bow-regions.  
- Data confound: zh→en SMS may contain a 120K in-domain subset within the general pool; unclear how much CNN is recovering this subset.  
- Tuning/data-split risk: Selection size is empirically tuned on held-out data; unclear if classifier training, selection tuning, and SMT tuning are strictly disjoint, risking optimistic results.  
- Missing baselines: No comparison to simple discriminative selectors (e.g., linear SVM/logistic with bag-of-ngrams or doc2vec) that might be strong in tiny-data settings.  
- Efficiency gap: Runtime and computational cost for classifying tens of millions of sentences with CNN are not reported or compared to LM-based scoring.  
- Question — data splits: Need clarification which sets are used for CNN positives, selection-size tuning, and SMT tuning and whether they are disjoint.  
- Question — source vs target: Request BLEU results for source-only and target-only selection and how often they disagree.  
- Question — ablations: Request performance of one-hot-only vs embeddings-only vs combined model, and seq- vs bow-region comparisons.  
- Question — zh→en subset: What fraction of selected sentences come from the 120K in-domain subset and do gains persist if those are removed?  
- Question — efficiency: How long does it take to score all sentences per language pair and how does that compare to LM-based scoring?  
- Overall: Important, practical problem with solid empirical gains and moderate novelty; recommend acceptance pending additional ablations and clarifications.
