Summary and main contributions
- Introduces a semi-supervised CNN classifier for SMT data selection under an extreme low-resource in-domain regime (as few as 100 sentences). The classifier combines one-hot (supervised) segment features with pre-trained word embeddings (unsupervised) in a unified convolutional architecture and is used to score source and target sides separately, selecting top-N parallel sentences by the summed scores.
- Demonstrates strong, consistent gains across four large-scale SMT settings (9–15M sentence pairs) and social media test domains, outperforming standard LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). Notably, achieves +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms with ~3% of the data and remains robust with only 100 in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU).
- Provides empirical evidence that CNN-based selection is particularly effective when in-domain data is tiny, and that combining source and target-side scores is superior to either alone.

Strengths
- Novel application and simple, bold idea with high impact potential: Using a semi-supervised CNN for SMT data selection specifically tailored to “tiny in-domain” conditions is, to my knowledge, new. While CNNs for text classification exist, mapping this paradigm to domain-driven selection for SMT—and showing it works when LM-based selection struggles—is a clear conceptual advance with practical value.
- Strong empirical results at scale and across languages/domains: Four directions with 9–15M parallel sentences, domains like sms/tweets/Facebook, and consistent BLEU gains over strong LM baselines. The zh→en sms gain (+3.1 BLEU) is especially compelling, and improvements of 0.7–1.4 BLEU on tasks without in-domain overlap still demonstrate effectiveness.
- Robustness in the truly low-resource regime: The “only 100 in-domain sentences” experiment is highly persuasive for the stated problem. The method outperforming LM selection by large margins in this setting (2.1 BLEU vs marginal LM gains) confirms the claimed advantage when labeled data is scarce; the reported low variance across resamples adds credibility.
- Practical, domain-agnostic recipe: The approach is straightforward to reproduce with public tools (word2vec, conText) and does not rely on domain labels or corpus source metadata. Scoring both source and target and selecting by the sum is an effective, simple heuristic.
- Sensible modeling choices and rationale: The convolution + max-pooling intuition for highlighting domain-indicative n-grams, combined with distributional generalization from pre-trained embeddings, is well-aligned with the task, and the ablation vs one-hot CNN (ohcnn) isolates the value of semi-supervision.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond adapting existing CNN text categorization to data selection: Architecturally this is a fairly direct adaptation of prior CNN/semi-supervised CNN work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang) to SMT sentence selection, with minor modifications (combining one-hot and embedding features). The conceptual novelty comes mainly from the application scenario rather than methodological innovation.
- Baseline coverage is narrow: Comparisons are only to LM-based selection. Missing discriminative baselines such as logistic regression/SVMs over n-gram features, or more recent (at the time) instance weighting/mixture/VSM approaches (Foster & Kuhn; Chen et al.). It remains unclear whether simpler linear models trained on the same tiny in-domain set could close the gap.
- Evaluation details and tuning protocol need clarification: The number of selected sentences is “determined by performance on held-out data,” but the held-out set is not clearly defined given the dev set is used for training the classifier. Potential risk of overfitting selection size to the only in-domain set available; stronger separation of train/validation would increase confidence.
- Analysis depth is limited: There is little ablation beyond ohcnn vs sscnn. Missing: impact of using only source vs only target vs sum (claimed best, but no quantitative breakdown), sensitivity to region size/stride/pooling, effect of embedding dimensionality or training corpus, and error analysis (e.g., domain confounds like sentence length or punctuation).
- Practicality/cost and scalability are underreported: While the labeled training is tiny, scoring millions of sentences requires CNN inference over both sides; runtime/memory and throughput are not discussed. A comparison of selection speed vs LM perplexity would help practitioners.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What exactly is the “held-out” set for tuning the number of selected sentences? Is it a partition of the dev set distinct from the positive examples used to train the CNN, or a separate set? Please clarify the split to rule out tuning on training positives.
- Can you report quantitative results for selection by source-only, target-only, and source+target scores on at least one task?
- How does the semi-supervised CNN compare to a simple linear classifier (e.g., logistic regression over bag-of-ngrams) trained on the same tiny in-domain set? This would help attribute gains to convolutional structure vs supervised discriminative learning per se.
- What is the inference throughput for scoring the full training pool (sentences per second per GPU/CPU), and how does it compare to LM-based selection?
- Did you try hard-negative mining (e.g., iteratively sampling negatives near the decision boundary) beyond the brief mention? Any measurable effect?
- How sensitive are the results to the embedding source (corpus size/domain), dimensionality, or to fine-tuning embeddings during supervised training?

Overall recommendation
- I lean toward acceptance. The paper introduces a clear, practically impactful idea for a challenging and timely setting (tiny in-domain adaptation), with convincing multi-language evidence and strong gains. Despite limited methodological novelty and missing baselines/ablations, the conceptual contribution and empirical robustness justify publication and will likely spur follow-up work in neural data selection and low-resource adaptation.
