Summary and contributions
This paper tackles domain adaptation for SMT when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (as little as ~100 sentences). The core idea is to train a monolingual sentence-level domain classifier using a CNN with both one-hot inputs and pre-trained word embeddings (semi-supervised CNN), apply it to both source and target sides of a large general-domain parallel corpus, rank by domain relevance (sum of source and target scores), select top-N sentence pairs, and train a phrase-based SMT system on the selected subset.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN classifier enables highly effective data selection with extremely small in-domain supervision, consistently outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis, RNNLM, and their combination) across four language directions and social-media test domains, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU and robust improvements even with only 100 in-domain sentences.
2) A practical selection recipe combining source- and target-side monolingual CNN scores, with large-scale experiments (9–15M sentence pairs), statistical significance tests, and a stability analysis for tiny supervision settings.
3) Evidence that small selected subsets (2.5–10% of training) can beat training on all data and in one case even beat training on a large “in-domain-only” subset, highlighting the utility of fine-grained selection.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under realistic constraints: The paper directly addresses an important and under-explored setting—adaptation with very limited in-domain data. Improvements are consistent across 4 directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and social media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook). Gains remain robust even when the in-domain labeled set is reduced to 100 sentences (sscnn +2.1 BLEU), with very low variance across random samples.
- Competitive and relevant baselines: The LM-based selection baselines (Moore-Lewis with 3-gram LM, RNNLM, and their combination) are strong and widely used, and sscnn beats them in all tasks (by ~0.5 BLEU on average), with significance testing.
- Practicality and scalability: The method is simple (monolingual CNNs + word2vec), scalable, and easy to deploy. It requires only a small in-domain dev set and unlabeled general-domain data. Selection ratios are small (2.5–10%), reducing training cost while improving accuracy.
- Sensible design choices corroborated empirically: Using both source and target monolingual models and summing scores, and pre-trained embeddings to mitigate tiny supervision, are reasonable and appear effective. The ablation comparing ohcnn vs sscnn supports the benefit of embeddings.
- Clear motivation and discussion: The qualitative analysis of why CNNs with embeddings help (local n-gram salience via convolution and similarity generalization via embeddings) is convincing for the tiny-data regime.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core technique is a fairly direct application of known CNN-based text classification with pre-trained embeddings to data selection. The architecture itself and semi-supervised setup follow prior work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang) without introducing new modeling ideas tailored to bilingual selection or SMT.
- Missing ablations and diagnostic analyses: The paper asserts that combining source and target scores works best but does not present comparative results for source-only vs target-only. There is no systematic ablation of the role of pre-trained embeddings (sscnn vs ohcnn is reported but without finer-grained analyses), region types (bow vs seq), filter/window sizes, or the effect of negative sampling strategies beyond a brief note. More diagnostics (e.g., lexical coverage, OOV reduction, length/style distributions) would strengthen understanding.
- Potential confound in zh→en: A 120K in-domain subset exists within the general training pool. The +3.1 BLEU result may largely reflect recovering that subset; the paper notes sscnn beats an “in-domain-only” baseline, but it does not quantify overlap with the known in-domain subset or show results when that subset is removed from the pool, limiting how broadly we can interpret the magnitude of gains.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: Important setup details are missing or brief: tokenization/normalization, vocabulary sizes and OOV handling for one-hot inputs, word2vec training settings (window, min-count, epochs), classifier training regimen (regularization, early stopping, dropout), and the exact procedure for tuning N (selected sentence count) and the validation metric. It’s also unclear if source/target CNNs are trained independently and whether any bilingual signals are used.
- Scope limited to phrase-based SMT and hard selection: The evaluation focuses on phrase-based SMT; while historically standard, showing transfer to NMT or neural adaptation methods would markedly increase impact. Additionally, the method uses hard selection; comparisons to sentence weighting by classifier scores or integration into phrase-table weighting could be illuminating and potentially stronger.

Questions to authors
- Source vs target vs sum: Please report BLEU when selecting by source-only, target-only, and sum scores across tasks. Are the source and target CNNs trained independently? Any benefit from joint or bilingual modeling?
- Overlap analysis for zh→en: What fraction of the selected sentences overlaps with the known 120K in-domain subset, and how do results change if that subset is excluded from the candidate pool?
- Ablations: Can you quantify the contribution of pre-trained embeddings (sscnn) vs one-hot only (ohcnn) beyond average deltas, and provide results for different region types (bow vs seq), window/filter sizes, and embeddings (dims, window, min-count)? What happens with randomly initialized embeddings fine-tuned on the tiny labeled set?
- Tuning N: How is N selected in a realistic setting without access to test references? What validation data/metric is used, and how sensitive are results to N? Can you show BLEU vs N curves for at least one more task beyond zh→en?
- Preprocessing and vocab: Please detail tokenization/segmentation (especially for Chinese), casing, punctuation normalization, maximum sentence length for CNNs, vocabulary sizes for one-hot inputs, and handling of rare/OOV words.
- Weighting vs selection: Did you try using classifier scores as instance weights (for sentence- or phrase-level weighting) rather than hard selection? If so, how do they compare?
- Efficiency: What is the training and inference cost of the CNN classifiers relative to LM- and RNNLM-based selection for the reported datasets?

Overall, this is a solid, practically useful contribution with strong empirical evidence in a realistic low-supervision adaptation scenario. Strengthening ablations, clarifying potential confounds, and broadening baselines/analyses would make it more compelling.
