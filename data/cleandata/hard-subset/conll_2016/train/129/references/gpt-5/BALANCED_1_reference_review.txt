Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (on the order of hundreds of sentences). The authors train a CNN-based sentence classifier (one-hot and a semi-supervised variant augmented with pretrained word embeddings) to score general-domain sentence pairs by in-domain relevance, then select the top-N pairs to train a phrase-based SMT system. They compare against Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination across four language directions and social-media-style test domains, and analyze sensitivity to the amount of labeled in-domain data.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN classifier can outperform strong LM-based data selection methods for SMT adaptation, especially in the very low in-domain data regime (down to 100 sentences), across multiple language directions and domains.
2) A practical, simple data-selection pipeline using both source and target side scores and pretrained word embeddings that yields substantial data reduction (2.5–10% of training data) with equal or better BLEU than using all data.
3) An analysis of performance versus labeled in-domain size, showing stability and clear advantages of the semi-supervised CNN when labeled data are scarce.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across multiple settings: The method yields consistent gains over “alldata” and outperforms n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination on four language pairs (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh). Improvements are statistically significant and nontrivial (e.g., +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms with only ~3% of data). This cross-lingual, cross-domain coverage increases confidence in robustness.
- Focus on a practically important regime: The paper directly targets the tiny in-domain data scenario common in social media and many deployment contexts. The experiment varying labeled in-domain size (down to 100 sentences) is particularly compelling; the semi-supervised CNN remains effective and stable where LM-based selection degrades.
- Sensible, reproducible pipeline and clarity of setup: Implementation choices (conText CNN, word2vec, region sizes, pooling, sample balancing) are clearly described; significance tests are reported; selection uses the sum of source and target side scores, which empirically works best. The amount of selected data is tuned on held-out data per task, mirroring realistic development.
- Evidence the method is not just rediscovering known in-domain subsets: On zh→en, where a 120K in-domain subset exists in the general pool, the semi-supervised CNN beats the model trained solely on that subset. This suggests the method finds additional useful data beyond the canonical in-domain portion.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core model is a fairly standard CNN for text classification with pretrained embeddings, close in spirit to Johnson & Zhang (2015b). The novel aspect is its application to SMT data selection rather than a substantially new algorithmic contribution. As such, the work is primarily empirical.
- Missing comparisons to strong non-neural discriminative baselines: Since the task is binary sentence classification with tiny labeled data, simple and strong baselines (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on bag-of-words/char n-grams; sentence embedding similarity via averaged fastText; doc2vec; TF-IDF cosine; or a simple MLP on averaged embeddings) are absent. These could be competitive, especially under tiny supervision, and are important to justify the need for CNNs.
- Potential data/protocol concerns: The classifier is trained with the dev set as positive examples and then used to select training sentences for a system evaluated on test sets from the same domain. This is reasonable but risks overfitting to dev-domain idiosyncrasies; it would help to show classifier performance (accuracy/ROC) and selection quality metrics, and to confirm no leakage from test to training (e.g., via near-duplicates). Tuning the number of selected sentences “on held-out data” should be clearly separated from the dev used as positives.
- Analysis gaps in selection behavior: For the zh→en case with a known in-domain subset, it would be informative to quantify overlap/recall of that subset among selected sentences and to characterize which additional sentences the CNN brings in. More generally, reporting domain classification metrics, the proportion of source/target selected lists’ overlap, and ablations (source-only vs target-only vs sum) would strengthen the claims.
- Scope limited to phrase-based SMT: While the paper’s context is SMT, modern MT is predominantly NMT. It remains unclear whether the proposed selection transfers to NMT training (both as hard selection and as instance weighting). Evaluating at least one NMT setting would considerably increase impact and contemporaneous relevance.

Questions to authors
- Embeddings: Are word embeddings trained separately per language on the general-domain side of your parallel data? Were any external monolingual corpora used? Did you fix embeddings during CNN training or fine-tune?
- Selection mechanics: How exactly are source and target side scores combined (sum of logits, probabilities, or raw scores)? What is the overlap between source- and target-selected sets?
- Protocol: Which set is used to tune the number of selected sentences—distinct from the “dev-as-positives” set? Please clarify to avoid potential tuning on the same domain sample.
- Quality of the classifier: What are the domain classification accuracy/ROC on held-out labeled data? Does accuracy correlate with downstream BLEU?
- zh→en in-domain subset: What fraction of the known 120K in-domain sentences are recovered by each method? Among CNN-selected sentences, how many are outside that subset?
- Baselines: How do simple linear baselines (e.g., logistic regression on BOW/TF-IDF, cosine similarity with averaged embeddings) compare under the same protocol?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed empirical study addressing an important practical problem with clear gains in low-supervision settings. However, the methodological novelty is modest and key comparative baselines and analyses are missing. I encourage the authors to add stronger non-neural baselines, report classifier/selection diagnostics, clarify tuning/protocol, and, if possible, demonstrate applicability to NMT or instance weighting. These additions would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution.
