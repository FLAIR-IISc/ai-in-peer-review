Summary and contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. It frames data selection as a sentence-level domain classification problem and proposes semi-supervised CNNs that leverage unlabeled, large general-domain corpora to build word embeddings and combine them with one-hot features for classification. The classifier assigns in-domain scores to both source and target sentences; top-ranked pairs are selected to train phrase-based SMT systems.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation that works with very small in-domain samples (down to ~100 sentences), outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines.
2) A large-scale, multi-language evaluation (four directions, 9–15M sentence pairs each) on social-media test domains showing consistent BLEU gains and statistical significance, including a striking +3.1 BLEU over training on all data.
3) Analysis of robustness to in-domain sample size and simple design choices (source vs target vs sum, negative sampling), demonstrating stability and effectiveness in low-sample regimes.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with tiny in-domain data: The proposed semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms training on all data and established n-gram and RNNLM cross-entropy selection across four diverse translation directions. Notably, zh→en sms gains +3.1 BLEU using only ~3% of training data. With only 100 in-domain sentence pairs, sscnn yields +2.1 BLEU (zh→en), with low variance across resamples, showing real utility when in-domain data is scarce.
- Sensible problem framing and effective use of unlabeled data: Casting selection as domain classification allows the model to exploit local n-gram-like cues via convolution and max pooling, while pretrained word embeddings propagate semantic similarity (e.g., Lenovo ~ Dell) beyond the tiny labeled set. This semi-supervised design is well-matched to the setting and explains robustness vs LM-based methods that can be brittle with very small in-domain corpora.
- Coverage, scale, and statistical rigor: Experiments span four language directions, multiple social-media domains, and millions of sentences; the authors report selected data sizes, significance via bootstrap resampling, and a validation-based choice of selection sizes. The method even surpasses an explicit 120K in-domain subset (indata) for zh→en sms at p < 0.05, indicating it is not merely rediscovering an existing subcorpus but effectively filtering the broader pool.
- Clear baselines and ablation: Comparisons to n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination establish a standard reference; the contrast between one-hot CNN (ohcnn) and sscnn isolates the value of unlabeled embeddings. The fixed-300K selection study vs varying amounts of labeled in-domain data gives insight into sample efficiency and stability.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: Using CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised word embeddings is established; the contribution lies primarily in applying these ideas to SMT data selection in a tiny in-domain regime. While practically valuable, methodological novelty is modest compared with prior discriminative selection/weighting approaches.
- Limited baseline coverage beyond LM-based selection: Important adaptation baselines are missing, e.g., instance/phrase weighting (Foster et al., Matsoukas et al.), mixture/adaptive models, vector space/distributed VSM weighting (Chen et al.). Showing that sscnn outperforms these would strengthen the case that the gains are not specific to the LM-selection comparison.
- Selection-size fairness and reporting: For the main table, different methods use different numbers of selected sentences (tuned per method). This makes it hard to attribute gains to model quality vs data volume. Although one fixed-size analysis is provided (zh→en only), a broader BLEU vs selected-size curve across methods and tasks would be more convincing.
- Details and reproducibility gaps: Several choices are under-specified: how the “separate validation set” is constructed when the dev set is already used as positives; classifier training regimen (epochs, optimizer, early stopping), class balance and calibration, and the precise protocol for combining source and target scores (thresholding vs ranking). The negative sampling variant is mentioned but not quantified. These details matter for replication and for understanding sensitivity.
- Scope of bilingual signals: The method scores source and target separately and sums them; no evidence is provided for bilingual joint modeling (e.g., concatenation or cross-lingual embeddings). Given this is SMT, a bilingual classifier could plausibly add value; at least a source-only vs target-only vs sum ablation table would clarify contributions of each side.

Questions to authors
- Validation protocol: What data was used to tune the number of selected sentences, given the dev set serves as positives for classifier training? Was a split of the dev set used, or a separate set? Please detail to avoid potential tuning-on-test leakage.
- Selection-size curves: Can you report BLEU as a function of selected data size for all methods on at least one additional language pair, with identical sizes across methods?
- Baseline coverage: Can you include or comment on results versus discriminative instance weighting or VSM/distributed VSM weighting, which are competitive non-LM baselines?
- Source vs target vs sum: Please provide quantitative results for each variant to substantiate the claim that sum is best.
- Efficiency/scalability: What is the computational cost of scoring 9–15M sentence pairs (per side)? Any engineering considerations for practitioners?

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and practically impactful paper with strong empirical results in a challenging, realistic setting. Despite limited methodological novelty, the contribution is meaningful for the community, and the study is convincing. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying experimental details and, if possible, strengthening baselines or fairness of comparisons.
