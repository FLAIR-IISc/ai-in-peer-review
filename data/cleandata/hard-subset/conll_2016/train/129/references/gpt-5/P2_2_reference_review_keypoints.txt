- Paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN for SMT data selection that leverages unlabeled general-domain corpora and works with tiny in-domain samples (~100 sentences).
- Main contributions: practical sscnn selection method, large-scale multi-language evaluation with consistent BLEU gains, and analysis of robustness to in-domain sample size and design choices.
- Strong empirical gains: sscnn outperforms training on all data and LM-based selection across four translation directions, e.g., +3.1 BLEU (zh→en sms) using ~3% of data.
- Effective use of unlabeled data and CNN architecture: convolution + pretrained embeddings propagate semantic similarity and increase robustness versus LM methods in low-sample regimes.
- Wide coverage and statistical rigor: experiments across multiple languages/domains, millions of sentences, reported selection sizes, bootstrap significance, and validation-based selection-size tuning.
- Clear baselines and ablations: comparisons to n-gram and RNNLM selection, and one-hot CNN vs sscnn to isolate embedding benefit; fixed-size vs varying labeled-data analyses add insight.
- Novelty is incremental: using CNNs and semi-supervised embeddings for text classification is established; main novelty is applying them to tiny-sample SMT selection.
- Missing important baselines: lacks comparisons to instance/phrase weighting, mixture/adaptive models, and VSM/distributed VSM weighting that are relevant non-LM approaches.
- Selection-size fairness concern: different methods use different tuned selection sizes, making it unclear if gains come from model quality or data volume; broader BLEU vs size curves are needed.
- Reproducibility/details gaps: unspecified validation split, training regimen (epochs/optimizer/early stopping), class balance/calibration, source–target score combination protocol, and unquantified negative sampling variant.
- Bilingual signal not fully explored: method scores source and target separately and sums them; no bilingual joint modeling or per-side ablation results are provided.
- Questions for authors: specify validation protocol for tuning selection size to avoid leakage.
- Request: provide BLEU vs selected-size curves for all methods on at least one more language pair with identical sizes.
- Request: include or discuss results vs discriminative instance weighting and VSM/distributed VSM baselines.
- Request: give quantitative source-only vs target-only vs sum ablation to justify using the sum.
- Request: report computational cost and engineering considerations for scoring 9–15M sentence pairs.
- Overall verdict: well-motivated and practically impactful with strong results; leans toward acceptance pending clarification of experimental details and stronger/fairer baseline comparisons.
