Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data (as few as 100–6k sentence pairs) is available. It trains a sentence-level domain classifier based on CNNs to rank general-domain sentence pairs by in-domain relevance and selects top-N for SMT training. The key technical element is a semi-supervised CNN that combines one-hot region features with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled text. The approach scores source and target sentences separately and selects by the sum of scores. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) show consistent gains over using all data and over n-gram/RNNLM-based selection, with especially strong improvements for zh→en sms. A study varying the labeled in-domain size shows robustness down to 100 sentence pairs.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical finding: Semi-supervised CNN-based sentence selection substantially outperforms LM-based selection in the extreme low in-domain setting (≤800 sentences), with robust gains across multiple language pairs and domains.
2) Practical method: A simple, scalable selection pipeline that uses pre-trained embeddings and separate source/target CNNs with summed scores; achieves competitive results with only a few percent of the full training data.
3) Low-resource analysis: A controlled study demonstrating stability of gains with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences.

Strengths
- Addresses a realistic and difficult setting. Many applications lack sizable in-domain data, especially in social media; showing consistent improvements with 100–800 labeled sentences is valuable and underexplored compared to prior work that assumed 10k–100k in-domain sentences.
- Strong baselines and scope. Comparisons include Moore-Lewis-style n-gram selection, RNNLM selection, and their combination, across four language pairs with 9–15M bilingual sentence pairs. The results are statistically validated and the selection sizes are reasonable (2.5–10%).
- Clear benefit of semi-supervised CNNs. The comparison to one-hot CNN indicates that unsupervised embeddings contribute meaningful robustness (≈0.5 BLEU average improvement), aligning with intuition about representation sharing for unseen but semantically similar words.
- Stability under tiny supervision. The 100-sentence regime shows sscnn outperforming LM-based selection by 1.2–2.0 BLEU and exhibiting low variance over repeated samplings, which strengthens the claim that the method is dependable in the intended use case.
- Practicality and reproducibility. The use of public data and standard toolkits (conText, word2vec, RNNLM) and a straightforward architecture makes the approach accessible to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. The architecture (semi-supervised CNN with region embeddings) closely follows Johnson and Zhang (2015b), and the task is a standard application (supervised domain classification for data selection). The main contribution is empirical, not methodological.
- Incomplete baselines for discriminative selection. The paper compares to LM-based selection but not to simpler or more direct discriminative alternatives that are natural in the tiny-label setting: e.g., logistic regression/SVM on character/word n-grams, TF-IDF/BM25 cosine similarity with averaged word embeddings, or domain-adversarial/PU-learning approaches that address prior shift.
- Potential leakage and unclear tuning protocol. The dev set is used as the positive (in-domain) sample; simultaneously, “meta-parameters are tuned on held-out data,” but it is not specified whether this held-out set is disjoint from the dev used for labeling and from the dev used to select N via BLEU. If selection size N and CNN hyperparameters are tuned on the same domain data used to train the classifier, this risks overfitting to domain idiosyncrasies and inflating gains.
- Calibration and class imbalance. The classifier is trained with equal numbers of positives and negatives but then used to rank a highly imbalanced pool (millions of mostly out-of-domain sentences). There is no analysis of score calibration, precision/recall at top-K, or robustness to negative sampling choices. PU-learning or importance weighting could better address the prior mismatch.
- Limited analysis and ablation. Key design choices are insufficiently dissected: source vs target vs sum (only a qualitative statement), effect of region size/pooling, whether embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned, and the impact of pretraining corpora. For zh→en, where a 120k in-domain subset is known, the paper does not report overlap/precision of the selected set—missing an opportunity to quantify selection quality. Runtime and throughput for scoring 10–30M sentences with two CNNs are also not reported.

Questions to authors
- Are the pre-trained word2vec embeddings kept fixed during CNN training or fine-tuned? On which corpora were they trained for each language, and how were OOVs handled (esp. for Chinese tokenization)?
- Please clarify the validation protocol: What constitutes the “held-out” set for CNN hyperparameter tuning? Is it disjoint from (i) the dev set used to label positives and (ii) the dev set used for tuning N via BLEU? If not, can you provide a clean split to rule out leakage?
- How is N (number of selected sentences) chosen per task? Is it tuned by maximizing dev BLEU, and if so, how sensitive are results to N? Can you provide selection-size curves for all language pairs?
- For zh→en sms: what fraction of the selected 300k overlaps the known 120k in-domain subset? This would quantify selection precision and help interpret the 26.0 BLEU vs 25.7 BLEU trained solely on the 120k.
- Can you compare against two lightweight discriminative baselines trained on the same tiny in-domain data: (a) cosine similarity of averaged word embeddings and (b) linear SVM/logistic regression on character/word n-grams? These would help isolate the gain due to convolutional modeling vs general discriminative selection.
- What is the computational cost (training time for CNNs, scoring throughput per sentence, total selection time) compared to LM-based methods? Is GPU required?
- How sensitive are the results to negative sampling (random seed, number of negatives relative to positives)? Did you try class-weighting or PU-learning to address class prior mismatch?
- Did you attempt a bilingual or joint model (e.g., concatenating source/target inputs, or using cross-lingual embeddings) rather than summing independent scores? If so, how did it compare?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper that addresses a timely practical problem with clear gains, but its methodological novelty is limited and several analysis and comparison gaps reduce the strength of the claims. With clarifications and additional discriminative baselines/ablations, it would be a strong contribution.
