Summary and contributions
The paper proposes using a convolutional neural network (CNN)–based domain classifier, augmented with pre-trained word embeddings (semi-supervised), to select in-domain parallel sentences from large general-domain corpora for SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain data are available. The classifier is trained with the small in-domain dev set as positives and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; sentences are scored on both source and target sides and the top-N pairs are used to train a phrase-based SMT system.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that semi-supervised CNN-based sentence selection can outperform standard LM- and RNNLM-based selection baselines across four language directions, especially when the in-domain seed is very small (down to ~100 sentences).
2) A practical recipe for robust data selection under severe in-domain scarcity, with multi-lingual evaluation and significance testing.
3) Evidence that combining source- and target-side domain scores for selection is beneficial (though limited ablation is provided).

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and under-explored scenario: adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data. The focus on “tiny” in-domain sets (as low as 100 sentences) is practically important.
- Solid empirical improvements over strong, established baselines. Across four directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es FB, en→zh FB), the semi-supervised CNN yields consistent gains over alldata and LM/RNNLM selection, with significance testing. The zh→en case shows a sizable +3.1 BLEU (and beats an “in-domain only” subset).
- Robustness to very small labeled seeds. The curve showing performance vs. number of labeled in-domain sentences suggests CNN-based selection degrades more gracefully than LM-based alternatives; at 100 sentences, sscnn still gives a notable boost.
- Method is simple and compatible with standard SMT pipelines, using off-the-shelf toolkits (word2vec, conText), and the selection outcome reduces training set size substantially (2.5–10% of original), which is practically attractive.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The method is a straightforward application of known CNN text classifiers with pre-trained embeddings to domain classification; similar semi-supervised CNNs for text categorization are known (Johnson & Zhang). The contribution is more engineering/evaluation than methodological.
- Potential data leakage and unclear validation protocol. The dev set is used as positive in-domain data for training the classifier. The paper also states the number of selected sentences is “empirically determined” on a “separate validation set,” but it is not clear how dev/validation/test are partitioned per domain, or whether any target-side information from the test domain creeps into selection tuning. More rigor is needed to ensure independence between model selection and evaluation.
- Baselines may be underpowered and missing. The n-gram baseline uses 3-gram Witten-Bell; stronger 5-gram Kneser-Ney (or at least 4-gram KN) is standard in Moore-Lewis. The RNNLM baseline details (architecture/size/regularization) are sparse; more modern LSTM-LMs would be a stronger comparator. Importantly, a simple discriminative classifier baseline (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on n-gram TF-IDF) is missing; such baselines often perform competitively with CNNs under small supervision and would test whether the gains stem from embeddings/CNNs or simply discriminative learning.
- Incomplete ablations and analysis. The paper states that using the sum of source and target scores is best, but does not report the actual numbers for source-only vs. target-only vs. sum. Similarly, both seq- and bow-regions are used, but there is no ablation to quantify each component’s contribution. It is unclear whether the pre-trained embeddings are frozen or fine-tuned; no analysis of embedding choices (dimensionality, training data) is provided.
- Practicality and generality concerns. Outside zh→en (where an in-domain subset exists in the pool), gains are modest (0.7–1.4 BLEU), though statistically significant; practical impact is debatable. The method relies on parallel in-domain dev (both source and target) to train two classifiers and to score target sentences; in many realistic settings only source-side in-domain text is available. Scalability/efficiency (training and scoring time for 9–15M sentences) is not reported, nor is variance across random negative samples/seeds.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the data splits: What exactly is used as “dev” (positive labels), what constitutes the “separate validation set” for tuning N, and how is test kept isolated? Is any target-side in-domain text used for tuning beyond the classifier training?
- Can you report ablations: (a) source-only vs. target-only vs. sum of scores; (b) bow vs. seq regions; (c) one-hot vs. embedding-only vs. combined inputs; (d) whether embeddings are frozen or fine-tuned in the supervised stage?
- How do stronger LM baselines perform (e.g., 5-gram KN Moore-Lewis/Axelrod, LSTM-LMs)? And how does a simple linear classifier (logistic regression or SVM with n-gram TF-IDF) compare under the same protocol?
- What is the computational cost (training time, scoring throughput, hardware) of sscnn vs. RNNLM selection on the largest setup (15M sentences)?
- How sensitive are results to negative sampling and random seeds? Please provide variance across multiple runs (not only across different 100-sentence samples), including initialization and negative sets.
- Does the approach still work when only source-side in-domain text is available (no target in-domain)? Please report source-only selection results.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the empirical gains are promising, especially under tiny supervision, but the methodological novelty is limited and several aspects of experimental rigor and baselines require strengthening before I would be comfortable endorsing this over established methods.
