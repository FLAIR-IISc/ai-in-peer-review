- Problem & method: Proposes semi-supervised CNN-based in/out-domain sentence classifier (one-hot + word2vec region embeddings) to score and select top-N sentence pairs from large general-domain corpora for SMT adaptation, scoring source and target independently and summing scores.

- Main empirical claim: Semi-supervised CNN selection outperforms LM-based selection in extreme low-label regimes (≤800 in-domain sentences), with up to +3.1 BLEU and +2.1 BLEU with only 100 sentences.

- Practical contribution: Validated a simple selection recipe (sum source+target CNN scores) at scale across four translation directions with significance testing.

- Method application: Demonstrates that region-embedding-enhanced text-CNNs transfer effectively to SMT data selection.

- Strength — realistic regime: Targets a practically important “tiny in-domain” setting (hundreds of pairs) poorly served by classic LM-based selection.

- Strength — strong empirical evidence: Large-scale experiments (9–15M pairs, multiple social-media test sets) show consistent improvements over training on all data and LM-based methods.

- Strength — simplicity & generality: Approach is conceptually simple and uses standard open-source tools, making it broadly applicable.

- Strength — sensible modeling & ablations: Uses both source and target scores and compares one-hot vs semi-supervised CNNs, with reasonable design choices (sum of scores works best).

- Strength — stability analysis: Repeats few-shot experiments (100 sentences) with multiple samples and reports small variance, supporting robustness.

- Weakness — limited novelty: Core model is similar to established CNN text classifiers; novelty mainly in application and low-label focus rather than modeling innovation.

- Weakness — incomplete baselines: Missing important comparators (instance weighting, submodular/FDA selection, TF-IDF or sparse linear classifiers, sentence-embedding cosine baselines, discriminative mixture/weighting).

- Weakness — reproducibility & clarity gaps: Lacks details on how bow/seq regions are fused, loss/regularization, epochs/early stopping, embedding fine-tuning, tokenization/OOV handling, and per-language model training.

- Weakness — selection tuning & validation ambiguity: Unclear how N and hyperparameters are tuned without leaking dev labels or reusing the labeled dev set.

- Weakness — computational cost unspecified: No account of the cost of scoring 9–15M sentences per side with CNNs.

- Weakness — potential task confound: zh→en SMS task contains a recoverable 120K in-domain subset in the general pool, possibly inflating the headline +3.1 BLEU result; few-shot curves shown only for zh→en.

- Weakness — hard selection only: Does not explore continuous weighting of sentence pairs by CNN score (instance weighting) or sensitivity of results to N or source-only vs target-only scoring.

- Question — embedding training: Are pre-trained word embeddings fixed or fine-tuned during supervised CNN training?

- Question — architecture fusion: How are bow-regions, seq-regions, and one-hot features combined (concatenation, multi-channel, etc.)?

- Question — optimization details: What is the exact loss, regularization (dropout/L2), number of epochs, and early stopping criteria?

- Question — training/validation split: What data is used as the separate validation set for tuning N and hyperparameters, and is there any risk of label/dev-set leakage?

- Question — source/target modeling: Are separate CNNs trained per language with language-specific embeddings and shared hyperparameters, and how do source-only and target-only selections perform quantitatively?

- Request — add simple baselines: Please include embedding-similarity (average word2vec + cosine) and TF-IDF / linear SVM or logistic regression baselines trained on the same tiny labels.

- Request — few-shot generality: Provide 100–1600 label learning curves for at least one task without an embedded in-domain subset (e.g., ar→en tweets) to show broader robustness.

- Request — weighting vs selection: Have you tried using CNN scores as continuous weights for phrase-table/LM training instead of hard selection, and how do results compare?

- Overall verdict: Solid empirical paper addressing an important problem; leans toward acceptance if authors strengthen baseline comparisons, clarify methodological details, and broaden few-shot analyses.
