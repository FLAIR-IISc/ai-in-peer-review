Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (hundreds of sentences). It proposes using a CNN-based in/out-of-domain classifier to score each sentence in the general-domain corpus, then selects the top-N sentences to train a phrase-based SMT system. To compensate for scarce in-domain labels, the authors use semi-supervised CNNs: one-hot region features combined with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled corpora. Source and target sides are scored independently and combined (sum). Experiments on four translation directions with very large training corpora show consistent BLEU gains over training on all data and over LM-based selection (Moore–Lewis with 3-gram LMs, RNNLM, and their combination). A key result is robustness when only 100 in-domain sentences are available.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical finding: semi-supervised CNN-based sentence selection achieves stronger and more stable adaptation than LM-based selection in the extreme low-label regime (≤800 in-domain sentences), with gains up to +3.1 BLEU and still +2.1 with 100 sentences.
2) Practical selection recipe combining source and target CNN scores, validated at scale across four directions with strong significance testing.
3) Application of semi-supervised CNNs with region embeddings to SMT data selection, showing that standard text-CNNs transfer effectively to this task.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important and under-explored regime. The “tiny in-domain” setting is realistic (social media, many topics) and not well served by classic LM-based selection that requires thousands of in-domain sentences. Demonstrating robust gains with only 100 labeled pairs is impactful.
- Strong empirical evidence across large-scale settings. Four directions with 9–15M sentence pairs, multiple social-media test domains, and consistent improvements over “alldata” and LM-based selection. Significance testing is reported; selection sizes are tuned and reported.
- Method simplicity and generality. The approach is conceptually simple (binary sentence-level domain classifier) and uses standard, open-source components (conText, word2vec), making it broadly applicable.
- Sensible modeling choices and ablations. Using both source and target sentence scores, and contrasting one-hot vs semi-supervised CNNs, is well motivated. The result that summing source+target scores works best is reasonable and practically useful.
- Stability analysis in the few-shot setting. Repeating the 100-sentence experiment with different samples and reporting small variance increases confidence in robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core model mirrors established CNN text classification with pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2015b; Kim 2014). The novelty lies primarily in the application to SMT data selection and the low-label emphasis. For a ConLL audience, the paper would benefit from either deeper modeling innovation (e.g., explicit domain contrastive training, multi-tasking, or weighting rather than hard selection) or a broader comparative study.
- Incomplete baseline coverage. Beyond Moore–Lewis and RNNLM, several competitive selection/weighting approaches are missing: instance weighting (Matsoukas et al., Foster et al.), submodular/FDA selection, TF-IDF or sparse linear classifier baselines, sentence-embedding similarity (e.g., averaging word2vec or doc2vec), and discriminative mixture/weighting of subcorpora. Given the method’s reliance on embeddings, a cosine-similarity baseline with averaged word2vec would be a particularly informative control.
- Clarity and reproducibility gaps. Important details are underspecified:
  - How bow-regions and seq-regions are combined (concatenation? multi-channel?), loss function, regularization, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, whether word embeddings are fine-tuned or frozen.
  - How separate source/target models are trained (one per language? identical hyperparameters?), tokenization/normalization (especially for Arabic/Chinese), and handling of OOVs.
  - How the selection size N is tuned (“separate validation set” is mentioned, but the dev set is already used for labels; potential leakage).
  - Computational cost of scoring 9–15M sentences per side with CNNs.
- Potential confound in zh→en sms. The presence of a 120K in-domain subset inside the general pool makes this task atypically favorable to selection; while you show improvements even without known in-domain subsets for other tasks, the marquee +3.1 BLEU is partly driven by recovering that subset. The few-shot analysis (100–1600 labels) is only shown for zh→en; analogous curves for other tasks would strengthen generality claims.
- Hard selection only. The paper does not explore using CNN scores as continuous weights (instance weighting) for translation and language models, which prior work suggests can outperform thresholding. Nor is there analysis of sensitivity to N, or comparisons of source-only vs target-only scoring (numbers are not reported).

Questions to authors
- Model details: Are pre-trained word embeddings fixed or fine-tuned during supervised training? How are bow and seq regions and one-hot vs embedding inputs fused (concatenation, summed projections, multi-channel)? What is the exact loss (softmax cross-entropy) and regularization (dropout, L2)? How many epochs/early stopping?
- Training/validation split: What data serves as the “separate validation set” for tuning N and hyperparameters, given that the dev set is used as positive labels? Is there any risk of using the same dev set for both training the classifier and tuning N?
- Source/target models: Do you train separate CNNs per language with language-specific embeddings? Are hyperparameters shared? How do source-only and target-only selection perform numerically relative to the sum?
- Baselines: Can you add a simple embedding-similarity baseline (e.g., average word2vec for in-domain vs candidate sentence, cosine) and a TF-IDF/linear SVM or logistic regression classifier baseline trained on the same tiny labels? These would help isolate the value of CNNs vs simpler models in the low-label regime.
- Few-shot generality: Can you provide the 100–1600 label learning curves for at least one of the tasks without an embedded in-domain subset (e.g., ar→en tweets), to demonstrate robustness beyond zh→en?
- Weighting vs selection: Have you tried weighting sentence pairs by CNN score instead of hard selection for phrase-table and LM training? If so, how do results compare?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper addressing an important practical problem with a simple, effective method. Strengthening baselines, clarifying methodology, and broadening the few-shot analysis would substantially improve the work. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the comparators and clarity issues.
