Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (hundreds to a few thousand sentences). It proposes to train a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score sentence pairs in a large general-domain corpus for domain relevance, then select top-scoring pairs to train phrase-based SMT systems. To mitigate the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, the method uses a semi-supervised CNN that combines one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) learned on unlabeled general-domain data. Selection is performed on both source and target sides, using the sum of scores. Experiments across four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh), including social media test domains, show consistent BLEU gains over using all data and over LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, combined). The method remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Methodological contribution: Applying a semi-supervised CNN classifier (combining one-hot and pre-trained embedding features) to SMT data selection for domain adaptation under extreme in-domain data scarcity, with a simple scoring scheme using both source and target sides.

2) Empirical contribution: Large-scale evaluation on four language pairs with strong LM-based selection baselines and significance testing, demonstrating consistent improvements and robustness down to 100 in-domain sentences.

3) Practical insight: Using the sum of source- and target-side domain scores improves selection; only 2.5–10% of the general corpus can outperform training on all data.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across multiple settings: The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms one-hot CNN and all LM-based baselines on all four tasks, with statistically significant gains (e.g., +3.1 BLEU zh→en sms, +1.4 BLEU ar→en and en→zh). The study spans 9–15M sentence corpora and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), enhancing external validity.

- Effectiveness under extreme low-resource in-domain labeling: Systematic analysis of in-domain sample size shows sscnn maintains clear gains and stability even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU over alldata, low variance across resamples). This directly supports the paper’s target scenario.

- Reasonable baselines and fair comparisons: The paper includes standard and stronger data selection baselines (Moore–Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination), uses bootstrap significance testing, and documents that sscnn improves over the best LM-based method by 0.3–0.5 BLEU when more labeled data exist.

- Clear, replicable high-level procedure: The selection pipeline is straightforward (train binary classifier with dev as positives and random negatives; score and rank general-domain sentence pairs; select top N; build SMT). The paper documents key CNN hyperparameters and word2vec setup, aiding reproducibility.

- Insightful analysis of why it works: The discussion connects CNN’s locality and max-pooling with domain-indicative n-grams and highlights the role of pre-trained embeddings in handling unseen but semantically similar tokens, which aligns with observed robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The approach adapts known semi-supervised CNN text classification (Johnson & Zhang, 2015) and word2vec features to SMT data selection. While the application to the “tiny in-domain” SMT adaptation setting is useful, the methodological novelty beyond combining one-hot and embedding-based region features is incremental.

- Incomplete methodological details and potential evaluation confounds: The paper uses the dev set as positive training data for the classifier and also (presumably) for SMT tuning, which could bias selection toward dev characteristics; the paper should clarify the separation between data used to choose selection size N (“held-out”) and dev/test. It is also unclear whether pre-trained embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned in the classifier, and whether bilingual coupling beyond summing source/target scores was explored.

- Ablation and sensitivity gaps: While the paper states that summing source and target scores performs best, it does not provide ablation numbers for source-only vs target-only vs sum across tasks, nor for bow vs seq inputs, or the contribution of embeddings vs one-hot in the semi-supervised model. This limits interpretability of where the gains come from.

- Missing comparisons and variants: No comparison to other discriminative selection/weighting approaches (e.g., linear or neural sentence classifiers beyond CNN, sentence embedding similarity, instance weighting rather than hard selection) is presented. Weighting sentences by classifier scores rather than hard selection could be a strong baseline.

- Computational considerations and scalability: There is no analysis of training/inference cost for CNN classification over tens of millions of sentences versus LM-based selection, nor any discussion of runtime/memory trade-offs. Practical deployment considerations would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to authors
- Data usage and validation: Precisely which data are used to (a) train the CNN classifier, (b) tune its hyperparameters, (c) determine the number of selected sentences N, and (d) tune SMT model weights? Is there a clean separation between data used to train the selector and the data used to tune/evaluate MT to avoid selection-overfitting to dev?

- Embeddings: Are the pre-trained word embeddings kept fixed during CNN training, or fine-tuned? What word2vec hyperparameters (window size, negative sampling vs hierarchical softmax) were used? Any OOV handling?

- Source vs target vs sum: Can you report per-task BLEU results for selection based on source-only, target-only, and sum, to quantify the benefit of combining both sides?

- Selection size: How is N chosen in practice? Is performance sensitive to N? Could a validation curve or criterion (e.g., dev-set perplexity) be used to pick N automatically?

- Weighting vs selection: Did you try using classifier scores as sentence weights in translation/language models instead of hard selection? If so, with what outcome?

- Efficiency: What are the training and inference times for the CNN selector compared to LM-based methods on your largest corpus?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound adaptation of semi-supervised CNN text classification to SMT data selection, with solid empirical evidence of effectiveness in the intended tiny in-domain setting. Novelty is moderate, and some methodological clarifications and ablations would strengthen the work.
