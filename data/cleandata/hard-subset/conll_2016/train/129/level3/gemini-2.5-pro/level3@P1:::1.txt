### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) from a large general-domain corpus. The core idea is to frame data selection as a classification task, training a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to distinguish between a small set of known in-domain sentences and randomly sampled out-of-domain sentences. To handle the scenario where in-domain data is extremely limited, the authors employ a semi-supervised CNN that leverages pre-trained word embeddings from a large unlabeled corpus to improve classification accuracy. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentences in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  The novel application of a semi-supervised CNN for the task of SMT data selection. The work demonstrates that this classification-based approach is highly effective, particularly in a very low-resource setting where traditional language model-based methods may struggle due to data sparsity.
2.  Strong and consistent empirical results across four different language pairs and three distinct domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The method shows substantial BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 points) over a strong baseline trained on all available data, and significantly outperforms established n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., a few hundred sentences). This is a highly relevant scenario for emerging domains like social media or for building systems adapted to fine-grained topics, where collecting large in-domain corpora is prohibitively expensive.
2.  **Compelling Low-Resource Performance:** The analysis in Figure 1, which varies the amount of in-domain seed data, is particularly strong. It clearly demonstrates that while all methods improve over the baseline, the proposed CNN-based methods, especially the semi-supervised variant, are more robust and effective when the seed data size is very small (100-800 sentences). This provides strong evidence for the central claim of the paper.
3.  **Strong Empirical Comparison:** The authors provide a solid comparison against relevant and widely-used data selection baselines, including methods based on n-gram LMs (Axelrod et al., 2011) and Recurrent Neural Network LMs (Duh et al., 2013). The consistent and statistically significant improvements of the proposed `sscnn` method over these baselines across all four tasks convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness.
4.  **Clear Methodological Description:** The description of the one-hot CNN and the semi-supervised extension in Section 3 is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for using pre-trained embeddings to overcome data sparsity is sound and well-explained.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Comparison to Bilingual Selection Baselines:** A significant weakness is the lack of comparison to bilingual data selection methods. The cited baselines (Moore and Lewis, 2010; Axelrod et al., 2011) primarily focus on monolingual scoring. However, Axelrod et al. (2011) famously proposed using the difference in cross-entropy between an in-domain and a general-domain LM, which has been extended to combining scores from both source and target sides. The authors' method of summing the source and target classification scores is conceptually very similar to a sum-of-cross-entropies approach, making this omission a critical gap in the experimental comparison. The authors should also cite the bilingual selection work of Moore and Lewis (2010).
2.  **Unclear Experimental Procedures and Potentially Unfair Comparison:** The main results in Table 2 are difficult to interpret fairly because different methods select a different number of sentences. While the paper states this number was determined on a validation set, this means the comparison is not controlled; one method might appear better simply because it had a more favorable selection size. A better comparison would fix the number of selected sentences for all methods (as is done in Figure 1) to isolate the quality of the selection criterion itself. Furthermore, the process for tuning CNN hyperparameters and the validation set used for this are not described, which hinders reproducibility.
3.  **Lack of Architectural Justification and Ablation:** The paper does not justify the choice of a CNN over other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs/LSTMs) which are also powerful sentence encoders. Additionally, the CNN model uses both bag-of-words (bow-regions) and sequential (seq-regions) representations, but there is no ablation study to show the relative importance of each component. This makes it unclear whether word order information captured by `seq-regions` is actually beneficial for this task.
4.  **Presentation and Minor Issues:** The paper would benefit greatly from a diagram illustrating the CNN architecture, particularly how the one-hot and pre-trained embedding inputs are combined in the semi-supervised model. The parenthetical citation style is also inconsistent throughout the paper (e.g., mixing `(Author, year)` and `Author (year)` styles improperly).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on why bilingual LM-based selection methods (e.g., cross-entropy difference or a sum of scores from source and target LMs) were not included as baselines? Given that you sum the source and target CNN scores, this seems like a very relevant comparison point.
2.  Could you please clarify the validation procedure used to determine both the optimal number of selected sentences for each method in Table 2 and the CNN hyperparameters? How sensitive are the results to these choices? A comparison where all methods select the same number of sentences would strengthen your claims.
3.  Your method excels with tiny amounts of in-domain data. How does it perform relative to baselines when a larger (e.g., 10k-50k sentences) in-domain set is available for training the selection model? Do the advantages of the semi-supervised approach diminish as the amount of labeled data increases?
4.  To support the claims in Section 5.4 regarding the benefits of word embeddings, could you provide a comparison against the method from Axelrod et al. (2015), which uses POS replacement to handle data sparsity in LMs? This would offer a more direct test of whether the advantage comes from the distributed representations of word2vec or from the CNN architecture itself.
5.  Could you provide a few qualitative examples of sentences that were highly ranked by your `sscnn` method but not by the LM-based methods, especially in the low-resource setting? This would provide valuable intuition for why the method works well.
