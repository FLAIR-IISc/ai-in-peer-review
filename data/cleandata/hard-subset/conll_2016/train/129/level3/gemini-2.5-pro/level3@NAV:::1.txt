**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for machine translation (MT) by using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) as a classifier. The CNN is trained on a small set of in-domain data (positive examples) and general-domain data (negative examples) to learn a domain relevance score. This model then ranks all sentences in a large general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are used to train an adapted MT system. The authors claim their method is particularly effective in low-resource scenarios and significantly outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel and effective application of a CNN-based classifier for MT data selection. This work demonstrates that a discriminative classification approach can be superior to established perplexity-based scoring methods for this task.
2.  The paper successfully shows that a semi-supervised approach, which integrates pre-trained word embeddings into the CNN, is highly effective for domain adaptation, especially when the amount of in-domain seed data is extremely limited.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper addresses the important and practical problem of domain adaptation for MT systems when very little in-domain parallel data is available, which is a common and challenging real-world scenario.
2.  The empirical results are strong and compelling. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms competitive baselines, including n-gram and RNNLM-based selection, across four different language pairs and domains, with reported gains of up to 3.1 BLEU.
3.  A key strength is the demonstration of the method's robustness in an extremely low-data regime (as few as 100 seed sentences), where traditional LM-based methods show more significant performance degradation. This highlights the model's utility for fine-grained or emerging domains.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The experimental validation has several weaknesses. Most notably, Table 2 compares different methods using varying amounts of selected sentences, which complicates a direct and fair comparison. Furthermore, the validation procedure for choosing both the quantity of selected data and the CNN hyperparameters is not clearly described, and no sensitivity analysis is provided.
2.  The paper omits comparisons to highly relevant prior work. Given that the final score is a sum of source and target classifications, a comparison to bilingual selection methods, such as the sum/difference of cross-entropies (Moore & Lewis, 2010), is essential but missing. The advantage of word embeddings is also not tested against simpler LM sparsity-reduction techniques (e.g., Axelrod, 2015).
3.  The architectural motivation and analysis are insufficient. The choice of a CNN over alternatives like an RNN/LSTM is asserted but not empirically justified. The paper also lacks an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of the bag-of-words and sequential components of the CNN input. A diagram of the network architecture is also needed for clarity.
