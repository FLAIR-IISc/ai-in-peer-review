- Summary: Proposes framing SMT data selection as a binary classification task solved by a semi-supervised CNN using a tiny in-domain seed, pretrained embeddings, and scoring a large general corpus to select data for domain-adapted SMT, showing large gains especially with ~100 in-domain sentences.

- Contribution 1: Novel application of a semi-supervised CNN for data selection that is effective in extreme low-resource in-domain settings.

- Contribution 2: Strong empirical validation across four language pairs and three domains, outperforming n-gram and RNNLM-based baselines.

- Strength 1: Tackles a practical, important problem and achieves substantial BLEU improvements with very small in-domain seeds.

- Strength 2: Compares against strong, relevant baselines (Axelrod et al. 2011 n-gram LM and Duh et al. 2013 RNNLM).

- Strength 3: Well-motivated semi-supervised use of pretrained word embeddings, with clear benefit shown by ohcnn vs sscnn comparison.

- Weakness 1: Omits key comparisons to bilingual selection methods (e.g., Moore & Lewis 2010 sum/difference of cross-entropies), making it unclear if gains come from CNN or bilingual scoring.

- Weakness 2: No experimental justification for choosing a CNN over other architectures (RNN/LSTM) and no ablation on BOW vs SEQ inputs to identify critical model components.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient detail on validation and hyperparameter tuning; Table 2 reports methods selecting different data amounts, complicating fair comparison.

- Weakness 4: Minor presentation issues — lacks a CNN architecture diagram and has inconsistent citation formatting.

- Question 1: Please clarify the validation procedure for selecting sentence counts and tuning CNN hyperparameters, and report sensitivity of BLEU to these choices.

- Question 2: Please comment on the similarity to Moore & Lewis (2010) bilingual scoring and provide a comparison to their sum/difference cross-entropy baseline.

- Question 3: Please provide Table 2 results where all methods select the same fixed number of sentences (e.g., optimal amount for sscnn) for fair comparison.

- Question 4: Please provide ablation analyses (e.g., BOW vs SEQ, CNN vs alternatives) and qualitative examples of sentences uniquely selected by your method.

- Question 5: Have you considered a truly bilingual CNN that processes source and target jointly instead of scoring them independently and summing?
