### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) by framing training data selection as a text classification task. The core idea is to train a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to distinguish between in-domain and general-domain sentences. This classifier, trained with a very small set of in-domain seed sentences (e.g., the development set) and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain sentences, is then used to score all sentences in a large general-domain corpus. The top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from initializing the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned on large unlabeled corpora. The authors claim that this approach is particularly effective in low-resource scenarios where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection. By treating selection as a classification problem, the method moves beyond traditional perplexity-based approaches and leverages the ability of CNNs to identify salient domain-specific features.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration that this classification-based approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based methods (both n-gram and RNNLM) across four language pairs. The method shows particular strength in the challenging scenario where seed in-domain data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences).

### Strengths

1.  **High-Impact Application of a Novel Method:** The paper successfully applies a modern deep learning architecture (CNNs) to the long-standing problem of data selection for SMT adaptation. Framing this as a classification task is a creative and effective shift from standard LM perplexity methods. The results are compelling, showing consistent and significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 points) over strong baselines, often while using a much smaller subset of the training data.
2.  **Addresses a Practical and Challenging Scenario:** The work's focus on adaptation with a "tiny amount" of in-domain data is highly relevant. In many real-world applications, especially for new or fine-grained domains (like social media), acquiring large in-domain parallel corpora is prohibitively expensive. The experiments in Figure 1 convincingly demonstrate the method's robustness in this low-resource setting, where the performance of LM-based methods degrades more sharply. This makes the proposed method practically very valuable.
3.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The method is validated on four different language directions and three distinct, challenging domains from social media (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). This demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the approach. The comparison against multiple established baselines, including n-gram and RNNLM-based selection, provides a solid context for interpreting the performance gains.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Uncontrolled Experimental Comparisons:** The primary results table (Table 2) compares the proposed method against baselines using different quantities of selected data. While the paper states this quantity was optimized on a validation set for each method, it prevents a direct, controlled comparison of the selection methods' quality. A fair comparison requires evaluating all methods on the same amount of selected data. While Figure 1 does this for one task, this controlled comparison should be the basis for the main results table to properly isolate the effect of the selection algorithm itself.
2.  **Missing Key Baselines and Incomplete Positioning:** The paper compares against monolingual LM-based selection methods but omits comparisons to common bilingual approaches, such as the cross-entropy difference method of Moore and Lewis (2010). The authors' approach of summing the source and target classification scores is conceptually very similar to the bilingual sum-of-cross-entropies heuristic and should be discussed in that context. Furthermore, a comparison to Axelrod et al. (2015), which uses POS tagging to address data sparsity in LMs for data selection, would have made for a more competitive baseline.
3.  **Lack of Model Analysis and Ablation:** The paper provides a high-level intuition for why CNNs are effective (Section 5.4) but lacks empirical evidence to support these claims. There is no analysis of the CNN model's components; for example, an ablation study is needed to understand the relative importance of the `bow-regions` versus `seq-regions` representations. The paper would also be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis showing examples of sentences that were highly ranked by the proposed CNN method but not by the LM baselines (or vice-versa), which would provide more concrete support for the claimed advantages. Finally, while the textual description of the model is clear, a simple diagram illustrating the CNN architecture would improve clarity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the validation procedure used to select the optimal number of sentences for each method in Table 2, as well as for tuning the CNN hyperparameters? How sensitive is the final SMT performance to these choices?
2.  Your work compellingly demonstrates the strength of the CNN approach in the "tiny" in-domain data setting. How does your method perform relative to the LM-based baselines when a larger amount of in-domain data (e.g., 30k sentences, as in Axelrod et al., 2011) is available? Does the performance gap between the methods narrow?
3.  The current method scores source and target sentences independently and sums the scores. Have you considered or experimented with a truly bilingual CNN architecture that takes the sentence pair as a single input to learn a joint representation for domain relevance? This might better capture translational equivalence and domain-specific lexical choices.
