### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to perform data selection for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT). The core idea is to train a CNN as a binary classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences. This classifier is trained using a very small set of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples. To overcome data sparsity, the model incorporates pre-trained word embeddings learned from a large unlabeled corpus, making it semi-supervised. Once trained, the classifier scores all sentences in the large general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The method is evaluated on four language pairs with social media test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), demonstrating improvements over strong language model-based data selection baselines.

### Contributions
1.  **Demonstration of CNNs for Data Selection with Tiny Seed Data:** The primary contribution is the empirical finding that a CNN-based classifier is highly effective for SMT data selection, especially when the amount of available in-domain seed data is extremely small (as few as 100 sentences). The paper shows that this approach is significantly more robust than traditional language model-based methods in low-resource adaptation scenarios.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance in Data Selection:** The paper presents a semi-supervised CNN approach that consistently outperforms strong, widely-used data selection baselines (n-gram and RNN-based language models) across four different language pairs and three domains. The consistent gains of ~0.5 BLEU over the best LM-based method establish this as a new state-of-the-art technique for this task.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Challenging Task:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of domain adaptation with very limited resources. The proposed semi-supervised CNN method consistently outperforms three strong, state-of-the-art baselines across four diverse language pairs. The improvements are statistically significant and demonstrate the practical value of applying modern neural classification techniques to this established problem.
2.  **Robustness in a Low-Resource Setting:** The paper's most compelling result is its demonstration of the method's effectiveness with tiny amounts of in-domain data. Figure 1 shows that while the performance of LM-based methods degrades sharply as the in-domain seed set shrinks, the CNN-based methods remain stable and effective. Achieving a 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 seed sentences (on the zh->en task) is a significant finding and makes this approach highly suitable for fine-grained topic adaptation or adaptation to new domains where data collection is expensive.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors validate their approach across multiple language directions (zh->en, ar->en, en->es, en->zh) and relevant, challenging social media domains. The use of large-scale training corpora (9-15M sentence pairs) ensures the experiments are realistic. Comparing against multiple established baselines, including those from Axelrod et al. (2011) and Duh et al. (2013), strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Potentially Misleading Headline Claim:** The reported gain of +3.1 BLEU on the zh->en task is somewhat misleading. As acknowledged, the training data for this task contains a 120K sentence in-domain subset. A baseline that simply trains on this known subset achieves a BLEU score of 25.7, which accounts for a +2.8 BLEU gain over the `alldata` baseline (22.9). The proposed method's score of 26.0 is thus only a +0.3 BLEU improvement over this strong "oracle" baseline. A more appropriate comparison for adaptation would be against a model that interpolates an in-domain model with a general-domain one.
2.  **Generalizability of the "Tiny Data" Result is Unproven:** The paper's strongest claim—that the method excels with very little seed data—is supported by an experiment run only on the zh->en task (Figure 1). This is the one task where a large, clean in-domain set exists within the training pool, which may make the classification task easier. To fully substantiate this central claim, this experiment should be replicated across the other language pairs, which represent a more realistic scenario where in-domain sentences are not guaranteed to be present in a distinct block.
3.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity:** The description of the semi-supervised CNN architecture in Section 3.2 is unclear. It is not specified how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and the segment embeddings (`ul(x)`) are combined as input to the second convolutional layer. An architectural diagram would greatly improve clarity. Additionally, the paper mentions minimizing a "designated loss function" without stating which one was used (e.g., cross-entropy). Important experimental details, such as the method for tuning the SMT system weights (presumably MERT), are also omitted.
4.  **Limited Discussion of Domain-Specific Phenomena:** The experiments are conducted on social media text, which is notoriously noisy and presents challenges like high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. The paper does not discuss whether any special preprocessing was performed to handle these issues. Data selection can improve lexical choice and phrasing for in-vocabulary items but cannot address novel OOVs. The analysis would be more insightful with qualitative examples showing the types of translation errors that are fixed by this method.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The impressive results with very small amounts of in-domain data (Figure 1) were only shown for the zh->en task. Can you provide results for the other three language pairs using only 100 in-domain sentences to demonstrate that this key finding generalizes to scenarios without a large, known in-domain subset in the training data?
2.  For the zh->en task, have you considered comparing your method against a baseline that linearly or log-linearly interpolates an SMT model trained on the 120K in-domain sentences with one trained on the remaining general-domain data? This would provide a clearer picture of your method's contribution beyond simply identifying this pre-existing subset.
3.  Could you please clarify the architecture of the semi-supervised CNN? Specifically, how are the one-hot representation and the pre-trained embedding-based representation combined in Equation 2? An illustration would be helpful. Also, what loss function was used to train the classifier?
4.  Given the use of social media test sets, did you perform any domain-specific preprocessing (e.g., to handle usernames, hashtags, OOVs)? Could you provide a few concrete translation examples that illustrate the improvements (e.g., better lexical choice, phrasing) your data selection method provides over the `alldata` baseline?
