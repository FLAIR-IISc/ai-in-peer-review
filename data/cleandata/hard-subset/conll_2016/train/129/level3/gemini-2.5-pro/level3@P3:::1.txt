### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method for training data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, particularly for scenarios where in-domain data is extremely scarce. The authors frame data selection as a binary classification task (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to solve it. A small set of in-domain sentences and an equally-sized set of randomly sampled general-domain sentences are used to train the CNN classifier. This classifier then scores all sentence pairs in a large general-domain corpus. The pairs with the highest scores are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The model is "semi-supervised" as it leverages pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec on large unlabeled corpora) as input features, which helps to overcome the sparsity of the labeled in-domain training data. Experiments on four language pairs with social media test domains demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms strong language model-based baselines, especially when the amount of seed in-domain data is as low as 100 sentences.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel and Effective Application of CNNs for Data Selection:** The paper introduces a new approach for SMT data selection by framing it as a classification problem solved by a semi-supervised CNN. This method proves highly effective for the practical and challenging scenario of domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The work provides strong empirical evidence across four different language pairs and three domains, showing that the proposed CNN-based method consistently and significantly outperforms established data selection techniques based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models. The robustness of the method in low-resource settings is a key finding.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation when in-domain data is scarce. The ability to achieve substantial BLEU improvements using as few as 100 in-domain sentences (as shown in Figure 1) is a compelling result that highlights the practical utility and high sample efficiency of the proposed method.
2.  **Strong and Relevant Baselines:** The authors compare their method against strong and appropriate baselines from the data selection literature, namely the n-gram LM approach (Axelrod et al., 2011) and the RNNLM approach (Duh et al., 2013). Consistent and significant improvements over these state-of-the-art methods provide convincing evidence for the superiority of the proposed technique.
3.  **Well-Motivated Semi-Supervised Approach:** The use of pre-trained word embeddings to enrich the input representation is a well-motivated strategy to combat the lack of labeled data. The comparison between the `ohcnn` (one-hot) and `sscnn` (semi-supervised) models clearly demonstrates the crucial contribution of these embeddings to the model's success.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Missing Key Comparisons to Bilingual Methods:** The authors score source and target sentences independently and sum their scores. This strategy is conceptually very similar to the sum/difference of cross-entropies method from Moore & Lewis (2010) for bilingual data selection. This seminal work is neither cited nor used as a baseline. This is a significant omission, as it is unclear if the performance gains stem from the CNN architecture itself or from simply using a bilingual scoring signal, which the monolingual LM baselines lack. It would also be interesting to compare against methods that address LM sparsity differently, such as the POS replacement strategy in Axelrod et al. (2015), to better isolate the specific advantages of word embeddings.
2.  **Lack of Architectural Justification and Analysis:** The motivation for choosing a CNN over other neural architectures like an RNN/LSTM is not experimentally justified. The paper claims that CNNs capture important local features, but this is not verified through analysis. Furthermore, the model uses both bag-of-words (BOW) and sequential (SEQ) region inputs, but no ablation study is performed to determine their relative contributions. This makes it difficult to understand which aspects of the model are most critical to its performance.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Experimental Protocol:** The validation procedure is opaque. The paper states that the number of selected sentences and CNN hyperparameters are tuned on held-out data, but provides no details on this process, impacting reproducibility. Moreover, Table 2 reports results where each method selects a different amount of data, making a direct and fair comparison difficult. Although Figure 1 provides a fixed-data-size comparison for one task, this should be the standard for all primary results.
4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The clarity of the paper could be improved. A diagram of the CNN architecture would be a valuable addition to complement the textual description. Additionally, the parenthetical citation style is inconsistent throughout the paper (mixing `\citet` and `\citep` styles), which detracts from the paper's polish.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the validation procedure used for choosing the optimal number of selected sentences and for tuning the CNN hyperparameters? How sensitive are the final BLEU scores to these choices?
2.  Your method of summing source and target scores is reminiscent of bilingual scoring in Moore & Lewis (2010). Could you comment on this similarity and perhaps provide a comparison against their sum/difference of cross-entropies baseline?
3.  For a fairer comparison, could you provide results in Table 2 where all methods select the same, fixed number of sentences (e.g., the optimal amount for your `sscnn` method)?
4.  Could you provide some analysis or an ablation study to support the claim that the CNN is effective because it captures important local features? For instance, what are the relative contributions of the BOW vs. SEQ inputs? Providing qualitative examples of sentences uniquely selected by your method would also be very insightful.
5.  Have you considered extending the model to a truly bilingual CNN that processes the source and target sentences jointly, rather than scoring them independently and summing? Such a model could potentially learn a more sophisticated combination of signals from both languages.
