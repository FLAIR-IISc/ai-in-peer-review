**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to select domain-relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus. The CNN is trained as a binary classifier using the small in-domain set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples. To overcome data scarcity, the model incorporates pre-trained word embeddings learned from the entire general-domain corpus. This trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentences are used to train a domain-adapted SMT system. Experiments on four language pairs with social media test domains show that this method outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines and is particularly robust when the in-domain data for training the classifier is reduced to as few as 100 sentences.

**Contributions**
1.  **Effective Application of CNNs for SMT Data Selection in a Low-Resource Setting:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN architecture, inspired by recent work in text classification, to the task of data selection for SMT adaptation. While data selection is a well-studied problem, this work demonstrates that a CNN-based classifier can outperform established LM-based methods. The most significant aspect of this contribution is the method's robustness when the available in-domain data is extremely limited (e.g., a few hundred sentence pairs), a scenario where traditional LM-based approaches degrade more sharply.

**Strengths**
1.  **Compelling Performance with Extremely Limited In-domain Data:** The paper's most impressive result is the demonstration that the proposed semi-supervised CNN method remains effective even when trained with a tiny amount of in-domain data. Figure 1 shows that with only 100 in-domain sentences for training the classifier, the `sscnn` method still achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the `alldata` baseline on the zh->en task. This is a significant practical advantage, as collecting large in-domain datasets is often expensive or impossible, making this technique highly relevant for fine-grained or rapidly evolving domains.

2.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines, including three state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods, across four different language pairs and three distinct social media domains (Table 2). The improvements over the best LM-based methods are statistically significant and average around 0.5 BLEU, demonstrating the general applicability and effectiveness of the approach. The experimental setup is solid, comparing against relevant and strong competitors on large-scale SMT tasks.

3.  **Novelty in SMT Domain Adaptation:** The use of a neural classifier for data selection, particularly a CNN that can capture local n-gram-like features without explicit feature engineering, is a novel approach in the context of SMT domain adaptation. The semi-supervised nature of the model, leveraging pre-trained embeddings to compensate for the lack of labeled data, is well-motivated and shown to be critical for achieving the best performance (comparing `sscnn` vs. `ohcnn`).

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Potentially Misleading Headline Result:** The claimed +3.1 BLEU improvement for the zh->en sms task is presented as a headline result but requires careful interpretation. The authors note that the general-domain data for this task contains a 120K sentence in-domain subset. A system trained only on this known subset achieves 25.7 BLEU, which is already a +2.8 BLEU improvement over the `alldata` baseline. This suggests that much of the gain comes from the presence of this "clean" in-domain data, and the proposed method is effective at identifying it. A stronger and more insightful baseline would be an interpolated model, combining a system trained on the 120K in-domain data with one trained on the remaining out-of-domain data. The reported +0.3 BLEU gain over the `in-domain data only` baseline is more representative of the method's contribution in this specific setting.

2.  **Limited Evidence Generalizing the "Tiny Data" Claim:** The paper's most compelling claim—that the method works well with very few (e.g., 100) in-domain sentences—is only experimentally verified for the zh->en task. As discussed above, this is the one task where a large, clean in-domain corpus is known to exist within the training pool. It is crucial to demonstrate that this robustness also holds for the other three tasks, where no such in-domain data is known to exist. Without this, the generalizability of this key finding is questionable.

3.  **Lack of Analysis on Social Media Phenomena:** The experiments are conducted on social media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook), which are characterized by unique linguistic phenomena such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, non-standard spellings, and emoticons. Data selection from a general-domain corpus cannot introduce new vocabulary to handle OOVs. The paper lacks any discussion of these challenges. It is unclear if any special preprocessing was performed or how the model handles these phenomena. Providing a few concrete translation examples would be highly beneficial to illustrate what kind of improvements (e.g., better lexical choice, improved fluency) the selected data provides, beyond the aggregate BLEU score.

4.  **Lack of Clarity in Model and Experimental Details:**
    *   Section 3.2, which describes the core `sscnn` model, is difficult to follow. It is unclear how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and the word-embedding-based segment vectors (`ul(x)`) are combined in Equation 2. An architectural diagram would greatly improve clarity. The paper also mentions a "designated loss function" without specifying what it is (e.g., cross-entropy).
    *   Section 5.2 does not state how the SMT system weights (e.g., for MERT tuning) were tuned. Was the development set used for both training the CNN classifier and for tuning the final SMT system? This overlap could be a concern and should be clarified.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the zh->en sms task: Could you compare your method against a stronger baseline that interpolates a model trained on the 120K in-domain subset with a model trained on the remaining general-domain data? This would provide a clearer picture of the gains attributable to your selection method versus simply identifying a known in-domain corpus.

2.  The experiments showing robustness with very small amounts of in-domain data (Figure 1) are very promising but are only presented for the zh->en task. To strengthen this central claim, could you provide results for the other three language pairs using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs to train the classifier?

3.  Could you provide some qualitative analysis or translation examples? Specifically, for the social media domains, how does your method improve translation? Does it help with lexical choice for in-vocabulary words, or does it improve fluency? How does the system handle domain-specific OOVs like hashtags or usernames that are likely absent from the general-domain training data?

4.  Could you please clarify the model architecture in Section 3.2? A diagram would be helpful. What specific loss function was minimized during training? Also, could you please specify the data used for tuning the SMT system weights for each experimental condition?
