### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for in-domain data selection to adapt Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) systems. The method trains a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, using a small set of labeled in-domain data (positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain data (negative examples). The semi-supervised aspect comes from using pre-trained word embeddings learned on large unlabeled corpora. This trained classifier then scores all sentences in a large general-domain training pool, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system. The main claim is that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art language model (LM)-based data selection methods and is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as little as 100 sentences) is available.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel application of CNNs for SMT data selection that is robust in low-data scenarios.** The primary contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN architecture (based on Johnson & Zhang, 2015) to the task of bilingual data selection for SMT adaptation. The most significant finding is that this method remains effective even with extremely small amounts of in-domain seed data (100-800 sentences), a scenario where traditional LM-based methods degrade more sharply.
2.  **An empirical demonstration that CNN-based classifiers can outperform strong LM-based methods for data selection.** The paper provides a solid empirical comparison against established data selection techniques (n-gram and RNN LMs), showing consistent, albeit modest, improvements of around +0.5 BLEU on average across four language pairs.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** Domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data is a frequent and challenging real-world problem, especially with the emergence of new domains like social media. This paper offers a novel and practical solution that moves beyond standard perplexity-based methods.
2.  **Compelling Results in the Low-Resource Setting:** The paper’s strongest result is presented in Figure 1, which shows that the CNN-based methods, especially the semi-supervised variant (`sscnn`), are significantly more stable and effective than LM-based methods when the amount of in-domain seed data is very small. The reported +2.1 BLEU gain over the `alldata` baseline using only 100 seed sentences is impressive and highlights the method's main advantage.
3.  **Strong and Relevant Baselines:** The authors compare their method against strong, widely-used data selection baselines, including the cross-entropy difference method with n-gram LMs and RNNLMs. This makes the reported improvements over these methods credible and demonstrates a clear advantage for the proposed technique.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Misleading Headline Claim and Evaluation Flaw:** The headline claim of a +3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh→en task is highly misleading. The authors state that the general-domain training data for this task contains a 120K-sentence in-domain subset, and that training a system on this subset *alone* achieves a 25.7 BLEU score. This is a +2.8 BLEU gain over the `alldata` baseline of 22.9. This means that simply identifying and using this pre-existing data accounts for the vast majority of the improvement. The proposed selection method only adds an additional +0.3 BLEU (26.0 vs 25.7) over this trivial "in-domain only" baseline. A much more informative comparison would be against a system that interpolates a general-domain model with a model trained on this 120K subset.
2.  **Limited Evidence for Generalizability of the Main Finding:** The most compelling result—the method’s robustness with tiny amounts of in-domain data—is only demonstrated on the zh→en task. As noted above, this task has a unique and somewhat artificial data condition. Without seeing this experiment replicated on the other three language pairs (which reportedly do not have in-domain data in their training sets), it is difficult to be confident that this key strength generalizes. The paper's central claim of effectiveness with "tiny" data would be much stronger if this experiment were reported for all tasks.
3.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Description and Experimental Details:** The paper lacks several key details needed for reproducibility and full understanding.
    *   In Section 3.2, the description of how one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and segment embeddings (`ul(x)`) are combined in Equation (2) is unclear. An architectural diagram would greatly clarify this.
    *   The "designated loss function" used to train the CNN classifier is never specified.
    *   Section 5.2 omits how the final SMT system weights were tuned (e.g., MERT on the development set), a standard but necessary detail.
4.  **No Qualitative Analysis or Discussion of Domain-Specific Phenomena:** The test sets are from social media (SMS, tweets, Facebook), which are known for high OOV rates, slang, usernames, and hashtags. Data selection cannot create translations for OOV words. The paper would be much stronger with a qualitative analysis showing *what kind of improvements* the selected data provides. For example, does it improve lexical choice for in-vocabulary words, or select for more appropriate sentence structures? Providing a few concrete translation examples would make the benefits much clearer. The paper also fails to mention whether any domain-specific preprocessing was performed.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the zh→en task: Given that training on the 120K in-domain subset already achieves 25.7 BLEU, can you re-frame the contribution of your method in light of this extremely strong baseline? How does your system compare to one that interpolates a model trained on all data with a model trained only on this 120K subset?
2.  Your most significant finding is the robustness of `sscnn` when using very little in-domain seed data (Figure 1). To strengthen this claim, could you provide results for this low-data experiment (e.g., using 100 seed sentences) on the ar→en, en→es, and en→zh tasks as well?
3.  Could you please clarify the model architecture in Section 3.2? Specifically, how are the one-hot vectors and the pre-trained segment vectors combined as inputs? Also, what loss function was used to train the CNN classifier?
4.  Did you apply any special preprocessing or filtering to handle social media-specific phenomena like hashtags, user mentions, or emoticons in your training or test data?
