**Summary of the paper and its main contributions:**

This paper proposes using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for in-domain data selection to adapt statistical machine translation (SMT) systems. The method trains a classifier on a small set of in-domain data, augmented with pre-trained word embeddings from a large general-domain corpus, to select the most relevant training sentences. The primary contribution is demonstrating that this approach is highly effective for domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain seed data (e.g., 100 sentences) is available, significantly outperforming traditional language model (LM) based methods in this challenging low-resource scenario. A secondary contribution is that the method achieves modest but consistent improvements (around +0.5 BLEU) over strong LM-based baselines in standard settings across several language pairs.

**Strengths:**

The paper's most compelling result is the robustness of the proposed CNN method in extremely low-resource settings. The experiment on the zh→en task shows that with only 100 in-domain sentences, the method still achieves a +2.1 BLEU improvement, while the performance of LM-based approaches degrades more sharply. This addresses a practical and important scenario where in-domain data is scarce. Secondly, the semi-supervised CNN approach consistently outperforms strong baselines (ngram, rnnlm) across four language pairs and three challenging social media domains, demonstrating its general effectiveness. The gains of +0.7 to +1.4 BLEU over the `alldata` baseline with much less data are significant.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness is the misleading presentation of the results for the zh→en task. The headline claim of a +3.1 BLEU improvement is inflated, as the authors note that training on a known in-domain subset of the training data already yields a +2.8 BLEU gain. This substantially overstates the contribution of the selection method itself. A comparison against an interpolated in-domain/general-domain model would be more appropriate. Secondly, the paper's key claim of effectiveness with tiny data is only validated on this single, potentially easier, zh→en task; this experiment should be replicated across all tasks to be convincing. Finally, the paper lacks crucial details for clarity and reproducibility. Section 3.2 fails to adequately describe the network architecture, specifically how different vector representations are combined and what "designated loss function" was used. Details on SMT system weight tuning are also missing.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the contribution on the zh→en task by comparing your system to a baseline that interpolates a general-domain model with one trained on the known 120K in-domain data subset?
2.  To strengthen the claim about tiny data adaptation, can you provide results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentences?
3.  Given the social media domains, was any special preprocessing applied for features like usernames or hashtags? Could you provide a few translation examples to illustrate the improvements?
