This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the challenge of limited in-domain resources. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, and then use this classifier to score and select sentences from a large general-domain corpus to augment the training data. Experiments across four language pairs and three test domains demonstrate performance improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model (LM)-based data selection methods. The key contribution lies in the effective utilization of CNNs – combined with pre-trained word embeddings – for data selection even with extremely small in-domain datasets (as few as 100 sentences). A secondary contribution is the demonstration of significant gains achievable with this method even when compared to systems trained on all available general-domain data. Finally, the paper highlights the potential of this approach for fine-grained topic-specific MT adaptation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents convincing experimental results across multiple language pairs and domains. The consistent BLEU score improvements over strong baselines, particularly when in-domain data is scarce, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU are substantial and showcase the method’s ability to leverage general-domain data effectively.
2. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** The work tackles a significant and practical challenge in SMT: domain adaptation with limited in-domain data. This is especially relevant in scenarios such as social media translation and emerging domains where collecting large parallel corpora is expensive or infeasible. The ability to achieve meaningful gains with as little as 100 in-domain sentences is a valuable contribution.
3. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The combination of CNNs with pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) is well-motivated and effectively addresses the data sparsity issue inherent in limited in-domain data scenarios. Leveraging information learned from large unlabeled corpora through word embeddings clearly enhances the performance of the classifier and, consequently, the SMT system.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The explanation of the CNN architecture, one-hot encoding, and semi-supervised learning approach is accessible to readers familiar with neural networks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper compares to LM-based data selection methods, a crucial comparison is missing: methods that perform *bilingual* data selection. For instance, methods based on the sum or difference of cross-entropies (Axelrod et al., 2011) should be included as baselines, as they represent a strong alternative approach.  The paper notes similarity to this work in the discussion, but lacks the direct experimental comparison.
2. **Motivation for CNN Choice is Weak:** The justification for using CNNs over other neural network architectures, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs, is not fully convincing. The argument that CNNs capture important local information is valid, but doesn’t fully explain why they outperform RNNs which are also capable of capturing contextual information.  A more thorough justification or comparative analysis would strengthen the paper.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the different CNN configurations (BOW vs. SEQ, number of layers, etc.).  It’s unclear which aspects of the CNN architecture are most crucial to its performance.  An ablation study systematically evaluating these factors would provide valuable insights.
4. **Lack of Detail in Experimental Setup:** The details of the validation procedure used to select the number of sentences and CNN hyperparameters are limited. The sensitivity of the results to these choices is not explored. A more rigorous description of the experimental setup would enhance the reproducibility and trustworthiness of the results. The fact that Table 2 does *not* compare methods on the same number of selected sentences – despite this being a key parameter – is problematic.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, such as those based on cross-entropy differences (Axelrod et al., 2011), as baselines?
2. What are the specific criteria used for selecting the CNN hyperparameters, and how sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters?  Could you share more details about the validation procedure?
3. Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing CNNs over other neural network architectures like RNNs or LSTMs for this task?
4.  Could you provide example sentences selected by different methods (your CNN approach vs. LM-based methods) to illustrate the differences in selection behavior and support the claims in Section 5.4?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a diagram illustrating the CNN architecture used.
* Inconsistent citation style (use \citet and \citep consistently).
* Consider comparing to Axelrod et al. (2015) which uses POS replacement to address LM sparsity, to directly test the advantage of using word2vec.
* The paper notes that summing source and target classification scores is optimal. The authors should cite Lewis and Moore’s work on summing/differencing cross-entropies, as there's a conceptual similarity.
* Explore extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting, allowing the model to learn weights for combining source and target scores. This could potentially improve performance further.
