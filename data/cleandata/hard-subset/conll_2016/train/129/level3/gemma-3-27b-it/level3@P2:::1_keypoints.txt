- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to classify in-domain vs out-of-domain sentences and select relevant data for SMT, showing gains across four language pairs and three domains, even with only 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Main contribution: demonstrating CNNs are effective for low-resource domain adaptation data selection; secondary: strong performance with very small in-domain training sets.  

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results with consistent BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) over strong baselines.  
- Addresses an important problem of domain adaptation when in-domain data is scarce.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness with minimal in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences).  
- Clear presentation and motivation for using CNNs and pre-trained embeddings.

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparisons to other bilingual data selection methods (e.g., cross-entropy difference approaches like Axelrod et al.).  
- No ablation studies to isolate the contributions of architecture choices (one-hot vs embeddings, BOW vs sequential).  
- Insufficient detail on validation/hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity; Table 2 lacks comparison at equal selection sizes.  
- Missing architecture diagram and qualitative examples of selected/misclassified sentences.

Questions for authors:
- What validation procedure was used to choose number of selected sentences and tune CNN hyperparameters, and how sensitive are results?  
- Have you compared directly to cross-entropy difference bilingual selection methods?  
- Can you provide example sentences selected by sscnn, ohcnn, and LM baselines to illustrate differences?  
- How does performance scale as in-domain data increases beyond the development sizes?

Additional comments:
- Ensure consistent citation style throughout the paper.  
- Consider comparing to Axelrod et al. (2015) and discussing POS replacement / LM sparsity in relation to word2vec benefits.  
- Exploring bilingual CNNs or learned weighting for combining source/target scores is a promising future direction.
