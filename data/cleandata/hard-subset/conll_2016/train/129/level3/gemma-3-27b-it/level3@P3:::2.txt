This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), aimed at scenarios with limited in-domain resources. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, then use this classifier to select high-scoring sentences from the general domain for training an adapted SMT system. The authors claim significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baseline systems and demonstrate effectiveness even with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

The most significant contribution of this work, in my view, is the demonstration of effective data selection with extremely limited in-domain data. While the core CNN architecture isn’t novel, the application to this low-resource adaptation scenario, and the positive results obtained, are valuable. A second, albeit less substantial, contribution is the highlighting of potential flaws in evaluating data selection methods – specifically, the misleading nature of reporting large gains when in-domain data is *already* present in the training set, and the importance of comparing against interpolated in-/out-domain models. Finally, the paper raises important considerations regarding data preprocessing for noisy social media data, which is a relevant and increasingly important application of MT.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Results in Low-Resource Setting:** The most compelling result is the consistent performance improvement even when training the data selection model with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs. This is a genuinely valuable finding, as it addresses a realistic and challenging problem in MT adaptation. The stability demonstrated by repeating the 100-sentence experiment three times further strengthens this argument.
2. **Critical Analysis of Evaluation Methodology:** The authors rightly point out the issues with the commonly reported BLEU gains in data selection research. Their observation that much of the reported improvement stems from simply including in-domain data in the training set, rather than effective selection, is a crucial critique.  Recommending comparison to interpolated models is sound advice.
3. **Relevance to Real-World Applications:** Focusing on social media data (tweets, Facebook posts) highlights the practical relevance of this work. These domains are characterized by limited parallel data and unique linguistic challenges, making effective adaptation methods essential. Raising the issue of OOV rates and special preprocessing is pertinent.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Approach:** The paper generally presents the method in a clear and understandable manner. The motivation, the CNN architecture (though needing clarification - see Weaknesses), and the experimental setup are all relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Misleading BLEU Gains & Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The claim of up to 3.1 BLEU improvement is overstated without a more nuanced discussion. The paper acknowledges that this gain is largely tied to the presence of in-domain data in the training set; however, this caveat isn’t sufficiently prominent. A direct comparison against a system trained with an interpolated mixture of in-domain and general-domain data is missing and crucial.
2. **Limited Scope of the Positive Result:** The compelling result regarding performance with minimal in-domain data (100 sentences) was only demonstrated on the zh→en translation direction, where in-domain data *was* present in the initial training data.  The paper doesn't establish whether this robustness extends to tasks where there is absolutely *no* initial in-domain representation. This limits the generalizability of the finding.
3. **Architectural Clarity:** Section 3.2 lacks sufficient detail about how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined within the CNN architecture. A diagram illustrating the network structure would significantly improve understanding. The description of the “designated loss function” is also vague.
4. **Missing Implementation Details:** The paper does not specify how system weights were tuned in Section 5.2.  Providing this information is essential for reproducibility and a complete evaluation of the experimental setup.
5. **OOV Discussion Needs Elaboration:** While the paper notes that data selection cannot address out-of-vocabulary words, it lacks concrete examples of *what* improvements data selection *does* produce. Showing examples of phrase translations that are improved through data selection would strengthen the argument.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed diagram illustrating the architecture described in Section 3.2, specifically how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined?
2.  Please clarify what “designated loss function” was used during CNN training and why it was chosen.
3.  How were the system weights tuned during the SMT system training (Section 5.2)? Please provide details of the tuning process and the tools used.
4.  Can you provide specific examples of phrase translations that are improved through data selection, demonstrating *what* changes the data selection process makes?
5.  Have you investigated the performance of the proposed method on language pairs *without* any initial in-domain data representation (i.e., extending the 100-sentence experiment to a truly zero-in-domain-initialization scenario)? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the vocabulary size and OOV rates for each language pair and test domain.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of the CNN training and data selection process.
