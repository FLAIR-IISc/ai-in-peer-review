- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier to score and select in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus to improve SMT when in-domain data is limited.  
- Main contribution: shows CNNs can effectively select data for SMT adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), outperforming LM-based methods.  
- Secondary contribution: empirically validated across four language pairs and three test domains, demonstrating robustness.  
- Provides clear description of the CNN architecture, semi-supervised training, and use of pre-trained embeddings, aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: Strong empirical and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, especially in very low-resource settings.  
- Strength: Novel focus on adaptation with tiny in-domain datasets, addressing an important practical problem.  
- Strength: Clear and detailed methodology, including both one-hot and semi-supervised variants.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation and valuable ablation on in-domain training data size.  
- Strength: High practical relevance for real-world MT adaptation where domain data is scarce.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparison to bilingual data selection methods (e.g., sum/difference of cross-entropies from Lewis & Moore 2010).  
- Weakness: Insufficient upfront motivation for choosing CNNs instead of RNNs/LSTMs or discussion of computational/architectural tradeoffs.  
- Weakness: No targeted ablation showing which input representation (BOW vs SEQ) is most effective and why.  
- Weakness: Missing details on validation procedure and hyperparameter selection, and no sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Table 2 compares methods with differing numbers of selected sentences, preventing direct comparison; should compare at equal selection sizes.  
- Question: Please provide a comparison to bilingual data selection methods (sum/difference of cross-entropies).  
- Question: What was the primary motivation for choosing a CNN over RNN/LSTM architectures?  
- Question: Describe the validation procedure for choosing number of selected sentences and CNN hyperparameters; how sensitive are results to these choices?  
- Question: Provide example sentences selected by each method to illustrate selection differences.  
- Question: Was Axelrod (2015) with POS replacement for LM sparsity considered; if so, why excluded?  
- Additional: Standardize inconsistent citation style throughout the paper.  
- Additional: Consider extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn combination weights instead of summing source and target scores.  
- Additional: Explicitly acknowledge and cite the similarity between summing source/target classification scores and Lewis-Moore’s sum/difference of cross-entropies.
