## Review for Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to data selection for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically targeting scenarios with limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain examples. The selected data is then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. While data selection is a well-established technique in SMT, the ability to achieve meaningful improvements with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences is a notable result. A secondary contribution is the successful application of semi-supervised learning, utilizing pre-trained word embeddings to enhance the performance of the CNN classifier. The experimental setup, encompassing four language pairs and multiple test domains (including social media data), adds to the value of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Compelling Results with Limited Data:** The most significant strength of this work is the demonstrated effectiveness of the proposed approach when faced with extremely limited in-domain resources. The experiments, particularly those showing improvements with only 100 labeled sentences, suggest a practical solution for adapting SMT systems to new domains where collecting large amounts of parallel data is prohibitive. This is particularly relevant for the increasing prevalence of low-resource domains.

2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The paper provides a thorough comparison against several strong baseline data selection methods, including n-gram and recurrent neural network language model-based approaches. The consistent performance gains achieved by the proposed CNN-based method, particularly in the low-data regime, convincingly demonstrate its superiority.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted across four language pairs and three test domains, including challenging social media data. This breadth of evaluation enhances the generalizability of the findings and provides confidence in the robustness of the proposed method.

4. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data and provides a strong motivation for exploring alternative data selection techniques beyond traditional language model approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Misleading Claim of Large BLEU Improvements:** The claim of up to 3.1 BLEU improvement should be viewed with caution. The authors acknowledge that this substantial gain is largely attributable to cases where in-domain data happens to exist *within* the general-domain training data. Importantly, the baseline of training solely on this in-domain subset already yields a +2.8 BLEU improvement. A more informative comparison would include results against systems trained with interpolated in-domain and out-of-domain data, reflecting a more realistic adaptation scenario.

2. **Limited Generalizability of Key Result:** The compelling result demonstrating the effectiveness of the method with very little in-domain data (second experiment) was primarily observed for the Chinese-to-English task, where in-domain data *is* present in the original training set. It remains unclear whether this performance holds true for tasks where the general-domain data is entirely devoid of in-domain examples, and therefore whether the embeddings can provide enough relevant signal for successful adaptation.

3. **Insufficient Ablation Study on Social Media Data:** The paper highlights the use of social media data, which presents unique challenges for SMT (high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, emoticons). However, it lacks a discussion of any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied to address these challenges. Were any normalization techniques applied to handle usernames or hashtags? How were emoticons treated? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the true effectiveness of the method on this data.

4. **Lack of Illustrative Examples:** The paper would benefit from providing concrete examples of the kinds of improvements achieved through data selection. What types of sentences were selected by the CNN classifier that were not captured by the baseline methods? Illustrative examples could help to solidify the understanding of the method's strengths and limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs for training the CNN classifier, to better support the claim of effectiveness in low-resource settings?
2.  What specific preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the social media data (tweets and Facebook posts) to address challenges such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?
3.  Can you provide illustrative examples of sentences selected by the CNN classifier that led to significant improvements in translation quality, compared to the baseline methods?
4.  In Section 3.2, could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined during training? An architectural diagram would be very helpful. Also, please clarify what “designated loss function” was used.
5.  Could you clarify the specifics of system weight tuning performed in Section 5.2?

**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive and well-organized. While the paper provides a good overview of related work, a more detailed discussion of recent advances in domain adaptation using neural machine translation (NMT) would be beneficial. The core ideas could potentially be extended to NMT settings.
