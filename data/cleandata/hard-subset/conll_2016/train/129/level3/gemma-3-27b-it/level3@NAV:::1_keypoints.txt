- Core idea: Train a semi-supervised CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain vs out-of-domain sentences and use it to score/select general-domain data for SMT adaptation with very little in-domain data.
- Primary contribution: Shows CNNs are effective for low-resource domain adaptation, outperforming strong baselines and working with as few as ~100 in-domain sentences.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains across four language pairs and multiple domains, up to +3.1 BLEU over baselines.
- Strength: Novel focus on extremely limited in-domain data, addressing a challenging and underexplored setting.
- Strength: Clear, well-explained CNN methodology (one-hot inputs and pre-trained embeddings).
- Strength: Comprehensive comparison against several competitive baseline methods.
- Weakness: Missing comparison to bilingual cross-entropy difference data selection methods (e.g., Axelrod 2015, Moore & Lewis 2010).
- Weakness: Insufficient initial justification for preferring CNNs over RNN/LSTM architectures; later arguments need more detail.
- Weakness: No ablation study to assess the relative importance of bag-of-words vs sequential representations in the CNN.
- Weakness: Unclear validation procedure and hyperparameter sensitivity (selection size, CNN hyperparameters); a layer diagram is missing.
- Weakness: Lack of qualitative insight—no example sentences showing differences between CNN-selected and LM-selected data.
- Question: Please provide a more detailed rationale for choosing CNNs over RNN/LSTM models.
- Question: How sensitive are results to the number of selected in-domain sentences; was a wider range than Table 2 explored?
- Question: Can you provide visualizations or example sentences selected by the CNN vs LM-based methods to illustrate why the CNN performs better?
