## Review of “Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data”

This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT), particularly when limited in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, then use this classifier to score and select general-domain data for retraining the SMT system. The authors report significant BLEU improvements, up to +3.1, compared to baseline systems, and demonstrate effectiveness even with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

The most compelling contribution of this work lies in the demonstration of effective data selection with extremely limited in-domain resources. While the overall concept of data selection isn’t novel, showing strong performance with just 100 sentence pairs is valuable, especially for low-resource scenarios and rapid adaptation to new topics. The experimental setup, encompassing four language pairs and multiple test domains, is also a strength.

However, the claimed +3.1 BLEU improvement is misleading. The authors acknowledge that this large gain is heavily reliant on the presence of in-domain data *within* the general-domain training set, and that training solely on this in-domain data already yields +2.8 BLEU. A fairer comparison would involve interpolating in- and out-of-domain data in a baseline, rather than presenting results where the method essentially rediscovers existing in-domain data.  The claim of significant improvement needs to be contextualized.

A further concern is the limited scope of the compelling “small data” experiment (100 sentence pairs). This was only evaluated for the zh→en language pair, where in-domain data *did* exist in the initial training set. It’s unclear whether this performance would generalize to scenarios lacking any initial in-domain representation, a situation arguably more representative of truly low-resource adaptation. Reporting results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs would strongly support the claims regarding small-data effectiveness.

The paper also lacks clarity regarding preprocessing and handling of social media-specific challenges, such as high Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Were any specific filtering or normalization steps applied?  Furthermore, the paper doesn't provide concrete examples of *what* kinds of improvements data selection leads to.  Does it preferentially select sentences with specific terminology, sentence structure, or topical focus?  

Finally, Section 3.2 needs further elaboration. The combination of word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors is unclear. A diagram illustrating the network architecture would greatly aid understanding. Additionally, details regarding the "designated loss function" are missing. The completeness of the system description would also be improved by specifying how system weights were tuned in Section 5.2.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please clarify the preprocessing steps applied to handle the challenges posed by social media data (OOV words, usernames, hashtags, emoticons).
2.  Can you provide examples of sentences selected by your method that demonstrate the type of in-domain relevance captured by the CNN classifier?
3.  Please provide an architectural diagram illustrating how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the CNN.
4.  What was the specific “designated loss function” used during training (Section 3.2)?
5.  Can you provide details on how system weights were tuned for each experiment (Section 5.2)?
6. Please provide the results of using 100 in-domain sentence pairs for all the tested language pairs?
