## Review of “Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data”

This paper proposes a novel approach to statistical machine translation (SMT) domain adaptation, utilizing semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training data when only a limited amount of such data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences and subsequently use this classifier to score and select relevant sentences from the general-domain corpus. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration of the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection *specifically* in low-resource domain adaptation scenarios. While CNNs have been applied to text classification, this work applies them strategically to the MT data selection problem and showcases their robustness when in-domain data is scarce, outperforming strong baselines under these conditions. A secondary contribution is the empirical finding that even a very small amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) can effectively train a CNN data selection model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results across four language pairs and multiple test domains consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baseline methods, including state-of-the-art language model-based techniques. The gains of up to 3.1 BLEU are substantial.
2. **Novelty in Low-Resource Setting:** The focus on extremely limited in-domain data (a challenging scenario) sets this work apart. Existing methods often require a sizable in-domain corpus.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot vectors and pre-trained embeddings, is well-explained and easy to follow. 
4. **Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper includes a detailed comparison against several competitive baselines, providing a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Missing Baseline:** A critical omission is a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, particularly those based on cross-entropy differences (e.g., Axelrod 2015, Moore & Lewis 2010). This is a well-established approach and a direct competitor.
2. **CNN Architecture Justification:** The initial motivation for using a CNN over recurrent networks (RNNs/LSTMs) was unclear. While the paper later argues that CNNs capture important sentence sections, a more detailed explanation of why this is advantageous for data selection would be helpful.
3. **Ablation Study:** There is no exploration of the relative importance of the bag-of-words (BOW) vs. sequential (SEQ) representations within the CNN. An ablation study determining which feature representation is most critical, or whether their combination is optimal, is necessary.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The validation procedure for selecting the number of in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameters is not clearly articulated. Reporting sensitivity to these choices would strengthen the findings. A layer diagram of the CNN would also enhance clarity.
5. **Limited Insight into Selection:** Providing example sentences selected by different methods (CNN vs. LM-based approaches) would provide more qualitative insights into *why* the CNN performs better, supporting the claims made in Section 5.4.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed rationale for choosing a CNN over RNN/LSTM architectures for this task?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the number of selected in-domain sentences? Did you explore a wider range of values than presented in Table 2?
3.  Could you provide a visualization or example of sentences selected by the CNN-based method compared to those selected by the LM-based methods?
