## Review for Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT), particularly when only a limited amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, then use this model to score and select relevant sentences from a larger general-domain corpus. The authors demonstrate improvements over strong baseline systems across four language directions and three test domains, with particularly notable gains when the amount of in-domain data is extremely small (down to 100 sentences).

The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection in the low-resource domain adaptation setting. While CNNs have seen increasing use in various NLP tasks, this work provides a compelling case for their utility specifically in selecting relevant data for SMT when in-domain resources are scarce. The experimental results convincingly show that the proposed method outperforms competitive baseline approaches, including those based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models. A secondary, valuable contribution is the demonstration that strong performance can be achieved with remarkably small amounts of in-domain training data for the data selection model itself – as little as 100 sentences – opening up possibilities for fine-grained, topic-specific translation adaptation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents solid experimental results across multiple language pairs and test domains. The consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The BLEU score improvements of up to 3.1 are significant.
2. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** The problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is a significant challenge in machine translation. The authors directly address this issue and propose a viable solution that is particularly relevant to scenarios where acquiring large amounts of labeled data is expensive or infeasible, such as adapting to emerging topics or social media content.
3. **Effectiveness with Minimal In-domain Data:** The results showing strong performance even with only 100 in-domain sentences are particularly compelling. This drastically lowers the barrier to entry for adapting SMT systems to new domains, making it a highly practical contribution.
4. **Clear Presentation of Method:** The explanation of the semi-supervised CNN approach, including the use of pre-trained word embeddings, is clear and well-structured. The authors effectively motivate the choice of CNNs for this task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While comparisons to language model-based methods are provided, the paper lacks a direct comparison to other bilingual data selection approaches, such as those based on cross-entropy differences (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2011). Establishing a clear performance gap against these methods would strengthen the contribution.  The connection to Lewis and Moore’s work on sum/difference of cross-entropies (cited but not fully contextualized) is also understated; the authors' approach of summing source and target classification scores bears a resemblance and warrants further discussion.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the contributions of different components of the CNN architecture. It is unclear whether the use of one-hot encoding, pre-trained word embeddings, or the choice of BOW vs. sequential representations are critical to the performance gains. An ablation study would clarify the importance of each component.
3. **Validation and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks a detailed description of the validation procedure used for selecting the number of selected in-domain sentences and for tuning the CNN hyperparameters. Reporting the sensitivity of the results to these choices would enhance the reproducibility and robustness of the findings. Table 2’s presentation would be improved by comparing all methods at the *same* number of selected sentences, facilitating a direct performance comparison.
4. **Missing Visualizations and Qualitative Analysis:** The paper would benefit from including a diagram illustrating the CNN architecture used. Furthermore, providing example sentences selected by different methods (and potentially misclassified sentences) would support the claims made in Section 5.4 regarding the effectiveness of the approach in capturing domain-specific information.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed description of the validation procedure used to determine the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences and to tune the CNN hyperparameters? How sensitive are the results to these choices?
2.  Have you experimented with other bilingual data selection techniques, such as those based on cross-entropy differences, and can you provide a direct comparison to your proposed method?
3.  Could you provide example sentences that were selected by different methods (sscnn, ohcnn, and the LM-based baselines) to illustrate the strengths and weaknesses of each approach?
4.  How does the performance of your method change as the amount of in-domain data *increases* beyond the development set size? Would the benefits of the CNN-based approach persist with larger in-domain datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please ensure consistency in the citation style (e.g., using \citet or \citep consistently throughout the paper).
* Consider comparing your method to Axelrod et al. (2015)’s work, which utilizes POS replacement to address LM sparsity and may offer insights into the benefits of the word2vec embeddings employed in your approach.
* Exploring a bilingual CNN setting, learning weights for combining source and target scores, could be a promising direction for future work.
