- Core idea: Train a CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain vs out-of-domain sentence pairs and use it to select high-scoring general-domain sentences for SMT adaptation.  
- Main contribution: Demonstrates effective data selection and SMT adaptation using extremely limited in-domain data (effective even with 100 sentence pairs).  
- Secondary contribution: Highlights pitfalls in evaluation — reporting large BLEU gains can be misleading if in-domain data is already in training; recommends comparisons to interpolated in-/out-domain models.  
- Application relevance: Focuses on noisy social media domains (tweets, Facebook) where parallel data is scarce and preprocessing/OOV issues matter.  
- Strength — low-resource performance: Consistent BLEU improvements and stability even when the CNN is trained with only 100 in-domain sentences (experiment repeated three times).  
- Strength — evaluation critique: Provides a valuable critical analysis of common evaluation methodology in data selection research.  
- Strength — real-world relevance: Addresses practical issues like OOV rates and special preprocessing for social media MT.  
- Strength — clarity: Method, motivation, CNN architecture overview, and experimental setup are generally clearly presented.  
- Weakness — BLEU claims: Reported up-to-3.1 BLEU gain is overstated without clearer discussion and a direct comparison to interpolated in-/out-domain baselines.  
- Weakness — limited generality: The 100-sentence positive result was shown only for zh→en where in-domain data was present initially, so robustness in true zero in-domain scenarios is unproven.  
- Weakness — architectural detail: Section 3.2 lacks clear explanation/diagram of how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined.  
- Weakness — vague loss description: The “designated loss function” used for CNN training is not specified or motivated.  
- Weakness — missing tuning details: The paper omits how SMT system weights were tuned (Section 5.2), harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness — OOV analysis shallow: Notes that data selection cannot fix OOVs but lacks concrete examples showing what phrase translations improve due to selection.  
- Question: Please provide a detailed diagram showing how embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the CNN.  
- Question: What exact loss function was used for CNN training and why was it chosen?  
- Question: How were SMT system weights tuned (methods/tools/details) in Section 5.2?  
- Question: Can you provide specific examples of phrase translations improved by data selection to illustrate concrete gains?  
- Question: Have you tested the method in truly zero-in-domain-initialization scenarios (no initial in-domain data); if so, what were the results?  
- Additional comment: Add a table summarizing vocabulary sizes and OOV rates for each language pair and test domain.  
- Additional comment: Discuss the computational cost of CNN training and the data selection process.
