This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data to improve statistical machine translation (SMT) performance, particularly when limited in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, then use this classifier to score and select relevant sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The method demonstrates improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that CNNs can effectively perform data selection for SMT adaptation *even* with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), outperforming established language model (LM)-based selection methods in those settings. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this approach across four language pairs and three test domains, offering robust evidence of its effectiveness. Finally, the paper provides a clear description of the CNN architecture and semi-supervised training process, making it accessible to the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods, across diverse language pairs and domains. The gains are particularly notable when the amount of in-domain data is very small (e.g., 100 sentences), highlighting the method’s strength in challenging adaptation scenarios. The statistically significant improvements reported bolster the validity of the findings.
2. **Novelty in Low-Resource Adaptation:** The focus on adaptation with *tiny* amounts of in-domain data is a key strength. Existing data selection approaches often require thousands of in-domain sentences. This work successfully addresses a crucial and increasingly relevant problem, especially for emerging domains and languages.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper clearly describes the CNN architecture (both one-hot and semi-supervised versions), training procedure, and experimental setup. The explanations of the semi-supervised approach and the use of pre-trained word embeddings are particularly well-presented. This transparency facilitates reproducibility and further research.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across four language directions and three test domains provides a strong basis for generalizing the findings.  The ablation study examining the impact of in-domain training data size is also valuable.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The ability to adapt MT systems with minimal in-domain data has significant practical implications for real-world applications, where collecting large amounts of domain-specific data can be expensive and time-consuming.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Missing Comparison to Bilingual Data Selection:** The paper lacks a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, such as those based on the sum or difference of cross-entropies (Lewis & Moore, 2010). These methods are established baselines for data selection and should be included for a more comprehensive evaluation.
2. **Motivation for CNN Choice:** While the paper offers a post-hoc rationale for using CNNs (capturing important sentence sections), the initial motivation for choosing CNNs over recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs is unclear. A more compelling justification for the CNN architecture is needed, potentially discussing computational efficiency or specific properties that make it suitable for this task.
3. **Lack of Ablation on Input Representations:** The paper explores BOW and SEQ representations within the CNN, but does not provide experiments demonstrating which representation (or combination) is most effective and *why*. A targeted ablation study would provide valuable insights into the contributions of each representation.
4. **Validation and Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper lacks a detailed explanation of the validation procedure used to choose the number of selected in-domain sentences and the CNN hyperparameters. The sensitivity of the results to these choices is not reported. This information is crucial for assessing the robustness of the method.
5. **Table 2 Presentation:** Table 2 would be more informative if it compared the methods at the *same* number of selected sentences. Currently, the number of selected sentences varies across methods, making direct comparison difficult. Figure 1 demonstrates the proposed method’s superiority even with varying selection sizes, suggesting a more direct comparison at equivalent selection sizes would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, such as those based on the sum or difference of cross-entropies?
2.  What was the primary motivation for choosing a CNN architecture over alternative architectures like RNNs or LSTMs?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the validation procedure used to determine the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences and the CNN hyperparameters?  How sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters?
4.  Could you provide example sentences selected by the different methods (your method, LM-based methods) to illustrate the differences in selection criteria and support the claims made in Section 5.4?
5.  Did you consider comparing against Axelrod (2015) which utilizes POS replacement for LM sparsity reduction? If so, why was it excluded?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., \citet vs. \citep). Please standardize the style throughout the paper.
*   Consider extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for combining source and target scores, potentially improving performance further. The current approach sums the scores, but learning optimal weights could be beneficial.
*   The similarity between summing source and target classification scores and Lewis-Moore’s sum/difference of cross-entropies should be explicitly acknowledged and cited.
