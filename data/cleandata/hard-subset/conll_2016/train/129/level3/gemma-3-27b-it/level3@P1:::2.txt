This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data to improve statistical machine translation (SMT) performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, using a small amount of in-domain data augmented with word embeddings learned from a larger general-domain corpus. The selected sentences are then used to train an SMT system. The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available, achieving improvements over state-of-the-art language model (LM)-based data selection methods. A secondary contribution lies in showcasing that the in-domain data required to train the CNN selection model can be as small as 100 sentences, enabling fine-grained topic-specific translation adaptation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Data Selection with Limited Resources:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the demonstrated ability of the proposed CNN-based method to effectively select in-domain data even with very limited in-domain resources (as few as 100 sentences). This is a significant advancement, as many real-world translation scenarios involve adapting to new domains where acquiring large amounts of in-domain data is impractical or impossible. The experimental results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, especially when the in-domain training data is scarce.
2. **Robustness of the CNN Approach:** The experiments demonstrate that the CNN-based data selection is more stable and reliable than LM-based methods when the amount of in-domain training data is small. While LM-based methods struggle to leverage very limited data, the CNN approach, through its ability to learn meaningful features and leverage pre-trained word embeddings, maintains a consistent performance gain.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup & Baselines:** The paper presents a well-defined experimental setup with clear baselines, including strong LM-based data selection methods. The use of diverse language pairs and test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts) adds to the robustness and generalizability of the findings.  The inclusion of statistical significance testing further strengthens the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Performance Gains:** The reported +3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English sms task is potentially misleading. This gain appears to be largely attributable to the presence of in-domain data *within* the general domain training set. The paper does not sufficiently emphasize that training solely on this in-domain subset already yields a substantial +2.8 BLEU improvement. A more informative comparison would involve contrasting the proposed method with interpolated in-domain/out-of-domain models, rather than solely against the all-out-of-domain baseline.
2. **Limited Generalizability of the Small-Data Experiment:** While the claim that the method works well with only 100 sentences is exciting, the supporting experiment (Figure 1) was conducted exclusively on the zh→en task, where in-domain data already exists within the training corpus. It's unclear whether these positive results would generalize to tasks where no in-domain data is present in the general training data. Demonstrating the effectiveness in such scenarios is crucial for establishing the broad applicability of the approach.
3. **Lack of Comprehensive Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study. While it compares different CNN configurations (one-hot vs. semi-supervised), it doesn’t explore the impact of different word embedding sizes, CNN architectures, or pooling strategies on performance. Understanding these factors could provide valuable insights into the optimal configuration for data selection.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide results for all four language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs for training the CNN classifier? This would strengthen the claim that the method performs well with extremely limited in-domain data and demonstrate its generalizability.
2.  The social media datasets (tweets, Facebook posts) are known to present challenges for MT systems due to high rates of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. What preprocessing or filtering steps, if any, were applied to these datasets?
3.  Data selection addresses the problem of domain mismatch, but it does not inherently solve the issue of out-of-vocabulary words. Could you provide concrete examples showing *what kinds* of improvements the data selection process produces?  For instance, does it primarily select sentences containing domain-specific terminology, or does it improve translation quality through other mechanisms?
4.  In Section 3.2, you mention combining word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors. Could you clarify this process with a more detailed explanation and, ideally, an architectural illustration? Furthermore, please specify the "designated loss function" used for training the CNN classifier.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Section 5.2 would be more complete if it explicitly stated how the weights for the SMT system were tuned, beyond mentioning standard settings.
*   The related work section is generally well-written. However, consider briefly discussing recent advancements in unsupervised domain adaptation techniques.
