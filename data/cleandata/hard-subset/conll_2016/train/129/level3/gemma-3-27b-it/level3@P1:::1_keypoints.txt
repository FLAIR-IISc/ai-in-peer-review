- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier using pre-trained word embeddings to score/select in-domain sentences from a general-domain corpus for SMT, working with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Demonstrates consistent BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) across four language pairs and three domains, outperforming strong LM-based baselines.  
- Shows the method can beat systems trained on all available general-domain data, highlighting effective selection rather than brute-force use of data.  
- Suggests potential for fine-grained topic-specific MT adaptation.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results across multiple languages/domains with substantial BLEU gains.  
- Tackles an important practical problem: domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain parallel data.  
- Effective semi-supervised design: CNNs combined with word2vec mitigate data sparsity and leverage unlabeled corpora.  
- Clear and accessible presentation of methodology and architecture.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks direct comparison to bilingual data-selection baselines (e.g., cross-entropy difference methods like Axelrod et al., 2011).  
- Weak motivation for choosing CNNs over other neural architectures (RNNs/LSTMs) and no comparative analysis.  
- Insufficient ablation study on CNN configurations (BOW vs SEQ, layers, etc.) to identify key contributors to performance.  
- Experimental setup details and validation procedure (hyperparameter/selection sensitivity) are under-specified; Table 2 comparisons use different numbers of selected sentences.

Questions for authors:
- Can you compare to bilingual selection methods based on cross-entropy differences (Axelrod et al., 2011) as baselines?  
- What criteria and validation procedure were used to choose CNN hyperparameters, and how sensitive are results to those choices?  
- Why were CNNs chosen over RNNs/LSTMs—can you elaborate or provide comparative results?  
- Can you provide example sentences selected by CNN vs. LM-based methods to illustrate selection differences?

Additional comments:
- Add a diagram of the CNN architecture for clarity.  
- Fix inconsistent citation style (\citet vs \citep).  
- Consider comparing to Axelrod et al. (2015) with POS replacement to evaluate the advantage of word2vec.  
- Cite Lewis and Moore regarding summing/differencing cross-entropies due to conceptual similarity with summing source/target scores.  
- Explore extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn optimal combination weights for source and target scores.
