## Review for Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

This paper proposes using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), particularly when only limited in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, and then use this classifier to score and select data for SMT training. The authors report improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU over baseline systems.

The most compelling contribution of this work is the demonstration that CNNs can effectively perform data selection even with very small amounts of in-domain data (down to 100 sentence pairs). This is a significant finding, as it addresses a practical challenge in domain adaptation where obtaining large in-domain datasets is often difficult or impossible. However, the reported 3.1 BLEU improvement is misleading. The gain is largely attributable to the presence of in-domain data *within* the general-domain training set, and training solely on this in-domain data already yields a substantial +2.8 BLEU improvement. The paper doesn't adequately disentangle the benefit of the CNN selection from the inherent advantage of having in-domain data present in the initial pool.

A second, though less substantial, contribution is the exploration of semi-supervised learning with CNNs for this task, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for limited in-domain data. The results suggest that this approach is more robust than purely supervised methods when in-domain data is scarce.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. The experiments were primarily conducted on zh→en, where in-domain data existed in the training set. It’s unclear whether the method generalizes to tasks where no in-domain data is initially present. The claim of effectiveness with small data needs stronger support; reporting results for *all* tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs would be crucial. The paper also lacks discussion of preprocessing steps for social media data, which are known to present challenges like high OOV rates and non-standard language. Concrete examples illustrating the types of improvements achieved through data selection would be helpful. Finally, Section 3.2 requires clarification regarding the combination of word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors – a diagram would significantly improve understanding. The tuning of system weights in Section 5.2 should also be explicitly stated.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the results change when *no* in-domain data is initially present in the general-domain training set?
2.  Please provide results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs for training the CNN classifier.
3.  What specific preprocessing steps (if any) were applied to handle the characteristics of social media data (OOV words, usernames, hashtags, emoticons)?
4.  Can you provide examples of sentence pairs that were selected by your method but would not have been selected by a baseline method, and explain how this selection led to an improvement in translation quality?
5.  Please provide a diagram illustrating how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in Section 3.2.
6.  How were the system weights tuned in Section 5.2?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding its reliance on the presence of some in-domain signal within the general-domain data.
*   Consider including a discussion of computational cost and scalability of the proposed method.
