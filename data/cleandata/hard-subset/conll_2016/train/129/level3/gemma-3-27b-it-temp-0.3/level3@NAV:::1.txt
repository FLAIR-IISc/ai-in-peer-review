## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT), particularly when limited in-domain resources are available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to mitigate the scarcity of in-domain data. The selected sentences are then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration of the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation scenario. While CNNs have been applied to text classification, applying them specifically to the task of *selecting* training data for MT, and showing gains with extremely limited in-domain data (as low as 100 sentences), is a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation across four language pairs and multiple test domains, providing a reasonably broad evaluation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection techniques, particularly in the low-resource setting. The BLEU improvements reported (up to 3.1) are significant.
2. **Low-Resource Focus:** The paper directly addresses a critical challenge in MT – adapting to new domains with minimal in-domain data. This is a practical and important problem.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The description of the CNN architecture and the semi-supervised training process is clear and well-explained. The use of pre-trained word embeddings is a sensible approach given the limited in-domain data.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted across multiple language pairs and test domains, increasing the confidence in the generalizability of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for CNN Choice:** The initial justification for using a CNN over other neural network architectures (RNNs/LSTMs) is weak. While the paper later argues that CNNs capture important sentence sections, a more thorough comparison or explanation of why CNNs are particularly suited to this task would be beneficial.
2. **Ablation Study Missing:** The paper doesn’t explore the relative importance of the BOW and sequence representations used as input to the CNN. An ablation study demonstrating which representation contributes more to performance, or whether a combination is optimal, is needed.
3. **Missing Comparison to Bilingual Data Selection:** The comparison to prior work is incomplete. The paper doesn't compare against methods that directly select *bilingual* data based on cross-entropy differences (e.g., Axelrod 2015), which are relevant baselines.
4. **Validation Procedure Details:** The paper lacks details regarding the validation procedure used to determine the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences and the CNN hyperparameters. Reporting sensitivity to these choices is crucial.
5. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The claim in Section 5.4 that the method selects meaningful in-domain sentences would be strengthened by including example sentences selected by different methods, illustrating the differences in selection criteria.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for choosing a CNN over other neural network architectures like RNNs or LSTMs for this task?
2.  Please elaborate on the validation procedure used to determine the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences and the CNN hyperparameters. How sensitive are the results to these choices?
3.  Could you include example sentences selected by different methods (e.g., your method vs. LM-based methods) to illustrate the differences in selection criteria and support your claims in Section 5.4?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please ensure consistent citation style (either \citet or \citep throughout the paper).
*   Consider adding a layer diagram of the CNN architecture for better clarity.
*   It would be valuable to compare the proposed method to Axelrod (2015) to assess its performance against bilingual data selection techniques.
