**Summary**

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT). The approach uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selects negative samples from the general-domain training data to train a CNN classification model. The learned model is then used to classify each sentence in the general-domain training data based on their domain relevance score, and the top N sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system. The authors claim that their method can improve the performance up to 3.1 BLEU over the baseline system trained with all the general-domain data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for in-domain data selection**: The authors propose a novel approach to in-domain data selection using semi-supervised CNN, which can effectively select a small amount of training data that outperforms baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data.
2. **Robustness with very small in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their method can achieve significant improvements even with very small in-domain data, as few as 100 sentence pairs.
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods**: The authors compare their method with three state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and show that their method achieves better performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of semi-supervised learning**: The authors effectively use semi-supervised learning to leverage large amounts of unlabeled data and improve the performance of their method.
2. **Robustness with small in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate the robustness of their method with very small in-domain data, which is a significant advantage over traditional data selection methods.
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, which helps to establish the effectiveness of their method.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Misleading claims**: The authors claim a 3.1 BLEU improvement, but this improvement is only observed when the in-domain data is included in the training set. When only general-domain data is used, the improvement is much smaller.
2. **Lack of clarity in experimental setup**: The authors do not provide enough details about the experimental setup, such as the tuning of system weights, which makes it difficult to replicate the results.
3. **Unclear handling of social-media-specific issues**: The authors do not provide enough details about how they handle social-media-specific issues, such as high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the experimental setup, including the tuning of system weights?
2. How do you handle social-media-specific issues, such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?
3. Can you provide more details about the architecture of the semi-supervised CNN model, including how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined?
4. Can you provide more concrete examples of the improvements achieved by your method, including the types of sentences that are selected and how they contribute to the improvement in BLEU score?
