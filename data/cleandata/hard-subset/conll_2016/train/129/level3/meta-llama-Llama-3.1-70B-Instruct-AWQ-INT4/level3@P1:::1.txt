This paper proposes a novel method for machine translation (MT) training data selection using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier. The method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on four different language directions with three test domains, achieving significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a novel method for MT training data selection using a semi-supervised CNN classifier, which is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on four different language directions with three test domains, achieving significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.
3. The authors show that their method can select a small amount of training data that is used to create a system which outperforms baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data.

**Strengths:**

1. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method, including the use of semi-supervised CNNs and the selection of in-domain data.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on four different language directions with three test domains, achieving significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
4. The authors provide a comparison to prior continuous and n-gram LM approaches, demonstrating the advantages of their method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The authors do not provide a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, which may be a more effective approach for MT training data selection.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the motivation for using a CNN over RNN/LSTM, which may be a more effective approach for text classification tasks.
3. The authors do not provide experiments demonstrating whether BOW, SEQ, or their combination is most important or why, which may be an important consideration for future work.
4. The authors do not provide a layer diagram of the CNN architecture, which may be helpful for understanding the details of the method.
5. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the validation procedure for choosing the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameter selection, which may be an important consideration for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, which may be a more effective approach for MT training data selection?
2. Can you provide a clear explanation of the motivation for using a CNN over RNN/LSTM, which may be a more effective approach for text classification tasks?
3. Can you provide experiments demonstrating whether BOW, SEQ, or their combination is most important or why, which may be an important consideration for future work?
4. Can you provide a layer diagram of the CNN architecture, which may be helpful for understanding the details of the method?
5. Can you provide a clear explanation of the validation procedure for choosing the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameter selection, which may be an important consideration for future work?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The authors may want to consider including example sentences selected by different methods to support their claims in Section 5.4.
2. The authors may want to consider comparing their method to Axelrod (2015) that uses POS replacement to reduce LM sparsity to test word2vec advantage.
3. The authors may want to consider extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for combining source and target scores.
