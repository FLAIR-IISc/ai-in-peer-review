**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the machine translation system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for data selection**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for selecting in-domain training data, which is effective even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. **Comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The authors compare their approach to state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and show that their approach outperforms these methods.
3. **Stability of the approach**: The authors demonstrate the stability of their approach by repeating the experiment with different in-domain sets and showing that the results are consistent.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective data selection**: The authors' approach is able to select a small amount of training data that is used to create a system which outperforms baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data.
2. **Robustness to small in-domain data**: The authors' approach is able to achieve good performance even when the in-domain data is very limited (e.g., 100 sentence pairs).
3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The authors provide a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, which demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of explanation for CNN architecture**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation for why they chose a CNN architecture over other architectures (e.g., RNN/LSTM).
2. **Unclear validation procedure**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation for how they validated their approach and chose the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameters.
3. **Limited analysis of results**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the results, including why their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods in some cases but not others.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation for why you chose a CNN architecture over other architectures (e.g., RNN/LSTM)?
2. Can you provide more details on the validation procedure used to choose the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameters?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the results, including why your approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods in some cases but not others?
