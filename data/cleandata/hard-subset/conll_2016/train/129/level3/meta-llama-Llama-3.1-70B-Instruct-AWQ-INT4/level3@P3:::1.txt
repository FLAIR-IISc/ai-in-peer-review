The paper "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data" proposes a novel approach to machine translation (MT) training data selection using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors aim to address the challenge of domain adaptation with very limited amounts of in-domain data, which is a common problem in MT.

The paper's main contribution is the use of a semi-supervised CNN to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain sentences. The CNN model is trained on a small amount of in-domain data and a large amount of general-domain data, and is used to select the most relevant sentence pairs for MT system training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on four language directions with three test domains, achieving significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to MT training data selection, its ability to adapt to new domains with very limited amounts of in-domain data, and its significant improvements over state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their results, including a discussion of the reasons why their approach performs well.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. One major weakness is the lack of comparison to bilingual data selection methods, which are commonly used in MT. The authors only compare their approach to language model-based data selection methods, which may not be the most effective approach for all MT tasks. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the CNN model's performance, including its ability to capture important sentence sections and its sensitivity to hyperparameter selection.

Another weakness is the lack of clarity in the paper's writing style. The authors use inconsistent citation styles, which can make it difficult to follow their arguments. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the CNN model's architecture and training procedure.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MT, and its approach has the potential to be widely adopted. The authors' use of semi-supervised CNNs for MT training data selection is a novel and effective approach that can adapt to new domains with very limited amounts of in-domain data.
