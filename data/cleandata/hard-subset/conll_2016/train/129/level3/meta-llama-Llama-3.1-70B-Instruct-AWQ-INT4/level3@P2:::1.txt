This paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to form the training sample for training the CNN classification model. The learned models are then used to classify each sentence in the general-domain training data based on their domain relevance score, and the top N sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The proposal of a semi-supervised CNN approach for selecting in-domain training data for SMT, which is effective even when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available.
2. The demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed approach through experiments on four different language directions with three test domains, showing significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.
3. The investigation of the effect of the size of the in-domain sample on the performance of the proposed approach, showing that even with as few as 100 in-domain sentence pairs, the approach can still achieve good performance.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The proposed approach is effective in selecting in-domain training data for SMT, even when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available.
2. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, covering four different language directions with three test domains.
3. The results are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach over strong baseline systems.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The motivation for using a CNN over RNN/LSTM is unclear, and more explanation is needed to justify this choice.
2. The paper lacks a comparison to bilingual data selection methods, which would be a useful addition to the experimental evaluation.
3. The validation procedure for choosing the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameter selection is unclear, and more information is needed to understand how these choices were made.
4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, including example sentences selected by different methods to support the claims made in Section 5.4.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more explanation for the choice of CNN over RNN/LSTM for this task?
2. How did you select the number of selected in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameters, and what was the sensitivity of the results to these choices?
3. Can you provide example sentences selected by different methods to support the claims made in Section 5.4?
4. Have you considered extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for combining source and target scores?

Additional comments:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, including a comparison of the proposed approach to other methods when more in-domain data is available.
* The paper could also benefit from a more detailed explanation of the CNN architecture and the choice of hyperparameters.
* The writing style is clear, but there are some minor issues with citation style and formatting.
* The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, but some of the sections could be more clearly labeled and separated.
