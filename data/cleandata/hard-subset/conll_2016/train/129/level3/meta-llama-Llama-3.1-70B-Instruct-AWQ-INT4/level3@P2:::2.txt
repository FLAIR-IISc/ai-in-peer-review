This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT). The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for in-domain data selection, which outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
2. The ability to select highly suitable in-domain sentences even when the in-domain data is very limited (as few as 100 sentence pairs).
3. Experimental results on four different language directions with three test domains, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The proposed semi-supervised CNN approach shows significant improvements over LM-based data selection methods, especially when the in-domain data is limited.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering four language directions and three test domains, and demonstrating the robustness of the proposed method.
3. The paper addresses a challenging problem in SMT, namely domain adaptation with limited in-domain data, and provides a novel solution.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

1. The claims of +3.1 BLEU improvement are misleading, as large gains occur only when in-domain data is in the training set. A more accurate comparison would be to interpolated in-/out-domain models.
2. The second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN with very little in-domain data, was only run on the zh→en task, where in-domain data exists in the training set. It is unclear whether this result holds for tasks without in-domain training data.
3. The paper does not provide enough information about the preprocessing or filtering of social media data, which may contain specific issues such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons.
4. The paper does not provide concrete examples of the improvements produced by data selection, which would be helpful in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details about the preprocessing or filtering of social media data, and whether any special techniques were used to address specific issues such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?
2. Can you provide concrete examples of the improvements produced by data selection, such as specific sentence pairs or phrases that were correctly translated due to the selected in-domain data?
3. Can you clarify how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the semi-supervised CNN architecture, and what the "designated loss function" was?
4. Can you provide results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs to better support the small-data claim?

Additional comments:

* Section 3.2 needs clarification on the combination of word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors, and an architecture illustration would be helpful.
* Section 5.2 should state how system weights were tuned for completeness.
* It would be helpful to provide more information about the computational resources used for the experiments, such as the number of GPUs and the training time.
