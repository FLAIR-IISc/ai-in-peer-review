**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT). The method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train the CNN model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train the SMT system. The authors evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains and achieve significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of general-domain data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel application of semi-supervised CNN for in-domain data selection**: The authors propose a new approach for in-domain data selection using semi-supervised CNN, which is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. **Significant improvements over strong baseline systems**: The authors achieve significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of general-domain data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Robustness with very small in-domain data**: The authors show that their approach can still achieve good performance even when the in-domain data is very limited, making it suitable for applications where in-domain data is scarce.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors use word embeddings learned from large amounts of general-domain data to improve the performance of their CNN model.
2. **Robustness to limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can still achieve good performance even when the in-domain data is very limited.
3. **Significant improvements over strong baseline systems**: The authors achieve significant improvements over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of general-domain data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Misleading claims of +3.1 BLEU improvement**: The authors claim a +3.1 BLEU improvement, but this is only achieved when the in-domain data is included in the training set. When the in-domain data is not included, the improvement is much smaller.
2. **Lack of comparison to interpolated in-/out-domain models**: The authors do not compare their approach to interpolated in-/out-domain models, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their approach.
3. **Unclear architecture and loss function**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation of their CNN architecture and loss function, making it difficult to reproduce their results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of your CNN architecture and loss function?
2. How do you handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words in your approach?
3. Can you provide more information on how you tuned the hyperparameters of your CNN model?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of social-media-specific issues, such as high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?
5. Can you provide more examples of the kinds of improvements your approach produces in terms of translation quality?
