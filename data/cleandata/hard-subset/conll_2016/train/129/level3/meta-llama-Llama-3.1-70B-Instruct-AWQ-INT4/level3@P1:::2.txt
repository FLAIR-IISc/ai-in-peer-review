This paper proposes a method for using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) systems. The method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to form the training sample for training the CNN classification model.

**Summary of the paper**

The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for domain adaptation in SMT systems. The approach uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to select in-domain sentence pairs from the general-domain data, which are used to train the SMT system. The authors evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains and show that it outperforms strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

**Contributions of the work**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN-based approach for domain adaptation**: The authors propose a novel approach for domain adaptation in SMT systems using semi-supervised CNNs. This approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. **Robustness with very small in-domain data**: The authors show that their approach is robust even when the amount of in-domain data is very small (e.g., 100 sentence pairs).
3. **Improved performance over LM-based selection methods**: The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms LM-based selection methods, achieving a gain of up to 3.1 BLEU points.

**Strengths of the submission**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach for domain adaptation in SMT systems using semi-supervised CNNs.
2. **Robustness with small in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach is robust even with very small amounts of in-domain data.
3. **Improved performance**: The authors show that their approach outperforms strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

**Weaknesses of the submission**

1. **Misleading claims**: The authors' claims of +3.1 BLEU points are misleading, as large gains occur only when in-domain data is in the training set.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The second experiment, which is the most compelling result, was run only on zh→en where in-domain data exists in the training set.
3. **Lack of clarity in Section 3.2**: The authors need to clarify how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined and what the "designated loss function" was.
4. **Lack of completeness in Section 5.2**: The authors need to state how system weights were tuned for completeness.

**Questions to authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you combined word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors in Section 3.2?
2. Can you provide more details on the "designated loss function" used in Section 3.2?
3. How did you tune the system weights in Section 5.2?
4. Can you provide concrete examples showing what kinds of improvements data selection produces?
5. Did you apply any special preprocessing or filtering to address social-media-specific issues (high OOV rate, usernames, hashtags, emoticons)?
