

Summary: This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection in statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The primary contribution is demonstrating that CNN-based data selection can achieve significant performance improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) with minimal in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), which is particularly valuable for adapting to new domains where domain-specific data is scarce. The secondary contribution is showing that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods by approximately 0.5 BLEU points on average.

Strengths:
1. The most compelling contribution is the demonstration that the method works effectively with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), which addresses a critical practical challenge in domain adaptation where collecting large amounts of domain-specific data is prohibitively expensive. The stability of results across multiple experiments (with standard deviation of 0.12 for the 100-sentence experiment) is particularly convincing.

2. The paper provides thorough evaluation across four language directions with diverse test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating the method's generalizability. The results show consistent improvements over baseline systems trained with all general-domain data, with the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieving the best performance across all tasks.

3. The semi-supervised approach effectively leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through word embeddings, which is a significant advantage over traditional language model-based methods that require larger in-domain datasets. The explanation for why CNNs work well (capturing domain-specific features while ignoring general words) is well-justified.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim of "up to 3.1 BLEU" improvement is misleading. The largest gains occur only when the in-domain data is already present in the training data (as evidenced by the 25.7 BLEU score on the in-domain subset), and training on the in-domain subset alone already yields 2.8 BLEU improvement over the baseline. The paper should compare against interpolated in-/out-domain models to properly contextualize the improvement.

2. The most compelling experiment (using only 100 in-domain sentences) was only conducted on the zh2en task where in-domain data exists in the training set. It's unclear whether this approach would work equally well for the other three tasks (ar2en, en2es, en2zh) where no in-domain data is present in the training data. The paper should report results for all tasks using 100 in-domain sentences.

3. The paper does not address social-media-specific challenges like high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons, which are critical for the Facebook and Twitter test sets. It's unclear if any special preprocessing was applied to handle these issues, which could significantly impact the results.

4. The paper does not provide concrete examples of how data selection improves translation quality or address how the method handles OOV words, which are particularly problematic in social media translation. Without these examples, it's difficult to understand the specific nature of the improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide results for all four language tasks using only 100 in-domain sentences to better support your claim that the method works with extremely limited data?

2. How did you handle social-media-specific challenges (high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, emoticons) in your data preprocessing? Were any special filters or transformations applied?

3. Please provide specific examples of sentences that were successfully selected by your method and how this selection led to improved translation quality.

4. Please clarify the architecture in Section 3.2 (how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined) and specify the loss function used in the CNN training.

5. How were the system weights (for the phrase-based SMT system) tuned in Section 5.2 to ensure fair comparison with baselines?
