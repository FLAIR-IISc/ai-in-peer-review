

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The method uses a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to select relevant sentences from general-domain training data. The CNN is trained using a small amount of in-domain data (labeled positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain data (labeled negative examples). The trained model then scores all sentences in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train the SMT system. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines across four language directions and three test domains.

## Main Contributions
1. **Practical domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data**: The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature by demonstrating effective domain adaptation with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, which is orders of magnitude fewer than required by prior approaches.
2. **Semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection**: The authors successfully leverage large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through pre-trained word embeddings to overcome the limitation of small in-domain data.
3. **Empirical demonstration of effectiveness**: The method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection approaches across multiple language pairs and test domains, with up to 3.1 BLEU improvement.

## Strengths
1. **Significant practical contribution**: The ability to adapt MT systems with minimal in-domain data addresses a real-world problem, particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media translation where in-domain data is scarce. The authors demonstrate that their approach works with only 100 in-domain sentences, which is a substantial advancement over prior work requiring thousands of sentences.
2. **Strong experimental results**: The paper presents comprehensive experiments across four language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with multiple test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The results consistently show that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms all baselines, with the most significant improvements (3.1 BLEU) on the Chinese-to-English SMS task.
3. **Clear demonstration of robustness**: The authors show that their method remains stable even with very small amounts of in-domain data (100 sentences), with only 0.12 standard deviation across three random samples of the dev set.
4. **Effective use of semi-supervised learning**: The approach of leveraging large amounts of unlabeled data through pre-trained word embeddings is well-justified and effectively implemented, providing a compelling solution to the limited in-domain data problem.

## Weaknesses
1. **Missing comparison to key baseline**: The paper compares well to n-gram and RNN language model approaches but omits comparison to bilingual data selection methods that sum or difference cross-entropies (e.g., Lewis-Moore), which is a standard approach in the field. This is particularly important since the paper's approach of summing source and target scores is conceptually similar.
2. **Insufficient justification for CNN choice**: The motivation for using CNN over RNN/LSTM is unclear and not adequately justified. The paper claims CNN captures important sentence sections, but provides no evidence or comparison to RNN/LSTM architectures for this specific task.
3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not provide experiments to determine whether BOW, SEQ, or their combination is most important for the CNN's performance. This is a fundamental question about the model's design that should be addressed.
4. **Unclear validation procedure**: The validation process for selecting the number of in-domain sentences and CNN hyperparameters is not clearly described, and sensitivity to these choices is not reported. Figure 1 suggests the method is robust, but the paper doesn't explain how these parameters were determined.
5. **Table 2 comparison issue**: Table 2 should compare methods at the same number of selected sentences, as Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms when selection sizes are equal.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a comparison with the Lewis-Moore sum/difference of cross-entropies approach, which is a standard bilingual data selection method that shares conceptual similarities with your sum-of-scores approach?
2. Why did you choose CNN over RNN/LSTM for this task? Could you provide a comparison or ablation study showing the relative performance of these architectures on your data selection task?
3. Could you include example sentences selected by different methods (e.g., ngram vs. sscnn) to better support the claims in Section 5.4 about domain-specific word capture?
4. How would your approach perform when in-domain data is available in the form of a test set rather than a development set, as is common in real-world scenarios?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical contributions to the field of domain adaptation in machine translation. The main weaknesses are methodological gaps in the comparison to key baselines and insufficient justification for key design choices, which should be addressed to strengthen the contribution. With these clarifications, the paper would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
