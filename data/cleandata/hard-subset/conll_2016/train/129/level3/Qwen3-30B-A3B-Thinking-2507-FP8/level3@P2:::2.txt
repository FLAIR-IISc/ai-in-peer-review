

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors train a CNN classifier using a small amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) and general-domain data to compute domain relevance scores for sentences in the general-domain corpus. The top-scoring sentences are then selected to train the SMT system.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. Demonstrating that CNN-based data selection can outperform language model-based approaches, particularly when in-domain data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences).
2. Providing evidence that fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation becomes feasible with this approach, enabling adaptation to new domains without requiring large amounts of in-domain data.
3. Showing that the method can still achieve meaningful improvements even when no in-domain data exists in the general-domain training data.

## Strengths
1. The most compelling contribution is the demonstration of effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), which enables fine-grained topic adaptation that wasn't previously feasible. This addresses a genuine practical challenge in machine translation where collecting large amounts of in-domain data for many topics is prohibitively expensive.

2. The paper provides a systematic evaluation across four language directions with multiple test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), showing consistent improvements over baseline methods. The comparison to state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods is well-executed.

3. The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated, building on solid prior work in text classification with CNNs and leveraging word embeddings from large general-domain corpora to overcome the limited in-domain data challenge.

## Weaknesses
1. The reported +3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh→en sms task is misleading. The training data already contained a 120K in-domain subset, and training on that subset alone achieved 25.7 BLEU (vs. 22.9 for all general-domain data). The comparison to the "alldata" baseline doesn't fairly represent the contribution since the in-domain data was already present in the training set.

2. The most compelling result (effectiveness with 100 in-domain sentences) was only tested on the zh→en task where in-domain data exists in the training set. The paper doesn't provide results for the other three tasks (ar→en, en→es, en→zh) using only 100 in-domain sentences, making it unclear whether this holds for tasks without in-domain training data.

3. The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of what kinds of sentence pairs were selected or how they improved translation quality. Without this, it's difficult to assess the practical impact of the data selection approach.

4. The paper doesn't address social-media-specific challenges (high OOV rate, usernames, hashtags, emoticons) and it's unclear whether any special preprocessing was applied to handle these, which is particularly important given the test domains.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide results for all four tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs to better support the claim that the method works with extremely limited in-domain data?

2. For the tasks where in-domain data wasn't present in the general-domain training data (ar→en, en→es, en→zh), what specific improvements did the data selection provide, and can you give concrete examples of sentence pairs that were selected and how they improved translation quality?

3. How was the model able to handle social-media-specific features (e.g., usernames, hashtags, emoticons) without special preprocessing? Did you apply any filtering or normalization to these elements?

4. In Section 3.2, could you clarify how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the semi-supervised CNN architecture? A brief description or diagram would be helpful.

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more nuanced comparison to baselines that include interpolated in-domain/out-domain models, rather than just comparing to "alldata" as the baseline. This would provide a more meaningful evaluation of the data selection approach. Additionally, Section 5.2 should explicitly state how system weights were tuned for completeness.
