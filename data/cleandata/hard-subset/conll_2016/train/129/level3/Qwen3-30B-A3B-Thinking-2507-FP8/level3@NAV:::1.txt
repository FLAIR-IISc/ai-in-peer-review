

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that CNN-based data selection can effectively adapt MT systems with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, (2) showing that semi-supervised CNNs (using pre-trained word embeddings) outperform existing LM-based methods, particularly when in-domain data is scarce, and (3) providing practical evidence that this approach can achieve significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) with only 2.5-10% of the training data.

Strengths include addressing a meaningful practical problem (domain adaptation with limited in-domain data), providing strong empirical evidence through experiments on four language directions across multiple test domains, and demonstrating the method's effectiveness even when in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences). The paper's experimental design is generally sound, and the results are compelling for the stated problem.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. First, the paper fails to compare with bilingual data selection methods (e.g., sum/difference of cross-entropies), which is a significant gap given the paper's focus on translation adaptation. Second, the motivation for using CNN over RNN/LSTM is unclear; the authors claim CNN captures important sentence sections, but provide no evidence to support this over other architectures. Third, the paper lacks experiments showing whether bag-of-words (BOW), sequence (SEQ), or their combination is most important for the CNN's performance. Fourth, the validation procedure for selecting the number of in-domain sentences and hyperparameter tuning is unclear, and sensitivity analysis to these choices is missing. Finally, Table 2 compares methods at different numbers of selected sentences (e.g., 300K vs 400K), making direct comparison difficult.

The paper would benefit from including a CNN layer diagram, addressing the inconsistent citation style, and providing example sentences selected by different methods to support Section 5.4 claims. I also recommend comparing to Axelrod (2015) and citing Lewis-Moore's work on cross-entropy differences.
