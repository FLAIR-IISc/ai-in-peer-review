

# Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The main contribution is a data selection framework that uses CNNs to score general-domain sentences based on domain relevance, enabling effective domain adaptation without requiring large in-domain datasets. The paper demonstrates that this method can work with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, making fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation feasible. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation showing significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) over strong baselines trained with large amounts of data.

## Strengths
1. **Practical Significance**: The paper addresses a genuine industry problem—domain adaptation with limited in-domain data—which is increasingly relevant as social media and new domains emerge with very limited training resources. The demonstration that their method works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences is a substantial practical contribution, making topic-specific translation systems feasible for many new domains.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering four language directions with multiple test domains. The results consistently show that the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all baselines, including three state-of-the-art language model-based approaches. The 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with only 3% of the training data is particularly impressive.

3. **Robustness with Limited Data**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their method maintains strong performance even when the in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences), with consistent results across three random samples (average BLEU 25.03, SD 0.12). This stability is a significant advantage over LM-based methods that degrade substantially with small in-domain sets.

## Weaknesses
1. **Missing Comparison to Key Baselines**: The paper lacks comparison to bilingual data selection methods that use sum/difference of cross-entropies (as used in Moore and Lewis, 2010), which would better contextualize the contribution. The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art language model based data selection methods," but the comparison is limited to n-gram and RNNLM approaches, not the most common bilingual selection techniques.

2. **Insufficient Justification for CNN Choice**: While the paper argues that CNNs capture important sentence sections, it does not sufficiently justify why CNNs are better suited than RNNs/LSTMs for this task. The comparison between CNN and RNN approaches is missing, which would strengthen the methodological claim.

3. **Unclear Validation Procedure**: The paper does not clearly explain how the number of selected in-domain sentences was determined (Table 2 shows varying numbers across methods), nor does it report sensitivity to this choice. Figure 1 suggests the proposed method outperforms others at the same training data size, but the paper does not explicitly compare at equal data sizes.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper does not investigate whether BOW, SEQ, or their combination is most important for performance. The claim that "the sum of source and target scores achieves the best performance" lacks supporting evidence from ablation studies.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a comparison of your method with the bilingual data selection approach of Moore and Lewis (2010) that uses the difference in language model perplexity? This would better contextualize your contribution.

2. The paper mentions using "the sum of source and target scores" for selection, which bears similarity to the Lewis-Moore approach that sums source and target cross-entropy differences. Could you clarify this relationship and cite the Lewis-Moore work?

3. Could you provide example sentences selected by your method versus the LM-based methods? This would help validate the claims in Section 5.4 about how the CNN captures domain-specific features.

4. The paper states "the size of the in-domain data could be as few as 100 sentences," but it's unclear whether these 100 sentences are from the development set or a separate in-domain set. Please clarify this point.

5. Could you report sensitivity to the number of selected sentences (N) and the CNN hyperparameters? How does performance vary when using different values for N or the number of neurons?
