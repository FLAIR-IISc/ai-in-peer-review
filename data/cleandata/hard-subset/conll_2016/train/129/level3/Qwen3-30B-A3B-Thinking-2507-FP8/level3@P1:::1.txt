

Summary: This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that semi-supervised CNNs can effectively select in-domain training data with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, and (2) showing that this method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection approaches, particularly when in-domain data is scarce.

Strengths: The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in machine translation where social media and niche domains often lack sufficient in-domain data. The experimental results are compelling, showing up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over strong baselines with only 3% of the training data. The demonstration that the approach works with just 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement) is particularly impressive and addresses a critical gap in the literature. The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing the method's stability across different in-domain data sizes, with standard deviations of only 0.12 BLEU when repeating experiments with different 100-sentence samples.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a critical comparison to bilingual data selection approaches that directly compare source and target language model scores (e.g., the Lewis-Moore method), which is a standard approach in the field. The motivation for using CNN over RNN/LSTM for this task is underdeveloped, merely stating that CNN captures "important local information" without demonstrating why this is superior to sequential models for text classification in this context. The paper does not compare different input representations for the CNN (BOW, SEQ, or their combination), which would provide important insights into the model's behavior. The validation procedure for selecting the number of sentences and hyperparameters is unclear, and Table 2 compares methods at different numbers of selected sentences, making direct performance comparisons difficult (Figure 1 suggests the proposed method outperforms even at the same selection size).

The paper also makes an important but unverified claim that word embeddings help with domain adaptation for unseen words like "Lenovo" (Section 5.4), but provides no evidence comparing the approach with and without pre-trained word embeddings. Additionally, the paper doesn't address why summing source and target classification scores (as they do) is better than the Lewis-Moore approach of summing or differencing cross-entropies, which would be a natural comparison.

Questions to Authors: 1) Could you provide a comparison with the Lewis-Moore bilingual data selection method, which directly compares source and target language model scores? 2) What evidence supports the claim that word embeddings specifically help with unseen words like "Lenovo" compared to using only in-domain word embeddings? 3) Could you clarify the validation procedure used to select the number of sentences to include in the training data and the CNN hyperparameters? 4) How does the CNN approach compare when using more in-domain data (e.g., 1,000 sentences) rather than just the development set?
