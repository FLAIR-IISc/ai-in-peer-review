

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper proposes using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) adaptation, specifically targeting scenarios with extremely limited in-domain data. The authors train a CNN classifier on a small amount of in-domain data (e.g., development set) and general-domain data to select the most relevant sentences from the general-domain corpus. They claim significant improvements over language model (LM)-based data selection methods, with up to 3.1 BLEU gain on some tasks and effectiveness with as few as 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstration that CNN-based data selection can be effective with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), addressing a practical challenge in social media translation where domain-specific data is scarce.
2. Application of semi-supervised CNNs with word embeddings trained on general-domain data to overcome the limitation of small in-domain datasets.
3. Empirical evidence showing CNN-based selection outperforms LM-based methods when in-domain data is limited.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem in machine translation: domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data. This is increasingly important as social media translation becomes more common, where domain-specific data is often scarce and expensive to collect.
2. The most compelling result—that CNN-based selection works with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs—has significant potential impact if properly validated across multiple domains and language pairs.
3. The paper provides a clear methodology that could be easily integrated into existing SMT pipelines, with a well-defined experimental setup and thorough comparison with relevant baselines.

## Weaknesses
1. The claim of +3.1 BLEU improvement is misleading. The largest gains occur only when in-domain data is already present in the training set (zh→en sms task), where training solely on the in-domain subset yields +2.8 BLEU. The paper should compare against interpolated in-/out-domain models rather than simply "all general-domain data" baseline.
2. The most compelling result (effectiveness with 100 in-domain sentences) was only tested on the zh→en task where in-domain data exists in the training set. The paper fails to demonstrate whether this holds for tasks without such in-domain data (ar→en, en→es, en→zh), undermining the generalizability claim.
3. The paper does not address social-media-specific challenges like high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It's unclear if any special preprocessing was applied, which is critical for social media translation evaluation.
4. The paper lacks concrete examples showing what specific improvements data selection produces, making it difficult to understand the nature of the gains.
5. Section 3.2 is poorly explained: it fails to clarify how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined (an architecture diagram would help) and doesn't specify the "designated loss function."

## Questions to Authors
1. The +3.1 BLEU claim on zh→en sms task appears misleading since training only on the in-domain subset (which exists in the training data) already gives +2.8 BLEU. Please clarify how your method differs from simply using the in-domain data subset and provide fair comparisons to interpolated in-/out-domain models.
2. The small-data claim (100 in-domain sentences) was only tested on zh→en. Please report results for all tasks using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs to better support your small-data claim.
3. Did you apply any special preprocessing for social-media-specific issues (high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, emoticons)? If so, please describe it.
4. Please provide concrete examples showing what kinds of improvements your data selection method produces (e.g., before/after translation examples).
5. Clarify the architecture in Section 3.2: how are word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors combined? What was the designated loss function?
6. In Section 5.2, explain how system weights were tuned for completeness.
