Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to select in-domain training data for SMT when only tiny amounts of in-domain text are available. A CNN trained on in-domain dev sentences (positives) and randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negatives) scores general-domain sentence pairs; top-ranked pairs (by source score, target score, or their sum) are used to train adapted SMT systems. The semi-supervised variant augments one-hot region features with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled data. Experiments on four translation directions and social-media test domains show consistent BLEU gains over training on all data and over LM-based selection methods; the method is especially robust when only a few hundred or even 100 in-domain sentences are available.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method that outperforms strong LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, combined) by about 0.5 BLEU on average and remains robust with very small in-domain samples.
- An empirical study across four language directions and three social-media domains demonstrating consistent improvements, including up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS with only ~3% of the training data.
- Evidence that effective selection is possible with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, with small variance across random samples.

Strengths
- Strong small-data story: The most compelling result is the robustness with tiny in-domain supervision. With only 100 labeled sentences, the semi-supervised CNN achieves +2.1 BLEU on zh→en, outperforming LM-based selection substantially. The reported standard deviation (~0.12 BLEU) over three random 100-sentence samples suggests the approach is stable.
- Consistent multilingual gains: The method improves over using all data in all four directions, with statistically significant gains in three of four settings (zh→en +3.1, ar→en +1.4, en→es +0.7, en→zh +1.4 BLEU), demonstrating cross-lingual applicability and benefit on social-media domains where parallel in-domain training data are scarce.
- Competitive to state of the art with simple architecture: The approach uses a straightforward CNN trained with one-hot features plus pre-trained embeddings, yet outperforms tuned LM selection pipelines. The choice to score source and target sides and combine scores is reasonable and empirically motivated.
- Sound evaluation practices: Statistical significance testing is reported; model capacity and hyperparameters are clearly noted; and the paper provides a strong baseline set (all-data, n-gram, RNNLM, combined LMs) and an informative “in-domain-only” baseline where an in-domain subset exists.

Weaknesses
- Overemphasis of “+3.1 BLEU” without disentangling the in-domain subset effect: On zh→en, there is a sizable in-domain subset (120K) within the general pool; training on that subset alone reaches 25.7 vs. 22.9 for all-data (+2.8). The proposed method’s 26.0 (+3.1 vs. all-data) is only +0.3 over the in-domain-only baseline. A more appropriate comparison would include interpolated/mixture models combining in-domain and selected out-of-domain data (e.g., phrase-table and LM interpolation), which might reduce the headline gap.
- Limited evidence for the “tiny in-domain” claim beyond one task: The 100-sentence experiment is shown only for zh→en, where hidden in-domain data are present in training. It is unclear whether similar robustness holds in settings with no in-domain component in the pool (ar→en, en→es, en→zh). This gap weakens the generality of the small-data claim.
- Methodological clarity gaps in Section 3.2: The description of how one-hot regions and embedding-based regions are combined is underspecified. Equation (2) suggests a linear combination pre-activation, but the network topology (number of convolutional layers, whether embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned in the supervised stage, pooling choices per stream, feature concatenation vs. summation) and the loss function (binary cross-entropy? hinge?) are not clearly stated. An architecture diagram would help.
- SMT training/tuning details missing: Section 5.2 does not specify how SMT weights were tuned (e.g., MERT/MIRA/PRO, tuning set, BLEU smoothing), whether the target-side LM was retrained only on selected data or kept large (e.g., Gigaword for English), and whether any domain-specific reordering or additional features were adjusted. These choices can materially affect the reported gains.
- Social-media preprocessing and OOV handling: Social media introduces high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, emoticons, code-switching, and non-standard orthography. The paper does not report any special normalization or filtering. Since data selection cannot create translations for true OOVs, it would be helpful to demonstrate what phenomena drive the observed gains (e.g., improved phrase-table coverage vs. LM fit).

Questions to Authors
- Can you report the 100-sentence (and 400/800-sentence) small-data results for ar→en, en→es, and en→zh, where no in-domain subset exists in training? This would substantiate the core claim.
- What exact supervised loss did you optimize (binary cross-entropy with logistic top layer vs. hinge/SVM), and were embeddings frozen or fine-tuned during supervised CNN training?
- Please provide a concise architecture diagram: separate convolutional streams for one-hot and embedding inputs vs. shared, pooling strategy per stream, and how you combine source and target sentence scores.
- How were SMT feature weights tuned (algorithm, tuning set/domain, stopping criteria)? Was the target LM trained on selected data only or combined with large background LMs?
- What preprocessing was applied to social-media text (tokenization, hashtag/user normalization, emoji handling, URLs)? Any filtering of noisy pairs?
- What is the computational cost of training the classifiers and scoring all sentences at the reported scales? How sensitive are results to region size/stride and to the source of unlabeled data for word2vec?
- Can you include qualitative analyses: examples of selected vs. rejected sentences, changes in OOV/coverage, and error types improved?
