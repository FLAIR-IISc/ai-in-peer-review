- Contribution: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to score general-domain sentence pairs for in-domain relevance and selects top-ranked pairs to train SMT systems with very small in-domain supervision.
- Contribution: Empirically shows CNN-based selection outperforms strong LM-based methods (n-gram, RNNLM), especially with 100–800 in-domain sentences across four language directions and social media domains.
- Contribution: Provides a practical selection recipe combining one-hot CNN features and word2vec segment embeddings, aggregating source and target scores to select 2.5–10% of training data.
- Contribution: Analyzes sensitivity to labeled in-domain data size with multiple random subsamples at 100 sentences, showing low variance and robustness.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains in realistic low-resource adaptation, including up to +3.1 BLEU and +2.1 BLEU with only 100 in-domain sentences, with significance testing.
- Strength: Focuses on challenging social media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook) where in-domain examples are scarce and LM selection degrades.
- Strength: Clear, implementable methodology using public tools and well-described model variants, region construction, and word2vec.
- Strength: Simple and effective source+target scoring by summing separate sentence scores aligns with bilingual selection intuition.
- Weakness: Comparisons may be unfair due to varying selection sizes across methods; need matched-size BLEU vs. #selected curves across tasks.
- Weakness: Limited ablation studies—unclear which CNN components (bow vs. seq, embeddings vs. one-hot, semi-supervised vs. one-hot) drive gains.
- Weakness: Incomplete baseline specification and positioning vis-à-vis classical bilingual selection (Moore–Lewis/Axelrod); aggregation methods should be made explicit and compared.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (selection size N, window size, pooling, neurons, embedding dim) are under-specified and lack robustness analysis.
- Weakness: Potential confound in zh→en results due to an in-domain subset present in the general pool; overlap complicates generalization claims.
- Question: Provide BLEU vs. #selected curves or matched-size comparisons (e.g., 200K, 400K, 800K) for all methods/tasks.
- Question: Does the “ngram” baseline implement Moore–Lewis/Axelrod difference-of-cross-entropies and sum source/target scores—if not, add that baseline?
- Question: Report ablations for (i) bow vs. seq vs. both, (ii) no pre-trained embeddings vs. word2vec, (iii) one-hot CNN vs. semi-supervised CNN, and (iv) source-only, target-only, sum selection.
- Question: Compare against Axelrod (2015)’s POS-replacement LM to test whether reducing LM sparsity closes the gap with word2vec CNN.
- Question: How does performance scale with more in-domain labeled data (e.g., a few thousand sentences)? Do CNN gains persist vs. LM-based selection?
- Question: Report training/scoring times and compute requirements for CNN selection versus LM-based selection on 9–15M pools.
- Question: Provide qualitative examples of sentence pairs selected by each method to substantiate claims about domain-specific cue capture.
- Comment: Add a simple architecture diagram showing one-hot and embedding branches and their fusion for clarity.
- Comment: Fix inconsistent citation styles and reference formatting.
- Comment: Report the actual numbers for source-only and target-only selection (currently only state that sum works best).
- Comment: Consider extending to a bilingual CNN that directly learns to weight and combine source/target signals rather than summing post-hoc.
- Comment: Release selection code or selection scores to facilitate reproducibility.
