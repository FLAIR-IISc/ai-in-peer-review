Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier (in the spirit of Johnson & Zhang, 2015) to score sentence pairs for in-domain data selection in SMT, using both one-hot and embedding-based region representations and selecting by combined source/target scores.
- The primary contribution is empirical: semi-supervised CNN selection yields consistent, modest gains over strong LM-based selection baselines, and remains robust when only tiny in-domain seeds are available (down to 100 sentences).
- A secondary contribution is a practical selection pipeline applicable across four language pairs and social-media test domains.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advantage over LM-based selection: across four directions, the semi-supervised CNN averages roughly +0.5 BLEU over the best LM selection, with significance testing reported.
- Strong small-data robustness: on zh→en, the method maintains stability down to 100 in-domain pairs, with small variance over multiple samples; this is a useful, practically relevant finding.
- Sensible modeling choices: combining source and target sentence scores is evaluated and found best; the setup is reproducible with public toolkits and conventional SMT pipelines.
- Ablations on selection size and supervision levels are reasonably explored (region types, pooling, use of pre-trained embeddings), supporting the claim that CNNs help when supervision is scarce.

Weaknesses
- Overstated headline gain: the +3.1 BLEU on zh→en arises where in-domain data exist in the training pool; training only on the in-domain subset already yields +2.8 BLEU. A fair comparison requires interpolated/mixed in-domain/out-of-domain systems (e.g., mixture/interpolation of LMs and phrase tables).
- Small-data claim is only compelling for zh→en; the 100-sentence experiment is not reported for the other three tasks where no in-domain training data are known to exist. This limits generality.
- Social-media specifics are under-addressed: high OOV rates, usernames/hashtags/emoticons, and tokenization/normalization choices can dominate gains; no preprocessing details or filtering strategy are given.
- Data selection cannot directly fix OOVs; the paper lacks qualitative analyses or concrete examples illustrating what kinds of errors improve (e.g., domain lexicon, phrase reordering, named entities).
- Section 3.2 is underspecified: how one-hot, segment embeddings, and architecture are combined, and what exact loss/objective and training schedule are used; an architecture diagram would help. Section 5.2 omits how system weights (log-linear features) are tuned.

Questions to authors
- Please compare to interpolated in-/out-domain models (e.g., phrase-table and LM interpolation, mixture adaptation). Does CNN selection still outperform?
- Report the 100-sentence in-domain experiments for all tasks; if infeasible, simulate with proxy domains without in-domain overlap.
- What preprocessing was applied for social media (tokenization, casing, hashtag/username segmentation, emoji handling)? Any filtering?
- Provide qualitative examples of selected sentences and resulting translation improvements; quantify OOV coverage changes.
- Clarify Section 3.2: precise network topology, how one-hot and segment vectors are fused, the designated loss, optimization hyperparameters, and whether embeddings are fine-tuned.
- Specify weight tuning details in 5.2 (optimizer, objective, development set, stopping criterion), and how N (selected size) was chosen without overfitting.
