Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score and select in-domain sentence pairs from large general-domain corpora for SMT training, targeting the regime where only tiny amounts of in-domain seed data are available (as few as 100 sentences). The approach trains a CNN-based domain classifier using the small in-domain set as positives and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; it augments one-hot inputs with pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) to improve robustness. The model assigns domain-relevance scores to both source and target sentences; selecting the top-N pairs (based on the sum of source and target scores) yields SMT systems that outperform strong LM-based selection baselines across four language directions and three social-media domains.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective semi-supervised CNN data-selection method for SMT adaptation that works with extremely scarce in-domain seed data. The method consistently improves BLEU over strong LM-based selection baselines and the “all data” baseline, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS using only ~3% of the data.

2) Comprehensive empirical evidence, across four language pairs (zh↔en, ar→en, en→es) and social-media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), that CNN-based selection is especially robust when the in-domain seed is very small (e.g., +2.1 BLEU with only 100 labeled sentences), with low variance across random seeds.

3) A practical finding that combining source- and target-side domain scores outperforms using either side alone for selection.

Strengths
- Strong improvements under realistic low-resource adaptation: Across tasks, sscnn outperforms “alldata” by up to +3.1 BLEU (zh→en SMS) and beats LM-based selection by up to +0.5 BLEU on average, with significance testing. The 100-sentence regime still gives +2.1 BLEU and shows low variance (std 0.12), highlighting robustness where LM approaches struggle.

- Broad, convincing evaluation: Results span four directions with 9–15M sentence-pair training pools and three distinct social-media domains where in-domain parallel data are scarce, suggesting the method generalizes beyond a single language/domain.

- Practicality and simplicity: The pipeline (train small binary classifier; rank and select; train standard phrase-based SMT) is easy to reproduce and deploy. Using off-the-shelf word2vec embeddings for semi-supervision is pragmatic and leverages unlabeled data effectively.

- Insightful analysis of why CNN helps: The discussion that convolution and max-pooling focus on informative n-grams and that embeddings group unseen but semantically similar words supports the observed gains in low-signal settings.

Weaknesses
- Missing or unclear comparisons to bilingual selection baselines: The paper cites Moore-Lewis (monolingual) and Axelrod et al. (bilingual) data selection, but it is unclear whether your ngram/rnnlm baselines implement bilingual selection (e.g., summing/differencing cross-entropies from source and target LMs). Given you sum source and target CNN scores, a fair bilingual LM baseline (including the 2015 POS-replacement variant to reduce sparsity) is essential.

- Fairness of Table 2 comparisons: Methods are compared at different selected data sizes, which can confound conclusions. Figure 1 partly addresses this for one condition, but a controlled comparison at fixed budgets across methods per task is needed to substantiate superiority claims.

- Ablations and architectural choices underexplained: The motivation for CNN over RNN/LSTM is stated qualitatively, but no empirical comparison is provided. Similarly, the relative importance of BOW vs SEQ regions and the contribution of embeddings vs one-hot are not ablated. Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains to architecture components.

- Hyperparameter and selection-budget tuning unclear: The procedure for choosing the number of selected sentences and CNN hyperparameters is not fully specified (e.g., what held-out data? any nested validation?). Sensitivity to these choices is not reported, which matters in low-data regimes.

- Limited qualitative evidence: Claims in Section 5.4 would be strengthened by showing example sentences selected by each method (LM vs ohcnn vs sscnn), illustrating CNN’s ability to capture domain-bearing n-grams and handle synonyms via embeddings.

Questions to Authors
- Do your LM baselines implement bilingual selection (e.g., Moore-Lewis/AXE source+target cross-entropy difference/sum)? If not, can you add this and the Axelrod (2015) POS-replacement variant? If yes, please detail exactly how scores are combined and tuned.

- How is N (the number of selected sentence pairs) chosen per method and task? Which set is used for tuning this budget, and is it disjoint from both the dev used as positive labels and the SMT tuning set? Please provide a sensitivity plot of BLEU vs N for each method.

- What is the exact validation protocol for CNN hyperparameters (region sizes, pooling, number of filters, embedding usage)? Is there nested validation to avoid overfitting in the tiny in-domain setting?

- Are the in-domain dev sentences used both to train the classifier and to tune SMT weights (e.g., MERT/MIRA)? If so, how do you mitigate potential overfitting or double-dipping? If not, please clarify which sets are used for each purpose.

- What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier and ranking the corpus compared to LM-based selection? Any throughput numbers would help practitioners.

Additional comments
- Please add a concise architecture diagram for the one-hot and semi-supervised CNN variants, showing how one-hot and embedding region features are combined.

- Include an ablation table: BOW-only, SEQ-only, BOW+SEQ; one-hot only vs embeddings only vs both; ReLU vs alternatives; and a simple RNN/LSTM baseline.

- Report results at matched selection sizes in the main table; keep best-N per method in an appendix to show upper bounds.

- Cite explicitly the relation between your source+target score summation and Moore-Lewis/Axelrod bilingual combinations, and discuss when one might prefer sum vs difference.

- Consider a bilingual CNN that ingests aligned sentence pairs to learn an optimal combination of source and target features end-to-end.

- Minor: fix inconsistent citation styles; standardize smoothing footnote; ensure references are complete and formatted.
