Summary
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score general-domain sentence pairs for in-domain relevance, selecting the top-ranked pairs to train phrase-based SMT systems. The CNN follows Johnson & Zhang (2015): inputs combine one-hot regions and region embeddings built from word2vec trained on large unlabeled data; the classifier is trained with tiny in-domain dev data as positives and an equal number of negatives sampled from the general pool. Selection is applied to both source and target sides, and scores are combined. Experiments on four directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and three social-media test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook) compare against strong data selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). The proposed semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) consistently outperforms the baselines, with reported improvements up to +3.1 BLEU over using all data, and shows robustness when trained with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (tested on zh→en).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method yields consistent gains over strong LM-based selection baselines across four language directions, with average improvements of roughly +0.5 BLEU over the best LM method and large reductions in training set size.
2) Evidence that CNN-based selection is more robust than LM-based selection when the amount of in-domain labeled data is very small (down to 100 sentences), at least on zh→en sms, where it delivers +2.1 BLEU over the all-data baseline with 300K selected pairs.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important setting: domain adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain labeled data, common for fast-moving social media domains. The proposed pipeline is simple, scalable, and uses widely-available tools (conText CNN, word2vec).
- Strong comparative evaluation: four translation directions, large training corpora (9–15M), three test domains, and statistically significant gains in most cases. The semi-supervised CNN beats one-hot CNN and all LM-based selectors on all four tasks, with clear selection-size reporting.
- Convincing small-data behavior (on zh→en): the 100-sentence experiment demonstrates stability (sd 0.12 BLEU across resamples) and superior gains vs LM-based selection, highlighting the value of region embeddings when labeled data are scarce.
- Practical guidance from ablations: combining source and target selection scores performs best; selection reduces training size to 2.5–10% of the full data while improving BLEU—useful for efficiency.

Weaknesses
- Overstated headline gain: the “+3.1 BLEU” on zh→en is largely attributable to the presence of a 120K in-domain subset in the general-domain pool. Training on that subset alone yields 25.7 BLEU vs 22.9 for all-data; sscnn yields 26.0. Thus, most of the 3.1-point gain is recoverable without CNN (by knowing/using the in-domain subset), and the method’s marginal benefit in this case is about +0.3 BLEU. A fairer analysis would compare against (a) in-domain-only, (b) interpolated/mixed in-/out-domain models, and (c) LM-based selection tuned to recover the same in-domain subset.
- Small-data claim not fully substantiated across tasks: the 100-sentence experiment is only shown for zh→en (where in-domain text exists in training). It is unclear whether similar robustness holds for ar→en and en→{es,zh} where no in-domain component is known to exist in training. This limits the generality of the “tiny in-domain” claim.
- Methodological clarity: Section 3.2 leaves key architectural/training details ambiguous. It is unclear how one-hot and embedding-based region features are combined (concatenation vs summed projections as in Eq. 2), whether embeddings are frozen or fine-tuned, what the loss function is (binary cross-entropy with class weighting?), regularization (dropout/L2), optimization, early stopping, class prior handling, and negative sampling strategy beyond “equal-size random negatives.” An architectural diagram or more explicit description would aid reproducibility.
- Limited analysis for social-media specifics: tokenization, normalization (usernames, hashtags, emojis), segmentation (especially for Chinese), and OOV handling are not described. Since data selection cannot directly fix OOV-driven errors, it would be useful to quantify OOV rates and show whether selection reduces them or primarily improves phrase/probability estimates. Concrete examples of improved translations would strengthen the case.
- Novelty is incremental: applying semi-supervised CNN text classification to SMT data selection is a straightforward adaptation of prior work. The paper would benefit from comparisons to simpler strong baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM on bag-of-ngrams, or a shallow FFNN on averaged embeddings) and from ablations isolating the contribution of one-hot regions vs embedding regions.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in the response)
- Please report the 100-in-domain-sentences experiment for ar→en, en→es, and en→zh. If computationally prohibitive, at least provide one additional direction without in-domain data in training (e.g., ar→en tweets). Does the robustness persist?
- For zh→en, please include “in-domain-only,” “in+out interpolated/mixed,” and LM-selected subsets tuned to approximate the 120K in-domain pool. Report BLEU relative to those baselines to quantify the marginal benefit of sscnn once in-domain content is controlled.
- Clarify the model architecture and training: how are wl(x) and ul(x) combined (concatenation vs separate linear maps added as in Eq. 2)? Are embeddings fixed or updated? What loss function, optimizer, learning rate schedule, regularization (dropout/L2), and early stopping criteria were used? What was the class ratio during training, and did you re-weight classes?
- Preprocessing: detail tokenization and normalization for social media (handling of @mentions, URLs, hashtags, emojis), truecasing, and Chinese segmentation. What are vocabulary sizes and OOV rates (train/dev/test) per task before and after selection?
- SMT tuning: which tuning algorithm (MERT/MIRA/TRAIN-OO?), dev set(s) used, stopping criteria, and whether selection size and system weights were chosen using only dev or any test information. How many tuning iterations, and were parameters re-tuned for each selected subset size?

Additional comments
- Consider adding an ablation comparing (i) one-hot only, (ii) embeddings only, (iii) their combination, and a simple linear classifier baseline. Also report training/selection runtime and throughput to highlight practicality.
- Please provide a few qualitative examples of selected vs non-selected sentences and corresponding translation differences, and report changes in LM perplexity and OOV counts after selection.
- The paper is generally clear and well organized; adding a schematic of the CNN and moving some hyperparameter details to an appendix would improve readability.
