Summary
This paper proposes a CNN-based classifier, inspired by Johnson & Zhang (2015), to score bilingual sentence pairs for in-domain relevance and select training data for SMT. The semi-supervised variant augments one-hot segment representations with embeddings learned from large unlabeled data. Experiments on four translation directions and social media test sets show consistent gains over LM-based selection methods; the most striking headline improvement (+3.1 BLEU) occurs on zh→en SMS where an in-domain subset exists in the training pool. A second experiment suggests the CNN remains effective when only about 100 in-domain sentences are available (shown for zh→en).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, semi-supervised CNN classifier for bilingual data selection that yields small but consistent gains (≈+0.5 BLEU on average) over strong LM-based selection across multiple directions and social-media test domains.
2) Evidence that CNN-based selection is comparatively robust when the amount of in-domain seed data is very small (down to 100 sentence pairs), at least for one setting (zh→en with in-domain material present in the pool).
3) A thorough comparative evaluation against three LM-based selection baselines under large data conditions.

Strengths
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines: Across four directions, semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) beats all LM-based selection methods by ≈0.3–0.9 BLEU, a meaningful gain for SMT adaptation. Improvements are statistically significant and achieved with only 2.5–10% of the available data.
- Robustness to small in-domain samples (most compelling): On zh→en, sscnn maintains clear advantages over LM-based selection as seed data shrink to 100 sentences (+2.1 BLEU over alldata; average over three samples with tiny variance), suggesting the approach is viable in low-resource adaptation scenarios.
- Sensible, well-motivated modeling: The architecture leverages convolution + max-pooling to focus on domain-indicative n-grams and complements sparse one-hot regions with dense embeddings trained on massive unlabeled text—an appropriate design for domain classification under label scarcity.
- Broad evaluation on social media: The test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook) are challenging and valuable; results demonstrate utility beyond canonical newswire settings.
- Methodological clarity in selection protocol: Scoring source and target separately and selecting by their sum is reasonable and appears to help; selection sizes are tuned on held-out data.

Weaknesses
- Headline +3.1 BLEU is misleading without stronger baselines: The large gain is confined to zh→en where 120K truly in-domain sentences exist in the pool. Training solely on those in-domain sentences already yields 25.7 BLEU (+2.8 over alldata), so sscnn’s additional gain is marginal. A fair comparison should include (a) explicit in-domain-only and (b) interpolated/mixed in-/out-domain models (e.g., phrase-table/LM interpolation, mixture/adaptive weighting as in Foster & Kuhn, Sennrich). Also report results when the in-domain subset is removed from the candidate pool.
- Small-data robustness shown only for zh→en: The strongest claim (effective with 100 in-domain sentences) is supported by a single task where in-domain data are present in training. It is unclear whether similar robustness holds for ar→en, en→es, and en→zh, where no known in-domain component exists. This threatens the generality of a key claim.
- Limited analysis of social-media-specific challenges: Social data have high OOV rates, usernames, hashtags, emoticons, and noisy casing/tokenization. The paper does not state any special preprocessing or filtering. Data selection alone cannot resolve OOVs or tokenization errors, so it is important to quantify OOV rates and describe handling (e.g., normalization, subwording, transliteration).
- Missing architectural and optimization details impede reproducibility: Section 3.2 is under-specified. It is unclear how one-hot and embedding-based segment vectors are combined (exact region construction, concatenation vs. parallel convolution, dimensionalities), and what the “designated loss function” is (cross-entropy/softmax? hinge? with what regularization). An architecture schematic would help.
- Incomplete SMT system details: Section 5.2 does not state how the log-linear model weights were tuned (MERT/MIRA/PRO, dev set used, stopping criteria). Given that selection sizes are chosen on held-out data, full tuning details are necessary to assess potential overfitting and fairness.

Questions to Authors
- Please clarify Section 3.2: How exactly are wl(x) (one-hot) and ul(x) (embedding) constructed and combined? Are there separate convolutional stacks whose outputs are summed (as Eq. 2 suggests), or are inputs concatenated before convolution? What are the segment/window definitions for the embedding stream? What is the exact loss function, output layer, and optimization method?
- In zh→en, how much of the selected 300K overlaps with the 120K in-domain subset? What is the BLEU if you: (a) train on the 120K alone, (b) 120K + top-k selected out-of-domain, (c) exclude the 120K from the selection pool?
- Please compare against standard interpolated/mixture baselines: e.g., linear interpolation of in-domain/out-of-domain LMs, mixture phrase tables, instance weighting (Foster et al., 2010; Sennrich, 2012). This will contextualize the +3.1 BLEU claim.
- Small-data claim: Can you report the “100 in-domain sentences” experiment for all four directions? Even a smaller subset of selection sizes would substantiate generality.
- Social-media preprocessing: Did you normalize or filter usernames, hashtags, URLs, emojis, character repetitions, casing, or script variants? What tokenizer/segmenter was used, and how did this affect OOV rates?
- What types of improvements does selection yield? Please provide concrete examples of source sentences, selected training pairs, and translation outputs before/after selection. Do gains come mainly from better lexical choice, phrase reordering, or noise reduction? How often are OOVs addressed (or not)?

Additional Comments
- Please specify SMT tuning method (e.g., kb-MIRA on dev, iterations, n-best size), and whether the same dev set doubles as positive examples for the classifier (risk of overfitting); if so, consider a separate dev for tuning.
- Report per-task OOV rates and tokenization settings; consider subword models or transliteration for robustness on social media.
- Include ablations: source-only vs. target-only vs. sum scoring; one-hot vs. embeddings vs. both; sensitivity to selection size with fixed tuning protocol.
- An architecture figure and a brief complexity/runtime comparison vs. LM baselines would improve clarity.
- Minor: references have formatting issues; please fix.
