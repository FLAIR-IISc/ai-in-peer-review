Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier to score and select general-domain sentence pairs for SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain seeds are available. One-hot and pre-trained embedding inputs are combined; source and target sentences are scored independently and summed for selection. Across four language directions and social-media test domains, the method outperforms strong LM-based data selection and, in one case, surpasses an in-domain-only subset, with stable performance even when trained from ~100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical contribution: A simple, scalable CNN-based selection pipeline that delivers consistent gains in extremely low in-domain regimes (down to 100 sentences) across multiple language pairs/domains.
2) Practical insight: Semi-supervised embeddings materially improve robustness over one-hot CNN and LM-based selection under data scarcity.
3) Cross-lingual validation: Broad experiments show generality across four directions with statistically significant improvements.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with tiny in-domain data: Up to +3.1 BLEU over alldata, and consistent improvements over n-gram/RNNLM baselines; stability results at 100-sentence supervision are particularly compelling.
- Cross-language/domain robustness: Results across zh↔en, ar→en, en→es for social media increase external validity.
- Clear textual description of the CNN and semi-supervised setup; the pipeline is straightforward to reproduce in principle.
- Sensible design choice to score source and target separately and combine, which aligns with bilingual selection intuition and likely drives gains.

Weaknesses
- Missing key baselines: No direct bilingual cross-entropy difference comparison (Moore–Lewis/Lewis–Moore sum/diff and Axelrod-style source+target), and no comparison to Axelrod (2015) POS replacement to test whether word2vec mainly reduces sparsity.
- Limited analysis/ablations: No evidence separating BOW vs SEQ (or their combination), and unclear why CNN is preferable to RNN/LSTM beyond intuition.
- Experimental protocol clarity: Validation procedure for selecting N sentences and CNN hyperparameters is under-specified; no sensitivity curves to N or to embedding choices.
- Fairness of comparisons: Table 2 does not hold the number of selected sentences constant across methods; a same-K comparison is needed (Figure 1 suggests the method remains strong).
- Presentation nits: Missing architecture diagram; inconsistent/incorrect parenthetical citation style.

Questions to Authors
- Please report same-K comparisons for Table 2 and sensitivity to N (and to hyperparameters/embedding types).
- Provide ablations isolating BOW vs SEQ vs combined inputs and a rationale/experiment comparing CNN to RNN/LSTM.
- Compare to bilingual cross-entropy sum/difference (source, target, and combined) and to Axelrod (2015) POS-replacement LMs.
- Include qualitative examples of sentences selected by different methods to support Section 5.4.
- Clarify the connection to Lewis–Moore sum/difference and consider a bilingual CNN that learns source/target weighting jointly.
