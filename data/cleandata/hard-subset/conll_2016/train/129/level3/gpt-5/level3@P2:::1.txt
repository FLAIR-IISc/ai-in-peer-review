Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to score general-domain sentence pairs for in-domain relevance, then selects the top-ranked pairs to train SMT systems. The approach is motivated by the practical setting where only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as little as 100 sentences) are available.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN-based selector substantially outperforms strong LM-based data selection methods (n-gram and RNNLM), especially in the extremely low in-domain regime (100–800 sentences), across four language directions and social media domains. Gains include up to +3.1 BLEU over training on all data, and +2.1 BLEU with just 100 in-domain sentences.
2) A practical selection recipe that combines one-hot CNN features with word2vec-based segment embeddings and aggregates source- and target-side sentence scores, yielding stable improvements while selecting only 2.5–10% of the training data.
3) Analysis of sensitivity to the size of labeled in-domain data, including multiple random subsamples at 100 sentences with low variance, indicating robustness.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results in a realistic, low-resource adaptation setting. The method consistently improves over “alldata” and over three LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, combined) across four translation directions, with significance testing. Notably, zh→en (sms) shows +3.1 BLEU, and the method achieves +2.1 BLEU with only 100 in-domain sentences, supported by variance analysis across repeats.
- Focus on social media domains and tiny in-domain supervision. The tasks (sms, tweets, Facebook) reflect challenging, high-variance text. The study directly targets a common practical constraint (few in-domain examples), demonstrating that CNN-based selection remains effective where LM-based selection degrades.
- Clear, implementable methodology leveraging public tools. The paper describes one-hot and semi-supervised CNN variants, region construction (bow/seq), model hyperparameters, and use of word2vec. The selection framework (train classifier on small labeled set; score and rank the full pool; select top-N) is straightforward to reproduce conceptually.
- Source and target scoring combination. Evaluating source and target sentences separately and selecting by the sum of scores is simple and effective, aligning with bilingual selection intuitions and contributing to the strong performance.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of comparisons due to varying selection sizes. Table 2 reports different numbers of selected sentences for each method, making it difficult to disentangle method quality from data volume. A controlled comparison at matched selection sizes (e.g., BLEU vs. #selected curves for all methods) is needed. Figure 1 partially addresses this for one task; extend it systematically across tasks.
- Limited ablations of the CNN design and inputs. The paper states bow- and seq-regions and word2vec embeddings are combined but does not report which components drive gains. Ablate: bow vs. seq, embeddings vs. one-hot only, one-hot CNN vs. semi-supervised CNN, and source vs. target vs. sum of scores (with numbers).
- Incomplete baselines and positioning vs. classical bilingual selection. Please clarify whether the “ngram” baseline implements the Moore–Lewis/Axelrod difference-of-cross-entropies and whether source and target cross-entropy differences are summed. The proposed sum of source/target CNN scores mirrors this idea; the paper should make the connection explicit and compare under the same aggregation. Also consider Axelrod (2015)’s POS-replacement LM to reduce sparsity—important for testing whether word2vec is the key advantage.
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity are under-specified. The procedure for choosing the number of selected sentences (“empirically determined on held-out”) and CNN hyperparameter tuning lacks detail, and no sensitivity analysis is reported. Given that performance may depend on N and hyperparameters (window size, pooling, neurons, embedding dimension), include robustness analyses.
- Confounding in zh→en due to in-domain material inside the general pool. The largest gain occurs in a setting where a 120K in-domain subset exists within the pool. While the method beats the in-domain-only baseline, this overlap complicates conclusions about generalization. Stronger emphasis on the three tasks without known in-domain overlap, or an analysis that removes the overlapped subset, would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
- Selection size control: Can you provide BLEU vs. #selected curves for all methods on all tasks, or at least matched-size comparisons at several N (e.g., 200K, 400K, 800K)?
- Baseline details: Does your “ngram” baseline use Moore–Lewis/Axelrod difference-of-cross-entropies and sum source/target scores? If not, please add that baseline and discuss how it compares to summing CNN scores.
- Ablations: Please report ablations for (i) bow vs. seq vs. both, (ii) no pre-trained embeddings vs. word2vec, (iii) one-hot CNN vs. semi-supervised CNN, and (iv) selection by source-only, target-only, and sum.
- Sparsity-robust LM baseline: Can you compare against Axelrod (2015)’s POS-replacement LM selection to test whether your word2vec-based semi-supervised CNN still dominates when LM sparsity is reduced?
- More in-domain data: Beyond using only dev sets, how does performance evolve when more in-domain labeled data is available (e.g., a few thousand sentences)? Do CNN gains persist relative to LM-based selection?
- Efficiency: What are training/scoring times and compute requirements for the CNN selector versus LM-based selection on the 9–15M pools?
- Qualitative analysis: Can you include example sentence pairs selected by each method to substantiate the claim in Section 5.4 about capturing domain-specific cues?

Additional comments
- A simple architecture diagram would improve clarity. The textual description is good, but a figure showing one-hot and embedding branches and their fusion would help.
- Please fix inconsistent citation styles and reference formatting.
- Report the actual numbers for source-only and target-only selection (you state that sum works best).
- Consider discussing extension to a bilingual CNN that directly learns to weight and combine source/target signals, rather than summing post-hoc scores.
- If possible, release selection code or selection scores to facilitate reproducibility.
