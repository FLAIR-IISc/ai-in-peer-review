Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier to score and rank general-domain sentence pairs for data selection in SMT, targeting the challenging setting where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. Using a small in-domain seed as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives, the model produces domain-relevance scores on both source and target sides; the sum of these scores is used to select training data. Word2vec embeddings trained on large unlabeled corpora are incorporated to mitigate sparsity. Across four translation directions and three social-media domains, the method yields consistent BLEU gains over systems trained on all data and over n-gram and RNNLM-based selection baselines, with particularly strong gains when only ~100 in-domain sentences are available.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method that delivers strong SMT adaptation when only tiny in-domain supervision is available, outperforming strong LM and RNNLM selection baselines across multiple language directions and domains.
2) An empirical finding that pre-trained embeddings within a CNN selection model confer robustness in extreme low-resource domain supervision (down to 100 sentences), with stable improvements and cross-domain generality.
3) A practical source+target scoring scheme that leverages both sides of parallel data for selection and works well in practice.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under realistic constraints: The method improves up to +3.1 BLEU and shows clear robustness when in-domain supervision shrinks to 100 sentences, a regime where LM-based selection struggles. Multi-language, multi-domain results enhance external validity.
- Thoughtful semi-supervised design: Incorporating pre-trained word embeddings at the segment level plausibly explains gains in sparse, domain-mismatched settings. The stability analysis (mean and low variance at 100 sentences) is convincing.
- Sensible use of both source and target signals: Scoring both sides and summing is a simple, effective way to exploit bilingual information during selection, and it appears consistently beneficial across tasks.
- Clear textual description and reproducible setup: The model description (one-hot vs semi-supervised inputs), toolkits (conText, word2vec), and SMT pipeline are reasonably detailed, easing reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Missing bilingual data-selection baselines and citations: No comparison to bilingual cross-entropy difference methods (e.g., Moore-Lewis on each side; Axelrod et al., 2011; sum/difference of cross-entropies). The proposed “sum of source and target CNN scores” is conceptually analogous; a direct comparison and explicit citation are needed to position novelty and quantify gains.
- Incomparable selection sizes in Table 2: Methods are compared at different numbers of selected sentences, confounding conclusions. A fair comparison at matched N (or recall@N curves) is necessary; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method may still win, but the main table should control for N.
- Limited ablations: The paper uses BOW and SEQ regions and pre-trained embeddings but does not ablate which components matter most (BOW vs SEQ vs both; pre-trained vs randomly initialized; source-only vs target-only vs weighted combination). This limits insight into what drives improvements.
- Unclear model/hyperparameter selection and sensitivity: The validation procedure for choosing N and CNN hyperparameters is insufficiently specified, and sensitivity to these choices is not reported. This is critical in low-resource regimes where overfitting and calibration can dominate.
- Motivation vs alternatives not fully justified: The rationale for CNNs over RNN/LSTM classifiers is asserted (capturing important local sections with pooling) but not empirically supported; no comparison to sequence models is provided. Also, a layer/block diagram would improve clarity.
- Incomplete positioning vs recent LM-sparsity remedies: No comparison to Axelrod (2015)’s POS-replacement approach, which directly addresses LM sparsity—the very issue the paper tackles via embeddings—leaves a gap in understanding the incremental benefit of word2vec+CNN.

Questions to Authors
- How exactly was N (the number of selected sentences) chosen per system? What validation set was used, and was test data ever consulted? Please include sensitivity curves (BLEU vs N) and report the range where your method dominates.
- Which components matter most? Please provide ablations for: BOW-only, SEQ-only, BOW+SEQ; with vs without pre-trained embeddings; source-only vs target-only vs sum vs learned weighted sum. Are the scores calibrated (e.g., via Platt scaling) before combining?
- How does your CNN classifier compare to an RNN/LSTM classifier trained in the same semi-supervised manner, at matched parameter budgets?
- Runtime and practicality: What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier(s) and scoring all sentences compared to LM and RNNLM baselines? Any guidance for scaling to tens/hundreds of millions of sentences?
- Negative sampling: You mention sampling negatives either uniformly or from first-round negatives with similar results. Can you quantify the difference? Did you try hard-negative mining (e.g., near the decision boundary)?
- More in-domain supervision: If more in-domain parallel data become available (e.g., thousands rather than hundreds), how do trends change? Does the advantage over LM baselines persist?

Additional Comments
- Please include a layer/block diagram of the CNN and the semi-supervised integration (Eq. 2), including the BOW/SEQ pathways and pooling, to complement the textual description.
- Ensure a fair, matched-N comparison in the main results table; alternately, add BLEU@N curves for all methods.
- Provide qualitative examples of sentences selected by different methods (LM vs CNN) to substantiate Section 5.4 claims about capturing domain-specific cues.
- Cite and compare against: Moore and Lewis (2010) and Axelrod et al. (2011) explicitly; Axelrod et al. (2015) POS-replacement. Discuss the relationship between summing source/target CNN scores and sum/difference of cross-entropies, and consider learning weights for combining source/target within a bilingual CNN.
- Minor writing: fix inconsistent/incorrect parenthetical citation style (\citet vs \citep) and general reference formatting issues.
