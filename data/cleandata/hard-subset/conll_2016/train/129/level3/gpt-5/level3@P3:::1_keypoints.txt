- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to score/rank sentence pairs for SMT data selection using a tiny in-domain seed, summing source+target scores and using word2vec; shows consistent BLEU gains, especially with ~100 in-domain sentences.

- Contribution 1: A simple, effective semi-supervised CNN selection method that outperforms n-gram and RNNLM selection baselines across languages and domains.

- Contribution 2: Empirical finding that pre-trained embeddings in the CNN give robustness in extreme low-resource domain supervision (down to ~100 sentences).

- Contribution 3: A practical source+target scoring scheme (sum of CNN scores) for bilingual data selection.

- Strength: Strong empirical gains (up to +3.1 BLEU) and robustness at very small in-domain sizes, validated across multiple languages/domains.

- Strength: Semi-supervised design with pre-trained embeddings plausibly mitigates sparsity and yields stable results (low variance) at 100 sentences.

- Strength: Using both source and target signals by summing scores is simple and consistently beneficial.

- Strength: Clear model description and reproducible experimental setup (toolkits and pipeline detailed).

- Weakness: Missing comparisons and citations to bilingual cross-entropy difference methods (Moore-Lewis; Axelrod et al. 2011) and lack of direct positioning vs those baselines.

- Weakness: Table 2 compares methods at different selection sizes (N), confounding conclusions; fair matched-N comparisons are needed.

- Weakness: Limited ablation studies—no isolated tests for BOW vs SEQ vs both, pre-trained vs random embeddings, or source-only vs target-only vs weighted sums.

- Weakness: Insufficient specification of model/hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analysis; unclear validation procedure for choosing N and CNN hyperparameters.

- Weakness: Rationale for CNN vs RNN/LSTM is asserted but not empirically supported; no comparison to sequence models and missing architectural diagram.

- Weakness: Incomplete comparison to other LM-sparsity remedies (e.g., Axelrod 2015 POS-replacement) that address the same sparsity issue.

- Question: How was N (number of selected sentences) chosen per system—what validation set was used and was test data consulted?

- Question: Please provide sensitivity curves (BLEU vs N) and the range where the method dominates.

- Question: Which components matter most? Request ablations for BOW-only, SEQ-only, combined, with/without pre-trained embeddings, and source/target combination strategies; are scores calibrated before combining?

- Question: How does the CNN classifier compare to an RNN/LSTM classifier trained similarly at matched parameter budgets?

- Question: What is the runtime and computational cost for training/scoring with the CNN vs LM and RNNLM baselines, and scalability guidance for very large corpora?

- Question: Details on negative sampling: quantify differences between uniform vs first-round negatives and whether hard-negative mining was tried.

- Question: How do results change with more in-domain parallel data (thousands of sentences)? Does the advantage over LM baselines persist?

- Additional comment: Include a layer/block diagram of the CNN and semi-supervised integration (BOW/SEQ pathways and pooling) to complement text.

- Additional comment: Ensure fair, matched-N comparisons in main results or add BLEU@N curves for all methods.

- Additional comment: Provide qualitative examples of sentences selected by different methods (LM vs CNN) to illustrate claimed domain-specific cue capture.

- Additional comment: Explicitly cite and compare to Moore & Lewis (2010), Axelrod et al. (2011), and Axelrod et al. (2015); discuss relationship between summed CNN scores and summed/differenced cross-entropies and consider learning combination weights.
