## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to select relevant training data from a larger general-domain corpus. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to mitigate the scarcity of in-domain data.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation scenarios. While CNNs have been applied to text classification, their application to *data selection* for SMT, particularly with extremely limited in-domain data (as low as 100 sentences), is a novel and valuable contribution. The experimental results across four language pairs and three test domains consistently show improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that pre-trained word embeddings significantly enhance performance in this low-data regime.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results showing consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines. The gains of up to 3.1 BLEU are substantial, particularly given the limited in-domain data used.
2. **Novel Application of CNNs:** Applying CNNs to the task of data selection for SMT is a novel idea, and the authors successfully demonstrate its effectiveness. The semi-supervised approach is well-motivated and addresses the key challenge of limited in-domain data.
3. **Demonstrated Scalability to Low-Resource Settings:** The results showing good performance even with only 100 in-domain sentences are particularly impressive and highlight the practical utility of the proposed method. This is a significant advantage over methods requiring thousands of in-domain examples.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted on a diverse set of language pairs and test domains, increasing the generalizability of the findings. The comparison to multiple strong baselines provides a robust evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper lacks a detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different CNN architectures (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes) and hyperparameter settings. A more thorough analysis would strengthen the understanding of *why* the CNN performs well.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are positive, the paper doesn't provide much insight into *what types* of sentences are being correctly/incorrectly selected. Error analysis could reveal potential areas for improvement.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper doesn't discuss the potential impact of using different embedding models or training them on different corpora.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study of the CNN architecture, including the impact of different filter sizes, number of layers, and pooling strategies?
2.  Can you provide some examples of sentences that were correctly/incorrectly selected by the CNN, and discuss the reasons for these outcomes?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the quality and source of the pre-trained word embeddings?
