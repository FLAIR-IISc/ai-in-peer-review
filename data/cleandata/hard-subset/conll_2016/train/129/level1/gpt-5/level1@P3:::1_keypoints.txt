- Proposal: use CNNs (with/without pre-trained embeddings) to score source and target sentences separately, sum scores to select bilingual training data for SMT; effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences and up to +3.1 BLEU.

- Contribution 1: Empirical demo that CNN-based sentence-level classifiers (especially with embeddings) outperform LM-based selection under very low in-domain supervision (100–800 sentences).

- Contribution 2: Practical bilingual selection protocol scoring source and target separately and summing scores, outperforming single-side scoring.

- Contribution 3: Evidence that performance scales favorably as in-domain size decreases and yields low variance even at 100 sentences.

- Strength: Strong empirical results across multiple language pairs and social-media domains with statistically significant BLEU gains.

- Strength: Focus on realistic low-resource in-domain settings with comparison curves down to 100 sentences and repeated trials showing robustness.

- Strength: Methodologically grounded, building on prior region-embedding CNN designs and situated within SMT adaptation literature.

- Strength: Sensible, reproducible experimental settings, standard baselines, provided toolkit/word2vec setup, and significance testing.

- Weakness: Limited ablation/diagnostic analysis — no quantification of seq vs. bow, region sizes/strides, or detailed contribution of one-hot vs. embeddings.

- Weakness: Unclear validation protocol and potential double use of the in-domain dev set for classifier training and SMT tuning, risking bias.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to strong non-neural discriminative classifiers (e.g., SVM/logistic with n-grams or tf-idf) that could be competitive in small-data regimes.

- Weakness: Computational cost and scalability not reported; scoring millions of sentences with CNNs may be expensive compared to LM-based methods.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of selected data quality — e.g., precision/recall against known in-domain subsets and lexical/topical diagnostics are missing.

- Question: Clarify validation protocol — which data were used to (a) train the CNN, (b) choose the number of selected sentences, and (c) tune the SMT system; were strict splits used?

- Question: Provide ablations for seq vs. bow, one-hot vs. embeddings vs. both, source-only vs. target-only vs. sum, and different region sizes.

- Question: Report training and inference time for ohcnn/sscnn per direction and hardware, and compare to LM-based selection runtimes.

- Question: Did you compare against linear SVM/logistic regression over bag-of-ngrams or tf-idf ranking, and how did they perform at 100–800 sentences?

- Question: For zh→en, what fraction of the known 120K in-domain subset is retrieved in top-N (e.g., N=300K) and do CNNs show higher precision than LM methods?
