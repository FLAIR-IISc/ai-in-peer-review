- Proposes semi-supervised CNNs to select in-domain sentence pairs for SMT when only tiny in-domain data is available.  
- Trains CNNs using dev set as positive and random general-domain sentences as negatives; two variants: one-hot and one-hot+word2vec.  
- Scores source and target sides separately and sums scores to select top-N bilingual pairs for SMT training.  
- Evaluated on four language directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and three social-media domains, showing consistent gains over strong baselines.  

Contributions:
- Practical data-selection framework using semi-supervised CNNs combining one-hot features with pretrained embeddings and bilingual scoring.  
- Comprehensive empirical study across four language pairs with significance testing, showing strong gains even with ~100 in-domain sentences.  
- Evidence that summed source+target CNN scores produce effective bilingual selection and robustness when in-domain data are sparse.  

Strengths:
- Strong empirical improvements in low-resource in-domain settings, including up to +3.1 BLEU and stable gains with only 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Modeling is well-motivated: CNNs capture local cues and embeddings mitigate sparsity for tiny dev sets.  
- Practical and simple to deploy using small dev sets, common toolkits (conText, word2vec), and standard SMT pipelines.  
- Cross-lingual applicability: monolingual CNNs on both sides and combined scoring are effective across diverse languages and scripts.  
- Stability analysis shows low variance in repeated 100-sentence experiments, indicating robustness to sample choice.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: uses established CNN classification and pretrained embeddings; novelty mainly in application to SMT selection.  
- Incomplete experimental details and potential overfitting: unclear validation protocol, hyperparameter tuning, and regularization practices.  
- Baseline fairness concerns: n-gram and RNNLM baselines under-specified and possibly weaker than stronger KN-smoothed or tuned alternatives.  
- Scalability and computational cost not reported: no runtimes, hardware, or throughput measurements for scoring millions of pairs.  
- Analysis gaps and missing ablations: lacks quantitative source-only vs target-only comparisons, window/pooling/embedding ablations, and quantification of recovered in-domain subsets.  

Questions to authors:
- What validation data and protocol were used to tune selection size and hyperparameters; was there a separate validation set distinct from the dev positives?  
- How were overfitting and early stopping controlled (epochs, batch size, dropout, L2)?  
- Why restrict n-gram baseline to 3-gram Witten-Bell; can you report KN-smoothed 4/5-gram results tuned for small data?  
- Please provide detailed RNNLM configurations (hidden size, epochs, vocab handling, training tricks).  
- Provide ablations for source-only, target-only, and summed scoring across tasks.  
- What is the end-to-end runtime to train/score per language direction, and what hardware (GPU/CPU) was used?  
- Describe negative sampling: class balance, hard-negative mining experiments, and their effects.  
- What corpora/preprocessing were used for pretrained embeddings; how were Arabic/Chinese segmentation and social-media tokens handled?  
- For tasks without embedded in-domain subsets, can you quantify how selected sentences differ from dev (perplexity/topic distance) and provide examples/statistics?  

Additional comment:
- Paper would benefit from fixing minor typos, clarifying references, and releasing selection code/scripts to improve reproducibility.
