Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. A CNN classifier trained with limited labeled in-domain/dev examples and randomly sampled negatives scores general-domain parallel sentences on both source and target sides; top-ranked pairs (by combined score) are used to retrain a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments across four language directions and three social-media-like domains show consistent BLEU gains over using all data and over LM-based selection, with notable robustness down to ~100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical, simple-to-implement data selection pipeline using semi-supervised CNNs that consistently outperforms strong LM-based selection under very low in-domain data regimes.
- A multi-language, multi-domain empirical study demonstrating strong gains (up to +3.1 BLEU) with 2.5%–10% of the data, including stability analyses with extremely small labeled sets.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored regime: adaptation with ≤800 and even ~100 in-domain sentences, where LM-based selection degrades; the proposed SSCNN shows 0.3–0.5 BLEU gains over LM selection at ≥1600 sentences and up to +2.1 BLEU at 100 sentences.
- Broad, convincing evaluation: four translation directions, three social-media domains, large training pools (9–15M), and statistical significance testing; improvements hold consistently and often with fewer selected sentences than LM baselines.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: concrete CNN hyperparameters, public toolkits (conText, word2vec), and clear selection procedure (source/target scoring, combined score).
- Sensible ablation: one-hot CNN vs semi-supervised CNN confirms the benefit of leveraging unlabeled data via embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the contribution is an application of known CNN/embedding techniques to data selection rather than a new modeling idea; architectural choices are standard.
- Potential confound in zh→en sms where an in-domain subset exists inside general data; while SSCNN beats that in-domain-only baseline, it may inflate perceived gains for that task.
- Baseline scope: comparison is only to LM-based selection in phrase-based SMT; no comparison to modern NMT or data weighting/importance sampling methods, limiting contemporary relevance.
- Tuning and selection budget are chosen on held-out data; risks of overfitting threshold/budget are not analyzed, and there is little discussion of computational cost or scalability of scoring entire corpora.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are results to the size/domain of the unlabeled corpus used for word2vec? Have you tried alternative embeddings (e.g., GloVe) or fine-tuning embeddings?
- What is the impact of using only source-side vs only target-side scores? Any analysis of disagreements and their effect?
- How do you prevent leakage/near-duplicate overlap between dev/test and selected training sentences?
- Can the approach transfer to NMT training, and if so, do gains persist relative to NMT domain-adaptation baselines?
