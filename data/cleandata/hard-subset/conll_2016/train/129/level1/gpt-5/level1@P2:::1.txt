Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score and select in-domain-like sentence pairs from large general-domain parallel corpora for SMT adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) are available. The model uses one-hot region representations combined with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on large unlabeled general-domain text; scores are computed separately on source and target sides and combined.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) Demonstration that semi-supervised CNN-based data selection substantially outperforms strong LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM, combination) and “train on all data” baselines across four language directions and three social-media test domains, with up to +3.1 BLEU and consistent significance gains, while using only ~2.5–10% of training data.
  2) Evidence that the approach remains effective in the extreme small in-domain regime (100–800 sentences), with the semi-supervised CNN maintaining clear advantages over LM-based methods and one-hot CNN, and showing low variance.
  3) Practical recipe for selecting data using separate source/target CNNs and summing scores, with reasonable hyperparameter settings and publicly available toolkits.

Strengths
- Strong, multi-setting empirical gains with significance testing: The method beats “alldata” and three LM-based selection baselines in 4 directions (zh↔en, ar→en, en→es) and 3 domains (sms, tweets, Facebook). The reported improvements are non-trivial for SMT (e.g., zh→en sms: 22.9→26.0, +3.1 BLEU, p<0.01) using as little as 300K selected pairs out of 12.2M. Significance is carefully assessed via bootstrap resampling and superiority to the best LM-based baseline is also tested.
- Convincing small-data story: The sample-size study (Figure 1) is well aligned with the paper’s motivation. When in-domain sets are small (<800 sentences), the performance drop of LM-based selection is pronounced, while the semi-supervised CNN remains stable and distinctly better (e.g., with 100 sentences, +2.1 BLEU over “alldata” vs. marginal gains for LM methods). Repeated runs at 100 sentences show tiny variance (stdev 0.12 BLEU), arguing robustness.
- Appropriate baselines and breadth: The paper compares to Moore-Lewis-style n-gram selection, RNNLM selection, and their combination—widely accepted baselines for SMT data selection—and shows that the proposed method either matches or exceeds them in all conditions examined. Phrase-based SMT pipeline choices are standard and strong, reducing the risk that results are artifacts of a weak backbone.
- Clear intuition and plausible mechanism: The explanation for why convolution plus max pooling and pretrained embeddings help (focus on domain-diagnostic n-grams, generalization via semantic proximity in embedding space) is sound and consistent with the literature on text CNNs and domain classification.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations on modeling choices: While the paper compares semi-supervised CNN to a one-hot CNN, it lacks simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM on bag-of-words or character n-grams), which could test whether convolution and/or semi-supervised region embeddings are necessary for the reported gains, especially in the tiny-data regime. Similarly, there is no ablation on the contribution of the source vs. target CNN scores or on region size/pooling choices.
- Potential confound in zh→en sms due to in-domain subset: The zh→en training pool contains a 120K in-domain subset. The method’s large gain (+3.1 BLEU) could partly reflect an easier selection problem compared to other tasks. Although results on the other three tasks are still positive, the paper would be stronger if it quantified how much of the selected data overlaps with the in-domain subset and reported performance when that subset is explicitly removed from the pool.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility gaps: Important training details are missing (optimizer, learning rate, batch size, epochs, regularization/dropout, early stopping, class rebalancing if any). The “held-out” set used to tune the number of selected sentences is not clearly specified; since the dev set is also used as positive labels, this raises a risk of tuning on data used for supervision. Clarifying the tuning protocol is essential. No code or pretrained models are released, which will limit reproducibility despite use of public toolkits.
- Fairness and strength of LM baselines: The n-gram baseline uses 3-grams with Witten-Bell smoothing; stronger setups (e.g., 4- or 5-grams with Kneser-Ney and pruning) are common in selection work. For RNNLM selection, hyperparameters (hidden size, context length, regularization) are not reported, making it hard to judge whether the LM baselines are fully competitive. Given that the claims hinge on surpassing LM selection in the tiny-data regime, stronger baselines would increase confidence.
- Computational considerations not addressed: Scoring both source and target sides of 9–15M sentence pairs with CNNs can be computationally expensive. The paper does not report training/inference time or hardware, which matters for practitioners evaluating the trade-off versus simpler LM scoring.

Questions to authors
- What exact held-out data were used to choose the number of selected sentences? If it was the dev set, how did you avoid overfitting given that dev also served as positive labels? Did you use a split of the dev set or a separate validation set?
- For zh→en, how many of the selected sentences came from the known 120K in-domain subset? What happens if that subset is excluded from the selection pool?
- Could you provide ablations showing: (a) target-only vs. source-only vs. sum-of-scores; (b) semi-supervised embeddings disabled; (c) region sizes other than 5; (d) a simple linear BoW classifier baseline?
- What are the key training hyperparameters for the CNNs (optimizer, epochs, batch size, dropout/regularization), and how sensitive are results to them?
- How long does scoring the full corpus take per language direction, and on what hardware?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important, practical problem—domain adaptation with extremely scarce in-domain data—and presents a simple, well-motivated method with solid empirical gains across multiple settings. The main limitations are missing ablations, some uncertainty around one dataset’s in-domain overlap, and reproducibility details. With clarifications and a couple of targeted experiments, this would be a strong accept; as is, it is a promising submission with meaningful contributions and high potential impact.
