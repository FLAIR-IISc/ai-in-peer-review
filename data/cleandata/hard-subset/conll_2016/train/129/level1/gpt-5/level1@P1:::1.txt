Summary
The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier for SMT data selection when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The model treats a small in-domain dev set as positive examples and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives, learns region-based features via CNNs, and augments supervised training with unsupervised word embeddings (word2vec) learned on the large general corpus. It scores each source and target sentence separately and selects high-scoring sentence pairs (by summing both sides’ scores) to train a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M general-domain sentences show consistent gains over strong baselines (all-data, Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM and combined selection), with up to +3.1 BLEU, and demonstrate robustness when only ~100 in-domain sentences are available (+2.1 BLEU). The approach uses off-the-shelf toolkits and standard SMT pipelines.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective, simple data selection method for SMT using a semi-supervised CNN that leverages unlabeled word embeddings, yielding consistent and sizable gains across four large-scale settings, especially under very low in-domain sample regimes.
2) An empirical study showing that CNN-based selection substantially outperforms LM-based cross-entropy methods when in-domain data is scarce (≤800 sentences), including stability with as few as 100 sentences.
3) Practical insights on bilingual selection (summing source and target scores) and selection sizes that outperform training on all data and even an embedded in-domain subset.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across diverse conditions: The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all baselines on four language directions, with +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms and +1.4 BLEU on ar→en and en→zh. Improvements are statistically significant and achieved with only 2.5–10% of training data, demonstrating both effectiveness and efficiency.
- Robust in low-resource in-domain regimes: With only 100 in-domain sentences for training the selector and a fixed 300k selected pairs, sscnn yields +2.1 BLEU on zh→en, while LM-based methods provide marginal gains. The authors report low variance across repeated samples (std 0.12 BLEU), evidencing stability.
- Sensible bilingual selection strategy: Scoring source and target sides separately and combining scores is a pragmatic, data-driven design for parallel data selection; the paper reports it works best among tried options.
- Scalable and usable: The method uses widely available toolkits (conText CNN, word2vec), standard CNN hyperparameters, and requires only a tiny labeled seed. This lowers barriers to replication and deployment for new domains (e.g., social media) where parallel in-domain data is scarce.
- Contextual justification: The discussion convincingly explains why CNNs with embeddings help—capturing local domain cues via convolution/pooling and sharing semantic information via embeddings (e.g., grouping “Lenovo” with “Dell”).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing discriminative baselines: Using a supervised domain classifier for selection is well-established; the novelty here is primarily the CNN architecture plus embedding augmentation. The paper does not compare against standard strong discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM/Naive Bayes with n-grams, or fastText) that are simple, fast, and competitive for text classification. Without these, it is unclear whether the gains stem from CNN-specific modeling or simply from supervised discriminative selection with embeddings.
- Incomplete ablations: While “ohcnn” vs “sscnn” shows the effect of embeddings, ablations are missing for (i) source-only vs target-only vs sum, (ii) bow vs seq regions, (iii) different window/stride/pooling choices, (iv) using only pre-trained embeddings with a linear classifier. These would clarify which components drive the improvement and how sensitive the method is to design choices.
- Potential bias in zh→en results: The general data pool contains a 120K in-domain subset for zh→en. Although the authors acknowledge this and show sscnn > training on the 120K subset, a more controlled analysis (e.g., excluding that subset from the pool) would better quantify generalization and guard against inadvertently rewarding recovery of embedded in-domain data.
- Fairness and detail of LM baselines: RNNLM and n-gram selection are sensitive to smoothing, vocabulary, and in-domain size. The paper gives minimal hyperparameter and training details; it is unclear whether LM baselines were as optimized as CNNs (e.g., RNNLM architecture, training data/epochs). Also, cross-entropy selection typically benefits from more than a few hundred in-domain lines; the low-resource advantage for CNNs may partly reflect this.
- Reproducibility and preprocessing details: The paper omits specifics on tokenization/segmentation (especially for Chinese), normalization of social media artifacts (emoticons, hashtags), vocabulary sizes, OOV handling in classifiers, and compute/training time. These choices can materially affect results in noisy domains.

Questions to Authors
- Could you compare against simpler discriminative baselines (e.g., multinomial Naive Bayes, logistic regression/SVM with character/word n-grams, or fastText with pre-trained embeddings)? This would help isolate the value of CNNs over generic supervised classifiers.
- Please provide ablations for source-only vs target-only vs sum scoring, and for bow vs seq regions. How much does each contribute? Are results consistent across language pairs?
- How were RNNLMs configured (network size, context, training epochs, vocabulary, smoothing) and were they tuned under the same constraints as CNNs? Did you try LSTM/GRU LMs or subword LMs to mitigate data sparsity in low in-domain regimes?
- Did you evaluate excluding the known 120K in-domain subset from the zh→en general pool to measure performance when no embedded in-domain data exists?
- What are the preprocessing details (tokenization, Chinese segmentation tool, normalization of sms/tweets/Facebook, handling of emojis/URLs/hashtags)? Do such normalizations affect classifier accuracy and selection quality?
- How do training time and memory compare across selection methods (ngram, RNNLM, ohcnn, sscnn), and what are the typical wall-clock costs for training the selector and computing scores over 9–15M sentences?
- How sensitive are results to the number of negatives, class balance, and the strategy for negative sampling (global vs “hard negatives”)? Did more rounds of hard-negative mining ever help?
- Can this approach extend to NMT data selection and to target-side LM data selection (your stated future work)? Any preliminary results?
