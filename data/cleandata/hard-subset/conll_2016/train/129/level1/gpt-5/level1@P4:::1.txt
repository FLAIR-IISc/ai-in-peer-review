Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training sentence pairs for SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. A CNN classifier is trained to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentences using the dev set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives. Two variants are considered: a supervised one-hot CNN and a semi-supervised CNN that augments one-hot inputs with word2vec embeddings trained on unlabeled general-domain data. Source and target sides are scored separately and summed to select top-N bilingual pairs for SMT training. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and three social-media domains show consistent gains over strong baselines (all-data and LM-based selection), including large improvements with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A practical data selection framework for SMT domain adaptation that employs semi-supervised CNN sentence classification, combining one-hot features with pretrained word embeddings and leveraging both source and target-side scores.

2) A comprehensive empirical study across four language directions with significance testing, demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art LM-based selection methods and strong performance when the in-domain sample is extremely small (down to ~100 sentences).

3) Evidence that summing source and target CNN domain-relevance scores yields effective bilingual selection and that the approach remains robust even without explicit in-domain data embedded in the training pool (for three of the four tasks).

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under low-resource in-domain settings. Across four diverse language pairs/domains with 9–15M training pairs, the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms the all-data baseline and three LM-based selection baselines (ngram, rnnlm, combined). Gains include up to +3.1 BLEU (zh→en sms) and consistent +0.7 to +1.4 BLEU on the other tasks; with only 100 in-domain sentences, sscnn still yields +2.1 BLEU on zh→en. Improvements are supported by bootstrap significance tests.

- Technically sound and well-motivated modeling. The use of CNNs to capture local n-gram cues with max pooling aligns with the goal of detecting domain-specific key phrases anywhere in a sentence. Augmenting with pretrained word embeddings addresses sparsity when in-domain data are tiny and enables generalization to unseen but semantically similar words.

- Practicality and simplicity. The method requires only a small dev set as positives and no expensive in-domain corpora. It uses widely available toolkits (conText, word2vec) and standard SMT pipelines, making the approach reproducible in principle and straightforward to deploy.

- Cross-lingual applicability and bilingual scoring. Training monolingual CNNs on both sides and combining scores is sensible for filtering parallel data and appears effective across all evaluated language directions, including morphologically and script-diverse languages.

- Stability analysis for very small in-domain sets. The repeated 100-sentence experiment with small standard deviation (0.12 BLEU) suggests the approach is not overly sensitive to the particular tiny in-domain sample drawn.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised use of pretrained embeddings are established; the primary novelty is the application to SMT data selection. The paper lacks deeper algorithmic contributions (e.g., new architectures, bilingual/joint modeling, training objectives tailored to selection).

- Incomplete experimental detail and potential overfitting concerns. The dev set serves as positive training examples, and the paper states that the number of selected sentences is “determined by performance on held-out data,” but the exact validation protocol is unclear. If the same dev set guided both classifier training and selection-size tuning, selection may be overfit to dev. Details on train/validation splits, early stopping, and regularization (epochs, batch size, dropout, L2) are not provided.

- Baseline strength and fairness. The n-gram baseline is restricted to 3-grams with Witten-Bell smoothing; stronger and commonly used settings (e.g., 4/5-gram Kneser-Ney with tuning for small-data regimes) are not reported. RNNLM baseline configuration (hidden size, training data, vocabulary handling) is under-specified. Additional competitive baselines (e.g., sentence/phrase instance weighting, mixture models tuned to sub-corpora) are not compared.

- Scalability and computational cost are not discussed. Scoring both sides of up to ~15M sentence pairs with CNNs, potentially multiple times during tuning, has nontrivial cost. The paper does not report runtime, hardware, or throughput, leaving open questions about practicality at larger scales or in settings requiring frequent adaptation.

- Analysis gaps and ablations. While the paper states that summing source and target scores is best, it does not provide quantitative comparisons versus source-only and target-only selection. There is no analysis of the impact of window sizes, pooling choices, or embedding domains; nor a study of domain-specific preprocessing (e.g., tokenization/normalization for social media). The impressive zh→en improvement is partly attributable to an in-domain subset within training data; while acknowledged, the paper does not quantify how much of the gain stems from recovering that subset.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Validation protocol: What data were used to tune the number of selected sentences and hyperparameters? Was there a separate validation set distinct from the dev set used as positive examples? How was overfitting controlled (early stopping, regularization)?

- Baselines: Why restrict n-gram to 3-gram with Witten-Bell? Can you report results for stronger KN-smoothed 4/5-gram LMs, tuned for small in-domain data? Please provide RNNLM configuration details (hidden units, training epochs, vocabulary, noise-contrastive/HS, BPTT).

- Source vs target vs sum: Please provide ablations quantifying the performance of source-only, target-only, and summed scores across tasks.

- Computational cost: What is the end-to-end runtime to train the CNN(s) and score the full corpora per language direction (hardware, GPU/CPU usage)? How many epochs and what batch size were used?

- Negative sampling strategy: Did you test class imbalance (more negatives than positives) or hard-negative mining beyond a brief pilot? Any observed effects on classifier quality and final BLEU?

- Pretrained embeddings: What corpora and preprocessing were used per language? For Arabic and Chinese, did you apply segmentation/normalization? For social media, how were hashtags, emojis, and OOV tokens handled?

- Robustness: For tasks without embedded in-domain subsets, can you quantify how far selected sentences are from the dev domain (e.g., via perplexity or topic distance), and provide examples or statistics of selected data?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from cleaning up references and minor typos, and from releasing selection code or detailed scripts to improve reproducibility.
