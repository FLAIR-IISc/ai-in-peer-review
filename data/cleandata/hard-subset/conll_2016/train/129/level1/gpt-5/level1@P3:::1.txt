Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes using convolutional neural networks (CNNs), with and without pre-trained word embeddings (“semi-supervised CNN”), to score general-domain sentence pairs for their in-domain relevance when only tiny in-domain samples (as few as 100 sentences) are available. The scores are computed separately on source and target sides and summed to rank pairs; top-ranked data are used to train phrase-based SMT systems. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) show improvements over training on all data and over strong LM-based data selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination), with gains up to +3.1 BLEU and consistent advantages when in-domain samples are ≤800 sentences.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) Empirical demonstration that CNN-based sentence-level domain classifiers, particularly with pre-trained word embeddings, select markedly better training subsets for SMT than LM-based selection under extremely low in-domain supervision (100–800 sentences), leading to substantial BLEU gains.
2) A practical selection protocol that scores both source and target with separate CNNs and sums scores, providing a simple bilingual signal without bilingual features, and shown to outperform single-side scoring.
3) Evidence that performance scales favorably with decreasing in-domain size and remains stable (low variance) even at 100 in-domain sentences, highlighting feasibility for fine-grained topic/domain adaptation.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results across multiple language pairs and social media domains. The proposed sscnn consistently outperforms three well-established LM-based selection baselines, with statistically significant gains on all four tasks. The zh→en sms case is particularly compelling (+3.1 BLEU vs. alldata; surpasses a 120K “in-domain only” upper bound), strengthening the case that the method can recover high-quality domain data from a large pool.

2) Focus on realistic low-resource in-domain settings. The comparative curves vs. in-domain size (down to 100 sentences) are valuable and relatively rare; they convincingly show sscnn’s robustness and stability where LM-based methods degrade or plateau. The repeated trials at 100 sentences and low variance support practical reliability.

3) Methodologically grounded and leveraging relevant prior NLP work. The model design follows Johnson & Zhang’s region-embedding paradigm, with both one-hot and embedding channels, and applies it coherently to MT data selection. The paper situates contributions against a thorough backdrop of SMT adaptation literature (Moore-Lewis, Axelrod et al., Duh et al., mixture/instance weighting), clarifying the gap it addresses.

4) Sensible, reproducible settings and comparisons. Baselines are standard and reasonably strong; the SMT pipeline is conventional; the CNN toolkit and word2vec setup are provided; significance testing is reported.

Weaknesses
1) Limited ablation and diagnostic analysis of the selection model. The paper states that both seq and bow regions are fed into CNN and that summing source and target scores works best, but it does not quantify contributions of each design choice. Missing ablations include: one-hot vs. embeddings (beyond the top-line ohcnn vs. sscnn), seq vs. bow, single-side vs. sum, and different region sizes/strides. Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains precisely or guide practitioners.

2) Possible issues of data usage and validation protocol. The in-domain “dev” set is used as positive training for the classifier; it is also typically used for SMT tuning. The paper mentions choosing the number of selected sentences “based on held-out data”/“separate validation set,” but it’s unclear whether this is distinct from the dev used to train the classifier and to tune SMT. Any double use of dev for selection/tuning could bias results; clearer separation (e.g., splitting dev into classifier-train and SMT-tune, or using a second dev) should be documented.

3) Missing comparisons to strong non-neural discriminative classifiers for domain selection. Given the small labeled data regime, SVM/logistic regression with character/word n-grams or tf-idf cosine similarity could be competitive and computationally lighter. The absence of these non-LM baselines leaves open whether the benefit stems from CNNs per se or simply from a stronger discriminative classifier than LM-based cross-entropy.

4) Computational cost and scalability not discussed. Scoring 9–15M sentences on both source and target with CNNs can be expensive. The paper does not report training/inference time or resource usage vs. LM-based methods. For practical deployment, especially for dynamic topic adaptation, such costs matter.

5) Limited analysis of selected data quality. The zh→en condition contains a known 120K in-domain subset: this provides an excellent opportunity to quantify precision/recall of selection against a gold partition and to examine lexical/topical properties of selected data. Such diagnostics would strengthen the claim that CNNs better capture domain signals than LM-based selection.

Questions to Authors
- Please detail the validation protocol: Which data were used to (a) train the CNN classifier, (b) choose the number of selected sentences, and (c) tune the SMT system? Were there strict splits to avoid using the same sentences for multiple roles?
- Can you provide ablations for: seq vs. bow regions; one-hot only vs. embeddings only vs. both; source-only vs. target-only vs. sum; and different region sizes? Which choices matter most?
- How much training and inference time did ohcnn/sscnn require per language direction, and on what hardware? How does this compare to LM-based selection pipelines?
- Did you compare against a linear SVM/logistic regression over bag-of-ngrams or tf-idf cosine ranking? If tried, how did they perform in the 100–800 sentence regime?
- In the zh→en task, what fraction of the known 120K in-domain subset is retrieved by your top-N selection at N=300K? Do CNNs achieve higher precision than LM-based methods?

Overall, this is a timely and practically meaningful contribution with solid empirical gains in a challenging setting. Clarifying the validation protocol and adding targeted ablations/diagnostics would significantly strengthen the paper.
