- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier for SMT parallel data selection using a tiny in-domain dev set as positives, random negatives, region-based CNN features, and unsupervised word2vec embeddings.  
- Scores source and target sentences separately and selects parallel pairs by summing both sides’ scores to train phrase-based SMT.  
- Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M general sentences show consistent BLEU gains (up to +3.1) and robustness with only ~100 in-domain sentences.  
- Contribution: a simple, effective data selection method leveraging CNNs plus embeddings that yields consistent gains, especially in very low in-domain regimes.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that CNN-based selection outperforms LM-based cross-entropy methods when in-domain data is scarce (≤800 sentences), remaining stable down to ~100 sentences.  
- Contribution: practical bilingual selection insight—scoring each side separately and summing works best—and guidance on selection sizes that can beat training on all data.  
- Strength: strong, statistically significant empirical gains across diverse conditions while using only 2.5–10% of training data.  
- Strength: robust performance and low variance in extremely low-resource in-domain settings (e.g., +2.1 BLEU with 100 sentences).  
- Strength: pragmatic bilingual scoring strategy (source+target) validated empirically.  
- Strength: scalable and replicable — uses off-the-shelf CNN and word2vec toolkits and standard SMT pipelines.  
- Strength: convincing motivation that CNNs with embeddings capture local domain cues and shared semantics (example: brand grouping).  
- Weakness: limited novelty—approach is primarily an application of supervised classifiers (CNN + embeddings) to selection and lacks comparison to standard discriminative baselines.  
- Weakness: incomplete ablative analysis—missing comparisons for source-only/target-only vs sum, bow vs seq regions, hyperparameter choices, and linear classifiers with pretrained embeddings.  
- Weakness: potential bias in zh→en results because a 120K in-domain subset exists in the general pool; controlled exclusion analysis is missing.  
- Weakness: LM baselines (RNNLM, n-gram) lack hyperparameter/training detail, raising fairness questions about baseline optimization.  
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—missing preprocessing/tokenization details (esp. Chinese), social-media normalization, vocabulary/OOV handling, and compute/training time.  
- Question: compare to simpler discriminative baselines (Naive Bayes, logistic/SVM with n-grams, fastText with pretrained embeddings) to isolate CNN’s benefit?  
- Question: provide ablative results for source-only vs target-only vs sum scoring and bow vs seq region choices; are effects consistent across language pairs?  
- Question: how were RNNLMs configured/tuned (architecture, vocab, epochs)? Were LSTM/GRU or subword LMs tried for low in-domain regimes?  
- Question: did you test excluding the 120K in-domain subset from the zh→en general pool to measure true generalization?  
- Question: what preprocessing was applied (tokenization, Chinese segmentation, normalization of emojis/hashtags/URLs) and how sensitive are results to these choices?  
- Question: how do training time and memory compare across selection methods, and what are typical wall-clock costs to score 9–15M sentences?  
- Question: how sensitive are results to number of negatives, class balance, negative sampling strategy, and did hard-negative mining help?  
- Question: can the method extend to NMT data selection or target-side LM data selection, and are there any preliminary results?
