- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier that uses a tiny in-domain parallel seed and pretrained word embeddings to select relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus for SMT adaptation.  
- Shows the method outperforms using all data and three state-of-the-art n-gram and RNNLM-based selection methods, across four language pairs and three social-media domains.  
- High impact and relevance: addresses practical SMT domain adaptation when only extremely small in-domain corpora (as few as 100 sentences) are available.  
- Strong empirical results: consistent, statistically significant BLEU improvements (e.g., up to +3.1 over all-data baseline) across languages and domains.  
- Excellent low-data analysis: robust performance shown as seed size decreases, including stable 2.1 BLEU gain with only 100 in-domain sentences and stability across random seeds.  
- Clear and sound presentation: methods and experimental setup are well explained and compare against appropriate strong baselines.  
- Missing computational-cost analysis: no runtime, selection-time, or hardware comparisons between the CNN scoring and much faster LM-based methods.  
- Limited qualitative analysis: paper lacks concrete examples of sentences selected by the CNN but not by LM methods to illustrate what domain characteristics the CNN captures.  
- Fixed architecture and training choices: no exploration of simpler classifiers, hard negative mining, or alternative source/target score combination strategies to justify the CNN and design decisions.  
- Question: please report computational overhead (runtimes, hardware) for scoring the full general-domain corpus with the CNN vs. LM baselines.  
- Question: have you tried alternative ways to combine source and target scores (min, max, geometric mean) and is there evidence the sum is optimal?  
- Question: please provide qualitative examples (e.g., in the 100-sentence scenario) of instances selected by sscnn but missed by rnnlm to illustrate practical differences.
