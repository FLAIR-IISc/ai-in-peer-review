**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a setting where only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select relevant training sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The model is trained on the small in-domain set (positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negative examples). The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from leveraging pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) on the entire general-domain corpus, which helps the classifier generalize from the sparse labeled data. This trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentences are used to train the final SMT system. The authors conduct experiments on four language pairs with social media test domains, showing significant BLEU improvements over strong baselines, especially when the in-domain seed data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences).

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  A compelling empirical demonstration that a classification-based approach using semi-supervised CNNs is a highly effective method for SMT data selection, particularly in low-resource domain adaptation scenarios. The paper's key finding is that this method is significantly more robust than perplexity-based language model methods when the amount of in-domain seed data is minimal.
2.  A successful and well-executed application of established text classification techniques (CNNs with pre-trained embeddings) to a practical and important problem in SMT. While the neural architecture itself is not novel, its application here is sound, thoroughly evaluated, and yields state-of-the-art results for this specific task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Under-Explored Problem:** The core focus on adaptation with a "tiny amount" of in-domain data is highly relevant. Much of the prior work on data selection assumes the availability of thousands of in-domain sentences. This paper tackles the much more common and challenging real-world scenario where one might only have a small development set or a handful of example translations. The experiments showing strong performance with as few as 100 sentences (Figure 1) directly address this important gap.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a convincing case through extensive experiments.
    *   The use of four different language pairs and three challenging, contemporary domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook) demonstrates the general applicability of the method.
    *   The baselines are strong and represent the standard state-of-the-art in data selection (ngram LM, RNNLM). Outperforming them, especially the RNNLM which also uses neural methods, is a significant achievement.
    *   The results in Table 2 are impressive, showing consistent and often statistically significant gains. The +3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh-en task is particularly noteworthy.
    *   The analysis in Figure 1 is the paper's strongest component. It clearly visualizes the robustness of the CNN-based methods compared to LM-based ones as the seed data shrinks, providing powerful evidence for the central claim.

3.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Described Methodology:** The paper provides clear motivation for its architectural choices. The authors correctly argue that CNNs are well-suited for identifying key domain-specific phrases regardless of their position, and that leveraging pre-trained embeddings is essential to overcome the data sparsity of the in-domain set. The overall pipeline—train a classifier, score the corpus, select top-N—is straightforward, easy to understand, and appears to be reproducible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core technical components are off-the-shelf. The CNN architecture is a standard one for text classification (e.g., Kim, 2014), and the semi-supervised approach of incorporating pre-trained word embeddings is also well-established (e.g., Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), whom the authors cite. The contribution lies entirely in the novel application of these techniques to SMT data selection and the resulting empirical findings, not in the development of new neural network architectures or learning paradigms. This should be acknowledged as it frames the paper as a strong application paper rather than a core methodology paper.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Why CNNs Outperform RNNLMs:** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides some intuition but lacks depth. Both the proposed `sscnn` model and the `rnnlm` baseline can leverage pre-trained word embeddings and are powerful sequence models. The paper does not offer a deep analysis of why the explicit binary classification objective of the CNN is superior to the generative, perplexity-based scoring of the RNNLM for this task. An RNNLM implicitly learns what constitutes "in-domain" language by assigning higher probability to it. A qualitative analysis showing examples of sentences that are correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the RNNLM (and vice-versa) would have provided much stronger insight into the mechanisms behind the performance difference.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Considerations (Efficiency/Cost):** The paper proposes a practical method but omits any discussion of its computational cost relative to the baselines. Training a CNN, even on a small dataset, and then using it to score millions of sentence pairs on both the source and target side is non-trivial. How does the wall-clock time for the entire selection process compare to training an n-gram LM or an RNNLM and scoring the corpus? For a method intended for practical application, this is an important detail that is currently missing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the fundamental advantage of the discriminative classification approach over the generative scoring of the RNNLM? Given that both models can capture complex linguistic patterns and leverage word embeddings, is the key advantage simply the directness of the binary classification objective for this filtering task? A brief error analysis or a few illustrative examples would be very helpful.

2.  Could you provide an estimate of the computational costs (e.g., time to train the selection model and time to score the 12M sentence general-domain corpus) for your `sscnn` method compared to the `rnnlm` baseline? This would help practitioners assess the trade-offs involved.

3.  The negative examples for training the CNN are chosen via random sampling. Have you considered more sophisticated negative sampling strategies, such as hard negative mining (i.e., selecting out-of-domain sentences that the current classifier finds most difficult to distinguish from in-domain sentences)? Do you believe this could lead to a stronger classifier and further improvements?
