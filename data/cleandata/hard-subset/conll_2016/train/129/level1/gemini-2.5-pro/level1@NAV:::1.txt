**Summary:** This paper proposes a data selection method for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, designed for scenarios with very limited in-domain data. The approach reframes data selection as a text classification task, training a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to identify in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The model is trained on a small set of labeled in-domain sentences and leverages pre-trained word embeddings from unlabeled data. The resulting classifier scores the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are used to train an adapted SMT system.

**Contributions:**
1. The primary contribution is a strong empirical validation of a semi-supervised classification approach for SMT data selection. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that this method significantly outperforms established language model-based techniques, particularly in extremely low-resource settings (e.g., with only 100 in-domain sentences).
2. A secondary contribution is the successful application of semi-supervised CNNs to this specific task, highlighting the benefits of pre-trained word embeddings for overcoming data sparsity in domain classification for MT.

**Strengths:**
1. The method achieves substantial and statistically significant BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 points) over strong baselines across four different language pairs and three distinct social media domains. The results are compelling and consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed `sscnn` model.
2. The work addresses the critical and practical problem of domain adaptation with a "tiny" amount of seed data. The analysis in Figure 1 clearly shows the proposed model’s superior robustness and stability compared to LM-based methods as the amount of in-domain data shrinks, which is a key selling point for real-world applications.
3. The evaluation is thorough, including comparisons to multiple state-of-the-art baselines, significance testing, and an analysis of the model's performance curve with varying amounts of seed data. This rigorous approach lends strong credibility to the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The technical novelty is somewhat limited. The use of semi-supervised CNNs for text classification is a well-established technique. The main contribution is its novel application and verification in the context of SMT data selection, not the method itself.
2. The experiments are conducted exclusively on phrase-based SMT. As the field has largely shifted to Neural Machine Translation (NMT), the paper’s impact is limited by the absence of experiments showing whether these significant gains transfer to NMT systems, which have different sensitivities to training data.
3. The paper provides few details on hyperparameter tuning for the CNN model. The optimal size of the selected data is determined empirically for each task, making the method less straightforward to apply without a dedicated tuning process.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. What is the computational overhead of your CNN-based scoring approach compared to the LM-based methods, especially when applied to large general-domain corpora?
2. You combine source and target side scores for final selection. Could you elaborate on the performance impact of using only source-side or target-side scores?
