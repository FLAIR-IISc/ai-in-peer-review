- Problem & approach: Frame SMT data selection with tiny in-domain parallel data as a binary classification task using a semi-supervised CNN that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to score and select general-domain sentence pairs.  
- Pipeline: Train CNN on a small in-domain positive set and sampled general-domain negatives, score a large pool, and select top pairs to adapt SMT.  
- Empirical result: Method yields substantial BLEU gains (up to ~3.1) across four language pairs and three social-media domains, especially with only a few hundred in-domain sentences.  

- Contribution 1: Proposes a novel, well-motivated method specifically for ultra-low-resource domain adaptation (100–1000 sentences).  
- Contribution 2: Provides strong empirical validation showing consistent, significant improvements over n-gram and RNNLM-based selection baselines.  

- Strength 1: High practical relevance—enables domain adaptation with minimal annotation in realistic, challenging domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook).  
- Strength 2: Technically sound—CNN captures local domain phrases; pre-trained embeddings mitigate data sparsity and enable generalization to related words.  
- Strength 3: Thorough experiments and stability analysis demonstrate robustness even with as few as 100 labeled sentences.  

- Weakness 1: Missing architectural/implementation details (how wl(x) and ul(x) are constructed and combined), hurting reproducibility.  
- Weakness 2: Limited analysis of why the CNN outperforms LM methods; lacks qualitative examples or visualization of learned features.  
- Weakness 3: No comparison to simpler classifiers (e.g., logistic regression or SVM with bag-of-words), so unclear how much CNN architecture matters.  
- Weakness 4: Sensitivity to the number of selected sentence pairs is not analyzed; no plot showing MT performance vs. selected data size.  

- Question 1: Please clarify the CNN architecture—how wl(x) and ul(x) segment vectors are formed and combined for convolution (Eq. 2).  
- Question 2: Did you try other source/target score combination methods (product, min, max) or a single classifier on concatenated source+target?  
- Question 3: Why do you believe RNNLMs are more brittle than the sscnn in very low-data settings despite using continuous representations?
