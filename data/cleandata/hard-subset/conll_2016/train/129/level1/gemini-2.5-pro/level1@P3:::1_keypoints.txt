- Problem & approach: Domain adaptation for SMT with very small in-domain parallel data using a semi-supervised CNN classifier (pre-trained embeddings) to select relevant sentences from a large general corpus.

- Contribution 1: Empirically shows classification-based semi-supervised CNN selection is highly effective and more robust than perplexity-based LM methods when seed data is minimal.

- Contribution 2: Solid application of standard CNN + pre-trained embeddings to SMT data selection that yields state-of-the-art empirical results, though the architecture itself is not novel.

- Strength: Tackles a critical, under-explored real-world scenario (works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences).

- Strength: Strong, comprehensive experiments across four language pairs and three social-media domains with strong baselines and significant BLEU gains (e.g., +3.1 zh-en).

- Strength: Well-motivated, clearly described, and reproducible pipeline (train classifier → score corpus → select top-N).

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—the work mainly applies existing models rather than introducing new architectures or learning paradigms.

- Weakness: Lacks deep analysis explaining why the CNN outperforms the RNNLM; missing qualitative error analysis or illustrative sentence-level comparisons.

- Weakness: No discussion of practical efficiency/cost (training and scoring time) relative to n-gram and RNNLM baselines.

- Question: Please clarify the fundamental advantage of the discriminative CNN objective over generative RNNLM scoring, ideally with error analysis or examples.

- Question: Provide estimates of computational cost (training and scoring the ~12M sentence corpus) for the sscnn versus the rnnlm.

- Question: Have you tried more sophisticated negative sampling (e.g., hard negative mining), and might that improve classifier quality and selection performance?
