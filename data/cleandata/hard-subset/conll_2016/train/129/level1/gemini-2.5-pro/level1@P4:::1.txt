**SUMMARY AND CONTRIBUTIONS**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The classifier is trained using the small in-domain dataset as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from initializing the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned from a large unlabeled corpus, which helps to overcome the data scarcity of the labeled (in-domain) set. The trained CNN then assigns a domain-relevance score to every sentence pair in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring pairs are used to train the final SMT system. The method is evaluated on four language pairs across three social media domains, showing significant improvements over strong baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for the task of data selection for SMT adaptation. The method is particularly well-suited for and demonstrates strong performance in extremely low-resource scenarios, where as few as 100 in-domain sentences are available.
2.  A thorough empirical evaluation showing that the proposed data selection method significantly outperforms not only a baseline using all available data but also three state-of-the-art data selection methods based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models.

**STRENGTHS**

1.  **High Impact and Relevance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and practical problem. Domain adaptation is crucial for SMT performance, but obtaining large, matched in-domain parallel corpora is often prohibitively expensive or impossible. The specific focus on adaptation with a "tiny amount" of in-domain data makes the work highly relevant for practical applications, such as adapting to emerging topics or user-specific content on social media.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing. The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method consistently outperforms all baselines across four different language directions and three distinct, challenging domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The reported BLEU score improvements are substantial (e.g., up to +3.1 over the `alldata` baseline and consistently significant gains over the best LM-based methods). The use of significance testing adds statistical rigor to these claims.

3.  **Excellent Analysis of the Low-Data Regime:** A key strength is the experiment that directly evaluates the method's performance as the amount of in-domain seed data is reduced (Figure 1). This analysis provides compelling evidence for the paper's central claim: the proposed method is more robust to data scarcity than existing LM-based approaches. Demonstrating a 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is a powerful and impressive result. The authors further strengthen this finding by showing the result is stable across different random samples of the 100 sentences.

4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained well, building logically on existing work in CNNs for text classification. The experimental setup is sound, employing a standard phrase-based SMT pipeline and comparing against appropriate and strong baselines from the literature.

**WEAKNESSES**

1.  **Lack of Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baselines. Training a CNN and, more importantly, using it to score millions of sentences in a large general-domain corpus can be computationally expensive. In contrast, scoring with n-gram language models is typically very fast. A discussion of the trade-offs in terms of selection time, training time, and hardware requirements (e.g., CPU vs. GPU) would be valuable for assessing the practical applicability of the method.

2.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the selected data. The discussion in Section 5.4 offers plausible hypotheses for the method's success but does not provide concrete evidence. Including a few examples of sentences that were highly ranked by the `sscnn` model but not by the LM-based methods could provide valuable insight into what kind of domain similarity the CNN is capturing (e.g., lexical, syntactic, or semantic). This would help to move the explanation from speculation to evidence-based analysis.

3.  **Fixed Model Architecture and Training choices:** The paper adopts a specific CNN architecture and training regimen from prior work. There is no exploration of whether a simpler classifier (e.g., logistic regression or an SVM using averaged word embeddings as features) could achieve similar results, which would help to justify the choice of a CNN. Furthermore, the strategy for selecting negative samples (random sampling) is simple; it is unclear if more sophisticated strategies, such as hard negative mining, could further improve the classifier's performance. Finally, the source and target scores are combined via a simple sum, and it is not clear if this is optimal.

**QUESTIONS TO AUTHORS**

1.  Can you provide a comparison of the computational overhead for the data selection step (i.e., scoring the entire general-domain corpus) between your CNN-based methods and the LM-based baselines? Specifically, what are the approximate runtimes and hardware requirements?

2.  The source and target scores are combined using a simple sum. Have you investigated other combination functions, such as taking the minimum, maximum, or a geometric mean? Is there a reason to believe the simple sum is the most effective approach?

3.  Could you provide a few qualitative examples from your experiments (e.g., in the 100-sentence training scenario) of sentences that were correctly identified as in-domain by `sscnn` but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline? This would help to better understand the practical differences between the selection criteria learned by the two models.
