### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose framing data selection as a binary classification task and using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to perform this classification. The model is trained on the tiny in-domain set (as positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain data (as negative examples). The semi-supervised aspect comes from incorporating pre-trained word embeddings learned on large unlabeled corpora, which helps combat the data sparsity of the small labeled set. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in a large general-domain pool, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. Experiments on four language pairs and three social media domains show that this method significantly outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines, especially when the in-domain data is limited to just a few hundred sentences.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel and Effective Method for Low-Resource Adaptation:** The primary contribution is a new method for SMT data selection that is specifically designed for and highly effective in scenarios with a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., 100-1000 sentences). Framing data selection as a classification problem and using a semi-supervised CNN to leverage pre-trained word embeddings is a novel and well-motivated approach to this challenging task.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence across four diverse language pairs that the proposed method significantly outperforms established state-of-the-art data selection techniques based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models. The gains are substantial (up to 3.1 BLEU) and are shown to be most pronounced in the targeted low-resource setting.

### Strengths
1.  **High Practical Relevance and Impact:** The paper tackles a critical and practical problem in machine translation. The ability to adapt an SMT system to a new, specific domain using only a handful of examples (like a small development set) is extremely valuable. This makes fine-grained, topic-specific adaptation feasible where it was previously impractical due to the high cost of collecting thousands of in-domain sentences. The chosen test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) reflect modern, challenging use cases where this problem is particularly acute.
2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed method is clever and well-reasoned. Using a CNN is motivated by its ability to identify important local features (key domain phrases) irrespective of their position. The semi-supervised component is crucial; using pre-trained word embeddings provides a robust representation that mitigates the severe data sparsity of the tiny in-domain set and allows the model to generalize to related but unseen words (e.g., "Lenovo" vs. "Dell" as discussed in Section 5.4). This combination is what makes the model so effective in the low-data regime.
3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experimental Results:** The evaluation is thorough and strongly supports the paper's claims. The authors test on four language pairs and show consistent, significant improvements over multiple strong baselines. The most compelling evidence is presented in Figure 1, which clearly demonstrates that while all methods degrade as the in-domain training data shrinks, the proposed `sscnn` method degrades far more gracefully and maintains a significant advantage over LM-based approaches, even with as few as 100 labeled sentences. The stability analysis, showing a low standard deviation across different random samples of 100 sentences, further strengthens the reliability of the findings.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Architectural and Implementation Details:** The paper's description of the CNN model in Section 3 is very high-level and relies heavily on citations to external work and toolkits. Key details that are essential for reproducibility are missing. For instance, it is unclear how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and embedding-based vectors (`ul(x)`) for a "segment" are constructed and combined. Are they concatenated? Are they bag-of-words representations of the segment? Equation (2) suggests two separate weight matrices, which is an interesting architectural choice that warrants more explanation. A more detailed description or a small diagram of the network architecture would significantly improve the paper's clarity and self-containedness.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Model Behavior:** While the BLEU scores are impressive, the paper offers limited insight into *why* the CNN-based selection works so much better than the LM-based methods. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but speculative. The paper would be much stronger with a more concrete analysis. For example, presenting a few examples of sentences that are correctly selected by `sscnn` but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline (or vice-versa) could provide valuable intuition. A qualitative analysis of the features learned by the CNN filters could also help explain what domain-specific signals the model is capturing that an LM's perplexity score does not.
3.  **Missing Comparison to Simpler Classification Models:** The paper compares the CNN against LM-based scoring methods but not against simpler, more traditional classification models. It would be useful to include a baseline like a logistic regression or an SVM classifier using bag-of-words or n-gram features. This comparison would help disentangle the benefits of the classification framework itself from the specific advantages of the more complex CNN architecture. It is possible that a simpler classifier could achieve a large portion of the gains, which would be an important finding.
4.  **Sensitivity to the Amount of Selected Data:** The number of sentence pairs selected for SMT training is a critical hyperparameter that is "empirically determine[d]... based on experimental results on a separate validation set." However, the paper does not show how sensitive the final MT performance is to this choice. A graph showing the validation set BLEU score as a function of the number of selected sentences would be highly informative for practitioners seeking to apply this method, as it would reveal whether performance is stable across a wide range of selected data sizes or if it requires careful tuning.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide more detail on the CNN architecture? Specifically, how are the one-hot segment vectors `wl(x)` and the embedding-based segment vectors `ul(x)` formed from the input text, and how are they combined as input to the convolution layer as described in Equation (2)?
2.  You mention that selecting based on the sum of source and target scores was best. Did you experiment with other combination strategies (e.g., product, min, max)? Furthermore, have you considered a single classification model that takes the concatenated source and target sentences as a single input sequence?
3.  The performance gap between your `sscnn` model and the LM-based methods widens dramatically as the in-domain seed set shrinks (Figure 1). Your discussion suggests this is due to the generalization power of word embeddings. Could you elaborate on why you believe LMs, particularly RNNLMs which also use continuous representations, are so much more brittle in this low-data scenario?
