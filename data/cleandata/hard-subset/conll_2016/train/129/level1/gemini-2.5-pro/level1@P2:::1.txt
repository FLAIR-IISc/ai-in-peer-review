### **Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, specifically targeting the challenging scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors frame data selection as a binary classification task: identifying whether a sentence from a large general-domain corpus belongs to the target domain. They employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this task. The classifier is trained using the tiny in-domain dataset as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from leveraging pre-trained word embeddings (learned on large unlabeled corpora) as input features to the CNN, which helps the model generalize from the limited labeled data. After training, this classifier is used to score every sentence pair in the general-domain corpus. The top-scoring pairs are then selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system. The method is evaluated on four language directions against strong baselines, demonstrating significant improvements in translation quality.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **An effective classification-based framework for data selection in a low-resource setting:** The primary contribution is successfully demonstrating that a semi-supervised CNN classifier can be a powerful tool for SMT data selection. This approach is shown to be particularly robust and effective in the practical but difficult scenario where in-domain seed data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences), a setting where traditional language model-based methods are known to falter.
2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation:** The paper provides a rigorous evaluation across four language pairs and three distinct social media domains. The proposed method is benchmarked against four strong baselines, including an "all data" system and three state-of-the-art language model-based selection methods. The consistent and statistically significant improvements across all tasks strongly validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experimental results are the paper's strongest asset. The semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method consistently outperforms all baselines on all four translation tasks, with BLEU gains as high as 3.1 points over a strong baseline trained on all available data. Crucially, it also shows significant improvements over well-established data selection methods like the RNNLM-based approach of Duh et al. (2013). This provides convincing evidence of the method's superiority.
2.  **Addresses a Highly Relevant and Practical Problem:** The ability to adapt an MT system with only a "tiny" amount of in-domain data (e.g., a small development set) is extremely valuable. Many real-world applications involve emerging domains or fine-grained topics where collecting large-scale in-domain parallel corpora is infeasible. This work offers a practical and high-performing solution to this common problem.
3.  **Excellent Analysis of the Low-Data Regime:** The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which shows system performance as the amount of in-domain seed data is reduced from 6,000 to 100 sentences, is a key strength. It clearly illustrates that while all methods suffer with less data, the CNN-based approaches, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more robust. This analysis directly supports the central claim that the method is uniquely suited for low-resource adaptation. The stability check (repeating the 100-sentence experiment) further bolsters this finding.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the proposed method is easy to understand. The authors build upon existing work and utilize publicly available toolkits for the CNN, word embeddings, and even the baseline systems they compare against. This transparency greatly aids in the potential reproducibility of their findings.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works but offers little insight into *why* it works better than the baselines. A qualitative analysis of the selected data would be highly beneficial. For example, showing a few sentences that are highly ranked by the CNN but lowly ranked by the LM-based methods (and vice-versa) could illuminate the different types of domain-relevant information each model is capturing. Is the CNN better at identifying topical keywords, stylistic features, or something else?
2.  **Computational Cost Is Not Discussed:** The proposed method requires scoring every sentence in a multi-million-sentence general-domain corpus using a CNN forward pass. This is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than scoring with a pre-computed n-gram language model. While RNNLM-based scoring is also non-trivial, a discussion of the computational trade-offs (training time for the selector, scoring time for the corpus) is missing. This information would be critical for anyone looking to apply this method in practice.
3.  **Focus on Phrase-Based SMT:** The evaluation is performed exclusively on phrase-based SMT systems. By 2016, Neural Machine Translation (NMT) was rapidly becoming the dominant paradigm. While the comparison to SMT baselines is fair, the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion on how this data selection technique might apply to NMT. For instance, how would this approach compare to the standard NMT adaptation technique of fine-tuning on the small in-domain data? This limits the paper's forward-looking impact.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis comparing sentences selected by `sscnn` versus `rnnlm`? Specifically, can you show examples of sentences where the models strongly disagree on the domain-relevance score, and offer some intuition as to what linguistic phenomena might be causing this divergence?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational requirements of your method? In particular, what was the approximate time taken to score the full general-domain corpora (e.g., the 12.2M sentence zh-en corpus) with the trained `sscnn` model, and how does this compare to the time taken by the `rnnlm` baseline?
3.  Given the rise of NMT, how do you see this data selection method fitting into an NMT adaptation workflow? Do you believe selecting a subset of data for continued training or fine-tuning would be more effective than simply fine-tuning on the small in-domain set alone, and why?
