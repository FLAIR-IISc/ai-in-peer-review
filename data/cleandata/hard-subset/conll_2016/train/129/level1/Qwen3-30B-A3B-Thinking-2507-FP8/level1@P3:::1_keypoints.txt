- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN framework for SMT domain adaptation that needs as few as 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness across four language pairs and three social-media domains, outperforming LM-based methods.  
- Shows up to 3.1 BLEU improvement using only 3% of training data and strong gains with just 100 sentences.  
- Tackles a practical real-world problem of adapting MT to new domains with minimal in-domain data.  
- Presents comprehensive, convincing empirical results, including a compelling ablation showing benefits with 100 sentences.  
- Provides a well-motivated technical rationale for using CNNs and semi-supervised learning with embeddings.  
- Clearly compares against three LM-based baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) and shows consistent advantages in low-resource settings.  
- Integrates smoothly with existing SMT pipelines and includes implementation details facilitating replication.  
- Does not compare CNNs to other text classification models (RNNs, transformers, TF-IDF+SVM), leaving the best-architecture question open.  
- Assumes availability of dev set as in-domain data, which may be unrealistic in many deployment scenarios.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis of the selected sentences to explain what domain-relevant features the CNN captures.  
- Omits analysis of computational cost (training time, memory) relative to LM baselines, hindering practical assessment.  
- Does not discuss failure modes or scenarios where the method may perform poorly (e.g., unrepresentative in-domain data).  
- Question: How would the method perform with no in-domain data available before training?  
- Question: Can the authors compare CNNs to RNN- or transformer-based classifiers in an ablation study?  
- Question: What are the computational costs (training time, memory) versus LM baselines, and are gains worth the cost?  
- Question: Can the authors provide qualitative examples of selected sentences and explain what CNN-detected features make them domain-relevant?
