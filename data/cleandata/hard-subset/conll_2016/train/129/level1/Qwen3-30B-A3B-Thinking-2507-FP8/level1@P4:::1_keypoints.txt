1. Semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection that works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences for fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation.
2. Method outperforms LM-based selection baselines, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement when in-domain data is scarce.
3. Empirical validation across four language directions and three domains, showing robustness even with extremely limited in-domain data.
4. Addresses a practical real-world challenge by enabling domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data (practical relevance).
5. Comprehensive, rigorous evaluation covering multiple languages, domains, strong baselines, ablations, and statistical tests.
6. Technically sound justification for using CNNs and semi-supervised word embeddings drawn from large general-domain corpora.
7. Demonstrates scalability and stability with repeated 100-sentence experiments (avg BLEU 25.03, sd 0.12).
8. Practical to implement within standard SMT pipelines using existing CNN toolkits.
9. Limited novelty: the CNN architecture and semi-supervised embeddings are largely established; novelty is mainly in the application.
10. Inadequate comparisons to recent small-data approaches (e.g., transfer learning, meta-learning) that could be stronger baselines.
11. Insufficient analysis explaining why semi-supervised CNNs outperform one-hot CNNs beyond the observed ~0.5 BLEU gain.
12. Lacks discussion of limitations under strong domain shift when general-domain embeddings may be unrepresentative.
13. Missing implementation specifics (exact CNN hyperparameters, negative sampling criteria, word2vec parameters) hindering reproducibility.
14. Request for exact CNN hyperparameters (layers, filter sizes, dropout rates) to enable replication.
15. Question about performance when general-domain data is unrepresentative of the test domain (e.g., news vs. medical).
16. Clarification needed on how the general-domain data for embeddings was selected and whether it matched SMT training data.
17. Ask why summing source and target scores worked best for selection and whether this generalizes beyond translation.
18. Request elaboration on challenges and details for applying the method to selecting from large target-language corpora.
