

# Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors train a CNN classifier using a small set of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) and general-domain data to select domain-relevant sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The selected sentences are then used to train an adapted SMT system. The main contributions are: 1) a novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in SMT adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data; 2) empirical demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms existing language model-based methods; and 3) evidence that the method works effectively with as little as 100 in-domain sentences.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful real-world problem**: The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in the literature: domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors provide a compelling motivation by noting that social media domains (e.g., SMS, tweets) often have very limited parallel data, making traditional adaptation methods impractical. This is a genuine problem with practical relevance in modern NLP applications.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines (up to 3.1 BLEU points) across four language directions and multiple test domains. The results are statistically significant, and the method consistently outperforms all baselines, especially when in-domain data is limited. The paper also shows that the approach can achieve substantial gains (2.1 BLEU) with only 100 in-domain sentences, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

3. **Technical soundness and practicality**: The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of how word embeddings from general-domain data help the model generalize. The method is practical to implement and can be applied to real-world translation scenarios where in-domain data is scarce. The authors also provide a good discussion of why CNNs work well for this task, particularly regarding the ability to capture domain-specific features through convolution and pooling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technique**: While the application to data selection is novel, the CNN architecture itself (one-hot and semi-supervised) is not particularly novel. The authors build directly on Johnson and Zhang (2015), and the main contribution is the application rather than the technical innovation. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their specific CNN implementation from prior text classification work, making it difficult to assess the true novelty.

2. **Insufficient comparison with alternative neural approaches**: The paper only compares against language model-based baselines, but doesn't compare against other neural network approaches for data selection. For example, why not compare against recent neural data selection methods that might use different architectures? This limits the assessment of whether the CNN approach is truly superior or just another neural method.

3. **Ambiguity around "tiny" in-domain data**: While the authors claim to work with "tiny" amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), they don't clearly establish how representative these 100 sentences are of the domain. The paper mentions they repeated the experiment with different samples (standard deviation of 0.12), but it's not clear if these samples were selected to be representative or randomly sampled. This ambiguity weakens the claim that the method works with "tiny" data.

4. **Lack of failure case analysis**: The paper presents strong positive results but doesn't discuss scenarios where the method might fail. For example, how does the method perform when the in-domain data is very different from the general-domain data? A balanced view of the method's limitations would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you ensure that the 100 in-domain sentences used for training are representative of the domain, rather than being randomly selected and potentially unrepresentative? Could you provide more details about how these samples were selected?

2. The paper mentions that the semi-supervised CNN uses word embeddings trained on general-domain data. How did you determine the optimal size of the word embeddings (300 dimensions) and the specific general-domain corpus used for training? Would different embedding parameters or corpora yield different results?

3. The paper shows that using the sum of source and target scores for selection works best. Can you provide more insight into why combining both scores is more effective than using just one, and how this might generalize to other domains?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of domain adaptation in machine translation when in-domain data is scarce. The empirical results are strong, and the method is practical for real-world applications. While the core CNN technique isn't highly novel, its application to this specific problem with such limited in-domain data is valuable. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed comparison with alternative neural approaches, clearer discussion of the representativeness of the tiny in-domain samples, and analysis of failure cases. 

Given the significance of the problem addressed, the strong empirical results, and the practical applicability of the approach, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised above. The paper represents an important step toward enabling fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation in resource-constrained settings.
