

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The main contributions are: (1) A semi-supervised CNN framework that effectively selects domain-relevant training data using as few as 100 in-domain sentences; (2) Demonstration that this method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection approaches, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with only 3% of training data; (3) Validation that the approach works effectively even when no in-domain data component exists in the general-domain corpus.

Strengths: First, the paper addresses a genuinely important practical problem in machine translation adaptation where sufficient in-domain data is unavailable. Second, the experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements across four language directions and multiple test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts) with statistically significant gains. Third, the method's ability to work with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (a scenario previously unaddressed in the literature) is particularly valuable for fine-grained topic adaptation in resource-constrained settings.

Weaknesses: First, the paper lacks comparison against simpler text classification baselines (e.g., TF-IDF or bag-of-words approaches) that might achieve similar results with less complexity. Second, the claim that 100 in-domain sentences work well isn't sufficiently validated - the paper doesn't demonstrate that these sentences were randomly selected from the dev set, raising concerns about selection bias. Third, the paper focuses exclusively on SMT systems, neglecting to compare with recent neural machine translation approaches that might benefit from similar adaptation techniques. Fourth, the paper doesn't adequately explain why CNNs specifically (as opposed to other deep learning architectures) are necessary for this task.

Questions to Authors: How do you ensure the 100 in-domain sentences used for training were randomly sampled from the dev set rather than containing bias toward the test domain? Have you considered applying this approach to neural machine translation systems, which are now more prevalent than SMT? Could the method be extended to work with even fewer than 100 in-domain sentences?
