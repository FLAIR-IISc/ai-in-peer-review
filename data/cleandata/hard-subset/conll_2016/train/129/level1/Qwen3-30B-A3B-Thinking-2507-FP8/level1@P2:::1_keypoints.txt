- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier to select domain-relevant sentences from a large general-domain corpus for SMT adaptation using extremely limited in-domain data.  
- Demonstrates the approach significantly outperforms language model–based selection methods.  
- Shows the method can be effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

- Addresses the practical problem of domain adaptation with tiny in-domain datasets (e.g., social media).  
- Reports strong, statistically significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 points) across multiple language pairs and domains.  
- Method is technically sound and practical; CNNs with general-domain embeddings help generalization and capture domain features.

- CNN architecture and semi-supervised setup are not especially novel and largely build on prior work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2015).  
- Lacks comparisons to other neural data-selection approaches, limiting claims of superiority over alternative neural methods.  
- Unclear whether the 100 in-domain sentences are representative of the domain or just random samples, weakening the “tiny data” claim.  
- No analysis of failure cases or conditions under which the method might perform poorly (e.g., highly divergent in-domain vs general-domain data).

- How were the 100 in-domain sentences selected and how is representativeness ensured or measured?  
- How were embedding size (300d) and the general-domain corpus chosen, and would different embeddings/corpora change results?  
- Why does summing source and target scores work best for selection, and how generalizable is that choice to other domains?

- Overall: meaningful and practical contribution with strong empirical results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address novelty comparison, sample representativeness, and failure-case analysis.
