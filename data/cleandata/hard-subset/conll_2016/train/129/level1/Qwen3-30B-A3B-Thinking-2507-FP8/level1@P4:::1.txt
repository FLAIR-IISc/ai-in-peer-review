

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose a method using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training data from general-domain corpora. The main contributions are:

1. A semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection that effectively works with tiny amounts of in-domain data (100 sentences), enabling fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation.
2. Demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, particularly when in-domain data is scarce (achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement).
3. Empirical validation showing the method's effectiveness across four language directions and three test domains, with results that remain robust even when the in-domain data is extremely limited.

## Strengths

1. **Strong practical relevance**: The paper addresses a significant real-world challenge in machine translation where domain adaptation is needed but in-domain data is scarce (e.g., social media translation). The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method can work with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, which is a substantial practical contribution compared to existing approaches requiring thousands of sentences.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The evaluation is comprehensive, covering four language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) across three test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The authors compare against three strong baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) and include ablation studies showing the importance of the semi-supervised aspect and the minimal data requirements. The results consistently show significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baselines, with statistical significance testing.

3. **Technical soundness of the approach**: The paper provides a clear justification for using CNNs for this task, explaining how convolutional layers capture domain-specific features and how word embeddings from large general-domain corpora help with domain adaptation. The semi-supervised framework is well-motivated and properly implemented, with the authors correctly identifying that pre-trained word embeddings from general-domain data can compensate for limited in-domain data.

4. **Clear demonstration of scalability**: The paper successfully shows that the method can work with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), which is a major contribution to the field. The stability of the approach is demonstrated through repeated experiments with different 100-sentence in-domain samples, showing consistent results (average BLEU 25.03 with standard deviation 0.12).

5. **Practical implementation**: The method is straightforward to implement using standard SMT pipelines and existing CNN toolkits, making it easily adoptable by practitioners without requiring major architectural changes to existing systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical approach**: While the application to data selection for domain adaptation is novel, the CNN architecture itself (with semi-supervised word embeddings) has been well-established in prior text classification work (e.g., Johnson and Zhang 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between the novelty of the application versus the novelty of the CNN approach itself.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent methods**: The authors compare against three LM-based baselines but don't include more recent approaches that might be better suited for small-data scenarios, such as those using transfer learning or meta-learning techniques. This limits the evaluation of whether the approach represents a significant advance over the current state of the art.

3. **Insufficient analysis of why semi-supervised CNNs outperform one-hot CNNs**: The paper states that sscnn outperforms ohcnn by ~0.5 BLEU on average but provides only superficial explanation in Section 5.4. A more detailed analysis of what specific aspects of the semi-supervised framework contribute to the improvement would strengthen the paper.

4. **Lack of discussion on domain shift limitations**: The paper assumes that the general-domain data used to train word embeddings is sufficiently representative of the test domain. It doesn't address potential limitations when the test domain is extremely different from the general-domain data, which could be a significant issue in practice.

5. **Insufficient implementation details**: The paper mentions using the conText toolkit but lacks specific implementation details needed for reproducibility, such as exact CNN hyperparameters, negative sample selection criteria, and word2vec training parameters. This makes it difficult to replicate the experiments.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on the exact hyperparameters used for the CNN (e.g., number of convolutional layers, filter sizes, dropout rates) to enable better reproducibility?

2. How does the method perform when the general-domain data is not sufficiently representative of the test domain (e.g., training on news data and testing on medical domain)? Could you provide some examples or analysis of this scenario?

3. The paper mentions that the semi-supervised CNN uses word embeddings from general-domain data, but doesn't specify how the general-domain data was selected. Was it the same as the training data used for the SMT system, or a different corpus? How does this choice impact the results?

4. Could you clarify why the sum of source and target scores worked best for selection, as mentioned in Section 4? Is this due to the nature of the translation task, or would it generalize to other text classification tasks?

5. The paper claims that the method can be applied to "select data from large size target language corpus," but doesn't provide details on this future work. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of applying this approach to target language corpus selection?
