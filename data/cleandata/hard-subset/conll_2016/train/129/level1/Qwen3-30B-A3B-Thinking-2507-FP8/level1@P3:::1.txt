

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the practical challenge of adapting statistical machine translation (SMT) systems to new domains when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) are available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection that leverages both limited in-domain data and large amounts of general-domain data. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain adaptation in SMT that requires minimal in-domain data (100 sentences) to achieve significant improvements over baseline systems.
2. Demonstrated effectiveness of the approach across four language pairs and three test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art language model-based methods.
3. Thorough empirical validation showing that the method achieves up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with only 3% of training data, significantly outperforming baselines when in-domain data is scarce.

## Strengths

1. **Practical relevance**: The paper tackles a critical real-world challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains with minimal in-domain data. With the rise of social media and emerging domains, this is a significant practical contribution that addresses a genuine pain point in translation system deployment.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and test domains, with the most impressive results on the Chinese-to-English SMS task (3.1 BLEU improvement). The ablation study showing effectiveness with only 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement) is particularly compelling evidence of the method's value.

3. **Well-motivated technical approach**: The authors provide a strong justification for using CNNs for domain classification, highlighting their ability to capture local domain-specific features and the benefits of semi-supervised learning with word embeddings. The explanation of why CNNs outperform language models (e.g., handling out-of-vocabulary words through word embeddings) is insightful and well-supported by the results.

4. **Clear comparison with state-of-the-art**: The comparison with three LM-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) is thorough and shows clear advantages of the proposed approach, especially in low-resource scenarios. The authors effectively demonstrate that their method outperforms LM-based approaches by 0.5 BLEU on average when in-domain data is limited.

5. **Practical implementation**: The method integrates seamlessly with existing SMT pipelines and requires minimal modification to standard translation systems. The authors provide clear implementation details that would make replication straightforward.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with alternative text classification approaches**: While the paper justifies using CNNs for text classification, it fails to compare with other effective text classification architectures (e.g., RNNs, transformers, or even simpler methods like TF-IDF with SVM). Without this comparison, it's difficult to determine if the CNN approach is truly the best choice for this specific task.

2. **Assumption about dev set availability**: The paper assumes that the dev set can be used as in-domain data for training the classifier, which is unrealistic in many practical scenarios. In real-world applications, you often don't have access to any in-domain data before training the model, making this assumption problematic for deployment.

3. **Insufficient analysis of selected data**: The paper doesn't provide analysis of what makes the selected sentences domain-relevant. Without understanding which features the CNNs capture that language models miss, it's hard to assess the method's fundamental contribution to the field. A qualitative analysis of selected sentences would strengthen the paper.

4. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training the CNN model compared to the baseline LM-based methods. This is a significant omission as it's crucial for practical adoption, especially when considering the trade-off between performance gains and resource requirements.

5. **Limited discussion of failure modes**: The paper doesn't explore scenarios where the method might fail (e.g., when the in-domain data is highly unrepresentative of the test domain). Understanding these limitations would provide more balanced insights into the method's applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper assumes the dev set can be used as in-domain data for training the classifier. How would your method perform if no in-domain data (including dev set) is available before training? Could you provide some analysis of this scenario?

2. The paper doesn't compare CNNs with other text classification approaches. Could you provide an ablation study comparing your method with RNN-based or transformer-based classifiers for this task?

3. What is the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of your CNN-based approach compared to the LM-based baselines? Is the performance gain justified by the additional computational requirements?

4. Could you provide some qualitative analysis of the selected sentences? What specific features or patterns do the CNNs identify that make them domain-relevant, which language models miss?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the practical challenges of domain adaptation in machine translation, particularly for scenarios with minimal in-domain data. While the experimental results are strong, addressing the weaknesses noted above would significantly strengthen the paper's technical contribution and practical relevance.
