- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN for SMT domain adaptation that uses as few as 100 in-domain sentences to select relevant data from large general corpora.
- Practical solution enabling fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data.
- Novel semi-supervised CNN data selection method that outperforms traditional language model–based methods, especially in low-data regimes.
- Comprehensive empirical validation across four language directions and three domains, showing up to 3.1 BLEU improvement and 2.1 BLEU gain with 100 sentences.
- Addresses an important real-world problem as many domains lack thousands of in-domain sentences for adaptation.
- Strong and consistent empirical results across multiple language pairs strengthen the method’s claims.
- Technical innovation: leverages word embeddings trained on large general corpora to mitigate scarce labeled data in the CNN.
- Demonstrates practical utility by selecting relevant data from large corpora (e.g., Gigaword) without prior domain knowledge.
- Compares only to older language-model–based baselines and omits modern neural approaches and NMT comparisons.
- Lacks sufficient detail on model selection and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., how bow/seq regions were combined and which worked best).
- No failure analysis for extreme domain shifts or cases where in-domain data is dissimilar from general-domain data.
- Does not discuss computational cost of training CNNs versus baselines, raising scalability concerns for many-topic adaptation.
- Theoretical explanation for CNN advantages over language models is superficial and lacks concrete evidence.
- Question: How does CNN training cost compare to baseline language models, and is it practical for adapting many topics?
- Question: How stable is the 2.1 BLEU gain with 100 sentences across different random samples, and how does variance compare to baselines?
- Question: How would the approach integrate with modern NMT systems—directly applicable or requiring significant modification?
- Question: How was the 120K in-domain subset in the general data for zh→en identified, and what does that imply for generalizability when such subsets don’t exist?
- Question: Would additional evaluation metrics (human evaluation, TER, domain-specific scores) be used beyond BLEU to better assess adaptation quality?
