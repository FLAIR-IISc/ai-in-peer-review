

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The method uses a small amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) to train a CNN-based data selection model, which then identifies the most domain-relevant sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A practical solution for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), making fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation feasible.
2. A novel semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection that outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce.
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across four language directions and three test domains, demonstrating significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) with only a small fraction of training data.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of the problem addressed**: The paper tackles a genuinely important challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains with minimal resources. As social media and specialized content proliferate, the ability to adapt translation systems with only a few hundred sentences is highly valuable. The authors convincingly argue that previous work assumes "reasonable size in-domain data" (thousands of sentences), which is often unavailable for new domains.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The experiments demonstrate clear advantages of the semi-supervised CNN approach over language model baselines across multiple language pairs and domains. The most compelling evidence is that the method achieves 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences, while language model baselines show significantly worse performance at this low data regime. The consistency of results across four language directions strengthens the claim.

3. **Technical innovation in semi-supervised learning**: The paper effectively leverages word embeddings trained on large general-domain data to overcome the scarcity of in-domain examples. This semi-supervised approach (using unlabeled data for word embeddings) is well-motivated and properly implemented, addressing a key limitation in traditional CNN approaches for text classification with limited labeled data.

4. **Clear demonstration of practical utility**: The paper provides concrete evidence that their method can select relevant data from large corpora (e.g., "Gigaword" corpus) without prior domain knowledge, making it applicable to real-world scenarios where in-domain data is scarce but general-domain data is abundant.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to modern approaches**: The paper exclusively compares against language model-based data selection methods from 2011-2013, without considering more recent neural approaches or modern neural machine translation (NMT) systems. Given that NMT has largely replaced SMT in the field, the relevance of this work for current translation systems is questionable. The authors should address how their approach might integrate with or benefit NMT systems.

2. **Insufficient detail on model selection and hyperparameters**: While the paper mentions "meta-parameters" were tuned on held-out data, it lacks specifics about the hyperparameter search process, which is crucial for reproducibility. For example, the paper states they "generate both bow-regions and seq-regions" but doesn't explain how these were combined or which performed better.

3. **Lack of failure analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where the approach might fail or underperform. For instance, what happens when the in-domain data is completely dissimilar from the general-domain data? How does the method handle extreme domain shifts? This is particularly important given the authors' claim about "fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation."

4. **Computational efficiency concerns**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of training the CNN models compared to the baseline language models. For systems that need to adapt to many topics (e.g., hundreds of topics), the training time for multiple CNN models could become prohibitive, which is a significant practical limitation.

5. **Underdeveloped theoretical justification**: The explanation for why CNNs work better than language models (Section 5.4) is somewhat superficial. The paper mentions word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words but doesn't provide concrete evidence or analysis to support this claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the computational cost of training the semi-supervised CNN compare to training the language models used in the baselines? Would this be a practical concern for real-world deployment, especially when adapting to many different topics?

2. The paper states that the semi-supervised CNN achieved 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences. How stable is this result across different random selections of these 100 sentences? The paper mentions a standard deviation of 0.12, but it would be helpful to see how this compares to the variability of the baseline methods.

3. Given the dominance of neural machine translation (NMT) systems today, how would your approach adapt to work with NMT systems instead of SMT? Would the same CNN-based data selection method be applicable, or would significant modifications be needed?

4. In your experiments, you mention "there is a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data" for the zh2en task. How did you identify this subset, and what does this imply about the generalizability of your approach when such in-domain subsets aren't present in the general-domain data?

5. The paper uses BLEU as the evaluation metric. Given the known limitations of BLEU for domain adaptation tasks, would you consider using additional metrics (e.g., human evaluation, TER, or other domain-specific metrics) to better assess the quality of the adapted systems?
