**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) systems. The method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to train the CNN model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system. The authors evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains and show that it can improve the performance of SMT systems by up to 3.1 BLEU points.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach that leverages a small amount of in-domain data and a large amount of general-domain data to select in-domain training data for SMT systems.
2. **Effectiveness of the approach with tiny amounts of in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can improve the performance of SMT systems even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., 100 sentence pairs).
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods**: The authors compare their approach with three state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and show that their approach outperforms these methods in terms of BLEU score.

**Strengths**

1. **Effectiveness of the semi-supervised CNN approach**: The authors demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN approach can effectively select in-domain training data for SMT systems, even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. **Robustness of the approach**: The authors show that their approach is robust to different amounts of in-domain data and can still achieve good performance even when the in-domain data is very limited.
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors provide a thorough comparison of their approach with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, which helps to establish the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains, which may not be representative of all possible language pairs and domains.
2. **Lack of analysis on the learned word embeddings**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the learned word embeddings and how they contribute to the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **No discussion on the computational cost**: The authors do not discuss the computational cost of their approach, which may be an important consideration for large-scale SMT systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the learned word embeddings and how they contribute to the effectiveness of your approach?
2. How does the computational cost of your approach compare to other data selection methods?
3. Have you considered evaluating your approach on other language pairs and domains to further establish its effectiveness?

**Additional Comments**

The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach, and the experimental results are thorough and well-analyzed. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the learned word embeddings and the computational cost of the approach.
