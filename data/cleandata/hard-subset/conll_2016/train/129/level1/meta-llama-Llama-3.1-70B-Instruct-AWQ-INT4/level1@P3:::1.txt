**Summary**

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for translation adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data. The method uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selects the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the machine translation system. The authors evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains and demonstrate that it outperforms strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

**Contributions**

1. **Effective use of semi-supervised CNN for data selection**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach that leverages both labeled and unlabeled data to train a CNN classification model. This approach is particularly effective when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available.
2. **Improved performance with limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve significant improvements in BLEU score even when the in-domain data is limited to as few as 100 sentences.
3. **Robustness to domain mismatch**: The authors show that their approach can select highly suitable in-domain sentences even when the in-domain data is very limited, and the domain mismatch between the training and test data is significant.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors use word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data to improve the performance of their CNN classification model. This approach allows the model to capture the semantic relationships between words and improve its ability to select in-domain sentences.
2. **Robustness to out-of-vocabulary words**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can handle out-of-vocabulary words by using word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data. This approach allows the model to generalize to unseen words and improve its performance on the test data.
3. **Improved performance over state-of-the-art baselines**: The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains. It is unclear how well their approach will generalize to other language pairs and domains.
2. **Lack of comparison to other CNN-based approaches**: The authors do not compare their approach to other CNN-based approaches for data selection, making it unclear how their approach compares to other state-of-the-art methods.
3. **Limited analysis of the impact of hyperparameters**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the performance of their approach, making it unclear how to optimize the hyperparameters for different language pairs and domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other language pairs and domains?
2. How do the authors plan to compare their approach to other CNN-based approaches for data selection?
3. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the performance of their approach?

**Additional Comments**

The authors should consider providing more details on the implementation of their approach, including the specific hyperparameters used and the computational resources required. Additionally, the authors should consider providing more analysis on the impact of their approach on the quality of the selected sentences, including metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score.
