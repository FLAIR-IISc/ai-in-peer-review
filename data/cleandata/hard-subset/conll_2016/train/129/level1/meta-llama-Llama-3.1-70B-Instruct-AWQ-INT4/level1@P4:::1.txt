**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is designed to work with tiny amounts of in-domain data, which is a common challenge in SMT. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select negative samples from the general-domain data to train a CNN classification model. The model is then used to select sentence pairs from the general-domain data that are most similar to the in-domain data. The selected sentence pairs are used to train an SMT system, which is evaluated on a test set.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for domain adaptation**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in SMT, which can work with tiny amounts of in-domain data.
2. **Use of word embeddings**: The authors use word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data to improve the performance of the CNN classification model.
3. **Data selection for SMT**: The authors use the CNN classification model to select sentence pairs from the general-domain data that are most similar to the in-domain data, which are then used to train an SMT system.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of tiny amounts of in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can work with as few as 100 sentence pairs of in-domain data, which is a significant improvement over previous methods.
2. **Improved performance over baseline systems**: The authors show that their approach can improve the performance of SMT systems over baseline systems trained with large amounts of general-domain data.
3. **Robustness to different language pairs and domains**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can work with different language pairs and domains, including social media data.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a limited number of language pairs and domains, which may not be representative of all possible scenarios.
2. **No comparison to other deep learning approaches**: The authors do not compare their approach to other deep learning approaches for domain adaptation in SMT, which may be a limitation.
3. **No analysis of the learned word embeddings**: The authors do not provide any analysis of the learned word embeddings and how they contribute to the performance of the CNN classification model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the word embeddings are learned and how they are used in the CNN classification model?
2. How do you select the hyperparameters for the CNN classification model, such as the number of layers and the number of neurons in each layer?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the performance of the approach on different language pairs and domains, including an analysis of the errors made by the system?
