**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify each sentence in the general-domain training data based on their domain relevance score, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train the machine translation system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel application of semi-supervised CNNs**: The authors propose a new application of semi-supervised CNNs for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Effective use of limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can effectively select in-domain training data even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available, which is a common challenge in machine translation.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The authors show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art language-model-based data selection methods in terms of BLEU score, which is a widely used evaluation metric in machine translation.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors use pre-trained word embeddings to improve the performance of their CNN model, which is a key contribution to the field.
2. **Robustness to limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach is robust to limited in-domain data, which is a common challenge in machine translation.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The authors show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art language-model-based data selection methods in terms of BLEU score.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on four language directions, which may not be representative of all language pairs.
2. **No comparison to other deep learning methods**: The authors do not compare their approach to other deep learning methods, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers, which may be more effective for machine translation.
3. **No analysis of computational complexity**: The authors do not provide an analysis of the computational complexity of their approach, which may be important for large-scale machine translation applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN model?
2. How do you plan to extend this approach to other language pairs and domains?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your approach?
