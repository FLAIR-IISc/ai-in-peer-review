## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the scenario of extremely limited in-domain resources. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging word embeddings pre-trained on large general-domain corpora to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The selected high-scoring sentences are then used to train the SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection *when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available*. While data selection for MT is not novel, previous approaches typically require substantial in-domain resources. This work shows strong performance even with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, a significant advancement in practical applicability for low-resource scenarios. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of the approach across four language directions and multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing evidence for its robustness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and significant BLEU score improvements over strong baselines (including state-of-the-art LM-based methods) across diverse language pairs and domains. The reported gains (up to 3.1 BLEU) are substantial and suggest a practical impact.
2. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The scenario of extremely limited in-domain data is a common bottleneck in MT adaptation, especially for emerging domains or low-resource languages. This work directly tackles this problem, offering a viable solution.
3. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervision:** Leveraging pre-trained word embeddings effectively mitigates the data scarcity issue, enhancing the performance of the CNN classifier and ultimately leading to better data selection.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, including comparisons against multiple baselines and ablation studies (examining the impact of varying the amount of in-domain data).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is relatively standard and lacks significant innovation. The core contribution resides in its application to the data selection task *specifically in the low-resource setting*.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the method works. Understanding which types of sentences are effectively selected and which are not would provide valuable insights.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to different CNN hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters) or embedding dimensions. A more thorough hyperparameter search might reveal further performance gains.


**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of sentences that are selected by your method compared to the LM-based baselines? Are there specific linguistic features or topics where your approach excels?
2.  What is the computational cost of training the CNN data selection model compared to training the LM-based data selection models? 
3.  Have you explored different methods for combining the source and target sentence scores in the data selection process beyond simply summing them?
