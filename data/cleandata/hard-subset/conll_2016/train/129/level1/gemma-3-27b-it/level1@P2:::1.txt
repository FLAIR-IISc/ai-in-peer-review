This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to data selection for statistical machine translation (SMT), particularly addressing the low-resource scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain data. The selected data is then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective method for domain adaptation in SMT when in-domain resources are severely limited. Existing data selection techniques often rely on a reasonable amount of in-domain data to train language models. This paper demonstrates that a semi-supervised CNN approach can achieve significant improvements – up to 3.1 BLEU – even with as little as 100 sentences of in-domain data, outperforming strong baselines and state-of-the-art language model-based methods.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of CNNs for this specific task.  The authors show that CNNs, combined with pre-trained word embeddings, are capable of learning meaningful representations for domain classification with minimal labeled data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial and practical challenge in SMT: domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain resources. This is a common scenario, particularly with the rise of new domains in social media and specialized areas. The problem is clearly motivated and well-defined.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling.  The reported BLEU score improvements across four language pairs and three test domains demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The results are consistently better than strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based approaches.  The consistent improvement across different languages is a strong indicator of the method’s robustness.
3. **Demonstrated Scalability to Low-Resource Settings:** The key finding that the method performs well even with only 100 in-domain sentences is particularly noteworthy.  This addresses the core challenge the authors set out to solve and makes the approach highly practical for real-world applications. The ablation study showing performance with varying amounts of training data (Figure 1) provides further support for this claim.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in detail, allowing for reproducibility. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches to domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using a CNN, there's limited discussion regarding the choice of CNN architecture (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes). A more thorough exploration of different CNN configurations and their impact on performance would strengthen the work. Was hyperparameter optimization performed, and if so, how?
2. **Potential Sensitivity to Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings. The performance could be sensitive to the quality and relevance of these embeddings to the specific domains being addressed.  A discussion of the potential impact of different pre-trained embedding sources would be valuable. What happens if the pre-trained embeddings don't cover all the vocabulary in the test domain?
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the system still makes could provide insights into areas for future improvement. For example, are there specific types of sentences or domains where the method struggles? Investigating this would increase understanding of the method's limitations.
4. **Baseline Selection:** While the baselines are strong, it would be helpful to compare against more recent data selection techniques, particularly those utilizing neural networks beyond the RNNLM baseline used.  The field has rapidly evolved since the cited baselines were published.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality and coverage of the pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., different training corpora or embedding sizes)?
2.  What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier compared to training the language models used in the baseline methods?
3.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter optimization process for the CNN architecture? Specifically, how did you choose the number of layers, filter sizes, and other relevant parameters?
4.  Can you provide any examples of misclassified sentence pairs (false positives or false negatives) and discuss why the CNN might have made those errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite comprehensive.
*   The experimental setup is well-defined and the use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing is appropriate.
*   The writing is clear and concise.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. While there are some areas for improvement, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance.
