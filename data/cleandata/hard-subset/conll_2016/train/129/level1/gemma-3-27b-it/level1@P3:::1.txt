This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to data selection for statistical machine translation (SMT) adaptation, specifically addressing the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain training data. The authors demonstrate improvements over strong baselines on four language directions and three test domains, even with as little as 100 sentences of in-domain data.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection with Limited In-Domain Data:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating a practical and effective method for domain adaptation when extremely limited in-domain resources are available. Existing data selection techniques often require thousands of in-domain sentences; this work shows strong performance with only 100. This opens up possibilities for adapting MT systems to niche or emerging domains where data scarcity is a significant hurdle.
2. **Successful Application of Semi-Supervised CNNs to Data Selection:** The paper successfully adapts CNNs, combined with semi-supervised learning via pre-trained word embeddings, to the task of identifying in-domain data. This showcases the power of leveraging general domain knowledge to overcome the limitations of sparse in-domain data, a common challenge in MT adaptation.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The comprehensive experiments across four language pairs and multiple test domains provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The gains over strong baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios, are notable and demonstrate the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in SMT – adapting to new domains with limited resources. The scenario of having only a tiny amount of in-domain data is common in real-world applications, and this work provides a valuable solution.
2. **Novelty in Combining Techniques:** While CNNs have been used in NLP, their application to data selection for SMT with a semi-supervised approach using pre-trained embeddings is novel. The combination of these techniques appears to be a key factor in the method’s success.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thorough, covering multiple language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts).  The inclusion of strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based approaches, strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, despite the technical details. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-documented.
5. **Demonstrated Scalability and Robustness:** The results demonstrate that the method performs well even with minimal in-domain data (100 sentences), and the standard deviation reported for the 100-sentence experiment indicates reasonable stability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN approach, it lacks a detailed analysis of the CNN architecture itself. Specifically, it would be valuable to investigate the impact of different convolutional filter sizes, pooling strategies, and network depths on performance. Understanding *why* certain architectural choices work better would contribute to a deeper understanding of the method.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a qualitative error analysis. Understanding *what types* of sentences are correctly/incorrectly classified by the CNN could provide insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest potential improvements. For instance, are there specific linguistic phenomena or domain-specific terminology that cause difficulties?
3. **Word Embedding Choice:** The paper uses pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec). While this is a reasonable approach given the limited in-domain data, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential impact of using different pre-trained embeddings or exploring fine-tuning the embeddings during the CNN training process.  Different embeddings might capture domain-specific nuances better.
4. **Comparison to More Recent Data Selection Techniques:** While the baselines are strong, the field of data selection has advanced since the publication of some of the cited works (e.g., Duh et al., 2013). A comparison to more recent approaches, such as those utilizing neural machine translation (NMT) or advanced representation learning techniques, would further contextualize the contributions of this paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the process of selecting the negative samples for CNN training? Was any form of active learning or hard negative mining employed to improve the discriminator's performance?
2.  What is the computational cost of training the CNN data selection model compared to the baseline LM-based data selection methods?
3.  The paper mentions that the test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) do not have in-domain data in the general domain training data. How was this verified? Was any investigation done to check for overlap or bleed-through of domain-specific terms?
4.  Could you provide some examples of sentence pairs that were correctly/incorrectly classified by the CNN, along with an explanation of why the CNN likely made those decisions? This would provide a more intuitive understanding of the model’s behavior.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions beyond those already mentioned.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for the CNN training process.
*   Consider adding a visualization (e.g., t-SNE plot) of the word embeddings to demonstrate their effectiveness in capturing domain-specific information.
