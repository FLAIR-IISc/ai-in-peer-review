This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for translation adaptation with limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data. The trained model then scores general-domain sentences, and the top-scoring sentences are used to train the SMT system. The primary contributions of this work are: 1) the successful application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation for SMT, demonstrating significant improvements over strong baselines; 2) demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach even with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences); and 3) a thorough empirical evaluation across four language pairs and three test domains, providing strong evidence of the method’s robustness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baseline systems, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods. The BLEU score gains, particularly on the Chinese-to-English sms task (up to 3.1 BLEU), are substantial and suggest a practical benefit.
2. **Effectiveness with Limited Data:** The ability to achieve significant improvements with only 100 in-domain sentences is a key strength. This is particularly important for scenarios where obtaining large amounts of in-domain data is expensive or impractical, such as adapting to new social media domains. The ablation study confirming this result is well-designed and reported.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across four language directions and three test domains provides strong evidence that the proposed approach is not specific to a particular language pair or domain. This broad evaluation enhances the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Sound Methodology:** The use of semi-supervised CNNs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, is a reasonable and well-motivated approach. Combining one-hot vectors with embeddings is sensible, addressing the data scarcity issue effectively. The chosen CNN architecture and hyperparameters seem appropriate, and the implementation details are sufficiently described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While the application of CNNs to data selection is effective, the core CNN architecture itself is not particularly novel. The paper builds upon established CNN techniques for text classification. The novelty lies more in the specific application to SMT data selection in a low-resource setting, but a stronger articulation of this distinction could be beneficial.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the model makes certain selection decisions (and errors) could provide valuable insights into the approach's strengths and weaknesses, and guide future improvements.
3. **Baseline Limitations:** The LM-based baselines, while strong, are somewhat dated. More recent data selection approaches, potentially leveraging neural networks beyond simple RNNLMs, might offer a more challenging comparison. It is unclear if the improvements are simply due to the superiority of CNNs in general or specifically related to the semi-supervised aspect in this low-resource scenario.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not adequately discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., CNN architecture, embedding size, window size). A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of sentences that are misclassified by the CNN and discussing potential reasons for these errors?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText)?
3. Have you considered more recent and advanced data selection baselines that leverage neural network architectures beyond RNNLMs? If so, how does your approach compare to these more recent methods?
4. The paper mentions selecting sentence pairs based on the sum of source and target scores. Was this decision based on empirical evaluation, and can you provide details of the experiments conducted to determine the best combination strategy?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sentences could be rephrased for improved readability.
* The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
* Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to the baselines.
