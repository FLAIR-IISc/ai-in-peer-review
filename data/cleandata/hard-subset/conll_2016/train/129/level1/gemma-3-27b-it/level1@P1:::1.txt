This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for translation adaptation with limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, and then use this classifier to select relevant data from a large general-domain corpus for training an SMT system. The authors demonstrate improvements over strong baselines on four language directions and three test domains, particularly when only a small amount (even 100 sentences) of in-domain data is available.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that CNNs, combined with a semi-supervised learning approach utilizing pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively perform data selection for SMT adaptation even with extremely limited in-domain resources. This is significant because obtaining large in-domain datasets is often a bottleneck in practical MT deployment, especially for new or niche domains. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation showing that a relatively small in-domain dataset (100 sentences) can be sufficient to train a reasonably effective data selection model using this approach. Finally, the experimental setup and comprehensive results across multiple language pairs and domains provide a valuable benchmark for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely important challenge in SMT: domain adaptation when in-domain data is severely limited. This scenario is common in real-world applications and hasn’t been adequately addressed by existing methods that typically require thousands of in-domain sentences.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple language pairs and domains, including a substantial 3.1 BLEU improvement in the Chinese-to-English SMS task, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The significance testing strengthens these claims.
3. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The semi-supervised approach, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, is crucial. This allows the CNN to generalize effectively even with limited labeled in-domain data, overcoming a key obstacle in domain adaptation. The justification for combining one-hot vectors with pre-trained embeddings is well-articulated.
4. **Robustness to Small In-Domain Data:** The demonstration that the method performs well with as few as 100 in-domain sentences is particularly impressive. This makes the approach highly practical for scenarios where collecting large in-domain datasets is infeasible.
5. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conducted thorough experiments, varying the amount of in-domain data and comparing against several strong baselines, providing a solid foundation for the claims made.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using a CNN, it lacks a detailed analysis of the optimal CNN architecture for this specific task. Exploring different layer configurations, filter sizes, and pooling strategies could potentially yield further improvements. Why were specific hyperparameters chosen?
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the data selection process. Understanding *why* the CNN selects certain sentences and *why* it fails to select others could provide valuable insights for improving the method. What types of sentences are consistently misclassified?
3. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The success of the approach relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper doesn’t discuss the potential impact of using different word embedding models or training the embeddings on different corpora. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings?
4. **Baseline Comparison Could Be Stronger:** While the baselines are reasonable, recent advancements in neural machine translation (NMT) domain adaptation techniques (e.g., fine-tuning pre-trained NMT models) are not considered. Comparing against these state-of-the-art NMT-based approaches would provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The paper focuses on SMT, which might be less representative of current MT trends.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Did you investigate different CNN architectures (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes, pooling strategies) and, if so, what were the results?
2. Can you provide an error analysis of the data selection process? What types of sentences are most often misclassified by the CNN?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models or training corpora?
4. How would the results compare against state-of-the-art NMT-based domain adaptation techniques, such as fine-tuning pre-trained NMT models?
5. Could you provide more details on the implementation? Is the code publicly available?

**Additional Comments:**

- The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
- The related work section provides a good overview of existing domain adaptation techniques.
- The experimental setup is well-defined and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.
- Consider adding a visualization of the learned filters in the CNN to provide some insight into what features the model is learning.
