**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training data. The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to train the CNN model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for domain adaptation**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN framework that leverages word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data to improve the performance of domain adaptation in SMT.
2. **Effective use of limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve significant improvements in SMT performance even with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs).
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors compare their approach with several state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and show that their approach outperforms these methods in most cases.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors' use of word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data is a key strength of their approach, as it allows the CNN model to capture semantic relationships between words that may not be present in the limited in-domain data.
2. **Robustness to limited in-domain data**: The authors' approach is robust to very limited in-domain data, making it a useful technique for domain adaptation in SMT where in-domain data may be scarce.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The authors' approach achieves state-of-the-art performance in several SMT tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving SMT performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on word embeddings**: The authors' approach relies heavily on the quality of the word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data. If the word embeddings are not effective, the performance of the approach may suffer.
2. **Computational cost**: The authors' approach requires training a CNN model, which can be computationally expensive, especially when working with large amounts of data.
3. **Limited interpretability**: The authors' approach is a black box, making it difficult to interpret the results and understand why certain sentence pairs are selected over others.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex SMT tasks, such as those involving multiple languages or domains?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the importance of different components of their approach, such as the use of word embeddings or the CNN architecture?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of computational cost in their approach, especially when working with large amounts of data?
