**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The approach uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selects the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for domain adaptation**: The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in SMT, which is effective even with a tiny amount of in-domain data.
2. **Use of word embeddings**: The paper uses word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data to improve the performance of the CNN model.
3. **Data selection for SMT**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method for data selection in SMT, which can improve the performance of the SMT system even with a small amount of in-domain data.

**Strengths**

1. **Effectiveness of semi-supervised CNN**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in SMT, which can improve the performance of the SMT system even with a tiny amount of in-domain data.
2. **Use of word embeddings**: The paper shows that using word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data can improve the performance of the CNN model.
3. **Robustness to small in-domain data**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed method can still achieve good performance even with a very small amount of in-domain data (e.g., 100 sentence pairs).

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed method on four language directions and three test domains, which may not be representative of all possible scenarios.
2. **Comparison to other methods**: The paper only compares the proposed method to three language model-based data selection methods, which may not be the only alternatives.
3. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of why the semi-supervised CNN approach is effective for domain adaptation in SMT.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the proposed method compare to other deep learning-based approaches for domain adaptation in SMT?
2. Can the proposed method be extended to other NLP tasks, such as sentiment analysis or text classification?
3. How does the size of the in-domain data affect the performance of the proposed method? Is there a minimum size of in-domain data required for the method to be effective?
