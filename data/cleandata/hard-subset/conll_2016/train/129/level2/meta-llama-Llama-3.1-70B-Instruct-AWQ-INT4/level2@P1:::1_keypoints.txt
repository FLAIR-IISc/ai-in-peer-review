Summary: Uses a semi-supervised CNN trained with a tiny in-domain set (positives) and random general-domain negatives to score and select in-domain-like sentence pairs for SMT training, effective with very limited in-domain data.

Contribution 1: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN framework that leverages word embeddings from large unlabeled data for SMT domain adaptation.

Contribution 2: Shows significant SMT improvements with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs).

Contribution 3: Outperforms several state-of-the-art language model–based data selection methods in most cases.

Strength 1: Effectively leverages pre-trained word embeddings to capture semantic relationships missing from small in-domain data.

Strength 2: Robust to extremely limited in-domain data, making it practical when in-domain data is scarce.

Strength 3: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on several SMT tasks.

Weakness 1: Performance heavily depends on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings.

Weakness 2: Training the CNN can be computationally expensive at scale.

Weakness 3: Limited interpretability—selection decisions are largely a black box.

Question 1: How will the approach be extended to more complex SMT scenarios (multiple languages or domains)?

Question 2: Can the authors provide insight or ablation on the importance of components like embeddings versus CNN architecture?

Question 3: How do the authors plan to address the computational cost for large-scale data?
