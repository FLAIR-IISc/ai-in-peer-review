- Summary: Proposes semi-supervised CNN domain adaptation for SMT using tiny in-domain positive samples and equal-sized random negatives to score and select top general-domain sentence pairs for SMT training.
- Contribution 1: Introduces a semi-supervised CNN approach that works with very small amounts of in-domain data for domain adaptation in SMT.
- Contribution 2: Leverages word embeddings trained on large general-domain data to improve CNN performance.
- Contribution 3: Presents a CNN-based data selection method that yields SMT systems outperforming baselines trained on all general-domain data.
- Strength 1: Effectively uses extremely limited in-domain data for adaptation.
- Strength 2: Demonstrates improved translation performance over baseline systems despite using much less data.
- Strength 3: Shows robustness to varying in-domain data sizes, working down to ~100 sentences.
- Weakness 1: Evaluation is limited to four language directions and three test domains, risking poor generalizability.
- Weakness 2: Comparisons are restricted to three baselines, omitting other possible domain-adaptation approaches.
- Weakness 3: Lacks detailed analysis of the impact and contribution of word embeddings to performance.
- Question 1: How were CNN hyperparameters (layers, neurons, activation) selected?
- Question 2: How would this approach generalize or be extended to other NLP tasks like language modeling or sentiment analysis?
- Question 3: Why does the semi-supervised CNN outperform supervised CNN under very limited in-domain data—can you provide deeper insights?
