**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is designed to work with tiny amounts of in-domain data, which is a common challenge in SMT. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for domain adaptation**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in SMT, which can work with tiny amounts of in-domain data.
2. **Use of word embeddings**: The authors use word embeddings learned from a large amount of general-domain data to improve the performance of the CNN model.
3. **Data selection for SMT**: The authors propose a data selection method based on the CNN model, which can select a small amount of training data that is used to create a system that outperforms baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of limited in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can work with tiny amounts of in-domain data, which is a common challenge in SMT.
2. **Improved performance over baseline systems**: The authors show that their approach can outperform baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data, even when using only a small amount of in-domain data.
3. **Robustness to varying amounts of in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can work well even when the amount of in-domain data is reduced to as few as 100 sentences.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on four language directions with three test domains, which may not be representative of all possible scenarios.
2. **Comparison to other methods**: The authors only compare their approach to three baseline methods, which may not be the only possible approaches to domain adaptation in SMT.
3. **Lack of analysis on the impact of word embeddings**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis on the impact of word embeddings on the performance of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you selected the hyperparameters for the CNN model, such as the number of layers, the number of neurons, and the activation function?
2. How do you plan to extend your approach to other NLP tasks, such as language modeling or sentiment analysis?
3. Can you provide more insights on why the semi-supervised CNN approach performs better than the supervised CNN approach, especially when the amount of in-domain data is limited?
