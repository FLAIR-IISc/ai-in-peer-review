- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN method for domain adaptation in SMT that works with very small in-domain data.
- Trains the CNN using a few in-domain positives and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain negatives, then selects top-scoring general-domain sentence pairs to train SMT.
- Main contribution: semi-supervised CNN approach effective for domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data.
- Main contribution: uses word embeddings learned from large general-domain corpora to improve CNN performance.
- Main contribution: demonstrates data selection method that improves SMT even with limited in-domain data.
- Strength: Empirically effective at improving SMT performance using minimal in-domain data.
- Strength: Shows benefit of incorporating pre-trained word embeddings into the CNN.
- Strength: Robust to extremely small in-domain datasets (e.g., 100 sentence pairs).
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to four language directions and three test domains, risking limited generalizability.
- Weakness: Baseline comparisons restricted to three language model–based selection methods, lacking broader baselines.
- Weakness: No theoretical analysis explaining why the semi-supervised CNN succeeds for domain adaptation.
- Question: How does this method compare to other deep-learning–based domain adaptation approaches for SMT?
- Question: Can the method be extended to other NLP tasks (e.g., sentiment analysis or text classification)?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the size of in-domain data and is there a minimum effective size?
