**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select in-domain training data. The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train the CNN model. The trained model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Semi-supervised CNN for domain adaptation**: The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach that leverages both labeled and unlabeled data to select in-domain training data for SMT.
2. **Effective use of tiny amounts of in-domain data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve significant improvements in SMT performance even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
3. **Comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The authors compare their approach with several state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and show that their approach achieves better performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to domain adaptation**: The authors propose a novel approach to domain adaptation that leverages semi-supervised CNNs to select in-domain training data.
2. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors use word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data to improve the performance of their approach.
3. **Significant improvements in SMT performance**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve significant improvements in SMT performance, even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a limited number of language pairs and domains.
2. **Lack of analysis**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of why their approach is effective and how it compares to other methods.
3. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach and how it can be improved.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other language pairs and domains?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why your approach is effective and how it compares to other methods?
3. How do you plan to address the limitations of your approach, such as the need for large amounts of unlabeled data?
