

Summary: The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection in statistical machine translation, specifically designed for scenarios with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences). The main contributions are: 1) a method that significantly outperforms existing language model-based data selection techniques when in-domain data is scarce, 2) demonstration that the approach achieves meaningful improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) with minimal in-domain data, and 3) evidence that the method can be applied across multiple language directions and domains.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in machine translation: adapting systems when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. This is a meaningful gap in the literature, as previous work typically assumes thousands of in-domain sentences are available.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with four language directions, multiple test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), and comprehensive comparison to strong baselines including ngram, rnnlm, and comblm methods. The results consistently show that the proposed semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms baselines, particularly with limited in-domain data.
3. The paper provides valuable empirical evidence about the method's behavior with varying amounts of in-domain data. The analysis showing that the method maintains performance even with only 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement) is particularly compelling and demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient comparison with other deep learning architectures that could potentially address the same problem. While the authors justify CNNs for text classification, they do not contrast with RNNs, transformers, or other architectures that have been applied to similar tasks. This weakens the claim of the CNN approach being particularly suited for this problem.
2. The paper does not provide enough insight into why the semi-supervised CNN approach works well for data selection. The discussion of "why" is limited to general statements about CNNs capturing local features and word embeddings being useful, without specific analysis of the features learned or how they relate to domain relevance.
3. The computational complexity and resource requirements of the proposed method are not discussed. It's unclear whether the method is practical for large-scale translation systems given the additional training time and resources required for the CNN model.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the proposed semi-supervised CNN approach compare to other deep learning architectures (e.g., RNNs, transformers) for the same data selection task, particularly when in-domain data is limited?
2. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of the features learned by the CNN model that make it effective for domain classification? For example, which words or n-grams are most important for classification decisions?
3. What are the computational requirements (training time, memory usage) of the proposed method compared to baseline data selection approaches? Is the additional overhead justified by the performance gains, particularly in resource-constrained settings?

Additional Comments:
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, what happens when the in-domain data is not representative of the test domain? The authors mention that the method works well with tiny amounts of in-domain data, but don't address scenarios where the available in-domain data is highly unrepresentative.

The paper could also strengthen its contribution by providing more concrete examples of the selected training data. Showing specific sentence pairs that were selected by the CNN method but not by baseline approaches would help illustrate the value of the approach.

The evaluation is strong but could be further enhanced by including error analysis to understand when the method fails and why. This would provide deeper insights into the method's strengths and weaknesses.
