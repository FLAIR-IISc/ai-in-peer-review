

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection in statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The main contributions are: (1) a novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification that effectively leverages limited in-domain data with general-domain word embeddings, (2) experimental validation demonstrating significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) over strong baselines with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, and (3) a practical demonstration that fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation becomes feasible with this approach.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, it addresses a meaningful practical problem in machine translation where domain adaptation is needed but in-domain data is scarce - a critical gap in current approaches that typically require thousands of sentences. Second, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with experiments across four language directions and three test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), using statistically significant comparisons against strong baselines including state-of-the-art LM-based methods. Third, the ablation studies clearly demonstrate the superiority of the semi-supervised approach over both supervised CNNs and LM-based methods, especially when in-domain data is limited.

The work is well-grounded in relevant literature, correctly citing key papers in SMT adaptation and CNN text classification. The methodology builds logically on established techniques while addressing a clear limitation in current approaches.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The limitation discussion is insufficient - it doesn't explore the performance ceiling when in-domain data is reduced below 100 sentences, which directly challenges the paper's core claim of "tiny amounts." The paper also lacks analysis of what constitutes a "good" 100-sentence sample, which would be crucial for practical implementation. Additionally, while the method works well on the tested social media domains, the paper doesn't address potential challenges with more specialized domains (medical, legal, etc.) where vocabulary might differ significantly from general-domain data.

The semi-supervised CNN approach is innovative and practically useful, but the authors should clarify the method's limitations regarding the minimum viable in-domain data size and provide more insight into the characteristics of effective small in-domain samples. With these clarifications, this work has strong potential for real-world application in machine translation systems.
