

Summary: This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors train a CNN classifier using a small amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) and general-domain data to select the most relevant training sentences from large general-domain corpora. The approach is evaluated across four language directions with multiple test domains.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection in SMT that works effectively with minimal in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences).
2. Demonstrated significant improvements over existing language model-based data selection methods, with up to 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task.
3. Validation of the approach's effectiveness for fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a genuinely important problem in machine translation: adapting to new domains with very limited in-domain data, which is increasingly relevant as social media and niche domains emerge with minimal parallel data. The authors correctly identify that existing approaches require thousands of in-domain sentences, which is often impractical.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate that their method works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement) and outperforms LM-based methods significantly in low-resource scenarios. The ablation studies showing performance across different in-domain data sizes are particularly convincing.

3. The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated and properly implemented. The authors correctly identify that word embeddings trained on large general-domain corpora can compensate for the limited in-domain data, which is a key insight that enables their approach.

4. The paper provides clear empirical evidence that their method can select high-quality in-domain training data even when there's no in-domain component within the general-domain data, which demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's focus on Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) is a major limitation. The field has largely moved to Neural Machine Translation (NMT), and the authors don't address whether their approach would be applicable to NMT systems. Given that SMT is largely obsolete, this significantly limits the paper's relevance to contemporary machine translation research.

2. The comparison with baselines is outdated. The authors compare with LM-based methods from 2011-2013 (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013), but don't compare with more recent neural approaches to data selection. For instance, more recent work has used neural language models (e.g., RNNLM) for data selection, but the authors don't compare with these methods or discuss how their approach would integrate with modern neural translation systems.

3. The paper lacks sufficient analysis of why CNNs outperform language models for this specific task. While the authors briefly mention that CNNs can capture local features and that word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words, they don't provide concrete evidence or deeper analysis of the model's internal behavior that would explain the performance difference.

4. The paper doesn't discuss computational costs or practical considerations for deployment. Training CNNs, especially with semi-supervised learning, likely requires more computational resources than the baseline LM-based methods. This is an important consideration for practical adoption that the authors should address.

5. The paper makes a strong claim about enabling "fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation" but doesn't provide evidence of this capability beyond the specific domains tested. It would be valuable to see examples of how the system adapts to different topics within the same language pair.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would this approach perform if applied to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems instead of Statistical Machine Translation? Have you considered adapting your method for NMT?

2. Could you compare your method with more recent data selection approaches that use neural networks (e.g., using neural language models or other neural approaches) to demonstrate the relative advantages of your semi-supervised CNN approach?

3. Could you provide more details on the computational cost of training and using your CNN model compared to the baseline LM-based methods, especially considering the resource constraints of practical translation systems?
