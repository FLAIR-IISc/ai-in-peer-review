- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification to select data when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available.  
- Novelty: leverages limited in-domain data together with general-domain word embeddings in a semi-supervised CNN.  
- Empirical result: shows BLEU improvements up to 3.1 over strong baselines with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Practical claim: enables fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation.  
- Strength: tackles a meaningful real-world problem—domain adaptation with very scarce in-domain data.  
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across four language directions and three social-media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), with statistically significant baselines including state-of-the-art LM methods.  
- Strength: ablation studies demonstrate clear superiority over supervised CNNs and LM-based methods, especially with limited in-domain data.  
- Well-situated in literature, building logically on established SMT adaptation and CNN text-classification work.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of performance when in-domain data drops below 100 sentences (performance ceiling not explored).  
- Weakness: no investigation of what makes an effective 100-sentence in-domain sample (sample selection characteristics).  
- Weakness: limited domain scope—does not address challenges for specialized domains (e.g., medical, legal) with divergent vocabulary.  
- Suggestion: authors should clarify minimum viable in-domain size and characterize effective small-sample properties to improve practical applicability.
