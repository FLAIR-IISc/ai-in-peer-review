

Summary: This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selected general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classifier. The key innovation is the semi-supervised aspect, where word embeddings trained on large general-domain data are used to enhance the CNN's performance. The selected in-domain data is then used to train the translation system, with experiments showing significant improvements over baseline methods.

Main Contributions:
1. A semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection in machine translation that works effectively with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences).
2. Empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, particularly when in-domain data is scarce.
3. Evidence that the method enables fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation, which was previously impractical due to the high cost of collecting sufficient in-domain data.

Strengths:
1. The problem addressed is highly relevant to modern machine translation challenges, especially with the proliferation of new domains (e.g., social media) where large in-domain datasets are unavailable. The authors clearly articulate this gap in the literature, particularly highlighting how existing approaches require thousands of in-domain sentences, making fine-grained adaptation impractical.

2. The experimental results are strong and well-documented across four language directions and three test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The paper demonstrates up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over baselines, with particularly compelling results when in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences), where the method achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline. The comparison with LM-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) is thorough and shows consistent superiority of the semi-supervised CNN approach.

3. The paper provides convincing evidence that the method is stable even with minimal in-domain data. The authors repeat the 100-sentence experiment three times, showing a standard deviation of only 0.12 BLEU, which demonstrates the reliability of their approach when data is scarce.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the CNN architecture and hyperparameter tuning. While the authors mention using "one-hot CNN" and "semi-supervised CNN," they don't explain why these specific architectures were chosen or how they were tuned for this particular task. For instance, why was the region size set to 5 and stride to 1? How were the number of neurons (500) and pooling method (max pooling) determined? This lack of detail makes it difficult to replicate the experiments or understand the design choices.

2. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the semi-supervised approach outperforms the supervised one. The authors mention that word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words, but they don't provide specific examples or analysis showing how this works in practice. For instance, what are some specific words that were correctly classified due to the word embeddings, but would have been missed by the supervised approach?

3. The paper doesn't address potential limitations of the approach. For example, how does the method perform when the in-domain data is extremely limited (fewer than 100 sentences)? What are the computational costs of training the CNN models compared to the LM-based baselines? How does the performance scale as the amount of in-domain data increases beyond 100 sentences? These limitations would help readers understand the practical boundaries of the approach.

4. The negative sampling strategy is not well explained. The authors mention "randomly sampling from the whole data pool" for negative samples, but don't discuss how this might affect the results. For instance, if the general-domain data is already somewhat domain-specific, random sampling might not provide a good negative example. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of this sampling strategy.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details about how the CNN architecture was specifically designed for this task, including the rationale for the chosen hyperparameters (region size, stride, number of neurons, etc.)?

2. Can you provide specific examples where the word embeddings from general-domain data helped classify sentences that would have been misclassified by the supervised approach alone?

3. How does the performance of the semi-supervised CNN method change when the in-domain data is reduced below 100 sentences? What is the minimum amount of in-domain data needed for the method to be effective?

4. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the CNN models compared to the LM-based baselines, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements?
