- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN method for data selection in statistical MT tailored to very small in-domain datasets (as few as 100 sentences).
- Claims the method significantly outperforms LM-based selection techniques when in-domain data is scarce.
- Reports meaningful BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU; ~2.1 BLEU with only 100 in-domain sentences).
- Demonstrates applicability across multiple language directions and domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts).
- Addresses a practical gap: adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data where prior work assumes thousands of sentences.
- Evaluation is thorough: four language directions, multiple domains, and strong baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm).
- Empirical analysis shows consistent gains and behavior across varying amounts of in-domain data.
- Lacks comparison with other deep learning architectures (RNNs, transformers), weakening the claim that CNNs are the best choice.
- Provides limited insight into why the semi-supervised CNN works—no detailed analysis of learned features or their relation to domain relevance.
- Omits discussion of computational complexity and resource requirements, leaving practicality for large-scale systems unclear.
- Asks for comparisons to other DL architectures (RNNs, transformers) for the same task under limited in-domain data.
- Requests detailed analysis of CNN-learned features (e.g., influential words or n-grams) driving classification decisions.
- Requests reporting of computational requirements (training time, memory) versus baseline methods to assess overhead vs. gains.
- Suggests discussing limitations, especially when available in-domain data is unrepresentative of the test domain.
- Recommends showing concrete examples of selected training sentences and performing error analysis to illuminate failure cases and strengths.
