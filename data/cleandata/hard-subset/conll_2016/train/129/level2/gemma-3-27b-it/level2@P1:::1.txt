This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically targeting scenarios with limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging word embeddings pre-trained on large general-domain corpora to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data. The selected sentence pairs are then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective data selection method for low-resource domain adaptation in SMT. The authors demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection techniques, especially when only a tiny amount (e.g., 100 sentences) of in-domain data is available. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses a real-world challenge in MT—adapting to new domains with minimal labeled data. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of semi-supervised CNNs in this context, highlighting the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings when in-domain data is scarce. Finally, the experimental setup with four language pairs and three test domains provides a reasonably thorough evaluation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a practical problem:** The problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is highly relevant and practical. Existing methods often require substantial in-domain resources, which are not always available. This work provides a viable solution for scenarios where data is scarce.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over strong baselines across multiple language pairs and test domains. The gains, particularly with only 100 in-domain sentences, are impressive and highlight the method’s effectiveness. The statistical significance testing further strengthens these results.
3. **Effective use of semi-supervised learning:** The integration of pre-trained word embeddings into the CNN framework is well-justified and demonstrably improves performance.  This demonstrates a good understanding of leveraging unlabeled data to enhance model performance in a low-resource setting.
4. **Thorough experimental setup:** The paper includes experiments across four language pairs and three test domains, providing a relatively comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art techniques strengthens the validity of the results. 
5. **Clear and concise presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the key concepts are explained in a way that is easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited analysis of error cases:** While the results are impressive, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the system. Understanding *why* the system makes certain errors could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific types of sentences or domains where the method struggles?
2. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning the CNN hyperparameters, but doesn't provide much detail about the tuning process or the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.  Understanding the robustness of the method to different hyperparameter choices would be valuable.
3. **CNN architecture details:** The CNN architecture is described at a relatively high level. Providing more details about the specific layers, filter sizes, and activation functions used could help others reproduce the results and potentially improve upon the method. A diagram illustrating the network architecture would be beneficial.
4. **Comparison to more recent data selection methods:**  The baselines are largely based on work from 2013 and earlier.  It would be beneficial to compare against more recent advances in data selection, particularly those leveraging neural networks.
5. **Lack of analysis of the learned domain classifier:** The paper focuses primarily on the downstream MT performance. It would be insightful to analyze the learned CNN classifier itself - what features does it learn to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain data?  Visualizing the learned filters or attention weights could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the system? Are there specific domains or sentence structures where the method consistently struggles?
2. How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings for the CNN? Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the importance of different components of the architecture (e.g., the pre-trained word embeddings)?
3. Could you provide a more detailed description of the CNN architecture, including the number of layers, filter sizes, and activation functions used? A diagram of the architecture would be helpful.
4. Have you considered comparing your method to more recent data selection techniques that utilize neural networks (e.g., those based on transformers)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears to be comprehensive and includes relevant publications.
*   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments.
