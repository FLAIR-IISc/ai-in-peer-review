This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for translation adaptation, specifically addressing the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for limited in-domain data. The trained model then scores general-domain sentences, and the highest-scoring sentences are used to train the SMT system. The main claimed contributions are a novel semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection, demonstration of effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), and achieving improved translation quality compared to strong baselines and state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods. 

The most significant contribution of this work lies in successfully adapting SMT systems with a remarkably small amount of in-domain data. Previous methods generally require thousands of in-domain sentences, while this work demonstrates competitive performance with only 100. This is particularly valuable for emerging domains or scenarios where data collection is expensive or time-consuming. The successful application of CNNs to this problem also represents a valuable addition to the toolkit for domain adaptation. The experimental results, covering four language pairs and three test domains, provide compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed method.

Several strengths of this work are noteworthy. First, the experimental setup is well-designed and comprehensive, including comparisons to strong baselines, including recent LM-based approaches. The use of diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) enhances the generalizability of the findings. Second, the ablation study (reducing in-domain data size) provides valuable insights into the method’s robustness and sensitivity to data availability. The reported standard deviation for the 100-sentence experiment further supports the reliability of the results. Third, the inclusion of detailed explanations of the CNN architecture and training process improves reproducibility. Finally, the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations in the related work section, positioning their contribution effectively. 

However, there are several areas for improvement. First, while the paper provides a high-level explanation of why CNNs are effective (capturing local indicators, leveraging pre-trained embeddings), a more detailed analysis of *what* the CNN is learning would be beneficial. Visualizing the filters or analyzing the activation patterns could offer insights into the features that contribute most to domain classification. Second, the choice of 300K selected sentences appears somewhat arbitrary.  A more systematic exploration of the optimal selection size, potentially using a learning curve analysis, could strengthen the conclusions. Third, the paper does not thoroughly discuss the computational cost of training and applying the CNN compared to the LM-based baselines. While the performance gains are promising, it is important to consider the trade-offs in terms of computational resources. Fourth, the sentence-level selection approach is used. Considering phrase-level selection could potentially lead to more fine-grained adaptation and improved performance. Finally, while the paper acknowledges the absence of domain information during training, it would be valuable to briefly discuss how the approach might be extended to incorporate such information if available.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of what features the CNN is learning to discriminate between in-domain and out-of-domain data?
2.  How was the number of selected sentences (300K) determined? Was a learning curve analysis performed to evaluate different selection sizes?
3. What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the proposed CNN-based data selection approach compared to the LM-based baselines?
4. How might the approach be extended to incorporate explicit domain information, if available?

Additional Comments:
- The references are well-cited and relevant.
- The writing is generally clear and concise.
- Consider adding a brief discussion of potential error analysis to understand the types of sentences that are misclassified by the CNN.
