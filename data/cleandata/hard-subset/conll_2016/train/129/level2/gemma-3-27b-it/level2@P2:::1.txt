This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the scenario of limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, using a small amount of in-domain data augmented with word embeddings trained on a large general-domain corpus. The selected data is then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective method for adapting SMT systems when only a very small amount (even 100 sentences) of in-domain data is available. This is a significant problem in many real-world translation scenarios, and the proposed approach demonstrably outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based methods, across multiple language pairs and domains. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration of the benefits of semi-supervised learning (using pre-trained word embeddings) in this context, highlighting its importance when in-domain data is scarce. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation with four language directions and three test domains provides strong support for the effectiveness of the method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely important and challenging problem in SMT: domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data. Existing methods often require thousands of in-domain sentences, which is frequently unavailable.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results are compelling, consistently showing improvements over strong baselines (including recent language model-based approaches) across diverse language pairs and domains. The gains are statistically significant, and the results on the Chinese-to-English SMS task are particularly impressive.
3. **Demonstrates scalability to low-resource scenarios:** The finding that the method performs well even with only 100 in-domain sentences is a crucial result. This makes the approach applicable to a wide range of practical scenarios where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is infeasible.
4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is described in detail, and the experiments are presented logically.
5. **Thorough experimental setup:** The use of four language pairs and three test domains demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed approach. The inclusion of several strong baselines allows for a fair comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited analysis of CNN architecture:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using a CNN, it lacks a detailed analysis of the optimal CNN architecture for this specific task. The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters, but doesn't provide details about the search space or the results of ablation studies to determine the contribution of different CNN components (e.g., different layer sizes, pooling strategies).
2. **Insufficient discussion of negative sample selection:** The paper mentions random selection of negative samples but doesn’t thoroughly explore alternative strategies for selecting more informative negative samples. For example, hard negative mining (selecting negative samples that the CNN misclassifies) might further improve performance.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the CNN makes certain mistakes could provide valuable insights for improving the method. Are there specific types of sentences or domains where the approach struggles?
4. **Limited comparison to other recent methods:** While the comparison to LM-based methods is strong, it would be helpful to briefly discuss more recent approaches to domain adaptation, such as those based on neural machine translation (NMT) and transfer learning. Although the focus is on SMT, acknowledging these alternative approaches would provide a more complete context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the CNN architecture search and the results of any ablation studies conducted? Specifically, what range of layer sizes and pooling strategies were explored, and what was the impact of each component on performance?
2. Did you experiment with any alternative strategies for selecting negative samples, such as hard negative mining? If so, what were the results?
3. Could you provide a brief error analysis, highlighting common types of errors made by the CNN and potential reasons for those errors?
4. How might this approach be adapted or extended to leverage the benefits of neural machine translation (NMT) models?
