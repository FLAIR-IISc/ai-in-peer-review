- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain vs out-of-domain for SMT data selection, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to handle very limited in-domain data.

- Primary contribution: a practical and effective data selection method for low-resource domain adaptation in SMT, especially strong with only ~100 in-domain sentences.

- Empirical results show the method outperforms strong baselines, including LM-based selection, across multiple language pairs and domains with statistically significant gains.

- Effectively integrates pre-trained word embeddings into a semi-supervised CNN, demonstrating benefits of leveraging large general-domain corpora when in-domain data is scarce.

- Evaluation is reasonably thorough: experiments on four language pairs and three test domains, compared to existing state-of-the-art techniques.

- Paper is clearly and concisely written with well-articulated motivation and methods.

- Lacks detailed error analysis to explain what types of sentences or domains cause failures and why the model errs.

- Insufficient information on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity; robustness to hyperparameter choices is unclear.

- CNN architecture is described at a high level; more specifics (layers, filter sizes, activations) and a diagram would aid reproducibility.

- Baselines are somewhat dated (mostly pre-2013); comparisons to more recent neural data selection methods are missing.

- Little analysis of the learned domain classifier itself (e.g., learned features, filters, or attention visualizations).

- Question: Please provide a detailed error analysis—are there domains or sentence structures where the method consistently fails?

- Question: How sensitive are results to CNN hyperparameters and which components (e.g., embeddings) are most important (ablation studies)?

- Question: Please provide detailed CNN architecture specs (number of layers, filter sizes, activations) and ideally a diagram.

- Question: Have you compared to more recent neural data selection approaches (e.g., transformer-based methods)?

- Reference list appears comprehensive and relevant.

- Recommend a more in-depth discussion of limitations and future work.

- Suggest adding a table summarizing hyperparameter settings used in experiments.
