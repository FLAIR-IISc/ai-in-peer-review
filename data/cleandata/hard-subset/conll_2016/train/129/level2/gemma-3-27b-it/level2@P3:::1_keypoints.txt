- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to distinguish in-domain vs out-of-domain sentence pairs and select top-scoring general-domain sentences for SMT adaptation.
- Leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for extremely limited in-domain data.
- Claims effective adaptation with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, outperforming strong baselines and LM-based selection methods.
- Main contribution: enabling competitive SMT domain adaptation with a remarkably small in-domain corpus.
- Empirical evaluation covers four language pairs and three test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook), showing broad applicability.
- Experimental setup is comprehensive with strong baselines and comparisons to recent LM-based approaches.
- Ablation study (varying in-domain size) and reported standard deviation for 100-sentence experiments bolster result reliability.
- Paper provides detailed CNN architecture and training descriptions, aiding reproducibility.
- Authors appropriately situate their work within related literature and acknowledge limitations.
- Lacks detailed analysis of what features the CNN actually learns (e.g., filter visualizations or activation analyses).
- The choice of selecting 300K sentences appears arbitrary; no systematic learning-curve analysis of selection size is provided.
- Paper omits a discussion of computational cost (training/inference) compared to LM-based baselines.
- Uses sentence-level selection only; phrase-level selection could offer finer-grained adaptation and potential gains.
- Does not discuss extensions to incorporate explicit domain information when available.
- Questions for authors requested: feature-analysis of CNN, rationale/analysis for 300K selection, computational cost comparison, and approaches to incorporate domain metadata.
- Additional notes: references are relevant, writing is clear, and an error analysis of misclassified sentences is recommended.
