## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of adapting statistical machine translation (SMT) systems when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to select relevant training data from a larger general-domain corpus. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to mitigate the impact of scarce in-domain data. 

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN approach can effectively select in-domain data for SMT adaptation, *even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences*. This is a significant finding as it expands the applicability of data selection techniques to scenarios where obtaining substantial in-domain resources is impractical.  A second contribution lies in the strong empirical results; the method achieves improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU across four language pairs and three test domains, consistently outperforming strong baselines including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.

The strengths of the paper are its clear motivation, well-defined experimental setup, and compelling results. The use of semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings is a sensible approach given the limited in-domain data.  The comprehensive comparison against several baselines provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The ablation study exploring the impact of in-domain data size (down to 100 sentences) is particularly insightful.  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The analysis of *why* the semi-supervised CNN performs so well is relatively shallow.  A deeper exploration of what features the CNN is learning to discriminate between domains would be valuable.  While the results are strong, the paper lacks a discussion of computational costs – the overhead of training the CNN and performing data selection is not addressed.  Finally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to more recent neural machine translation (NMT) adaptation techniques, even if just to acknowledge the shifting landscape of MT.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of features learned by the CNN classifier? (e.g., what words or n-grams are most strongly associated with in-domain vs. out-of-domain data?)
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) associated with the data selection process using your semi-supervised CNN approach?
3.  How does your method compare to recent NMT adaptation techniques, such as fine-tuning pre-trained NMT models with limited in-domain data?
