- Paper proposes framing SMT domain-adaptive data selection as a binary classification task using a semi-supervised CNN trained on very small in-domain seeds and pre-trained embeddings to score and select sentences from a large general-domain pool.  
- The method trains on few in-domain positives and randomly sampled negatives, then selects top-scoring general-domain pairs to train adapted SMT systems.  
- Experiments on four language pairs and three social-media domains show the CNN-based selector (sscnn) substantially outperforms baselines and LM-based methods, especially with only a few hundred in-domain sentences.  
- Contribution: novel and effective use of a semi-supervised CNN for SMT data selection, showing classification-based selection can beat perplexity-based methods in low-resource adaptation.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation that the method remains robust and advantageous even with extremely small seed sizes (as few as ~100 sentences).  
- Strength: tackles a realistic and important problem—domain adaptation with extremely scarce in-domain parallel data common in emerging domains.  
- Strength: thorough experiments across multiple languages/domains with consistent, substantial BLEU improvements (e.g., +3.1 zh-en).  
- Strength: convincing low-data analysis, including seed-size variation and stability checks, demonstrating superior performance and stability of sscnn vs. LM-based methods.  
- Strength: technically sound—convolutions and pooling capture domain-specific phrase patterns and pre-trained embeddings mitigate sparsity and enable semantic generalization.  
- Weakness: insufficient architectural and implementation details (e.g., construction of bow/seq regions, how one-hot and embeddings are combined, hyperparameters) hurt reproducibility.  
- Weakness: no qualitative analysis of selected sentences to illustrate what features the CNN captures versus LM methods (topic, style, etc.).  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational cost/trade-offs for training the CNN and scoring large corpora compared to LM-based selection.  
- Question: please clarify the CNN architecture—how are one-hot vectors and pre-trained embeddings combined in Equation 2 (concatenation, summation, or other)?  
- Question: were more informed negative sampling strategies or hard-negative mining considered instead of random sampling to strengthen the classifier?
