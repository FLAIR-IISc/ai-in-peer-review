**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose a data selection method that frames the problem as a classification task. A semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) is trained to distinguish in-domain sentences from general-domain sentences, using the tiny in-domain set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from leveraging pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) learned on large unlabeled corpora, which helps the model generalize from the small labeled training set. This trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the large general-domain pool, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for the task of data selection in SMT adaptation. This approach is specifically tailored to the challenging and practical scenario where in-domain seed data is extremely scarce (as few as 100 sentences).
2.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation on four language pairs and three challenging social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results demonstrate significant BLEU score improvements (up to +3.1) over a strong baseline trained on all data, as well as over three state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
3.  A clear demonstration of the proposed method's robustness in low-data regimes. An analysis shows that as the amount of in-domain seed data is reduced, the performance of the semi-supervised CNN approach degrades much more gracefully than that of competing LM-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant, Practical Problem:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. In many real-world scenarios, it is prohibitively expensive or impossible to collect large amounts of in-domain data for every new domain or topic. The ability to effectively adapt an MT system using just a few hundred sentences (e.g., from a small development set) is a significant practical advantage. The method enables fine-grained adaptation that would be infeasible with methods requiring thousands of seed sentences.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The proposed method (`sscnn`) consistently and significantly outperforms all baselines across four different language pairs and three distinct domains. The gains are not marginal; an improvement of up to 3.1 BLEU is substantial. The inclusion of significance tests strengthens the claims, and the comparison against the widely-used LM-based methods of Duh et al. (2013) provides a very strong point of reference.
3.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Method:** The choice of a semi-supervised CNN is well-justified. The paper provides clear intuition for why this architecture is suitable: the convolutional/pooling layers can identify key domain-specific phrases regardless of their position, and the pre-trained word embeddings provide crucial semantic knowledge that compensates for the lack of labeled data. The explanation of the methodology is clear and appears to be reproducible.
4.  **Excellent Analysis of Low-Data Performance:** The experiment detailed in Figure 1 is a key strength of the paper. It directly tests the central claim—that the method excels with *tiny* amounts of in-domain data. The graph compellingly shows that while all methods perform comparably with several thousand seed sentences, the `sscnn` model is far more stable and effective when the seed set size is reduced to just a few hundred, a scenario where LM-based methods struggle.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty in the CNN Architecture:** The paper's primary contribution is the application of an existing model to a new task. The one-hot and semi-supervised CNN architectures are adopted directly from Johnson and Zhang (2015a,b). While the application is novel and impactful, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning its contribution as one of application rather than novel model development. A brief discussion on why this particular architecture was chosen over other neural classifiers could also be beneficial.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but provides little insight into *why* it works better than the baselines. What kind of sentences does the `sscnn` model select that the LM-based models miss? Does it focus more on topical keywords, style, sentence structure, or a combination? Including a few examples of highly-ranked sentences by different models would provide valuable intuition and a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.
3.  **Unclear Tuning of a Critical Hyperparameter:** The amount of data selected (the `#sent` column in Table 2) varies widely between methods and tasks. The paper states this crucial hyperparameter was "determined by the performance on held-out data," but the process is not detailed. This makes direct comparisons slightly less controlled, as one method might outperform another simply due to being allocated a more optimal amount of training data. A sensitivity analysis showing how BLEU score varies with the number of selected sentences for each method would make the results more robust.
4.  **No Discussion of NMT:** While the work focuses on phrase-based SMT, which was still prominent in 2016, a brief discussion of the method's potential applicability to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) would have been welcome. For instance, this data selection approach could be used to create a dataset for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model. This would have broadened the paper's potential impact.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the process for selecting the optimal number of sentences for each system in Table 2? Was a single validation set used to tune this hyperparameter for all methods on a given task, and how sensitive were the final BLEU scores to this choice?
2.  You combine the scores from the source and target side classifiers to rank sentence pairs. Have you experimented with using only the source-side classifier? This would be relevant for scenarios where only monolingual in-domain data is available and would clarify the contribution of the target-side signal.
3.  The zh-en task had a known 120K in-domain subset within the general training data, and this task saw the largest BLEU gains. To what extent do you think the CNN's strong performance here is due to its ability to simply "rediscover" this pre-existing clean subset, versus constructing a novel, more targeted subset?
