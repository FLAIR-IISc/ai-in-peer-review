- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN using pre-trained word embeddings to score and select general-domain sentence pairs for SMT adaptation from extremely small in-domain seeds.
- Evaluates the method on four language pairs and three social-media domains, showing up to +3.1 BLEU over a strong baseline and LM-based selection methods.
- Shows the proposed approach is robust in low-data regimes, degrading more gracefully than LM-based methods as in-domain seed size shrinks.

- Tackles a practical and significant problem: adapting MT with only a few hundred in-domain sentences.
- Presents strong, thorough empirical results with significance testing and comparisons to established LM-based baselines.
- Method is well-motivated and clearly explained: CNN convolution/pooling and pre-trained embeddings justify generalization from tiny labeled sets.
- Provides a strong low-data analysis (Figure 1) demonstrating stability and effectiveness when seed data are very limited.

- Limited novelty in model architecture: CNN setup largely adopted from prior work (Johnson & Zhang), so contribution is primarily application.
- Lacks qualitative analysis showing what sentence types the sscnn selects versus LM methods, limiting insight into why it works better.
- Critical hyperparameter (number of selected sentences) tuning is under-specified, risking uncontrolled comparisons; sensitivity analysis is missing.
- No discussion of applicability to NMT or how the selection approach could be used for neural fine-tuning.

- How was the optimal number of selected sentences determined for each system, and how sensitive are BLEU scores to this choice?
- Did you try using only the source-side classifier (relevant when only monolingual in-domain data exist), and how much does target-side signal help?
- For zh-en, to what extent did the CNN simply rediscover the known 120K in-domain subset versus constructing a different, targeted subset?
