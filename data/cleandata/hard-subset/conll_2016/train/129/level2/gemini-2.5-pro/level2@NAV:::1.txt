**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel data selection method for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, leveraging semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The core idea is to train a CNN classifier using a very small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and general-domain data as negative examples. This classifier then scores all sentences in a large general-domain corpus for relevance, and the top-scoring sentences are used to train an adapted SMT system.

The primary contributions are:
1.  An effective and practical method for SMT adaptation that achieves significant performance gains even when the available in-domain data is extremely limited (e.g., 100 sentences). This demonstrates the feasibility of fine-grained adaptation.
2.  A strong empirical evaluation across four language pairs, showing the proposed semi-supervised CNN approach consistently and significantly outperforms established language model-based data selection baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial and statistically significant improvements in BLEU scores (up to 3.1 points) over strong baselines. The experiments are thorough, covering multiple language pairs and challenging social media domains. The consistent outperformance of the `sscnn` model is compelling.
2.  **High Practical Impact:** The work addresses a crucial real-world problem: adapting MT systems with minimal resources. The finding that significant gains are possible with as few as 100 in-domain sentences is a key result, potentially enabling rapid adaptation to new or niche topics where data collection is expensive.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Low-Data Scenarios:** Figure 1 provides a clear and powerful illustration of the method's robustness in low-data regimes. It convincingly shows that while LM-based methods degrade sharply with less in-domain data, the CNN-based approach, especially the semi-supervised variant, remains stable and effective, highlighting its main advantage.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the CNN model succeeds where LMs falter. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but speculative. Including a qualitative analysis with examples of sentences selected by the CNN but missed by LMs would provide valuable intuition and strengthen the paper’s conclusions.
2.  **Lack of Detail on Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational overhead of this method compared to the LM-based baselines. Training a CNN and scoring millions of sentences could be significantly more time-consuming. A brief comparison of training and inference times would be beneficial for assessing the method's practicality.
3.  **Methodological Novelty is in Application:** The CNN architecture itself is fairly standard for text classification. The main novelty lies in its application to SMT data selection. This is perfectly acceptable, but the contribution should be framed clearly as a novel application rather than a new model architecture.
