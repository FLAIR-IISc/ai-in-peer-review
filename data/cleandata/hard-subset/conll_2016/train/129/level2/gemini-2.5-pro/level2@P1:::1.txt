### Review

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper proposes a novel method for data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, framing it as a classification task solved with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The key idea is to use a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples to train a CNN classifier. To handle the scenario where in-domain data is extremely limited, the authors employ a semi-supervised approach where pre-trained word embeddings, learned from a large general-domain corpus, are used as additional features in the CNN. This trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain pool, and the highest-scoring pairs are selected to train the adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of a semi-supervised CNN for the task of data selection for SMT adaptation. This presents a compelling alternative to the dominant language model (LM) perplexity-based approaches.
2.  The paper demonstrates that this method is highly effective in a practical and challenging scenario: when the amount of available in-domain data is extremely small (as few as 100 sentences).
3.  The work provides strong and comprehensive empirical results across four language pairs and three distinct domains, showing significant BLEU score improvements (up to +3.1) over strong baselines, including systems trained on all available data and data selected by state-of-the-art LM-based methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Sound Motivation:** The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to the data selection problem. Moving from perplexity-based scoring to a discriminative classification framework using CNNs is an interesting and well-motivated shift. The semi-supervised aspect, incorporating information from large unlabeled corpora via word embeddings, is a particularly strong design choice that directly addresses the paper's central challenge of data scarcity.

2.  **Impressive and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are a key strength of the paper. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms not only a baseline trained on all data but also three competitive LM-based selection methods, including one using recurrent neural networks. These gains are demonstrated across a variety of language pairs and challenging social media domains (SMS, Twitter, Facebook), highlighting the general applicability of the approach.

3.  **Addressing a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the highly relevant problem of domain adaptation with very limited resources. The experiment shown in Figure 1, which analyzes performance as the amount of in-domain seed data is reduced to as little as 100 sentences, is particularly compelling. It clearly demonstrates the robustness of the CNN-based methods and their superiority over LM-based approaches in this low-resource regime, which is a common scenario for fine-grained or rapidly evolving domains.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is well-designed. The authors compare against multiple appropriate and strong baselines from prior work and use statistical significance testing to validate their claims. The inclusion of an analysis on the impact of the in-domain data size provides valuable insight into the method's behavior and strengthens the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis and Interpretation:** While the quantitative results are excellent, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth qualitative analysis. The discussion in Section 5.4 offers some high-level intuitions about why CNNs work well (capturing key phrases, generalizing via embeddings), but it lacks concrete evidence. Providing a few examples of sentences that were highly ranked by the `sscnn` model but not by the LM-based methods would offer valuable insight into what the model is learning and why it outperforms the baselines.

2.  **Unclear Architectural Details:** The description of the CNN model architecture is somewhat brief and relies heavily on citations. For reproducibility and clarity, a more self-contained description would be beneficial. For example, Section 5.2 mentions that both "bow-regions" and "seq-regions" are used as input, but it is not clear how these different representations are combined within the network. A small diagram or a more precise formulation of the input layer, especially for the semi-supervised model combining one-hot and embedding features, would greatly improve the clarity of the method.

3.  **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not address the computational efficiency of the proposed method. Training a CNN classifier and subsequently using it to score millions of sentences in the general-domain corpus could be computationally intensive. An analysis of the time and resource requirements of this process, particularly in comparison to the LM-based baselines, is an important practical consideration that is currently missing.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a few illustrative examples of sentences that are selected by your `sscnn` model but are assigned a low rank by the `rnnlm` baseline? This would help build intuition about the different signals being captured by the two approaches.

2.  Could you please provide more detail on the specific CNN architecture? Specifically, how are the "bow-regions" and "seq-regions" for both the one-hot and word embedding inputs combined before or within the convolutional layer?

3.  Have you analyzed the computational overhead of your method? How does the time required to train the classifier and score the entire general-domain corpus with `sscnn` compare to the time required for the `rnnlm` baseline?
