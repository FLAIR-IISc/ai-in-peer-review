Summary and contributions
This paper proposes using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for domain-relevance scoring of sentence pairs, to select in-domain training data for SMT when only very small in-domain samples are available. The approach uses a CNN over one-hot regions augmented with pre-trained word embeddings learned from large unlabeled general-domain corpora; the classifier scores both source and target sentences and selects top-scoring sentence pairs for SMT training. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and three social-media domains show consistent improvements over strong baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) An effective, practical data-selection method based on semi-supervised CNNs that yields sizable SMT gains, especially in the low in-domain data regime (as few as 100 sentences).
2) A comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple language pairs/domains vs. established LM-based baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination), including significance testing and stability analysis.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains in realistic settings with scarce in-domain data. The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) improves BLEU over training on all data by +3.1 (zh→en sms), +1.4 (ar→en tweets), +0.7 (en→es Facebook), and +1.4 (en→zh Facebook), outperforming one-hot CNN and all LM-based selection baselines with significance markers. The result that sscnn surpasses training on a known 120K in-domain subset in zh→en sms is notable.
- Clear demonstration of robustness to tiny in-domain sets. With a fixed 300K selection budget, sscnn maintains sizable gains even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU on zh→en), with low variance over multiple random samples (std ~0.12). This directly addresses an important practical pain point.
- Sensible design and alignment with prior CNN text classification literature. The architecture (region-based CNN, max pooling, ReLU) and semi-supervised use of pre-trained embeddings are grounded in established work, increasing plausibility and reproducibility. The choice to score both source and target and sum the scores is reasonable for bilingual selection.
- Breadth and scale of evaluation. Experiments span four language directions and three challenging social media domains, with large training pools (9–15M sentence pairs) and standard phrase-based SMT configurations. Significance testing via bootstrap resampling is appropriate.
- Practicality. The method requires only a small in-domain dev set and publicly available toolkits (conText, word2vec), making it easy to adopt.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is limited. Conceptually, this is an application of CNN-based text classification with pre-trained embeddings to SMT data selection. While the application is valuable and the empirical results are strong, the modeling contribution beyond prior CNN domain classification work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang, 2015) is modest.
- Baseline coverage could be broader. The comparisons are limited to LM-based selection. Missing baselines include: mixture/instance weighting approaches (Foster & Kuhn, Foster et al.), vector space/distributed VSM methods (Chen et al., 2013/2014), and simpler discriminative classifiers (e.g., tf-idf + linear models). These would strengthen claims that CNNs are preferable in the tiny in-domain setting.
- Potential tuning leakage and selection-size determination. The paper uses the dev set as in-domain positive data and states that the number of selected sentences is determined on “held-out data,” but the split of dev into train/held-out is not described. Without a clear protocol, there is risk of overfitting selection size and hyperparameters to the same domain evidence later used for translation tuning/testing.
- Limited ablations on key design choices. Although the paper states that source, target, and summed scores were evaluated and that the sum works best, no numbers are provided. Similarly, there is no ablation isolating the contribution of pre-trained embeddings vs. one-hot features across tasks, or of bow vs. seq regions, window sizes, and embedding dimensionality.
- Computational aspects are underreported. Training and applying CNNs to score tens of millions of sentences can be resource-intensive. The paper does not report training time, inference throughput, or memory footprint, nor compare efficiency against LM-based selection, which is important for deployability at scale.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete. Some important implementation specifics are missing: tokenization/casing, vocabulary sizes and pruning, how OOVs are handled in embeddings, whether embeddings are trained separately per language (and on which corpora), and whether any data from the test domain leaks into embedding training.

Questions to authors
- How exactly did you split the in-domain dev set between training the CNN and tuning hyperparameters and selection size? Was there a separate held-out subset distinct from the data used to train the classifier and from the MT tuning set?
- Can you provide quantitative ablations for: source-only vs. target-only vs. sum; with vs. without pre-trained embeddings; bow-only vs. seq-only inputs; and sensitivity to region size and embedding dimension?
- What are the runtime characteristics? Please report training time per classifier, scoring speed (sentences/sec), and hardware. How do these compare to Moore-Lewis and RNNLM-based selection?
- For tasks without known in-domain subsets, can you provide qualitative examples of selected vs. rejected sentences, and any classifier accuracy/precision-recall on a small labeled holdout?
- Did you calibrate the CNN scores (e.g., via Platt scaling) given the balanced training and highly imbalanced test distribution? If not, do you observe that ranking is robust across domains?
- Are the word embeddings trained per language side separately? On which corpora (parallel sides or additional monolingual)? Any effect of using bilingual embeddings?

Additional comments
- It would be interesting to explore using the CNN scores as instance weights (rather than hard selection), or to integrate them into mixture/instance-weighted SMT training.
- A bilingual or Siamese CNN that ingests the sentence pair jointly might capture cross-lingual cues beyond independent source/target scoring.
- Releasing code and selection lists for at least one language pair would strengthen reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated empirical paper with practical impact. While the modeling novelty is incremental, the results in the tiny in-domain regime are compelling and broadly useful.
