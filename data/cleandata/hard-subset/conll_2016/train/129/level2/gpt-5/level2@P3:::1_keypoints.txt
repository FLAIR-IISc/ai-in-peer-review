- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) that combines one-hot inputs with region embeddings learned from large unlabeled corpora to score source and target sentences and select parallel data for SMT adaptation.
- Uses top-scored bilingual pairs to train phrase-based SMT when only tiny in-domain labeled samples are available.
- Evaluated on four language-direction/domain pairs (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M candidate parallel sentences.
- Shows consistent, statistically significant BLEU gains over training on all data and over strong LM-based selectors (n-gram, RNNLM, combined).
- Demonstrates robustness in extremely limited supervision, retaining improvements down to 100 in-domain labeled sentences and low variance across resamples.
- Addresses a practically important, underexplored regime of domain adaptation with very small in-domain labeled sets (100–800 sentences).
- Empirical gains are consistent and statistically tested across typologically diverse languages and social-media domains.
- Stability is supported by learning-curve analysis and low standard deviation (~0.12 BLEU) across tiny-data resamples.
- Method is simple and general: uses unlabeled data for embeddings, no prior domain labels for the pool, and standard SMT pipelines.
- Bilingual scoring (source+target sum) and random negative sampling are sensible design choices that appear effective.
- Missing implementation and preprocessing details (tokenization, segmentation, normalization, vocab sizes, OOV handling, optimizer, epochs, regularization, class balancing) hinder reproducibility.
- Limited ablation studies: no per-task source-only vs. target-only vs. sum results, no isolated test of pre-trained region embeddings vs. one-hot, and no analysis of negative-sampling strategy.
- Potential data/reuse ambiguity: dev set appears to be used as the positive in-domain sample and it's unclear if it was also used for SMT tuning or selecting N, risking leakage.
- Baseline coverage is narrow: lacks simple strong baselines (TF-IDF cosine, linear SVM/logistic, fastText), instance-weighting, or VSM-based selection to contextualize CNN advantages.
- Practicality and efficiency are not quantified: training/scoring cost for 9–15M pairs and throughput comparisons with LM-based selection are missing.
- Requests clarification on preprocessing per language and corpora used to train embeddings, and whether embedding data overlapped with dev/test.
- Asks whether the same dev set was used for selector training and SMT tuning/selection of N, and how leakage was prevented.
- Requests per-task results for source-only, target-only, and source+target score combinations to verify consistency of summation gains.
- Requests detailed CNN training hyperparameters (optimizer, batch size, epochs, early stopping, regularization, vocab cutoff) and effect of varying negative-sample ratios.
- Requests runtime estimates: training time and scoring throughput on largest settings and comparison with n-gram and RNNLM pipelines.
- Suggests adding missing implementation/preprocessing details and clarifying data-split/tuning protocol for reproducibility.
- Suggests lightweight ablations (score combination, embedding contribution) and including a strong linear baseline (e.g., TF-IDF + logistic) where possible.
- Recommends reporting computational cost, practical guidance for choosing N without large held-out sets, and optionally releasing selected-subset indices.
- Overall assessment: useful, well-motivated contribution with convincing empirical gains in scarce-data domain adaptation, but would be substantially strengthened by clarifications and minimal ablations.
