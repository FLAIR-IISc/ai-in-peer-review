Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for bilingual data selection to adapt phrase-based SMT when only tiny amounts of in-domain text are available. A CNN classifier is trained with in-domain (positive) and randomly sampled general-domain (negative) sentences; word embeddings pretrained on large unlabeled corpora are incorporated (“semi-supervised CNN”). The classifier scores all general-domain sentence pairs on source and target sides, and top-ranked pairs (by sum of scores) are selected to train the SMT system. Experiments across four language directions and three social-media domains show consistent improvements over a strong “all data” baseline and over LM-based selection methods (Moore–Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). The method remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, simple semi-supervised CNN framework for bilingual data selection that is effective with extremely small in-domain seeds, yielding consistent SMT gains over strong LM-based selection baselines.
2) A comprehensive empirical evaluation across 4 translation directions with large training corpora, demonstrating robustness (including with only 100 labeled sentences) and statistically significant improvements.
3) Evidence that combining source- and target-side CNN scores improves selection; analysis and discussion of why convolution + pretrained embeddings help for domain relevance.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with practical impact: The method improves BLEU by up to +3.1 over alldata (zh→en sms), and by +0.7–1.4 on other tasks without known in-domain components. Improvements over the best LM-based baseline are statistically significant in most settings. The 100-sentence experiment is compelling (avg +2.1 BLEU; low std 0.12), underscoring suitability for fine-grained, low-resource adaptation.
- Solid baselines and breadth: The paper compares against well-established selection methods (Moore–Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, combined), and evaluates in four directions (zh↔en, ar→en, en→es) with large training pools (9–15M pairs), social-media test domains, and proper significance testing (bootstrap per Koehn).
- Clear methodological framing: Training separate source and target CNNs and summing scores is straightforward and reproducible; hyperparameters and toolkits are provided. The choice of semi-supervised CNN is well-motivated by prior text classification successes and the low-label regime.
- Robustness to tiny labels: The performance trend as the in-domain seed shrinks is informative; CNN-based methods degrade gracefully and outperform LM-based methods especially below 800 sentences, aligning with the paper’s core claim.
- Insightful qualitative rationale: The discussion on why convolution + max-pooling and embeddings (capturing local cues and lexical similarity) improve domain relevance detection is convincing.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and selection tuning: The paper states the number of selected sentences is “determined by the performance on held-out data,” but the only in-domain labeled set appears to be the dev set, which is also used for SMT tuning. If the same dev set is used both to (i) train the selector, (ii) tune selection size N, and (iii) tune SMT, there is risk of overfitting to dev characteristics. Clarify the held-out protocol; otherwise reported gains may be optimistic.
- Baseline parity and details: It is unclear whether LM-based baselines use bilingual selection on both sides and how their combination is tuned (equal weights may be suboptimal). RNNLM configuration (hidden size, training data size, vocab) and smoothing choices could materially affect results. Matching the number of selected sentences across methods, or plotting BLEU vs. selection size curves for all methods, would better isolate method quality.
- zh→en “sms” case may overstate generality: The largest gains coincide with the presence of a 120k in-domain subset in the pool. While this is a realistic setting, the method may largely rediscover that subset. Reporting recall/precision of retrieving the known in-domain subset, and contrasting with LM-based retrieval, would clarify the advantage. On tasks without such a component, gains are modest (≤1.4 BLEU).
- Missing ablations: It is not disentangled how much of the gain comes from pretrained embeddings vs. the CNN architecture. Comparisons to (a) one-hot CNN vs. CNN with randomly initialized embeddings, (b) simple embedding-based similarity (e.g., averaged word vectors cosine) and (c) source-only vs. target-only scoring, would strengthen the causal story. The claim that sum(source,target) is best is not numerically supported in the paper.
- Reproducibility and technical specifics: Important details are missing, including tokenization/casing, OOV and vocabulary handling for CNNs, training corpora for word2vec (per language, monolingual vs. bilingual), scoring normalization across sentence lengths, runtime/throughput for scoring millions of sentences, and whether negative sampling for CNN training is stratified beyond uniform random.

Questions to authors
- What exact held-out set is used to choose the selection size N? Is it distinct from the dev set used for CNN training and SMT tuning?
- Did LM-based baselines use bilingual selection (both source and target) with cross-entropy difference, and were their combination weights tuned?
- How were word2vec models trained (corpus, tokenization, vocabulary size, min-count) for each language? Are embeddings shared across tasks?
- What is the relative contribution of embeddings vs. CNN? Can you report ohcnn vs. sscnn gaps across all seed sizes, and results for source-only vs. target-only scores?
- What is the computational cost (training and inference) of the selector, and how does it scale with corpus size?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider comparisons to alternative selection strategies (e.g., VSM/distributed VSM and grouped VSM from Chen et al. 2013/2014, submodular selection), and to data weighting approaches, to broaden context.
- Provide BLEU vs. selection size curves for all methods to show robustness across N, and a qualitative analysis of selected sentences.
- If feasible, test the selector for NMT data selection, which is increasingly relevant.
- Clean up reference formatting and typos (e.g., “GloV e”, inconsistent author-year markers) to improve professionalism.
- Releasing code and selection lists would significantly aid reproducibility.

Overall, this is a timely and practically useful contribution. Addressing the evaluation protocol and baseline parity, and adding ablations, would substantially strengthen the paper.
