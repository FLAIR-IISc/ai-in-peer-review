Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes a semi-supervised convolutional network (CNN) for selecting in-domain parallel data to adapt phrase-based SMT when only tiny in-domain samples are available. The classifier combines one-hot inputs with region embeddings learned from large unlabeled corpora and scores both source and target sentences; top-scored pairs are used to train SMT.
- Provides a comparative evaluation on four language directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with 9–15M parallel sentences, showing consistent and statistically significant gains over training on all data and over strong LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, combined).
- Demonstrates robustness under extremely limited supervision, with improvements even when the in-domain labeled set is reduced to 100 sentences (e.g., +2.1 BLEU on zh→en), and reports low variance across resampled tiny sets.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important and underexplored regime: domain adaptation with very limited labeled in-domain data. Prior selection work (e.g., Moore-Lewis; Axelrod et al.; Duh et al.) assumes thousands to >100k in-domain sentences, whereas this work shows effectiveness with 100–800 sentences.
- Consistent, statistically supported improvements across multiple directions and domains. For zh→en sms, the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) yields 26.0 BLEU vs. 22.9 for using all data (+3.1), and outperforms the best LM-based selector (25.7) by +0.3 (p<0.01). For ar→en tweets, 19.0 vs. 17.6 (+1.4), +0.6 over the best LM-based baseline; for en→es Facebook, 27.5 vs. 26.8 (+0.7), +0.5 over LM; for en→zh Facebook, 11.4 vs. 10.0 (+1.4), +0.9 over LM. The use of bootstrap significance testing strengthens the evidence.
- Demonstrates stability and effectiveness in the tiny-data regime. The learning-curve study (zh→en) shows that sscnn maintains a performance margin over LM-based selection as the in-domain set shrinks below 800 sentences, and remains effective at 100 sentences (+2.1 BLEU), with low standard deviation (~0.12 BLEU across resamples).
- Simple, general recipe with realistic constraints. The method requires no prior domain labels for the large pool, leverages unlabeled data for embeddings, and uses standard SMT training; it is applicable across typologically diverse languages and social-media domains.
- Sensible design choices for bilingual selection. Scoring both source and target sides and using the sum appears effective; using randomly sampled negatives simplifies training without harming results.

Weaknesses
- Missing implementation and preprocessing details hinder reproducibility. The description lacks tokenization/segmentation and normalization specifics (especially for Chinese word segmentation and Arabic morphology), vocabulary sizes, OOV handling, optimizer, learning rate, number of epochs, early stopping, regularization (e.g., dropout), and class-balancing strategies beyond 1:1 negatives. These details are important for replicating the classifier and understanding its robustness.
- Limited ablations. While ohcnn vs. sscnn is compared, there is no systematic ablation of (a) source-only vs. target-only vs. sum scoring (claimed best, but no per-task numbers); (b) effect of pre-trained region embeddings alone vs. one-hot alone within the sscnn architecture; (c) negative-sampling strategy across rounds. These would clarify which factors drive gains.
- Potential data/reuse ambiguity for tuning. The dev set is used as the positive in-domain sample to train the selector. In SMT, dev is typically used for feature tuning/validation; the paper mentions determining the number of selected sentences on “held-out data” but does not specify whether this is the same dev set. Clarification is needed to rule out overfitting or optimistic selection of N.
- Baseline coverage could be broader. Comparisons are restricted to LM-based selection. Strong, inexpensive text classification/selectors (e.g., TF-IDF cosine similarity at the sentence level, linear SVM/logistic regression on n-grams, or fastText) are absent; prior adaptation techniques such as mixture-model/instance-weighting (Foster & Kuhn; Foster et al.) or VSM-based selection (Chen et al.) are cited but not evaluated. Including a simple linear baseline would help quantify the specific advantage of CNNs beyond embeddings.
- Practicality and efficiency not quantified. The cost of training the CNN and scoring 9–15M sentence pairs is not reported, nor is throughput vs. LM-based selection. Since selection must scale, a brief runtime/memory profile and discussion of batching/indexing would aid adoption.

Questions to authors (for clarification during rebuttal)
- What preprocessing was applied per language (tokenization/segmentation, normalization, casing), and what corpora were used to train word embeddings for Arabic and Chinese? Were embeddings trained on data disjoint from dev/test?
- Is the same dev set used both to train the selector and to tune SMT model weights and/or to select N? If so, what measures ensure no leakage or overfitting (e.g., a separate held-out set for selecting N)?
- Provide per-task results for source-only, target-only, and source+target score combination. Are the observed gains from summation consistent across all four tasks?
- Training details for the CNNs: optimizer, batch size, epochs, early stopping criterion, regularization (dropout/L2), and vocabulary cutoff. Did increasing the number of negative samples beyond 1:1 help?
- Runtime: approximate training time and scoring throughput on the largest setting; comparison with n-gram and RNNLM selection pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include the missing implementation/preprocessing details and clarify the data split/tuning protocol. This is essential for reproducibility and fair assessment.
- Add a light ablation on score combination and embedding contribution; at minimum, report source-only vs. target-only vs. sum per task. If space permits, a comparison with a strong linear baseline (e.g., TF-IDF + logistic regression) would strengthen the methodological case.
- Report computational cost and provide practical guidance (e.g., how to choose N without a large held-out set, or using classifier score distributions to set thresholds). Optionally, release indices of selected subsets to facilitate replication.

Overall, the paper makes a useful, well-motivated contribution to SMT domain adaptation under scarce in-domain supervision, with convincing empirical gains and a method that is broadly applicable. Addressing the clarifications and adding minimal ablations would substantially strengthen the submission.
