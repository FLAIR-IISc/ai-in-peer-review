Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN classifier for SMT data selection when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The classifier scores both source and target sentences; top-scoring pairs are used to train an adapted SMT system.
- Contributions as I see them: (1) A practical semi-supervised CNN architecture that combines one-hot regions with embeddings learned from large unlabeled corpora, and aggregates source/target scores for bilingual selection; (2) A thorough empirical study across four language directions and three social-media domains, showing consistent and statistically significant gains over strong LM-based selection baselines; (3) An analysis of the tiny-data regime demonstrating effectiveness with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Strengths
- Strong, multi-setting empirical evidence: Gains up to +3.1 BLEU over training on all data and consistent improvements over Moore-Lewis/RNNLM baselines across four directions, with significance testing. The zh→en SMS result is particularly compelling and replicates across multiple small in-domain samples (low variance).
- Addresses a high-impact, practical scenario: The tiny in-domain regime is common in real applications (e.g., social media). The method demonstrably works with 100–800 sentences where LM-based selection degrades or plateaus.
- Sound modeling choice and clear design: Using CNNs to capture local domain cues and semi-supervised word embeddings to generalize to unseen domain terms is well-motivated and aligns with recent text-CNN literature.
- Sensible bilingual scoring: Empirical finding that summing source and target scores helps is intuitive and useful to practitioners.
- Reasonable reproducibility: Public datasets, toolkits and key hyperparameters are reported; significance testing is conducted.

Weaknesses
- Missing scalability analysis: No measurement of training/inference cost for classifying 9–15M sentences (both sides). LM-based scoring is extremely fast; the compute trade-off should be quantified.
- Limited ablations: The paper does not isolate contributions of (i) embeddings vs. one-hot, (ii) bow vs. seq regions, (iii) source-only vs. target-only vs. sum, beyond a brief statement. An ablation table would strengthen the causal claims.
- Selection-size tuning: The number of selected sentences is tuned per task on held-out data; this risks overfitting and reduces plug-and-play usability. An automatic stopping criterion (e.g., score thresholding or validation proxy) is desirable.
- Modern applicability: Evaluation is only on phrase-based SMT; results on a standard NMT system (even small-scale) would improve the paper’s relevance and demonstrate method generality.
- Reproducibility gaps: No code or selected subsets released; tokenization/normalization for noisy social media data are not detailed, which can materially affect results.

Questions to authors
- What is the end-to-end runtime for training the classifier and scoring the full corpus per domain/language, and what hardware was used?
- How sensitive are results to embedding source/quality (e.g., domain-mismatched embeddings)?
- Can you report ablations for source-only vs. target-only vs. sum and for bow vs. seq regions?
- Could you provide an automatic criterion for selecting N (e.g., score threshold calibrated on dev)?
