## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the challenge of limited in-domain resources. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The selected sentences are then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating the effectiveness of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation scenarios. The authors show consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based methods, across four language pairs and three test domains. A significant finding is the ability to achieve substantial gains even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences, highlighting the method's practicality for fine-grained topic-specific adaptation. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation of the benefits of incorporating pre-trained word embeddings within the CNN framework, particularly when in-domain data is limited.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results demonstrating consistent and statistically significant improvements over competitive baselines. The BLEU score gains, particularly in low-resource settings, are noteworthy.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – domain adaptation with limited in-domain data – is highly relevant to real-world MT applications, especially in emerging domains like social media translation.
*   **Clear Methodology:** The proposed approach is well-defined and clearly explained, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is thorough, including appropriate baseline comparisons and statistical significance testing.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The discussion section provides valuable insights into why the semi-supervised CNN approach performs well, attributing it to the CNN’s ability to capture domain-specific features and the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While the application of CNNs to data selection is effective, the core CNN architecture itself isn't novel. The primary innovation lies in the specific application to this low-resource SMT adaptation problem and the semi-supervised training approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions meta-parameters were tuned, but lacks specific details about the tuning process (e.g., search space, optimization method). Providing more details would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis of the data selection process would be beneficial. Understanding *which* types of sentences are correctly/incorrectly classified could offer further insights and guide future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN, including the search space and optimization method used?
2.  Did you investigate the impact of different pre-trained word embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText) on performance?
3.  Can you provide examples of sentences that were correctly/incorrectly classified by the CNN, and discuss potential reasons for these outcomes?



Overall, this is a solid paper with strong empirical results and practical relevance. While the core CNN architecture isn't groundbreaking, the application to low-resource SMT adaptation is well-motivated and effectively demonstrated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
