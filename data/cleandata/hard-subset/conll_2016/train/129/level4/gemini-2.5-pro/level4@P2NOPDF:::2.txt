This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier. Following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015), the proposed method aims to identify sentence pairs from a large, general-domain corpus that are most similar to a small, in-domain dataset. The authors report that their CNN-based approach outperforms traditional language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points. A key strength highlighted is the method's robustness, as it remains effective even when the available in-domain data is extremely limited.

While the paper claims an improvement of up to 3.1 BLEU, the presentation of this result could be clarified. The results table indicates that a baseline system trained exclusively on the in-domain data already achieves a +2.8 BLEU improvement over the general-domain system. This suggests that the primary gain comes from the presence of in-domain data itself, rather than solely from the selection method. The actual contribution of the data selection appears to be around 0.3-0.5 BLEU over this strong in-domain baseline. To better situate the contribution, it would be valuable to compare the proposed method against other standard domain adaptation techniques, such as interpolating language and translation models trained separately on in-domain and out-of-domain data.

A more compelling result, and a key selling point of the paper, is the demonstration that the CNN classifier remains effective with very little in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs). In my opinion, this is the most impressive finding. However, this experiment was conducted only for the Chinese-to-English task. The paper notes that this task is unique in that its general-domain training set already contains some in-domain data, which might make the selection task easier. To substantiate the claim of robustness more broadly, it would be highly beneficial to replicate this experiment across all language pairs, particularly the English-to-Spanish and English-to-Chinese tasks where the training data is purely out-of-domain. The existing results for these tasks, which use development sets of only a few hundred sentences, already hint that the method is effective, but an explicit experiment with a 100-sentence training set for the selector across all tasks would make the conclusion much stronger.

The paper focuses on the social media domain, which presents unique translation challenges, such as a high rate of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words due to non-standard spellings, slang, and special tokens like hashtags, usernames, and emoticons. The paper does not specify whether any special preprocessing or filtering was applied to handle these phenomena. Given that data selection alone cannot mitigate the OOV problem, it would be insightful to include a qualitative analysis of the improvements. For instance, what types of translation errors are being corrected by adapting the SMT system with the selected data? Providing a few translation examples from the baseline and the proposed system would help illustrate the practical benefits of the method beyond the BLEU score.

**Questions and Suggestions for Clarification:**

*   **Section 3.2 (Model Architecture):** The description of how the different input vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined within the CNN architecture could be clearer. An architectural diagram or illustration would be very helpful for readers to fully understand the model.
*   **Section 3.2 (Loss Function):** The paper mentions a "designated loss function" but does not specify which one was used (e.g., cross-entropy). Please clarify this detail.
*   **Section 5.2 (Tuning):** For completeness and reproducibility, please specify the method used for tuning the SMT system weights (e.g., MERT, PRO).
