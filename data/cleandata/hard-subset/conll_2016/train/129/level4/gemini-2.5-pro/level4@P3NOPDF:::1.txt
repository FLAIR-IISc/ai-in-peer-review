This paper proposes a novel approach for machine translation training data selection, utilizing a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to score and rank sentences from a general-domain corpus for their relevance to a specific domain. The paper is well-written, the method is clearly described, and the experiments largely support the authors' claims.

**Strengths**

*   **Motivation and Rationale:** A key strength of this work is its well-articulated motivation for using a CNN. The authors convincingly argue that a CNN architecture is particularly well-suited for this task, as it can identify salient n-gram features indicative of in-domain text, effectively focusing on the most relevant parts of a sentence. This provides a clear advantage over models that treat the sentence as an undifferentiated sequence or bag of words.
*   **Context and Comparison:** The paper is well-contextualized within existing literature. The comparisons against established data selection methods, such as those based on n-gram and continuous language models, are thorough and effectively demonstrate the proposed method's advantages.
*   **Clarity of Description:** The technical description of the CNN model, including its use of one-hot vectors versus semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed. The authors do a good job of explaining the components of their model.

**Points for Clarification and Improvement**

While the paper is strong, several areas could be improved with further clarification or experimentation, which would significantly strengthen the work.

1.  **Experimental Rigor and Reproducibility:** The experimental methodology requires further clarification. The process for determining the optimal number of selected sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set, is not fully described. It is crucial for the authors to specify:
    *   Which validation data was used for this purpose?
    *   On what data were the CNN hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of layers) tuned?
    *   A sensitivity analysis regarding these hyperparameters would also strengthen the paper's conclusions by showing how robust the model is to these choices.

2.  **Fairness of Comparison:** The comparison in Table 2 could be made more direct and compelling. Currently, different methods select a different number of sentences, which complicates a direct comparison of BLEU scores. A more controlled experiment, where all methods are constrained to select the same number of sentences, would provide a clearer demonstration of the proposed method's superiority. While Figure 1 suggests the authors' approach would still prevail in such a scenario, presenting this in a tabular format would be more rigorous and convincing.

3.  **Ablation Study on Input Representation:** While the paper proposes a combination of Bag-of-Words (BOW) and sequence-based (SEQ) representations, it lacks an ablation study to experimentally validate this choice. It would be insightful to see a comparison of the performance of models using only BOW, only SEQ, and the combination of both. This would clarify the relative contribution of each representation and justify the complexity of the combined model.

4.  **Comparison to Bilingual Selection Methods:** The paper focuses on monolingual scoring but does not explicitly compare against established bilingual selection techniques. The method of summing the source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to the Moore-Lewis method, which uses the sum of differences in cross-entropies between in-domain and general-domain language models. Acknowledging this connection (e.g., around line 435) and potentially including it as a baseline would provide valuable context for the results.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Model Visualization:** The textual description of the CNN is good, but a diagram illustrating the architecture would significantly improve clarity. Visualizing the layers and showing how the different input representations are combined would complement the text effectively.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** To provide more qualitative insight, the authors could include a few example sentences that were uniquely selected by the proposed CNN method compared to the baselines. This would help substantiate the claims made in Section 5.4 regarding the model's ability to abstract away from surface forms and identify topically relevant content.
*   **Additional Baseline:** Regarding the discussion in Section 5.4 on abstracting from surface forms, an interesting additional baseline could be the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaces some words with POS tags to reduce data sparsity. Comparing against such a method could further highlight the specific advantages of using pre-trained word embeddings over other forms of abstraction.
*   **Future Work on Bilingual Modeling:** For future work, it would be interesting to explore extending the model to a truly bilingual setting. Instead of simply summing the source and target scores, one could potentially learn the optimal weights for this combination or even design a unified bilingual CNN architecture that considers the parallel sentence pair jointly.
*   **Citation Formatting:** Please review the citation formatting for consistency (e.g., using `\citet` vs. `\citep` appropriately, as noted around line 385).

Overall, this is a solid paper with a well-motivated and promising approach. Addressing the points above, particularly those related to experimental clarification and the fairness of the comparison, would make it an even stronger contribution to the field.
