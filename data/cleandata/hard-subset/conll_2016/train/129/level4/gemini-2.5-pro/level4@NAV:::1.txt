This paper introduces a method for MT training data selection that employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier to score and rank sentences from a general domain. The authors provide a solid comparison to previous work utilizing n-gram or continuous space language models. However, it remains unclear whether the proposed method was also benchmarked against bilingual data selection techniques, such as those based on the sum of cross-entropy differences.

While the initial motivation for selecting a CNN over an RNN/LSTM architecture was not immediately apparent, the paper effectively argues for this choice by highlighting the CNN's ability to identify and weigh the importance of specific sentence segments. A notable omission, however, is an experimental ablation study to determine the relative importance of the Bag-of-Words (BOW) versus the sequential (SEQ) representations, or their combination, and to explain the results.

The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their CNN model, covering its use of one-hot and semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings and highlighting key architectural aspects. Nevertheless, the manuscript would be significantly enhanced by including a diagram illustrating the network's layers and the flow of data, which would offer more immediate clarity than the text alone.

Overall, the paper is well-written, although there are minor formatting issues with citations where `\citep` might be more appropriate than `\citet` (e.g., line 385).

The experimental results successfully support the paper's central claims. However, I have a concern regarding the methodology used to determine the quantity of selected sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set:
- The paper does not specify which validation data was used for this purpose, nor does it detail the dataset used for tuning the CNN's hyperparameters. The sensitivity of the models to these choices is also not discussed.
- For a more direct comparison, Table 2 should present results for all methods using an identical number of selected sentences. Based on Figure 1, it appears the proposed approach would still demonstrate superior performance under such a controlled comparison.

Other comments:
- An experiment evaluating the proposed technique against the baselines with a larger volume of in-domain data, beyond just the development set, would be a valuable addition.
- Including qualitative examples of sentences selected by the different methods in the results or discussion would provide concrete support for the claims made in section 5.4.
- To further substantiate the claim in section 5.4 about abstracting from surface forms, a comparison to the work of Axelrod (2015) would be insightful. This would test whether word2vec embeddings offer benefits beyond simpler abstraction methods like replacing words with POS tags to reduce language model data sparsity.
- The approach of summing source and target classification scores bears a strong resemblance to the bilingual language model data selection method of Moore and Lewis (2010), which sums the difference of cross-entropies. Citing this related work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- Finally, as a point of future consideration, it would be interesting to explore whether the weights for combining the source and target classification scores could be learned directly by extending the CNN model to a bilingual, parallel data setting.
