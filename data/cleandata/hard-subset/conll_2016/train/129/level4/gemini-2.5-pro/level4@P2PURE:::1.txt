This paper proposes a data selection approach for Machine Translation that uses a CNN classifier to score and rank sentences from a general-domain parallel corpus. The comparison to prior work, particularly methods based on n-gram or continuous-space language models, is well-executed. However, it is unclear whether the authors also compared their method against bilingual data selection techniques, such as those based on the difference of cross-entropies.

A key strength of the paper is its justification for using a CNN over an RNN/LSTM architecture. The authors argue that a CNN is well-suited to identifying specific, highly-indicative segments within a sentence, which is a more nuanced approach than evaluating the sentence as a whole. Despite this strong motivation, the paper lacks an experimental ablation study to determine the relative importance of the bag-of-words (BOW), sequential (SEQ), and combined representations, or to explain why one might be superior to the others.

The description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot vectors or pre-trained semi-supervised embeddings, is clear and detailed. Nevertheless, the exposition could be significantly enhanced by including a diagram illustrating the network's layers and showing how inputs are processed and combined.

Overall, the paper is well-written, though there are minor formatting issues, such as the unnecessary use of `\citet` where `\citep` would be appropriate (e.g., line 385).

While the experiments and evaluation generally support the paper's claims, I have a concern regarding the method for determining the number of selected sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set.
*   The paper does not specify which validation set was used for this purpose. It is also unclear what data was used for tuning the CNN models' hyperparameters and how sensitive the models are to these choices.
*   For a more direct and fair comparison, Table 2 should report scores for all methods using the same number of selected sentences. Based on Figure 1, it appears the proposed approach would still outperform the baselines under such a controlled comparison, which would further strengthen the paper's claims.

Other Comments:
*   It would be insightful to see an experiment comparing the proposed technique against baselines when a larger in-domain dataset is available, beyond just the development set.
*   Including qualitative examples of sentences selected by the different methods would provide concrete support for the claims made in the discussion in Section 5.4.
*   Regarding the argument in Section 5.4 about abstracting from surface forms, a relevant baseline to consider would be the work of Axelrod et al. (2011), who replace some words with POS tags to reduce language model data sparsity. Comparing against such a method would help clarify the additional advantages provided by word2vec embeddings.
*   The method of summing source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to the bilingual Moore-Lewis data selection method (sum of cross-entropy differences). A reference to this work would be appropriate around line 435.
*   Finally, as a direction for future work, have the authors considered extending the CNN model to a bilingual setting to directly learn the optimal weights for combining the source and target scores, rather than simply taking their sum?
