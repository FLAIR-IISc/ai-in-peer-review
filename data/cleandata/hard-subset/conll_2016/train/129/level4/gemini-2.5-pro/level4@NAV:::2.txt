This work presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network classifier, leveraging the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, in contrast to other methods, maintains its effectiveness even when provided with a very small in-domain dataset.

The authors claim an improvement of 3.1 BLEU points. However, a closer look at the results indicates that gains of this scale are achieved only when in-domain data is present in the training set; training exclusively on the in-domain data already yields a +2.8 BLEU improvement. It might be insightful to also compare this approach to a system that interpolates separate models trained on in-domain and out-of-domain data.

A more compelling finding, in my opinion, arises from the second experiment, which shows that the CNN classifier remains effective with minimal in-domain data. This experiment, however, is conducted solely on the zh-en task, which includes genuine in-domain data within its training set, a factor that might simplify the selection process. A key question is whether this result would hold for the other tasks where the training set lacks in-domain data. The results for the en-es and en-zh tasks, whose development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs, already suggest this might be the case. The paper's central claim would be more robustly supported if results were reported for all tasks when only 100 sentence pairs are used for classifier training.

The translation of social media text introduces distinct challenges not common to other domains, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-standard spelling (at least for Latin-based scripts). These texts also frequently contain special sequences such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. Was any specialized preprocessing or filtering applied to the data to handle these characteristics?

Given that data selection does not inherently solve the OOV problem, it would be valuable to understand in greater detail the nature of the improvements gained from adaptation via data selection, perhaps by providing illustrative examples.

The following points pertain to specific sections:

**Section 3.2:**
*   The manuscript could more clearly articulate how the different vector representations (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are integrated within the model. A diagram illustrating the architecture would be highly beneficial.
*   The term "designated loss function" is ambiguous; please specify the exact loss function that was utilized.

**Section 5.2:**
*   For completeness and reproducibility, the paper should mention how the system weights were tuned.
