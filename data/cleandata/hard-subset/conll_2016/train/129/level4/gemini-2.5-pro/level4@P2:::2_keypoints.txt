- Proposed method: semi-supervised CNN trains a domain classifier from a very small in-domain seed and random negatives to score and select relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus for SMT adaptation.
- Contribution 1: Empirically demonstrates robust data selection with extremely limited seed data (as few as 100 sentence pairs).
- Contribution 2: Shows strong empirical gains across multiple language pairs/domains by applying a CNN-based classifier for SMT data selection.
- Strength 1: Tackles the practical, important problem of domain adaptation when in-domain parallel data is scarce (e.g., social media).
- Strength 2: Excellent low-resource performance—CNN methods remain stable where LM-based selection degrades, with up to 2.1 BLEU gain using only 100 seed sentences.
- Strength 3: Comprehensive experiments on four language directions and three domains with large corpora, with consistent, statistically significant improvements over strong LM baselines.
- Weakness 1: Improvement magnitude may be misleading on zh→en because a 120K in-domain subset already exists; sscnn’s 26.0 BLEU vs in-domain-only 25.7 BLEU suggests modest net gain beyond finding that subset.
- Weakness 2: The "tiny data" robustness claim is supported by a single zh→en SMS experiment possibly aided by that in-domain subset; generalizability to other tasks is not fully established.
- Weakness 3: Evaluation is BLEU-only with no qualitative examples, leaving unclear which error types or phenomena (e.g., slang, OOVs) are actually improved by selection.
- Question 1: Was any special preprocessing/filtering applied for non-standard social media tokens (usernames, hashtags, emoticons, unconventional spelling)?
- Question 2 & 3: Please clarify CNN implementation details—how wl(x) and ul(x) are combined in Eq.2, the training loss used for the classifier, and how SMT feature weights were tuned (e.g., MERT and tuning set).
