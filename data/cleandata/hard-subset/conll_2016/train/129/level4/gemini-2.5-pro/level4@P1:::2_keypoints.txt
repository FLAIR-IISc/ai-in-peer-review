- Proposal: Semi-supervised CNN classifier for in-domain data selection in SMT — train on few in-domain vs random general sentences, score large corpus, select top sentences to adapt SMT.

Contributions:
- Shows CNN-based data selection remains effective with very small in-domain seeds (as few as 100 sentences), outperforming LM-based methods in low-resource scenarios.
- Semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforms strong LM-based baselines across four language pairs and three social media domains, with statistically significant BLEU gains.

Strengths:
- Very robust with extremely scarce seed data (e.g., 2.1 BLEU gain over `alldata` with 100 seeds), while LM methods degrade below ~800 sentences.
- Thorough experimental evaluation across diverse language pairs (zh-en, ar-en, en-es, en-zh) and challenging social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook).
- Semi-supervised variant using pre-trained embeddings (`sscnn`) outperforms one-hot model (`ohcnn`) by ~0.5 BLEU, showing benefit from unlabeled corpora.

Weaknesses:
- The 3.1 BLEU improvement on zh-en SMS is potentially misleading because the general corpus contains a 120k true in-domain subset; gains may reflect finding that subset rather than selecting similar-style sentences.
- The key low-seed robustness claim is mainly validated on zh-en (which may be an outlier); the seed-size ablation should be replicated for other language pairs without known in-domain subsets.
- Architectural description is unclear—specifically how one-hot inputs and pre-trained region embeddings are combined in the convolutional layer; needs clearer text/diagram for reproducibility.

Questions to authors:
- Was any special preprocessing or filtering applied for social media data (handling OOVs, usernames, hashtags, emoticons), and can you provide qualitative examples of translation improvements?
- What specific loss function was used to train the CNN classifier in Section 3.2?
- How were SMT system weights tuned for each data selection condition—was MERT run on the dev set per condition?
