This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, applying the framework from Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, unlike competing methods, demonstrates robustness even when only a very small in-domain dataset is available for training the classifier.

The paper claims improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, this gain is measured against a baseline trained without any in-domain data. A system trained exclusively on the in-domain data already achieves a 2.8 BLEU point improvement, which suggests that the main gain comes from simply including this data, rather than from the selection method itself. It would be more informative to compare the proposed selection method to a system that interpolates separate models trained on in-domain and out-of-domain data, which is a more common and competitive baseline for domain adaptation.

In my view, the most compelling result is from the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier's effectiveness even with very little in-domain training data. However, this experiment was only conducted on the zh->en task, where the general training corpus already contains some in-domain data, potentially simplifying the selection task. To strengthen this claim, it would be beneficial to see if these results hold for the other language pairs (en->es and en->zh), where no in-domain data is present in the general training set. The existing results for these tasks, which use small development sets of only a few hundred sentence pairs, already hint that the method is robust. Reporting results for all tasks when training the classifier on just 100 sentence pairs would provide much stronger support for this conclusion.

Translating social media text presents unique challenges not typically found in other domains, most notably a high OOV rate due to non-standard spelling (at least for Latin scripts). This type of text also frequently contains special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Was any specialized preprocessing or filtering applied to the data to handle these phenomena?

Given that data selection does not directly address the OOV problem, it would be insightful to understand the specific types of translation improvements gained through this adaptation. Providing a few examples of source sentences and their translations from the baseline and adapted systems would help clarify the benefits of the proposed method beyond BLEU scores.
