### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a novel method for training data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) aimed at domain adaptation, particularly when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The core of the approach is a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) trained as a binary classifier to distinguish in-domain sentences from general-domain sentences. The classifier is trained using the small in-domain dataset as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. To overcome the data scarcity of the in-domain set, the model incorporates pre-trained word embeddings (learned from a large unlabeled corpus) as features, making it a semi-supervised approach. This trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in a large general-domain corpus for their relevance to the target domain. The top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The authors conduct experiments on four language pairs across three social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating that their method significantly outperforms strong baselines, including SMT systems trained on all available data and systems using data selected by state-of-the-art language model-based techniques.

### Contributions
1.  **A highly effective data selection method for SMT using a semi-supervised CNN.** The primary contribution is the novel application of this architecture to the data selection task. The paper demonstrates that this approach is particularly powerful in low-resource adaptation scenarios (e.g., with as few as 100 in-domain seed sentences), where it significantly outperforms established language model-based methods. The use of pre-trained word embeddings is critical to its success in this setting, allowing the model to generalize beyond the limited vocabulary of the seed data.

2.  **A strong and convincing empirical evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains.** The paper validates its claims with a thorough experimental setup on four different translation tasks. The results show consistent and significant BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 points) over multiple strong baselines. The analysis of the model's performance as the size of the in-domain seed data decreases (Figure 1) provides compelling evidence of the method's robustness and practical value.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent empirical results and strong performance in a challenging setting.** The most compelling argument for acceptance is the method's performance. It consistently outperforms not only the baseline trained on all general-domain data but also three state-of-the-art language model-based selection methods across all tested conditions. The improvements are substantial and statistically significant. The demonstration that the method remains effective with only 100 in-domain sentences is a particularly strong result that highlights its practical utility.

2.  **Well-motivated use of the CNN architecture for this task.** The paper provides a clear and persuasive justification for choosing a CNN. The argument in Section 5.4—that CNNs with max-pooling can identify key domain-specific n-grams regardless of their position and that pre-trained embeddings help generalize from seen words to unseen but semantically similar ones (e.g., "Dell" to "Lenovo")—offers a strong intuition for why this method surpasses n-gram or RNN-based models.

3.  **The work addresses a practical and relevant problem.** The paper tackles the important challenge of domain adaptation when collecting large amounts of in-domain parallel data is expensive or impossible. The ability to achieve significant gains with a very small seed dataset makes this approach highly applicable for adapting MT systems to emerging domains (like social media) or to numerous fine-grained topics where data collection for each is not feasible.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Ambiguity in the experimental protocol for model and data selection.** The procedure for determining the optimal number of selected sentences is a significant concern. The paper states this is "based on experimental results on a separate validation set" (line 443), but this validation set is not identified. It is unclear if this set is disjoint from the `dev` set used to train the CNN classifier. This ambiguity could be a potential confound. Furthermore, Table 2 compares systems trained on different amounts of data, which hinders a direct comparison of the selection methods' quality. A more rigorous comparison would involve fixing the data size for all methods and reporting their respective performance.

2.  **Lack of architectural ablation and visualization.** While the description of the CNN model is clear, it lacks experimental justification for some design choices. For example, the model uses both bag-of-words and sequential region representations as input, but no ablation study is presented to show the relative contribution of each. A diagram of the network architecture, illustrating how the one-hot and pre-trained embedding inputs are combined, would also greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

3.  **Limited comparison to related bilingual selection methods.** The paper presents strong comparisons against monolingual LM-based selection methods. However, it is not clear if these baselines were implemented in their bilingual form (e.g., summing scores from source and target language models), as is common practice. The proposed method's use of a sum of source and target scores is conceptually very similar to bilingual extensions of the Moore-Lewis method, and a direct comparison to such a baseline would be necessary to fully establish the superiority of the CNN-based approach.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify which "separate validation set" was used to determine the optimal number of selected sentences for each method reported in Table 2? Was this set held out from the `dev` set used for training the CNN classifier? Similarly, on what data were the CNN hyperparameters tuned?

2.  Your method scores sentence pairs by summing the classification scores from the source and target sides. Did your language model baselines (`ngram`, `rnnlm`) also use a bilingual selection criterion (e.g., summing the cross-entropy difference scores from source and target LMs)? An explicit comparison to a strong bilingual LM baseline would be very informative.

3.  Building on the previous question, have you considered extending your CNN model to be truly bilingual, for instance, by having a shared final layer that takes inputs from both a source and a target CNN to produce a single, joint classification score? This seems like a natural extension and could potentially capture bilingual domain-specific cues.
