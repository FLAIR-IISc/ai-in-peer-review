### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) by using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to select relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The approach is specifically designed to be effective in low-resource scenarios where only a very small amount of in-domain seed data is available. The authors train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and general-domain sentences and then use this classifier to score and rank all sentences in the general-domain corpus, selecting the top-scoring pairs to train the final SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A highly effective data selection method for low-resource domain adaptation.** The primary contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN for data selection, which is shown to be particularly powerful when the in-domain seed data is extremely limited (e.g., a few hundred sentences). The reported results, showing significant BLEU score improvements over strong language model-based baselines in this setting, represent a valuable contribution to the field of SMT adaptation.

2.  **A strong empirical validation of the proposed technique.** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across four different language pairs and three distinct social media domains. The analysis showing the method's robustness as the amount of in-domain seed data decreases (Figure 1) is particularly compelling and strengthens the paper's claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Strong Empirical Results:** The key strength of this paper is its novel and well-motivated application of semi-supervised CNNs to the data selection task. This approach yields substantial and statistically significant improvements over established baselines, including n-gram and recurrent neural network language models. The gains are especially impressive in the very low-resource setting (e.g., a 2.1 BLEU improvement using only 100 in-domain sentences for the zh-en task), which is a challenging and practical problem.

2.  **Convincing Rationale for the CNN Architecture:** The authors provide a clear justification for choosing a CNN. The argument that a CNN's convolution and max-pooling operations are well-suited to identify salient, domain-specific n-grams regardless of their position in a sentence is intuitive and well-explained. This provides a good theoretical grounding for why this architecture might outperform sequential models like RNNs for this classification-based selection task.

3.  **Well-Motivated Semi-Supervised Approach:** The use of pre-trained word embeddings within a semi-supervised framework is a crucial and well-justified component of the model. Given the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, leveraging large unlabeled corpora to learn rich word representations is essential for generalizing beyond the specific words seen in the small seed set. This helps the model recognize semantically similar words and makes the classifier more robust.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Methodology:** The process for determining the optimal number of selected sentences is not sufficiently explained. The paper states that this number was determined "based on experimental results on a separate validation set" (line 443), but it is unclear what this validation set is. This is a critical detail, as the performance of all data selection methods is sensitive to this cutoff. Similarly, the paper does not specify what data was used for tuning the CNN hyperparameters. This lack of detail makes the experiments harder to reproduce and assess.

2.  **Inconsistent Comparison in Main Results Table:** The primary results presented in Table 2 are difficult to compare fairly, as each method uses a different amount of selected training data. While this likely reflects individual tuning for each method, it prevents a direct, apples-to-apples comparison of the quality of the selected data. For a more direct evaluation of the selection criteria, a comparison where all methods select the same number of sentences would be much more informative. Although Figure 1 provides such a comparison for one task, this should have been the standard for the main results table.

3.  **Absence of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis of the data selected by the different methods. The discussion in Section 5.4 hypothesizes why the CNN performs well (e.g., by focusing on domain-specific words and abstracting away from surface forms via embeddings). Presenting a few example sentences that were correctly selected by the proposed `sscnn` method but missed by the LM-based baselines would provide concrete evidence to support these claims and offer deeper insight into the model's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify which dataset was used as the validation set for tuning CNN hyperparameters and for determining the optimal number of sentences to select for each method in Table 2? Was this a held-out portion of the provided development set, or a separate dataset entirely?

2.  The strategy of summing the source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to bilingual data selection methods that use the sum of cross-entropy differences from in-domain and general-domain language models. Did you consider comparing your method against such a bilingual LM-based baseline?

3.  Section 5.4 argues that word embeddings help abstract away from surface forms, which is particularly useful in low-resource settings. Another approach that attempts to mitigate data sparsity is that of Axelrod et al. (2015), who replace some words with their POS tags. Could you comment on how you see your method's advantages over such a technique, which does not rely on pre-trained embeddings?

4.  Following up on the use of source and target scores, have you considered learning the combination weights for these scores instead of using a simple sum? For instance, this could be achieved by extending the CNN model to a bilingual setting that jointly processes the source and target sentence.
