This paper proposes a method for machine translation (MT) training data selection that uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier to score and rank sentences from a general-domain corpus. The comparison to prior work, particularly methods based on n-gram or continuous-space language models, is well-executed. However, it is unclear whether the authors also compared their approach against bilingual data selection techniques, such as those based on the sum of cross-entropy differences.

The motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was not immediately apparent. However, a key strength of the paper is its argument that CNNs are well-suited for this task because they can effectively identify which parts of a sentence are most indicative of its domain. This is a compelling justification. Nevertheless, the paper does not provide an experimental analysis to determine whether the BOW, SEQ, or combined representation is most effective, nor does it explain the potential reasons for any performance differences.

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including its use of one-hot vectors and pre-trained semi-supervised embeddings, is clear and detailed. However, the explanation would have been significantly improved by a diagram illustrating the network's layers and how inputs are processed and combined.

The paper is well-written overall, though there are minor formatting issues with citations (e.g., using `\citet` where `\citep` would be more appropriate, as on line 385).

The experiments and evaluation generally support the paper's claims. However, I have some concerns about the methodology, particularly regarding the use of a separate validation set to determine the number of sentences to select (line 443):
- The paper does not specify which validation data was used for this purpose. It is also unclear what data was used to tune the CNN models' hyperparameters, and there is no analysis of the models' sensitivity to these choices.
- For a more direct and fair comparison, Table 2 should present results for all methods using the same number of selected sentences. While Figure 1 suggests that the proposed approach would still outperform the baselines in a fixed-size scenario, including these results in the table would strengthen the paper's claims.

Additional Comments and Suggestions:
- It would be beneficial to see a comparison against the baselines in a setting with a larger in-domain training set, rather than only using the development data for this purpose.
- To support the claims in Section 5.4 regarding the model's ability to abstract away from surface forms, the discussion would be enhanced by including a few example sentences selected by the proposed method versus the baselines.
- Further strengthening this point, an additional baseline could be included based on the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced some words with POS tags to reduce language model sparsity. This would help clarify whether the performance gains from word2vec embeddings go beyond those achievable with simpler lexical abstractions.
- The approach of summing source and target classification scores is analogous to the bilingual data selection method of Moore and Lewis (2010), which uses the sum of cross-entropy differences. A citation to this work would be relevant around line 435.
- As a potential direction for future work, the authors might consider extending the CNN model to a bilingual setting to learn the optimal weights for combining the source and target classification scores, rather than relying on a simple sum.
