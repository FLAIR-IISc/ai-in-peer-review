**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes a data selection method for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). Following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015b), the authors train a CNN classifier on a small set of in-domain sentences (positive examples) and general-domain sentences (negative examples). This classifier then scores all sentences in a large general-domain corpus for their relevance to the target domain, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are used to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN classifier is highly effective for data selection in SMT, particularly in scenarios with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences). In this low-resource setting, the method shows significantly more robustness and better performance than established language model-based approaches.
2.  A strong empirical evaluation across four language pairs and three social media domains, showing that the proposed method consistently and significantly outperforms strong data selection baselines, including those based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models.

**Strengths**
1.  The method's primary strength is its robustness in very low-resource adaptation scenarios. Figure 1 clearly illustrates that while the performance of LM-based methods degrades sharply when the in-domain seed set is smaller than 800 sentences, the proposed `sscnn` approach remains effective, yielding a 2.1 BLEU improvement from only 100 sentences. The stability of this result is also verified. This makes the approach highly valuable for adaptation to novel or fine-grained domains where collecting large in-domain corpora is impractical.
2.  The proposed `sscnn` model achieves state-of-the-art results, consistently outperforming strong and widely-used data selection baselines across all four tested language pairs. The improvements over the best LM-based methods are statistically significant in most cases (Table 2), validating the hypothesis that the feature representations learned by the CNN are better suited for identifying domain relevance than perplexity-based scores.
3.  The paper is supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The evaluation spans multiple language directions (zh-en, ar-en, en-es) and challenging social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating the broad applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The claimed 3.1 BLEU improvement for the zh2en task is potentially misleading. As the authors note, the general-domain training data for this task already contains a 120K-sentence in-domain subset. Training a system on this subset alone yields a +2.8 BLEU gain over the `alldata` baseline. This suggests the primary achievement is locating this existing data rather than adapting a general model. The contribution would be stronger if compared against a baseline that explicitly handles this data composition, such as a system with interpolated in-domain and general-domain models.
2.  The most impressive result—the method's effectiveness with tiny amounts of in-domain data—is only experimentally validated for the zh2en task. This is the one task where a large, clean in-domain corpus is known to exist within the training set, which may make the selection task artificially easier. To fully substantiate this key claim, the experiment from Figure 1 should be replicated on the other tasks, which do not have this characteristic.
3.  The description of the model architecture and training process lacks important details. In Section 3.2, it is unclear how the one-hot vectors and the pre-trained region embeddings are combined in Equation 2. An architectural diagram would greatly enhance clarity. Furthermore, crucial details such as the loss function for the CNN classifier and the tuning method for the SMT system weights are omitted.
4.  The paper does not address the specific challenges of translating social media text, such as high OOV rates from non-standard spellings, usernames, and hashtags. Since data selection does not directly solve the OOV problem, it would be valuable to understand what types of translation improvements are being made. A qualitative analysis with examples would help clarify whether the gains come from better lexical choice for in-vocabulary words, improved grammar, or other factors.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify the model architecture in Section 3.2? Specifically, how are the one-hot representation `wl(x)` and the embedding-based representation `ul(x)` combined as input to the convolutional layer? What was the specific loss function used to train the CNN classifier?
2.  The results in Figure 1 are very compelling but are only presented for the zh2en task. Could you provide results for the other language pairs to demonstrate that the method's robustness in low-resource settings generalizes to cases where no large in-domain subset is present in the training data?
3.  The experiments focus on social media domains, which are known for noisy text. Did you apply any special text normalization or preprocessing steps to the data? Could you provide a few translation examples that illustrate the kinds of improvements your method achieves compared to the baselines?
