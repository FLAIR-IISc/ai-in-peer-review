- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to classify in-/out-of-domain sentences and select the top-scoring general-domain sentences for SMT adaptation, yielding up to +3.1 BLEU.  
- Demonstrates the approach is especially effective in very low-resource in-domain settings (100–800 sentences), outperforming LM-based methods.  
- Strong empirical results across four language pairs and three domains, consistently beating a strong baseline trained on all data.  
- Well-motivated use of CNNs: convolution + pooling can identify domain-specific phrases regardless of position.  
- Clear, detailed technical description of one-hot and semi-supervised CNNs and justified use of pre-trained embeddings.  
- Thorough comparison to relevant baselines, using original authors' code where available.  
- Unclear and potentially unfair experimental comparison because the number of selected sentences was tuned on an unspecified validation set and varies across methods.  
- Missing comparisons/acknowledgement of related work (e.g., Moore & Lewis bilingual selection) and methods that address sparsity (e.g., POS replacement).  
- Insufficient ablation and qualitative analysis: no breakdown of bag-of-words vs sequential inputs and no example sentences showing where the CNN wins.  
- Presentation could be improved with a CNN architecture diagram and fixed citation formatting.  
- Question: Clarify which validation set was used for selecting the number of sentences and tuning CNN hyperparameters, and sensitivity to those choices.  
- Question: How does the method perform when larger in-domain data (thousands of sentences) is available — does the advantage shrink?  
- Question: Have you considered a bilingual CNN or jointly learned/weighted combination of source and target scores instead of independent scoring and summation?
