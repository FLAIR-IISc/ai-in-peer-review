This paper presents a novel approach for machine translation training data selection, utilizing a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier to score and rank sentences from a general-domain corpus. The comparison to monolingual data selection methods, such as those based on n-gram or continuous-space language models, is thorough and well-executed. However, the experimental setup appears to lack a comparison against established bilingual data selection techniques, such as the cross-entropy difference method.

The paper provides a compelling motivation for using a CNN over recurrent architectures, arguing effectively that a CNN's structure is well-suited to identifying salient n-grams that indicate a sentence's domain relevance. This is a strength of the paper. The textual description of the CNN architecture—including the one-hot and pre-trained embedding input representations—is clear and detailed. However, its clarity would be significantly enhanced by a diagram illustrating the network's layers and how inputs are processed. Furthermore, a notable omission in the analysis is an experimental comparison to determine the relative importance of the Bag-of-Words (BOW) and sequence (SEQ) representations, or their combination.

While the experiments generally support the paper's claims, there are concerns regarding the experimental methodology. The process for determining the optimal number of sentences to select, which relies on a separate validation set (line 443), requires clarification. Specifically, the paper should detail:
- Which validation set was used for this purpose?
- On what data were the CNN's hyperparameters tuned, and how sensitive are the models to these choices?

For a more direct and fair comparison, Table 2 should report performance metrics for all methods using an identical number of selected sentences. Based on Figure 1, it appears the proposed method would still maintain its advantage under such a condition, which would make the results even more convincing.

The paper is generally well-written, though care should be taken with citation formatting (e.g., parenthetical citations are used on line 385 where textual citations, `\citet`, would be more appropriate).

Other Comments and Suggestions:
*   The results or discussion section would benefit from a qualitative analysis, such as presenting example sentences selected by the different methods to intuitively support the claims made in Section 5.4.
*   Regarding the argument in Section 5.4 about abstracting from surface forms: an additional strong baseline would be the work of Axelrod et al. (2015), who replace some words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity. Comparing against this would help clarify whether the word2vec embeddings provide an advantage beyond simpler abstraction techniques.
*   The approach of summing source and target classification scores is conceptually very similar to the Moore and Lewis (2010) data selection method (sum of cross-entropy differences). A reference to this related work would be appropriate around line 435.
*   It would be interesting to see an evaluation where more in-domain data is available (beyond just the development set) to assess how the technique compares to the baselines under different data conditions.
*   As a final thought, I wonder if it would be possible to learn the weights for combining the source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting.
