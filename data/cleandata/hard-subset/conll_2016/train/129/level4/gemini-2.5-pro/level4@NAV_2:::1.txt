**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a data selection method for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, which is particularly effective when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The core of the method is a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) trained to classify sentences from a general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. This classifier is then used to score all sentences in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The novel application of a semi-supervised CNN for the task of bilingual data selection in SMT. This approach is shown to be highly effective for adaptation, especially in a low-resource scenario where the in-domain seed data may consist of as few as 100 sentences.
2.  A strong empirical evaluation across four language pairs and three distinct domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results demonstrate that the proposed CNN-based method significantly outperforms strong baselines trained on all available data, as well as three state-of-the-art data selection methods based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Robustness:** The paper presents compelling and well-supported experimental results. The proposed semi-supervised CNN method achieves substantial BLEU score improvements (up to +3.1) over a strong baseline trained on all data, while using only a small fraction of the training corpus (3-10%). Crucially, it also consistently outperforms established language model-based data selection techniques. The experiments demonstrating that the method remains effective even when the in-domain seed set is reduced to just 100 sentences are particularly convincing and highlight the robustness of the approach.
2.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The work addresses the highly practical and challenging problem of domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain resources. The motivation for using a CNN—to capture key domain-specific phrases or n-grams irrespective of their position in a sentence—is clearly articulated and provides a sound justification for the model architecture choice over purely sequential models.
3.  **Clarity of Methodological Description:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the one-hot CNN and the semi-supervised CNN models. The intuition that pre-trained word embeddings can compensate for the lack of labeled in-domain data by capturing semantic similarities is well-explained and central to the success of the semi-supervised approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Hyperparameter Tuning and Validation:** The paper states that the number of selected sentences was determined "based on experimental results on a separate validation set," but provides no details on this set. It is also unclear what data was used to tune the CNN's hyperparameters (e.g., region size, stride, number of neurons) and how sensitive the model is to these choices. This omission makes it difficult to fully assess the experimental methodology and hinders reproducibility.
2.  **Inconsistent Comparison in Main Results Table:** In Table 2, the various methods are compared using different numbers of selected sentences, which makes a direct, fair comparison of the selection methods themselves challenging. For example, in the `zh2en` task, the proposed `sscnn` method uses 300K sentences while the `comblm` baseline uses 400K. While Figure 1 presents a more controlled comparison for a single task, the main results table should ideally compare all methods on an equal footing by fixing the amount of selected data.
3.  **Missing Comparison to a Key Bilingual Baseline:** The paper compares its approach to several LM-based methods but seems to have overlooked a strong and directly analogous baseline. The strategy of summing the source and target scores is conceptually similar to the widely used bilingual data selection method that sums the cross-entropy differences from in-domain and general-domain language models (e.g., Moore and Lewis, 2010; Axelrod et al., 2011). A comparison against such a baseline would have provided a more complete picture of the proposed method's advantages.
4.  **Absence of Qualitative Analysis:** The discussion in Section 5.4 makes claims about why the CNN approach is superior (e.g., capturing domain-specific words and leveraging semantic similarity via embeddings). This section would be significantly more convincing if it included a few qualitative examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the CNN approach but missed by the LM-based methods, providing concrete evidence to support these claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify what data was used as the "separate validation set" for tuning both the number of selected sentences and the CNN's hyperparameters? How was this set constructed to ensure it was independent of the development set (used as positive training examples) and the final test set?
2.  Your method of summing the source and target classification scores proved effective. Did you consider or experiment with extending the CNN architecture to a truly bilingual model that processes the source and target sentences jointly to produce a single classification score, potentially learning the optimal way to combine information from both sides?
3.  The paper focuses on the "tiny amount of in-domain data" scenario. How does the performance advantage of your method over the LM-based baselines change as the amount of available in-domain seed data increases (e.g., to 10k or 50k sentences)? Does the advantage of the semi-supervised CNN approach persist, or do the methods begin to converge in performance?
