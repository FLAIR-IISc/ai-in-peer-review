- Novel motivation: argues convincingly that CNNs better capture salient local features/key phrases for in-domain relevance than RNNs.  
- Clear architecture: CNN design and use of one-hot or pre-trained embeddings are described in detail.  
- Baselines: provides solid comparisons against established monolingual n-gram and continuous-space selection methods.  
- Missing bilingual comparison: fails to compare against bilingual selection methods (e.g., Moore & Lewis 2010), despite conceptual similarity.  
- BOW vs SEQ unclear: no ablation or analysis to determine the relative contribution of Bag-of-Words and sequential CNN representations.  
- Validation ambiguity: unclear which validation set determined the number of selected sentences and which data tuned CNN hyperparameters; sensitivity not analyzed.  
- Unequal-size comparisons: Table 2 compares methods that select different numbers of sentences, weakening fairness; fixed-size evaluations should be shown explicitly.  
- Diagram request: a CNN architecture diagram showing layers and input combination would improve clarity.  
- Qualitative examples requested: include sentences selected by the proposed method but missed by baselines (and vice versa) to support claims about abstraction.  
- More in-domain data experiment: test scalability by using more in-domain data beyond the development set.  
- Compare to Axelrod et al.: suggest comparing to methods that replace low-frequency words with POS tags to isolate benefits of pre-trained embeddings.  
- Bilingual extension: propose exploring a unified bilingual model that jointly weights source and target signals instead of summing independent scores.  
- Minor formatting: correct citation formatting issues (e.g., use \citet vs \citep where appropriate).
