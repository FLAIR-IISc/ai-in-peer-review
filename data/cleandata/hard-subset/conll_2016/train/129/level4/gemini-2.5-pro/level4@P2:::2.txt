### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for SMT domain adaptation that uses a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to select relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The key idea is to train a domain classifier using a very small set of in-domain sentences as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. This classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain corpus for relevance, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Demonstration of Robust Data Selection with Extremely Limited Seed Data:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that the proposed semi-supervised CNN approach is highly effective and robust even when the amount of in-domain seed data is minimal (e.g., as few as 100 sentence pairs). This addresses a challenging and practical scenario where traditional language model-based data selection methods degrade significantly.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Challenging Task:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across four language pairs and three social media domains, showing that the CNN-based selection method consistently and significantly outperforms strong, state-of-the-art language model-based baselines. While the CNN architecture itself is adapted from prior work in text classification, its successful application and rigorous evaluation for SMT data selection is a valuable contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the highly relevant problem of domain adaptation when in-domain parallel data is scarce. This scenario is common for fine-grained topic adaptation or when targeting new domains like social media, where collecting large, curated datasets is often infeasible or prohibitively expensive. The proposed method offers a practical and effective solution for this setting.

2.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The most compelling result is the method's robustness when the amount of in-domain seed data is tiny. As shown in Figure 1, while the performance of LM-based methods drops sharply when trained on fewer than 800 in-domain sentences, the CNN-based methods remain stable and effective. The ability to achieve a 2.1 BLEU improvement over a strong baseline using only 100 in-domain sentences for training the classifier is a very strong result that highlights the primary advantage of this approach.

3.  **Comprehensive and Strong Experimental Results:** The authors conduct experiments on four different language directions and three distinct domains, using large-scale training corpora (9-15M sentence pairs). The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines, including three strong LM-based methods, across all tasks (Table 2). The improvements are statistically significant and demonstrate the general applicability of the approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Misleading Claims on the Magnitude of Improvement:** The claimed 3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh2en task, while technically correct against the `alldata` baseline, warrants more nuanced analysis. The paper notes that the training data contains a 120K sentence in-domain subset, and a system trained on this subset alone achieves 25.7 BLEU. The proposed `sscnn` method achieves 26.0 BLEU. This suggests that much of the gain comes from simply identifying this pre-existing in-domain data, and the improvement over a simpler "in-domain only" baseline is modest (0.3 BLEU). A comparison against a baseline that interpolates models trained on the in-domain subset and the remaining general-domain data would provide a clearer picture of the method's contribution in this specific setting.

2.  **Generalizability of the "Tiny Data" Result is not Fully Established:** The paper's strongest claim—robustness with very little seed data—is supported by an experiment (Figure 1) conducted only on the zh2en sms task. As discussed above, this task is unique in that the general-domain corpus contains a large, clean in-domain subset, which might make the selection task easier. To fully substantiate this crucial claim, it would be beneficial to replicate this experiment on at least one of the other tasks where no such in-domain data is known to exist.

3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The evaluation relies entirely on BLEU scores, leaving the reader to speculate on the nature of the improvements. For social media domains, challenges like non-standard spelling, slang, and a high OOV rate are common. Since data selection primarily addresses lexical choice and style rather than the OOV problem, it would be highly informative to include examples of translations from the baseline and adapted systems. This would help clarify what kinds of errors are being fixed and provide deeper insight into the practical impact of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The test domains (tweets, Facebook posts) often contain non-standard language, such as usernames, hashtags, emoticons, and unconventional spelling. Was any special preprocessing or filtering applied to the data to handle these phenomena? Clarifying this would help in understanding the experimental conditions and potential OOV rates.

2.  The description of the semi-supervised CNN in Section 3.2 could be clearer. Could you please elaborate on how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and the pre-trained region embeddings (`ul(x)`) are combined in Equation 2? Are they concatenated before the dot product with a combined weight matrix, or handled differently? An illustration of the network architecture would be very helpful.

3.  For completeness, could you please specify the loss function used to train the CNN classifier? Additionally, how were the feature weights for the final phrase-based SMT systems tuned (e.g., was MERT used, and what was the tuning set)?
