**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a data selection method for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) domain adaptation, using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus for their relevance to a target domain. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier using a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain data as negative examples. Pre-trained word embeddings from a large unlabeled corpus are used to initialize the network, making the approach semi-supervised. This trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The approach is shown to be particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain seed data is available. Experiments across four language pairs show that the proposed method outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the demonstration that a CNN-based classifier for data selection is highly robust to a scarcity of in-domain seed data. The paper shows that the method maintains a significant performance advantage over strong baselines even when the classifier is trained on as few as 100 in-domain sentences. This makes the approach highly suitable for fine-grained or rapidly emerging domains where collecting large amounts of labeled data is impractical.

2.  A secondary contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN framework, similar to that used in text classification, to the SMT data selection task. The paper establishes that this approach is highly effective, consistently surpassing several state-of-the-art language model-based methods across four different language pairs and three distinct social media domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging and highly relevant problem of SMT domain adaptation in a low-resource setting. The ability to adapt a system using only a few hundred, or even one hundred, in-domain sentences is a significant practical advantage, especially for domains like social media where data is sparse and topics evolve quickly.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) method is shown to be highly effective, consistently outperforming three strong language model-based baselines (`ngram`, `rnnlm`, `comblm`) across all four tested language pairs. The improvements over the best LM-based method are statistically significant and range from 0.3 to 1.0 BLEU points, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed technique.

3.  **Robustness with Scarce In-Domain Data:** The most compelling evidence for the method's utility is presented in the experiment that varies the amount of in-domain seed data (Figure 1). While the performance of all methods degrades as the seed data size decreases, the CNN-based methods, particularly `sscnn`, are far more stable and maintain a substantial advantage. Achieving a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the `alldata` baseline with just 100 seed sentences is a very impressive result and strongly supports the paper's central claim.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potentially Misleading Headline Claim:** The claim of an improvement of "up to 3.1 BLEU" for the zh-en task needs to be contextualized. As the paper notes, the general-domain training data for this task already contains a 120K sentence in-domain subset. Training a system solely on this subset yields a BLEU score of 25.7. The `sscnn` method achieves 26.0, which is an improvement, but the vast majority of the 3.1 BLEU gain over the `alldata` baseline (22.9) is attributable to the presence of this in-domain data. It would be more transparent to compare against a baseline that interpolates models trained on the in-domain and general-domain portions separately.

2.  **Limited Scope of the Key Experiment:** The paper's strongest and most novel claim—that the method is effective with a tiny amount of in-domain data—is supported by experiments on only one of the four tasks (zh-en). As discussed above, this particular task may be an easier case for data selection due to the significant overlap between the in-domain test data and a subset of the training data. The claim of general robustness would be substantially stronger if this experiment were replicated on the other tasks, especially those where no known in-domain data exists in the training pool.

3.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Description:** The description of the semi-supervised CNN model in Section 3.2 could be improved. It is not immediately clear how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and the word-embedding-based segment vectors (`ul(x)`) are combined as input to the convolution layer. An architectural diagram would be very helpful for clarity. Furthermore, crucial details like the "designated loss function" used for training the classifier are not specified.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The experiment showing robustness with as few as 100 in-domain sentences is a key result. To strengthen the paper's claims of generality, could you report results for the other three tasks (ar-en, en-es, en-zh) under this minimal data condition?

2.  The social media domains (tweets, Facebook posts) present unique challenges like non-standard spelling, hashtags, and usernames, which can lead to high OOV rates. What, if any, specific text normalization or preprocessing was applied to the training and test data? Providing some qualitative examples of translation improvements would also be insightful.

3.  For reproducibility, could you clarify how the SMT system weights were tuned for the final evaluation? Was the provided development set used for MERT tuning in all conditions, including for the baseline systems?
