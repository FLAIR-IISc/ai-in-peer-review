This paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) by leveraging a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier. The approach follows the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015) to identify sentence pairs from a large out-of-domain corpus that are similar to a small, in-domain dataset. The experiments show that the proposed method outperforms a language model-based data selection baseline by approximately 0.5 BLEU points. A key strength of the method is its apparent robustness, maintaining its effectiveness even when the provided in-domain dataset is very small.

The most impressive result presented is the method's effectiveness with limited in-domain training data. The second experiment demonstrates that the CNN classifier remains a strong performer even when trained on only a few hundred sentence pairs. However, this experiment was conducted only on the zh-en translation task. This particular task is noted to have actual in-domain data within its general training set, which may make the selection task easier and could be a confounding factor. The claim of robustness would be significantly strengthened if this experiment were replicated across all translation tasks (en-es and en-zh). The results on these other tasks, which used development sets of only a few hundred sentence pairs, already suggest the method might generalize well, but explicitly testing the small training data scenario across all conditions would provide more conclusive evidence. Reporting results for all tasks when only 100 or a similarly small number of sentence pairs are used for training the classifier would make this a much stronger contribution.

While the paper claims an improvement of up to 3.1 BLEU points, the presentation of this result could be more nuanced. The results table shows that a baseline system trained exclusively on the in-domain data already achieves a gain of +2.8 BLEU over the out-of-domain baseline. This suggests that the majority of the reported improvement comes from the presence of the in-domain data itself, rather than solely from the data selection technique. The incremental gain from data selection is therefore closer to 0.3-0.5 BLEU over a strong in-domain-only baseline. To provide a more complete picture, it would be valuable to compare the proposed method against another common adaptation technique, such as a system that interpolates separately trained in-domain and out-of-domain translation models.

The paper focuses on translating social media text, a domain that presents unique challenges, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-conventional spelling, slang, and special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It is unclear if any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data to handle these phenomena. Since data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, a qualitative analysis of the improvements would be highly beneficial. Providing a few translation examples that showcase what kinds of errors are corrected (e.g., improved lexical choice for in-domain terms, better phrasing, more appropriate style) would offer deeper insight into the benefits of this adaptation approach.

Finally, some details regarding the methodology could be clarified to improve the paper's reproducibility and clarity:

*   **Section 3.2 (Model Architecture):** The description of how the different input vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined within the CNN architecture is not entirely clear. An illustration or diagram of the model architecture would be very helpful for readers to fully understand the data flow. Additionally, the paper mentions a "designated loss function" without specifying what it is (e.g., cross-entropy). This detail should be explicitly stated.

*   **Section 5.2 (Tuning):** For completeness, the paper should mention the method used for tuning the SMT system weights (e.g., MERT, MIRA, PRO). This is a standard detail in experimental descriptions for SMT.
