This paper proposes a data selection method for machine translation that uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to score and rank sentences from a general-domain corpus for their relevance to a specific domain. The paper is generally well-written, and the experiments support its central claims.

**Strengths:**

*   A key strength of the paper is its novel motivation for using a CNN. The authors convincingly argue that, unlike RNNs, a CNN is well-suited for identifying salient local features or key phrases within a sentence that signal its in-domain relevance.
*   The description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot vectors or pre-trained semi-supervised embeddings, is clear and detailed.
*   The paper provides a solid comparison against established monolingual data selection baselines, including methods based on n-gram and continuous-space language models.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns:**

*   A significant omission is the lack of comparison to bilingual data selection methods. The proposed technique of summing source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to the Moore and Lewis (2010) approach, which uses the difference in cross-entropies. A direct comparison to this or similar bilingual baselines is necessary to fully situate the contribution of this work. A reference to this related work would be appropriate around line 435.
*   While the paper introduces both Bag-of-Words (BOW) and sequential (SEQ) representations for the CNN, it lacks an ablation study or analysis to determine the relative importance of each. It would be valuable to understand which representation (or their combination) contributes more to the performance and why.
*   The experimental setup raises some concerns regarding clarity and fairness. Firstly, the procedure for determining the quantity of selected sentences (line 443) is based on a validation set, but it is not specified which validation set was used. Similarly, the paper does not mention the data used for tuning the CNN's hyperparameters or analyze the model's sensitivity to these choices.
*   Secondly, the main comparison in Table 2 is weakened by the fact that different methods select a different number of sentences. For a fair comparison, all methods should be evaluated on corpora of the same size. While Figure 1 suggests the proposed method would still outperform the baselines under a fixed-size condition, presenting this explicitly in a table would make the results much stronger.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   A diagram illustrating the CNN architecture, showing the layers and how inputs are combined, would greatly improve the paper's clarity and complement the textual description.
*   The discussion in Section 5.4 would benefit from concrete examples of sentences that were selected by the proposed method but missed by the baselines (and vice-versa). This would provide qualitative support for the claim that the CNN abstracts away from surface forms.
*   It would be interesting to see an experiment where more in-domain data is available for selection, beyond just the development set, to test the method's scalability and behavior in a data-rich scenario.
*   To further validate the claim about abstracting from surface forms (Section 5.4), a comparison against the work of Axelrod et al. (2011) could be insightful. Their method of replacing low-frequency words with POS tags also reduces data sparsity; comparing against it could help isolate the specific advantages of using pre-trained word embeddings for this task.
*   A potential direction for future work could be to extend the model to a truly bilingual setting. Instead of scoring source and target sentences independently and summing the scores, could the model learn to weigh the source and target signals jointly within a single, unified architecture?

**Minor Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written, but there are some minor citation formatting issues (e.g., using `\citep` when `\citet` would be more appropriate, as in line 385), which should be corrected.
