### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a data selection method for statistical machine translation (SMT) adaptation, particularly for scenarios with very limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain. This classifier then scores all sentences in a large general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train the SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a semi-supervised CNN for SMT data selection. The method proves highly effective, yielding significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) over strong baselines that use the full training data.
2.  A demonstration that this approach is particularly robust in low-resource settings, outperforming state-of-the-art language model-based methods when only a very small amount of in-domain seed data (e.g., 100-800 sentences) is available.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results in a Challenging Scenario:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of domain adaptation with only a tiny amount of in-domain data. The experimental results convincingly show that the proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms not only a strong baseline trained on all data but also three competitive language model-based selection methods across four language pairs and three domains.
2.  **Well-Motivated Choice of Model:** The paper provides a clear and compelling rationale for using a CNN. The argument that CNNs, via their convolution and pooling operations, are well-suited to identify key domain-specific phrases regardless of their position in a sentence (Section 5.4) is a key strength and helps explain the model's success.
3.  **Clear Technical Description:** The description of the one-hot and semi-supervised CNN models is clear, detailed, and highlights the important components. The methodology is easy to follow, and the use of pre-trained word embeddings to overcome data scarcity is well justified.
4.  **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The authors compare their method against strong and relevant baselines from prior work (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013), using the original authors' code where available. This makes the reported improvements more credible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Unfair Experimental Comparison:** A significant concern is the method for determining the number of selected sentences for each system. The paper states this number is determined "based on experimental results on a separate validation set" (line 443). This raises two issues:
    *   **Clarity:** It is unclear what validation data was used for this tuning process and for setting the CNN hyperparameters.
    *   **Fairness:** Table 2 compares different methods using different amounts of selected data (e.g., for zh2en, `ngram` uses 300K sentences while `comblm` uses 400K). A more direct and fair comparison would be to fix the number of selected sentences for all methods and compare their resulting BLEU scores. While Figure 1 suggests the proposed method would still win, the primary results table should reflect a controlled comparison.
2.  **Missing Comparisons to Relevant Work:** The evaluation could be strengthened by including other relevant baselines and acknowledging related techniques.
    *   The proposed method of summing source and target scores is conceptually very similar to the bilingual data selection method of Moore and Lewis (2010), which uses the sum of cross-entropy differences. This parallel should be acknowledged, and a comparison to that approach would be informative.
    *   The argument in Section 5.4 regarding the advantage of word embeddings (e.g., handling unseen words like "Lenovo") could be tested more directly. A comparison against a method like Axelrod et al. (2015), which replaces words with POS tags to reduce data sparsity in LMs, would help isolate whether the gains come from the CNN architecture itself or from the generalization power of word embeddings.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Ablation:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the model's behavior.
    *   The authors use both bag-of-words and sequential representations (`bow-regions` and `seq-regions`) as input to the CNN but do not provide an ablation study to show their relative importance.
    *   The claims in Section 5.4 about how the model works are insightful but would be much more powerful if supported by qualitative evidence, such as examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the LM-based methods.
4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** While generally well-written, the paper's presentation could be improved. A diagram illustrating the CNN architecture, showing how the one-hot vectors and pre-trained embeddings are combined, would significantly improve clarity. Additionally, there are minor citation formatting issues (e.g., incorrect use of parenthetical citations like `\citep` where textual citations `\citet` are needed).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify which validation set was used to determine the optimal number of selected sentences for each method (line 443) and to tune the CNN hyperparameters? How sensitive are the final results to these hyperparameter choices?
2.  The paper focuses on the "tiny amount of in-domain data" setting. How does your method compare to the baselines when a larger amount of in-domain data (e.g., several thousand sentences) is available? Does the advantage of the semi-supervised CNN diminish?
3.  The current method computes scores for the source and target sentences independently and sums them. Have you considered extending the CNN model to a bilingual setting to learn a joint representation or a weighted combination of the source and target scores directly?
