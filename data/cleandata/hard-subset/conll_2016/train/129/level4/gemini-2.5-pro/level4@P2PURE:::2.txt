This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method outperforms a language model-based selection baseline by approximately 0.5 BLEU points. Notably, unlike other methods, the CNN-based approach demonstrates robustness even when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available for training the classifier.

The authors claim an improvement of 3.1 BLEU points. However, this figure is potentially misleading, as the results show that improvements of this magnitude are only achieved on a task where in-domain data is already present in the general-domain training set. In fact, a system trained exclusively on the in-domain data already achieves a +2.8 BLEU gain over the general-domain baseline. A more informative comparison would be against a system that interpolates separately trained in-domain and out-of-domain models.

A more compelling finding, in this reviewer's opinion, is presented in the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier's effectiveness with very limited in-domain data. This experiment, however, was only conducted on the Chinese-to-English task, which contains in-domain data within its training corpus, potentially simplifying the selection task. It is unclear if this robustness would extend to the other tasks (English-to-Spanish and English-to-Chinese) where the training corpus lacks such in-domain data. The existing results for these tasks, which use development sets of only a few hundred sentence pairs, already suggest the method is robust. The paper's claim could be substantially strengthened by reporting results for all tasks when the classifier is trained on a similarly small set of, for instance, 100 sentence pairs.

Translating social media text presents unique challenges, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-conventional spelling, as well as the presence of special tokens like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It would be beneficial to clarify whether any special preprocessing or filtering was applied to the data to handle these phenomena.

Since data selection does not inherently address the OOV problem, a qualitative analysis would be valuable. Providing translation examples could help illustrate the specific kinds of improvements (e.g., in lexical choice or phrasing) that are gained through this adaptation method.

The following remarks concern specific sections:

**Section 3.2:**
*   The description of how the different input vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined in the model could be improved. A diagram of the model architecture would be very helpful for clarity.
*   The paper should specify which "designated loss function" was used.

**Section 5.2:**
*   For completeness, the paper should describe the method used for tuning the system weights.
