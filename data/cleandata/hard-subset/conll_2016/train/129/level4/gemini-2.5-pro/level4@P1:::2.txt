This paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier. The approach is based on the text classification framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015b). The core idea is to train a CNN to distinguish between a small set of in-domain sentences and randomly sampled general-domain sentences. This trained classifier is then used to score all sentences in the large general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  It demonstrates that a CNN-based data selection method is highly effective even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available for training the classifier. In these low-resource scenarios, the proposed method is shown to be significantly more robust and effective than traditional language model-based selection methods.
2.  The proposed semi-supervised CNN method consistently outperforms several strong language model-based baselines across four language pairs and three social media domains, achieving statistically significant improvements in BLEU score.

### Strengths

1.  The most compelling result is the method's robustness when in-domain seed data is extremely scarce. Figure 1 clearly shows that while LM-based methods degrade significantly with fewer than 800 sentences, the proposed CNN approach maintains a strong advantage, achieving a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the `alldata` baseline with only 100 seed sentences. This makes the approach highly practical for fine-grained adaptation or adaptation to novel domains where collecting large in-domain corpora is infeasible.
2.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering four diverse language pairs (zh-en, ar-en, en-es, en-zh) and three distinct and challenging social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The consistent improvements across all conditions provide solid evidence for the general applicability and effectiveness of the proposed technique.
3.  The semi-supervised approach, which leverages pre-trained word embeddings, proves to be clearly beneficial. The `sscnn` model consistently outperforms the `ohcnn` model by an average of 0.5 BLEU, highlighting the value of incorporating information from large unlabeled corpora, especially when the labeled (in-domain) data is limited.

### Weaknesses

1.  The headline claim of a 3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh-en SMS task is somewhat misleading. The paper notes that the general-domain corpus for this task already contains a 120k sentence in-domain subset, and a system trained only on this subset achieves a BLEU score of 25.7 (+2.8 over the `alldata` baseline). This suggests that much of the gain comes from the classifier successfully identifying this pre-existing, perfectly in-domain data, rather than selecting sentences that are merely *similar* to the in-domain style from a heterogeneous corpus. A more informative baseline for this specific task would be a system that interpolates models trained on the known in-domain and out-of-domain portions of the data.
2.  The most impressive claim—the method's effectiveness with very little seed data—is only experimentally validated on the zh-en task. As noted above, this task may be an outlier due to the presence of a large, clean in-domain subset in the training data, which could make the selection task easier. To strengthen this central claim, the experiment varying the amount of in-domain seed data (Figure 1) should be replicated for the other language pairs, particularly those without a known in-domain component in their training sets. The results for en-es and en-zh, which use dev sets of only a few hundred sentences, already hint that the method is effective, but an explicit ablation study would be more convincing.
3.  The description of the semi-supervised CNN architecture in Section 3.2 is unclear. It is difficult to understand precisely how the one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and the pre-trained region embeddings (`ul(x)`) are combined as input to the convolutional layer in Equation 2. An architectural diagram or a more detailed textual description would significantly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The social media domains tested are known to have high OOV rates and unconventional orthography (e.g., usernames, hashtags, emoticons). Was any special preprocessing or filtering applied to the training or test data? Since data selection alone cannot address OOV issues, it would be very insightful to see a brief qualitative analysis or some examples of the translation improvements. What kinds of errors are being fixed by the adapted systems?
2.  In Section 3.2, what was the "designated loss function" used to train the CNN classifier?
3.  For the experiments in Section 5, could you clarify how the SMT system weights were tuned? Was MERT run on the development set for each data selection condition?
