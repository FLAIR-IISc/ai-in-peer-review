The paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building upon the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). This approach yields an improvement of approximately 0.5 BLEU points over language model-based data selection and demonstrates robustness even when only a small in-domain dataset is available.

However, a closer examination of the results reveals that the claimed improvement of 3.1 BLEU points may be overstated. Upon reviewing the results, it becomes apparent that improvements of this magnitude are primarily achieved when in-domain data is included in the training set, as training solely on in-domain data already yields a 2.8 BLEU point improvement. A comparison with a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness.

In my opinion, the more significant result is presented in the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier's ability to perform well even with minimal in-domain data. Nevertheless, this experiment was only conducted on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training set, potentially making data selection easier. It would be valuable to investigate whether this result holds for the other tasks, where no in-domain data is present in the training set. The results for the en2es and en2zh tasks, which have development sets containing only a few hundred sentence pairs, suggest that this may be the case. Reporting results for all tasks when only 100 sentence pairs are used for training would provide stronger support for this claim.

When translating social media text, one often encounters unique challenges that differ from other domains, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-standard spellings, particularly for Latin scripts. Additionally, these texts frequently contain special character sequences, such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. It would be beneficial to know if any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data to address these challenges.

As data selection alone cannot resolve the OOV problem, it would be interesting to gain a deeper understanding of the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection. Providing examples or case studies could help illustrate the benefits of this approach.

Regarding specific sections of the paper:

Section 3.2:
- A clearer explanation of how the different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined in the model would be helpful. An illustration of the architecture would greatly facilitate understanding.
- The "designated loss function" used in the model is not explicitly stated and could be clarified.

Section 5.2:
- For the sake of completeness, it would be beneficial to mention how the system weights were tuned.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to in-domain data selection for SMT, but could benefit from additional clarification and supporting results to strengthen its claims.
