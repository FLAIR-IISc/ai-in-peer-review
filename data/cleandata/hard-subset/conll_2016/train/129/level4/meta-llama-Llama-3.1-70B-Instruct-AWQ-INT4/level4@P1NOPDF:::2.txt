The paper presents a method for in-domain data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, which builds upon the framework introduced by Johnson and Zhang (2015). This approach yields an improvement of approximately 0.5 BLEU points over language model-based data selection methods. Notably, it demonstrates robustness even when only a minimal amount of in-domain data is available.

The authors claim an improvement of 3.1 BLEU points. However, upon closer examination of the results, it becomes apparent that such significant gains are only achieved when in-domain data is included in the training set. Specifically, training solely on in-domain data already results in a 2.8 BLEU point improvement. It would be intriguing to compare this method to a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models.

In my opinion, the more impressive result stems from the second experiment, which showcases the effectiveness of the CNN classifier even with very limited in-domain data. Nevertheless, this experiment was only conducted on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training set, potentially making data selection easier. It remains to be seen whether this result would hold for the other tasks, where no in-domain data is present in the training set. The results for the en2es and en2zh tasks, which have development sets containing only a few hundred sentence pairs, already suggest a positive trend. To further substantiate this claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks when only 100 sentence pairs are used for training.

When translating social media text, one often encounters distinct challenges that differ from other domains. A notable issue is the high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-standard spellings (particularly for Latin scripts). Additionally, these texts frequently contain special character sequences such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. I would like to know if any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data.

Since data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, it would be interesting to gain a deeper understanding of the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection. Providing concrete examples would be beneficial in this regard.

Regarding specific sections:

Section 3.2:
- The combination of different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) within the model could be clarified. An illustration of the architecture would be highly beneficial.
- The authors mention a "designated loss function," but its nature remains unclear.

Section 5.2:
- For the sake of completeness, it would be helpful to mention how the system weights were tuned.
