**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors claim that their method can improve the performance of SMT systems by up to 3.1 BLEU points compared to strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

The main contributions of this paper, in order of strength, are:

1. The use of semi-supervised CNNs for domain classification, which allows for effective data selection with limited in-domain data.
2. The demonstration that the proposed method can outperform state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, even with a tiny amount of in-domain data.
3. The exploration of the effectiveness of the proposed method in various language directions and test domains.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to data selection for SMT, which is well-motivated and clearly explained.
2. The authors provide a thorough comparison of their method with prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models.
3. The experimental results support the claims made in the paper, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The paper does not experimentally compare the performance of different representations (BOW or SEQ) for the CNN classifier.
2. The method of determining the number of selected in-domain sentences based on a separate validation set is not clearly explained.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the CNN models to hyperparameters.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the validation data used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences?
2. How sensitive are the CNN models to the choice of hyperparameters, and how did you select the hyperparameters used in the experiments?
3. Would it be possible to compare the technique of the paper against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set?

**Additional Comments**

1. It would be interesting to see an experiment that compares the technique of the paper against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
2. The results or discussion section could feature some example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims made in section 5.4.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the effectiveness of the proposed method in various language directions and test domains.
4. Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very similar to source & target Lewis-Moore LM data selection: sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be reasonable.
5. Finally, I wonder if you could learn weights for the sum of both source & target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting.
