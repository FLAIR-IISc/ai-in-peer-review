The paper presents a machine translation (MT) training data selection approach that leverages a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. The comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models is well executed, although it is unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

The motivation for using a CNN instead of a recurrent neural network (RNN) or long short-term memory (LSTM) network was initially unclear, but the paper effectively argues that certain sections of a text or sentence are more important than others, which is achieved by the CNN architecture. However, the paper does not experimentally investigate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation (or a combination of both) is more important and why.

The textual description of the CNN, including one-hot and semi-supervised representations using pre-trained embeddings, is clear, detailed, and highlights the important aspects. However, a diagram illustrating the layer structure and how inputs are combined would be highly beneficial.

The paper is overall well-written, but some citations in parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., \citet vs. \citep, line 385).

The experiments and evaluation support the claims made in the paper, but I have some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set. Specifically, it is unclear what validation data is used and how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to investigate the sensitivity of the models to these hyperparameters.

Table 2 should ideally compare scores of different approaches with the same number of sentences selected. As Figure 1 suggests, the approach presented in the paper still appears to outperform the baselines in this case.

Other comments and suggestions:

* I would be interested in seeing an experiment that compares the technique presented in the paper against baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond the development set.
* The results or discussion section could benefit from featuring example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims made in section 5.4.
* Regarding the argument of abstracting away from surface forms in section 5.4, another potential baseline to compare against could be the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced some words with part-of-speech (POS) tags to reduce language model data sparsity. This could help determine whether the word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage over this approach.
* The use of the sum of source and target classification scores is similar to the source and target Lewis-Moore LM data selection method, which uses the sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be reasonable.
* Finally, I wonder whether it would be possible to learn weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual or parallel setting.
