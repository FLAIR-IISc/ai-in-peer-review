The paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier. The method performs about 0.5 BLEU points better than language model-based data selection and is robust even with a very small in-domain data set.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper presents a novel approach to domain adaptation for SMT using a semi-supervised CNN to select in-domain training data. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A semi-supervised CNN-based method for in-domain data selection, which outperforms language model-based methods.
2. The ability to adapt to new domains with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs).
3. Experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on four language directions with different test domains.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant challenge in SMT, namely, domain adaptation with limited in-domain data.
2. The proposed method is robust and effective even with very small in-domain data sets.
3. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over language model-based methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper claims improvements of 3.1 BLEU points, but this improvement is only achieved when there are in-domain data in the training set. The actual improvement over language model-based methods is around 0.5 BLEU points.
2. The second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN classifier with very little in-domain data, is only run on the zh2en task. It would be more convincing to report results for all tasks with only 100 sentence pairs used for training.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the improvements made through adaptation via data selection. Providing examples or a more detailed analysis would strengthen the claims made in the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing and filtering steps applied to the social media text data?
2. How do you plan to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, which is a significant challenge in translating social media text?
3. Can you provide more information on the "designated loss function" used in the semi-supervised CNN model?
4. How were the system weights tuned for the experiments?

**Additional Comments**

1. Section 3.2 could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined in the model. An illustration of the architecture would be very helpful.
2. For completeness' sake, it would be useful to mention how the system weights were tuned in Section 5.2.
