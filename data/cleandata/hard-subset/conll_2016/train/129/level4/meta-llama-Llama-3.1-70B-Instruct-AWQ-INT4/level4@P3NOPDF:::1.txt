The paper presents an innovative approach to machine translation (MT) training data selection, utilizing a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. A notable strength of the paper is its thorough comparison to prior work, including continuous and n-gram based language models. However, it is unclear whether the authors also compared their approach to bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

Initially, the decision to employ a CNN over a recurrent neural network (RNN) or long short-term memory (LSTM) model was not immediately apparent. Nevertheless, the authors provide a compelling argument for the use of CNNs, highlighting their ability to emphasize certain sections of a text or sentence. This is a valuable contribution, as it acknowledges the non-uniform importance of different text segments. However, the paper does not experimentally investigate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation is more effective, nor does it explore the potential benefits of combining both approaches.

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, effectively conveying the essential aspects of the model. A diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would be a welcome addition, as it would provide a visual representation of the model's inner workings.

The paper is generally well-written, although some citations could be reformatted for consistency (e.g., line 385). The experiments and evaluation support the authors' claims, but there are some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443). Specifically, it is unclear what validation data is used for this purpose, and how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen. The sensitivity of the models to these factors is also not explicitly addressed.

Table 2 would benefit from a comparison of scores across different approaches with the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 suggests, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines in this scenario. To further strengthen the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Conduct an experiment comparing the proposed technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond the development set.
* Include example sentences selected by different methods in the results or discussion section to provide concrete support for the claims made in section 5.4.
* Consider an additional baseline that replaces certain words with part-of-speech (POS) tags to reduce language model data sparsity, as proposed by Axelrod (2015). This would help to determine whether the word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage over this approach.
* Acknowledge the similarity between the proposed method and the source and target Lewis-Moore LM data selection approach, which uses the sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be a valuable addition.
* Explore the possibility of learning weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual or parallel setting.

By addressing these points, the authors can further strengthen their contribution and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their approach.
