**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The method uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selects the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train the CNN classifier. The trained CNN model is then used to classify the sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach for selecting in-domain training data for SMT using a semi-supervised CNN, which can effectively handle the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. The method achieves significant improvements in BLEU scores over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data, with an average improvement of 3.1 BLEU points.
3. The paper demonstrates that the semi-supervised CNN approach can select a small amount of training data that is used to create a system that outperforms baseline systems trained with all the general-domain data, even when the in-domain data is very limited.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the semi-supervised CNN approach, including the architecture of the network and the training process.
2. The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in selecting in-domain training data for SMT.
3. The paper provides a thorough comparison with prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models, which helps to establish the novelty and significance of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not experimentally show whether a BOW or SEQ (or the combination of both) representation is more important and why, which could provide additional insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. The method of determining the number of selected in-domain sentences based on a separate validation set is not clearly explained, and it is not clear what validation data is used or how sensitive the models are to this.
3. The paper could benefit from additional experiments that compare the technique against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the method of determining the number of selected in-domain sentences based on a separate validation set, including what validation data is used and how sensitive the models are to this?
2. Have you considered experimenting with different representations (BOW or SEQ) and comparing their performance?
3. Can you provide additional experiments that compare the technique against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set?
4. How do you plan to extend the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for the sum of both source & target classification scores?
