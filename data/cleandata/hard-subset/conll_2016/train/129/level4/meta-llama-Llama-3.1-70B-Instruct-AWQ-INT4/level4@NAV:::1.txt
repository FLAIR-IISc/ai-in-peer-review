The paper presents an MT training data selection approach that utilizes a CNN classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. A thorough comparison is made to prior work employing continuous or n-gram based language models, although it is unclear whether bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies, were also considered.

Initially, the motivation behind choosing a CNN over an RNN/LSTM was unclear, but the paper effectively argues that certain sections of a text or sentence hold greater importance, which is effectively captured by a CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally investigate whether a BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both) representation is more significant and why.

The textual description of the CNN, including its implementation using one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, highlighting essential aspects. Nevertheless, a visual representation of the layers, illustrating how inputs are combined, would greatly enhance understanding.

The paper is well-written overall, but some citation parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., \citet vs. \citep, line 385).

The experiments and evaluation support the paper's claims, but concerns arise regarding the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set:
- The origin and composition of the validation data used in this process are unclear.
- It is also unclear how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen and how sensitive the models are to this choice.
- Table 2 should ideally compare scores of different approaches with the same number of selected sentences, as suggested by Figure 1, which indicates that the paper's approach still outperforms the baselines in this scenario.

Additional comments include:
- An experiment comparing the paper's technique to baselines with more in-domain data available, beyond the development set, would be of interest.
- Including example sentences selected by different methods in the results or discussion section could support the claims made in section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument of abstracting away from surface forms in section 5.4, a comparison to Axelrod (2015), who replaced words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity, could provide insight into whether word2vec embeddings offer an advantage over this approach.
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores bears resemblance to the source and target Lewis-Moore LM data selection method, which calculates the sum of difference of cross-entropies; a reference to this work around line 435 would be suitable.
- Finally, exploring the possibility of learning weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting could be a valuable extension.
