The paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, following the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method yields an improvement of approximately 0.5 BLEU points over language model-based data selection and demonstrates robustness even with very limited in-domain data.

However, the paper's claim of a 3.1 BLEU point improvement warrants closer examination. Upon inspecting the results, it becomes apparent that improvements of this magnitude are only achieved when in-domain data is present in the training set, as training solely on in-domain data already results in a 2.8 BLEU point gain. It would be interesting to compare this approach to a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models.

In my opinion, the more notable result comes from the second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN classifier even with minimal in-domain data. Nevertheless, this experiment is only conducted on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training set, potentially making data selection easier. It is unclear whether this result would hold for the other tasks, where no in-domain data is present in the training set. The results for the en2es and en2zh tasks, which have development sets with only a few hundred sentence pairs, suggest a positive trend. To better support this claim, it would be beneficial to report results for all tasks when using only 100 sentence pairs for training.

When translating social media text, one often encounters unique challenges distinct from other domains, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate due to non-conventional spelling (particularly for Latin scripts). Additionally, these texts may contain special character sequences, such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. It would be helpful to know if any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data.

As data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, it would be interesting to gain insight into the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection, possibly by providing concrete examples.

Several specific sections of the paper warrant further clarification:

Section 3.2:
- A clearer explanation of how the different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined in the model would be beneficial. An illustration of the architecture would greatly enhance understanding.
- The "designated loss function" used in the model is not explicitly stated.

Section 5.2:
For the sake of completeness, it would be useful to mention how the system weights were tuned.
