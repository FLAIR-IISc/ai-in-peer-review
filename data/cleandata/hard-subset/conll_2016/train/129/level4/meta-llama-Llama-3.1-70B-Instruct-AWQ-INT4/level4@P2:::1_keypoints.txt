- Paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN to select in-domain SMT training data when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.  
- Method trains CNN using few in-domain positives and equal-number randomly sampled general-domain negatives, then ranks general-domain sentences to pick top in-domain-like pairs.  

- Contribution: novel semi-supervised CNN approach tailored for tiny in-domain data scenarios.  
- Contribution: achieves average BLEU improvements of 3.1 points over strong baselines.  
- Contribution: can select a small subset of data that outperforms systems trained on all general-domain data.  

- Strength: clear, detailed description of the CNN architecture and training process.  
- Strength: well-presented experiments that demonstrate effectiveness of the selection method.  
- Strength: thorough comparison with prior continuous and n-gram based selection methods.  

- Weakness: no experiments disentangling the importance of BOW vs SEQ representations or their combination.  
- Weakness: unclear procedure for choosing the number of selected sentences via a separate validation set and what validation data is used.  
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to baselines when more-than-tiny in-domain data is available.  

- Question: please clarify how the number of selected in-domain sentences is determined, what validation data is used, and sensitivity to this choice.  
- Question: have you compared different input representations (BOW vs SEQ) and their performance?  
- Question: can you provide experiments comparing your method to baselines when more in-domain data is available?  
- Question: how will the CNN be extended to bilingual/parallel settings to combine source and target classification scores?
