This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The method uses a small amount of in-domain data and a large amount of general-domain data to train a CNN classifier, which then selects the most relevant sentence pairs from the general-domain data to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a novel application of semi-supervised CNN for in-domain data selection in SMT, which achieves significant improvements over traditional language model-based data selection methods.
2. The method is robust and effective even when only a very small in-domain data set is provided, making it suitable for fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on four different language directions with three test domains, including social media texts.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to addressing the challenge of domain adaptation in SMT with limited in-domain data.
2. The experimental results show significant improvements over strong baseline systems, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
3. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the results, including a discussion on the effectiveness of the proposed method in different scenarios.
4. The authors demonstrate the robustness of the method by showing that it can still achieve good performance even with a very small in-domain data set.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including those that use separate in- and out-domain models.
2. The second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN classifier with very little in-domain data, is only run on the zh2en task, and it would be interesting to see results for all tasks.
3. The paper could provide more information on the preprocessing and filtering steps applied to the data, especially for social media texts.
4. The authors could provide more insights into the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection, such as examples of improved translations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more information on the preprocessing and filtering steps applied to the data, especially for social media texts?
2. How do you plan to address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, which is a common challenge in social media text translation?
3. Could you provide more insights into the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection, such as examples of improved translations?
4. Have you considered using separate in- and out-domain models, and if so, how does the proposed method compare to such an approach?
