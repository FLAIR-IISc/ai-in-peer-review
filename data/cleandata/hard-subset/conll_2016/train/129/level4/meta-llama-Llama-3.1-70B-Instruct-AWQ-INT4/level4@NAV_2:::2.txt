The paper proposes a novel approach for in-domain data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). This method is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors claim improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN-based method for in-domain data selection, which outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
2. The method is robust even when only a very small in-domain data set is provided, achieving significant improvements over baseline systems.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on four different language directions with three test domains.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a novel and effective approach for in-domain data selection in SMT, which addresses the challenge of limited in-domain data.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons with strong baseline systems and state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
3. The results demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method, even when only a small amount of in-domain data is available.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection, including examples of specific improvements.
2. The authors do not provide a clear illustration of the CNN architecture, which could help to clarify the combination of different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) in the model.
3. The paper does not discuss the potential impact of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words on the performance of the proposed method, particularly in the context of social media text.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the data, particularly in the context of social media text?
2. How do you plan to address the OOV problem, which is a common challenge in SMT, particularly in the context of social media text?
3. Can you provide more information on the "designated loss function" used in the semi-supervised CNN model?
4. How were the system weights tuned in the experiments?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work on SMT adaptation techniques and CNNs for text classification.
2. The authors may want to consider providing more information on the computational resources required for training the semi-supervised CNN model.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the potential limitations and future work directions.
