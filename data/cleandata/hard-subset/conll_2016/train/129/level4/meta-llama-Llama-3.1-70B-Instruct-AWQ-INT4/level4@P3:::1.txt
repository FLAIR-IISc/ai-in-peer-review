**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for selecting in-domain training data for statistical machine translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The method uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selects the same number of sentences from the general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classification model. The trained model is then used to classify each sentence in the general-domain training data based on their domain relevance score, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Effective use of semi-supervised CNNs for domain classification**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of semi-supervised CNNs in selecting in-domain training data for SMT, even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.
2. **Improved performance over state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods**: The paper shows that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, including n-gram and recurrent neural network language models.
3. **Robustness to limited in-domain data**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed method can still achieve good performance even when the in-domain data is very limited, with as few as 100 sentence pairs.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear and detailed description of the CNN architecture**: The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot and semi-supervised CNNs.
2. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data to improve the performance of the CNN model.
3. **Robust experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a robust experimental evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of visual illustration of the CNN architecture**: The paper could benefit from a visual illustration of the CNN architecture, including how the inputs are combined.
2. **Unclear method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences**: The paper does not clearly explain the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences, including the validation data used and the sensitivity of the models to this parameter.
3. **Limited comparison with other methods**: The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other methods, including bilingual data selection methods and other CNN architectures.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences, including the validation data used and the sensitivity of the models to this parameter?
2. Can you provide a visual illustration of the CNN architecture, including how the inputs are combined?
3. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used for the CNN models and the sensitivity of the models to these parameters?
4. Can you provide a comparison with other methods, including bilingual data selection methods and other CNN architectures?
5. Can you provide more details on the use of word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled data, including the size of the word embeddings and the method used to train them?
