**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly select the same number of sentences from the general-domain training data as negative samples to train the CNN classification model. The trained CNN model is then used to classify sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train the SMT system.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel method for in-domain data selection using semi-supervised CNNs, which outperforms state-of-the-art language-model-based data selection methods.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method even when only a very small in-domain data set is provided.
3. The paper shows that the semi-supervised CNN classifier can still select in-domain bilingual sentences to train an adapted SMT system, even when the in-domain data is very limited.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel and effective method for in-domain data selection, which can be useful for SMT systems, especially when dealing with limited in-domain data.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons with state-of-the-art language-model-based data selection methods and an analysis of the results.
3. The paper demonstrates the robustness of the semi-supervised CNN classifier even when only a very small in-domain data set is provided.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the improvements made through adaptation via data selection, such as providing examples of how the selected data affects the translation quality.
2. The authors do not provide a clear illustration of the architecture of the semi-supervised CNN model, which could make it difficult for readers to understand the combination of different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) in the model.
3. The paper does not discuss any special preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the data, which could be relevant for translating social media text with high OOV rates and non-conventional spelling.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide more details on the improvements made through adaptation via data selection, such as examples of how the selected data affects the translation quality?
2. Could you provide an illustration of the architecture of the semi-supervised CNN model to clarify the combination of different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) in the model?
3. Were there any special preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the data, especially for translating social media text with high OOV rates and non-conventional spelling?
