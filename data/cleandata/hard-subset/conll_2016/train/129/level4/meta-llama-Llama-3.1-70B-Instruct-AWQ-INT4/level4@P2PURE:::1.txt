The paper presents an MT training data selection approach that leverages a CNN classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. A thorough comparison with prior work utilizing continuous or n-gram based language models is provided, although it is unclear whether the paper also considers bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

The authors' decision to employ a CNN over an RNN/LSTM is initially unclear, but the paper effectively argues that certain sections of a text or sentence hold greater importance than others, a notion better captured by a CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally investigate whether a BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both) representation is more significant and why.

The textual description of the CNN, including one-hot and semi-supervised approaches using pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, highlighting essential aspects. Nevertheless, a visual representation of the layers, illustrating how inputs are combined, would greatly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well-written, but some citations in parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., \citet vs. \citep, line 385). The experiments and evaluation support the paper's claims, but the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set raises concerns:
- The validation data used in this process is unclear, as is the data used to choose the hyperparameters of the CNN models. It is also uncertain how sensitive the models are to these choices.
- Table 2 should compare scores of different approaches with the same number of sentences selected. As Figure 1 suggests, the paper's approach appears to outperform the baselines even in this case.

Other comments:
- An experiment comparing the paper's technique to baselines with more in-domain data available, beyond the development set, would be of interest.
- The results or discussion section could benefit from example sentences selected by different methods to support the claims in section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument of abstracting away from surface forms in 5.4, comparing against the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity, could provide insight into whether word2vec embeddings offer an additional advantage.
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores bears resemblance to the source & target Lewis-Moore LM data selection method, which calculates the sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- Finally, it is worth exploring whether weights for the sum of both source & target classification scores could be learned by extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting.
