The paper presents an innovative approach to machine translation (MT) training data selection by utilizing a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. The authors provide a thorough comparison with prior work that employed continuous or n-gram based language models, demonstrating the effectiveness of their method. However, it is unclear whether the paper also compared its approach against bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

Initially, the decision to use a CNN over a recurrent neural network (RNN) or long short-term memory (LSTM) was not immediately apparent. Nevertheless, the authors convincingly argue that certain sections of a text or sentence hold more importance than others, making CNN a suitable choice for this task. One potential area for improvement is the lack of experimental comparison between bag-of-words (BOW) and sequential (SEQ) representations, or a combination of both, to determine their relative importance and impact on the results.

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, highlighting the essential aspects of the model. However, a visual representation of the layers, illustrating how inputs are combined, would greatly enhance the clarity of the explanation.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some minor issues with citation formatting, such as the unnecessary use of parentheses in certain instances (e.g., line 385).

The experiments and evaluation support the claims made in the paper, but there are some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set. Specifically, it is unclear what validation data is used for this purpose, and how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the models to these hyperparameters is not discussed.

Table 2 would benefit from a comparison of scores across different approaches using the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 suggests, the proposed approach still outperforms the baselines in this scenario.

Additional suggestions for improvement include:

* Conducting an experiment to compare the proposed technique against baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond just the development set.
* Including example sentences selected by different methods in the results or discussion section to support the claims made in section 5.4.
* Considering an additional baseline that replaces certain words with part-of-speech (POS) tags to reduce language model data sparsity, as proposed by Axelrod (2015), to evaluate the benefits of using word2vec embeddings.
* Providing a reference to the work of Lewis-Moore on source and target LM data selection, which uses a similar approach to the sum of source and target classification scores.
* Exploring the possibility of learning weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual or parallel setting.
