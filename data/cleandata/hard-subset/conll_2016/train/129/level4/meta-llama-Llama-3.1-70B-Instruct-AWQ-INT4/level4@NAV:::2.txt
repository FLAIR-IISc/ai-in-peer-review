The authors present a novel approach for in-domain data selection in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) utilizing a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, mirroring the framework introduced by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method demonstrates a 0.5 BLEU point improvement over language model-based data selection and exhibits robustness even with minimal in-domain data.

The paper reports a 3.1 BLEU point improvement, but upon closer examination, it appears that this gain is largely attributed to the presence of in-domain data in the training set, with a significant 2.8 BLEU point increase already achieved through training solely on in-domain data. A comparison with a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models could provide additional insights.

A more notable finding, in my opinion, is the second experiment's demonstration of the CNN classifier's effectiveness with limited in-domain data. However, this experiment was only conducted on the zh2en task, which included in-domain data in the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It would be beneficial to investigate whether this result holds for other tasks lacking in-domain training data. Preliminary evidence from the en2es and en2zh tasks, which utilized development sets with only a few hundred sentence pairs, suggests a positive trend. To strengthen this claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks using only 100 sentence pairs for training.

When translating social media text, unique challenges arise, including high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates due to non-standard spellings and special character sequences such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It would be helpful to know if any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data.

As data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, it would be interesting to explore the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection, potentially by providing concrete examples.
