**Summary of the Paper**
The paper proposes a novel approach to statistical machine translation (SMT) by utilizing semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for translation adaptation with limited in-domain data. The method involves training a data selection model with a semi-supervised CNN, which computes domain relevance scores for all sentences in the general-domain data set. The top-scoring sentence pairs are then selected to train the SMT system. The authors claim that this approach is particularly effective when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available.

**Main Contributions**
1. The paper introduces a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for SMT adaptation with limited in-domain data, which achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
2. The authors demonstrate that their approach can select a small amount of training data that outperforms baseline systems trained with all general-domain data, even with as few as 100 in-domain sentence pairs.
3. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach on four language directions with three test domains, showcasing its effectiveness in various settings.

**Strengths**
1. The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to SMT adaptation with limited in-domain data, which addresses a significant challenge in the field.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including comparisons with state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods and an analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters.
3. The paper is well-written, and the textual description of the CNN architecture is clear and detailed.

**Weaknesses**
1. The paper does not experimentally compare the effectiveness of different input representations (BOW vs. SEQ) for the CNN classifier.
2. The method of determining the number of selected in-domain sentences based on a separate validation set is not clearly explained, and it is unclear how sensitive the models are to this hyperparameter.
3. The paper does not provide a visual representation of the CNN architecture, which would help to clarify the input combination process.

**Questions to Authors**
1. Can you provide more details on the validation data used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences and how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen?
2. Would it be possible to include a visual representation of the CNN architecture to clarify the input combination process?
3. Have you considered experimenting with different input representations (BOW vs. SEQ) for the CNN classifier, and if so, what were the results?
4. Can you provide some example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims made in section 5.4?
5. Have you considered comparing the technique of the paper against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set?
