The paper presents a novel approach to selecting machine translation (MT) training data by scoring and ranking general-domain sentences using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier. The comparison to prior work utilizing continuous or n-gram based language models is well-executed, although it is unclear whether the paper also compares its method to bilingual data selection approaches, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

Initially, the motivation for using a CNN over a recurrent neural network (RNN) or long short-term memory (LSTM) model was unclear. However, the paper effectively argues that certain sections of a text or sentence are more important than others, which can be captured by a CNN. Nevertheless, the paper does not experimentally investigate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequence (SEQ) representation (or a combination of both) is more important and why.

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, highlighting the key aspects. A diagram illustrating the layer structure and how inputs are combined would be a valuable addition, as it would provide a more intuitive understanding of the model.

The paper is generally well-written, although some citations in parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., line 385, where \citet could be replaced with \citep). The experiments and evaluation support the paper's claims, but I have some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set. Specifically, it is unclear what validation data is used and how the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen. The sensitivity of the models to these hyperparameters is also not discussed.

Table 2 should ideally compare scores of different approaches with the same number of sentences selected. As Figure 1 shows, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines in this case.

Additional comments:

* I would be interested in seeing an experiment that compares the proposed technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond just the development set.
* Including example sentences selected by the different methods in the results or discussion section would help support the claims made in section 5.4.
* Regarding the argument in section 5.4 about abstracting away from surface forms, an additional baseline to compare against could be the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced words with part-of-speech tags to reduce language model data sparsity. This would help determine whether the word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage over this approach.
* The use of the sum of source and target classification scores is similar to the source and target Lewis-Moore LM data selection approach, which uses the sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be reasonable.
* Finally, I wonder whether it would be possible to learn weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual or parallel setting.
