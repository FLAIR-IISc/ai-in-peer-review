The paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building upon the framework introduced by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed approach achieves a 0.5 BLEU point improvement over language model-based data selection and demonstrates robustness even with very limited in-domain data.

However, a closer examination of the results reveals that the claimed 3.1 BLEU point improvement is only realized when in-domain data is included in the training set. Training solely on in-domain data already yields a 2.8 BLEU point gain, suggesting that the incremental benefit of the proposed method may be lower than reported. It would be interesting to compare this approach to a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models to better understand its effectiveness.

A more impressive result, in my opinion, is the second experiment, which shows that the CNN classifier remains effective even with very little in-domain data. However, this experiment is only conducted on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training set, potentially making data selection easier. It is unclear whether this result would hold for the other tasks, where no in-domain data is present in the training set. The results for the en2es and en2zh tasks, which have development sets with only a few hundred sentence pairs, suggest that this might be the case. To better support this claim, it would be beneficial to report results for all tasks using only 100 sentence pairs for training.

When translating social media text, one often encounters unique challenges, such as high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates due to non-standard spellings and special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. It is unclear whether any special preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data to address these issues.

Since data selection alone cannot mitigate the OOV problem, it would be interesting to know more about the types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection. Providing examples or further analysis would be helpful in this regard.

Regarding specific sections of the paper:

Section 3.2:
- The combination of different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) in the model could be clarified. An illustration of the architecture would greatly enhance understanding.
- The "designated loss function" is not explicitly defined and could be explained in more detail.

Section 5.2:
- For completeness, it would be beneficial to mention how the system weights were tuned.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to in-domain data selection for SMT using a CNN classifier. While the results are promising, some clarifications and additional analysis would strengthen the claims and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.
