Summary
The paper proposes an MT training data selection approach that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. It compares the method to prior work based on continuous and n-gram language models. The motivation is that certain spans within a sentence carry more domain-relevant signal, and the CNN can capture such localized importance.

Strengths
- The comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram-based language models is thorough.
- The motivation for using a CNN—to capture that some sections of a text or sentence are more important than others—is ultimately convincing and a strength of the paper.
- The textual description of the CNN variants (one-hot vs. semi-supervised with pre-trained embeddings) is clear and highlights key design choices.
- The experiments and evaluation generally support the paper’s claims, and the proposed method appears to outperform baselines based on Figure 1.

Weaknesses and concerns
- It is unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection (e.g., sum of differences of cross-entropies), which is a strong and widely used baseline.
- Although the paper motivates CNNs well, it does not experimentally analyze whether BOW or SEQ representations (or their combination) drive the gains, nor why. An ablation isolating these contributions is missing.
- While the CNN architecture is described clearly in text, a figure illustrating layer connections and how inputs are combined would substantially aid comprehension.
- Some citation formatting is unnecessary or inconsistent (use of \citet vs. \citep), e.g., line 385.

Selection-size determination and hyperparameters (line 443)
I am concerned about the procedure for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences using a separate validation set.
- What validation data is used here? Please specify the source, domain, and size.
- On what data are the hyperparameters of the CNN models chosen? How sensitive are the results to these choices?
- Table 2 should compare different approaches with the same number of selected sentences. Based on Figure 1, the proposed approach still seems to outperform the baselines under equal-size selection; please reflect that in Table 2 for a fair comparison.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Please include an experiment comparing the proposed technique against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- Consider adding a few example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate the qualitative claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the goal of abstracting away from surface forms (Section 5.4): a relevant baseline is Axelrod (2015), which replaces some words with POS tags to reduce LM sparsity. This would help assess whether word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage beyond such abstraction.
- Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very close to source-and-target Lewis-Moore LM data selection via the sum of differences of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- It may be possible to learn weights for the source and target classification scores by extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting; this could be a promising direction for future work.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper with a clear method, strong motivation for CNN-based selection, and empirical results that largely support the claims. Addressing the comparability in Table 2, clarifying the validation and hyperparameter selection procedure, adding an analysis of BOW vs. SEQ contributions, and situating the method relative to bilingual selection baselines (e.g., Lewis-Moore) would significantly strengthen the work. A model diagram and selected-sentence examples would further improve clarity and interpretability.
