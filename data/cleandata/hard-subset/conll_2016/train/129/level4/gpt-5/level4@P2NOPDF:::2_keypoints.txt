- Proposes CNN-based in-domain data selection for SMT that outperforms LM-based selection (~+0.5 BLEU) and remains effective with very small in-domain sets.  
- Reported claim of +3.1 BLEU is misleading: gains largely occur only when in-domain data are present; training solely on in-domain already gives +2.8 BLEU, so selection’s incremental benefit is modest.  
- Recommend adding a stronger baseline: interpolate separate in-domain and out-of-domain models to disentangle selection effects from standard domain adaptation.  
- Most compelling result is that the CNN classifier works with very limited in-domain data, but this experiment was only run on zh→en where the pool contains true in-domain material.  
- Question whether the small-data gains generalize to other tasks (en→es, en→zh) that lack in-domain data; suggest reporting results with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs for all language pairs.  
- Social-media text has high OOVs and special tokens (usernames, hashtags, emoticons); ask authors to clarify preprocessing/filtering (token normalization, user/hashtag handling, emoji segmentation).  
- Since data selection doesn’t directly address OOVs, request qualitative examples showing error types reduced by selection (better lexical choice, less noise, improved sentence fluency).  
- Section 3.2: clarify how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined (concatenation vs summation vs gating), and show layer locations—an architecture diagram would help.  
- Section 3.2: specify the “designated loss function” (e.g., cross-entropy, hinge) and any regularization used.  
- Section 5.2: report how system weights were tuned (MERT/PRO/MIRA, batch vs online), tuning set size, and whether tuning differed across selection conditions.  
- Overall: method is sound and promising for low-resource in-domain adaptation; expanding small-data experiments to all pairs, adding interpolation baseline, and clarifying preprocessing/model details would significantly strengthen the paper.
