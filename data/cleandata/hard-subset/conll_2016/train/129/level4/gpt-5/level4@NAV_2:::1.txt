Summary and Contributions
- This paper proposes a data selection method for SMT that uses a CNN classifier (in both one-hot and semi-supervised variants with pre-trained embeddings) to score and rank general-domain sentence pairs for domain relevance, using a tiny in-domain seed as positives and randomly sampled negatives. The top-scored sentence pairs—based on source, target, or combined source+target scores—are then used to train the MT system.
- Empirically, the main contribution is demonstrating that a semi-supervised CNN data selector outperforms strong LM-based selectors (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) across four translation directions and three test domains, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU over training on all data, and with robustness when the in-domain seed is extremely small (as few as 100 sentences).
- A practical contribution is showing that fine-grained, topic-dependent adaptation is feasible with minimal in-domain data and that combining source and target sentence scores is effective.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence across diverse settings: Four language directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and social media test domains yield consistent improvements over both the “all data” baseline and established LM-based selection methods. Gains are statistically significant and, in one case, exceed an in-domain-only baseline, underscoring the method’s effectiveness.
- Particularly robust in low-resource in-domain scenarios: The semi-supervised CNN maintains meaningful improvements even with only ~100 in-domain sentences, and shows low variance across different small samples, supporting claims about stability and suitability for fine-grained adaptation.
- Clear and informative method presentation: The description of the CNN variants (one-hot vs. semi-supervised with pre-trained embeddings), region construction (BOW vs. SEQ), and pooling rationale makes the modeling choices understandable. The motivation—that CNNs can capture salient local cues via convolution and max-pooling—is well argued and aligns with the task.
- Comparative evaluation is appropriate and thorough: Baselines include strong LM-based selectors (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination). Reporting of selected data sizes and statistical testing is helpful, and the observation that summing source and target scores works best is practically valuable.

Weaknesses
- Comparability across methods is weakened by varying selected set sizes: Table 2 reports different numbers of selected sentences per method, which complicates direct comparisons. Although Figure 1 suggests the proposed method remains strong under fixed-size selection, a table with matched selection sizes would make the case clearer.
- Insufficient detail on validation protocol and sensitivity: It is unclear which validation data are used to choose the number of selected sentences and tune CNN hyperparameters, and how sensitive results are to these choices. More transparency on data splits and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the evaluation.
- Missing ablations on region representations: The paper does not empirically disentangle the contributions of BOW vs. SEQ regions (or their combination) to performance. An ablation would clarify which components are most critical.
- Incomplete positioning relative to bilingual selection baselines: The use of summed source and target CNN scores is closely related to bilingual Moore–Lewis-style selection (sum of cross-entropy differences). A direct comparison and explicit citation would better situate the contribution.
- Limited analysis depth: No illustrative examples of sentences selected by different methods are provided to support the qualitative explanation in the discussion section; such examples would help substantiate the claimed mechanisms.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- What data are used for validation when selecting the number of in-domain sentences and tuning CNN hyperparameters? How are the dev/test/validation splits organized, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can you provide a comparison table where all methods select the same number of sentence pairs (e.g., fixed K) across language pairs?
- What is the relative impact of BOW vs. SEQ regions (and their combination) on performance? An ablation would be helpful.
- Could you include examples of sentence pairs selected by each method to illustrate differences in practice?
- How does the approach compare directly to bilingual Moore–Lewis selection (sum of source and target cross-entropy differences)? Given the conceptual similarity to summing source and target CNN scores, a head-to-head comparison would be informative.
- Have you tried a baseline that reduces LM sparsity by replacing some words with POS tags (e.g., Axelrod, 2015)? This would test whether word embeddings add value beyond surface-form abstraction.
- How does performance evolve when more in-domain data are available beyond the dev set? A curve showing gains vs. in-domain size would clarify scaling.
- Could you learn weights for combining source and target scores, or train a joint bilingual CNN to directly score parallel pairs?

Additional Comments
- The exposition is generally clear. A figure depicting the CNN architecture and how one-hot and embedding-based inputs are combined would improve readability.
- Minor writing note: some citation parentheses choices seem unnecessary; consider consistent use of citation styles.
