Summary and primary contributions
- The paper proposes a CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation that scores general-domain sentence pairs for in-domain relevance. It focuses on the extreme low-resource setting, where only a tiny in-domain seed (as small as ~100 sentences) is available.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) Empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN (with pre-trained embeddings) is a strong selector in low in-domain data regimes, outperforming n-gram and RNNLM Moore-Lewis-style baselines by up to 3.1 BLEU on four translation directions and several social media domains.
  2) Practical selection recipe that combines source and target sentence scores, and shows robustness and stability down to 100 in-domain sentences.
  3) A clear, well-specified classification framework that separates one-hot CNN and semi-supervised CNN variants.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains and breadth of evaluation: Across zh→en, ar→en, en→es, and en→zh, the proposed method achieves consistent improvements, with up to +3.1 BLEU over training on all data and statistically significant gains over LM-based data selection. The cross-domain focus on social media makes the results practically relevant.
- Low-resource emphasis with stability: The analysis varying the size of the labeled in-domain set is particularly compelling. The semi-supervised CNN remains effective and stable even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (≈+2.1 BLEU), where LM-based methods degrade.
- Clear methodological description: The paper provides a careful description of the CNN classifiers (one-hot vs semi-supervised), model components (windows, pooling, ReLU), and training setup, making the approach reproducible in spirit.
- Sensible use of both source and target: Selecting by the sum of source and target scores is a reasonable bilingual heuristic and appears to yield the best performance.
- Strong baselines: The inclusion of Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination strengthens the empirical case.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of comparison and selection size control: Table 2 compares different methods at different selected-data sizes (N), with N tuned on a validation set. This can confound conclusions. A paired comparison at the same N (several Ns) would be more convincing; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still wins, but this should be reflected in the main table.
- Limited comparison to bilingual selection baselines: It is unclear whether the paper compares against the bilingual Moore-Lewis “sum of differences of cross-entropies” variant on both source and target. Given that the method sums source and target CNN scores, this baseline is directly relevant and should be reported.
- Missing ablations: There is no experimental breakdown of bow-CNN vs seq-CNN (and their combination), nor an ablation of the contribution of pre-trained embeddings vs one-hot only. These ablations are important to support the architectural choices and to explain where the gains come from.
- Validation protocol clarity and sensitivity: The paper states that N is chosen on a separate validation set, but the origin and domain of this validation data are not clearly described, nor is the sensitivity of results to this choice. Similarly, it is unclear on what data hyperparameters (region size, stride, neurons) were tuned and how sensitive performance is to these settings.
- Presentation gaps and minor writing issues: A compact architecture figure would significantly improve clarity on how one-hot and embedding-based inputs are combined. Citation formatting (e.g., unnecessary parentheses; \citet vs \citep) needs cleanup.

Questions to the authors
- Validation and tuning:
  - What validation set is used to choose N and tune CNN hyperparameters? Is it the same domain as the test set (e.g., the dev set), and if so, how do you avoid overfitting? Please report sensitivity analyses: BLEU vs N curves per method and performance variance across hyperparameters.
- Baselines:
  - Did you implement the bilingual Moore-Lewis baseline that sums source and target cross-entropy differences? If not, can you add it? Likewise, how does your method compare to POS-abstracted LM selection (Axelrod, 2015), which reduces sparsity via POS replacements?
- Source–target combination:
  - You sum source and target CNN scores. Did you try learning weights for this sum, or training a joint bilingual CNN that directly scores sentence pairs? Any evidence that a learned combination outperforms a simple sum?
- Ablations:
  - Which representation matters more—bow-CNN, seq-CNN, or their combination? How much does pre-trained word2vec contribute relative to one-hot only?
- Qualitative analysis:
  - Can you provide example sentence pairs selected by different methods to substantiate the discussion in §5.4?

Additional comments
- Consider adding an architecture diagram showing how one-hot and embedding-based segment representations feed into convolution and pooling layers, and how source and target classifiers are combined.
- When discussing summing source and target scores (around line 435), please cite the Moore-Lewis bilingual variant (sum of source and target cross-entropy differences).
- Minor style fixes: remove superfluous parentheses around citations (e.g., line 385) and standardize citation style.
- It would be informative to report results when more in-domain data are available (beyond the dev set), to understand whether the CNN advantage persists or diminishes as in-domain data grows.
