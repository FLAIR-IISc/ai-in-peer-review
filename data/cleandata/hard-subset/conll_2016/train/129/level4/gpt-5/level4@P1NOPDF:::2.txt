The paper presents an in-domain data selection method for SMT using a convolutional neural network classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The approach yields roughly +0.5 BLEU over language model–based selection and—unlike competing methods—remains robust even when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available.

The paper claims overall improvements of 3.1 BLEU. However, the results suggest that gains of this magnitude occur only when the training set already includes in-domain data; training solely on the in-domain subset already yields +2.8 BLEU. It would be informative to compare against a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models.

In my view, the more compelling finding comes from the second experiment, which shows that the CNN classifier remains effective with very limited in-domain data. However, this experiment is reported only for the zh2en task, where the training set includes actual in-domain data, potentially making selection easier. Would these results also hold for the other tasks, where no in-domain data is present in the training set? The en2es and en2zh results already point in this direction, since their development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs. The claim would be better supported by reporting results for all tasks when only 100 sentence pairs are used for training.

Translating social media text poses challenges distinct from other domains, most notably high OOV rates due to nonstandard spelling (for Latin scripts, at least), as well as special token types such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Did you apply any special preprocessing or filtering to handle these phenomena?

Since data selection does not directly address the OOV problem, it would be helpful to detail the kinds of improvements achieved via adaptation through data selection, perhaps by providing qualitative examples.
