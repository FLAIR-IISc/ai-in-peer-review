- Proposes a semi‑supervised CNN for SMT data selection using tiny in‑domain positives and random general‑domain negatives, with pre‑trained word2vec to mitigate sparsity; scores source and target separately and selects pairs by summed scores.
- Main claim: CNN selection outperforms strong LM‑based methods (n‑gram, RNNLM, combination) across four translation directions and multiple social‑media domains, up to +3.1 BLEU and robust with as few as 100 in‑domain sentences.
- Secondary claim: empirical analysis shows CNN performance degrades more gracefully than LM methods as labeled in‑domain data decreases.

- Strong empirical results: consistent, statistically significant gains over baselines and training on all data, including robustness with only 100 in‑domain sentences.
- Clear methodological description: one‑hot and semi‑supervised CNN variants are well explained, with rationale for CNNs (local indicators + max‑pooling).
- Sensible scoring strategy: summing source and target scores is intuitive and effective, mirroring bilingual LM selection criteria.
- Careful significance testing and reproducibility: bootstrap resampling, standard SMT toolkits, and publicly available corpora/tools increase credibility.
- Informative label‑budget analysis: experiments varying in‑domain training size show CNN advantage is not solely due to selection size differences.

- Main table compares methods with different selection sizes; lacks a controlled comparison where all methods select the same number of sentences.
- Validation and hyperparameter protocol unclear: unspecified validation data used to determine selection size and tune hyperparameters; sensitivity not reported.
- Missing bilingual LM baseline: no explicit comparison to bilingual Moore–Lewis (sum of source and target cross‑entropy differences), which is a natural baseline given the scoring approach.
- Limited architectural analysis: no ablation isolating BOW vs SEQ region representations or their combination to show which matters most.
- Presentation issues: lacks a schematic figure of the semi‑supervised CNN (one‑hot + embeddings) and has inconsistent citation formatting.
