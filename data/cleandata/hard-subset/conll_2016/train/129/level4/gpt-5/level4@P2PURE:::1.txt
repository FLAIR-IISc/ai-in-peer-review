The paper presents an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work based on continuous or n-gram language models is solid, although it is unclear whether the paper also evaluates against bilingual data selection methods (e.g., the sum of differences of cross-entropies).

The initial motivation for preferring a CNN over an RNN/LSTM was unclear to me, but the paper persuasively argues that certain parts of a sentence are more informative than others and that a CNN can capture this. However, the experiments do not show whether a BOW or SEQ representation (or their combination) is more important, nor explain why.

The textual description of the CNN (one-hot or semi-supervised with pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights key design choices. That said, a figure of the architecture showing how inputs are combined would be extremely helpful.

The paper is overall well written, but some parenthetical citations are unnecessary (\citet vs. \citep), e.g., line 385.

The experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but I am concerned about how the number of selected in-domain sentences is determined (line 443) using a separate validation set:
- What validation data is used? It is also unclear on which data the CNN hyperparameters are chosen. How sensitive are the models to these choices?
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 suggests, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines under this constraint.

Other comments:
- I would be interested in an experiment comparing the proposed technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- The results or discussion section could include example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument about abstracting away from surface forms in Section 5.4: another useful baseline would be Axelrod (2015), which replaces some words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity, to test whether word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage beyond this.
- Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very similar to source & target Lewis–Moore LM data selection (sum of differences of cross-entropies). A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.

Finally, I wonder whether one could learn weights for the sum of the source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting.
