This submission proposes an SMT in-domain data selection approach using a convolutional neural network classifier, largely following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method yields roughly +0.5 BLEU on average over language model–based selection methods and, unlike those baselines, remains robust even when the available in-domain set is extremely small.

The paper reports gains up to 3.1 BLEU. However, the results indicate that improvements of this magnitude occur only when in-domain material is present within the general training pool—training exclusively on the in-domain subset already yields about +2.8 BLEU. It would be informative to compare against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-of-domain models.

In my view, the most compelling evidence comes from the second experiment, showing that the CNN classifier remains effective with very limited in-domain data. That said, this experiment is conducted only on the zh→en task, which includes genuine in-domain content in the training pool and may make selection easier. Would the same trend hold for the other settings where the training pool lacks identifiable in-domain material? The en→es and en→zh results already hint in that direction, given their development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs. The claim would be strengthened by reporting results for all tasks when restricting the classifier training to 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

When working with social media, one often encounters challenges that differ markedly from other domains, notably high OOV rates due to non-standard spellings (at least for Latin scripts). Inputs may also include special tokens such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. Were any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied?

Since data selection by itself does not directly address OOV issues, it would be helpful to detail what types of improvements are achieved through this adaptation (e.g., illustrative examples).
