Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based method to score and select in-domain bilingual training data for SMT when only tiny amounts of in-domain text are available. The classifier is trained using one-hot features augmented with embeddings learned from large unlabeled corpora.
- The approach is evaluated on four translation directions and three social-media domains, consistently outperforming strong LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) and even the full-data baseline, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU. Notably, it remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.
- A practical finding is that summing source- and target-side domain scores performs best for selection; the method is robust to small labeled samples and scales to large training pools.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across diverse settings. The method improves over training on all data with only 2.5–10% of the corpus, and beats three state-of-the-art LM-based selection techniques. The zh→en SMS setup shows large improvements (+3.1 BLEU) and significance testing is carefully conducted.
- Addresses a realistic and challenging scenario. The focus on extremely small in-domain sets (down to 100 sentences) fills an important gap: conventional LM-based selection typically assumes thousands to hundreds of thousands of in-domain sentences. The stability analysis with repeated 100-sentence samples (low variance) strengthens this claim.
- Clear and credible modeling rationale. The paper articulates why CNNs are appropriate for domain discrimination (local n-gram cues + max pooling) and why pretrained embeddings help with lexical sparsity and synonymy—both are compelling for tiny in-domain regimes.
- Comprehensive comparisons and transparency. The experiments span multiple languages and domains, and include both one-hot and semi-supervised CNN variants versus established LM baselines. The method is described in sufficient detail to be reproducible with public toolkits.
- Practical insights for selection. The result that combining source and target scores helps, and that semi-supervised CNNs outperform one-hot CNNs by ~0.5 BLEU on average, provides actionable guidance for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Baseline coverage may omit strong bilingual selection. It is unclear whether bilingual Moore–Lewis/AXELROD-style selection (difference of cross-entropies computed on both source and target sides) was included. Given the paper’s use of source+target scores, a direct comparison would be informative and fair.
- Selection budget not controlled across methods in Table 2. The number of selected sentences differs by method and is tuned per approach, which confounds comparisons. While Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still wins at fixed budgets, the main table should report matched-budgets or selection curves for a fair comparison.
- Validation/tuning protocol is under-specified. The paper selects N using a “separate validation set,” but the origin and domain of that set are not clearly described. If the dev set is used both to train the classifier and to select N, there is a risk of overfitting. The sensitivity of CNN hyperparameters to this choice is also not reported.
- Missing ablations on CNN inputs. The paper uses both BOW and SEQ region inputs but does not provide ablations isolating their contributions (BOW vs SEQ vs both), nor the effect of embedding dimensionality, window size, or using embeddings alone versus one-hot alone. These would clarify which design choices matter most.
- Limited qualitative analysis and alternative sparsity controls. The discussion argues convincingly for CNNs and embeddings, but lacks example sentences selected by different methods to illustrate the behavior. In addition, a comparison to POS-abstraction approaches (e.g., Axelrod 2015) would test whether embeddings confer advantages beyond reduced sparsity.

Questions to authors
- Validation protocol: What data is used to select the number of sentences N? Is it strictly held out from both classifier training and MT tuning? If the dev set is used for classifier training, what prevents overfitting when also using it to pick N?
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive are results to CNN hyperparameters (region size, pooling, number of filters, embedding dimension)? Please provide a brief sensitivity analysis or justify chosen values.
- Bilingual baselines: Did you compare against bilingual Moore–Lewis/AXELROD selection (sum of source and target cross-entropy differences)? If not, can you add it or discuss why not?
- Combining source and target: Beyond a simple sum, did you try learning weights for combining source and target classifier scores, or training a joint bilingual CNN to score pairs directly?
- Runtime and scalability: What are the computational costs to train classifiers and score 9–15M sentences? Any practical guidance on throughput and resource requirements?

Additional comments
- Please add a figure illustrating the semi-supervised CNN architecture and how one-hot and embedding-based region representations are combined.
- Report matched-budget results in the main table or provide selection curves to complement Table 2.
- Include ablations: BOW-only vs SEQ-only vs both; one-hot-only vs embeddings-only vs combined.
- Consider adding qualitative examples of sentences selected by each method to support the discussion in Section 5.4 (e.g., cases where embeddings help with unseen but semantically related terms).
- Cite Moore and Lewis (2010)/Axelrod et al. for the analogy to summing source and target scores, and clarify how your approach differs.
- Minor: clean up citation styles and parentheses; some citation formatting appears inconsistent.

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper with solid empirical results and a clear motivation. Addressing the baseline coverage, selection-budget control, and ablation/validation details would significantly strengthen the work.
