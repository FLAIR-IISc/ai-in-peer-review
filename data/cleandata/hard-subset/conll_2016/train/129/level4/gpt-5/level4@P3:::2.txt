Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) for in-domain data selection in phrase-based SMT, targeting scenarios with extremely limited in-domain data. A CNN classifier trained on a tiny in-domain set and randomly sampled negatives assigns domain-relevance scores to general-domain bilingual data; top-ranked sentence pairs (using source and target scores, summed) are selected to train the adapted SMT system. The authors evaluate across four translation directions and social-media test domains, reporting consistent gains over strong LM-based selection baselines and demonstrating robustness when only 100 in-domain sentences are available.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical data-selection method that uses a semi-supervised CNN (with pre-trained word embeddings) to outperform n-gram and RNNLM-based selection on multiple language pairs/domains, particularly when in-domain data are very small.
2) An empirical study showing that selection guided by a CNN trained on as few as 100 in-domain sentences can yield substantial improvements, indicating robustness and potential for fine-grained, topic-dependent adaptation.
3) Implementation details and recipe (source/target scoring, pooling, region sizes, architecture hyperparameters) that make the approach reproducible and applicable in resource-constrained adaptation settings.

Strengths
- Strong and consistent empirical gains over competitive baselines: Across four directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh), semi-supervised CNN selection (sscnn) outperforms “alldata” and three LM-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, combined) with significance testing. The gains are sizeable in zh→en sms (+3.1 BLEU using ~3% of data) and meaningful elsewhere (+1.4 BLEU in ar→en and en→zh; +0.7 in en→es).
- Robust to tiny in-domain samples: The second experiment shows that the CNN approach maintains improvements as in-domain supervision shrinks from 6,000 to 100 sentences; at 100 sentences, sscnn still yields +2.1 BLEU over “alldata.” The repeated sampling experiment (mean 25.03 BLEU, sd 0.12) suggests stability and practical viability when in-domain data collection is expensive.
- Sensible modeling choices supported by prior work: The semi-supervised CNN leverages pre-trained embeddings to generalize beyond observed in-domain vocabulary, while convolution + max pooling focuses on salient domain indicators. This explains why the method can outperform LM-based selection when labels are scarce and domain cues are local.
- Clear comparison setup and significance testing: The paper compares to standard LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis/Axelrod; RNNLM; combined), controls selection size based on held-out tuning, and reports significance with bootstrap resampling. Implementation details (word2vec size, CNN region sizes, pooling, etc.) are provided.

Weaknesses
- Confounding in the largest reported gain: The zh→en sms task contains an in-domain subset within the training pool; a system trained only on this in-domain subset already yields +2.8 BLEU. The reported +3.1 BLEU improvement, while strong, conflates selection quality with availability of genuine in-domain material. A stronger comparison would include: (a) in-domain-only; (b) interpolation/mixture of in- and out-domain models; (c) oracle vs selected overlap analysis.
- Limited generalization of the “100-sentence” result: The small-supervision experiment is only shown for zh→en, the task with known in-domain overlap. It is unclear whether similar robustness holds for ar→en, en→es, and en→zh where no in-domain portion exists in the training pool. This is central to the paper’s claim of effectiveness with tiny in-domain sets.
- Missing ablations and architectural clarity: The paper states that summing source and target scores works best, but does not quantify the gap vs using only source or only target scores. Likewise, the benefit of semi-supervised CNN over one-hot CNN is summarized but not dissected (e.g., region type, embedding-only vs one-hot-only, pooling choices). A schematic showing how one-hot features and embedding-based segment features are combined would improve reproducibility.
- Incomplete methodological details: The loss function used to train the classifier is unspecified; similarly, optimization details (class weighting, regularization, early stopping) are not reported. SMT system weight tuning is not described (e.g., MERT/MIRA/PRO, tuning set choice), which affects the validity and comparability of BLEU scores.
- Social-media preprocessing and OOV handling: Social media introduces noisy orthography, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. The paper does not describe tokenization/normalization, filtering, or segmentation choices. Since data selection does not reduce OOVs, it would be useful to quantify OOV rates pre/post selection or provide examples of qualitative improvements to understand what the method is actually fixing.

Questions to authors
- Please replicate the “100 in-domain sentences” experiment on at least one task without in-domain overlap (ar→en or en→es) to validate the core claim of robustness under tiny supervision when no in-domain data exist in the pool.
- How exactly are the one-hot segment vectors and embedding-derived segment vectors combined? A diagram of the two-layer convolutional architecture and the concatenation/combination scheme would help. What is the loss function (e.g., cross-entropy with softmax) and how is class imbalance handled?
- Can you provide ablations for: source-only vs target-only vs sum of scores; one-hot CNN vs semi-supervised CNN; bag-of-words regions vs sequential regions; max vs average pooling?
- How were SMT system weights tuned (e.g., MERT, MIRA)? On which dev set(s)? Were tuning sets kept strictly separate from any data used for classifier training/selection?
- What preprocessing was applied to social-media data (tokenization, normalization, user mentions, URLs, hashtags, emoticons)? Do you observe changes in OOV rates or vocabulary coverage after selection?
- What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier and scoring the corpus compared to LM-based selection? Any guidance on runtime and memory for large corpora?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, practical contribution to SMT domain adaptation with a clear advantage under very limited supervision. Addressing the confound in the largest gain and extending the tiny-supervision experiment to tasks without in-domain overlap would materially strengthen the paper. Clarifying the architecture and training details, and discussing preprocessing/OOVs, would improve transparency and reproducibility. I am inclined to view the work positively, contingent on these clarifications.
