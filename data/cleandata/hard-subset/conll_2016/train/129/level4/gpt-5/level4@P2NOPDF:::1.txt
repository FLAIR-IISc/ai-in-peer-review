The paper proposes an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work based on continuous or n-gram language models is solid, though it is unclear whether you also compare against bilingual/parallel data selection approaches (e.g., the sum of cross-entropy differences as in Moore–Lewis/Axelrod).

I initially found the choice of a CNN over an RNN/LSTM insufficiently motivated, but the paper persuasively argues that certain spans in a sentence are more informative than others and that CNNs can capture such salient local patterns. However, the paper does not include an ablation to determine whether BOW or SEQ (or their combination) is most important, nor why.

The textual description of the CNN, including one-hot versus semi-supervised models using pre-trained embeddings, is clear and highlights key design choices. Still, a figure illustrating the architecture and how inputs are combined would make the explanation more accessible.

The paper is generally well written. Some citation styles with parentheses are unnecessary (\citet vs. \citep), e.g., line 385.

The experiments and evaluation broadly support the claims, but I am concerned about how the number of in-domain sentences is chosen (line 443) using a separate validation set:
- What validation data is used for this selection?
- On what data are the CNN hyperparameters tuned? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 suggests, your method likely still outperforms baselines under that constraint.

Other comments and suggestions:
- It would be informative to evaluate the method when more in-domain data is available than just the development set, to assess scalability and robustness.
- Please include example sentences selected by different methods, to substantiate the claims in Section 5.4 with qualitative evidence.
- Regarding the claim in Section 5.4 about abstracting away from surface forms: consider a baseline that reduces LM sparsity by replacing some words with POS tags (e.g., Axelrod, 2015) to test whether word2vec embeddings provide additional benefits beyond this form of abstraction.
- Combining source and target classification scores by summation closely parallels bilingual Moore–Lewis selection (sum of cross-entropy differences). A citation around line 435 would be appropriate.
- You might also learn weights for the source and target scores rather than using a fixed sum, or extend the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn this combination jointly.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and presents a well-motivated, clearly described approach with promising results. The work would be strengthened by:
- Clarifying whether bilingual/parallel data selection baselines were included.
- Adding ablations to disentangle the contributions of BOW vs. SEQ (and their combination).
- Detailing the validation data and hyperparameter selection process, and analyzing sensitivity.
- Ensuring fair comparisons with matched selection sizes in tables.
- Providing qualitative examples of selected sentences to support the discussion.
