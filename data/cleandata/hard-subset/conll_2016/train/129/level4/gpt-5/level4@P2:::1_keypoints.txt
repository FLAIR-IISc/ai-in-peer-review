- Proposes a CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation targeting extreme low-resource settings (as few as ~100 in-domain sentences).
- Empirically shows a semi-supervised CNN with pre-trained embeddings outperforms n-gram and RNNLM Moore-Lewis baselines by up to +3.1 BLEU across four translation directions and social media domains.
- Provides a practical selection recipe that combines source and target sentence scores and is robust/stable down to ~100 in-domain sentences.
- Presents a clear classification framework distinguishing one-hot CNN and semi-supervised CNN variants.

- Strong empirical gains and broad evaluation across zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh with statistically significant improvements over LM-based selection.
- Convincing low-resource analysis showing the semi-supervised CNN remains effective and stable with very small labeled sets (~100 sentences).
- Careful methodological description of CNN variants and training setup that aids reproducibility.
- Sensible bilingual selection heuristic: summing source and target scores appears to yield the best performance.
- Uses strong baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM) which strengthen empirical claims.

- Fairness concern: comparisons in Table 2 use different selected-data sizes (N) tuned on validation, which can confound results; paired comparisons at identical Ns are needed.
- Limited baseline comparisons: unclear if the bilingual Moore-Lewis (sum of source and target cross-entropies) or POS-abstracted LM baselines were included.
- Missing ablations: no breakdown of bow-CNN vs seq-CNN, their combination, or the contribution of pre-trained embeddings vs one-hot.
- Validation/tuning clarity lacking: origin/domain of validation set for choosing N and hyperparameters not described, and sensitivity to these choices is not reported.
- Presentation issues: need an architecture figure showing how one-hot and embedding inputs combine, and citation/style cleanup.
