The paper presents an approach to in-domain data selection for SMT using a convolutional neural network classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method yields about a 0.5 BLEU improvement over language-model-based selection and, unlike alternative approaches, remains robust even when the available in-domain set is very small.

The paper reports gains of 3.1 BLEU. However, the results indicate that improvements of this scale are only achieved when in-domain data are included in the training set—training solely on the in-domain data already gives +2.8 BLEU. It would also be informative to compare against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models.

In my view, the more compelling evidence comes from the second experiment, which shows the CNN classifier remains effective with very limited in-domain data. However, this experiment is only conducted on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training pool, potentially making selection easier. Would the result generalize to the other tasks where the training set lacks in-domain data? The en2es and en2zh results already hint in this direction, since the development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs. I believe the claim would be strengthened by reporting results for all tasks when only 100 sentence pairs are used for training.

When translating social media text, one often encounters challenges distinct from other domains, most notably high OOV rates due to non-standard spellings (at least for Latin scripts). The texts may also include special character sequences such as usernames, hashtags, or emoticons. Was any special preprocessing or filtering applied?

Since data selection does not address the OOV issue directly, it would be helpful to know more precisely what types of improvements are achieved through adaptation via data selection, perhaps with illustrative examples.
