Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an in-domain data selection method for SMT using a CNN classifier, following the Johnson and Zhang (2015) framework. Empirically, the approach yields consistent improvements over language-model (LM)–based selection (about +0.5 BLEU) and appears robust when only a very small in-domain set is available for supervision. The work addresses a practically important problem—domain adaptation for noisy, social-media text—and the results suggest the method can outperform common baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple yet effective application of CNNs to data selection.
- Consistent improvements over LM-based selection across reported conditions.
- Notable robustness in low-data supervision scenarios, where many adaptation techniques degrade.
- The focus on social-media text is timely and relevant for real-world MT applications.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Interpreting the headline gain. The paper claims +3.1 BLEU, but the tables suggest gains of that magnitude occur only when in-domain sentences are already present in the training pool; simply training on in-domain data alone yields +2.8 BLEU. Please separate the benefit of adding the selected out-of-domain data from the effect of in-domain-only training, and report the net contribution of selection over both (i) training on all data and (ii) training on in-domain data alone.
- Missing comparison to model interpolation. A standard SMT adaptation baseline is to train separate in-domain and out-of-domain LMs/TMs and then interpolate them (or tune mixture weights). This comparison is important to position the proposed selection method among strong, widely used baselines.
- Generalization of the small-data experiment. The second experiment—showing the CNN’s effectiveness with very limited in-domain supervision—is persuasive, but it is only reported for zh→en, where in-domain sentences are present in the training set. This may make selection easier. To substantiate the claim of robustness, please:
  - Replicate the small-supervision experiment (e.g., 100 in-domain sentence pairs) for en→es and en→zh, where the training pool does not contain in-domain sentences.
  - Report whether the observed gains persist when the development sets are themselves very small (a few hundred pairs), as suggested by your current setups.
- Analysis of what improves. Data selection will not directly address OOVs—an especially acute problem for social media due to nonstandard spellings, user mentions, hashtags, and emoticons. It would be valuable to:
  - Describe any special preprocessing/normalization: tokenization, truecasing, de-duplication, username/URL masking, hashtag segmentation, emoticon handling, length filtering, or spelling normalization. These choices can materially affect both selection and translation quality.
  - Provide qualitative examples or a brief error analysis illustrating what kinds of improvements result from selection (e.g., better lexical choice on in-domain terms, fewer mistranslations of slang or abbreviations, improved phrase segmentation), alongside OOV statistics before and after selection.
- Statistical significance and variance. Please report significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap resampling) for key comparisons and, if feasible, mean/variance across multiple runs or seeds, especially in the small-data setting where variance can be high.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Section 3.2 (model architecture):
  - Clarify how the different representations—word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors—are combined. Is the one-hot vector concatenated at the input, added after convolution, or used as a gating/conditioning signal?
  - An architecture diagram would greatly aid reproducibility: embedding layer(s) (dimensions, initialization), convolution widths and number of filters, pooling strategy, fully connected layers, activation functions, and regularization (dropout, weight decay).
  - Specify the “designated loss function”: is it cross-entropy over binary labels, hinge loss, or something else? Include the optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, number of epochs/early stopping, and any class balancing.
  - Clarify whether the classifier uses source-only, target-only, or bilingual features, and how sentence pairs are represented for scoring and selection.
- Data and selection protocol:
  - Detail the size of the in-domain supervision used by the classifier for each language pair, the size of the candidate out-of-domain pool, and the selection budget (absolute or percentage).
  - Describe how selected data are integrated into SMT training (e.g., replacing, augmenting, or weighting), and whether selection is iterative.
- Section 5.2 (tuning):
  - State explicitly how SMT system weights were tuned (e.g., MERT, PRO, or MIRA), tuning set size/domain, number of iterations, and whether re-tuning was performed after selection/interpolation.
- Reporting:
  - Include baseline scores for “in-domain only,” “out-of-domain only,” “all data,” and “interpolated models,” to isolate the added value of selection.
  - Provide selection precision/recall proxies (e.g., average domain score of selected vs. nonselected data) to help interpret why the CNN outperforms LM-based selection.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and practically useful approach to SMT domain adaptation. The empirical gains over LM-based selection and the apparent robustness under minimal supervision are notable strengths. However, the paper’s central claim would be substantially strengthened by (i) disentangling the contribution of selection from the presence of in-domain training data, (ii) adding a comparison to model interpolation, and (iii) extending the small-supervision experiment to tasks without in-domain sentences in the training pool, together with clearer methodological details and an analysis of what improves. With these additions and clarifications, the work would make a solid and convincing contribution.
