The paper presents an in-domain data selection approach for SMT using a convolutional neural network classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method yields roughly a 0.5 BLEU-point gain over language model–based selection and, unlike other approaches, remains robust even when only a very small in-domain dataset is available.

The paper claims improvements of 3.1 BLEU points. However, the results indicate that gains of this magnitude occur only when in-domain data are included in the training set—training solely on the in-domain data already yields +2.8 BLEU. It would be informative to compare against a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models.

In my view, the more compelling finding is from the second experiment, which shows the CNN classifier remains effective with very limited in-domain data. That said, this experiment is conducted only on zh2en, where the training set contains actual in-domain data, potentially making selection easier. Would the same hold for the other tasks that lack in-domain data in the training set? The en2es and en2zh results already suggest this may be the case, given that their development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs. The claim would be more convincing if results were reported for all tasks when training on just 100 sentence pairs.

Translating social media text presents distinct challenges, notably high OOV rates due to non-standard spelling (at least for Latin scripts), as well as special tokens such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Were any special preprocessing or filtering steps applied? Since data selection alone cannot solve the OOV problem, it would be useful to detail what types of improvements are achieved through adaptation via data selection, ideally with illustrative examples.
