Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based method for data selection in SMT when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. A CNN classifier is trained with in-domain sentences as positives and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; pre-trained word2vec embeddings are incorporated to mitigate data sparsity. The model scores source and target sentences separately and selects top-ranked sentence pairs (using the sum of source and target scores) for SMT training.
- The main contribution, as I see it, is demonstrating that a semi-supervised CNN can outperform strong LM-based selection methods (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) across four translation directions and multiple social-media domains, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU and robust improvements even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.
- A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of how performance varies with the size of labeled in-domain data, showing that CNN-based selection degrades more gracefully than LM-based methods under low-resource conditions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results under realistic constraints: The method consistently outperforms training on all data and three state-of-the-art LM-based selection baselines across four language directions, with statistically significant gains. The robustness with only 100 in-domain sentences (up to +2.1 BLEU) is compelling for fine-grained or rapidly evolving domains.
- Clear methodological setup: The paper clearly describes the one-hot and semi-supervised (pre-trained embeddings) CNN variants, and explains why CNNs are appropriate for domain classification (local indicators plus max-pooling).
- Sensible scoring strategy: Selecting by the sum of source and target scores is intuitive and empirically effective; it mirrors prior bilingual selection criteria in LM-based approaches.
- Careful significance testing and reproducibility signals: Use of bootstrap resampling and standard SMT components/toolkits enhances credibility. The use of publicly available corpora and well-known toolkits (conText, word2vec, RNNLM) helps with reproducibility.
- Useful analysis of label budget: The experiment varying the in-domain training size is informative; Figure 1 suggests the CNN-based method remains superior even when the number of selected sentences is fixed, indicating the advantage is not merely due to different selection sizes.

Weaknesses
- Selection size comparability: Table 2 compares methods with different numbers of selected sentences. For fairness, it should also report a comparison where all methods select the same number of sentences. Figure 1 suggests the proposed method would still win, but the main table should include this controlled comparison.
- Validation and hyperparameters: The paper states that the number of selected sentences is “determined by performance on a separate validation set” (around line 443), but it is not clear what validation data are used, nor on what data hyperparameters are tuned. Please clarify the validation protocol and report sensitivity to these choices.
- Missing bilingual LM-based baseline: It is not clear whether you compare against bilingual data selection via the sum of differences of cross-entropies (Moore-Lewis on both source and target). Given your use of source+target scores, this is a natural baseline and reference.
- Limited architectural analysis: While the text argues well for CNNs, the paper does not analyze the relative impact of BOW vs. SEQ region representations (or their combination). A small ablation showing which representation matters more—and why—would strengthen the case.
- Presentation: A schematic figure of the semi-supervised CNN, showing how one-hot inputs and segment embeddings are combined, would greatly improve clarity. Also, some citation parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., line 385; use \citet vs. \citep appropriately).

Questions to authors
- What validation set is used to determine the number of selected sentences? Is it a held-out portion of the dev set, a separate in-domain set, or something else? On what data are CNN hyperparameters tuned, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can you add a table where all methods select the same number of sentences (e.g., 300K), complementing Table 2?
- Did you compare against the bilingual Moore–Lewis selection (sum of source and target cross-entropy differences)? If not, can you do so and cite the relevant work where you describe summing source and target scores (around line 435)?
- Can you provide an ablation comparing BOW, SEQ, and BOW+SEQ inputs for the CNNs? Which contributes most?
- Could you show example sentence pairs selected by each method to support the discussion in Section 5.4?

Additional comments
- It would be informative to evaluate how the proposed method scales when more in-domain data are available (beyond the dev set), to see whether the advantage persists or diminishes as LM-based models become stronger.
- In relation to abstracting away from surface forms (Section 5.4), a relevant baseline is Axelrod et al. (2015), who reduce LM sparsity by replacing some words with POS tags. Comparing against this would help isolate the benefit of embeddings over POS-based abstraction.
- Since you sum source and target CNN scores, have you considered learning weights for this combination, or extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel architecture that directly models pairs?
- Please include a figure of the semi-supervised CNN architecture showing how wl(x) and ul(x) are combined.
- Minor: clean up citation styles (e.g., line 385).
