- Proposes a CNN-based data selection method (one-hot and semi-supervised with pre-trained embeddings) that ranks general-domain sentence pairs using a tiny in-domain seed and selects top-scored pairs (source, target, or combined) to train MT.
- Empirically shows the semi-supervised CNN outperforms strong LM-based selectors (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combo) across four language directions and three domains, with up to +3.1 BLEU and robustness down to ~100 in-domain sentences.
- Demonstrates practical feasibility of fine-grained, topic-dependent adaptation with minimal in-domain data and that combining source and target scores is effective.

- Strong empirical evidence across diverse settings (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh; social media domains) with statistically significant gains, sometimes exceeding in-domain-only baseline.
- Robust in low-resource in-domain scenarios: meaningful improvements and low variance with only ~100 in-domain sentences.
- Clear, informative presentation of CNN variants, region construction (BOW vs. SEQ), and pooling rationale.
- Thorough comparative evaluation against strong LM baselines, reporting selected data sizes and statistical testing; practical insight that summing source and target scores performs best.

- Comparability across methods is weakened by differing selected-set sizes; matched-size comparisons are needed for clarity.
- Insufficient detail on validation protocol and sensitivity to choices for number of selected sentences and CNN hyperparameters.
- Missing ablation study disentangling contributions of BOW vs. SEQ region representations.
- Incomplete positioning relative to bilingual selection baselines (e.g., Moore–Lewis-style); lacks direct comparison and citation.
- Limited qualitative analysis: no example sentence pairs illustrating differences between selection methods.
