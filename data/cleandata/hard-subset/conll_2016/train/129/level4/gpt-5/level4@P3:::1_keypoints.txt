- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN that uses one-hot features plus pretrained embeddings to score/select in-domain bilingual SMT data when only tiny in-domain sets are available.  
- Evaluated on four language pairs and three social-media domains, outperforming strong LM-based baselines and sometimes the full-data baseline, with up to +3.1 BLEU and effectiveness down to 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Finds summing source- and target-side domain scores works best for selection; method is robust to small labeled samples and scales to large pools.  

- Strong empirical gains across settings, beating three state-of-the-art LM selection methods and improving MT using only 2.5–10% of data.  
- Tackles a realistic, challenging scenario (very small in-domain sets) and demonstrates low variance in repeated 100-sentence samplings.  
- Provides clear modeling rationale: CNNs capture local n-gram cues and pooling; pretrained embeddings mitigate lexical sparsity and synonymy.  
- Experiments are comprehensive across languages/domains and described with enough detail for reproducibility.  
- Practical takeaway: combining source+target scores helps and semi-supervised CNNs outperform one-hot CNNs by ~0.5 BLEU on average.  

- Missing comparison to bilingual Moore–Lewis/Axelrod-style selection (difference of cross-entropies on both sides) despite using source+target scores.  
- Table 2 comparisons confounded by inconsistent selection budgets per method; matched-budget reporting or selection curves are needed.  
- Validation/tuning protocol under-specified: unclear origin and independence of the validation set used to choose N and tune classifiers, raising overfitting concerns.  
- Lacks ablations isolating BOW vs SEQ inputs, embedding dimensionality, window sizes, and one-hot vs embeddings contributions.  
- Limited qualitative analysis and no comparison to alternative sparsity-control methods (e.g., POS abstraction); no example sentences illustrating selection behavior.  

- Clarify validation: what data selects N and is it held out from classifier training and MT tuning?  
- Report sensitivity to CNN hyperparameters (region size, pooling, filters, embedding dim) or justify chosen values.  
- Did you compare to bilingual Moore–Lewis/Axelrod selection; if not, please add or explain omission.  
- Beyond summing scores, did you try learning weights for source/target combination or training a joint bilingual CNN?  
- Provide runtime/scalability details for training classifiers and scoring 9–15M sentences, with throughput/resource guidance.  

- Add a figure showing the semi-supervised CNN architecture and how one-hot and embedding regions combine.  
- Report matched-budget results or selection curves in the main table to ensure fair comparisons.  
- Include ablation studies: BOW-only vs SEQ-only vs both; one-hot-only vs embeddings-only vs combined.  
- Add qualitative examples of sentences selected by different methods to illustrate strengths of embeddings.  
- Cite Moore & Lewis (2010)/Axelrod et al. for source+target score analogy and clarify differences; fix inconsistent citation formatting.  

- Overall: strong, timely paper with solid empirical results; addressing baseline coverage, budget control, and ablation/validation details would substantially strengthen the work.
