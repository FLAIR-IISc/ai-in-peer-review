The paper proposes an MT training-data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work based on continuous or n-gram language models is solid, though it is not clear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection (e.g., the sum of differences of cross-entropies).

The initial motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was unclear to me, but the paper convincingly argues that certain regions of a sentence can be more informative than others and that CNNs can capture this. However, the paper does not empirically show whether BOW or SEQ (or their combination) is more important, nor explain why.

The textual description of the CNN variants (one-hot vs. semi-supervised with pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights the key design choices. That said, a figure illustrating the architecture and how inputs are combined would greatly improve clarity.

The paper is generally well written, but some parenthetical citations are unnecessary; consider using \citet rather than \citep where appropriate (e.g., line 385).

The experiments and evaluation mostly support the claims, but I am concerned about how the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) is chosen using a separate validation set:
- What validation data is used here? It is also unclear which data is used to tune CNN hyperparameters. How sensitive are the models to these choices?
- Table 2 should compare methods with the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 suggests, the proposed approach likely still outperforms the baselines under this control.

Other comments:
- I would like to see an experiment comparing the proposed technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- The results or discussion could include example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate the claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the claim in Section 5.4 about abstracting away from surface forms: another useful baseline would be Axelrod (2015), which replaces some words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity. This would test whether word2vec-style embeddings provide an additional advantage over such abstraction.
- Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very similar to Moore–Lewis LM data selection (the sum of differences of cross-entropies). A citation and brief discussion around line 435 would be appropriate.

Finally, could you learn weights for the source and target classification scores—rather than summing them equally—by extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting? This might further improve selection quality.
