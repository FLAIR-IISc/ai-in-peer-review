The paper presents an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison with prior work based on continuous or n-gram language models is well executed, although it is unclear whether the paper also compared against bilingual data selection (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies).

At first, the choice of a CNN over an RNN/LSTM was not obvious to me, but the paper persuasively argues that certain parts of a sentence are more informative than others, which a CNN can capture. However, the paper does not empirically show whether a BOW or SEQ representation (or the combination of both) is more important, and why.

The textual description of the CNN (one-hot or semi-supervised using pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights the key elements. However, a diagram of the layers showing how inputs are combined would be very helpful.

The paper is generally well written, but some parenthetical citations are unnecessary (\citet vs. \citep) (e.g., line 385).

The experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but I am somewhat concerned about the procedure for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set:
- What validation data is used here? It is also unclear on what data the CNN hyperparameters are chosen. How sensitive are the models to this?
- Table 2 should really compare different approaches with the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 shows, the paper’s approach still appears to outperform the baselines under this condition.

Other comments:
- I would be interested in an experiment comparing the proposed technique against baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- The results or discussion section could include example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims made in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument in 5.4 about abstracting away from surface forms: another baseline could be Axelrod (2015), which replaces some words with POS tags to reduce LM sparsity, to test whether word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage over this.
- Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very similar to source & target Lewis-Moore LM data selection: sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
Finally, I wonder whether you could learn weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting.
