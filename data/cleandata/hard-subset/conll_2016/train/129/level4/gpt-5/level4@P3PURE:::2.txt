This paper presents an in-domain data selection method for SMT using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The approach outperforms language-model-based selection by about 0.5 BLEU and, importantly, remains effective even when only a very small in-domain dataset is available.

The paper claims gains of 3.1 BLEU. However, the reported results suggest that improvements of this magnitude occur only when in-domain data are included in the training set; training solely on the in-domain data already yields +2.8 BLEU. It would be informative to compare against a system that interpolates independently trained in-domain and out-of-domain models.

The second experiment is, in my view, the most compelling: it shows that the CNN classifier remains effective with very limited in-domain data. That said, this experiment is conducted only on the zh2en task, which includes actual in-domain data in the training set, potentially making selection easier. Would the same effect hold for the other tasks where no in-domain data are present in the training set? The en2es and en2zh results already hint in this direction, as their development sets comprise only a few hundred sentence pairs. To strengthen the claim, please report results for all tasks when only 100 in-domain sentence pairs are used for training.

When translating social media text, one often encounters challenges distinct from other domains, notably high OOV rates due to nonstandard spellings (for Latin scripts) and the presence of special tokens such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Were any special preprocessing or filtering steps applied? Because data selection alone cannot address OOVs, it would also be helpful to detail what kinds of improvements adaptation via data selection actually yields, perhaps with concrete examples.
