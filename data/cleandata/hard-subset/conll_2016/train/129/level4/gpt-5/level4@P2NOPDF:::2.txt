This paper proposes an in-domain data selection method for SMT using a CNN classifier, following the framework of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The approach outperforms language model–based selection by about 0.5 BLEU and, importantly, remains effective even when only a very small in-domain set is available.

The paper claims gains of 3.1 BLEU, but the reported results suggest that such improvements occur only when in-domain data are present in the training pool. Training solely on the in-domain data already yields +2.8 BLEU, so the incremental benefit attributable to selection is more modest. A stronger comparison would include systems that interpolate separate in-domain and out-of-domain models, to disentangle the effects of selection from standard domain adaptation baselines.

In my view, the more compelling contribution is the second experiment, which shows the CNN classifier remains useful with very limited in-domain data. However, this experiment is only conducted on the zh→en task, where the training pool includes actual in-domain material, potentially simplifying selection. Would similar gains hold for the other tasks that lack in-domain data in the training set? The en→es and en→zh development sets already contain only a few hundred sentence pairs, hinting at this scenario. The claim would be more convincing if results were reported for all tasks when only 100 in-domain sentence pairs are available for training.

Translating social media text introduces challenges distinct from other domains, notably high OOV rates due to non-standard spellings, as well as special tokens such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Please clarify whether any preprocessing or filtering was applied (e.g., token normalization, user/hashtag handling, emoji segmentation). Since data selection does not directly address OOVs, it would help to characterize the qualitative improvements achieved via selection—ideally with examples illustrating error types reduced (e.g., better lexical choice, reduced noise, improved sentence-level fluency).

Section-specific comments:
- Section 3.2:
  - Please clarify how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined within the model (concatenation vs. summation vs. gating), and at which layers. An architecture diagram would make the setup easier to follow.
  - Specify the “designated loss function” (e.g., cross-entropy, hinge loss) and any regularization used.
- Section 5.2:
  - For completeness, indicate how system weights were tuned (e.g., MERT, PRO, MIRA, batch vs. online settings, tuning set size), and whether tuning differed across selection conditions.

Overall, the method is sound and the results are promising, particularly in the low-resource in-domain setting. Expanding the small-data experiment to all language pairs, adding a model interpolation baseline, and clarifying preprocessing and model details would significantly strengthen the paper.
