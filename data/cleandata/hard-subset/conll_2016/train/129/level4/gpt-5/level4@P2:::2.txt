Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a data selection approach for SMT that uses a CNN classifier to score sentence pairs by in-domain relevance. The classifier is trained with a tiny labeled in-domain set (dev) as positives and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives. A semi-supervised variant leverages word embeddings trained on large unlabeled text (à la Johnson & Zhang, 2015).
- The method is evaluated on four translation directions and three social-media test domains. Selected subsets (2.5–10% of training data) yield higher BLEU than using all data and outperform three LM-based selection baselines (n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination).
- As I see it, the main contributions are:
  1) An empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN (with region/segment embeddings) is a strong selector under very limited in-domain supervision, consistently outperforming LM-based selectors.
  2) Evidence that effective adaptation is possible with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (shown on zh→en).
  3) A practical, bilingual selection strategy that scores source and target sides and selects by the combined score.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over strong baselines: Across all four settings, the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) beats using all data and all LM-based selectors, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU (zh→en sms), and consistent improvements of 0.7–1.4 BLEU in the other three tasks. Significance is assessed via bootstrap resampling.
- Robustness with tiny in-domain supervision: The zh→en study shows that with only 100 labeled sentences, sscnn still achieves +2.1 BLEU over the full-data baseline, substantially outperforming LM-based selectors in this low-resource regime. This directly addresses the paper’s motivating scenario.
- Sensible design choices and clarity of setup: The paper details CNN hyperparameters, the embedding training setup, data splits, and selection sizes, facilitating reproducibility. The finding that summing source- and target-side scores works best is practical and intuitive.
- Scalability and simplicity: Training a binary classifier with equal positive/negative samples and then ranking the pool is computationally reasonable. Pretrained embeddings help leverage large unlabeled data without requiring large in-domain corpora.

Weaknesses
- Interpretation of the largest reported gain: The headline +3.1 BLEU improvement occurs in a condition where the general pool contains a sizable in-domain subset (120k). Training on that subset alone already yields +2.8 BLEU. This conflates the benefit of the selector with the presence of in-domain data in the pool. A clearer decomposition (e.g., comparing to interpolation of in-domain and out-of-domain models) would calibrate how much of the gain comes from selection vs. domain presence.
- Limited generalization evidence for the “100-sentence” result: The small-label experiment is only shown on zh→en—the setting with an in-domain component in the pool—potentially making selection easier. It remains unclear whether similar robustness holds in en→es and en→zh, where no in-domain component is known to exist in the pool.
- Model description lacks some clarity: Section 3.2 does not fully specify how one-hot region vectors and embedding-based region vectors are combined (Equation 2 is brief but an architectural diagram would help). The “designated loss function” is unspecified, and optimization details (regularization, early stopping, class weighting) are missing.
- Preprocessing and OOV handling for social media are underdescribed: Social media brings nonstandard spellings, usernames, hashtags, emojis, and higher OOV rates. The paper does not state whether any normalization, token filtering, or special handling was applied, nor how such tokens are treated in embeddings and selection.
- Missing baselines likely to strengthen the case: A standard domain adaptation baseline is interpolation of in-domain and out-of-domain SMT models (or instance/mixture weighting). Including such baselines, at least for the zh→en case with an in-domain subset, would contextualize the advantage of CNN-based selection.

Questions to authors
- Generalization of the tiny-label result: Can you report the “100 in-domain sentences” experiment for en→es and en→zh (and ar→en if feasible)? Even a smaller range (e.g., 100/400/800) would substantiate the robustness claim across conditions without known in-domain subsets in the pool.
- Baseline comparison: How does sscnn compare to (i) interpolation between in-domain-only and full out-of-domain models, and (ii) mixture/instance weighting approaches? For zh→en, is sscnn significantly better than training on the 120k in-domain subset alone?
- Architecture and loss: Please clarify the exact architecture for combining wl(x) (one-hot region vectors) and ul(x) (embedding-based region vectors). Are these concatenated and fed to a shared convolution, or processed by parallel convolutions whose outputs are merged? What is the loss (logistic cross-entropy? hinge?), and are class weights used?
- Optimization details: What regularization (dropout, L2), early stopping criteria, batch size, and optimizer were used? How were hyperparameters selected, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Preprocessing for social media: Did you normalize usernames, hashtags, URLs, emojis, spelling variants, or perform any token filtering? How are such tokens represented in word2vec (UNK vs. surface forms), and how might this affect selection and OOV rates?
- Selection mechanics: Are classifier outputs calibrated probabilities? What thresholding/ranking strategy is used, and how are selection sizes determined per task (fixed grid on a dev set)? Could you provide an ablation of source-only vs. target-only vs. sum-of-scores across tasks?
- SMT tuning: How were SMT system weights tuned (e.g., MERT/MIRA/Mert+Pro)? Were the same dev sets used both for in-domain labeling and SMT tuning, and if so, how do you avoid overfitting or selection bias?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid empirical paper with a clear, practical method and convincing gains over well-established LM-based selection. The main contribution is empirical rather than methodological novelty (the model builds on Johnson & Zhang), but the demonstration that selection remains effective with extremely limited in-domain supervision is valuable. Addressing the questions above—especially generalizing the tiny-label experiment to other language pairs, clarifying the model and loss, detailing social-media preprocessing, and adding a model interpolation baseline—would strengthen the paper’s claims and impact. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying these points.
