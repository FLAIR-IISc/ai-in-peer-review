Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based method for in-domain data selection for SMT. The classifier is trained with a tiny in-domain set (positives) and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negatives), using one-hot inputs and region embeddings learned from unlabeled data. Sentences in the general pool are scored and the top-N pairs are used to train SMT.
- Across four translation directions and three social-media domains, the approach generally outperforms LM-based selection, with the semi-supervised CNN yielding about 0.5 BLEU improvement over LM-based selection on average, and demonstrating robustness when only very small in-domain datasets are available.
- A second experiment (on zh→en) shows the method remains effective even when the labeled in-domain set is reduced to 100 sentences.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical semi-supervised CNN framework for SMT data selection that improves over strong LM-based selection baselines and remains robust under extreme scarcity of in-domain data.
2) An empirical finding that useful adaptation is possible with as few as 100 in-domain sentence pairs (shown for zh→en).
3) A multi-direction, social-media evaluation demonstrating that selecting 2.5–10% of the data can outperform training on all data.

Strengths
- Robust gains with minimal in-domain data: The method maintains improvements when the labeled in-domain set is extremely small (down to 100 sentences in zh→en), where LM-based selection becomes unstable or yields only small gains. This addresses a practically important regime for rapid, fine-grained, or topic-specific adaptation.
- Strong, competitive baselines and settings: The paper compares against three established LM-based selection methods (n-gram, RNNLM, combined) and a train-on-all baseline, using large training corpora and realistic social-media test domains. The comparison is fair and informative.
- Efficiency of selected subsets: Training on a small selected subset (roughly 2.5–10% of the full data) yields better BLEU than training on all data, underscoring the value of targeted selection and potential computational savings.
- Clear selection strategy: Scoring both source and target sentences and selecting by the sum improves performance relative to using either side alone, a simple and reproducible design choice.

Weaknesses
- Headline gain depends on in-domain overlap: The reported +3.1 BLEU improvement occurs in zh→en where the general training pool contains a sizable in-domain subset; training solely on that in-domain subset already gives +2.8 BLEU. This weakens the headline claim as a general statement. A stronger comparison would include a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-of-domain models (e.g., mixture/interpolation baselines).
- Limited scope of the “tiny in-domain” experiment: The experiment demonstrating effectiveness with 100 in-domain sentences is only conducted for zh→en—the case that contains in-domain material in the general pool. It is unclear whether the same robustness holds for the other directions where no such overlap is known. Reporting the 100-sentence experiment for all tasks would substantially strengthen the claim.
- Missing architectural/training details: Section 3.2 does not clearly explain how one-hot vectors and segment embeddings are combined in the network. An explicit description (or figure) of the architecture and data flow would help. The “designated loss function” is not specified, nor are optimization details.
- Social-media preprocessing not described: For tweets, SMS, and Facebook posts, OOVs, nonstandard spellings, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons are common. The paper does not state whether any normalization, token filtering, or special handling was applied. Since data selection will not directly solve OOV issues, clarifying preprocessing is important to interpret where gains come from.
- Limited qualitative analysis: The paper does not illustrate what kinds of sentences are being selected or how translations improve. Examples or brief error analyses would help clarify whether gains stem from better lexical choice, reduced domain mismatch, or other factors.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide results with a standard in-/out-domain interpolation baseline (e.g., separate LMs/TMs with tuned interpolation) to contextualize the +3.1 BLEU claim on zh→en?
- Will you report the “100 in-domain sentences” experiment for the other translation directions where no in-domain component exists in the general pool? If not, please justify why the zh→en results should generalize.
- Section 3.2: Please clarify the exact model architecture. How are the one-hot segment vectors wl(x) and the unlabeled-data segment embeddings ul(x) combined (concatenation vs. additive projections as in Eq. 2), how many convolutional layers are used, what pooling is applied at each stage, and what is the loss function and optimizer?
- What SMT tuning procedure was used (e.g., MERT/MIRA/PRO, tuning set, iterations, stopping criteria)? This would improve reproducibility.
- What preprocessing was applied to social-media text (tokenization, normalization of usernames/hashtags/URLs, emoji handling, spelling normalization)? Did you apply any subword segmentation to mitigate OOVs?
- Could you include a few examples of selected sentences and corresponding translation improvements to illustrate the nature of the gains?
