This paper proposes a CNN-based classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences for machine translation (MT) training data selection. The comparisons to prior monolingual approaches based on continuous or n-gram language models are generally solid. However, it is not clear whether the method was compared against bilingual data selection baselines (e.g., Moore–Lewis style sum of differences of cross-entropies on both source and target sides).

I initially found the choice of a CNN over RNN/LSTM models insufficiently motivated, but the paper persuasively argues that certain spans within a sentence are more informative—a behavior CNNs can capture via convolution and pooling. That said, the paper does not disentangle the contributions of the BOW and SEQ representations (or their combination). An ablation study would clarify which representation is most beneficial and why.

The model description (including one-hot vs. pre-trained embeddings) is clear and focuses on the right details. A schematic figure showing how inputs are combined through the convolutional and pooling layers would substantially improve readability and reduce ambiguity.

Writing and presentation are overall good. Some citation parentheses are unnecessary (\citet vs. \citep), e.g., line 385. A light pass to standardize citation style would help.

Experiments generally support the claims, but I have concerns about how the number of selected in-domain sentences is determined using a separate validation set (line 443). This choice affects fairness and interpretability of comparisons.

Major comments:
- Validation and hyperparameters: What validation data is used to decide the selection size? On what data are CNN hyperparameters tuned? Please report sensitivity to these choices (e.g., different validation splits or budgets), and clarify whether any test data leakage could occur.
- Fixed-budget comparisons: Table 2 should compare methods under the same number of selected sentences. Figure 1 suggests your approach still outperforms baselines when controlling for selection size; please add a fixed-budget comparison in Table 2 (or an additional table) to confirm this.
- Bilingual baselines and related work: Using the sum of source and target classification scores closely parallels bilingual Moore–Lewis selection (sum of differences of cross-entropies on both sides). Please add an explicit comparison or, at minimum, a clear discussion and citation around line 435. This will situate your contribution relative to established bilingual selection practices.
- Representation ablations: Include ablations isolating BOW vs. SEQ (and their combination), with and without pre-trained embeddings. This will substantiate the claim that CNNs capture salient subsequences better than simpler alternatives, and clarify whether gains stem from architecture, embeddings, or both.
- Alternative sequence models: While your motivation for CNNs is reasonable, an empirical comparison against a lightweight RNN/LSTM (or a pooled bag-of-ngrams classifier) would strengthen the case that CNNs are the right inductive bias here.
- Data availability regime: Please evaluate performance when more in-domain data is available than just the development set. This would reveal how robust the method is across data regimes and whether gains persist as in-domain supervision increases.
- Qualitative analysis: Include examples of sentences selected by different methods to support the analysis in Section 5.4. Such examples can make the advantages of your approach more concrete.
- Abstraction from surface forms: In relation to Section 5.4, consider a baseline following Axelrod (2015) that replaces some tokens with POS tags to mitigate LM sparsity. This would test whether word2vec-style embeddings provide further advantages over this type of abstraction.
- Learning source/target weights: Instead of summing source and target classification scores uniformly, consider learning combination weights. An extension to a bilingual/parallel CNN could estimate these weights end-to-end and potentially improve selection quality.

Minor comments:
- Add an architectural figure illustrating how inputs (one-hot or embedded) are combined, the convolution/pooling operations, and the final scoring layer.
- Standardize citation formatting (\citet vs. \citep), e.g., line 385.
- Clarify the procedure on line 443 for choosing the number of selected sentences, and report the same selection size across methods in the main tables.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Clear task formulation; well-motivated use of CNNs to capture salient spans; good comparisons to monolingual LM-based selection; clear model description; promising empirical gains.
- Weaknesses: Ambiguity around validation and hyperparameter selection; lack of fixed-budget comparisons; missing ablations (BOW vs. SEQ, embeddings, architecture); limited discussion and/or comparison to bilingual selection baselines; limited qualitative analysis.

Recommendation: The paper presents a promising approach with convincing intuition and encouraging results. Addressing the experimental design issues (validation protocol and fixed-budget comparisons), adding key ablations, and strengthening the connection to bilingual selection literature would significantly improve the rigor and impact of the work. Adding a small set of qualitative examples and an architectural figure would further enhance clarity.
