This paper introduces a novel method for machine translation training data selection, utilizing a CNN classifier to score and rank sentences from general-domain corpora. The comparison to existing techniques based on continuous or n-gram language models is thorough. However, it remains unclear whether the approach was evaluated against bilingual data selection methods, such as those employing the sum of cross-entropy differences.

The initial motivation for employing a CNN over recurrent architectures (RNN/LSTM) wasn’t immediately apparent, but the paper effectively argues for the CNN’s ability to identify and prioritize salient textual regions. A key strength lies in this reasoning. Nevertheless, the work lacks experimental validation regarding the relative importance of bag-of-words (BOW) versus sequential (SEQ) representations, or a combination thereof, and why the chosen representation performs best.

The description of the CNN architecture – including both one-hot encoding and semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings – is clear and comprehensive, highlighting crucial design choices. A visual diagram illustrating the layer interactions and input combinations would significantly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well-written, though some citation styles (\citet vs. \citep) appear unnecessarily parenthetical (e.g., line 385). 

The experimental results and evaluation largely support the paper’s claims. However, the method for determining the optimal number of in-domain sentences to select (line 443), based on a separate validation set, raises some concerns: 
- Details regarding the composition of this validation data are needed. Furthermore, the process of hyperparameter selection for the CNN models isn’t fully explained, and the sensitivity of the models to these hyperparameters should be investigated.
- Table 2 would benefit from a direct comparison of scores across different approaches using a consistent sentence count.  However, as Figure 1 demonstrates, the proposed approach appears to outperform the baselines even under such conditions.

Further considerations:
- An experiment comparing the technique against baselines with larger amounts of in-domain data, beyond the development set, would be valuable.
- Including example sentences selected by each method in the results or discussion section (Section 5.4) would strengthen the support for the presented claims.
- Regarding the discussion of abstracting away from surface forms (Section 5.4), comparing against the work of Axelrod (2015) – which uses POS tags to address LM data sparsity – could help determine the added benefit of word2vec embeddings.
- The use of a sum of source and target classification scores closely resembles the Lewis-Moore language model data selection approach (sum of cross-entropy differences). A citation of this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- Finally, exploring the potential of learning weights for the combined source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting warrants investigation.
