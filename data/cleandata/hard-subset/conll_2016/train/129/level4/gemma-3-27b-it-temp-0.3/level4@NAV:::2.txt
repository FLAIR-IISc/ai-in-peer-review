This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) utilizing a convolutional neural network classifier, building upon the framework established by Johnson and Zhang, 2015. The proposed method demonstrates approximately 0.5 BLEU points of improvement over language model-based data selection and, notably, maintains robustness even with limited in-domain data.

The authors report improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points; however, the presented results indicate that these gains are largely dependent on the presence of in-domain data within the initial training set. Training solely on the in-domain data already yields a +2.8 BLEU point improvement. A comparison against a system employing interpolation between separate in- and out-of-domain models could provide valuable context. 

The most compelling aspect of this work, in my view, is the second experiment, which showcases the CNN classifier’s effectiveness even with scarce in-domain data. However, this experiment is confined to the zh2en task, which *does* include in-domain data in the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It remains unclear whether these results generalize to other tasks lacking in-domain training data. The current results for en2es and en2zh suggest this may be the case, given the small development sets (a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen the claim, reporting results for all tasks trained with only 100 sentence pairs would be beneficial.

Considering the application to social media translation, which frequently presents unique challenges such as high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rates stemming from non-standard spelling (particularly in Latin scripts), and the presence of special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons, it would be helpful to understand any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the data. Given that data selection alone cannot resolve the OOV issue, further detail regarding the specific improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection – perhaps with illustrative examples – would be valuable.
