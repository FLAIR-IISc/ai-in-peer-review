This paper describes an MT training data selection approach that scores and ranks common-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. It compares well with previous work using continuous or N-gram-based language models, even though it is unclear whether the paper also compared against bilingual data selection (such as the sum of the difference of cross- entropies).

The motivation for using CNN instead of RNN/LSTM was initially unclear to me, but it is a strength of the paper that it argues that some parts of a text/sentence are more important than others, and this is achieved by CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally show whether the representation of BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both) is more important and why.

CNN's textual description (semi-supervised using one-hot or pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and points out important aspects. However, a picture of the layers shows how the input is combined, which would be equivalent to a thousand words.

Overall, the paper is well-written, but some parentheses are not necessary for the quotes (\citet vs \citep) (for example, line 385).

The experiments and evaluations support the paper's claims, but I am a little concerned about the method of determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set:

- What validation data is used here? It is also unclear on what data the CNN model's hyperparameters are chosen from. How sensitive are the models to this?
- Table 2 should actually compare the scores of different approaches with the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 shows, the paper's approach still seems to outperform the baseline in this case.

Other comments:

- I would be interested in an experiment that compares the paper's technique to the baseline when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- The results or discussion section may contain some example sentences selected by different methods to support the claims made in section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument to move away from surface forms in 5.4:

Another baseline to compare could be the work of Excelrod, 2015, who replaced some words with POS tags to reduce the data sparsity of the LMT data to see if word2vec embeddings provided additional benefits on this.
- Using the sum of the source and target classification scores is similar to the source and target Lewis-Moore LMT data selection: the sum of the difference of cross- entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.

Finally, I wonder if you could learn weights for the sum of both the source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting.
