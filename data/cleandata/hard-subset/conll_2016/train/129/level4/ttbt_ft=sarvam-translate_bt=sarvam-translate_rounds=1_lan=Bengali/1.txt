This paper describes an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks common-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. Comparison with previous work using continuous or N-gram-based language models is done well, although it is not clear whether the paper is also compared with bilingual data selection (such as the sum of the difference in cross-entropy).

The motivation for using a CNN instead of RNN/LSTM was initially unclear to me, but it is a strength of the paper that it argues that some parts of a text/sentence are more important than others and this is achieved by a CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally show whether a BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both) representation is more important and why.

CNN's textual description (using one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings) highlights the clear, detailed, and important aspects. However, a picture of the layers would be worth a thousand words to show how the inputs are combined.

Overall, the paper is well-written, but some parentheses for quotes (e.g., \citet vs. \citep) (for example, line 385) are unnecessary.

The tests and evaluations support the paper's claims, but I am a little concerned about the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443):

- What validation data has been used here? It is also not clear from what data the hyperparameters of the CNN models were selected. How sensitive are the models to this?
- Table 2 should really compare the scores of different methods with the same number of selected sentences. As shown in Figure 1, in this case, the paper's method still performs better than the baselines.

Other comments:

- I would be interested in conducting an experiment that compares the paper's technique with the baselines when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
- The results or discussion section may contain some example sentences selected by different methods that support the claims made in section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument to abstract from the surface in 5.4:
Another baseline for comparison could have been the work of Accelrod, 2015, who replaced some words with POS tags to reduce the LM data sparsity and see if word2vec provided additional benefits over the embeddings.
- Using the sum of the source and target classification scores is similar to the source and target Lewis-Moore LM data selection: the sum of the difference in cross-entropy. A reference to this work around line 435 would be reasonable.

Finally, I am wondering if you could learn to weight the sum of both the source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model in a bilingual/parallel setting.
