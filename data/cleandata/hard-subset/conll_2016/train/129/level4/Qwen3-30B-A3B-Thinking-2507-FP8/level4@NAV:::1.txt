

The paper presents an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work employing continuous or n-gram language models is well-executed, though the paper does not explicitly compare against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., sum of differences in cross-entropies).  

The rationale for choosing a CNN over RNN/LSTM was initially unclear, but the paper strengthens its contribution by arguing that specific text segments carry greater importance, which the CNN effectively captures. However, the work lacks experimental validation on whether a BOW, SEQ, or combined representation is more critical and why.  

The textual explanation of the CNN (using one-hot or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings) is clear and comprehensive. A diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would significantly enhance clarity.  

The paper is well-written, though unnecessary parentheses in citations (\citet vs. \citep) appear in places (e.g., line 385).  

Experiments support the paper’s claims, but concerns arise regarding the selection of in-domain sentence count (line 443), determined via a separate validation set:  
- The validation data source is unspecified, and it remains unclear which CNN hyperparameters were tuned on this set or how sensitive the models are to this choice.  
- Table 2 should compare all approaches using identical sentence counts; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms baselines under this constraint.  

Additional suggestions:  
- An experiment comparing the technique against baselines with larger in-domain datasets (beyond the development set) would be valuable.  
- Including example sentences selected by different methods in Section 5.4 would better substantiate claims.  
- For the surface-form abstraction argument in Section 5.4, comparing against Axelrod (2015), which uses POS tags to reduce LM sparsity, would clarify if word2vec embeddings offer additional benefits.  
- The sum of source and target classification scores closely mirrors Lewis-Moore LM data selection (sum of cross-entropy differences); a reference to this work near line 435 is warranted.  
- Extending the CNN to learn weights for the bilingual sum (source + target scores) could be explored.
