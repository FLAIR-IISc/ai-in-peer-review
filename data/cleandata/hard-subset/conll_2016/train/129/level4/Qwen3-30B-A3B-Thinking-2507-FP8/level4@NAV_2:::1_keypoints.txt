- Summary: Semi-supervised CNN approach for selecting in-domain SMT training data effective with very limited in-domain supervision (as few as 100 sentences).

- Contribution 1: Shows CNNs can select in-domain training data with extremely limited supervision, enabling fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates the semi-supervised CNN outperforms LM-based data selection by up to 3.1 BLEU points when in-domain data is minimal.

- Contribution 3: Provides evidence the method works even when the general-domain training data contains no explicit in-domain component.

- Strength: Addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation by working with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, useful for social media translation.

- Strength: Achieves consistent and compelling BLEU improvements (e.g., 2.1 BLEU over baselines) across four language directions and multiple test domains.

- Strength: Shows stability across multiple runs with different 100-sentence subsets, with low BLEU variance (std dev 0.12).

- Weakness: Lacks comparison to bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies), weakening novelty claims.

- Weakness: Unclear methodology for selecting the optimal number of sentences and the source of validation data, raising reproducibility concerns.

- Weakness: Does not experimentally determine which text representation (BOW vs. SEQ or combination) is most effective for the CNN, despite emphasizing this choice.

- Question: Please compare your method against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., Lewis-Moore) using more in-domain data, not just the dev set.

- Question: Provide example sentences selected by your method versus baselines to illustrate selection quality.

- Question: How does the semi-supervised CNN compare to Axelrod et al. (2015) POS-replacement approach for data sparsity?

- Question: Can the CNN be extended to handle bilingual/parallel data directly rather than scoring source and target separately?
