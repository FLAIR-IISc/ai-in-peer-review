

The paper proposes a CNN-based approach for scoring and ranking general-domain sentences in MT training data selection. The comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram language models is thorough, though it remains unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual selection methods (e.g., Lewis and Moore's sum of cross-entropy differences).  

The motivation for using a CNN over RNNs/LSTMs initially seemed unclear, but the paper effectively argues that CNNs better capture localized, salient features within sentences. However, the paper does not experimentally evaluate whether Bag-of-Words (BOW), Sequence (SEQ), or a combined representation is more critical, nor does it justify the choice.  

The textual description of the CNN (including one-hot inputs and semi-supervised embeddings) is clear and technically precise. A diagram illustrating the layer architecture and input combination would significantly enhance understanding.  

The paper is well-written overall, though unnecessary parentheses in citations (e.g., `\citet` vs. `\citep` at line 385) should be standardized.  

Experiments support the core claims, but concerns arise regarding the selection of in-domain sentence counts (line 443):  
1. The validation data source is unspecified, and it is unclear how CNN hyperparameters were tuned. Sensitivity analysis of these choices would strengthen the methodology.  
2. Table 2 should compare all approaches using identical numbers of selected sentences (as Figure 1 suggests the proposed method outperforms baselines under this constraint).  

Additional suggestions:  
- Evaluate the method against baselines when more in-domain data is available (beyond the development set).  
- Include example sentences selected by each method in Section 5.4 to concretely illustrate claims about selection quality.  
- Consider comparing against Axelrod et al. (2015), who replace words with POS tags to mitigate LM sparsity, to assess whether word2vec embeddings provide additional gains beyond this approach.  
- Reference Lewis and Moore (2011) when discussing the sum-of-scores approach (line 435), as it closely parallels their "sum of cross-entropy differences" method.  
- Explore extending the CNN to jointly learn weights for source and target classification scores in a bilingual setting, as this could enhance the approach.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to data selection, but addressing these points—particularly the validation methodology, representation comparison, and additional baselines—would significantly strengthen its impact. The core idea is sound, and the technical execution is generally robust, but these refinements are necessary for full clarity and rigor.
