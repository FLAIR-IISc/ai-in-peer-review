- Summary: Semi-supervised CNN selects in-domain sentences from general-domain data to adapt SMT and works with very small in-domain datasets.

- Contribution 1: Semi-supervised CNN data-selection yields up to +3.1 BLEU using as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

- Contribution 2: Method shows robustness across multiple language directions and social-media test domains, outperforming LM-based selection.

- Contribution 3: Enables fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation by selecting highly relevant in-domain sentences without large in-domain corpora.

- Strength 1: Delivers substantial gains over strong baselines, notably on Chinese→English SMS.

- Strength 2: Thorough experiments demonstrate consistent improvements even with extremely limited in-domain data.

- Strength 3: Effectively leverages large unlabeled general-domain data via word embeddings in a semi-supervised framework.

- Weakness 1: The 3.1 BLEU gain may be partly due to a 120K in-domain subset present in the general-domain data; no direct baseline using that subset is reported.

- Weakness 2: The 100-sentence experiment was only run on Chinese→English SMS (which contains in-domain data in training), limiting claims of general applicability.

- Weakness 3: Paper does not address social-media-specific challenges (high OOVs, misspellings, usernames, hashtags, emoticons) or corresponding preprocessing.

- Question 1: How are word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors combined in the CNN (please illustrate)?

- Question 2: What loss function was used to train the semi-supervised CNN?

- Question 3: How were system weights tuned for the final SMT systems?

- Question 4: What preprocessing or filtering was applied to social media data to handle unconventional spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?

- Question 5: Can you provide specific translation examples showing improvements, especially for high-OOV/challenging cases?
