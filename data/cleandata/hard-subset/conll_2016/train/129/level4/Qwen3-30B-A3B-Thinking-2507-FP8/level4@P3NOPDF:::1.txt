

The paper presents a CNN-based approach for scoring and ranking general-domain sentences in MT training data selection, with a clear comparison against continuous and n-gram language models. However, the comparison lacks inclusion of bilingual data selection methods (e.g., Lewis-Moore’s sum of cross-entropy differences), which is a notable omission given the method’s similarity to such approaches. The rationale for using a CNN over RNN/LSTM is initially unclear but strengthens the paper’s contribution by emphasizing the CNN’s ability to prioritize salient text segments. Unfortunately, the authors do not experimentally validate whether a bag-of-words (BOW), sequence (SEQ), or combined representation is more effective, leaving this critical design choice unexamined.

The CNN architecture description (including one-hot and semi-supervised embedding variants) is technically sound and well-explained. A schematic diagram of the layer architecture would significantly enhance clarity, as textual descriptions alone cannot fully convey how inputs are processed. The writing is generally strong, though minor inconsistencies in citation formatting (e.g., \citet vs. \citep) appear in places (e.g., line 385).

The experimental evaluation supports the core claims, but two methodological points require clarification. First, the selection of in-domain sentence counts (line 443) relies on a validation set whose source and composition are unspecified. Crucially, it is unclear how CNN hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensions, filter sizes) were tuned or how sensitive results are to these choices. Second, Table 2 should compare methods using the *same* number of selected sentences, as Figure 1 suggests the proposed approach’s advantage persists under this constraint—this would strengthen the comparison. 

Additional suggestions for improvement include:  
1. **Scalability testing**: Extend experiments beyond the development set to assess performance when more in-domain data is available, addressing practical deployment scenarios.  
2. **Qualitative analysis**: Include example sentences selected by the proposed method versus baselines (e.g., in Section 5.4) to concretely illustrate differences in selection patterns.  
3. **Baseline expansion**: Compare against Axelrod et al. (2015), who replace words with POS tags to reduce LM sparsity, to isolate the contribution of word embeddings beyond surface-form abstraction.  
4. **Bilingual connection**: Explicitly reference Lewis-Moore (2015) when discussing the sum of source/target classification scores (line 435), as this mirrors their sum-of-differences approach.  
5. **Model extension**: Explore jointly learning weights for source and target scores by adapting the CNN to a bilingual framework, which could further optimize the selection criterion.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to data selection, but these gaps—particularly the unaddressed bilingual comparison, methodological transparency around validation, and missing ablation studies—limit the strength of its claims. Addressing them would significantly bolster the work’s impact. Overall, the approach is well-motivated and technically sound, but the current evaluation requires deeper scrutiny to fully establish its advantages over existing paradigms.
