

The paper proposes an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work employing continuous or n-gram language models is well-executed; however, it remains unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., sum of differences in cross-entropies).  

The motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was initially unclear but is strengthened by the paper’s argument that certain text/sentence segments hold greater importance, which the CNN effectively captures. However, the paper does not experimentally evaluate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation (or their combination) is more critical and why.  

The textual description of the CNN (using one-hot or semi-supervised representations with pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights key aspects. A diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would greatly enhance understanding.  

The paper is overall well-written, though some citation commands unnecessarily use parentheses (\citet vs. \citep; e.g., line 385).  

Experiments and evaluation generally support the paper’s claims, but concerns arise regarding the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set:  
- What validation data is used for this determination? It is also unclear which hyperparameters of the CNN models are optimized using this validation set, and how sensitive the models are to this choice.  
- Table 2 should compare different approaches using the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 demonstrates, the proposed method still outperforms baselines under this condition.  

Additional comments:  
- An experiment comparing the proposed technique against baselines with more in-domain data (beyond the development set) would be valuable.  
- Including example sentences selected by different methods in the results or discussion section would strengthen the claims in Section 5.4.  
- For the argument about abstracting from surface forms in Section 5.4, an additional baseline could be Axelrod (2015), which replaces words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity, to assess whether word2vec embeddings offer advantages over this approach.  
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores closely parallels source & target Lewis-Moore LM data selection (sum of differences in cross-entropies). A reference to this work near line 435 is warranted.  
- Finally, extending the CNN model to the bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for the sum of source and target classification scores could be explored.
