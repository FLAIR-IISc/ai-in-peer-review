

The paper proposes a CNN-based approach for in-domain data selection in SMT, extending Johnson and Zhang (2015). While the method achieves a 0.5 BLEU point improvement over language model-based selection and demonstrates robustness with minimal in-domain data, several critical issues require clarification and stronger validation.  

The most significant concern is the overstated claim of a 3.1 BLEU point improvement. The results indicate that this gain is only realized *when in-domain data is present in the training set*; training solely on the in-domain data already yields +2.8 BLEU. The authors should explicitly compare their method against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models to isolate the contribution of data selection. Without this comparison, the claimed 3.1 BLEU improvement conflates the benefits of in-domain data availability with the selection method itself, undermining the novelty of the proposed framework.  

The second experiment—demonstrating effectiveness with minimal in-domain data (e.g., 100 sentences)—is compelling but lacks sufficient validation. The experiment is conducted *only* on the zh-en task, which inherently includes in-domain data in the training set (making selection easier). This raises questions about generalizability: Would the method perform equally well on tasks without in-domain data in training (e.g., en-es and en-zh, where development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs)? The authors should report results for all tasks using 100 in-domain sentences to substantiate the robustness claim. Current evidence from en-es/en-zh suggests promise, but a unified analysis across all tasks is essential for a credible conclusion.  

The paper also neglects a critical challenge in social media translation: high OOV rates due to non-standard spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. The authors do not describe any preprocessing or filtering for these artifacts. Given that data selection cannot resolve OOV issues, it is vital to clarify *what specific improvements* the method delivers. Providing concrete examples (e.g., "In-domain selection reduced OOVs by X% for hashtags like @username") would strengthen the analysis and contextualize the method’s practical utility.  

**Specific Section Comments:**  
- **Section 3.2:** The model architecture requires clearer explanation. How are word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors combined? A schematic diagram would greatly aid understanding. Additionally, the "designated loss function" should be explicitly named (e.g., cross-entropy, margin-based loss) to enable reproducibility.  
- **Section 5.2:** The hyperparameter tuning process (e.g., system weight optimization) warrants brief mention for completeness. How were weights selected? Were they tuned on development data or via cross-validation?  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The CNN-based data selection framework shows promise, particularly for low-resource in-domain adaptation. However, the primary claim of a 3.1 BLEU improvement is misleading without proper baseline comparisons, and the robustness claim lacks cross-task validation. Addressing these gaps—through a direct comparison with interpolated models, expanded task coverage for the minimal-data experiment, and explicit discussion of OOV handling—would significantly strengthen the contribution. The technical details in Section 3.2 also require clarification to support methodological transparency.  

The paper’s core insight (CNNs enable effective selection with scarce in-domain data) is valuable, but current evidence does not fully support its scope. With the requested revisions, this work could make a meaningful contribution to SMT adaptation. As currently presented, the results are partially contingent on task-specific data availability, limiting the generalizability of the conclusions.
