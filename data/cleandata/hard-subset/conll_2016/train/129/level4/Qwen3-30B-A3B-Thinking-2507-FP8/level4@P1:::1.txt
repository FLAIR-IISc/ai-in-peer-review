

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for data selection in statistical machine translation adaptation, particularly effective when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The primary contribution is a novel data selection method that uses CNNs to identify domain-relevant sentences from large general-domain corpora. The secondary contribution is demonstrating that this method significantly outperforms existing language model-based approaches even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences for training.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important practical problem: domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data, which is increasingly relevant with the rise of social media domains where labeled data is scarce. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method achieves up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over strong baselines while using only 3% of the training data.

2. The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated and properly justified. The authors effectively explain why CNNs are more suitable than RNNs/LSTMs for this task, highlighting that CNNs can identify important local features within text regardless of their position. The incorporation of pre-trained word embeddings from large general-domain data is particularly well-executed and addresses the critical challenge of limited in-domain data.

3. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across four language directions and multiple test domains, including challenging social media domains. The ablation study showing performance with only 100 in-domain sentences is particularly compelling, demonstrating the method's practical utility for fine-grained topic adaptation.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately compare against bilingual data selection methods that use similar scoring approaches (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies). While the authors compare against n-gram and RNN language models, they omit comparison to methods that directly use sentence pair similarity, which would strengthen their claim of superiority.

2. The method for determining the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences (based on a separate validation set) lacks sufficient detail. It's unclear what validation data was used, how hyperparameters were selected, or how sensitive the results are to these choices. Table 2 compares methods with different numbers of selected sentences, which makes direct comparison difficult despite Figure 1 showing consistent advantages.

3. The paper does not experimentally compare BOW versus SEQ representations (or their combination) to determine which is more important for the task. While the authors describe both approaches, they do not provide evidence on which representation yields better performance or why one might be preferable.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify what validation data was used to determine the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences? How sensitive are your results to this choice?

2. Could you provide examples of sentences selected by your method versus the baseline approaches to illustrate the difference in selection quality?

3. How would your approach perform if the in-domain data were more abundant (e.g., 1000+ sentences)? Would the advantage over language model methods diminish as more in-domain data becomes available?

4. The paper mentions that using the sum of source and target scores is similar to Lewis-Moore LM data selection. Could you provide a reference to this method around line 435 and clarify the relationship between the two approaches?

5. Could the CNN model be extended to learn weights for the source and target scores rather than simply summing them, potentially improving performance further?
