

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only minimal in-domain data is available. The authors train a CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentences using a small labeled dataset (as few as 100 sentences) and pretrained word embeddings from general-domain data. This model then ranks general-domain sentences by domain relevance, selecting top candidates to train an adapted translation system. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel data selection method that enables effective translation adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), (2) a semi-supervised CNN framework that leverages large unlabeled data to overcome the small in-domain data constraint, and (3) empirical validation showing significant improvements over strong language model-based baselines, particularly in low-data regimes.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection**: The paper makes a compelling case for using CNNs over traditional language models for domain adaptation when in-domain data is scarce. The authors correctly identify that CNNs can effectively capture domain-relevant local features (e.g., "Dell" in "I have a Dell desktop") while ignoring general-domain words, which is particularly valuable for fine-grained adaptation. This is a significant contribution as it addresses a clear gap in the literature where existing methods require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements across four language directions and three test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieves up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over the "alldata" baseline with only ~3% of training data, outperforming all baselines (including state-of-the-art language model methods) especially when in-domain data is limited (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences). The stability of results across repeated experiments with different 100-sentence samples further strengthens the claims.

3. **Clear technical description**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the CNN architecture (one-hot vs. semi-supervised variants) and the semi-supervised learning framework. The justification for using word embeddings trained on general-domain data to compensate for limited in-domain data is well articulated and addresses a critical challenge in the problem setting.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Incomplete comparison to relevant baselines**: The paper does not adequately compare against bilingual data selection methods like Axelrod et al. (2011, 2015), which also use cross-entropy differences between in-domain and out-domain language models. The authors claim their method "outperforms three state-of-the-art language model based data selection methods," but it's unclear whether they directly compared against bilingual approaches that use similar metrics (e.g., sum of source and target scores, which resembles the Lewis-Moore method). This omission weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of ablation study for input representations**: The paper uses both bag-of-words (BOW) and sequential (SEQ) representations but does not experimentally demonstrate which representation contributes more to performance or why. The claim that "BOW representation loses word order information but is more robust to data sparsity" is not supported by empirical evidence. An ablation study would strengthen the methodological contribution.

3. **Unclear validation procedure**: The paper states that the number of selected sentences is determined by "empirical determination on a separate validation set" (line 443), but it doesn't specify what data constitutes this validation set or how hyperparameters were tuned. This is particularly concerning given that the validation set might contain domain-specific data that could bias the selection. The authors should clarify whether the validation set was truly independent (e.g., not from the same test domain) and report sensitivity analysis on the number of selected sentences.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your method compare to Axelrod et al. (2015), which uses POS tagging to handle data sparsity in language model adaptation? Could the word2vec embeddings provide a comparable or additional advantage over their approach?

2. In Figure 1, the "sscnn" method achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement with 100 in-domain sentences, while LM-based methods show minimal gains. Could you provide example sentence pairs selected by your method versus LM-based baselines (e.g., "ngram" or "rnnlm") for one of the test domains? This would help illustrate why your approach is more effective with limited data.

3. You use the sum of source and target classification scores for selection. How would extending the CNN model to jointly process bilingual sentence pairs (rather than processing source and target separately) impact performance? Would this allow for learning weights for the sum of scores?
