

The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), building upon Johnson and Zhang (2015). It reports a 0.5 BLEU point improvement over language model-based selection and highlights robustness when only minimal in-domain data is available. However, the claimed 3.1 BLEU point improvement requires careful interpretation. The results indicate this gain is only achieved when in-domain data is already present in the training set; training solely on in-domain data yields +2.8 BLEU. The authors should clarify whether this represents a net gain over a baseline trained on out-domain data alone. Comparing against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models would provide stronger context for the claimed improvement.

The most compelling finding is the CNN’s effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data (e.g., 100 sentences). However, this experiment is restricted to the zh2en task, where the training set inherently contains in-domain data, potentially simplifying the selection task. The results for en2es and en2zh, which feature development sets with only a few hundred sentence pairs, suggest the approach may generalize, but the claim lacks cross-task validation. Reporting results for all tasks using exactly 100 in-domain sentences would significantly strengthen this contribution.

The paper addresses social media translation, a domain characterized by high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates due to unconventional spelling (e.g., "u" for "you") and non-standard elements like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. The authors should explicitly state whether any specialized preprocessing (e.g., normalization of usernames, handling of emoticons) was applied to the training or test data. Without this, it is unclear how the method handles these challenges. Furthermore, since data selection cannot mitigate OOV issues, the paper would benefit from concrete examples illustrating *what specific translation improvements* arise from data selection (e.g., better handling of slang, reduced mistranslation of hashtags), beyond general BLEU gains.

Regarding technical details:
- **Section 3.2**: The combination mechanism of word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors is not sufficiently explained. A diagram of the model architecture would greatly clarify how these components integrate. Additionally, the "designated loss function" requires explicit specification.
- **Section 5.2**: For completeness, the paper should briefly describe how system weights were tuned (e.g., via development set optimization).

The paper makes a valid contribution by demonstrating CNN-based data selection’s robustness with scarce in-domain data. However, the primary claim of a 3.1 BLEU point improvement hinges on a specific experimental setup that may overstate the method’s incremental value. The zh2en-only validation of the low-data regime also limits the generalizability of this key finding. Addressing these points—particularly by reporting cross-task results with minimal in-domain data, clarifying preprocessing for social media text, and providing concrete examples of translation improvements—would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and credibility. The technical suggestions regarding model architecture and weight tuning are also essential for reproducibility.
