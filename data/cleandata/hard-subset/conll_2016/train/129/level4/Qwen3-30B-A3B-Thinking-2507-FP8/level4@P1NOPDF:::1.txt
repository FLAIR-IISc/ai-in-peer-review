

The paper presents a novel approach for selecting MT training data by scoring and ranking general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison against prior work employing continuous or n-gram language models is thorough. However, it remains unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection methods, such as the sum of differences in cross-entropies, which would strengthen the evaluation.

The motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM initially seemed ambiguous, but the paper effectively argues that certain sentence segments carry more importance—precisely what CNNs capture through their local receptive fields. Nevertheless, the paper does not experimentally compare BOW versus SEQ (or combined) representations to demonstrate which is more critical for performance, leaving this key design choice unvalidated.

The textual description of the CNN architecture (including one-hot encoding and semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings) is clear and technically precise. A visual diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would significantly enhance understanding, as a picture would indeed be worth a thousand words.

The paper is well-written overall, though redundant parentheses in citations (e.g., `\citet` vs. `\citep` at line 385) should be standardized for consistency.

Experiments robustly support the paper’s claims, but concerns arise regarding the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set. Specifically:
- What data constitutes this validation set? 
- How are CNN hyperparameters tuned, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the *same* number of selected sentences (as Figure 1 suggests the proposed method outperforms baselines under this constraint).

Additional suggestions:
1. Extend experiments to scenarios with *more* in-domain data (beyond the development set), to assess scalability.
2. Include example sentences selected by each method in Section 5.4 to concretely illustrate the claims about representation quality.
3. For the argument about abstracting surface forms (Section 5.4), consider comparing against Axelrod (2015), who replaces words with POS tags to reduce LM sparsity—a relevant baseline for evaluating word2vec’s added value.
4. The sum-of-scores approach (source + target classification) closely mirrors Lewis & Moore’s bilingual data selection (sum of cross-entropy differences). A reference to this work around line 435 would contextualize the contribution more effectively.
5. Finally, could the CNN be extended to the bilingual setting to *learn weights* for the source-target score combination, rather than using a fixed sum? This would be a natural progression.
