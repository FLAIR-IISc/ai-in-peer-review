

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only minimal in-domain data is available. The main contributions, ordered by significance, are:  
1) A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification that effectively leverages limited in-domain data and large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data.  
2) Empirical demonstration that this method significantly outperforms language model-based approaches when in-domain data is scarce (as few as 100 sentences), achieving up to 2.1 BLEU improvement.  
3) Demonstration of fine-grained topic-dependent adaptation feasibility, which was previously unattainable due to data collection constraints.  

**Strengths**  
1) **Robust performance with minimal in-domain data**: The method achieves substantial improvements (up to 2.1 BLEU) even when training the classifier with only 100 in-domain sentences, a scenario where all baseline methods show diminished returns. This addresses a critical gap in the literature where existing methods require thousands of in-domain sentences. The stability across repeated experiments (standard deviation of 0.12 BLEU) is particularly convincing.  

2) **Superior performance on challenging domains**: The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all baselines (including the state-of-the-art RNNLM-based method) across all four language directions. Notably, it achieves a 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task—surpassing a baseline trained on the entire in-domain subset (25.7 BLEU vs. 26.0 BLEU). This demonstrates the method's ability to identify high-quality in-domain data even when the training set contains some in-domain content.  

3) **Practical applicability**: The approach requires no domain-specific preprocessing or specialized resources, making it directly applicable to emerging domains like social media translation. The authors show that the method can select data from large general-domain corpora (e.g., 9-15M sentence pairs) to build effective translation systems, which is valuable for real-world deployment where in-domain data is scarce.  

**Weaknesses**  
1) **Overstated BLEU improvements**: The paper claims "up to 3.1 BLEU" improvement, but this is achieved only when the training data contains a substantial in-domain subset (120K sentences for zh2en). The baseline trained on this subset already achieves 25.7 BLEU, making the absolute improvement less impressive. The authors should clarify whether the 3.1 BLEU is relative to the full general-domain system (22.9 BLEU) or the in-domain-only baseline (25.7 BLEU).  

2) **Limited evaluation of the "tiny data" scenario**: The critical experiment with 100 in-domain sentences is only reported for the zh2en task, which has a significant in-domain component in the training data. For the other three tasks (ar2en, en2es, en2zh), the in-domain training data is limited to the dev set (600-800 sentences), making the results less generalizable. The authors should report results for all tasks when using only 100 in-domain sentences to validate their claim that the method works universally.  

3) **Insufficient discussion of social media challenges**: The experiments focus on social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), which typically have high OOV rates and unconventional text (e.g., usernames, hashtags). However, the paper does not describe any preprocessing steps to handle these issues. Without addressing how OOVs were managed, it's unclear whether the observed improvements are due to domain adaptation or simply better handling of noisy data.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1) In Table 2, the "sscnn" method achieves 26.0 BLEU on zh2en SMS, while the in-domain-only baseline (indata) achieves 25.7 BLEU. How much of the 0.3 BLEU improvement is due to the CNN classifier selecting better data than the in-domain subset itself? Could this be due to the CNN identifying sentences that are slightly more relevant than the dev set?  

2) For the experiments with 100 in-domain sentences, the authors only report results for zh2en. Given that the other tasks (ar2en, en2es, en2zh) have no known in-domain data in the training set, how do the results for these tasks compare when using 100 in-domain sentences?  

3) The paper states that "the in-domain data used to train the selection model could be as few as 100 sentences." However, for social media domains like SMS, the dev set (used as in-domain data) is 6,016 sentences. How does the method perform when the in-domain data is reduced to 100 sentences *from the dev set*, rather than using the full dev set as the in-domain reference?  

4) The paper does not discuss how OOVs were handled during translation. Given that social media text has high OOV rates, how do the authors account for translation quality differences due to OOV handling versus domain adaptation?
