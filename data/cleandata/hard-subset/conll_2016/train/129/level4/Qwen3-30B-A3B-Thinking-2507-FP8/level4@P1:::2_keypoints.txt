- Proposes semi-supervised CNN classifier for in-domain data selection in SMT, building on Johnson & Zhang (2015).
- Achieves ~0.5 BLEU improvement over language model-based data selection methods.
- Shows robustness: CNN remains effective when trained with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences).
- Claims up to 3.1 BLEU gains, but much of this appears due to presence of in-domain data in the training corpus.
- Training only on the in-domain subset yields a 2.8 BLEU gain, questioning how much benefit comes from the selection method itself.
- Missing baseline: should compare against interpolation of separate in-domain and out-domain models to better isolate method effectiveness.
- Second experiment’s strong finding (robustness with 100 sentences) was only run on zh→en, which has substantial in-domain data in general training — limits generality.
- Should report 100-sentence training results for en→es and en→zh where training lacks in-domain data to validate robustness across tasks.
- Social media translation has high OOV rates and special tokens (usernames, hashtags, emoticons); paper does not state whether preprocessing/filtering was applied.
- Data selection does not solve OOV issues; paper should provide concrete examples or analyses of the types of improvements achieved.
- Section 3.2 needs clearer explanation (and diagram) of how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the architecture.
- Section 3.2 mentions a “designated loss function” but fails to specify which loss was used.
- Section 5.2 should briefly describe how system weights were tuned for completeness.
- Main contribution: demonstrating semi-supervised CNNs can select in-domain data for SMT adaptation with very limited resources.
- Secondary contribution: method outperforms language model-based selection across multiple language pairs and test domains including social media.
- Insight: CNNs capture domain-relevant features via convolution and pooling, while general-domain embeddings help generalize to unseen domain-specific terms.
- Strengths: clear demonstration with minimal in-domain data, thorough multi-language/domain evaluation, practical relevance to domains like social media.
- Weaknesses: limited scope of the 100-sentence experiment, lack of interpolation baseline comparison, and insufficient discussion of social-media-specific preprocessing and OOV handling.
