

**Summary and Contributions**  
The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT), addressing the challenge of domain adaptation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) are available. The authors' key contributions are: (1) a novel semi-supervised CNN framework that leverages general-domain data to train a domain classifier, enabling effective data selection with minimal in-domain supervision; (2) empirical demonstration that this method achieves significant gains (up to 3.1 BLEU) over LM-based baselines, particularly when in-domain data is scarce; and (3) robust performance across multiple language pairs and social media domains, showing consistent improvements even with extremely limited in-domain samples.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper tackles a critical practical challenge in SMT: adapting systems to new domains with minimal in-domain data. This is increasingly relevant as social media domains emerge with limited parallel data. The proposed semi-supervised CNN framework is well-motivated, leveraging pretrained word embeddings from general-domain data to compensate for scarce in-domain samples, which is both elegant and practical.

2. The method demonstrates exceptional robustness with minimal in-domain data. The paper shows that with only 100 in-domain sentences, the semi-supervised CNN achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over baselines, while LM-based methods show negligible gains. This represents a significant practical advantage for real-world deployment, where collecting thousands of in-domain sentences is often infeasible.

3. The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating the method across four language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) and three social media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The results consistently show that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms LM-based baselines, with improvements ranging from 0.7 to 3.1 BLEU points across tasks, and the gains are statistically significant.

4. The ablation study on varying in-domain data sizes (from 100 to 6,000 sentences) provides strong evidence of the method's stability. The semi-supervised CNN maintains consistent performance even as in-domain data decreases, while LM-based methods show significant degradation. This demonstrates the framework's practical utility for fine-grained domain adaptation where data collection is expensive.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper claims up to 3.1 BLEU improvement, but this result appears to be partially driven by a scenario where in-domain data already exists within the general-domain training set (zh2en task). Specifically, the authors note that training on the in-domain subset alone yields 25.7 BLEU, while the semi-supervised CNN outperforms this baseline. This raises questions about whether the method truly handles *completely* out-of-domain training data, which would be the most challenging scenario.

2. The most compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness with tiny in-domain data (100 sentences) comes from a single task (zh2en), which may have been easier due to the presence of in-domain data in the training set. The paper does not provide results for the other three tasks with only 100 in-domain sentences, despite the development sets for en2es and en2zh being similarly small (800 and 650 sentences). Reporting results for all tasks would better support the claim that the method generalizes across domains.

3. The paper does not address how the method handles social media-specific challenges like high OOV rates, non-standard spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. These are common in social media text and could significantly impact translation quality. Without preprocessing or filtering steps to address these issues, the reported BLEU improvements might not reflect real-world performance.

4. The paper lacks concrete examples showing *what* improvements data selection provides. For instance, it would be valuable to see specific translation examples where the adapted system outperforms the baseline, particularly in handling domain-specific terms or social media expressions. This would help clarify whether the improvements are due to better handling of domain-specific vocabulary or other factors.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. In the zh2en task, the training data contains a 120K in-domain subset. How does the method's performance compare to a system trained *only* on this in-domain subset (25.7 BLEU) versus the semi-supervised CNN? Is the improvement over this baseline due to better data selection, or does the CNN effectively combine in-domain and out-of-domain data?

2. The paper claims the method works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences. Could you provide results for all four language pairs with exactly 100 in-domain sentences? This would strengthen the claim that the approach generalizes across domains, especially for tasks like en2es and en2zh where in-domain data is not present in the training set.

3. Social media text often contains OOV words, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Were any preprocessing steps applied to handle these challenges? If so, please describe them; if not, how does the method address these issues in translation?
