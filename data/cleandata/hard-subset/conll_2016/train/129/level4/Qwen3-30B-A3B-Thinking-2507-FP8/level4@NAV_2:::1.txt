

Summary: The paper presents a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for selecting in-domain training data in statistical machine translation, particularly effective when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The main contributions of this work, as assessed by the reviewer, are: 1) Demonstrating that CNNs can effectively select in-domain training data with extremely limited in-domain supervision (100 sentences), which enables fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation; 2) Showing that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms existing language model-based data selection methods by up to 3.1 BLEU points when using minimal in-domain data; 3) Providing evidence that the method works effectively even when no in-domain data component exists within the general-domain training data.

Strengths: The paper makes a strong contribution by addressing a critical gap in domain adaptation for machine translation where previous approaches require thousands of in-domain sentences. The semi-supervised CNN approach demonstrates remarkable effectiveness with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, achieving 2.1 BLEU improvement over baselines, which is particularly valuable for social media translation tasks with limited in-domain data. The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements across four language directions and multiple test domains, with the semi-supervised CNN outperforming all LM-based baselines significantly when in-domain data is scarce. The paper also provides robust evidence of stability through multiple runs with different 100-sentence subsets, with minimal standard deviation (0.12) in BLEU scores.

Weaknesses: The paper does not clearly compare against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., using sum of difference of cross-entropies), which weakens the novelty claim and makes it difficult to assess the full scope of the contribution. The methodology for determining the optimal number of selected sentences (based on a separate validation set) lacks clarity regarding the validation data source and hyperparameter selection process, raising concerns about reproducibility and sensitivity to this choice. The paper fails to experimentally determine which text representation (BOW vs. SEQ or their combination) is most effective for the CNN, which is a significant omission given the authors' emphasis on this aspect in their methodology.

Questions to Authors: Could you compare your method against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., Lewis-Moore method) when more in-domain data is available, not just the development set? Please provide example sentences selected by your method versus the baselines to illustrate the quality of data selection. How does your semi-supervised CNN approach compare against Axelrod et al. (2015), which replaces words with POS tags to handle data sparsity? Could you extend your CNN model to handle bilingual/parallel data directly rather than scoring source and target sentences separately?
