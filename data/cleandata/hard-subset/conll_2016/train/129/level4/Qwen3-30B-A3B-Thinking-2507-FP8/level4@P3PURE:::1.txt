

The paper presents an MT training data selection method that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work employing continuous or n-gram language models is thorough, though it remains unclear whether the paper also compares against bilingual data selection approaches (e.g., sum of differences in cross-entropies).  

The motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was initially unclear but becomes a strength through the paper’s argument that specific text/sentence segments hold greater importance, which the CNN effectively captures. However, the paper does not experimentally assess whether a bag-of-words (BOW) representation, a sequence (SEQ) representation, or their combination is more critical, nor does it explain why.  

The textual description of the CNN (using either one-hot encoding or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights key aspects. A diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would significantly enhance clarity.  

The paper is well-written overall, though unnecessary parentheses in citations (e.g., using \citet versus \citep, as seen on line 385) should be addressed.  

Experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but concerns arise regarding the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set:  
- What validation data is used? Additionally, it is unclear how hyperparameters for the CNN models are selected, and the sensitivity of the models to this choice remains unexamined.  
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the same number of selected sentences; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms baselines under this condition.  

Additional comments:  
- An experiment comparing the method against baselines with more in-domain data (beyond the development set) would be valuable.  
- The results or discussion section could include example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate claims in Section 5.4.  
- For the argument regarding abstraction from surface forms in Section 5.4, a comparison against Axelrod (2015), which replaces words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity, would be relevant to assess whether word2vec embeddings offer additional advantages.  
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores closely mirrors the Lewis-Moore LM data selection method (sum of differences in cross-entropies). A reference to this work around line 435 is warranted.  
- Finally, extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for the sum of source and target scores could be explored.
