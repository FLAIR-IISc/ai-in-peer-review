

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only *extremely limited* in-domain data (e.g., 100 sentences) is available. The core contribution is a semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method that leverages general-domain data to identify relevant in-domain training sentences, enabling fine-grained adaptation for emerging domains (e.g., social media). The primary contribution is **demonstrating effective adaptation with minimal in-domain supervision** (as few as 100 sentences), outperforming prior language model (LM)-based methods. A secondary contribution is **providing a scalable solution for high-frequency domain adaptation** (e.g., hundreds of topics), as the approach avoids the prohibitive cost of collecting thousands of in-domain sentences per topic. The paper does not claim novelty in CNN architecture itself but rather in its *application to data selection under severe data scarcity*.

**Strengths**  
1. **Solves a critical, underexplored problem**: The paper tackles a genuine industry pain point—adapting SMT to new domains (e.g., SMS, Facebook posts) where in-domain data is scarce or nonexistent. The results (2.1 BLEU gain with only 100 in-domain sentences) directly address this gap, making the work highly relevant for real-world deployment in social media and emerging domains.  
2. **Robust empirical validation**: The method consistently outperforms LM-based baselines (e.g., n-gram, RNNLM) across four language pairs and three test domains. Crucially, the advantage widens as in-domain data shrinks (e.g., 2.1 BLEU vs. 0.5 BLEU for LM methods at 100 sentences), proving the approach’s stability under data scarcity.  
3. **Practical scalability**: The method requires no domain-specific data collection beyond a small dev set (e.g., 6,000 sentences), enabling rapid adaptation for hundreds of topics. This is a significant operational advantage over prior work (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013), which assumes thousands of in-domain sentences per domain.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Incomplete comparison to bilingual data selection**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art LM-based methods" but fails to compare against *bilingual* data selection approaches (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2015, which uses POS tags to reduce sparsity). This is critical, as the paper’s score-sum approach (source + target) is nearly identical to Lewis-Moore’s LM-based method (sum of cross-entropy differences). Omitting this comparison undermines the claimed novelty.  
2. **Unclear validation methodology**: The number of selected sentences (e.g., 300K for *zh2en*) is determined via a "separate validation set," but the paper does not specify: (a) whether this is held-out dev data, (b) how hyperparameters (e.g., CNN layers, embedding size) were tuned, or (c) sensitivity to these choices. Table 2’s inconsistent sentence counts across methods (e.g., "ohcnn" uses 300K vs. "sscnn" uses 300K but with different BLEU gains) obscure the true performance gap.  
3. **Unjustified representation choice**: The paper uses a combination of BOW and SEQ representations without empirical justification (e.g., "BOW loses word order but is robust; SEQ preserves order"). Figure 1 suggests SEQ may be suboptimal (e.g., "ohcnn" underperforms "sscnn"), yet the authors do not test which representation dominates or why. A clear ablation study is missing.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your method compare to Axelrod et al. (2015), which uses POS-tagged bilingual data selection to handle sparsity? Their approach may achieve similar gains without word embeddings, making your "semi-supervised" claim less distinctive.  
2. Can you clarify the validation set used for selecting the number of sentences (e.g., is it a held-out portion of the dev set?) and provide sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (e.g., CNN depth, embedding dimension)?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper’s claim that "CNNs capture domain-relevant phrases better than LMs" (Section 5.4) is plausible but lacks evidence. Including example sentences selected by your method vs. LM baselines would strengthen this argument.  
- The abstract claims "3.1 BLEU improvement" but Table 2 shows only 3.1 BLEU *over the baseline* (not absolute). Clarify this in the abstract.  
- The draft’s citation style (e.g., "\citet" vs. "\citep") is inconsistent; standardize to match conference guidelines.  

**Overall Recommendation**  
The paper makes a strong contribution to a meaningful problem with compelling results under data scarcity. However, the omission of key comparisons (bilingual methods) and methodological ambiguities (validation, representation choice) prevent full endorsement. Addressing these in revision would significantly strengthen the work. **Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions).
