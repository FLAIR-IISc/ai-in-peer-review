

The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in SMT, extending Johnson and Zhang (2015). It reports 0.5 BLEU point gains over language model-based selection and claims robustness with minimal in-domain data. However, the claimed 3.1 BLEU improvement appears overstated: the results show that training *only* on in-domain data yields +2.8 BLEU, suggesting much of the gain stems from using in-domain data itself rather than the selection method. I recommend comparing against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-domain models to isolate the selection method’s contribution.

The most compelling result—the CNN’s effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences)—is limited to the zh2en task, which inherently includes in-domain data in the training set. This likely simplifies the selection problem. For broader validity, results across all tasks (including en2es and en2zh, where development sets contain only a few hundred sentence pairs) should be reported when using just 100 training sentences. The current results for en2es/en2zh hint that the approach may generalize, but this requires explicit confirmation.

The paper addresses social media translation, where high OOV rates (due to unconventional spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons) are common. However, it omits details on data preprocessing or filtering. Given that data selection cannot mitigate OOV issues, it would be valuable to see specific examples demonstrating *what improvements* the method actually delivers (e.g., better handling of domain-specific terms vs. general fluency).

Specific section comments:  
**Section 3.2:** The model architecture’s combination of word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors lacks clarity. A diagram would greatly aid understanding. Additionally, the "designated loss function" is not specified—please clarify its form and rationale.  
**Section 5.2:** For completeness, briefly describe how system weights were tuned.  

Overall, the CNN approach shows promise, but the evaluation requires stronger support for its claimed advantages, particularly regarding the scope of the "minimal data" experiment and the practical impact of the selected data. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.
