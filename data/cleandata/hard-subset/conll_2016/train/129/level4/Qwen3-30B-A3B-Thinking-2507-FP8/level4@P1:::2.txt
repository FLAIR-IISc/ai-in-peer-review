

The paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building on the framework introduced by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The approach achieves approximately 0.5 BLEU points higher performance than language model-based data selection methods, and notably maintains effectiveness even when trained on extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences).

The paper claims up to 3.1 BLEU improvements, but the results indicate these gains are largely attributable to the presence of in-domain data within the training corpus. Specifically, training exclusively on the in-domain subset already yields a 2.8 BLEU improvement, suggesting the observed gains may not solely reflect the data selection method's effectiveness. It would be valuable to compare these results with a system that interpolates separate in-domain and out-domain models, which could provide a more direct baseline for the adaptation approach.

The most compelling finding is from the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier's robustness even when trained on extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences). However, this experiment was conducted solely on the zh2en task, which contains a substantial in-domain subset within the general-domain training data, potentially facilitating the selection process. It would strengthen the claim if results were reported across all tasks when using only 100 in-domain sentences for training, particularly for the en2es and en2zh tasks where no in-domain data is present in the training set (development sets contain only a few hundred sentences).

Social media translation presents unique challenges, particularly high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates due to unconventional spelling and the presence of special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It would be helpful to know if any specific preprocessing or filtering was applied to address these challenges. Given that data selection does not address OOV issues, it would be valuable to understand the specific types of improvements achieved through this adaptation method, potentially through illustrative examples.

Regarding specific sections:

Section 3.2:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined in the model architecture. An illustrative diagram would greatly enhance understanding.
- The paper mentions a "designated loss function" but does not specify it. Clarifying this would be helpful.

Section 5.2:
- For completeness, it would be helpful to briefly describe how the system weights were tuned.

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that semi-supervised CNNs can effectively select in-domain data for SMT adaptation even with extremely limited in-domain resources (as few as 100 sentences). This addresses a significant practical challenge in adapting SMT systems to new domains where collecting substantial in-domain data is prohibitively expensive.

The second most significant contribution is the demonstration that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms language model-based methods across multiple language pairs and test domains, including social media text (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), where in-domain data is typically scarce.

The third contribution is the insight that CNNs capture domain-relevant information through convolutional operations and pooling, which focus on the most informative domain-specific features within sentences, while word embeddings learned from general-domain data enable the model to generalize to unseen domain-specific terms.

The paper's strengths include the clear demonstration of effectiveness with minimal in-domain data, the thorough experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains, and the practical relevance to emerging translation domains like social media. The semi-supervised CNN approach represents a meaningful advancement in data selection for domain adaptation where traditional methods fail due to insufficient in-domain data.

The main weaknesses are the limited scope of the second experiment (only one task tested with minimal in-domain data), the lack of comparison with interpolation approaches that might better isolate the contribution of the data selection method, and insufficient discussion of handling social media-specific challenges like OOV words and special character sequences. The paper would be strengthened by addressing these points in the revision.
