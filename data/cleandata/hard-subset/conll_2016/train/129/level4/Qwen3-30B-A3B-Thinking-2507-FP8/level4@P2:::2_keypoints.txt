1. Proposes a semi-supervised CNN that leverages general-domain data to train a domain classifier for effective in-domain data selection with minimal labeled in-domain examples.  
2. Shows empirical gains up to 3.1 BLEU over LM-based baselines, especially when in-domain data is scarce.  
3. Demonstrates robust improvements across multiple language pairs and social media domains, even with extremely limited in-domain samples.  
4. Tackles practical SMT domain-adaptation with minimal in-domain data, motivated by emerging social media domains.  
5. Uses pretrained general-domain word embeddings to compensate for scarce in-domain supervision.  
6. Achieves notable BLEU gains (e.g., +2.1 BLEU) with only 100 in-domain sentences, outperforming LM-based methods.  
7. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across four language directions and three social media domains showing consistent, statistically significant gains.  
8. Ablation over in-domain sizes (100–6,000) shows the method remains stable as in-domain data decreases, unlike LM baselines.  
9. The reported 3.1 BLEU gain may be partly driven by a scenario where in-domain data exists in the general-domain training set (zh2en), raising concerns about true out-of-domain handling.  
10. The strongest tiny-data (100-sentence) evidence comes mainly from zh2en, and results for the other three tasks at 100 sentences are not reported, limiting claims of generalization.  
11. The paper does not address social-media-specific issues (high OOVs, non-standard spelling, usernames, hashtags, emoticons) or describe preprocessing to handle them.  
12. Lacks concrete translation examples illustrating what types of improvements data selection yields (e.g., domain-specific terms or social-media expressions).  
13. Question: How does semi-supervised CNN compare to training only on the 120K in-domain subset in zh2en—does it truly improve selection or just combine data better?  
14. Question: Can the authors provide results for all four language pairs with exactly 100 in-domain sentences to validate generalization?  
15. Question: Were any preprocessing steps applied for social-media artifacts (OOVs, usernames, hashtags, emoticons); if not, how are these handled?
