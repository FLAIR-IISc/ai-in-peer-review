- Presents a CNN-based method for scoring/ranking general-domain sentences for MT data selection, compared to continuous and n-gram LMs.  
- Fails to include comparison with bilingual data selection methods (e.g., Lewis-Moore’s sum-of-cross-entropy-differences), an important omission given methodological similarity.  
- Initial rationale for choosing CNN over RNN/LSTM is unclear, but the paper argues CNNs better prioritize salient text segments.  
- Does not experimentally test whether bag-of-words (BOW), sequence (SEQ), or combined representations are most effective—a critical unexamined design choice.  
- CNN architecture (including one-hot and semi-supervised embedding variants) is well explained and technically sound.  
- Recommends adding a schematic diagram of the layer architecture to improve clarity beyond textual description.  
- Writing quality is generally strong, though there are minor citation-formatting inconsistencies (e.g., \citet vs. \citep).  
- Experimental evaluation supports core claims but omits details on the validation set source/composition used to select in-domain sentence counts.  
- Unclear how CNN hyperparameters were tuned or how sensitive results are to choices like embedding dimensions and filter sizes.  
- Table 2 should compare methods using the same number of selected sentences (as Figure 1 implies the proposed method’s advantage holds under that constraint).  
- Suggests scalability testing with larger in-domain data to assess practical deployment and robustness.  
- Recommends qualitative analysis: show example sentences selected by the proposed method versus baselines to illustrate selection differences.  
- Suggests expanding baselines to include Axelrod et al. (2015) (POS-tag replacement) to isolate the contribution of word embeddings.  
- Advises explicitly referencing Lewis-Moore (2015) when discussing summed source/target classification scores due to conceptual overlap.  
- Proposes extending the model to jointly learn weights for source and target scores in a bilingual CNN framework to potentially improve selection.  
- Overall: contribution is valuable and technically sound, but gaps in bilingual comparison, validation transparency, and ablation studies weaken the claims and should be addressed.
