

Summary: The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for in-domain data selection in statistical machine translation, particularly effective when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors train a CNN classifier to identify domain-relevant sentences from general-domain data, then use these selected sentences to train an adapted SMT system.

Main contributions:
1. A semi-supervised CNN-based method for data selection that achieves significant performance improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) with as few as 100 in-domain sentences for training.
2. Demonstrated robustness of the approach across multiple language directions and test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts) compared to state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
3. The ability to select highly relevant in-domain sentences without requiring substantial in-domain data, enabling fine-grained topic-dependent translation adaptation.

Strengths:
1. The method delivers substantial performance gains (up to 3.1 BLEU) over strong baseline systems trained with large amounts of data, particularly on the Chinese-to-English SMS task, representing a meaningful contribution to domain adaptation in SMT.
2. The robustness of the approach when using extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is thoroughly demonstrated through comprehensive experiments across multiple language pairs and test domains, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods.
3. The semi-supervised CNN framework effectively leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through word embeddings, providing a solution to the challenge of limited in-domain data that previous approaches could not address.

Weaknesses:
1. The claimed 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task appears to be partially attributable to the presence of a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data, as training on this subset alone achieves 25.7 BLEU (compared to 22.9 BLEU for the baseline). The paper does not sufficiently compare against a system that directly uses this in-domain subset.
2. The second experiment demonstrating effectiveness with only 100 in-domain sentences was conducted exclusively on the Chinese-to-English SMS task, which includes in-domain data within the general-domain training set. The results for other tasks (where no in-domain data exists in the training set) are not fully explored, weakening the claim of general applicability.
3. The paper does not adequately address how the method handles common challenges in social media translation, such as high out-of-vocabulary rates due to unconventional spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons, which are particularly relevant given the focus on social media domains.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify how the different vectors (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) are combined in the CNN architecture? An illustration would be helpful for understanding the model.
2. What was the specific loss function used for training the semi-supervised CNN model?
3. How were the system weights tuned for the final SMT systems?
4. What preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the social media data (sms, tweets, Facebook posts) to handle unconventional spelling, usernames, hashtags, and emoticons?
5. Could you provide specific translation examples showing the types of improvements achieved through data selection, particularly for challenging cases with high OOV rates?
