This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) utilizing a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building upon the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed approach outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, crucially, demonstrates robustness even when limited in-domain data is available – a notable advantage over other methods.

The paper reports overall improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, a closer examination of the results reveals that these substantial gains are largely contingent on the presence of some in-domain data within the initial training set. Training solely on the in-domain data already yields a +2.8 BLEU point improvement, suggesting that the data selection process contributes a more modest gain in such scenarios. An interesting extension would be to compare this method against a system that interpolates separate models trained on in- and out-of-domain data.

The most compelling aspect of this work, in my view, lies in the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier’s continued effectiveness even with very scarce in-domain data. However, this experiment is currently limited to the zh2en task, which *does* include in-domain data in its training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It remains unclear whether these results would generalize to the other tasks (en2es and en2zh), where the initial training data lacks in-domain examples. The existing results for en2es and en2zh hint at a similar trend, given the small size of their development sets (a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen this claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks when training is performed using only 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

The application of this method to social media translation is particularly relevant, given the unique challenges inherent in this domain. Social media text often presents a high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rate, primarily due to non-conventional spelling (particularly in Latin scripts), and the frequent inclusion of special character sequences such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. It would be valuable to understand if any specific preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to address these issues.

Given that data selection cannot directly resolve the OOV problem, further detail on the specific improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection would be insightful. Providing illustrative examples of how the selected data enhances translation quality would be particularly helpful.

Several points require clarification regarding specific sections of the paper:

**Section 3.2:**

*   The combination of different vector representations (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) within the model could be explained more clearly. A diagram illustrating the model architecture would significantly improve comprehension.
*   The "designated loss function" should be explicitly defined.

**Section 5.2:**

*   For completeness, please detail the method used to tune the system weights.
