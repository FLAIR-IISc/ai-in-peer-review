- Proposes CNN-based MT training data selection scoring and ranking general-domain sentences; comparisons to continuous/n-gram LMs are thorough but unclear if bilingual selection baselines were evaluated.  
- The argument that CNNs capture important sentence sections is a strength, but the choice over RNN/LSTM needs clearer motivation.  
- No experimental ablation showing which representation (BOW vs SEQ or their combination) is more important or why.  
- Textual description of the CNN (one-hot vs pre-trained embeddings) is clear and detailed; a diagram of layers and input combination would help.  
- Paper is generally well written but citation formatting is inconsistent (unnecessary parentheses; \citet vs \citep).  
- Experimental support is good but the method for choosing number of selected in-domain sentences via a validation set is unclear (what validation data, how hyperparameters were chosen, and sensitivity).  
- Table 2 should compare methods using the same number of selected sentences; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms baselines under equal conditions.  
- Recommend experiments with more in-domain data (beyond the development set) to test robustness.  
- Suggest including example sentences selected by different methods to support claims in Section 5.4.  
- To evaluate abstraction from surface forms, compare against Axelrod et al. (2015) POS-replacement to see if word2vec embeddings add advantage.  
- The sum of source and target classification scores is similar to the Lewis-Moore sum-of-difference-of-cross-entropies approach; add a reference around line 435.  
- Possible extension: learn weights for source/target score combination by extending the CNN to a bilingual/parallel setting.
