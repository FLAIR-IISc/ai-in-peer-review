The paper describes an MT training data selection approach that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models is well done, although it is unclear whether the paper also compared against bilingual data selection methods (e.g., the sum of differences of cross-entropies). 

The initial motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was not immediately apparent, but the paper strengthens its argument by positing that certain sections of a text or sentence are more important than others, a characteristic well-suited to CNNs. However, the paper does not experimentally demonstrate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation – or a combination of both – is more important, nor does it explain why. 

The textual description of the CNN architecture (utilizing one-hot encoding or semi-supervised learning with pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights key aspects. A diagram illustrating the layers and input combinations would, however, greatly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well written, but some parenthetical citations are unnecessary (e.g., line 385; the distinction between \citet and \citep should be considered). 

The experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but I have some concerns regarding the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set. Specifically:

* What validation data is used for this purpose? Furthermore, it is not clear on what data the hyperparameters of the CNN models were chosen, nor how sensitive the models are to changes in these hyperparameters.
* Table 2 would benefit from comparing the scores of different approaches with the *same* number of sentences selected. As Figure 1 shows, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines in this scenario.

Other comments:

* I would be interested in an experiment comparing the technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond just the development set.
* The results or discussion section could include example sentences selected by the different methods to provide concrete support for the claims made in section 5.4.
* Regarding the argument in 5.4 about abstracting away from surface forms, a relevant baseline would be the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced words with POS tags to address LM data sparsity and assessed whether word2vec embeddings provided an additional advantage.
* The use of the sum of source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to the Lewis-Moore language model data selection approach, which uses the sum of differences of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
* Finally, I wonder if weights for the sum of source and target classification scores could be learned by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting.
