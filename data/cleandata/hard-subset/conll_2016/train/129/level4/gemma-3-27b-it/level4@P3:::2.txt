This paper proposes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building on the work of Johnson and Zhang (2015). The key strength of the approach is its robustness even with extremely limited in-domain data, outperforming language model-based data selection methods in such scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection when in-domain resources are scarce. While CNNs have been applied to text classification, their successful application to *data selection* for SMT, particularly with minimal in-domain data, is a valuable finding. The paper also demonstrates that pre-trained word embeddings significantly enhance performance in this low-resource setting, which is a noteworthy observation. A secondary contribution is the experimental validation on multiple language pairs and diverse test domains (social media posts), offering a reasonably comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance with Limited Data:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed semi-supervised CNN approach consistently outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based methods, when only a small amount of in-domain data is available. The reported gains of up to 3.1 BLEU, while dependent on the presence of some in-domain data in the training set (see weaknesses), are substantial and practically significant.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity:** The second experiment, highlighting the effectiveness of the CNN classifier even with only 100 training sentence pairs, is a key strength. This is particularly valuable for emerging domains or rapidly changing topics where acquiring large in-domain corpora is impractical. The results, though initially shown for zh2en, are further supported by the results for en2es and en2zh even though the gains are smaller, establishing a wider applicability.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments cover four language directions and three test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts), providing a reasonably broad evaluation of the proposed method's generalizability. The use of publicly available data and clear comparison to established baselines enhances the credibility of the results.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The focus on adapting SMT systems to social media data is highly relevant. Social media translation presents unique challenges (OOV words, non-standard language), and the proposed method offers a potential solution for improving translation quality in these scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Gains in Specific Cases:** The reported 3.1 BLEU improvement is largely driven by the presence of in-domain data *within* the general training set for the zh2en-sms task. While the method still improves over a system trained *only* on this in-domain data, the magnitude of the improvement is not consistently observed across all tasks and might be misleading as a general claim. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of this dependency.
2. **Limited Analysis of Improvement Sources:** While the paper establishes *that* data selection improves performance, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *why*. What specific types of errors are reduced by the selected data? A qualitative error analysis, or examples of sentences where the adapted system performs better, would strengthen the paper. The mention of OOV rate suggests this could be a focus.
3. **Lack of Detailed Architectural Description:** Section 3.2 lacks a clear explanation of how the one-hot vectors, segment vectors, and word embeddings are combined within the CNN architecture. A visual diagram illustrating the network's structure would significantly improve clarity and understanding. Similarly, the “designated loss function” is not specified.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Preprocessing:** The nature of the data (social media text) introduces challenges like OOV words and non-standard spelling. The paper does not explicitly detail any preprocessing steps taken to address these issues, which are crucial for the performance of any MT system, especially in these domains.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the CNN architecture, including a diagram illustrating how the different vector representations (one-hot, segment embeddings, word embeddings) are combined?
2. What specific loss function was used to train the CNN classifier?
3. Please provide a detailed account of any preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the social media data (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts). Specifically, how was the OOV problem addressed?
4. Can you provide examples of sentences where the adapted system, trained using your data selection method, significantly improves translation quality compared to a system trained on all general-domain data?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Section 5.2: Please include a brief description of how the SMT system weights were tuned.
*   The authors should consider reporting the results for all tasks when training the CNN classifier with only 100 sentence pairs, to better demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
*   It would be insightful to compare the performance to a system trained by interpolating separate in-domain and out-of-domain models, as suggested in the text.
