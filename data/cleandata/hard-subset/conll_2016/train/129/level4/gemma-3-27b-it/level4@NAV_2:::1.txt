The paper describes an MT training data selection approach that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. Comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models is well done, though it is not clear if the paper also compared against bilingual data selection methods (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies). 

The motivation to use a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was initially unclear, but a strength of the paper is its argument that certain sections of a text/sentence are more important than others, and this is effectively captured by a CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally demonstrate which representation—BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both)—is more important and why.

The textual description of the CNN (one-hot or semi-supervised using pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights important aspects. However, a diagram illustrating the layers and how inputs are combined would greatly enhance understanding. 

The paper is generally well written, but some citation parentheses are unnecessary (e.g., line 385; consider consistent use of \citet vs. \citep).

Experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but I have some concerns about the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set:
- What validation data is used here? It is also unclear how hyperparameters for the CNN models are chosen and how sensitive the models are to these choices.
- Table 2 would be more informative if it directly compared the scores of different approaches using the *same* number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 shows, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines under these conditions.

Other comments:
- I would be interested in an experiment comparing the technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond just the development set.
- The results or discussion section could include example sentences selected by the different methods to support the claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the argument about abstracting away from surface forms in 5.4, comparing against Axelrod et al. (2015)—who replaced words with POS tags to address LM data sparsity—could determine whether the word2vec embeddings offer an additional advantage.
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores is similar to the Lewis-Moore language model data selection approach—the sum of difference of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- I wonder if learning weights for the sum of source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting would be possible.
