The paper describes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network classifier, building on the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, crucially, remains robust even with limited in-domain data – a feature not shared by other approaches.

The paper reports improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, the results indicate that gains of this magnitude are only observed when in-domain data is present in the training set; training solely on the in-domain data already yields a +2.8 BLEU improvement. It would be interesting to compare this method to a system that interpolates separate in- and out-of-domain models.

I found the second experiment more compelling, as it demonstrates the CNN classifier's continued effectiveness even with very little in-domain data. However, this experiment was conducted solely on the zh2en task, which *does* include in-domain data in the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It remains unclear whether these results would generalize to the other tasks, where the training data lacks in-domain examples. The en2es and en2zh results already suggest this may be the case, given the small size of their development sets (only a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen the claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks when training is limited to just 100 sentence pairs.

When translating social media text, translators often encounter challenges distinct from those in other domains, most notably a high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rate stemming from non-conventional spelling (at least for Latin scripts). These texts frequently contain special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Were any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied to address these issues?

As data selection cannot directly solve the OOV problem, it would be valuable to understand in more detail *what* specific improvements are achieved through adaptation via data selection. Providing illustrative examples would be particularly helpful.
