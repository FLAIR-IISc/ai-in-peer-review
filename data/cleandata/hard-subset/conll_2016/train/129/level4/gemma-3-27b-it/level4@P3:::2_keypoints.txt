- Primary contribution: Shows CNNs are effective for in-domain data selection for SMT when in-domain resources are scarce, outperforming LM-based selection in low-resource settings.  
- Notable finding: Pre-trained word embeddings substantially improve CNN performance in the low-resource scenario.  
- Evaluation breadth: Experiments cover multiple language pairs and social-media domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook), using public data and strong baselines.  

Strengths:
- Strong performance with limited data: Semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforms baselines, with gains up to 3.1 BLEU in some cases.  
- Robustness to data scarcity: Effective even with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs, useful for emerging or fast-changing domains.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: Results presented for four language directions and three test domains, supporting generalizability.  
- Practical relevance: Directly addresses social-media MT challenges (OOVs, non-standard language) with a practical adaptation method.  

Weaknesses:
- Overstated gains in specific cases: The 3.1 BLEU improvement is driven by presence of in-domain data within the general training set and isn’t consistently observed across tasks.  
- Limited analysis of improvement sources: Paper shows that selection helps but lacks qualitative/error analysis explaining which errors are reduced.  
- Insufficient architectural detail: Section 3.2 omits clear explanation/diagram of how one-hot, segment, and embedding vectors are combined; loss function unspecified.  
- Inadequate preprocessing discussion: Paper does not detail preprocessing or OOV-handling steps for noisy social-media text.  

Questions for authors:
- Please provide a detailed CNN architecture description and a diagram showing how vector types are combined.  
- What specific loss function was used to train the CNN classifier?  
- What preprocessing/filtering steps were applied to social-media data and how was OOV handled?  
- Can you provide example sentences where the adapted system notably improves translation over general-domain training?  

Additional comments:
- Section 5.2 should briefly describe how the SMT system weights were tuned.  
- Report results for all tasks when training the CNN with only 100 sentence pairs to demonstrate generalizability.  
- Consider comparing to interpolation of separate in-domain and out-of-domain models as a baseline.
