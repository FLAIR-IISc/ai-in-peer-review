- Proposes a CNN-based method to score and rank general-domain sentences for MT training data selection, with thorough comparison to continuous and n-gram LMs but unclear comparison to bilingual selection methods (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies).
- Initial rationale for choosing a CNN over RNN/LSTM was weak, though the paper later argues CNNs are good at identifying and prioritizing important text segments.
- Lacks experimental comparison between bag-of-words (BOW), sequential (SEQ), or combined representations to show which is most effective and why.
- Textual description of the CNN architecture and use of one-hot and pre-trained embeddings is clear and detailed.
- Recommends adding a diagram showing layer-wise combination of inputs to improve clarity.
- Paper is generally well-written but has minor citation format inconsistencies (e.g., \citet vs. \citep).
- Experiments support claims, but the method for choosing the number of in-domain sentences (using a separate validation set) is unclear regarding what validation data is used and hyperparameter selection/sensitivity.
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the same number of selected sentences; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms baselines under equal conditions.
- Suggests evaluating the method with larger amounts of in-domain data beyond the development set.
- Recommends including example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate claims in Section 5.4.
- Proposes comparing to Axelrod (2015)-style baseline that replaces words with POS tags to assess abstraction from surface forms versus using word2vec embeddings.
- Notes the sum of source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to Moore–Lewis style selection (sum of difference of cross-entropies) and suggests citing that work and exploring learned weights for source/target scores via a bilingual/parallel CNN extension.
