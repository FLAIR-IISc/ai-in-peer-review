This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) utilizing a convolutional neural network classifier, following the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, crucially, maintains robustness even with limited in-domain training data – a feature not shared by other approaches. 

The paper reports improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, analysis of the results reveals that these gains are largely contingent on the presence of in-domain data in the training set; training solely on in-domain data already yields a substantial improvement of +2.8 BLEU. A comparison against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-of-domain models could provide valuable insights.

The more compelling result, in my view, is the demonstrated effectiveness of the CNN classifier when only a small amount of in-domain data is available. However, this observation is currently based solely on the zh2en task, which *includes* in-domain data in its training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It remains unclear whether this performance would generalize to other tasks where the initial training data lacks in-domain examples. The results for en2es and en2zh suggest this may be the case, given the small size of their development sets (only a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen this claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks when training is limited to just 100 sentence pairs.

Given the application to social media text, which often presents unique challenges – notably a high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rate stemming from non-conventional spelling (at least for Latin scripts) and the frequent use of special characters like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons – it would be useful to know if any specific preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data. 

As data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, further detail regarding the nature of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection would be beneficial, perhaps illustrated with specific examples.
