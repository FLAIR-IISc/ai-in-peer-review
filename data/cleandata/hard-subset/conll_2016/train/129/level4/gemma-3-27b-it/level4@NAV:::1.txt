The paper introduces a method for MT training data selection, leveraging a CNN classifier to score and rank sentences from general-domain corpora. The comparison to existing techniques based on continuous or n-gram language models is thorough; however, it would be valuable to know if the authors also considered bilingual data selection methods, such as utilizing the sum of cross-entropy differences. 

Initially, the rationale for choosing a CNN over recurrent architectures like RNNs or LSTMs wasn’t immediately apparent. However, the paper effectively justifies this choice by highlighting the CNN’s ability to identify and prioritize important textual segments within sentences. Nevertheless, the paper lacks experimental evidence demonstrating whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation – or a combination thereof – is more crucial for performance, and why.

The description of the CNN architecture, including the use of one-hot encoding or semi-supervised pre-trained embeddings, is clear and comprehensive, appropriately emphasizing key considerations. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the layer interactions and input combinations would significantly enhance understanding. 

While generally well-written, the paper contains instances of unnecessary citation formatting variations (\citet vs. \citep), such as on line 385. 

The experimental results generally validate the paper’s claims, though I have some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of in-domain sentences selected (line 443), relying on a separate validation set. Specifically:
- The composition and source of this validation data are not described. Furthermore, the process of hyperparameter selection for the CNN models lacks transparency.  An analysis of model sensitivity to these hyperparameters would be beneficial.
- Table 2 would be more informative if it presented a direct comparison of scores across different approaches, normalized to the same number of selected sentences. However, Figure 1 already suggests the proposed approach outperforms the baselines even under such conditions.

Further considerations:
- An experiment evaluating the technique’s performance with larger volumes of in-domain data, beyond just the development set, would be insightful.
- Including example sentences selected by different methods in the results or discussion section could provide stronger support for the claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the discussion of abstracting away from surface forms (Section 5.4), comparison with the POS-tagging approach of Axelrod (2015) could provide valuable context, particularly in assessing the benefits of word2vec embeddings.
- The approach of summing source and target classification scores bears a strong resemblance to Lewis-Moore language model data selection (sum of cross-entropy differences).  A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- Finally, extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for the combined source and target classification scores merits exploration.
