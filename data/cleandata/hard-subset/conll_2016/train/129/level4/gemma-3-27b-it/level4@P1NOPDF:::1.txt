The paper describes a machine translation data selection approach that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models is well done, although it’s unclear whether the paper also compared against bilingual data selection methods (e.g., the sum of differences in cross-entropies). 

Initially, the motivation for using a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM wasn’t clear, but a strength of the paper is its argument that certain parts of a text or sentence are more important than others, and that this can be effectively captured by a CNN. However, the paper does not experimentally demonstrate whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation – or a combination of both – is more important, or why.

The textual description of the CNN architecture (using one-hot encoding or semi-supervised learning with pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights key aspects. However, a diagram illustrating how the inputs are combined across the layers would significantly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well written, but some parenthetical citations are unnecessary (\citet vs. \citep) – for example, on line 385.

The experiments and evaluation support the paper’s claims, but I have some concerns about the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set. Specifically:

*   What validation data was used? It’s also unclear how the hyperparameters of the CNN models were chosen, and how sensitive the models are to these choices.
*   Table 2 would be more informative if it directly compared the scores of different approaches using the same number of selected sentences. As Figure 1 shows, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines even with this adjustment.

Other comments:

*   I would be interested in an experiment comparing the proposed technique to baselines when more in-domain data is available, beyond just the development set.
*   The results or discussion section could benefit from including example sentences selected by the different methods, to support the claims made in section 5.4.
*   Regarding the argument about abstracting away from surface forms in section 5.4, a relevant baseline comparison could be to the work of Axelrod (2015), who replaced words with POS tags to reduce language model data sparsity, allowing assessment of whether the word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage.
*   Using the sum of source and target classification scores is very similar to the source and target Lewis-Moore language model data selection method, which employs the sum of differences in cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
*   Finally, I wonder if it would be possible to learn weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting.
