The paper describes an MT training data selection approach that scores and ranks general-domain sentences using a CNN classifier. Comparison to prior work using continuous or n-gram based language models is well done, although it’s unclear whether the paper also compared against bilingual data selection methods (e.g., sum of difference of cross-entropies).

The motivation to use a CNN instead of an RNN/LSTM was initially unclear, but a strength of the paper is its argument that certain sections of a text/sentence are more important than others, and this is effectively captured by a CNN. However, the paper doesn't experimentally demonstrate which representation—BOW or SEQ (or a combination of both)—is more important, or why. 

The textual description of the CNN (one-hot or semi-supervised using pre-trained embeddings) is clear, detailed, and highlights the important aspects. However, a diagram illustrating the layers and how inputs are combined would greatly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well-written, although some parenthetical citations are unnecessary (e.g., line 385; consider consistency in using \citet vs. \citep).

Experiments and evaluation support the claims of the paper, but I have some concerns about the method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443) based on a separate validation set:

*   What validation data is used? It’s also unclear on what data the hyperparameters of the CNN models are chosen. How sensitive are the models to these hyperparameters?
*   Table 2 would be more informative if it compared scores of different approaches with the *same* number of sentences selected. As Figure 1 shows, the proposed approach still appears to outperform the baselines in this scenario.

Other comments:

*   I would be interested in an experiment that compares the technique of the paper against baselines when *more* in-domain data is available, not just the development set.
*   The results or discussion section could benefit from example sentences selected by the different methods, supporting the claims made in section 5.4.
*   Regarding the argument about abstracting away from surface forms in 5.4, another relevant baseline to compare against would be the work of Axelrod et al. (2015), who replaced some words with POS tags to reduce LM data sparsity. This would show whether the word2vec embeddings provide an additional advantage over this technique.
*   Using the sum of source and target classification scores is similar to the Lewis-Moore language model data selection approach—the sum of differences of cross-entropies. A reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
*   Finally, I wonder if the CNN model could be extended to a bilingual/parallel setting to learn weights for the sum of both source and target classification scores.
