This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building upon the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The key strength of the approach lies in its robustness – it outperforms language model-based data selection methods by approximately 0.5 BLEU points, and crucially, maintains performance even with extremely limited in-domain training data.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation scenarios for SMT. While the improvement over strong baselines isn't massive overall, the ability to achieve gains with only 100 in-domain sentences is a significant finding, addressing a common challenge in real-world MT applications. The authors correctly identify a gap in existing literature, where previous methods require considerably larger in-domain datasets.

However, the reported 3.1 BLEU improvement is somewhat misleading. Analysis of the results reveals this magnitude of improvement is largely contingent on the presence of in-domain data *within* the general training set – training solely on this in-domain data yields a substantial 2.8 BLEU improvement already.  A comparison against a system interpolating separate in- and out-of-domain models would provide valuable context.

The more compelling result is the demonstrated efficacy of the CNN classifier with minimal in-domain data, detailed in the second experiment. However, this experiment is currently limited to the zh2en task, which benefits from pre-existing in-domain data in the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process.  It’s unclear if these findings generalize to the other tasks (en2es and en2zh), which lack in-domain data in the initial training corpus. Reporting results for all tasks when training with only 100 sentence pairs would strengthen this claim significantly. The existing results for en2es and en2zh suggest the benefit may be less pronounced without initial in-domain data.

Given the focus on adapting to social media text, a critical consideration is the handling of characteristics common to such data, specifically high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rates resulting from non-standard spelling and the presence of special characters (usernames, hashtags, emoticons). The authors should clarify any preprocessing or filtering steps applied to address these issues.  Understanding *how* data selection improves performance, beyond the BLEU score, would be beneficial. Examples illustrating the types of sentences selected and their impact on translation quality, particularly in addressing OOV challenges, would be insightful.

Specific points for clarification:

* **Section 3.2:** The description of combining word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors is unclear. A diagram illustrating the CNN architecture would greatly improve understanding.  Furthermore, specifying the “designated loss function” is necessary.
* **Section 5.2:** The methodology for tuning system weights should be detailed for completeness.
