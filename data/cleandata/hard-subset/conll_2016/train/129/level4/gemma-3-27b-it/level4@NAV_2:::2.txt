The paper describes a method for in-domain data selection for SMT utilizing a convolutional neural network classifier, building upon the framework established in Johnson and Zhang, 2015. The method achieves approximately 0.5 BLEU points higher performance compared to language model based data selection techniques and, crucially, maintains robustness even with limited in-domain data availability. 

The primary contribution of this work is a semi-supervised CNN-based approach to data selection that demonstrably improves translation quality, particularly when in-domain resources are scarce. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that effective data selection can be performed even with extremely limited in-domain data—as few as 100 sentence pairs—a critical capability for low-resource scenarios and rapid adaptation to new topics. Finally, the experimental results on diverse language pairs and domains provide a strong empirical validation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results across four language directions and three test domains, consistently demonstrating improvements over strong baseline systems. The gains of up to 3.1 BLEU are significant, especially considering the limited in-domain data used for adaptation. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Robustness to Limited Data:** A key strength of this work is its ability to achieve substantial improvements even with only 100 sentences of in-domain data. This is a critical advantage over existing data selection methods that typically require larger in-domain corpora. This makes the approach practical for scenarios where acquiring significant amounts of in-domain data is difficult or expensive.
3. **Novel Application of CNNs:** The application of semi-supervised CNNs to the problem of data selection for SMT is novel and well-motivated. The use of pre-trained word embeddings effectively addresses the data sparsity issue, enabling the model to generalize well even with limited in-domain training data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Improvement in Specific Cases:** The claim of a 3.1 BLEU improvement requires closer scrutiny. This magnitude of improvement is largely observed when in-domain data is already present within the general training set, and training solely on this in-domain subset yields a substantial improvement (+2.8 BLEU). This suggests the method benefits from a pre-existing in-domain component rather than solely discovering it.  A comparison with a simple interpolation between in- and out-domain models might provide a more nuanced understanding of the method’s contribution.
2. **Limited Scope of Low-Data Experiment:** While the results demonstrating effectiveness with limited data are promising, the key experiment showcasing this (reducing in-domain data to 100 sentences) is currently only conducted on the zh2en task which *does* contain in-domain data in the initial training set. This may simplify the selection process.  Reporting results for all tasks with only 100 sentences for training would strengthen the claim of generalizability. The existing results for en2es and en2zh hint that the performance might be less pronounced in truly low-resource scenarios.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis & OOV Consideration:** The paper does not address the challenges of translating social media text, such as high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates and the presence of non-conventional language. As data selection alone cannot solve the OOV problem, a discussion of how the approach handles this issue, and potentially an analysis of the types of improvements achieved through adaptation, would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide an illustration of the network architecture described in Section 3.2, clarifying how the word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors are combined?
2. What specific loss function was used for training the CNN classifier, as mentioned in Section 3.2?
3. Could you report the results of the 100-sentence training experiment for all four language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, and en2zh)?
4. Was any specific preprocessing or filtering applied to the social media data (e.g., handling non-conventional spelling, special characters, usernames, hashtags)? If so, could you elaborate on the methods used?
