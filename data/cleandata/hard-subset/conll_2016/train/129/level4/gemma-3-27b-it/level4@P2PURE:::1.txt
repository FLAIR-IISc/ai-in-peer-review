The paper presents a method for machine translation training data selection, scoring and ranking general-domain sentences with a CNN classifier. The comparison to prior work employing continuous or n-gram language models is thorough; however, it remains unclear whether the paper also evaluates against bilingual data selection techniques, such as the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

Initially, the rationale for choosing a CNN over an RNN or LSTM was not evident. However, the paper effectively argues that a CNN is well-suited to identifying and prioritizing important segments within a text, a key strength of the approach. Despite this, the paper lacks an experimental demonstration of whether a bag-of-words (BOW) or sequential (SEQ) representation – or a combination of both – is more effective, and why.

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including both one-hot encoding and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, highlighting crucial design choices. A diagram illustrating the layer-wise combination of inputs would significantly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well-written, although some parenthetical citation formats (\citet vs. \citep) appear unnecessary (e.g., line 385). 

The experiments and evaluation provide support for the paper’s claims. However, I have some concerns regarding the method used to determine the number of in-domain sentences selected (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set:
- What specific validation data is utilized? Furthermore, the criteria for selecting hyperparameters for the CNN models are unclear, as is the model’s sensitivity to these hyperparameters.
- Table 2 would be more informative if it compared the scores of different approaches using the same number of selected sentences. Nevertheless, as shown in Figure 1, the proposed approach appears to outperform the baselines even under these conditions.

Other comments:
- An experiment comparing the proposed technique to baselines with more abundant in-domain data, beyond the development set, would be valuable.
- The results or discussion section could benefit from including example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate the claims in Section 5.4.
- Regarding the discussion of abstracting away from surface forms in 5.4, a relevant baseline would be the work of Axelrod (2015), which explored replacing words with POS tags to address language model data sparsity, allowing for a comparison of the advantage provided by word2vec embeddings.
- The use of the sum of source and target classification scores is conceptually similar to Lewis-Moore language model data selection based on the sum of difference of cross-entropies. Inclusion of a reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
- Finally, I suggest investigating whether weights for the sum of source and target classification scores could be learned by extending the CNN model to a bilingual/parallel setting.
