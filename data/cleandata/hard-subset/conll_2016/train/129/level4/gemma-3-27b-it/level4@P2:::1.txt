This paper presents a novel data selection approach for statistical machine translation, leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to score and rank general-domain sentences for inclusion in training data, particularly when in-domain resources are limited. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of CNNs – specifically, a semi-supervised approach utilizing pre-trained word embeddings – for this task, outperforming strong language model baselines. A second, valuable contribution is the demonstration that effective data selection is possible even with extremely small amounts of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), opening possibilities for fine-grained, topic-specific adaptation. Finally, the paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements over competitive baselines across four language directions and three test domains. The BLEU score gains, particularly on the challenging Chinese-to-English SMS task (up to 3.1 BLEU), are substantial and provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. 
2. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The authors effectively address the challenge of limited in-domain data by incorporating pre-trained word embeddings into a semi-supervised CNN framework. This allows the model to leverage information from large, unlabeled corpora, mitigating the sparsity issues inherent in small in-domain datasets. The experimental results strongly support the benefit of this approach, with the semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforming the purely supervised one-hot CNN.
3. **Demonstration of Low-Resource Effectiveness:** A key strength is the ability to achieve significant gains even with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences). This addresses a critical practical problem – the scarcity of labeled data for emerging or specialized domains – and highlights the potential of the method for real-world applications. The robustness analysis demonstrating consistent performance with varying small in-domain sets further strengthens this argument.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture Choices:** While the textual description of the CNN architecture is clear, the paper lacks a detailed investigation into the impact of key design choices. Specifically, the relative performance of bag-of-words (BOW) versus sequential (SEQ) representations within the CNN is not fully explored. An analysis of which representation is more effective, and under what conditions, would provide valuable insights.
2. **Validation Set Dependency and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The selection of the optimal number of in-domain sentences relies on a separate validation set. The paper does not adequately address the characteristics of this validation set (e.g., its size, source) nor provide a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate how robust the results are to different validation sets and hyperparameter settings. This lack of clarity introduces a potential concern about the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings.
3. **Comparison to Bilingual Data Selection Methods:** The paper primarily focuses on comparisons to monolingual data selection baselines. While a comprehensive comparison isn't always feasible, acknowledging and discussing the results of methods that directly select *bilingual* data (e.g., based on cross-entropy differences) would provide a more complete assessment of the proposed approach's relative strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the validation set used for determining the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences? Specifically, what is its size, source, and how representative is it of the test domain?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., window size, stride, number of neurons in the CNN)? Could you provide a brief ablation study to demonstrate the impact of these parameters on performance?
3. The experimental results show that the semi-supervised CNN performs well even with a very small number of in-domain examples (e.g., 100 sentences). Could you provide some examples of sentences selected by the semi-supervised CNN versus a baseline method (e.g., random selection) to illustrate *why* the CNN is able to identify more relevant sentences?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider referencing Lewis and Moore’s work on summing cross-entropy differences around line 435, as the approach of summing source and target classification scores shares similarities.
*   Exploring an extension of the CNN model to directly handle bilingual data (e.g., by learning weights for source and target scores) could be a promising direction for future research.
*   Comparing the proposed approach to methods that utilize POS tags to abstract away from surface forms (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2015) would provide a valuable benchmark.
*   Including a visual representation of the CNN architecture (e.g., a diagram showing the layers and data flow) would significantly enhance clarity.
