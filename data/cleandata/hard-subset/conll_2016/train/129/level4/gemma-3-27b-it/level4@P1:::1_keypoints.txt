- Proposes MT training data selection that scores/ranks general-domain sentences with a CNN; comparison to continuous/n-gram LMs is solid but unclear if bilingual selection methods were compared (e.g., sum/diff of cross-entropies).
- Motivation for choosing CNN over RNN/LSTM was initially unclear, though the argument that CNNs capture important sentence sections is a strength.
- The paper fails to experimentally determine which input representation (BOW, SEQ, or combination) is most important or why.
- Textual description of the CNN (one-hot vs. pre-trained embeddings) is clear and detailed, but a diagram of the architecture and input combination would improve understanding.
- Writing quality is good overall, but several parenthetical citations are unnecessary and citation style should be made consistent (\citet vs. \citep).
- Experimental claims are supported, but the method for choosing the number of selected in-domain sentences via a validation set is unclear (what validation data, hyperparameter selection, and sensitivity).
- Table 2 should compare approaches using the same number of selected sentences; Figure 1 suggests the proposed method still outperforms under equal-size selection.
- Recommend an experiment showing performance when more in-domain data is available (beyond the dev set).
- Results/discussion should include example sentences selected by different methods to substantiate claims in Section 5.4.
- Suggest comparing against Axelrod et al. (2015) POS-replacement baseline to test whether word2vec embeddings provide additional benefit over reducing LM sparsity.
- Point out that summing source and target classification scores is similar to Lewis-Moore’s sum/difference of cross-entropies and recommend adding that reference (around line 435).
- Propose exploring a bilingual/parallel extension of the CNN to learn weights for combined source and target classification scores.
