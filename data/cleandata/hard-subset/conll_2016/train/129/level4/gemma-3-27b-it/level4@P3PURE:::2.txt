The paper describes a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) using a convolutional neural network classifier, following the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, crucially, demonstrates robustness even with limited in-domain data – a capability not shared by other approaches.

The paper reports improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, a closer examination of the results reveals that gains of this magnitude are only observed when in-domain data is present in the initial training set; training solely on the in-domain data already yields an improvement of +2.8 BLEU. A potentially valuable comparison would be against a system that interpolates separate in- and out-of-domain models.

The more compelling result, in my opinion, stems from the second experiment, which demonstrates the CNN classifier’s continued effectiveness even when only a small amount of in-domain data is available. However, this second experiment is confined to the zh2en task, which *does* include in-domain data within the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It remains unclear whether this result generalizes to other tasks where the training set lacks in-domain data. The results for the en2es and en2zh tasks suggest this might be the case, given the small size of their development sets (only a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen this claim, I recommend reporting results for all tasks when training is limited to just 100 sentence pairs.

Given the application to social media translation, it's important to consider the unique challenges inherent in this domain, most notably the high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rate caused by non-conventional spelling (particularly in Latin scripts). The data also often includes special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Was any specific preprocessing or filtering applied to address these characteristics? Since data selection alone cannot resolve the OOV problem, it would be beneficial to understand in more detail *what* improvements are achieved through adaptation via data selection – perhaps by providing illustrative examples of translated segments.
