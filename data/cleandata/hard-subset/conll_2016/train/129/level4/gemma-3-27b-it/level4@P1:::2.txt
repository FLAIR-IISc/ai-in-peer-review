The paper describes a method for in-domain data selection for SMT using a convolutional neural network classifier, building on the framework presented in Johnson and Zhang (2015). The method outperforms language model-based data selection by approximately 0.5 BLEU points and, crucially, remains robust even with very limited in-domain data.

The paper claims improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, a closer examination of the results reveals that gains of this magnitude are primarily observed when in-domain data is already present within the training set – training solely on this in-domain data yields an improvement of +2.8 BLEU. A comparison to a system that interpolates separate in- and out-of-domain models could provide valuable context.

The most compelling result, in my view, is the demonstration that the CNN classifier remains effective even with minimal in-domain data. However, this second experiment is currently limited to the zh2en task, which benefits from the presence of in-domain data within the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. It would be beneficial to investigate whether these results generalize to the other tasks, where the training data lacks pre-existing in-domain examples. The existing results for the en2es and en2zh tasks suggest a potential trend, given the limited size (a few hundred sentence pairs) of their development sets. To strengthen the claim, reporting results for all tasks when training with only 100 sentence pairs would be highly valuable.

Adapting to social media text often presents unique challenges, most notably a high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate resulting from non-conventional spelling (particularly in Latin scripts) and the frequent occurrence of special character sequences like usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Could the authors detail any specific preprocessing or filtering steps applied to the data to address these issues?

Since data selection alone cannot resolve the OOV problem, it would be insightful to understand the specific improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection in more detail, perhaps by providing illustrative examples.
