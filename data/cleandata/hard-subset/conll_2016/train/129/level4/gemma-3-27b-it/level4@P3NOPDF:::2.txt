This paper presents a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) leveraging a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, building upon the framework established by Johnson and Zhang (2015). The proposed approach demonstrates a consistent improvement of approximately 0.5 BLEU points over language model-based data selection. Notably, it exhibits robustness even when provided with limited in-domain data – a strength not shared by all competing methods.

The paper claims overall improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points. However, a closer examination of the results reveals that these gains are largely contingent on the presence of some in-domain data in the initial training set. Training solely on the in-domain data already yields a substantial increase of +2.8 BLEU, suggesting that the method’s contribution, while positive, is somewhat overstated in the main claim. It would be valuable to compare the proposed method against a system employing interpolation between separate in- and out-domain models, as this would provide a more direct point of comparison.

The most compelling aspect of this work, in my opinion, lies in the second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN classifier even with minimal in-domain data. However, this experiment is currently limited to the zh2en task, which uniquely includes actual in-domain data within the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process. A critical question arises: would this positive result generalize to the other tasks (en2es and en2zh), where the training sets lack in-domain examples? The current results for en2es and en2zh offer preliminary indications that this might be the case, particularly given the small size of their development sets (only a few hundred sentence pairs). To strengthen this claim, I recommend reporting performance results for *all* tasks when trained with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs. 

The application of SMT to social media translation introduces unique challenges, primarily a high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rate driven by non-conventional spelling (particularly in Latin-script languages) and the frequent inclusion of special character sequences such as usernames, hashtags, and emoticons. Understanding the data preprocessing steps applied is therefore important. Specifically, were any specialized preprocessing or filtering techniques employed to address these characteristics?

Given that data selection alone cannot resolve the OOV problem, it would be beneficial to gain a more detailed understanding of *how* the adaptation via data selection manifests in translation quality. Providing specific examples of translations before and after data selection would illustrate the types of improvements achieved and clarify the method's impact.

The following points relate to specific sections of the paper:

**Section 3.2:**
- The combination of the different vectors used in the model (word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors) could be explained more clearly. A visual illustration of the CNN architecture would significantly enhance understanding.
- Please specify the “designated loss function” mentioned in the text.

**Section 5.2:**
- For completeness, it would be helpful to include a brief description of the method used to tune the system weights.
