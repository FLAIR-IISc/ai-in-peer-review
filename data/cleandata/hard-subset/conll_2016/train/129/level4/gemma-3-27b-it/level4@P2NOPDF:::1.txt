This paper presents a novel approach to machine translation training data selection, utilizing a CNN classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. The comparison to existing methods employing continuous or n-gram language models is thorough and insightful. However, the paper would benefit from a comparison against bilingual data selection techniques, such as those based on the sum of cross-entropy differences.

Initially, the rationale for choosing a CNN over more traditional RNN or LSTM architectures was unclear. However, the paper effectively argues for the CNN’s strength in identifying and prioritizing salient sections within sentences – a capability not readily available in sequential models. Despite this justification, the paper lacks experimental evidence demonstrating whether a bag-of-words (BOW), sequential, or combined representation yields the best performance, and crucially, *why*. 

The textual description of the CNN architecture, including the options for one-hot encoding and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained embeddings, is clear and detailed, appropriately highlighting important design choices. A visual diagram illustrating the layer structure and input combination would significantly enhance understanding.

The paper is generally well-written, although some instances of citation style (\citet vs. \citep) appear unnecessary (e.g., line 385) and could be streamlined.

The experimental results and evaluation strongly support the paper's claims. However, I have some concerns regarding the methodology used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set. Further clarification is needed regarding: the specific validation data employed; the criteria used to select hyperparameters for the CNN models; and the sensitivity of the models to variations in these hyperparameters. 

Table 2 would be more informative if it directly compared the scores of different approaches for a *fixed* number of selected sentences. The data presented in Figure 1 already suggests the proposed approach outperforms baselines even under these conditions.

Several additional experiments could further strengthen the work. First, it would be valuable to assess the performance of the technique when a larger quantity of in-domain data is available, beyond the development set. Second, including example sentences selected by each method in the results or discussion section (Section 5.4) would provide concrete evidence supporting the claims made about selection quality. 

Regarding the discussion of abstracting away from surface forms (Section 5.4), comparing against the work of Axelrod (2015), which utilizes POS tags to mitigate data sparsity in language models, would be a pertinent baseline. This would help determine whether the use of word2vec embeddings provides a substantial advantage.

The method of combining source and target classification scores—specifically, their sum—bears a strong resemblance to the Lewis-Moore language model data selection approach, which uses the sum of cross-entropy differences. Acknowledging this connection with a citation around line 435 would be appropriate.

Finally, exploring a potential extension of the CNN model to a bilingual or parallel setting, allowing for learned weights for the combined source and target classification scores, presents a promising avenue for future work.
