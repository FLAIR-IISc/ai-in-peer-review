This paper presents a novel approach to machine translation (MT) training data selection, utilizing a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier to score and rank general-domain sentences. The comparison to prior work based on continuous or n-gram language models is thorough and well-contextualized. However, it remains unclear whether the authors compared their method against bilingual data selection techniques, such as those employing the sum of difference of cross-entropies.

Initially, the motivation for employing a CNN rather than a recurrent neural network (RNN) or LSTM was not immediately apparent. However, the paper effectively argues for the strength of the CNN architecture in its ability to identify and prioritize salient sections within a sentence, acknowledging that not all parts of a text contribute equally to translation quality. A key weakness, however, is the lack of experimental evidence demonstrating whether a Bag-of-Words (BOW), sequential (SEQ), or combined representation is most effective, and crucially, *why* this is the case. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The textual description of the CNN architecture – encompassing both one-hot encoding and semi-supervised learning utilizing pre-trained embeddings – is clear and detailed, effectively highlighting the important design choices. A visual diagram illustrating the CNN layers and input combination process would dramatically improve comprehension and significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility.

Overall, the paper is well-written. However, there are instances of unnecessary parenthetical citations (e.g., line 385), where a simpler \citet style would suffice.

The experimental results and evaluation largely support the paper’s claims. My primary concern lies with the method used to determine the optimal number of in-domain sentences to select (line 443), which relies on a separate validation set. Several clarifying questions arise: What specific data constitutes this validation set? Furthermore, the paper lacks detail regarding the hyperparameters of the CNN models and a sensitivity analysis assessing the impact of these hyperparameters on performance. 

Table 2 would benefit from a direct comparison of scores achieved by different approaches when using the *same* number of selected sentences. Nevertheless, even with this consideration, Figure 1 suggests that the proposed approach consistently outperforms the baselines.

Several additional points could further strengthen the paper:

*   I would be interested in an experiment evaluating the technique’s performance when a larger corpus of in-domain data is available, extending beyond the development set used currently. This would provide a clearer understanding of scalability.
*   The results or discussion section would be greatly enriched by including example sentences selected by each method. Such examples would offer concrete evidence supporting the claims made in section 5.4 regarding the selection process.
*   Regarding the discussion of abstracting away from surface forms (section 5.4), it would be beneficial to compare against the work of Axelrod (2015), who employed POS tagging to address LM data sparsity. This would help determine whether the word2vec embeddings used in this paper provide an incremental advantage over this established technique.
*   The method of summing source and target classification scores bears a strong resemblance to the source and target Lewis-Moore language model data selection technique (sum of difference of cross-entropies). Including a reference to this work around line 435 would be appropriate.
*   Finally, an intriguing extension would be to explore learning weights for the sum of source and target classification scores by adapting the CNN model to a fully bilingual or parallel setting. This could potentially improve performance by allowing the model to directly learn the relative importance of source and target signals.
