This paper introduces a method for in-domain data selection for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT), utilizing a convolutional neural network classifier within a framework similar to Johnson and Zhang, 2015. The proposed method achieves approximately 0.5 BLEU points higher scores than language model-based data selection, and notably maintains robustness even with limited in-domain training data.

The paper reports improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points; however, the presented results indicate these gains are largely dependent on the presence of in-domain data in the initial training set. Training solely on the in-domain data already yields a substantial improvement of +2.8 BLEU. A comparison against a system interpolating separate in- and out-of-domain models could provide valuable context.

The most compelling aspect of this work, in my assessment, is the second experiment, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the CNN classifier even with minimal in-domain data. However, this experiment is currently limited to the zh2en task, which *does* include in-domain data within the training set, potentially simplifying the selection process.  It remains unclear whether these results generalize to other tasks lacking initial in-domain data. The en2es and en2zh results suggest this may be the case, given the small development sets (hundreds of sentence pairs); however, the claim would be strengthened by reporting results for all tasks trained on a uniform, small dataset – for example, 100 sentence pairs.

Considering the application to social media translation, which often presents unique challenges such as high Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) rates stemming from non-standard orthography (particularly in Latin scripts), and the frequent occurrence of special characters (usernames, hashtags, emoticons), it would be helpful to understand if any specific preprocessing or filtering steps were applied to the data. Since data selection alone cannot address the OOV problem, further detail regarding the specific types of improvements achieved through adaptation via data selection would be insightful, potentially with illustrative examples.

Specific points regarding sections of the paper:

Section 3.2:
- Clarification is needed on how the various vectors – word embeddings, segment vectors, and one-hot vectors – are integrated within the model. A diagram illustrating the architecture would significantly enhance understanding.
- Please specify the “designated loss function” used.

Section 5.2:
- For a complete picture, please briefly describe the method used to tune the system weights.
