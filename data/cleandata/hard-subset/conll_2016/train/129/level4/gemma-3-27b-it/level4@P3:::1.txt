This paper presents a novel approach to statistical machine translation (SMT) training data selection, leveraging semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to identify and prioritize in-domain sentences when only limited in-domain data is available. The core idea – using CNNs to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain – is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in low-resource domain adaptation. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across multiple language pairs and domains, consistently outperforming strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation setting. While CNNs have been used for text classification, their specific application to MT data selection with limited in-domain data, and the demonstrated performance gains, are significant. A secondary contribution lies in showing that reasonable performance can be achieved with as few as 100 in-domain sentences for training the data selection model, opening possibilities for fine-grained topic-specific MT systems. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation, covering four language directions and three test domains, strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates significant improvements in BLEU scores (up to 3.1) compared to strong baselines, including established language model-based data selection methods. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are statistically significant, as indicated by the bootstrap resampling tests.
2. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a relevant and challenging problem – domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data. This scenario is increasingly common, particularly with the rise of social media and specialized domains where creating large parallel corpora is impractical.
3. **Novel Application of CNNs:** The use of CNNs for data selection is a novel approach, effectively leveraging the ability of CNNs to identify important local features within sentences. The justification for using CNNs over recurrent models (focus on important sections of text) is a reasonable and insightful argument.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, conducted across diverse language pairs and test domains, which enhances the generalizability of the findings. The inclusion of a comparison against various baseline data selection techniques strengthens the assessment of the proposed method.
5. **Demonstrated Scalability to Low Resource Settings**: The experiment showcasing consistent improvements even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences, validates the applicability of this technique in truly low-resource scenarios, which is a key advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Ablation Study on CNN Architecture:** While the paper describes the CNN architecture, it lacks a detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different components (e.g., one-hot vs. embeddings, BOW vs. sequence representations, pooling methods). Understanding which architectural choices are most crucial would be valuable. While the authors mention the choices made, a more systematic exploration is missing.
2. **Validation Set Dependence:** The method for determining the number of selected in-domain sentences relies on a separate validation set, which introduces a potential source of bias and complexity. The paper lacks sufficient detail about this validation set (size, composition) and a sensitivity analysis demonstrating how the results vary with different validation set configurations.
3. **Limited Comparison to Bilingual Data Selection:** The paper acknowledges the absence of comparison to bilingual data selection methods, such as those based on the difference of cross-entropies. Including such a baseline would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art and strengthen the claims of superiority.
4. **Missing Qualitative Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. Including a qualitative analysis – presenting examples of sentences selected by different methods and explaining why they were chosen – would provide valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed approach and support the arguments made in section 5.4.
5. **Unclear Parameter Tuning:** The process for tuning the CNN hyperparameters is vaguely described. More details on the specific search space, optimization strategy, and computational cost would improve reproducibility and provide a better understanding of the model's sensitivity to these parameters.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the validation set used for determining the optimal number of selected in-domain sentences? Specifically, what is its size, composition, and how was it created?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the CNN model? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing the impact of different hyperparameters on performance?
3.  Why was a BOW representation chosen and how does it compare against other representation options, such as sequence embeddings?
4.  Could you provide examples of sentences selected by the proposed method and the baseline methods, illustrating the differences in their selection criteria?
5. How does the performance of this method change when a larger amount of in-domain data is available (e.g. 1000, 5000 sentences), compared to the baseline methods?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider referencing Axelrod et al. (2015) regarding POS tagging as a method for reducing data sparsity, as suggested in the original review.
*   Clarify the connection between the proposed method (sum of source & target scores) and Lewis-Moore data selection (sum of difference of cross-entropies) with a reference to the relevant work.
*   A visual representation of the CNN architecture would significantly improve clarity.
*   Ensure consistent citation style (e.g., use \citep or \citet consistently throughout the paper).
