## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:** This paper tackles the problem of low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors identify the inherent noise in projected data and propose a novel neural network model – a BiLSTM – that explicitly learns a “noise transformation” to better utilize this noisy supervision alongside a small amount of gold-standard data. This transformation is learned via a matrix *A* which models the relationships between projected and gold tags. The paper demonstrates improved performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages compared to existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The core contribution is the explicit modeling of noise inherent in cross-lingual projection as a learnable transformation. This is a conceptually sound approach that moves beyond simply filtering or weighting projected data.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both simulated low-resource scenarios and real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The consistent improvements demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Practical Application & Strong Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging real-world languages of Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, demonstrating practical impact.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Soundness of Approach:** The explicit modeling of projection noise is a novel and conceptually strong approach. It acknowledges that projection isn’t a perfect process and directly addresses the issue within the model’s architecture.  The use of the matrix *A* provides a flexible and interpretable way to learn these noise patterns.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper conducts a thorough evaluation. The use of both simulated and real-world low-resource languages provides a robust assessment of the model’s performance. The inclusion of several baselines (including a strong recent method by Duong et al.) strengthens the conclusions.
3. **Clear Presentation & Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The introduction effectively lays out the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the potential benefits of leveraging cross-lingual projection. The inclusion of Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the model architecture.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:**  The reported accuracy improvements, particularly on the real low-resource languages, are substantial and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method.  The 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda are impressive given the difficulty of these languages.
5. **Analysis of Learned Noise Matrix:** The visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) are insightful.  They provide some interpretability into what the model is learning about the projection noise and support the claim that the model is capturing meaningful relationships between tags.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Matrix:** While the visualization of matrix *A* is helpful, a more in-depth analysis would be beneficial. For instance, analyzing the *types* of noise learned (e.g., are specific tag misalignments common?) could provide valuable insights into the limitations of word alignment and the characteristics of the projection process. It isn't entirely clear *why* the model learns certain transformations.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't extensively discuss hyperparameter tuning or sensitivity analysis. The performance may be highly dependent on parameters such as the dimensionality of the LSTM layers or the learning rate. A brief discussion of these factors would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
3. **Complexity Increase:** The added noise layer introduces additional parameters. While the results justify this complexity, the paper could briefly address the trade-off between model complexity and performance gains.
4. **Dependence on Word Alignment Quality:**  The method still fundamentally relies on the quality of the word alignments.  The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion of the impact of different alignment tools or strategies could be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix *A*? Specifically, are there any recurring patterns in the learned misalignments between tags that could provide insights into the weaknesses of the word alignment process?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM layer size, learning rate)?  Did you perform any systematic hyperparameter tuning?
3. What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your model compared to the baseline models?
4. Have you experimented with different initialization strategies for the matrix *A*?  (e.g., initializing it as an identity matrix).  Did this significantly impact the training process or final performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and presents a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projection is a clever and effective approach.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance.
