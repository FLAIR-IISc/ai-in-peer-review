This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from a rich-resource language (English). The authors propose a BiLSTM model augmented with a “noise layer” designed to learn the transformations between projected and native tag distributions. The core idea – explicitly modeling projection noise – is a reasonable approach to a known problem in cross-lingual transfer. The experimental evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrates improvements over several baselines, including a strong result on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of an explicit noise modeling approach within a neural network architecture for cross-lingual POS tagging. While the idea of accounting for projection noise isn’t entirely novel (heuristics have been used previously), integrating it directly into the learning process via a trainable transformation matrix is a worthwhile addition. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation across a reasonable number of languages, providing fairly convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness, particularly in the real-world low-resource scenarios. The results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly noteworthy, as they exceed prior state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of naive cross-lingual projection. The motivation for addressing projection noise is well-established in the literature.
2. **Methodological Rigor:** The proposed model, while not groundbreaking in its core architecture (BiLSTM), is well-defined and the addition of the noise layer is logically presented. The two-stage training process (pre-training then joint training) is a sensible approach to stabilize learning.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains observed on both simulated and, crucially, *real* low-resource languages are significant. The comparison against Duong et al. (2014a), a widely cited baseline, is a strong point. The fact that the method outperforms on both Kinyarwanda and Malagasy strengthens the claim of generalizability.
4. **Analysis of Noise Matrix:** The visualization of the learned transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) offers valuable insight into the types of noise the model is learning to correct. The observed patterns (strong diagonals, suppression of certain tag misalignments) are intuitive and support the core hypothesis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Architecture:** The use of a BiLSTM is fairly standard in sequence tagging. The primary novelty resides in the noise layer, and while that's a valuable addition, it feels incremental rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper would benefit from more explicitly positioning itself relative to other recent work in neural machine translation or domain adaptation that also addresses noise or transfer learning.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While the paper compares against Duong et al. (2014a), it lacks comparison with more recent advances in cross-lingual transfer learning, such as those utilizing pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., multilingual BERT). These models often achieve state-of-the-art results in cross-lingual tasks and should be included as strong baselines.  A comparison would demonstrate whether the gains achieved by the noise layer are competitive with the benefits of leveraging large pre-trained representations.
3. **Data Details & Parallel Corpus Quality:** The details on the parallel corpora, particularly for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, are somewhat sparse. The authors state the data was web-sourced, but do not provide specifics on the quality control measures applied. The quality of word alignments is critical for projection, and more information on alignment accuracy would be beneficial.  What percentage of words are aligned? How were NULL alignments handled beyond the stated frequency distribution?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters. Were different learning rates, hidden layer sizes, or regularization techniques explored?  Providing some insight into the hyperparameter tuning process would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the results compare against recent methods leveraging multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., multilingual BERT) as a baseline?
2.  Can you provide more details on the quality and characteristics of the parallel corpora used for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, including alignment accuracy and the methods used to clean/filter the data?
3.  Did you perform a hyperparameter search? If so, can you provide details on the range of hyperparameters explored and their impact on performance?
4.  The noise transformation matrix (A) seems particularly important. Have you investigated the stability of the learned matrix across different random initializations? Is the learned matrix consistent across languages with similar linguistic characteristics?
