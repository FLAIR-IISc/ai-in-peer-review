This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from a rich-resource language (English). The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the noise inherent in projected tags through a "noise layer," learning a transformation between projected and ground truth tags. The core idea of learning to trust projected data, rather than simply treating it as uniformly noisy, is a significant and promising contribution. The experimental results on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming existing methods, including a strong baseline from Duong et al. (2014a). 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the explicit modeling of projection noise. Existing approaches often treat projected data as simply noisy, applying heuristics or constraints. By learning a transformation matrix (A) to map projected tags to ground truth tags, the authors effectively capture the systematic biases and errors in the projection process. This is a crucial step toward making better use of cross-lingual resources. The visualization of the learned ‘A’ matrices (Figures 3 & 4) is particularly insightful, demonstrating the model's ability to identify and mitigate common projection errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The authors present comprehensive experimental results across eight simulated languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The consistent improvement over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  Achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda is a significant accomplishment.
3. **Well-Defined Problem and Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and motivates the use of cross-lingual projection. The limitations of existing approaches are well-explained, and the proposed solution directly addresses these limitations. The authors successfully demonstrate that parallel data, while noisy, can be a valuable resource for low-resource languages when properly leveraged.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation setup is well-defined, utilizing standard datasets and evaluation metrics. The comparison to relevant baselines is fair and comprehensive. The inclusion of both simulated and real-world experiments strengthens the validity of the findings. The ablative analysis implied by comparing different BiLSTM configurations (Annotated, Projected, Ann+Proj) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of Training:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) adds complexity. While justified by the authors, a more streamlined end-to-end training procedure would be desirable. The reasons for the instability of directly training the whole network are not fully explored.  Further investigation into alternative initialization strategies or regularization techniques could potentially simplify the training process.
2. **Dependence on Word Alignment Quality:** While the noise layer mitigates the impact of alignment errors, the overall performance is still fundamentally limited by the quality of the word alignments. The authors briefly mention this limitation, but a more detailed analysis of the correlation between alignment accuracy and tagging performance would be beneficial. Investigating more robust alignment techniques could further improve the results.
3. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Transformation Matrix:** While the visualizations of the 'A' matrix are insightful, a more quantitative analysis of its properties would be helpful. For example, analyzing the distribution of values in the matrix or identifying specific patterns of confusion could provide deeper insights into the types of errors that the model is learning to correct.  Is there a correlation between the types of projection errors (e.g. determiner to noun) and linguistic typological differences between the languages?
4. **Tagset universality:** While using the universal tagset is sensible, it implicitly assumes a degree of tagset comparability between the languages. A discussion of the potential challenges arising from significant tagset divergence (and how the method addresses it beyond the A matrix) would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a linear transformation (matrix A) to model the noise? Have you explored other, potentially more complex, transformations (e.g., non-linear layers)?
2.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of word alignment toolkit and parameters? Did you experiment with different alignment models?
3.  Can you provide more insights into the relationship between the structure of the learned noise transformation matrix (A) and the linguistic characteristics of the languages involved? Are there specific patterns in A that correlate with typological features?
4.  What is the computational overhead of the noise layer during inference? Does it significantly impact the speed of tagging?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   The code availability would further enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of using English as the source language for projection, especially for languages with significant syntactic divergence.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the questions raised above and consider incorporating the suggested improvements to further strengthen the paper.
