This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to explicitly model the “noise” introduced during projection as a transformation between tagsets, learning to trust projected tags selectively. The authors claim three key contributions: 1) a description of projection noise modeled with an additional layer; 2) integration of this model into a deep neural network for joint training; and 3) strong empirical results on simulated and real low-resource languages.

While the idea of explicitly modeling projection noise is reasonable, and the experimental results appear promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, ultimately leading me to recommend rejection. The presentation is frequently imprecise, and a disturbing number of details are glossed over or presented ambiguously.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance to Low-Resource NLP:** The problem addressed – POS tagging in low-resource languages – is undeniably important. Cross-lingual transfer, specifically via projection, is a common and often crucial technique in this setting.
2. **Intuitive Approach:** The notion of learning a noise transformation is intuitively appealing. It directly addresses a well-known problem with cross-lingual projection: the inherent inaccuracies in word alignments and tag mappings.
3. **Empirical Performance:** The reported results, particularly on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, are competitive and suggest the approach *can* be effective. Achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy is a notable result.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Ablation:** While the paper compares against several baselines, the ablation studies are weak. Specifically, the impact of the “noise layer” (Matrix A) is not adequately demonstrated. Simply showing the matrix in Figure 3 is insufficient. The authors need to demonstrate *how* the learned transformation improves performance relative to a model *without* this layer, providing more quantitative results. The discussion of the learned matrices is also superficial; simply stating "strong diagonal" isn’t insightful. What specific tag confusions are being modeled, and are these linguistically plausible?
2. **Ambiguous Training Procedure:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, then joint training) is described but lacks sufficient detail. What specific hyperparameters are used in each stage? How is the learning rate scheduled? Why is this two-stage approach necessary? The authors state direct training is "impractical" without justification.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Data Preprocessing:** The description of the parallel corpora is lacking. What is the source of the web-sourced Malagasy data? What preprocessing steps are applied to both the English and low-resource sides of the corpora? Is sentence alignment performed, and if so, using what tool? These details are critical for reproducibility.
4. **Formatting and Style Issues:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, inconsistent spacing) and stylistic errors (e.g., awkward phrasing, redundant sentences). While individually minor, these issues collectively detract from the paper’s professionalism and readability. The citation style is also inconsistent.
5. **Overstated Contributions:** The claim of being “the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations” is debatable. Previous work has certainly attempted to mitigate projection noise through heuristics and filtering. The novelty lies in *how* the noise is modeled, not the mere act of acknowledging its existence.
6. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are presented, there is a lack of error analysis. What types of errors does the model still make? Are these errors systematic or random? Are they related to specific linguistic phenomena in the low-resource languages?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed ablation study demonstrating the impact of the noise layer (Matrix A) on performance, comparing models with and without it, and reporting quantitative results.
2.  Provide a complete description of the training procedure, including hyperparameters, learning rate schedules, and justifications for the two-stage training approach.
3.  Please detail the preprocessing steps applied to both the English and low-resource sides of the parallel corpora, including sentence alignment details.
4.  Can you provide examples of specific tag confusions learned by the noise layer (Matrix A), and explain their linguistic plausibility?
5.  Provide an error analysis of the model's predictions on the test sets, identifying common error types and potential areas for improvement.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is incomplete and lacks proper formatting.
*   Figure 1 is unclear and doesn't effectively illustrate the challenges of POS projection.
*   The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading and editing pass.



In summary, while the core idea of this paper is promising, the numerous minor flaws and lack of detail prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper needs significant revision to address these issues and demonstrate the true value of the proposed approach.
