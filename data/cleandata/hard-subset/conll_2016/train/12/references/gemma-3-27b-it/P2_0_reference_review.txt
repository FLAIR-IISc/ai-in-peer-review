## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a rich-resource language (English). The core idea is to move beyond simply using projected tags as noisy labels and instead *learn* how much to trust those projected tags via a neural network architecture incorporating a "noise transformation" layer. This layer aims to model the systematic errors introduced during the projection process. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Modeling of Projection Noise:** The central contribution is the introduction of the "noise layer" (matrix A) within a BiLSTM framework.  This allows the model to learn a transformation that maps projected tags to more accurate low-resource tags, effectively quantifying and mitigating the inherent noise in cross-lingual projection. This is a nuanced approach that moves beyond simple filtering or heuristic adjustments.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on both simulated and real low-resource languages.  The consistent improvement over strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation & Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough experimental setup, including comparisons to several baselines and insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, offering a better understanding of the types of errors being corrected.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of the Approach:** Explicitly learning the noise characteristics of projection is a clever and well-motivated idea. It acknowledges the limitations of existing methods which tend to treat projected data as a fixed, albeit noisy, source of supervision. The use of a learnable transformation matrix is an elegant way to capture these systematic errors.
2. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The motivation for tackling projection noise is clearly laid out, and the authors effectively position their work within the existing literature.
3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results are convincing. The consistent performance gains across both simulated and real languages highlight the generalizability of the approach. The ablation studies comparing different data combinations (annotated only, projected only, combined) effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed noise layer.
4. **Insightful Analysis of the Noise Matrix:** The visualization and discussion of the learned noise transformation matrix A provides valuable insights into the types of errors being corrected by the model.  This helps to understand *why* the approach works and adds to the paper’s overall contribution.
5. **Relevance to Low-Resource NLP:** Addressing the challenges of low-resource languages is a critical area of research.  This paper provides a practical and effective technique for leveraging readily available parallel data in such scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of Training Procedure:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, then joint training) adds complexity. The justification for this approach is reasonable (preventing divergence due to noisy projections), but it raises the question of whether a simpler, end-to-end training scheme could be viable with appropriate regularization or initialization techniques. This should be addressed in the author response.
2. **Limited Analysis of Alignment Quality:** While the authors acknowledge the impact of word alignment errors, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of alignment quality for the different language pairs. It would be beneficial to investigate whether performance gains correlate with alignment accuracy.  Is the benefit of the noise layer greater when alignment quality is lower?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of the approach to various hyperparameters (e.g., the size of the noise matrix A, learning rate). A more comprehensive hyperparameter analysis would strengthen the robustness of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with end-to-end training (i.e., training the entire model, including the noise layer, from random initialization)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
2.  Did you analyze the quality of word alignments (e.g., using precision/recall metrics) for the different language pairs?  Does the performance of your model correlate with alignment quality?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the size of the noise transformation matrix *A*?  Did you explore different dimensions for *A* and observe any trends?
4. Can you elaborate on how the choice of Universal POS Tagset might have influenced the results? Would the approach work similarly well with tagsets more specific to the low-resource languages being investigated?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The code used for the experiments would be a valuable addition to the publication.
*   It would be interesting to see this approach applied to other NLP tasks beyond POS tagging, such as NER or dependency parsing.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual projections for low-resource POS tagging. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.  I recommend acceptance.
