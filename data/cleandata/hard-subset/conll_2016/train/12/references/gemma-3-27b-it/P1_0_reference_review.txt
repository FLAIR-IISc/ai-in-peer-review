## Peer Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both limited gold-standard annotations and noisy data obtained via cross-lingual projection from English. The core idea is to learn a "noise transformation" layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected (and potentially inaccurate) tags and the true underlying POS tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors demonstrate improved performance on simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), outperforming several baselines, including a strong prior work by Duong et al. (2014a).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction of the noise transformation layer.  Rather than treating projected tags as simply incorrect labels or relying on heuristics to filter them, the model *learns* the systematic errors introduced by projection, effectively adapting to the specific characteristics of the projection process. This is a conceptually elegant and potentially impactful approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Low-Resource Languages:**  The results on both the simulated and real-world low-resource languages are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Integrated Deep Learning Framework:** The integration of the noise layer within a BiLSTM architecture is well-motivated and allows for end-to-end training, enabling the model to learn complex interactions between the input text, projected tags, and gold-standard annotations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The explicit modeling of projection noise is a novel and insightful approach to a long-standing problem in cross-lingual NLP. It moves beyond simply attempting to “clean” the projected data and instead embraces the noise as a signal to be learned.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Methodology:**  The paper provides a clear explanation of the motivation for the work and the details of the proposed model. The rationale behind each component, including the noise layer and the BiLSTM architecture, is well-articulated. The inclusion of Figure 2 is particularly helpful in understanding the model's structure.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on a diverse set of languages (8 simulated + 2 real-world) and compare it against a range of relevant baselines, including a strong, recent state-of-the-art method.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:** The reported performance improvements, particularly on the real-world languages, are significant and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. The analyses presented in Table 1 and Table 2, along with the illustration of the noise transformation matrix in Figure 3 and Figure 4, provide further support for the model’s effectiveness.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Transformation Matrix:** While Figures 3 and 4 provide a visual representation of the learned noise transformation matrices, a more detailed analysis would be beneficial. For example, it would be insightful to quantify the amount of noise learned for each language or to identify specific patterns in the noise transformation that correlate with linguistic differences between the languages.  The authors mention patterns but don’t quantify them.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The performance of the method relies heavily on the quality of the word alignments used for projection. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t extensively investigate the impact of different alignment methods or error rates on the results.  A brief ablation study exploring this sensitivity would strengthen the paper.
3. **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the implementation details, a discussion of the computational cost of training and inference, particularly compared to the baselines, would be valuable. The increased complexity of the model (due to the noise layer) may impose a significant computational burden.
4. **Tagset Mismatch Discussion:** The final sentence of Section 3.2 mentions that the approach supports mismatching tagsets. However, this aspect isn’t explored in the experiments or results.  Adding a brief discussion of the potential benefits and challenges of applying the method to languages with significantly different tagsets would enhance the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more quantitative analysis of the noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), identifying specific patterns or characteristics that correlate with language-specific phenomena or differences in projection quality?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the word alignments? Have you considered evaluating the method with different alignment tools or with artificially corrupted alignments?
3. What is the computational cost of training and inference with your model compared to the baseline methods? 
4.  Could you elaborate on the potential benefits and challenges of applying your approach to languages with very different tagsets and provide some insight into how the `A` matrix would be interpreted in such cases?



**Additional Comments:**

* The referencing is generally good, but some references appear truncated (e.g., “Ammar et al.,? 2016, Ammar et al.,”). Please double-check the bibliography.
* Minor: In Section 4.1.1, the URL for the Malagasy parallel data should be clearly formatted as a hyperlink.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to low-resource POS tagging. The empirical results are compelling, and the methodology is clearly presented. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.  I recommend acceptance.
