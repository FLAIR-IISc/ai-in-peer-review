This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) incorporating a “noise layer” to explicitly model the discrepancies between projected tags and the true underlying POS tags. The core idea – learning a transformation to account for projection noise – is well-motivated and potentially valuable. The experimental evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrates promising results, exceeding state-of-the-art performance on Kinyarwanda and Malagasy.

**Contributions:**

1. **Noise Modeling via Transformation Matrix:** The most significant contribution is the introduction of a noise transformation layer (matrix A) that explicitly models the mapping between projected and true POS tags. This allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data, which is crucial given the inherent noisiness of cross-lingual projection.
2. **BiLSTM Architecture with Integrated Noise Handling:**  The authors effectively integrate the noise modeling component into a BiLSTM architecture, enabling end-to-end training and joint optimization of the tagger and the noise transformation. This design facilitates learning better representations by considering both the contextual information and the uncertainty associated with projected labels.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The paper provides a thorough evaluation on both simulated (8 languages) and real-world (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) low-resource scenarios. The comparison against existing methods demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, establishing a new baseline for these languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise using the transformation matrix A is a novel and insightful contribution. Prior work typically relies on heuristics or implicit regularization to address noise, while this approach directly learns the noise characteristics.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent improvements over strong baselines across both simulated and real-world datasets provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The gains on Kinyarwanda and Malagasy are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The framework is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of the noise transformation matrix visualizations (Figures 3 & 4) is helpful for understanding the learned relationships.
4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of related work, positioning their contribution within the broader context of low-resource NLP and cross-lingual transfer learning.
5. **Practical Significance:** The method addresses a crucial issue in low-resource NLP – effectively utilizing readily available parallel data despite its inherent noisiness. The approach has the potential to significantly improve POS tagging performance in a wide range of low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of the A Matrix Interpretation:** While the learned A matrices provide insight into the noise characteristics, fully interpreting and understanding the learned transformations can be challenging.  The authors could benefit from a more detailed analysis of specific entries in the matrix and their corresponding linguistic implications.
2. **Limited Analysis of Alignment Quality:** The paper acknowledges that word alignment errors contribute to projection noise, but there's limited discussion of how alignment quality impacts the performance of the model. Investigating the correlation between alignment accuracy and tagging performance could strengthen the analysis.
3. **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The two-stage training process (pre-training followed by joint training) might be sensitive to initialization.  The authors should discuss the robustness of their approach to different initialization schemes or consider alternative training strategies.
4. **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly mentioned, the added complexity of the noise layer may increase the computational cost of training and inference compared to simpler BiLSTM models. A discussion of the computational overhead would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed linguistic analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix A, focusing on specific examples of how the model learns to correct common projection errors?
2.  Have you investigated the correlation between the quality of word alignments (e.g., using precision/recall metrics) and the performance of your model?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the initialization of the noise transformation matrix A? Did you explore different initialization strategies?
4. What is the computational cost of the additional noise layer and how does it affect training/inference time relative to a standard BiLSTM model?
