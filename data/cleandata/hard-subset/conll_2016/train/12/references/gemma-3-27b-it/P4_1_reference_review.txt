This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold-standard annotated data and noisy data projected from a high-resource language. The key idea is to learn a "noise transformation" that maps projected tags to more accurate representations, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The model employs a BiLSTM architecture with an additional layer to model the noise. Experiments on simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The most significant contribution is the explicit modeling of the noise inherent in cross-lingual tag projection. By introducing a learnable transformation matrix, the model effectively de-noises the projected data and learns a mapping between potentially misaligned or incorrect projected tags and the true underlying POS tags. This is a novel approach that directly addresses a known limitation of cross-lingual transfer learning.
2. **Joint Training Framework:** The paper demonstrates a successful framework for jointly training a neural network on both gold-standard and noisy projected data. This allows the model to leverage the benefits of both resources, mitigating the limitations of relying solely on sparse gold data or unreliable projections.
3. **Empirical Validation on Low-Resource Languages:** The authors provide strong empirical evidence supporting their approach through experiments on both simulated and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods, solidifying the effectiveness of their proposed model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core Problem:** Cross-lingual projection is a common technique for low-resource NLP, but its reliance on potentially noisy word alignments is a significant drawback. This work directly tackles this issue with a principled and effective solution, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is well-motivated and clearly explained. The use of a BiLSTM architecture is appropriate for sequence tagging, and the introduction of the noise transformation layer is a clever and intuitive way to address the challenges of noisy projection data.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and include both simulated and real-world datasets. The comparison to strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art methods, provides compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The results are presented clearly and concisely, with tables and figures that effectively illustrate the performance gains achieved by the proposed model. The analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices provides valuable insights into the types of errors that the model is correcting.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention implementation details (clab/cnn) that could help with reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Transformation:** While the paper shows the learned noise transformation matrices, a more in-depth analysis of *what* the model is learning would be beneficial. For example, could the learned transformations be interpreted in terms of linguistic differences between the source and target languages? A more detailed discussion of error patterns and how the model corrects them could strengthen the paper.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** Although the paper addresses the noise in projections, the performance is still dependent on the quality of the word alignments used to create the projected data. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the potential impact of different alignment tools or techniques on the overall performance of the model.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks details on hyperparameter tuning. The optimal settings for the learning rate, embedding dimension, and hidden layer size may vary depending on the language and dataset. A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process would add to the robustness of the findings.
4. **Scalability:** While the experiments demonstrate strong performance on a limited number of languages, the scalability of the proposed approach to a larger number of low-resource languages is not addressed. The computational cost of training the model and the potential need for language-specific adjustments could be limiting factors.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices? Specifically, can you identify any consistent patterns or linguistic insights that emerge from these matrices?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or techniques?
3.  What hyperparameter tuning strategies did you employ? Were the same hyperparameters used for all languages, or were they adjusted based on language-specific characteristics?
4.  Do you anticipate any challenges in scaling this approach to a larger number of low-resource languages? What strategies might be employed to address these challenges?
