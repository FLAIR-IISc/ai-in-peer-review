This paper investigates cross-lingual POS tagging for low-resource languages, specifically focusing on mitigating the noise inherent in tag projection from a high-resource language. The authors propose a BiLSTM model incorporating a "noise layer" to learn the transformation between projected and actual tags. The key contribution, as presented, lies in explicitly modeling projection noise within a deep learning framework to improve the utilization of distant supervision. A secondary contribution is the evaluation on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating competitive results. A final, less substantial, contribution is the analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem:** The paper addresses a crucial problem in NLP – low-resource language processing – and specifically tackles the well-known challenge of noisy data from cross-lingual projection. This is a valuable research direction with practical implications.
2. **Clear Motivation and Framing:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem, existing approaches, and the limitations of those approaches. The motivation for explicitly modeling noise is well-defined and logically presented.
3. **Empirically Sound:** The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough. Testing on eight simulated languages, alongside two real-world languages, provides a good degree of confidence in the generalizability of the findings.  The comparison to established baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), provides a solid benchmark.
4. **Detailed Model Description:** The model architecture and training procedure are described with sufficient detail, allowing for replication. The inclusion of the equations for the noise transformation is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the idea of modeling projection noise is sensible, the proposed “noise layer” – a linear transformation – feels somewhat simplistic given the complexity of the underlying phenomena. The claim of *explicitly* modeling the noise is overstated. A linear transformation captures a basic correlation but doesn’t truly *model* the multifaceted sources of projection errors (word alignment errors, syntactic divergence, translation issues). This is a refinement of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Noise Matrix:** The paper presents the noise transformation matrix (A) but lacks in-depth analysis. While Figure 3 offers a visual representation, there's no quantitative analysis of the learned confusions. A more detailed examination – e.g., identifying the most frequent mis-projections, analyzing patterns based on POS tag categories – could provide valuable insights and justify the model's design choices. The discussion of what the matrix *should* look like (∝ I) is weak justification; empirical evidence supporting this expectation is missing.
3. **Reporting Inconsistencies & Minor Technical Issues:** Table 1 and the accompanying text mention “BiLSTM Ann+Proj”, however the experimental setup section does not detail the implementation of combining projected and annotated data other than simply using the union of the two. This could also be clarified. Also, the notation in equation (2) is confusing. `ot,i` is used to denote the probability of tag `i` from the supervised model, however the softmax is applied to `∑ i ai,jot,i`. This indicates the input to the softmax is a weighted sum of probabilities, not probabilities themselves, so the equation is technically incorrect.
4. **Data Handling Details:** The sentence "For tokens that are aligned to many English words or none at all (NULL), we assign a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." is concerning. Using the overall English tag distribution for NULL-aligned tokens feels like a very crude approach that introduces significant bias. The authors should justify this choice and consider alternative strategies (e.g., using the tag distribution within the aligned sentence).
5. **Formatting Issues:** Numerous minor formatting issues distract from the reading experience (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, missing punctuation, awkward phrasing). While individually minor, their accumulation suggests a lack of careful attention to detail.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the process for combining annotated and projected data during training (beyond simply using the union of the datasets)?
2.  What is the rationale behind using the overall English tag distribution for NULL-aligned tokens? Have you explored alternative approaches, and if so, what were the results?
3. Can you provide a more thorough quantitative analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (A), identifying the most frequent and significant mis-projections?
4. Could you clarify the mathematical correctness of equation (2), and provide a more detailed explanation of the role of the "noise layer" in the training objective?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors mention the model can be used for mismatching tagsets. An example would be extremely helpful to illustrate how the rectangular matrix A is utilized in this scenario.
*   The phrase “mild improvements” in the description of the BiLSTM Ann+Proj results feels like an understatement. The gains are modest, but not negligible.
*   The text referring to Figure 2 (“… showing on the left the gold supervised model and on the right the distant supervised components.”) is somewhat unclear. A more precise description would be beneficial.



Despite addressing a relevant problem and presenting a reasonably well-executed empirical evaluation, the incremental novelty of the proposed approach and the numerous, albeit minor, flaws in presentation and technical details lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the work is not without merit, the lack of a substantial contribution and the noticeable shortcomings raise concerns about its overall quality and impact.
