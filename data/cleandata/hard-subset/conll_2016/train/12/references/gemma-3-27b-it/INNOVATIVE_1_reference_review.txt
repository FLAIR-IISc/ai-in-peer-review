This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging by cleverly leveraging cross-lingual projection. The core idea – explicitly modeling the noise inherent in projected tags via a learnable transformation – is genuinely novel and represents a promising direction for improving distant supervision techniques. While the experimental validation isn't exhaustive, the consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly on the real-world low-resource languages, are encouraging. The work's primary contribution lies in this novel “noise layer” approach, moving beyond simply filtering projected data to actively learning the mapping between noisy and clean tags. A secondary, yet valuable contribution, is the demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach within a deep learning framework (BiLSTM). Finally, the thorough experimentation across a range of languages, including both simulated and genuinely low-resource scenarios, provides a reasonably solid empirical grounding for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Noise Modeling:** The central idea of learning a transformation to map projected tags to their likely correct counterparts is innovative. This moves beyond existing approaches that either heuristically filter noisy projections or treat them as equally reliable as gold data. This learned transformation effectively encodes assumptions about common projection errors (e.g., determiner tags being misapplied) and allows the model to adapt.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly compelling, indicating the method’s practical utility.
3. **Effective Integration with Deep Learning:** The seamless integration of the noise modeling into a BiLSTM architecture showcases the adaptability of the approach. Utilizing a powerful deep learning model allows for capturing complex contextual information alongside the noisy supervision signal.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across eight simulated languages alongside two real-world low-resource languages demonstrates a reasonable level of robustness and generalization. The use of a standard evaluation setting (following Duong et al., 2014a) facilitates comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Learned Transformation:** While the paper presents the learned transformation matrix (A) in Figure 4, a deeper analysis of *what* the model is learning would be beneficial. Are there consistent patterns of mis-mapping across languages? Are certain tag types particularly prone to noise? Understanding these patterns could provide insights into the effectiveness of the approach and potential areas for improvement.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The method’s reliance on word alignments introduces a dependency on alignment accuracy. The paper acknowledges this, but a more thorough investigation of the impact of alignment errors on performance would be valuable. Perhaps experiments with varying alignment quality thresholds or different alignment tools could shed light on this issue.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper provides limited detail regarding hyperparameter tuning, specifically regarding the noise layer. A more detailed discussion of how the parameters of the transformation matrix A were optimized would strengthen the results. Were any regularization techniques employed to prevent overfitting of this layer?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned transformation matrices (A) across different languages? Are there consistent patterns of mis-mapping that emerge?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or thresholds for alignment confidence?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for the noise layer, specifically regarding the optimization of the transformation matrix A? Were any regularization techniques used?
4.  The paper mentions the possibility of applying this approach to other tasks beyond POS tagging. Could you briefly discuss the potential challenges and modifications required to adapt this framework to tasks like NER or dependency parsing?

**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. Figure 2 is well-designed and effectively illustrates the model architecture. The choice of using the universal tagset is commendable for facilitating cross-lingual comparison.
