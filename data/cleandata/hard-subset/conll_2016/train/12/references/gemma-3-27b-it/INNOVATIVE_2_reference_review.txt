This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging by cleverly leveraging cross-lingual projection and, crucially, *modeling* the noise inherent in that projection. The core idea – a noise transformation layer to map projected tags to expected low-resource tags – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from simply filtering or heuristically correcting projected data. While the experimental setup is fairly standard, the results consistently outperform strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art methods, on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The explicit modeling of projection noise, rather than treating it as a fixed artifact, is the key strength.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The introduction of a learnable "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected (and often erroneous) high-resource tags and the target low-resource tags is the most significant contribution. This is a clever and conceptually sound approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the proposed model across multiple languages and datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach. Achieving SOTA results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is notable.
3. **End-to-End Learning:** The ability to jointly train the model with both gold and projected data in an end-to-end fashion simplifies the pipeline and allows the model to learn the noise transformation directly from the data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Idea:** The paper presents a genuinely new approach to a well-studied problem. Most existing work focuses on cleaning or filtering projected data, but this work directly addresses the noise through modeling. This is a high-risk, high-reward strategy, and it pays off here.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing cross-lingual projection methods.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages provides a strong validation of the approach. The inclusion of multiple baselines strengthens the conclusions.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Noise Layer:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of errors being modeled and the relationships between the tagsets. It's particularly interesting to see how the model learns to ignore certain projections.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The technique is directly applicable to any low-resource NLP task where cross-lingual projection is used, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the visualization of the noise layer is helpful, a more detailed qualitative analysis of specific error cases and how the model corrects them would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, examples of instances where the noise layer *corrects* a misprojected tag would be impactful.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate or the dimensionality of the hidden layers. Some discussion of this would be beneficial.
3. **Alignment Quality Assumption:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the word alignments generated by Giza++. While acknowledged, the paper doesn’t sufficiently discuss the potential impact of poor alignment quality on the results. A brief ablation study or error analysis focused on alignment errors would be valuable.
4. **Tagset Mismatch Handling (Limited Discussion):** The paper mentions the potential for handling mismatched tagsets but doesn’t elaborate on this aspect beyond a brief note. A more detailed discussion and experimental results on mismatched tagsets would broaden the applicability of the method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide specific examples of input sentences and projected tags where the noise layer successfully corrects an error?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to different word alignment tools or parameters?
3. Did you experiment with different initialization strategies for the noise transformation matrix A? If so, what impact did they have on performance?
4. Can you elaborate on the implementation details for handling mismatched tagsets, and present results on a dataset with significant tagset differences?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. The figures are well-chosen and informative. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive. Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem. I recommend acceptance.
