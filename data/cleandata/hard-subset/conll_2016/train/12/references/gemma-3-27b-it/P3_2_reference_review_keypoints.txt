- Summary: Tackles low-resource POS tagging via cross-lingual projection and proposes a BiLSTM with a trainable “noise layer” to model and learn from projection errors, showing strong gains on simulated and real languages.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a noise layer (trainable transformation matrix) that explicitly models discrepancies between projected and gold tags.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across eight simulated languages and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), outperforming strong baselines.

- Contribution 3: Uses a well-motivated, comprehensive experimental setup with ablations and comparisons that strengthen the empirical claims.

- Strength 1: The noise layer is a novel, intuitive approach that models transformation errors rather than just filtering or weighting projected tags.

- Strength 2: Experimental results are strong and consistent, with notable gains on low-resource real-world languages.

- Strength 3: Paper is clearly written and well organized, with informative figures and tables.

- Strength 4: Related work is thoroughly discussed and the work is well positioned within the literature.

- Strength 5: Analysis and visualization of the learned transformation matrix provide useful insights into projection errors.

- Weakness 1: Lacks detailed analysis of how word alignment quality correlates with tagging performance and whether higher-confidence alignments help.

- Weakness 2: Does not report comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity or robustness checks (learning rate, embeddings, LSTM size).

- Weakness 3: Computational cost of the added noise layer (training/inference overhead) is not quantified or compared to simpler models.

- Weakness 4: Handling of tagset mismatches via rectangular matrix A is under-explained, including how mappings were determined.

- Question 1: Please analyze the relationship between alignment quality (e.g., confidence scores) and model performance—did better alignments help?

- Question 2: What is the computational overhead (training time/inference speed) introduced by the noise layer versus a plain BiLSTM?

- Question 3: How was the tagset mapping handled for rectangular matrix A—manual, automatic, or hybrid, and what method if automated?

- Question 4: Would larger Kinyarwanda parallel data likely yield further gains, and roughly how large an improvement is expected?

- Additional Comment 1: Provide the link to the web-sourced Malagasy parallel texts for reproducibility.

- Additional Comment 2: Include discussion of limitations and future directions, e.g., applying the approach to other NLP tasks or more advanced noise models.

- Overall Assessment: Strong, novel, and well-executed paper with compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing listed questions and analyses.
