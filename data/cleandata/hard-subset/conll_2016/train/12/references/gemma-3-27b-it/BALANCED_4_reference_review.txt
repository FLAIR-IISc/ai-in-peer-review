This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in projected tags within a neural network framework, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a “noise layer” within a BiLSTM network that learns a transformation between projected tags and the true tag distribution, effectively de-noising the projected data during training. This is a well-motivated approach, as projected data is known to be noisy due to alignment errors and syntactic divergence.  A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation of this approach on both simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods.  Finally, the inclusion of insightful visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices provides valuable understanding of how the model adapts to the specific characteristics of each language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Noise Layer:**  The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a valuable contribution.  Existing approaches typically rely on heuristics or constraints to filter noisy data, while this work learns to compensate for the noise directly within the model. This is a more flexible and potentially more effective strategy.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art method by Duong et al. (2014a) on both simulated and real-world datasets. Achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda is a notable achievement.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation is thorough, encompassing both simulated data (allowing for controlled comparisons) and real-world languages. This strengthens the validity and generalizability of the findings.
4. **Informative Visualizations:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) offers valuable insights into the model's behavior and reveals how it adapts to different languages.  This is particularly helpful for understanding *why* the model performs well.
5. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly explained, and the proposed method is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Matrix:** While the visualization of the noise matrix is a strength, the analysis of these matrices is relatively superficial.  A more detailed examination of the learned transformations – for example, identifying systematic patterns of tag confusion – could provide deeper insights into the nature of the projection noise and the model's ability to address it.  Are specific tag types consistently misaligned?  Do the matrices differ substantially across languages, and if so, why?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide extensive details about hyperparameter tuning. It’s unclear how sensitive the performance is to different learning rates, embedding dimensions, or the size of the noise transformation matrix.  A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
3. **Comparison with Simpler Baselines:** Although strong baselines are included, the comparison lacks some simpler alternatives. For example, a simple linear interpolation between the gold and projected data probabilities, without the full complexity of the noise layer, could provide a useful point of reference.
4. **Data Filtering:** The use of Giza++ and subsequent filtering based on sentence alignment scores represents a pre-processing step that could impact results. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of this filtering step would be valuable.  What happens if the filtering is removed, or if a different alignment tool is used?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices? Specifically, are there systematic patterns of tag confusion that emerge across different languages?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, embedding dimension, and size of the noise transformation matrix?
3.  Have you explored simpler methods for combining the gold and projected data, such as linear interpolation of probabilities, as a baseline?
4.  What is the impact of the sentence alignment filtering step on the overall performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to “clab/cnn” is not particularly informative. Please provide a more complete citation or a link to the code repository.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of this approach. For instance, the method may not be effective if the tagsets of the source and target languages are significantly different.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to POS tagging in low-resource languages. The proposed noise layer is a significant contribution, and the experimental results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
