This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a BiLSTM model incorporating a “noise layer” to model and mitigate the inaccuracies inherent in projected tags. The primary contribution, as I see it, is a reasonably well-motivated attempt to explicitly model projection noise within a neural network framework. A secondary contribution is the demonstration, through experiments on both simulated and real low-resource languages, that this approach can achieve state-of-the-art results. Finally, the comprehensive experimental setup, including comparison to several baselines and the use of both simulated and real-world datasets, is a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a practical problem:** Low-resource NLP remains a crucial area, and the use of cross-lingual projection is a common, albeit noisy, strategy. The authors directly tackle the issue of noise, which is a significant limitation of existing approaches.
2. **Sound methodology:** The proposed noise layer is a conceptually reasonable approach. Learning a transformation between projected and gold tags allows the model to adapt to systematic errors in the projection process. The joint training of the model with both gold and projected data is also a sensible design choice.
3. **Empirical validation:** The results on both simulated and real languages demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The reported improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, are encouraging. The inclusion of a simulation setup is helpful for controlled experimentation.
4. **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The figures illustrating the noise transformation matrix are particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core idea:** While the implementation is well-executed, the fundamental idea of modeling noise in projected data isn’t entirely novel. Previous work (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a) has addressed this issue through heuristics or feature engineering. The “noise layer” can be seen as a more sophisticated way to achieve a similar goal, but the conceptual leap isn’t substantial.
2. **Insufficient analysis of the learned noise transformation:** The paper presents the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4), but the analysis is superficial. A more in-depth examination of these matrices could reveal valuable insights into the types of errors being corrected and the underlying linguistic phenomena driving these errors. What specific tag confusions are most frequent? Are there systematic biases in the projection process that the model is learning to compensate for?
3. **Dependence on strong baselines:** The performance gains are demonstrated relative to specific baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a). However, the superiority of these baselines themselves isn't thoroughly justified. A more comprehensive comparison to more recent and potentially stronger baselines (e.g., those employing pre-trained multilingual embeddings) would strengthen the results.
4. **Data limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the limited size of the parallel corpora, the impact of this limitation on the learned noise transformation is not fully discussed. The relatively small datasets might lead to overfitting and hinder the generalization ability of the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 & 4)? Specifically, what are the most frequent tag confusions that the model is learning to correct?
2. Have you experimented with different architectures for the noise layer (e.g., a more complex non-linear transformation)? If so, what were the results?
3. How sensitive are the results to the size and quality of the parallel corpora? Have you performed any experiments to assess the robustness of the model to data scarcity and noise?
4. How do the results compare to baselines employing pre-trained multilingual embeddings (e.g., BERT, XLM-RoBERTa) fine-tuned for POS tagging?
