## ConLL Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to move beyond simply using projected tags as noisy labels, and instead *learn* how much to trust these projected labels through a novel neural network architecture. This architecture incorporates a “noise transformation” layer that models the mapping between projected and true tags, effectively learning which projections are reliable and which should be downweighted. The authors demonstrate strong performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, outperforming existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Principled Modeling of Projection Noise:** The most significant contribution is the explicit modeling of the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection as a learnable transformation. This is a clever way to address the fundamental issue of inaccurate projections without resorting to hand-engineered heuristics or filtering methods.  This moves beyond simply *using* projected data to actively *understanding* its limitations.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper demonstrates convincingly that the proposed model outperforms strong baselines on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages.  The performance gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly notable, establishing a new state-of-the-art.
3. **Effective Deep Learning Architecture:** The integration of the noise transformation layer within a BiLSTM framework provides a robust and expressive model capable of capturing complex relationships between input tokens, projected tags, and true tags.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The idea of learning to trust noisy projections is a genuinely novel contribution. It's a significant step forward from previous work that has largely focused on *cleaning* projected data, rather than adapting to its inherent noise. The conceptual framing of the noise transformation as a learnable mapping is particularly insightful.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-designed, including both simulated and real-world evaluation. The use of the same evaluation setting as Duong et al. (2014a) allows for a direct and fair comparison. The ablation studies (comparing BiLSTM with and without the noise layer) provide further support for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The illustrations (e.g., Figure 2) are helpful in understanding the architecture.  The motivation for the approach is clearly articulated in the introduction.
4. **Practical Relevance:**  The problem addressed (low-resource POS tagging) is highly relevant to many real-world NLP applications. The method presented offers a promising approach for tackling this challenge in a scalable and effective manner.
5. **Analysis of Learned Noise Parameters:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insight into the model’s behavior and the types of errors it is learning to correct.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis.  It would be valuable to examine *specific* examples where the model succeeds or fails, and to understand the types of projection errors that are most effectively handled by the noise layer.  For instance, are certain POS tag confusions consistently learned and corrected?
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for projection.  The paper briefly acknowledges this, but doesn't investigate the impact of different alignment tools or parameters.  A more systematic exploration of alignment quality could be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn’t detail the extent of hyperparameter tuning performed. While the chosen parameters (embedding dimension, hidden layer size, learning rate) are reasonable, a more thorough search could potentially yield further improvements. Reporting the results of such a search, even briefly, would add to the rigor of the evaluation.
4. **Computational Cost:** The added complexity of the noise transformation layer may increase computational cost during training. The paper does not discuss the computational overhead of the proposed method compared to simpler baselines.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of errors made by the model and how the noise layer helps to correct them?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or parameters?
3.  What was the extent of hyperparameter tuning performed? What search strategy was used, and what range of values were considered for key parameters?
4. What is the approximate increase in training time compared to a baseline BiLSTM model without the noise layer?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to “clab/cnn” is a bit unclear. A more specific citation would be helpful.
* The phrase “well suited to the low-resource applications” in the introduction feels a bit redundant.
*  It would be interesting to see if the learned noise transformation matrix generalizes across different low-resource languages.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a novel and effective approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual projections for low-resource POS tagging. The theoretical insights, strong empirical results, and clear presentation make this a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly the lack of detailed error analysis) would further strengthen the paper.
