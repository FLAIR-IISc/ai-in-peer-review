This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold-standard annotations and noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to learn a “noise transformation” to map projected tags to the target language’s tags, effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data. The approach is evaluated on simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating improvements over existing methods.

**Contributions:**

1. **Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The primary contribution lies in explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual tag projection via a learnable transformation matrix (A). This allows the model to effectively de-noise the projected data and weigh its contribution during training, rather than treating it as equally reliable as gold data. This is a significant refinement over previous work that either simply filtered noisy projections or implicitly handled them within a feature set.
2. **Joint Training Framework:** Integrating the noise model into a BiLSTM framework and performing end-to-end joint training of the model with both gold and projected data allows for a more holistic and effective learning process. The model can learn to leverage the strengths of both data sources simultaneously.
3. **Empirical Validation & Strong Results:** The thorough evaluation on eight simulated languages and two real-world low-resource languages, achieving state-of-the-art results, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance gains, especially on Malagasy, are notable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise is a genuinely novel idea. Previous approaches have either relied on heuristics to filter noisy projections or attempted to implicitly address the problem through feature engineering. This work tackles the noise directly in a principled manner.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistently strong performance across both simulated and real-world datasets is a significant strength. The improvement over strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a), is compelling.
3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the potential benefits of leveraging cross-lingual projection. The motivation for addressing the noise inherent in projection is well-justified.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive experimental setup, including both simulated and real-world languages, strengthens the validity of the findings. The inclusion of multiple baselines provides a fair comparison.
5. **Interpretability of the Noise Matrix:** The visualization and discussion of the learned noise transformation matrix (A) provide valuable insights into the types of errors that occur during projection and how the model learns to compensate for them. The analysis of the noise matrix adds to the understanding of the method and its behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not delve into the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters, particularly those related to the noise transformation matrix (A). It would be beneficial to explore the impact of different initialization strategies, learning rates, and regularization techniques on the performance of the model. The two-stage training process could also benefit from analysis regarding the optimal point to switch from pre-training to joint training.
2. **Limited Analysis of Projection Quality:** While the paper acknowledges that word alignment quality impacts projection accuracy, there is limited analysis of *which* types of alignments contribute most to the noise. A more detailed analysis of alignment errors could inform strategies for improving the projection process itself. Could the model be extended to incorporate alignment confidence scores?
3. **Data Source Specificity:** The performance on the real-world languages is very strong, but it’s unclear how well the model generalizes to different types of parallel data. The web-sourced data for Malagasy and the ARL MURI data for Kinyarwanda may have different characteristics that contribute to the observed performance. Investigating the model’s robustness to varying data quality would be valuable.
4. **Complexity and Computational Cost:** The addition of the noise layer and the two-stage training process introduce additional complexity compared to simpler baselines. While the performance gains justify this complexity, it would be useful to discuss the computational cost and scalability of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the initialization of the noise transformation matrix (A)? Did you experiment with different initialization strategies?
2. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the alignment errors that contribute to the noise in the projected tags? Are certain alignment patterns particularly problematic?
3. How does the model perform when trained on parallel data of varying quality? Have you investigated the robustness of the approach to different data sources?
4. What is the computational overhead introduced by the noise layer and the two-stage training process? How does the training time compare to that of the baseline models?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are generally clear and informative. Consider adding a more detailed explanation of the equations in Section 3.2.
* A brief discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research would be beneficial. For example, exploring the use of different neural network architectures or incorporating morphological information could be considered.
* Typo: "raha ny marina" appears in Figure 2 without explanation.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to low-resource POS tagging. The explicit modeling of projection noise is a significant contribution, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions. I recommend acceptance.
