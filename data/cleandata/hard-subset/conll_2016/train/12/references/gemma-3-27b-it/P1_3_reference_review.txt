## Peer Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to move beyond simply using projected tags as hard labels and instead *learn* how much to “trust” them, explicitly modelling the noise inherent in the projection process via a neural network with a dedicated “noise transformation” layer. The authors demonstrate improved performance on both simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modelling:** The most significant contribution is the explicit modelling of the projection noise. By introducing a learnable transformation matrix, the model can adaptively weigh the reliability of projected tags, effectively de-noising them during training. This is a nuanced and pragmatic approach to a well-known problem in cross-lingual transfer.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over existing methods on a diverse set of languages, including two genuinely low-resource languages. The results provide solid evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The paper utilizes a robust experimental setup, including simulation studies with controlled data scarcity and evaluation on real-world languages, and comparison against strong baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modelling:** The explicit noise transformation layer is a novel and compelling idea.  Existing approaches often rely on heuristics or filtering to clean projected data. Learning the noise characteristics directly within the model is a more elegant and potentially more effective solution. The visualization of the A matrix in Figure 3 and 4 further reinforces this point, illustrating how the model learns to map between tagsets.
2. **Consistent Performance Gains:** The results consistently show improvement across a diverse range of languages.  This suggests the approach is not simply overfitting to a specific language pair or dataset. The performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is particularly noteworthy, demonstrating the practical applicability of the method to genuinely low-resource settings.
3. **Well-Defined Problem and Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and the challenges associated with cross-lingual projection. The motivation for the work is strong and the proposed solution directly addresses the identified issues.
4. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors thoroughly evaluate their approach using both simulated and real-world data, and compare against relevant baselines. The ablation studies and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity and Interpretability:** While the model is effective, the introduction of the noise transformation layer adds complexity.  The interpretation of the learned `A` matrix, while visualized, isn't fully explored. A deeper analysis of what specific noise patterns the model is capturing could strengthen the paper. For example, is it primarily correcting for alignment errors, syntactic divergence, or tagset differences?
2. **Limited Analysis of Alignment Quality:** The paper mentions word alignment errors as a source of noise but doesn't delve deeply into the *quality* of the alignments used. A more detailed analysis of alignment accuracy (e.g., using F1 score) and its correlation with tagging performance would be beneficial.  It is possible the method implicitly compensates for poor alignments, but quantifying this would be valuable.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings, particularly those related to the noise transformation layer.  Providing some insights into the robustness of the model to hyperparameter choices would be useful.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of what specific types of noise the learned transformation matrix *A* is capturing? Are there any consistent patterns across languages?
2.  How does the performance of your model change with different levels of alignment quality? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or techniques?
3.  What is the sensitivity of your model to the hyperparameters of the noise transformation layer (e.g., learning rate, regularization)?
4.  The authors claim the method is general and could be used for other noisy annotations. Could you elaborate on how the approach could be adapted to scenarios like distant supervision or crowd-sourced data and what challenges might arise in those contexts?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sentences are a bit verbose.
*   The related work section is well-written and provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   The code should be made available to allow reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The authors demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods and provide a compelling analysis of their approach.  While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and questions raised above.
