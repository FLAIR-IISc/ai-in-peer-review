- Tackles low-resource POS tagging by modeling noise in cross-lingual projected tags using a noise transformation layer on top of a BiLSTM.
- Jointly trains on limited annotated data and noisy projected data, showing strong performance on simulated and real low-resource languages.
- Novel noise transformation layer provides a principled way to learn when and how much to trust projected information.
- Empirical validation includes two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) and extensive simulations, strengthening real-world relevance.
- Demonstrates performance gains over prior state-of-the-art methods, particularly on real-world languages.
- Addresses a critical problem in low-resource NLP and is well-motivated given alignment errors and syntactic divergence.
- Experimental design is thorough with appropriate baselines and useful visualizations of the noise matrix.
- Model is clearly presented and can be trained end-to-end via backpropagation.
- Underlying BiLSTM architecture is standard; alternative architectures (e.g., Transformers) are not explored.
- Lacks sensitivity analysis for the noise transformation matrix A (initialization, robustness across language pairs).
- Discussion of how data scarcity affects learned noise parameters and generalization to other low-resource languages is limited.
- Reliance on Giza++ alignments is a potential limitation; impact of residual alignment errors is not explicitly analyzed.
- Requests an ablation study to isolate contributions of the noise layer versus the BiLSTM and projected-only training.
- Asks for details on initialization strategies for matrix A and their effects on performance and stability.
- Inquires whether learned A matrices show transferable patterns across language pairs or can be pre-trained.
- Suggests quantifying alignment error impact, e.g., via manual inspection of mis-tagged instances caused by alignment mistakes.
- Notes incomplete references (e.g., Ammar et al. 2016) and recommends discussing computational cost versus simpler baselines.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and effective paper with minor weaknesses; recommendation for acceptance.
