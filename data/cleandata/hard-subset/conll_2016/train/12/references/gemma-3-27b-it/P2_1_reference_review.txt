## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:** This paper tackles the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to *explicitly model* the noise inherent in projected tags via a "noise transformation" layer in a BiLSTM network. This allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data, combining it effectively with a small amount of gold-standard annotated data. The paper demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, exceeding state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The introduction of the “noise transformation” layer to explicitly model the discrepancies between projected and true POS tags is the most significant contribution. This is a thoughtful approach to address a known weakness of cross-lingual projection.
2. **Effective Integration of Distant and Supervised Data:** The proposed model provides a principled way to combine noisy, distantly supervised data with limited gold-standard annotations, leading to improved performance.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results across multiple languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly on real-world low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Problem:** Cross-lingual projection is a crucial technique for low-resource NLP, but its effectiveness is limited by noise. This paper directly confronts this challenge with a novel and well-motivated solution.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The BiLSTM architecture is a reasonable choice for sequence tagging, and the addition of the noise transformation layer is a logical extension. The two-stage training process (pre-training then joint training) is also a sensible approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation across eight simulated languages and two real-world languages is extensive and helps to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.  The comparison against strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), is crucial.
4. **Clear Explanation of Noise Layer:** The paper clearly explains the function of the noise transformation matrix A, linking it to expected confusion patterns between tags.  The visualization of the learned matrices (Figures 3 & 4) is insightful.
5. **Practical Relevance:**  The work directly addresses a real-world need in NLP: building systems for languages with limited resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Architecture:** While the noise layer is novel, the underlying BiLSTM architecture itself is not. The paper doesn’t significantly advance the *state-of-the-art* in neural network architectures for sequence tagging. The novelty lies more in the application of this layer to the specific problem of noisy projection.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The performance is still inherently tied to the quality of word alignments. While the noise layer mitigates the impact of noise, poor alignments will still limit the potential gains. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of alignment quality on performance.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper lacks details regarding hyperparameter tuning. How were the learning rate, embedding dimensions, and LSTM hidden layer sizes selected? More details would increase reproducibility and build confidence in the results.
4. **Ablation Study:** An ablation study that explicitly isolates the contribution of the noise layer (e.g., by training a BiLSTM *without* the noise layer on the combined dataset) would strengthen the claim that the noise layer is responsible for the observed improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and any specific strategies employed (e.g., grid search, random search)?
2.  How does the performance of your model vary with the quality of the word alignments?  Could you perhaps correlate alignment accuracy with POS tagging accuracy?
3.  Have you considered alternative methods for modeling the noise, such as using a different type of neural network layer or a different parameterization of the noise transformation matrix?
4. Can you elaborate on the potential applicability of this noise layer to other distant supervision scenarios beyond cross-lingual projection?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   The related work section is thorough and provides adequate context for the proposed approach.
*   The formatting is generally good, though some figure captions are slightly long.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The contribution of explicitly modeling noise in cross-lingual projection is valuable and the experimental results are compelling. While the architectural novelty is limited, the approach is well-motivated, effectively implemented, and demonstrates a significant improvement in POS tagging for low-resource languages. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly providing more details about hyperparameter tuning and conducting an ablation study) would further strengthen the paper.
