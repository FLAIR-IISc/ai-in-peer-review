## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:** This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English) and combining it with limited gold-standard data. The core contribution is a novel neural network architecture (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the "noise" inherent in projected tags through a learned transformation matrix. This allows the model to learn *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data, effectively mitigating the issues of poor word alignments and cross-lingual syntactic divergence. The paper demonstrates strong performance on simulated low-resource scenarios and achieves state-of-the-art results on two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The introduction of the "noise layer" and the learned transformation matrix (A) to model and compensate for inaccuracies in cross-lingual projection is the most significant contribution. This is a clever and principled way to address a known weakness of this technique.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent and substantial improvements over existing methods, particularly on the real-world low-resource languages, provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, including both simulated and real-world experiments, and comparisons against several strong baselines, strengthening the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The idea of explicitly modeling the noise in projected data, rather than relying on heuristics or pre-processing, is a novel and insightful contribution. This tackles the core problem of cross-lingual projection head-on.
2. **Strong Performance Gains:** The reported accuracy improvements, especially on the challenging real-world languages, are significant and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.  The 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda are impressive results.
3. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with clear baselines and a robust evaluation methodology. The use of both simulated and real-world data enhances the generalizability of the findings. The controlled simulation setup allows for isolating the impact of the proposed method.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental results. The inclusion of Figure 2, illustrating the model architecture, is particularly helpful.
5. **Analysis of Learned Noise Matrix:** The visualization and discussion of the learned transformation matrix 'A' (Figure 3 & 4) provide valuable insights into the types of noise the model is learning to compensate for. This helps validate the underlying assumptions of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper shows overall accuracy improvements, it lacks a detailed analysis of *specific* error cases and *why* the proposed model performs better in those cases. Understanding where the model still struggles and the nature of those struggles would be valuable.  For example, a discussion of alignment errors that the model *can't* overcome would be insightful.
2. **Dependence on Giza++:** The reliance on Giza++ for word alignments is a potential limitation.  Giza++ is known to be sensitive to parameters and can produce suboptimal alignments.  Exploring alternative alignment tools or incorporating alignment probabilities directly into the model could be beneficial.  Acknowledging this limitation more explicitly would be useful.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** While the noise layer adds a valuable component, it also introduces additional parameters. A more detailed discussion of the impact of the noise layer’s size (KxK matrix) and training stability would be beneficial.  Is this parameter sensitive?  Does it require careful tuning?
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  A more thorough ablation study would help to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For example, training the BiLSTM *without* the noise layer on both gold and projected data, and then *adding* the noise layer, would help demonstrate its true impact.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific examples where the proposed model outperforms the baselines and explaining *why* this improvement occurs?
2.  Have you considered using alternative word alignment tools besides Giza++? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the model to the size of the transformation matrix 'A'?  Have you explored different sizes or regularization techniques to prevent overfitting?
4.  Could you present an ablation study where you train the BiLSTM with and without the noise layer, evaluating the performance difference?
5.  The paper mentions a two-stage training process. What criteria are used to determine when to switch from pre-training to joint training?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "clab/cnn" is not immediately clear to someone unfamiliar with the codebase. A brief explanation would be helpful.
*   The phrase "mild improvements" in the description of BiLSTM Ann+Proj performance feels somewhat understated given the substantial differences in accuracy.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual POS tagging in low-resource settings. The explicit modeling of noise in projected data is a significant contribution, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the overall quality of the work is high, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.
