This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold-standard annotations and noisy data projected from a high-resource language. The core idea is to learn a "noise transformation" to map projected tags to more accurate representations, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their model on simulated low-resource scenarios and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating improved accuracy compared to existing methods.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the explicit modelling of projection noise within a neural network framework. While cross-lingual projection is a well-established technique, explicitly learning a transformation to account for the inherent inaccuracies has potential benefits. The secondary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource settings, providing a relatively robust assessment of the proposed method. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise via the learned transformation matrix (A) is a reasonably novel approach. It moves beyond simply filtering or heuristically correcting projected tags and instead attempts to learn the underlying distortions.  The results suggest that this is effective, at least in the tested scenarios.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both simulated and real low-resource languages are promising. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is a significant achievement. The consistent improvement across multiple languages lends credence to the generalizability of the approach.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is reasonably thorough.  The authors compare against a number of relevant baselines, including the previously strong method of Duong et al. (2014a). The use of both simulated and real-world data enhances the credibility of the findings.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 2, illustrating the model architecture, is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Learned Noise Matrix:** While the authors present the learned transformation matrices (A) in Figure 4, the analysis is superficial.  There is limited discussion of *why* certain noise patterns emerge. A more in-depth analysis of the matrix A could reveal valuable insights into the types of errors made during projection and how the model learns to correct them. For example, are there systematic biases towards certain tag mappings?  Are the learned transformations language-specific? 
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. Were the hyperparameters optimized for each language individually? A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of key hyperparameters on performance is missing. Without this, it's difficult to assess the robustness of the results.
3. **Data Preprocessing Details:** The paper states that Giza++ is used for word alignment but provides limited details on the preprocessing steps applied to the parallel corpora. The impact of different alignment settings on the projection quality is not discussed. A more detailed description of the data preprocessing pipeline is needed for reproducibility.
4. **Minor Formatting and Presentation Issues:** There are a number of minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper. For instance, citation styles vary. Equations are not consistently numbered.  The table captions are sometimes incomplete. These issues, while minor individually, detract from the overall polish of the paper. (e.g. Table 1 refers to a row labeled "BiLSTM Ann+Proj" yet this abbreviation is never defined prior)
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying specific instances where the model correctly handles noisy projections versus instances where it fails would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned transformation matrices (A), discussing the patterns and trends observed across different languages?
2.  Was a hyperparameter search performed? If so, what were the key hyperparameters tuned, and what ranges were explored?
3. Can you elaborate on the preprocessing steps applied to the parallel corpora, specifically regarding the settings used for Giza++?
4. What is the computational cost of the proposed model compared to the baselines? Specifically, how does the addition of the noise layer impact training and inference time?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation to Ammar et al. is incomplete.
*   Please ensure consistent formatting throughout the paper, including citation styles, equation numbering, and table captions.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of the noise layer specifically. Would the model perform similarly with a randomly initialized transformation matrix?



Despite the novelty of the proposed approach and promising empirical results, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough analysis and attention to detail. While the core idea is sound, the limited discussion of the learned noise patterns and the lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the findings. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses.
