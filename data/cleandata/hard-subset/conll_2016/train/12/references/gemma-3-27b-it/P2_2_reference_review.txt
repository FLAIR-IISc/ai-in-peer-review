## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors identify the inherent noise in projected data and propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly learns a "noise transformation" to better integrate this noisy data with a small amount of gold-standard annotations. The model learns to weigh the trust placed in projected tags, adapting to potential misalignments and cross-lingual differences.  The results on simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The core contribution is the introduction of a learnable “noise layer” (matrix A) that models the transformation between projected and ground truth tags.  This is a clever and intuitive way to address the well-known problems of cross-lingual projection and allows the model to adapt to systematic errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on both simulated and real low-resource languages, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art methods, including the previous best results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.
3. **Practical Framework for Low-Resource NLP:** The proposed framework offers a practical and effective approach for integrating distant supervision with limited labeled data, making it applicable to a broader range of low-resource NLP tasks.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of Approach:** The noise transformation layer is a genuinely novel idea. Existing approaches tend to focus on *filtering* noisy projections, while this work aims to *model* and *learn* from the noise itself. This is a more principled and potentially more effective approach.  The mathematical formulation of the noise layer is also clear and well-justified.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation, including both simulated experiments (using established benchmarks) and experiments on two real-world low-resource languages. This provides strong evidence for the generalizability of their approach. The use of both the Europarl corpus and web-sourced data for parallel corpora is a strength.
3. **Clear Explanation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the challenges of cross-lingual projection and the motivation behind their proposed solution.  The diagrams (particularly Figure 2) are helpful for understanding the model architecture. The discussion of the results is insightful, particularly the analysis of the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4).
4. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The achieved performance on both Malagasy and Kinyarwanda represents a significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art results.  This demonstrates the practical value of the proposed approach.
5. **Relevance to the CONLL Community:** The problem of low-resource language processing is highly relevant to the CONLL community, and the proposed solution is likely to be of interest to many researchers working in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their full model, a more detailed ablation study could have further strengthened their argument. For example, investigating the impact of different initialization strategies for the 'A' matrix (e.g., identity matrix vs. random initialization) or exploring alternative neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) would be beneficial.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The performance of cross-lingual projection heavily relies on the quality of word alignments.  The authors acknowledge this but do not explicitly investigate the impact of different alignment tools or parameters on their results. A brief analysis of this sensitivity would be helpful.
3. **Data Source Differences:** The data sources for the real-world languages (web-sourced Malagasy vs. ARL MURI Kinyarwanda) are quite different. It would be helpful to acknowledge this potential source of variability and discuss how it might affect the results.
4. **Complexity of Training:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) adds complexity.  While the authors provide a justification, it would be useful to explore whether a single-stage training approach could achieve comparable results.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically for the 'A' matrix learning rate and regularization?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or parameters?
3.  Given the differences in data sources for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, do you believe the observed performance differences are primarily due to the inherent difficulty of the languages or the characteristics of the data?
4.  Have you explored alternative neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) for this task? If so, how did they compare to the BiLSTM model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The code availability would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the results.
*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and well-organized.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to low-resource POS tagging. The explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projection is a significant contribution, and the empirical results are compelling. While some areas could be strengthened with further analysis, the paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and highly relevant to the CONLL community. I recommend **acceptance**.
