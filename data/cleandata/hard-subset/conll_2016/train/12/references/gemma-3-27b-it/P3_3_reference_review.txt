## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:** This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a rich-resource language (English). The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the noise inherent in projected tags through a "noise transformation" layer. This layer learns to map projected tags to the true tag distribution in the low-resource language, effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data. The model is evaluated on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The key contribution is the introduction of the noise transformation layer. This is a principled and effective way to address the well-known limitations of cross-lingual projection due to word alignment errors and syntactic divergence.  Moving beyond heuristics or simply combining data, this approach *learns* the nature of the projection errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates convincingly that the proposed model achieves strong performance, surpassing existing methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly notable.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation using both simulated data (allowing for controlled comparison) and real-world languages, providing a solid basis for assessing the generalizability of their approach.  The ablation studies (comparing different training configurations) are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Soundness of Approach:** The noise transformation layer is a clever and well-motivated idea.  It directly tackles the core problem of unreliable projections, framing it as a learnable mapping. This differs from previous work that largely focused on filtering or weighting projected data. The theoretical justification for learning this transformation is sound, and the implementation seems appropriate.
2. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is very well written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The diagrams (Figures 2 & 3) are helpful in understanding the model architecture and the learned noise parameters.
3. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide details on implementation (clab/cnn) and datasets, which increases the potential for reproducibility.  This is a positive aspect, particularly in an area where baselines are often difficult to implement.
4. **Strong Baseline Comparisons:** Comparing against strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a) and a maximum entropy model, strengthens the validity of the results.  The ablation studies within the BiLSTM framework further clarify the contribution of the noise layer.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Noise Matrix:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of projection errors the model is learning to correct. This analysis lends credibility to the approach and suggests potential areas for further improvement (e.g., languages with highly noisy projections).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Alignment Quality:** While the paper acknowledges the impact of word alignment errors, it lacks a detailed analysis of alignment quality for the specific language pairs used.  Knowing the error rate of the Giza++ alignments would provide further context for understanding the performance gains achieved by the noise layer. Specifically, investigating the types of alignment errors (e.g., many-to-many, NULL alignments) and their correlation with tagging errors would be helpful.
2. **Dependence on English as the Pivot:** The approach relies heavily on English as the pivot language for projection. While this is a common practice, it introduces potential biases and limitations, particularly for languages that are structurally or typologically distant from English.  Discussing these limitations and exploring the possibility of using alternative pivot languages would strengthen the paper.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not delve into the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters, especially the dimensions of the BiLSTM layers and the learning rate.  A brief exploration of these parameters would demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
4. **Computational Cost:** The addition of the noise layer adds parameters to the model, potentially increasing computational cost. The paper doesn't discuss the training time or resource requirements, which would be valuable information for practitioners.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the word alignment quality (using Giza++ statistics) for the language pairs used in your experiments?  Specifically, what is the percentage of one-to-one, many-to-one, one-to-many, and NULL alignments?
2. Have you considered the impact of using different pivot languages (other than English)?  Would the performance be significantly affected for languages that are typologically distant from English?
3. Did you perform any hyperparameter tuning? If so, what range of values were explored for the key hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM layer dimensions, learning rate)?
4. What is the computational overhead introduced by the noise transformation layer? How does the training time compare to a standard BiLSTM model without the noise layer?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of error analysis – what types of tagging errors still persist after applying the proposed model?
*   It would be interesting to explore the applicability of this approach to other NLP tasks besides POS tagging.
*   The reference list is quite extensive, which is positive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The proposed noise transformation layer is a clever and effective way to address the challenges of cross-lingual projection, and the experimental results are compelling. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the paper is of high quality and deserves acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for publication.
