This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold annotations and noisy data projected from a high-resource language. The core idea is to learn a “noise transformation” to map projected tags to their likely counterparts in the low-resource language, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their approach on both simulated and real low-resource languages, demonstrating improvements over existing methods.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) The formulation of cross-lingual projection noise as a learnable transformation within a neural network architecture, allowing the model to adaptively weight projected data. 2) A practical demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach on a range of languages, including two real low-resource scenarios (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), establishing a new state-of-the-art on these datasets. 3) The provision of insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, providing some interpretability as to how the model is handling projection errors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a novel contribution. Previous work often relies on heuristics or constraints to filter noisy projections. This method allows the model to *learn* how to deal with noise, potentially capturing more subtle patterns than hand-engineered rules.

2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistently reported gains over baseline methods, particularly on the real-world low-resource languages, demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is a significant achievement.

3. **Clear Presentation & Thorough Experiments:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The experimental setup is thorough, with comparisons to relevant baselines and an ablation study (comparing models with and without the noise layer). The inclusion of both simulated and real-world experiments strengthens the conclusions.

4. **Interpretability through Noise Matrix Analysis:** The visualization and discussion of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) offer a degree of interpretability. This helps to understand how the model is adapting to the specific challenges of each language and projection scenario. The observations regarding diagonal dominance and suppressed off-diagonal elements provide valuable insight.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Quality:** While the paper acknowledges the impact of word alignment errors, there’s limited discussion on the quality of the alignments used. Were any alignment filtering techniques beyond Yarowsky et al.’s sentence alignment score employed? The impact of alignment quality on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated.

2. **Complexity of the Model:** The introduction of the noise layer adds complexity to the model. While the results justify this complexity, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the model would be beneficial. Is there a significant increase in training time or memory requirements compared to simpler baseline models?

3. **Tagset Dependency:** The approach relies on having a reasonably aligned tagset between the source and target languages. The paper briefly mentions handling mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide detailed results or analysis in that scenario. How does performance degrade when the tagset alignment is poor? The discussion would be stronger with experimental results in that scenario.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the word alignment process? What specific parameters were used with Giza++, and were any post-processing steps applied to improve alignment quality (e.g., filtering based on alignment length or confidence scores)?

2. What is the computational overhead associated with the noise layer? Can you quantify the increase in training time and memory usage compared to a standard BiLSTM model without the noise layer?

3. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the size of the parallel corpus? Would the gains observed in Malagasy and Kinyarwanda still hold with significantly smaller parallel datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

- The citation style is inconsistent. Some references are given in full, while others are shortened (e.g., "Yarowsky et al., 2001"). Please standardize the citation format.
- The notation in equation (2) could be clarified. What is the dimensionality of matrix A? Is it KxK? A brief explanation would improve readability.
