Paper proposes learning a noise-transformation layer in a BiLSTM to model and mitigate cross-lingual projection noise for low-resource POS tagging, showing gains on simulated and real languages.

Introduces a learnable "noise layer" (matrix A) to transform projected tags into more accurate labels, explicitly modeling systematic projection errors.

Demonstrates strong empirical improvements over baselines and prior work on simulated and real low-resource languages (notably Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Provides comprehensive experiments and analyses, including ablations and visualization/interpretation of the learned noise matrices.

Novel and well-motivated approach: explicitly learning projection noise is elegant and addresses a key limitation of prior methods.

Clear presentation and solid positioning within related work, with well-articulated motivation and methodology.

Convincing experimental validation with consistent gains and useful ablation studies comparing annotated, projected, and combined data.

Insightful analysis of the noise matrix A that helps explain what types of projection errors are being corrected.

Highly relevant to low-resource NLP, offering a practical technique to leverage parallel data for POS tagging.

Training procedure is complex (two-stage pretrain + joint train); authors should justify whether end-to-end training is viable.

Limited analysis of word alignment quality and its correlation with model gains; unclear how alignment errors affect benefits of the noise layer.

Insufficient exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., size of A, learning rate) to establish robustness.

Question: Have you tried end-to-end training from random initialization, and if so, what were the results?

Question: Did you evaluate word alignment quality across language pairs and correlate it with performance?

Question: How sensitive is performance to the dimensionality/choice of the noise matrix A?

Question: Could the choice of the Universal POS tagset influence results; would language-specific tagsets change outcomes?

Request: Release of code would be valuable for reproducibility.

Suggestion: Applying the approach to other tasks (NER, parsing) would be interesting.

Overall recommendation: Strong, well-written paper with a novel, effective method and compelling results — recommend acceptance.
