- Proposes a neural POS tagger for low-resource languages that jointly uses small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projections via a learned noise transformation.
- Main contribution: explicit modelling of projection noise as a learnable transformation matrix that adapts to systematic projection errors.
- Validated empirically on simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Experimental setup is well-designed, using standard datasets and strong baselines (notably Duong et al. 2014a).
- Novel and insightful approach: learns how projected data is wrong rather than simply filtering it.
- Strong empirical results: consistent improvements and state-of-the-art on the two real languages; analyses of learned noise matrices provide insight.
- Clear, well-structured presentation with effective illustrations (example projections, figures).
- Uses a strong and relevant baseline, strengthening comparative claims.
- Shows practical awareness of parallel data quality and alignment choices (Giza++ one-to-one alignments).
- Limited in-depth analysis of why specific noise transformations are learned and their linguistic interpretations.
- Lacks detailed reporting on hyperparameter sensitivity and initialization strategy for the noise matrix A.
- Potential computational cost increase from the added noise layer is not discussed.
- Unclear how well the approach generalizes to different or substantially different tagsets (e.g., morphologically rich languages).
- Questions for authors on linguistic interpretation of learned matrices, sensitivity to A's initialization/learning rate, training time/memory overhead, and generalization to differing tagsets.
- Suggests tempering the claim of being the "first" to model projection noise and differentiating from prior filtering/correction work.
- Recommends adding discussion of future work avenues like alternative noise-layer architectures and applications beyond POS tagging.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with novel, effective approach; recommend acceptance.
