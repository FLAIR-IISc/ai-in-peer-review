This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a BiLSTM model incorporating a “noise layer” to learn the transformations between projected and actual tag distributions, effectively modeling the inherent inaccuracies of the projection process. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a reasonably well-executed application of a standard deep learning architecture (BiLSTM) to a well-known problem (cross-lingual POS tagging) with a thoughtful, if not revolutionary, modification – the noise layer. The experimental evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages is thorough, and the reported results are competitive with existing methods, demonstrating a modest but consistent improvement. A secondary contribution is the clear demonstration of the limitations of directly using projected tags and the benefit of explicitly modeling the projection noise.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The use of a BiLSTM is a reasonable and well-established approach for sequence tagging. The addition of the noise layer, while not a completely novel concept, is a sensible way to address the known issues of cross-lingual projection. The two-stage training approach (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) is also a pragmatic strategy.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents results on a diverse set of languages – eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages. This breadth of evaluation strengthens the claims about the generalizability of the proposed approach. The comparison to relevant baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), provides a useful benchmark.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The introduction effectively articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the potential benefits of cross-lingual projection. The paper clearly explains the shortcomings of existing projection methods and motivates the need for a model that can explicitly account for noise.
4. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The results consistently show that the proposed model outperforms or matches existing methods, including a state-of-the-art baseline. While the improvements aren’t dramatic, they are statistically significant and suggest that the approach is worthwhile.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the noise layer is a useful addition, it’s difficult to characterize it as a truly groundbreaking innovation. The concept of modeling noise in machine translation and other tasks is well-established. The specific implementation here feels like a relatively straightforward extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Layer:** The paper presents the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) but lacks a detailed analysis of *what* the model is learning. A more in-depth investigation into the patterns of noise captured by the matrix (e.g., specific tag confusions) would provide valuable insights and strengthen the claims about the model's interpretability. Why are certain tags consistently mis-projected, and what does the model learn to correct for these specific errors?
3. **Reliance on Established Resources:** The approach heavily relies on the quality of the word alignments generated by Giza++. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but doesn’t explore alternative alignment methods or strategies for mitigating the impact of alignment errors. The performance is thus critically dependent on the quality of these external resources.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study, systematically removing components of the model (e.g., the noise layer) to assess their individual contributions, would provide a more rigorous evaluation of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. How much improvement does the noise layer *actually* provide, beyond simply using a standard BiLSTM?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, highlighting specific tag confusions and explaining why the model learns to correct for them?
2. Have you experimented with alternative word alignment tools or strategies for mitigating the impact of alignment errors? If so, what were the results?
3. Could you perform an ablation study to assess the individual contribution of the noise layer? Specifically, what is the performance of a standard BiLSTM without the noise layer, trained on the combined gold and projected data?
