This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection and addressing its inherent noise. The core idea – explicitly modeling the transformation between projected and ground truth tags via a "noise layer" within a BiLSTM – is genuinely novel and a bold approach. While the experimental setup isn't groundbreaking (using established datasets and baselines), the framing of projection noise as a learnable transformation, rather than simply a filtering or heuristic problem, is a significant conceptual shift. The results, particularly exceeding state-of-the-art on the real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), lend credence to this approach. The paper's contribution isn't a methodological leap in neural network architecture, but a clever application of deep learning to a specific problem in cross-lingual transfer, focusing on a well-defined and practical issue: noisy projections.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is the paper’s strongest point. Previous work largely treats projection errors as something to be filtered *out*, whereas this work embraces the noise and attempts to *understand* it, making it a truly innovative direction.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. This isn't just a theoretical contribution; it demonstrably improves performance in challenging scenarios.
3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing cross-lingual projection techniques, establishing a strong motivation for the proposed work.  The problem is well-defined and the proposed solution directly addresses the identified issues.
4. **Interpretability of Noise Layer:** The visualization of the learned 'A' matrix (Figures 3 & 4) offers a degree of interpretability. While not a deep dive into linguistic analysis, seeing how the model learns to map projected tags to ground truth provides valuable insight into the nature of the projection noise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Layer:** While the visualization of the noise layer is a good start, the paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of *what* the model is learning in this layer. Are specific types of projection errors consistently mapped, and do these mappings align with known linguistic differences between the languages? More detailed linguistic analysis would strengthen the claims.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The performance gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are promising, but it's unclear how well this approach generalizes to other language pairs with different characteristics.  The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation (even if limited) on a wider range of language pairs would be valuable.
3. **Pre-training Dependency:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper does not sufficiently motivate *why* this two-stage approach is necessary. Is the model unstable if trained directly with the noisy data? An ablation study exploring different training strategies would be beneficial.
4. **Evaluation Focus:** The reliance on accuracy as the sole evaluation metric is limiting. While a standard metric, it doesn’t fully capture the nuance of POS tagging. Reporting precision, recall, and F1-score for individual tags, or examining error patterns, could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed linguistic analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (A)? Are there specific systematic mappings between projected and ground truth tags that shed light on the nature of projection errors?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or parameters?
3. Could you explore alternative training strategies? Specifically, would the model converge and perform comparably if trained end-to-end without the pre-training stage?
4. What is the computational overhead associated with the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model? Does it significantly increase training or inference time?
