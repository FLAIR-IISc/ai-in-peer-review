This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection and, crucially, modeling the noise inherent in that projection process. The core idea – learning a transformation to map projected tags to more accurate low-resource tags – is a clever and well-motivated approach. The experimental results on both simulated and real low-resource languages are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods, including a strong state-of-the-art result on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Contributions:**

1. **Noise-Aware Projection Modeling:** The primary contribution is the novel approach to explicitly modeling the noise introduced by cross-lingual tag projection. This is achieved through a learnable transformation matrix (A) that maps projected tags to a distribution over possible gold tags, allowing the model to effectively "de-noise" the projected data. This is a significant departure from previous work that treated projected data as either reliable or simply discarded it.
2. **Joint Training Framework:** Integrating the noise modeling within a deep learning framework (BiLSTM) and jointly training on both gold and projected data is a strong contribution.  This end-to-end training allows the model to learn the noise transformation in conjunction with the underlying POS tagging task, resulting in improved performance.
3. **Empirical Validation:** Thorough evaluation on eight simulated languages and two real-world low-resource languages provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent improvements over established baselines and state-of-the-art methods demonstrate the practical value of the work. The ablation studies and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightfulness:** The core idea of learning a noise transformation is innovative and addresses a critical limitation of cross-lingual projection methods. The authors clearly articulate the problems with traditional projection approaches and provide a compelling solution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results are highly convincing. The consistent improvements across multiple languages, especially the strong performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines and state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of both simulated and real-world data strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The technical details are explained thoroughly, and the experimental setup is well-documented. The figures and tables are informative and effectively communicate the results.
5. **Analysis of Learned Parameters:** Providing visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) is helpful for understanding what the model is learning and provides insight into the types of errors common in cross-lingual projection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Exploration of Noise Transformation:** While the learnable noise transformation matrix is a key innovation, the paper lacks a deeper exploration of its properties and potential variations. For example, the matrix is treated as a global transformation; investigating context-dependent transformations (e.g., based on word embeddings or POS tag distributions) could potentially further improve performance.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The method still relies on the quality of word alignments. While the noise transformation mitigates the effects of alignment errors, it does not eliminate them entirely. A discussion of the impact of alignment quality on performance, and potential methods for improving alignment robustness, would be valuable. A more formal error analysis would strengthen the work.
3. **Computational Cost:** Deep learning models, including BiLSTMs, can be computationally expensive to train, especially with large datasets. A discussion of the computational resources required for training and the potential for model optimization (e.g., pruning, quantization) would be helpful.
4. **Tagset Dependency:** The method currently relies on a common tagset between the source and target languages. While this is a common assumption, exploring methods for handling tagset mismatches more effectively would broaden the applicability of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you experimented with different architectures for the noise transformation layer, such as a multi-layer perceptron or a recurrent neural network?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments? Have you evaluated the model with different alignment tools or with artificially noisy alignments?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors that the model still makes after applying the noise transformation? This would help identify areas for further improvement.
4. What are the computational requirements for training the model on larger datasets? Are there any strategies for reducing the computational cost without sacrificing performance?



**Additional Comments:**

* The citations are generally well-maintained but a few seem truncated (e.g. "Ammar et al\.,? 2016"). Please check for completeness.
* It would be beneficial to include a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential future research directions.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and effective approach to low-resource POS tagging. The strong experimental results, clear writing, and insightful analysis make this a valuable contribution to the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
