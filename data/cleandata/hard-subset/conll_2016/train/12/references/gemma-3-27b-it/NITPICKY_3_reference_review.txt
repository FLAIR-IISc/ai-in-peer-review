This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that incorporates distant supervision via cross-lingual projection, explicitly modeling the noise inherent in this process through a “noise transformation” layer. The authors claim three contributions: 1) studying and modeling the noise of projected data, 2) integrating this model into a deep neural network for joint training, and 3) achieving state-of-the-art results on simulated and real-world low-resource languages.

While the idea of explicitly modeling projection noise is conceptually sound, the execution and presentation fall short of justifying acceptance. The core contribution – the noise layer – feels more like a parameterization of a known issue (projection inaccuracy) than a genuinely novel approach. The “noise transformation matrix” A, while learnable, simply represents a confusion matrix between tags, and its learned values (shown in Figure 3 and 4) are hardly insightful or groundbreaking. The description of the matrix A as representing "confusion scores" is overly generous; it merely indicates how often projected tags are re-mapped to different gold tags.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem:** Addressing POS tagging in low-resource languages is an important and active area of research. The use of cross-lingual projection as a form of distant supervision is a reasonable strategy given the scarcity of annotated data.
2. **Clear Experimental Setup:** The paper utilizes a relatively standard evaluation setup with both simulated and real low-resource languages, allowing for comparison with existing work. The inclusion of both datasets strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The comparison against established baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), provides a benchmark for evaluating the proposed method. The reported improvements, while not massive, are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The “noise layer” is not a fundamentally new concept. Confusion matrices are a standard tool for analyzing and correcting errors in classification. Framing this as a novel “transformation” feels like a semantic trick. The core idea – acknowledging and attempting to mitigate projection noise – has been explored in prior work, albeit using different mechanisms.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper lacks a thorough ablation study. For example, it would be valuable to demonstrate the contribution of the noise layer *specifically* by comparing a BiLSTM model *without* the noise layer trained on the combined (gold + projected) data to the full model. The presented results only show the impact of adding projected data generally, not the effectiveness of the noise layer itself. Tables 1 and 2 do not cleanly isolate this effect.
3. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of achieving "state-of-the-art" results should be qualified. The improvement over Duong et al. (2014a) is modest, and the authors do not convincingly demonstrate that their method offers a significant advancement in the field. The gap between their results and the SOTA is small enough that variations in hyperparameter tuning could easily explain the difference.
4. **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper contains several instances of imprecise language and unnecessary jargon. The explanation of the training objective (equation 3) is convoluted and difficult to follow. The figure captions are sometimes uninformative. For example, the figure of the architecture (Figure 2) is too abstract to be of great utility.
5. **Minor Methodological Concerns:** The use of Giza++ with only one-to-one alignments is a potentially limiting factor. Many-to-one and one-to-many alignments can capture valuable information, even if they introduce noise. Dismissing them outright seems overly conservative. Also, the choice to pre-train the model on gold data before fine-tuning with projected data is not fully justified. Why not train end-to-end from the beginning?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a direct comparison between a BiLSTM model *without* the noise layer, trained on the combined (gold + projected) data, and the full model presented in the paper? This would isolate the contribution of the noise layer.
2. What is the justification for pre-training the model on gold data before fine-tuning with projected data? Have you explored end-to-end training?
3. Can you elaborate on the interpretation of the learned values in the noise transformation matrix A? What specific linguistic phenomena do these values seem to capture?
4. Have you considered experimenting with different methods for handling many-to-one and one-to-many word alignments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the table captions is inconsistent.
*   The citation style is also inconsistent.



**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a reasonable approach, the lack of significant novelty, insufficient ablation study, overstated claims, and clarity issues outweigh the strengths. The contribution, as presented, does not warrant publication in a highly competitive venue like CoNLL.
