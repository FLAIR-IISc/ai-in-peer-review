This paper investigates low-resource POS tagging through cross-lingual projection, proposing a neural network model that learns to trust projected data based on a “noise transformation” layer. The core idea is to model the mapping between noisy projected tags and true tags, allowing the model to effectively utilize projected data alongside limited gold annotations.

The primary contribution is the proposed neural network architecture with the noise transformation layer, enabling joint training on gold and projected data. This layer effectively models the discrepancies between tagsets and projection errors, leading to improved performance. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation of this approach across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods. The third contribution is the insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, offering a qualitative understanding of projection errors and tag relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Noise Layer:** The introduction of the “noise layer” to explicitly model projection errors is a significant contribution. Existing work often relies on heuristics or joint feature sets, but this approach directly learns the transformation between noisy and clean tags, offering a more adaptive and potentially more effective solution. This is a well-motivated and clearly presented idea.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance gains across a diverse set of languages (both simulated and real-world) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. Achieving state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is particularly compelling, given the challenges associated with these low-resource languages. The thorough experimental setup, including comparisons to established baselines (including Duong et al., 2014a), strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Interpretability through Noise Matrix Analysis:** The analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices provides valuable insights into the types of errors that occur during cross-lingual projection. Visualizing these matrices (Figures 3 & 4) helps understand tag confusions and allows for a qualitative assessment of the model’s learning process. This improves transparency and builds confidence in the method.
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The use of both simulated and real low-resource language settings, coupled with a comparison against strong baselines (including Duong et al., 2014a and Garrette and Baldridge, 2013) demonstrates a comprehensive and rigorous evaluation of the proposed approach. The choice of datasets is appropriate, and the reported results are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. While the authors mention some settings (e.g., embedding size, hidden layer size), the process by which these parameters were chosen is not clearly explained. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results and to replicate the experiments.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for projection. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it doesn't explore the impact of different alignment methods or techniques for improving alignment quality. A brief investigation of this aspect could further strengthen the work.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** While the noise layer is a valuable contribution, it adds complexity to the model. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with this complexity, and a discussion of potential strategies for reducing it (e.g., dimensionality reduction techniques for the noise matrix).
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** Although the noise matrix provides some insight into tag confusions, a more detailed error analysis could be performed to identify specific types of errors that the model still struggles with. This could help guide future research and development efforts.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search strategy was used, and how were the optimal hyperparameters selected?
2. Have you experimented with different word alignment tools or techniques for improving alignment quality? If so, how did these choices affect the performance of your model?
3. What is the computational overhead associated with the noise layer? Have you explored any strategies for reducing its complexity without sacrificing performance?
4. Can you provide examples of specific errors that the model still makes, even after incorporating the noise layer? This would help identify areas for future improvement.

**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are generally well-designed and informative.
* The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow.
* It would be interesting to explore the applicability of this approach to other NLP tasks, such as named entity recognition or dependency parsing.
