## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a rich-resource language (English). The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the noise introduced by projection through a "noise transformation" layer. This layer learns to map projected tags to more accurate low-resource tags, effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data. The method is evaluated on simulated low-resource scenarios with several European languages and on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating improvements over existing approaches.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The core contribution is the introduction of a learnable “noise layer” that directly addresses the inaccuracies inherent in cross-lingual projection. This is a conceptually sound and practical way to mitigate the negative impact of noisy projected labels, going beyond simple filtering or heuristic adjustments.
2. **Empirical Validation on Real Low-Resource Languages:**  The evaluation on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, beyond simulated scenarios, strengthens the paper's findings and demonstrates the practical applicability of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art results on these languages is a significant accomplishment.
3. **Well-Framed Problem and Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and motivates the use of cross-lingual projection as a viable, though noisy, solution.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of the Approach:**  The noise transformation layer is a novel and well-motivated idea.  It’s not merely applying a standard neural network to the problem, but rather adding a mechanism specifically designed to handle the unique challenges of noisy data from cross-lingual projection. The model’s ability to learn tag confusions is particularly appealing.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing methods across a range of languages and experimental settings. The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are especially compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach on real-world, challenging datasets.  The comparison to Duong et al. (2014a) is relevant and favorable.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors present results on both simulated and real low-resource languages, using appropriate baselines. The ablation studies comparing different training strategies (gold data only, projected data only, combined) are informative. The inclusion of a visual representation of the learned noise transformation matrix is insightful.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental setup. The figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture and the noise transformation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Matrix:** While the visualization of the noise matrix (Figure 3 & 4) is a good start, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *what* the model is learning.  Are there predictable patterns in the learned noise transformations related to specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., differences in syntactic structure between languages)? A more qualitative and quantitative analysis of the noise matrix would add significant insight.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The method's performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the word alignments. While the paper acknowledges this, it doesn't thoroughly explore the impact of different alignment methods or strategies for handling alignment errors beyond simply filtering with sentence alignment scores.  A brief investigation into the robustness of the model to variations in alignment quality would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide much detail about hyperparameter tuning, and it’s unclear how sensitive the results are to different settings (e.g., learning rate, LSTM hidden layer size, the size of the noise matrix). Reporting some sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
4. **Comparisons to More Recent Approaches:** While the comparison to Duong et al. (2014a) is relevant, the field of low-resource NLP has advanced considerably since then.  Comparing to more recent techniques utilizing pre-trained multilingual embeddings (e.g., multilingual BERT) or more sophisticated transfer learning methods would provide a stronger benchmark.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figures 3 & 4)? What specific linguistic patterns or confusions does the model seem to be capturing?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the word alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or strategies for handling alignment errors (beyond sentence alignment score filtering)?
3.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? How sensitive are the results to different learning rates, LSTM hidden layer sizes, and the dimensionality of the noise matrix?
4.  Have you considered comparing your approach to more recent techniques based on pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., multilingual BERT)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The code availability would be a valuable addition to facilitate reproducibility.
*   The discussion of related work is good, but could benefit from mentioning recent advances in cross-lingual transfer learning using pre-trained models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to leveraging cross-lingual projection for low-resource POS tagging. The proposed noise transformation layer is a clever solution to a challenging problem, and the empirical results are convincing. While some areas could benefit from further analysis, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
