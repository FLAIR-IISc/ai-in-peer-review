This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that aims to better leverage noisy data created via cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to learn a “noise transformation” to map projected, often inaccurate, tags to more likely correct tags in the target language. The authors evaluate on both simulated low-resource scenarios and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating improvements over existing methods.

The primary contribution is a reasonably well-executed application of a neural network to a common problem (low-resource POS tagging) with a specific, and intuitively plausible, mechanism for handling noisy projected data. The explicit modeling of the projection noise, while not entirely novel in spirit, is a worthwhile addition to the existing toolkit for cross-lingual transfer learning. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across multiple languages, including real-world low-resource settings. The results consistently demonstrate a benefit from the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed model builds upon established techniques (BiLSTMs) and adds a relatively simple, yet effective, component (the noise transformation layer). This incremental approach is preferable to radical departures from well-understood methods. The joint training of the model on gold and projected data is also a sensible approach.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments, covering both simulated and real-world low-resource languages. The consistent performance gains across these settings lend credibility to the proposed approach.  The use of the same evaluation setting as Duong et al. (2014a) allows for direct comparison and demonstrates a clear improvement.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the model architecture (Figure 2) is particularly helpful.
4. **Relevance to the Community:** Low-resource NLP remains a crucial area of research.  Effective methods for leveraging transfer learning, particularly via cross-lingual projection, are highly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of BiLSTMs and a noise transformation layer is reasonable, the individual components are not particularly groundbreaking. The idea of modeling noise in projected data is not entirely new, and the specific implementation feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize this work within the broader landscape of noise-robust learning methods.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of the Noise Transformation:**  The paper presents the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) but lacks a detailed analysis of *what* the model is actually learning. Are there systematic patterns of mis-projection that the model consistently corrects? A more in-depth investigation of the learned parameters could provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the approach and potentially guide future improvements. Simply showing the matrices doesn’t tell us *why* the model works.
3. **Dependence on Giza++:** The reliance on Giza++ for word alignments introduces a potential source of error and limits the generalizability of the approach.  More recent alignment tools or methods that are less sensitive to alignment quality might yield further improvements. The sensitivity of the results to the alignment quality is not adequately discussed.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the overall accuracy numbers are encouraging, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. What types of errors does the model still make? Are there specific POS tags that are particularly difficult to predict? Understanding the remaining error patterns could inform future research directions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices? Specifically, what are some examples of systematic corrections that the model makes?
2. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the word alignments produced by Giza++? Have you experimented with other alignment tools or methods?
3. Could you provide an error analysis, identifying the most common types of errors made by the model?
4. The paper states the noise layer is parameterized by a K x K matrix A. How does this scale with the size of the tagset? Is this computationally feasible for very large tagsets?
