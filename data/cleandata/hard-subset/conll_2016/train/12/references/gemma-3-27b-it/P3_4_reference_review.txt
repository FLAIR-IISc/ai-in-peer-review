## Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:** This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging distant supervision via cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that incorporates a "noise layer" to explicitly model and learn the mapping between noisy projected tags and true tags in the target language. They demonstrate competitive results on both simulated and real low-resource languages, exceeding state-of-the-art performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The most significant contribution is the introduction of the 'noise layer' which explicitly models the transformation between projected and ground truth tag distributions. This is a conceptually sound approach, moving beyond simply treating projected data as inherently unreliable, and instead attempting to *understand* and *learn* the nature of the noise.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents consistently strong results across a range of languages (both simulated and real). The improvements on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, exceeding prior state-of-the-art, provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, using both simulated low-resource scenarios and real low-resource languages, providing a strong basis for assessing the generalizability of their method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of Approach:** The idea of explicitly modeling the projection noise is a novel and sensible way to address the inherent limitations of cross-lingual projection.  It moves beyond heuristic filtering and allows the model to *learn* how to best utilize the noisy signal. The mathematical formulation of the noise layer (the matrix A) is clear and well-defined.
2. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results are convincing. The performance gains on the real-world low-resource languages are particularly noteworthy, demonstrating the practical utility of the approach. The consistent outperformance of baselines, including state-of-the-art methods, adds further credibility.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, the experimental setup, and the results. The inclusion of Figure 2, illustrating the model architecture, is very helpful.  The discussion of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) offers valuable insights into how the model is adapting to the projection noise.
4. **Realistic Setting:** The focus on leveraging parallel data – a relatively accessible resource for many low-resource languages – is a practical and important direction. The use of limited annotated data also reflects a realistic scenario for low-resource NLP.
5. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** Comparing against Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013) provides a solid benchmark and demonstrates a clear advance in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Matrix (A):** While the authors present the learned noise matrices, the analysis is somewhat limited.  A more in-depth exploration of the patterns within these matrices – beyond simply noting the diagonal dominance – could provide further insight into the types of errors introduced by projection and how the model is correcting them.  For example, are certain tag confusions more prevalent in specific languages?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices, particularly the dimensions of the BiLSTM hidden layers and the learning rate.  Understanding how these parameters impact performance would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
3. **Computational Cost:** While the improvements are promising, the paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of training the more complex model with the noise layer compared to simpler baselines.  This information is important for practical adoption.
4. **Word Alignment Quality:** The paper acknowledges the impact of word alignment errors, but relies on standard Giza++ alignments. A brief discussion of the potential benefits of incorporating more advanced alignment techniques (e.g., those leveraging neural machine translation models) would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (A)? Specifically, are there any recurring patterns of tag confusions that vary across languages, and how do these relate to known linguistic differences between the source and target languages?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the dimensionality of the BiLSTM hidden layers and the learning rate? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization, and if so, what were the results?
3. What is the computational cost of training your model compared to the baseline models (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a)?  How does this impact the feasibility of applying your approach to other low-resource languages?
4.  Have you considered incorporating more sophisticated word alignment techniques (e.g. alignments from Neural Machine Translation) instead of relying on Giza++?  How might that impact the quality of the projected labels and the performance of your model?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears to be incomplete, particularly regarding some of the cited papers (e.g.,  "Ammar et al.,? 2016").  Please ensure a complete and accurate bibliography.
*   The use of the universal tagset is commendable, facilitating cross-lingual comparison.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to leveraging cross-lingual projection for low-resource POS tagging. The explicit modeling of projection noise is a key contribution, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are a few areas where the analysis could be further strengthened, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and answering the questions raised above.
