This paper addresses the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from English. The core idea is to model the noise inherent in projected tags using a “noise transformation” layer within a BiLSTM network, learning to trust projected data selectively. The authors claim three main contributions: 1) a characterization of projection noise as a learnable transformation, 2) integration of this model into a deep neural network for joint training, and 3) improved performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

I find the most significant contribution to be the *empirical demonstration* of improved performance on the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda datasets, exceeding existing state-of-the-art. While the idea of modeling projection noise isn't entirely novel (heuristics have been used for this purpose previously), demonstrating consistent gains on real-world low-resource languages is valuable. The simulation experiments, while useful for broader coverage, are less compelling as they rely on artificially creating a low-resource scenario. The inclusion of these experiments does, however, provide a more thorough evaluation. A secondary, although valuable, contribution is the visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices. These matrices offer some insight into *how* the model is handling projection errors, revealing tag confusions and instances where projected tags are effectively ignored.

However, this paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the proposed “noise transformation” feels like a relatively straightforward extension to existing neural sequence tagging models. It’s not a fundamentally new architectural component, and the mathematical formulation (Equation 2) is arguably more a re-parameterization than a breakthrough. The authors haven't convincingly demonstrated why this specific formulation is superior to alternative noise modeling approaches. A more thorough comparison against simpler noise reduction techniques (e.g., weighting projected tags based on alignment score) would strengthen the argument. Second, the ablation studies are insufficient. While the results in Table 1 show the impact of adding projected data, they don't dissect the contribution of the noise layer itself. What happens if the noise layer is included, but the 'A' matrix is initialized randomly and *not* learned? This would help isolate the impact of the learning process. Third, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. While the noise matrices offer some clues, a qualitative examination of mis-tagged examples, particularly those resulting from poor projections, would provide a deeper understanding of the model’s limitations. Finally, the paper's writing is occasionally verbose and repetitive.

Here are some questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the chosen formulation of the noise transformation layer (Equation 2)? Why is this approach preferable to, for example, simply weighting the projected tags based on alignment confidence?
2.  Please present results from an ablation study where the noise layer is included, but the 'A' matrix is not learned (initialized randomly). This will help isolate the contribution of the learning process.
3.  Can you provide a qualitative error analysis, showcasing examples of mis-tagged sentences and identifying the root cause of the errors (e.g., poor alignment, incorrect noise modeling)?
4.  The paper mentions that the BiLSTM models use 128 dimensional word embeddings and hidden layers. Were these hyperparameters tuned? If so, what search space was used and what were the results of tuning?
