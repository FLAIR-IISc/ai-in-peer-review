## Peer Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English).  The authors propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the "noise" introduced by projection via a learnable transformation matrix.  This allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected tags, jointly training on both gold-standard and projected data.  The paper demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, exceeding state-of-the-art results in several cases.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling:** The core contribution lies in explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection as a learnable transformation. This is a practical and insightful approach that moves beyond simply filtering or heuristically correcting projected labels. It allows the model to *learn* the systematic errors of projection.
2. **Effective Integration of Distant and Gold Supervision:** The paper successfully integrates noisy projected data with limited gold-standard data within a deep learning framework, demonstrating a path to effectively utilize both resources for improved POS tagging.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach and establishing new state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.


**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  The explicit modeling of projection noise via the transformation matrix is a novel and well-motivated idea.  This is a significant improvement over prior work that either ignores the noise or attempts to filter it based on heuristics. The idea of a “noise layer” is a strong conceptual contribution.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The results consistently demonstrate improvements over existing methods on both simulated and real datasets. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is a strong indicator of the method's effectiveness. The use of diverse evaluation sets (simulated *and* real) is commendable.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.  The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture. The related work section is comprehensive and provides good context.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to NLP research, particularly in the context of supporting low-resource languages. The proposed approach offers a practical solution for leveraging readily available parallel data.
5. **Analysis of Learned Noise Transformation:** The visualization of the learned transformation matrix (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insight into the types of errors the model is learning to correct.  This analysis strengthens the argument for the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Quality:** While the paper acknowledges the impact of word alignment quality on projection accuracy, it lacks a detailed analysis of *how* alignment errors affect performance.  A more thorough investigation of alignment quality metrics and their correlation with tagging accuracy would be beneficial.  Were different alignment tools explored?
2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper doesn’t detail a sensitivity analysis regarding key hyperparameters of the model (e.g., dimensionality of embeddings, hidden layers, learning rate). How robust is the performance to changes in these parameters? Reporting results with different settings would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Tagset Mismatch Handling:**  While the authors mention handling mismatched tagsets, the description is brief. A more detailed explanation of how the transformation matrix `A` is adapted for different tagset sizes and the potential challenges involved would be helpful. The model’s performance on languages with drastically different tagsets would also be interesting.
4. **Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training and inference compared to simpler baselines. The BiLSTM with noise layer is likely more computationally expensive.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between word alignment quality (e.g., precision/recall of alignments) and the performance of your model?  Were different alignment tools explored, and how did the choice of alignment tool impact results?
2.  Did you perform a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis?  If so, could you share the results and discuss the robustness of your model to changes in key hyperparameters?
3.  How does the performance of your model degrade when applied to language pairs with very different tagsets?  Could you provide results on such a scenario or discuss the limitations of your approach in this context?
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of your model compared to the baseline MaxEnt model or the BiLSTM without the noise layer?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential applications beyond POS tagging, highlighting the broader applicability of the noise modeling approach.
*   It would be interesting to investigate whether pre-training the noise transformation matrix `A` on a larger parallel corpus before fine-tuning on the specific low-resource language improves performance.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to POS tagging in low-resource languages. The explicit modeling of projection noise is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results are compelling.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
