This paper investigates low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, proposing a neural network model that learns to trust projected data based on a learned “noise transformation.” The core idea – explicitly modeling the discrepancies between tagsets and projection quality – is a valuable contribution. The experimental validation on both simulated and real low-resource languages, and the consistent improvements over strong baselines, strengthen the impact of this work. A further contribution is the comprehensive dataset setup and clear reporting of experimental results, facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation (matrix A) is the strongest aspect of this work. Previous approaches often treated projected data as simply noisy or relied on heuristics to filter it. This learned transformation allows the model to adapt to systematic biases in the projection process, leading to significant performance gains. The visualizations of the learned matrices (Figures 3 & 4) provide compelling evidence for the model’s ability to capture these relationships.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on both simulated and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The consistent outperformance of the proposed model over strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a), demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach. The 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda are particularly impressive.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Dataset:** The authors thoughtfully evaluate their approach using both simulated data (allowing for controlled experimentation) and real-world low-resource languages. Providing results on two actual low-resource languages increases the paper's practical relevance. The detailed description of the datasets, including parallel data sources and annotation details, enhances reproducibility.
4. **Clear Model Architecture and Explanation:** The model architecture, based on a bidirectional LSTM with a noise layer, is clearly explained and well-illustrated in Figure 2. The mathematical formulation of the noise transformation and the training objective are also presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Transformation:** While the visualization of the learned matrix A provides some intuition, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *what* the model is learning. For example, are specific types of errors consistently captured by the noise transformation? Are there systematic patterns in the learned relationships between tags? A more detailed analysis would strengthen the understanding of the model's behavior and provide insights into the types of projection errors it is most effectively addressing.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, such as the dimensionality of the embeddings and hidden layers, or the learning rate.  It’s important to demonstrate that the observed performance improvements are robust and not highly dependent on specific hyperparameter settings. A brief ablation study, or a discussion of hyperparameter tuning, would address this concern.
3. **Projection Quality Dependence:** Although the model attempts to *learn* the noise, the initial quality of the word alignments significantly impacts the performance. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't quantify the relationship between alignment accuracy and the effectiveness of the noise transformation.  Investigating how the model performs with varying levels of alignment quality would be valuable.
4. **Limited Discussion of Tagset Mismatch:** The paper mentions that the approach supports mismatching tagsets, but does not fully explore this capability. Presenting results with significantly different tagsets between the source and target languages would demonstrate the generality and robustness of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix A, identifying specific error patterns that the model is capturing? For example, are certain tag confusions consistently represented in the matrix?
2. How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameters? Did you perform any hyperparameter tuning, and if so, what was the tuning procedure?
3. How does the performance of the model vary with different levels of word alignment quality? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or filtering strategies?
4. Have you explored the model’s performance with more substantially different tagsets between the source and target languages? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style is slightly inconsistent.
* Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed model compared to the baselines.
* The phrase “clean annotated data” is used repeatedly; consider using “gold standard data” or a similar term for consistency.
