## Peer Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages, leveraging both small amounts of gold-standard annotated data and noisy data obtained via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to learn a "noise transformation" – a matrix that maps projected tags to their likely counterparts in the low-resource language – effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Modelling of Projection Noise:** The most significant contribution is the explicit modelling of the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection as a learnable transformation.  This moves beyond simply filtering noisy projections and allows the model to actively adapt to and compensate for systematic errors in the projection process.  This is a novel and potentially impactful approach.
2. **Empirical Validation on Real Low-Resource Languages:** The authors provide empirical evidence on two real low-resource languages, which is a valuable contribution, demonstrating the practicality and effectiveness of their approach. While cross-lingual transfer is often evaluated on simulated low-resource settings, validation on actual low-resource languages is crucial.
3. **Well-Designed Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully set up their experiments, using standard evaluation datasets and comparing against relevant baselines, including a strong baseline from Duong et al. (2014a), allowing for a fair and meaningful comparison.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The concept of a learnable noise transformation is a clever and insightful way to tackle the inherent inaccuracies of cross-lingual projection.  Rather than treating projected data as uniformly unreliable, the model learns to understand *how* it's wrong, leading to more effective utilization of this valuable resource.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results presented consistently show improvements over existing methods, both in simulated and real-world settings. Achieving state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is a significant achievement.  The detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insight into the types of errors being corrected.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the method is explained in a comprehensive manner, and the experimental setup is clearly described.  The inclusion of example projections (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the challenges the model addresses.
4. **Use of a Strong Baseline:** Comparison with the work of Duong et al. (2014a) is pertinent, as it's a widely recognized approach in the field. Consistently outperforming this baseline strengthens the authors’ claims.
5. **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing that parallel data quality varies and using Giza++ with one-to-one alignments, and acknowledging the challenge of many-to-many alignments demonstrates awareness of practical issues in cross-lingual transfer.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Transformation:** While the authors *show* the learned noise transformation matrices, there is limited in-depth analysis of *why* specific transformations are learned.  A more detailed discussion of the types of errors being corrected, and how these relate to linguistic differences between the source and target languages, would be beneficial.  For instance, how does syntactic divergence manifest in the matrix A?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide a detailed account of hyperparameter tuning, particularly for the noise transformation matrix (A). It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to the initialization and learning rate of this matrix.  A brief ablation study examining the impact of different initialization strategies for A would be valuable.
3. **Computational Cost:**  The introduction of the noise layer adds parameters to the model. While not explicitly stated, there's a potential increase in computational cost (training time, memory usage). A discussion of this trade-off between performance and computational complexity would be useful.
4. **Tagset Dependency:** While the use of the universal tagset is a good practice for comparability, the authors should briefly discuss how well their approach generalizes to different tagsets or cases where the target language uses a significantly different tagset structure.  Would a rectangular matrix A suffice in these scenarios?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the linguistic reasons behind the specific patterns observed in the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4)?  Are there particular types of errors (e.g., related to grammatical gender, verb tense) that the model consistently learns to correct?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the initialization and learning rate of the noise transformation matrix (A)? Did you experiment with different initialization strategies (e.g., identity matrix, random initialization)?
3.  Can you provide a rough estimate of the increase in training time and memory usage due to the addition of the noise layer?
4.  How well would your approach generalize to scenarios where the target language uses a tagset that is substantially different from the source language tagset (e.g., a highly morphologically-rich language with a large number of fine-grained tags)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is well-populated and includes relevant work.
*   The phrasing "Our work is the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations" is a strong claim and needs to be carefully justified. It's important to differentiate this work from other attempts to filter or correct projected data.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential future work, such as exploring different architectures for the noise layer or applying the approach to other NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual data for low-resource POS tagging.  The explicit modeling of projection noise is a significant contribution, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
