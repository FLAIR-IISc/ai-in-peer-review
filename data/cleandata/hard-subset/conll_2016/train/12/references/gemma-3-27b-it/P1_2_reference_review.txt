## Peer Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose a neural network model (BiLSTM) that explicitly models the noise inherent in projected data through a "noise transformation layer." This layer learns to map projected tags to more accurate low-resource tags, effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust the projected information.  The model is trained jointly on both annotated and projected data and demonstrates strong performance on simulated and real low-resource languages, outperforming existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer):**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The core contribution is the introduction of the noise transformation layer. This is a conceptually sound approach to address the inherent inaccuracies in cross-lingual projection and offers a principled way to integrate noisy data with limited gold-standard annotations.
2. **Empirical Validation on Real Low-Resource Languages:** The paper provides a robust empirical evaluation on two *actual* low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), alongside extensive simulations.  This is crucial as simulated data doesn’t always reflect the complexities of real-world scenarios.
3. **Strong Performance:**  The demonstrated performance gains over existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the real-world languages, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** Low-resource NLP is a significant area, and improving POS tagging is a fundamental step towards building more comprehensive NLP systems for these languages. The paper directly addresses a key bottleneck – leveraging noisy data effectively.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges of cross-lingual projection (word alignment errors, syntactic divergence) and provide a logical rationale for their proposed solution. The motivation for learning a noise transformation is convincing.
3. **Strong Experimental Design:** The experimental setup is thorough, including both simulations and real-world language evaluations.  The comparison against relevant baselines (including Duong et al. 2014a) is appropriate and strengthens the claims.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly, and the experiments are described in sufficient detail.  The inclusion of the noise transformation matrix visualizations is helpful.
5. **End-to-End Training:** The fact that the entire model can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation is a significant advantage, allowing for optimization across all components.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the BiLSTM Architecture:** While the noise transformation layer is novel, the underlying BiLSTM architecture is relatively standard. The paper doesn’t explore alternative neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) that might offer further performance improvements. This feels like a missed opportunity.
2. **Sensitivity Analysis of the Noise Layer:** The paper lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis of the noise transformation matrix (A).  How does the performance change with different initialization strategies for A?  How robust is the learned A across different language pairs?  Understanding these aspects would provide further insight into the model’s behavior.
3. **Data Scarcity Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge the limited size of the parallel corpora for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, the discussion of the impact of data quantity on the learned noise parameters could be more detailed.  Do the learned noise parameters generalize well to other low-resource languages with similar data constraints?
4. **Alignment Quality Concerns:** The reliance on Giza++ for word alignments is a potential limitation. Giza++ is known to produce errors.  While the authors filter alignments, the impact of remaining alignment errors on the overall performance isn’t explicitly analyzed.  Exploring alternative alignment methods or incorporating alignment confidence scores could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Ablation Study:** Could you provide an ablation study demonstrating the individual contributions of the noise layer and the BiLSTM architecture to the overall performance? Specifically, what performance is achieved by a BiLSTM trained *without* the noise layer, using only the projected data?
2. **Initialization of A:** What initialization strategy was used for the noise transformation matrix A?  Did you experiment with different initialization methods, and if so, how did they affect performance and training stability?
3. **Generalization of A:**  Do the learned noise transformation matrices (A) exhibit any patterns or similarities across different language pairs?  Could a pre-trained A matrix (trained on a set of language pairs) be effectively transferred to a new, unseen language pair?
4. **Alignment Error Analysis:** Could you quantify the impact of alignment errors on the performance of your model?  For example, by manually inspecting mis-tagged instances, can you identify cases where incorrect word alignments were the primary cause of error?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears incomplete (e.g., Ammar et al. 2016 is listed without full details).
*   Consider discussing the computational cost of the proposed model compared to simpler baselines.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
