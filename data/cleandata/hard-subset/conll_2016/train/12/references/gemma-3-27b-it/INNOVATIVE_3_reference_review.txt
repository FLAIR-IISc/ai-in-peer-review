This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection with a novel approach to handling the inherent noise. The central idea – learning a “noise transformation” to map projected tags to more accurate low-resource tags – is genuinely interesting and represents the core contribution. A secondary, but valuable contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages, convincingly demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method, exceeding current state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. Finally, the inclusion of visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides useful insights into the types of errors the model is correcting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of explicitly modelling the noise in cross-lingual projection as a learnable transformation is genuinely novel. Most prior work focuses on filtering or constraining projected data; this work intelligently *adapts* to it. This is a bold approach and a significant departure from standard practice.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy & Kinyarwanda) is a strong indication of the method's practical utility. The consistent gains across simulated languages further bolster these findings.
3. **Insightful Visualization:** The visualization of the noise transformation matrices is extremely valuable. It provides concrete evidence that the model is not just memorizing data but is learning meaningful relationships between noisy projected tags and correct low-resource tags. This strengthens the argument for the method's interpretability and effectiveness.
4. **Well-Defined Problem & Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing cross-lingual projection techniques. The motivation for the proposed approach is well-justified.
5. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of both simulated and real-world data, coupled with comparisons to relevant baselines (including a strong baseline from Duong et al.), provides a robust evaluation of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the visualization of the noise transformation matrices is helpful, a more detailed analysis of specific failure cases (i.e., instances where the model *still* makes errors after applying the transformation) would be valuable. Understanding the types of errors the model struggles with could inform future improvements.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide much discussion of hyperparameter tuning. A brief exploration of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension, size of the noise transformation matrix) would strengthen the analysis.
3. **Scalability Concerns:** The computational cost of learning the noise transformation matrix, particularly for languages with large tagsets, isn’t discussed. It’s unclear how well this approach would scale to languages with significantly more complex tagsets than those evaluated.
4. **Parallel Data Quality:** The paper briefly mentions the quality of the parallel data for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, but doesn’t provide a detailed assessment. The quality of the parallel data heavily influences the effectiveness of cross-lingual projection; a more thorough analysis of the data’s characteristics would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of specific failure cases, illustrating instances where the model incorrectly tags words despite applying the noise transformation?
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the size of the noise transformation matrix (A)?
3. Have you considered any techniques to improve the scalability of the noise transformation matrix for languages with very large tagsets?
4. Can you elaborate on the quality of the parallel data used for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, including any steps taken to filter or clean the data?
