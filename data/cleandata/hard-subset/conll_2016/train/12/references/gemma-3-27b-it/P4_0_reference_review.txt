This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold-standard annotations and noisy data projected from a high-resource language. The core idea is to learn a “noise transformation” to account for the inaccuracies inherent in cross-lingual projection, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their model on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating improvements over existing methods.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the explicit modeling of projection noise within a deep learning framework. Previous approaches often treated projected data as simply additional training examples, or attempted to filter out noisy projections with heuristics. This work, in contrast, learns a transformation matrix that captures the systematic differences between the tagsets and the types of errors introduced by projection. This is a novel and potentially impactful approach to leveraging cross-lingual resources for low-resource NLP. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation, including both simulated scenarios and real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Finally, the insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable understanding of the projection errors and how the model adapts to them.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a strong contribution. It goes beyond simply using projected data and actively addresses the challenges of cross-lingual transfer. The learned transformation matrices provide interpretable insights into the nature of projection errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The consistent improvement across different languages suggests the approach is robust.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough experimental setup, including both simulation and real-world data, strengthens the validity of the findings. Comparing against strong baselines (including Duong et al., 2014a) provides a clear benchmark.
4. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed method is well-motivated and logically presented, building on prior work in cross-lingual projection.
5. **Interpretability:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) offers valuable insights into the model’s behavior and the types of errors it is learning to correct.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The model introduces additional parameters (the noise transformation matrix) which increases complexity and potentially requires more careful hyperparameter tuning. The paper could benefit from discussing the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters related to this noise layer.
2. **Data Preprocessing and Alignment Quality:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of word alignments generated by Giza++. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t thoroughly discuss the potential impact of alignment errors on the results. Exploring the correlation between alignment quality and performance could be insightful.
3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper provides some analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, it lacks a detailed investigation of the types of errors the model still makes. Examining specific failure cases could reveal further areas for improvement.
4. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address the computational cost of training and inference compared to simpler baselines. While the results are promising, understanding the computational overhead is important for practical adoption.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the initialization and regularization of the noise transformation matrix A?
2. Did you experiment with different alignment tools or techniques for improving alignment quality, and if so, how did this impact performance?
3. Can you provide examples of specific tagging errors the model still makes, and explain why the noise transformation matrix might not be sufficient to correct them?
4. What is the computational cost (training time and inference time) of the proposed model compared to the baseline methods, particularly the MaxEnt classifier?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be more consistent throughout the paper.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used in the experiments.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in cross-lingual transfer learning.
