This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging, leveraging cross-lingual projection from English. The core idea – learning a “noise transformation” to explicitly model and compensate for errors inherent in projected data – is genuinely novel and represents a promising direction. While the experimental setup relies on established benchmarks and techniques, the proposed approach offers a compelling alternative to simply filtering or weighting projected labels. The fact that the model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art, on both simulated and real low-resource languages, reinforces this positive assessment. The empirical results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly encouraging.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The introduction of a learned “noise layer” to model the transformation between projected and ground-truth tags is the strongest contribution. This moves beyond treating projected data as simply noisy examples and instead attempts to *understand* and *correct* for the nature of that noise.
2. **Effective Integration of Distant Supervision:**  The paper demonstrates a successful method for integrating distant supervision from cross-lingual projection with limited annotated data within a deep learning framework. The joint training approach clearly yields benefits.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The consistently strong performance across multiple languages and datasets provides robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Idea & Conceptual Clarity:** The noise transformation layer is a conceptually clean and elegant solution to a known problem.  It's not just a tweak to an existing model; it fundamentally alters how projected data is treated.
2. **Consistent Performance Gains:**  The paper demonstrates clear and consistent performance gains over strong baselines, lending credence to the efficacy of the proposed method. The results aren’t marginal; they are significant.
3. **Well-Established Experimental Setup:** Utilizing established datasets and evaluation methodologies, alongside comparison with key prior work (Duong et al., 2014a), makes the results easily interpretable and comparable.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the model architecture is clearly explained with the help of the illustrative figure. The motivation and contribution are very clear. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Matrix:** While the paper *shows* the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4), it lacks a deeper analysis of what these matrices reveal about the nature of the projection errors. More detailed interpretation of these learned transformations—what types of errors are being corrected, for which languages, and why—would strengthen the paper considerably.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters, particularly those related to the noise transformation layer. Are the results stable across different initializations or learning rates? A brief ablation study would be helpful.
3. **Computational Cost:** The addition of the noise layer introduces additional parameters and computational complexity. A discussion of the practical implications of this increased complexity (training time, memory usage) would be beneficial.
4. **Projection Quality Dependency:** While the model *handles* noisy projections, its performance is still fundamentally limited by the quality of the initial word alignments. A brief discussion of the impact of alignment quality on the final results would be insightful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed qualitative analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices? What specific types of projection errors are being corrected for different language pairs?
2. How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings for the noise transformation layer (e.g., the learning rate, regularization)?
3. What is the computational overhead introduced by the noise transformation layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model without this layer?
4. How does the performance of your model degrade as the quality of the word alignments decreases (e.g., using alignments generated with different alignment tools or with different parameter settings)?
