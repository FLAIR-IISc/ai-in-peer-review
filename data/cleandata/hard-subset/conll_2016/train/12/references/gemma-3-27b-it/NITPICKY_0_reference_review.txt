This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both gold annotations and noisy data projected from a high-resource language. The core idea is to learn a “noise transformation” to model the discrepancies between projected and actual tags, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their model on simulated low-resource scenarios and two real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating improvements over existing methods.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the *explicit* modeling of projection noise within a neural network framework. While cross-lingual projection is a well-established technique, directly accounting for the inevitable inaccuracies through a learnable transformation is a reasonable approach. The empirical results, particularly on the real-world languages, suggest this modeling provides a tangible benefit. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to several baselines and state-of-the-art methods across a variety of languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Motivation & Problem Setting:** The problem of low-resource POS tagging is well-defined and significant. The reliance on distant supervision via cross-lingual projection is a practical approach, but the inherent noise in this process necessitates methods for mitigating its impact, as this work attempts to do.
2. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation (matrix A) is a novel aspect. This allows the model to adapt to the specific errors introduced by the projection process, rather than treating all projected data equally. The visualization of the learned ‘A’ matrix (Figures 3 & 4) offers valuable insights into the types of errors the model is learning to correct.
3. **Competitive Empirical Performance:** The model achieves competitive results on both simulated and real-world datasets, exceeding the performance of several strong baselines. The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly encouraging.
4. **Clear Presentation & Structure:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The framework and methodology are clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Justification for BiLSTM Architecture:** The choice of a BiLSTM is presented without strong justification. While BiLSTMs are commonly used in sequence tagging, the paper doesn’t provide a compelling argument for *why* this architecture is particularly well-suited to modeling projection noise compared to, say, a simpler feedforward network or a conditional random field.
2. **Limited Analysis of Learned Noise Transformation:** While the visualization of the 'A' matrix is helpful, a more detailed analysis of *what* the model is learning would be valuable. For example, are specific types of alignment errors consistently reflected in the matrix? Are there discernible patterns related to linguistic differences between the source and target languages? The authors state "strong diagonal", but quantify this observation. What percentage of the off-diagonal elements are significant? Without this deeper analysis, the learned transformation remains somewhat of a "black box."
3. **Overstated Claims of Novelty:** The claim of being the "first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations" is slightly misleading. While this *specific* approach of learning a transformation matrix is novel, previous work has certainly addressed projection noise through heuristics, filtering, or joint training strategies. The paper should more accurately position itself within the broader landscape of research on cross-lingual projection.
4. **Data Preprocessing Details Omitted:** The paper states they used standard Stanford POS tagger, but lacks detail regarding specific versions or parameters. The impact of these choices isn't clear. Similar concerns apply to the Giza++ alignment process – specific settings and filtering parameters are not described. Reproducibility is somewhat hampered by these omissions.
5. **Minor Formatting Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in the use of italics and boldface. Some citations are also incomplete. (e.g. “Ammar et al\.,? 2016, Ammar et al\., ” )

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned transformation matrix 'A', including quantifiable metrics regarding the magnitude and distribution of off-diagonal elements?
2.  What experiments were conducted to justify the choice of a BiLSTM architecture over alternative models?
3.  Please clarify the specific versions and parameters used for the Stanford POS tagger and Giza++ alignment process.
4.  Can you elaborate on how the two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) affects the learned noise transformation matrix 'A'?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous ablation study to assess the contribution of the noise layer. Specifically, it would be helpful to compare the performance of the full model with a variant that lacks the noise layer but is otherwise identical. Additionally, the discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in neural machine translation and cross-lingual transfer learning. The paper’s overall quality is high, but the relatively numerous minor issues, and lack of deeper analysis, prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance. I lean towards **rejection** pending substantial revisions.
