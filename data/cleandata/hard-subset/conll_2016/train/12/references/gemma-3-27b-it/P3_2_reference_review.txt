## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors identify the inherent noise in projected data and propose a novel neural network model (BiLSTM with a “noise layer”) designed to learn when and how much to trust the projected tags. The model jointly trains on both gold-standard and distant supervision data, explicitly modelling the transformation between tagsets.  The results on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling for Projected Data:** The core contribution is the introduction of the “noise layer” – a trainable transformation matrix that explicitly models the discrepancies between projected and ground truth tags. This is a nuanced and effective approach to handling the inherent inaccuracies of cross-lingual projection.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance gains across eight simulated low-resource languages and on two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), outperforming established baselines including Duong et al. (2014a). The gains on the real-world languages are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Well-motivated and Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is thorough, utilizing both simulated low-resource scenarios and real-world languages. The comparison against strong baselines and detailed ablation studies contribute to the credibility of the results.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The proposed “noise layer” is a clever and intuitive solution to the problem of noisy projected data. It goes beyond simply filtering or weighting projected tags and directly models the transformation process, allowing the model to learn from the projection errors.  This represents a significant step forward in effectively utilizing distant supervision in low-resource scenarios.
2. **Strong Performance and Consistent Improvements:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistently better performance compared to strong baselines validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly impressive given the limited resources available.
3. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the key findings.
4. **Thorough Related Work Discussion:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work in low-resource NLP, cross-lingual transfer, and distant supervision. They effectively position their work within the existing literature and highlight the novel contributions.
5. **Analysis of the Learned Noise Transformation:** The visualization of the learned transformation matrix A (Figures 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of errors made during cross-lingual projection and how the model learns to compensate for them.  This analysis enhances the understanding of the model’s behavior.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Alignment Quality:** While the authors acknowledge the impact of word alignment errors, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the correlation between alignment quality and tagging performance. Investigating the performance of the model with different alignment schemes or filtering strategies would strengthen the findings. Did the model perform better when alignments were higher confidence?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't report a comprehensive hyperparameter search.  Reporting sensitivity analysis regarding the learning rate, embedding size, or the size of the LSTM hidden layers could help demonstrate the robustness of the results.
3. **Computational Complexity:** The introduction of the noise layer adds parameters to the model. The authors do not explicitly address the computational cost of training and inference with the added complexity, or perform a comparison of inference speed to simpler models. 
4. **Tagset Mismatch Handling:** Although mentioned, the handling of tagset mismatches is somewhat glossed over. The rectangular matrix A is introduced, but there's little discussion of how this impacts performance or how the authors determined the mapping between tagsets. More detail here would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between word alignment quality (e.g., using alignment confidence scores) and the performance of your model? Specifically, did the model perform better with higher quality alignments?
2.  What was the computational overhead introduced by the noise layer in terms of training time and inference speed compared to a BiLSTM model without the noise layer?
3.   When dealing with tagset mismatches and using the rectangular matrix A, how was the mapping between the two tagsets determined? Was this done manually, automatically, or using a combination of both? If automated, what method was used?
4.   The results show a strong performance improvement, especially on Malagasy. Given the relatively small size of the parallel corpus for Kinyarwanda, do you think further improvements could be achieved with a larger Kinyarwanda parallel corpus, and if so, what would be the expected magnitude of these improvements?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the web-sourced collection of parallel texts for Malagasy would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions, such as extending the approach to other NLP tasks or exploring more sophisticated noise modeling techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in low-resource NLP. The proposed method is novel, effective, and supported by compelling experimental results. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
