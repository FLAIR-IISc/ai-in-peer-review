### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy, distantly-supervised data. The noisy data is generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core contribution is a neural sequence tagging model (a BiLSTM) augmented with a "noise layer." This layer is a trainable KxK matrix that explicitly models the transformation (or confusion) between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the observed noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data, using a combined loss function. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling distant supervision noise.** The primary contribution is the "noise layer," a trainable linear transformation matrix that learns the systematic errors introduced by the cross-lingual projection process. This allows the model to effectively de-noise the training signal from the large, projected dataset during backpropagation, integrating it cleanly into an end-to-end neural architecture.
2.  **Strong empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on ten different languages, including two genuinely low-resource ones. The proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines and prior state-of-the-art results, demonstrating its practical utility and effectiveness.
3.  **Analysis and interpretability of projection noise.** By visualizing the learned noise matrix, the authors provide valuable insights into the specific types of errors and confusions that arise from cross-lingual projection for different language pairs, adding an analytical dimension to the work.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and well-motivated model.** The idea of explicitly modeling the noise as a learnable transformation matrix is intuitive and elegant. It avoids complex pre-processing heuristics for cleaning the projected data and instead integrates the noise-handling mechanism directly into the learning objective. The shared BiLSTM layers for both data sources is a natural way to ensure the core tagger benefits from all available data.
2.  **Impressive and convincing experimental results.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda and outperforms a strong baseline (`Duong et al., 2014a`) across eight simulated languages. The inclusion of several baselines (BiLSTM on annotated, projected, and their union) clearly demonstrates that the performance gain comes from the proposed noise modeling technique, not just from using a BiLSTM or more data.
3.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is explained well with a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experimental setup is sound. The analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) is a great addition that helps the reader understand what the model is learning.
4.  **High potential for broader impact.** Although applied to POS tagging via cross-lingual projection, the proposed noise layer is a general technique. It could be readily applied to other tasks and other forms of distant or weak supervision (e.g., learning from crowd-sourced labels with varying annotator quality, or rule-based heuristics) where systematic noise is expected.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited discussion of related work on learning from noisy labels.** The idea of using a confusion matrix to model label noise is a well-established concept in the broader machine learning literature, particularly in areas like learning from crowdsourcing or dealing with noisy datasets. The paper frames the noise layer as a novel contribution to cross-lingual projection but would be strengthened by situating it within this wider context and discussing how it relates to or differs from prior work on noise-robust learning.
2.  **The noise model's assumptions may be overly simplistic.** The model uses a single, static transformation matrix `A`. This assumes that the noise process is stationary and independent of the specific token or its context. In reality, projection errors can be highly context-dependent. For example, a common English word with multiple senses might be more prone to projection errors than a monosemous one. The paper does not discuss this limitation.
3.  **Lack of ablation on the two-stage training process.** The authors state that they first pre-train the network on the clean data and then train jointly, because training from scratch is "impractical." This is a critical implementation detail that warrants empirical justification. An ablation study comparing this two-stage approach to joint training from a random initialization would quantify the importance of this pre-training step and help understand the optimization challenges. It would also be interesting to see how performance degrades as the amount of pre-training data is reduced.
4.  **Unclear handling of unaligned/multi-aligned tokens.** The paper states that for tokens aligned to multiple words or NULL, a distribution based on tag frequencies in the English sentence is used. This seems like a very weak and potentially uninformative signal. It would be beneficial to know what percentage of projected tokens fall into this category and whether this strategy is better than simply ignoring these tokens.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your proposed noise layer and prior work on modeling label noise using confusion/transition matrices in other domains, such as learning from multiple annotators?
2.  The two-stage training process seems crucial to your model's success. Could you provide results for a model trained jointly from a random initialization without pre-training on the gold data first? This would help clarify whether the pre-training is essential for stable learning or simply provides a better starting point.
3.  Your noise model assumes a global, token-independent noise distribution. Have you considered or explored models where the noise transformation could be conditioned on lexical or contextual features (e.g., a dynamic `A` matrix for each token)? Do you have any intuition on whether this added complexity would be beneficial given the low-resource setting?
