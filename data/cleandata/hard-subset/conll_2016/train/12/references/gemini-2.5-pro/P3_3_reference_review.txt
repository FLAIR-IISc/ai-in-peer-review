### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger augmented with a novel "noise layer." This layer is a trainable transformation matrix that explicitly models the systematic errors and confusion patterns between the model's "clean" internal predictions and the noisy projected labels. The model is trained jointly with a two-part objective function, one for the clean annotated data and one for the noisy projected data. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective method for learning from distant supervision:** The core contribution is the "noise layer," an elegant mechanism to explicitly model label noise. This approach allows the model to learn a de-noising function directly from the data, rather than relying on heuristics to filter the noisy corpus beforehand. This idea is generalizable to other tasks and sources of distant supervision.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results for low-resource POS tagging:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation, showing that the proposed model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a), across all ten languages tested. The strong performance on real low-resource languages like Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is particularly compelling.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The central idea of adding a trainable linear transformation (the matrix `A`) to model the noise distribution is simple, intuitive, and effective. It directly addresses the core problem of distant supervision: the labels are useful but not entirely trustworthy. Instead of treating projected labels as hard targets, the model learns a confusion matrix that captures the probabilistic relationship between the true tags and the projected ones. This is a more principled approach than simply mixing clean and noisy data or using complex pre-processing heuristics.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup is rigorous and convincing. The authors evaluate on a large set of languages, including both simulated (8 European languages) and real-world (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) low-resource scenarios. Crucially, they compare against the previous state-of-the-art on the exact same data splits, making the claims of improvement highly credible. The inclusion of multiple baselines, including naive uses of the projected data with a standard BiLSTM, clearly demonstrates the value added by the proposed noise layer.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper goes beyond reporting accuracy scores by visualizing the learned noise matrices (`A`) for the different languages. This analysis provides valuable insight into what the model learns. For instance, the strong diagonals confirm that projected labels are often correct, while the off-diagonal weights highlight systematic cross-lingual divergences or alignment errors (e.g., PRT/X confusion in Swedish). This interpretability strengthens the paper's claims and provides a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed model is explained clearly with a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Noise Model:** While effective, the noise layer is a simple linear transformation applied to the output distribution. It models the noise process as a static, context-independent confusion matrix. This assumption may be too simplistic, as projection errors can be highly context-dependent. For example, the likelihood of an English verb being projected onto a Malagasy noun might depend on the surrounding words. The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore more expressive, context-aware noise models (e.g., conditioning the transformation on the BiLSTM's hidden state).

2.  **Lack of Ablation on the Training Strategy:** The authors state that pre-training the BiLSTM on the small annotated dataset is necessary before joint training, as starting from a random initialization is "impractical." This is a critical implementation detail that is not experimentally verified. An ablation study is needed to quantify the impact of this pre-training step. How much does performance drop without it? This is important for understanding the model's robustness, especially in extremely low-resource settings where the initial annotated set may be too small to yield a good initialization.

3.  **Unjustified Choice for Handling Noisy Alignments:** The paper describes a specific strategy for handling tokens that are unaligned or aligned to multiple words: assigning a sentence-level tag frequency distribution. This seems to be a weak and potentially noisy signal. Alternative strategies, such as assigning a special `UNKNOWN` tag, using a global background distribution, or simply ignoring these tokens during the noisy-data training phase, are not discussed or compared. The impact and justification for this particular design choice are unclear.

4.  **Insufficient Discussion of Broader Related Work on Noise Modeling:** The related work section focuses well on low-resource POS tagging but could be strengthened by positioning the "noise layer" within the broader literature on learning from noisy labels. Techniques like label correction models, modeling label uncertainty with latent variables, or curriculum learning have been explored for similar problems. A brief discussion of how this work relates to or differs from these more general paradigms would better situate the paper's contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you quantify the importance of the two-stage training process? What is the performance if you skip the pre-training phase on the annotated data and train the full model jointly from a random initialization? This would help clarify whether the pre-training is a crucial component or simply a helpful optimization.

2.  The noise model `A` is static and context-independent. Have you considered a dynamic noise model where the transformation is conditioned on the local context (e.g., the BiLSTM hidden state `h_t`)? Do you believe this could capture more complex syntactic divergences and lead to further improvements?

3.  Your method for handling unaligned tokens is to use a sentence-level tag distribution. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice over simpler alternatives, such as ignoring these tokens or using a uniform distribution? Have you experimented with these alternatives?
