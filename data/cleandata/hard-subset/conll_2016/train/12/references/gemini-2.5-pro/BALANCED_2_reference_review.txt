### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging that effectively combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a large amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The model is based on a standard BiLSTM architecture but is augmented with a novel "noise layer." This layer, a trainable linear transformation matrix, is designed to explicitly model the systematic errors and transformations between the true tag distribution predicted by the core model and the noisy projected tag distribution. The model is trained jointly using a composite loss function, with the gold data supervising the base BiLSTM directly and the projected data supervising the BiLSTM through the noise layer. This allows the model to learn to "de-noise" the supervisory signal from the projected data. The authors conduct extensive experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating that their approach consistently outperforms strong baselines and achieves new state-of-the-art results.

### Main Contributions
1.  **An explicit and learnable noise-modeling mechanism for distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a simple yet powerful "noise layer," which is a linear transformation that explicitly models the confusion patterns between clean and noisy labels. This allows the model to learn how to interpret and leverage noisy data from cross-lingual projection in a principled manner, rather than treating noisy labels as ground truth or relying on heuristics for data filtering. This is a generalizable approach for learning from noisy supervision.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a robust and comprehensive evaluation across ten languages. The proposed model sets a new state-of-the-art on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda and consistently outperforms the prior state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a) on simulated low-resource settings. The strong empirical results validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and well-motivated model.** The core idea of adding an explicit noise transformation layer is intuitive, simple, and directly addresses the known problem of systematic noise in cross-lingual projection. By learning the noise distribution itself, the model can more effectively exploit the signal in the large but imperfect projected corpus. The formulation of the joint training objective is clear and theoretically sound.
2.  **Thorough and convincing evaluation.** The experimental setup is strong. By evaluating on both simulated (8 languages) and genuine (2 languages) low-resource settings, the authors make a compelling case for the model's effectiveness and robustness. The comparison against a comprehensive set of baselines, including previous state-of-the-art work on the same data splits, is fair and clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach.
3.  **Excellent analysis and interpretability.** The paper provides valuable insights by visualizing and analyzing the learned noise transformation matrices (A). These visualizations (Figures 3 and 4) offer concrete evidence that the model is capturing meaningful cross-lingual divergences and alignment errors (e.g., confusion between PRT and X in Swedish, greater noise in Kinyarwanda). This analysis adds a layer of interpretability that strengthens the paper's claims and helps the reader understand what the model has learned.
4.  **High potential for broader impact.** Although the application is POS tagging, the proposed technique for handling noisy labels is task-agnostic. The authors rightly note its potential applicability to other tasks that rely on distant supervision (e.g., relation extraction) or other forms of noisy labels like crowdsourced annotations. This generality significantly increases the potential impact of the work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited novelty of the core architecture.** The base model is a standard BiLSTM, which is not novel. The key contribution is the noise layer, which is a single linear transformation. While effective, the idea of modeling label confusion with a transition matrix is not entirely new in the broader machine learning literature on learning with noisy labels. The paper would be stronger if it better contextualized its noise modeling approach within this existing literature, drawing clearer distinctions and connections.
2.  **Potential model instability and lack of hyperparameter analysis.** The authors state that a two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data first) is "impractical" to avoid and necessary for good performance. This suggests the joint training objective may be difficult to optimize or sensitive to initialization. An ablation study quantifying the impact of this pre-training step is missing but would be very informative. Furthermore, the paper does not mention any weighting between the two loss terms in the objective function. It is a critical detail whether they are equally weighted or if a hyperparameter is used to balance the influence of the small gold set and the large noisy set.
3.  **Unclear handling of projection data.** There is a contradiction in the description of how projected data is created. Section 3.1 states that for tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments, a sentence-level tag distribution is used. This seems like a weak heuristic. However, Section 4.1.2 later states that, following prior work, "only one-to-one alignments" are retained. This needs clarification. If only 1-to-1 alignments are used, a significant portion of the parallel data may be discarded, which is an important detail about the method's data efficiency.
4.  **The comparison with the main baseline could be more direct.** The paper correctly identifies Duong et al. (2014a) as the main competitor. Their method uses projected tags as features within a joint model. The performance gain of the proposed model could be due to the stronger BiLSTM backbone, the noise modeling strategy, or both. A more direct comparison, such as a BiLSTM baseline that uses projected tags as features (mimicking the spirit of Duong et al.'s approach), would help to better isolate and quantify the specific contribution of the proposed noise layer versus the architectural improvements.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the procedure for generating the projected training data? Specifically, do you use the sentence-level tag distribution for non-1-to-1 alignments as described in Section 3.1, or are these tokens discarded in favor of only 1-to-1 alignments as mentioned in Section 4.1.2? If the latter, what proportion of tokens from the parallel corpus are ultimately used for training?
2.  The two-stage training process is mentioned as a practical necessity. Could you provide results for a model trained jointly from a random initialization to quantify the performance degradation and confirm the importance of the pre-training step?
3.  Regarding the joint loss function, are the cross-entropy terms for the gold and projected data given equal weight? Have you experimented with a hyperparameter to balance their contributions, and if so, how sensitive are the results to this choice?
4.  To better disentangle the benefits of the BiLSTM architecture from your noise modeling approach, have you considered a baseline that uses the BiLSTM but incorporates projected tags as features (e.g., by concatenating a projected tag embedding to the word embedding), which would more closely parallel the feature-based approach of Duong et al. (2014a)?
