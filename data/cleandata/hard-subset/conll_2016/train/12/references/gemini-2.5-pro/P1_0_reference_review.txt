### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural sequence tagging model (a BiLSTM) augmented with a novel "noise layer". This layer is a linear transformation, parameterized by a learnable matrix `A`, that explicitly models the systematic errors and discrepancies between the model's internal predictions and the noisy projected tags. The entire model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel and elegant method for integrating distant supervision:** The primary contribution is the "noise layer," which provides an explicit, learnable mechanism to model the noise distribution of a distant supervision source (projected POS tags) within an end-to-end neural model. This is a simple but powerful idea that allows the model to learn to "de-noise" the training signal from the projected data during backpropagation.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across ten languages, including eight simulated low-resource languages from Europarl and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The proposed model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a) on the same data splits, demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and intuitive model:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise from distant supervision is very well-motivated. The paper convincingly shows that naively mixing clean and noisy data (`BiLSTM Ann+Proj` baseline) can degrade performance. The proposed noise layer is an elegant solution that directly addresses this problem with a minimal number of additional parameters (a K×K matrix, where K is the tagset size).
2.  **Rigorous and extensive evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. Using both simulated low-resource settings (which allows for controlled experiments and direct comparison to prior work) and genuine low-resource languages (which demonstrates real-world applicability) is commendable. The choice of baselines is solid, including ablations of their own model and the relevant state-of-the-art system.
3.  **State-of-the-art results:** The method achieves new state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda and consistently outperforms the strong baseline of Duong et al. (2014a) across all eight simulated languages. The gains, while sometimes modest, are uniform and demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
4.  **Good analysis and interpretability:** The authors include visualizations of the learned noise matrix `A` for all languages (Figures 3 and 4). This analysis provides valuable insight into what the model is learning about the projection process. For instance, the strong diagonal indicates that many projections are trusted, while specific off-diagonal entries reveal systematic confusions (e.g., PRT/X in Swedish). This adds a layer of interpretability often missing in neural models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplicity of the projection method:** The method used to generate the noisy labels is quite standard and potentially suboptimal. The authors use Giza++ and retain only one-to-one alignments, discarding a significant amount of potential signal from many-to-one or one-to-many alignments. For unaligned tokens, they fall back to a sentence-level tag distribution. While the noise layer is designed to handle this noise, it is possible that a more sophisticated projection strategy could provide a better starting point and lead to even stronger results. The work focuses on modeling the noise, but not on minimizing it at the source.
2.  **Unclear handling of label distributions in the loss function:** In Section 3.1, the authors state that for tokens with multiple or no alignments, they "assign a distribution over tags". However, the loss function for the projected data in Section 3.2, `-<y_tilde, log softmax(A*o)>`, typically assumes `y_tilde` is a one-hot vector (standard cross-entropy). If `y_tilde` is a soft probability distribution, this becomes a KL-divergence minimization, which is fine, but this detail is not explicitly stated. Clarifying how these distributional labels are practically incorporated would improve the paper's clarity.
3.  **Missing a key feature-based baseline:** A common and effective way to incorporate information like projected tags is to use them as input features (e.g., via a "projected tag embedding" concatenated with the word embedding). This would be a simpler alternative to the proposed architecture. Without comparing against such a baseline, it is difficult to ascertain whether the complexity of the noise output layer is strictly necessary over a simpler feature-based integration of the projected information. The provided `BiLSTM Ann+Proj` baseline is arguably a weak way to combine the two data sources.
4.  **Lack of ablation on the two-stage training process:** The authors state that pre-training the model on the small gold dataset is necessary to avoid poor local optima, after which joint training is performed. This is a plausible heuristic, but its necessity is not experimentally justified. An ablation study showing the performance of a model trained jointly from scratch would strengthen this claim and provide a clearer picture of the model's training dynamics and sensitivity to initialization.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the treatment of unaligned or multi-aligned words, could you please clarify how the "distribution over tags" is used as the target label `y_tilde` in the cross-entropy objective? Is `y_tilde` treated as a soft label distribution in these cases?
2.  Have you considered an alternative baseline where the projected tag is provided as an input feature to the BiLSTM (e.g., via a learned embedding for each projected tag, concatenated with the word embedding)? This seems like a strong and simple alternative for integrating the projected information. How do you think your noise-layer approach would compare?
3.  The two-stage training procedure (pre-training on gold data first) is mentioned as a practical necessity. Could you comment on what happens if the model is trained jointly on both data sources from a random initialization? Does performance degrade significantly, and if so, by how much?
