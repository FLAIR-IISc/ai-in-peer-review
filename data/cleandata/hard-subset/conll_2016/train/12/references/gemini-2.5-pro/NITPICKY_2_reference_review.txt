**Summary of the paper:**
This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data and a larger, noisy dataset created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core proposal is a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that incorporates an additional linear transformation layer, termed a "noise layer". This layer, parameterized by a confusion matrix A, is trained to explicitly model the systematic errors and transformations between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both the small clean corpus (optimizing the BiLSTM's output directly) and the large noisy corpus (optimizing the BiLSTM's output after passing through the noise layer). The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), claiming state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a simple yet effective mechanism to explicitly model the transformation between clean and noisy labels within a neural network. This allows for joint training on a small gold dataset and a large, distantly supervised dataset by learning a task-specific confusion matrix.
2.  A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation of this method. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art model, across a wide range of simulated and two real-world low-resource settings.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Effective and Intuitive Model:** The central idea of adding a trainable transformation matrix to model label noise is elegant and intuitive. It directly addresses the known problem of systematic errors in cross-lingual projection without resorting to complex heuristics for data filtering. The strong performance gains shown in Tables 1 and 2 validate that this simple addition is highly effective.
2.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. Evaluating on eight simulated languages allows for controlled comparison with prior work and demonstrates the general applicability of the method. Furthermore, testing on two genuine low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, grounds the work in a realistic application scenario and shows its practical utility.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization and analysis of the learned noise matrix A in Figures 3 and 4 are a valuable addition. They provide qualitative evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful and language-specific noise patterns (e.g., the higher degree of noise in Kinyarwanda compared to Malagasy), which strengthens the authors' claims and provides insight into the nature of projection errors.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** The paper suffers from a significant lack of detail regarding the experimental setup, hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Hyperparameters:** The authors only specify word embedding/hidden layer dimensions (128) and a learning rate (1.0). Critical details are missing: the specific optimizer used (SGD is mentioned, but what about momentum?), batch size, dropout rates, initialization strategy for the noise matrix A, and the number of training epochs for pre-training and joint training stages.
    *   **Data Availability:** The Kinyarwanda parallel data is described as having been "provided directly by Noah Smith." This makes the Kinyarwanda experiment completely unreproducible for the research community. A public link or repository for this data is necessary.
    *   **Projection Heuristics:** The method for handling tokens with non-one-to-one alignments (assigning a distribution based on the *entire English sentence's tag frequencies*) is highly questionable and poorly justified. This heuristic seems likely to introduce uniformative noise, as the tag distribution of a whole sentence is a very weak prior for a specific unaligned word. The authors should have explored and compared this against other, more plausible heuristics (e.g., using a background unigram distribution from the corpus, or simply marking them with a special `UNK` tag).

2.  **Inconsistent Claims and Descriptions:** There is a notable contradiction in the paper's description of the training process. The introduction states the model "can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation." However, the end of Section 3.2 explicitly states that direct training is "impractical" and that a two-stage process is used: pre-training on clean data, followed by joint training. This is not end-to-end training. This inconsistency should be clarified; the model is fine-tuned, not trained end-to-end from a random initialization.

3.  **Poor Formatting and Lack of Professionalism:** The paper is marred by significant formatting issues, most egregiously in the References section. The bibliography is riddled with raw BibTeX artifacts (`et al\.,?`, `\Q2016\E`), duplicate entries (`Duong et al., 2014a` and `2014b` point to the same malformed entry), and inconsistent formatting. This demonstrates a severe lack of attention to detail and makes the manuscript appear rushed and unprofessional. Minor inconsistencies also exist in the main text, such as the use of "cross lingual" and "cross-lingual". While these are minor points individually, their sheer number detracts from the overall quality of the submission.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you please provide a full account of all hyperparameters and training details necessary to reproduce your BiLSTM experiments? This includes optimizer details, batch size, dropout, initialization of the noise matrix A, and convergence criteria or number of epochs for both training stages.
2.  Can you provide a more thorough justification for your heuristic of using the entire source sentence's tag distribution for unaligned or multiply-aligned words? Did you experiment with alternative, more localized heuristics, and if so, how did they compare?
3.  Regarding the Kinyarwanda parallel corpus, can you make this data publicly available to ensure the reproducibility of your results?
4.  Could you clarify the description of your training procedure? Do you consider a two-stage pre-training and fine-tuning process to be "end-to-end"? Would it not be more accurate to describe it as a fine-tuning approach?
