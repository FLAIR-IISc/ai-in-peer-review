### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a much larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The primary contribution is a novel and elegant method for handling noisy labels from distant supervision. Instead of relying on heuristics to filter the noise, the authors propose explicitly modeling it with a dedicated "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation. This layer learns a confusion matrix that maps the model's 'clean' internal predictions to the observed noisy projected labels, allowing the model to effectively learn from the noisy data by "de-noising" the error signal during backpropagation. This is a significant conceptual advance for utilizing distant supervision.
2.  A secondary but important contribution is the successful application of this method to low-resource POS tagging. The authors integrate their noise layer into a standard BiLSTM tagger and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a comprehensive set of 8 simulated and 2 real-world low-resource languages, outperforming strong baselines and previous specialized methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's core idea is its greatest strength. Moving away from heuristic data filtering or treating projected tags as simple features, the authors reframe the problem as one of explicitly learning the noise distribution. The concept of a learnable transformation matrix that models the systematic errors of a distant supervision source is a bold, principled, and highly generalizable idea. This approach has the potential for significant impact beyond POS tagging, in any domain where large-scale but noisy labeled data is available.

2.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The implementation of the core idea is remarkably simple—a single KxK matrix added to an otherwise standard neural architecture. This simplicity makes the method easy to understand, implement, and integrate into other models. The fact that such a simple mechanism yields substantial and consistent performance gains across ten languages is a powerful testament to the validity of the underlying concept.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental setup is convincing. The authors show consistent improvements not just over simple baselines (like training on annotated, projected, or combined data) but also over the previous state-of-the-art method specifically designed for this task (Duong et al., 2014a). The evaluation on both simulated settings (allowing for controlled comparison) and two real, challenging low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrates the robustness and practical utility of the proposed model.

4.  **Interpretability:** A welcome feature of the model is the interpretability of the learned noise matrix `A`. The visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 provide fascinating insights into the specific types of confusion that arise from projection for different language pairs (e.g., the PRT/X confusion in Swedish, or the greater overall noise in Kinyarwanda vs. Malagasy). This transparency helps build confidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful patterns about the noise process.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas where the work could be strengthened. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Noise Model:** The paper presents the learned noise matrices but stops short of a deeper analysis. For example, how does the structure of the learned noise relate to known typological divergences between English and the target language? How much of the noise is due to alignment errors versus genuine syntactic differences? A more detailed error analysis connecting the learned matrix `A` to specific linguistic phenomena would make the contribution even more compelling.

2.  **Unexplored Sensitivity of the Training Regimen:** The authors state that a two-stage training process (pre-training on clean data, then joint fine-tuning) is critical to avoid poor local optima. This is a crucial detail that warrants more investigation. An ablation study is needed to quantify the performance drop without pre-training. Furthermore, it's unclear how sensitive the model is to the amount of clean data available for this pre-training. Is there a minimum threshold of annotated tokens below which the model fails to learn a useful noise transformation? This is a key question for real-world applicability.

3.  **Lack of Context within Broader Label Noise Literature:** The paper frames its contribution primarily within the NLP and low-resource literature. However, learning from noisy labels is a well-established field in machine learning. It would strengthen the paper to connect the proposed "noise layer" to existing work on noise transition/confusion matrices and other techniques for robust learning. While the application and integration into a deep sequence model are novel, situating the core idea within this broader context would clarify its relationship to prior art.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the necessity of the two-stage training process? What happens if you initialize the BiLSTM randomly and train jointly from the start? Does initializing the noise matrix `A` as an identity matrix help stabilize joint training from scratch?

2.  The noise layer is a deterministic transformation. This is closely related to classic work on modeling a noise transition matrix `P(noisy_label | true_label)`. Have you considered a more probabilistic formulation of your noise layer, and do you see any potential benefits or drawbacks to such an approach compared to your current formulation?

3.  For tokens with non-one-to-one alignments, you assign a tag distribution based on the source sentence frequency. This seems like a reasonable heuristic, but how critical is it to the model's success? Have you explored simpler alternatives, such as assigning a special `UNALIGNED` tag or simply excluding these tokens from the projected training set?
