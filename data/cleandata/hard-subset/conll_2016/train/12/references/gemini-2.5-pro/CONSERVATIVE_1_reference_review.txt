**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that is jointly trained on both data sources.

The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a trainable linear transformation matrix that explicitly models the systematic discrepancies between the model's predicted tag distribution (for clean data) and the observed noisy labels from the projection process. By learning this transformation, the model aims to "de-noise" the error signal from the projected data during backpropagation, allowing it to better exploit this abundant but unreliable resource. The method is evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over several baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and simple approach:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem in low-resource NLP: how to effectively use noisy, distantly supervised data. The proposed solution is elegant in its simplicity. Rather than relying on complex heuristics for data filtering or feature engineering, the authors propose adding a single, interpretable layer (a KxK matrix) to a standard neural architecture. This layer directly models the noise channel, a classic concept, which makes the approach methodologically sound and easy to understand.

2.  **Thorough and convincing empirical evaluation:** The experimental setup is a key strength. The authors validate their method across a wide range of languages (ten in total) and in two distinct settings: simulated low-resource (using Europarl) and genuinely low-resource (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). This provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach.

3.  **Strong baselines and ablations:** The paper includes the necessary baselines to properly situate the contribution. Most importantly, the comparison against a BiLSTM trained on the simple union of annotated and projected data (`BiLSTM Ann+Proj`) clearly demonstrates that the performance gain is not just from using a neural model or more data, but specifically from the proposed noise-aware training objective. The model consistently outperforms this baseline, validating the utility of the noise layer. Furthermore, the inclusion of the prior state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a), evaluated on the same data splits, provides a direct and meaningful comparison point.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited methodological novelty:** While the application is novel, the core mechanism—modeling label noise with a confusion matrix—is a well-established idea in machine learning. The base model is a standard BiLSTM. The contribution is therefore an effective integration of existing components rather than a fundamental methodological advance. The authors claim this is the first work to *explicitly* model this type of noise for projection, but the conceptual leap is modest.

2.  **Comparison to modern semi-supervised methods is absent:** The main point of comparison, Duong et al. (2014a), uses a Maximum Entropy model. While this is the appropriate direct comparison, the field of semi-supervised and noise-aware learning has advanced considerably. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion or comparison against other established strategies for learning from noisy labels, such as down-weighting the loss on noisy examples, using the projected data for pre-training followed by fine-tuning, or other multi-task learning frameworks. The current set of baselines does not convince me that this specific approach is superior to other modern alternatives for combining clean and noisy data.

3.  **The analysis of the learned noise matrix is superficial:** The authors provide visualizations of the learned transformation matrix A, which is a good first step. However, the analysis is largely descriptive, pointing out strong diagonals and some off-diagonal confusion. A deeper, more rigorous analysis is needed to fully support the claim that the model is "learning when to trust" the data. For example, are the learned confusions linguistically plausible? Can they be correlated with known typological differences between English and the target language (e.g., lack of determiners, different verb morphology) or with systematic word alignment errors? Without this connection, the matrices are just a qualitative illustration rather than a source of insight.

4.  **The two-stage training regime raises concerns about robustness:** The paper states that the model is pre-trained on the small clean dataset before being jointly trained on both data sources, as direct joint training is "impractical." This suggests a certain fragility in the optimization process. This reliance on a specific initialization and training schedule feels ad-hoc and lacks a principled justification. The paper should provide more details on the sensitivity of the model to this pre-training step. How much pre-training is required? How does performance degrade without it? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding the stability of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your `BiLSTM Ann+Proj` baseline is a simple but reasonable approach to data combination. Could you justify your choice of the explicit noise layer over other established semi-supervised learning techniques for handling noisy labels, such as simply weighting the loss term for the projected data by a tunable hyperparameter, or using the projected data as a regularizer?

2.  The two-stage training process seems crucial to the model's success. Have you investigated the sensitivity of your final results to the duration of the pre-training stage? For instance, how does performance change if you pre-train for half as many or twice as many epochs? Is it possible to achieve similar results with a single-stage process by carefully weighting the two loss terms (e.g., with a curriculum learning-style schedule)?
