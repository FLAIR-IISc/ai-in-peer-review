### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The central idea is to explicitly model the systematic errors inherent in the distant supervision process. The proposed model consists of a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger, augmented with a "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation matrix—that learns to map the model's 'clean' tag predictions to the observed noisy tag distribution from the projected data. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and principled method for modeling noise in distant supervision.** The core contribution is the "noise layer," an elegant mechanism that explicitly learns a confusion matrix between true and projected tags. This is more sophisticated than common approaches that either treat noisy labels as gold-standard or apply complex heuristics for filtering. The method is simple, end-to-end trainable, and generalizable to other tasks using distant supervision.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation.** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method across a wide range of languages. The evaluation on eight simulated low-resource European languages provides a controlled comparison against prior work, while experiments on two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) prove its real-world applicability. The consistent improvements over strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art model, substantially strengthen the paper's claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Novelty:** The proposed noise layer is a simple yet powerful concept. By framing the problem as learning a transformation from a latent clean distribution to an observed noisy one, the model can effectively leverage the large but unreliable projected dataset. The error signal from the projected data is "de-noised" as it backpropagates through the learned transformation matrix, allowing the underlying BiLSTM to learn a more accurate representation of the target language's syntax without being corrupted by the noise. This explicit modeling is a significant improvement over implicitly handling noise by simply mixing data or using projected tags as features.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is a major strength.
    *   **Baselines:** The paper compares against appropriate and strong baselines, including a standard supervised BiLSTM, a BiLSTM trained on various combinations of data, and the state-of-the-art model from Duong et al. (2014a), which this work is directly building upon. This allows for a clear assessment of the proposed model's contribution.
    *   **Data:** The use of both simulated (8 Europarl languages) and real-world (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) low-resource settings is commendable. It demonstrates that the method is not only effective in a controlled environment with high-quality parallel corpora but also robust in more realistic scenarios with smaller and potentially lower-quality data.
    *   **Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming all baselines consistently across all 10 languages. The improvements are not marginal, particularly for the real low-resource languages (e.g., an absolute improvement of 4.2% on Kinyarwanda over Duong et al.).

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Interpretability:** The authors go beyond simply reporting accuracy scores by visualizing the learned noise transformation matrix `A` for all languages (Figures 3 and 4). This analysis provides valuable insights into the nature of projection noise and demonstrates that the model is learning meaningful patterns. For example, the strong diagonals indicate high confidence, while specific off-diagonal elements highlight systematic confusions between certain POS tags (e.g., PRT/X in Swedish). This strengthens the paper's central claim that explicitly modeling noise is beneficial and provides a qualitative understanding of why the model works.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Exploration of the Training Strategy:** The authors state that they use a two-stage training process (pre-train on clean data, then fine-tune jointly) because direct training is "impractical." This claim is not substantiated with experimental evidence. A key component of the method's practicality hinges on this training regime, but its sensitivity is not analyzed. It is unclear how much pre-training is necessary or what happens if one attempts to train from scratch with different learning rates or initializations. A more thorough ablation study of the training process would strengthen the paper's reproducibility and clarify the conditions under which the model succeeds.

2.  **Conflation of Model Architecture and Noise Modeling Contributions:** The baseline from Duong et al. (2014a) uses a Maximum Entropy classifier, while the proposed model uses a BiLSTM. The performance gains reported are therefore a combination of a stronger base architecture (BiLSTM vs. MaxEnt) and the novel noise layer. While the BiLSTM baselines in Table 1 provide some ablation, the paper does not compare its noise layer approach against a more direct BiLSTM-based adaptation of Duong et al.'s method (e.g., using projected tags as input features to the BiLSTM). Such a comparison would more precisely isolate the contribution of the explicit noise modeling from the contribution of using a more powerful underlying sequence model.

3.  **Simplifying Assumption of Context-Independent Noise:** The model learns a single, global transformation matrix `A` to represent the noise process. This assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being projected as a noisy tag `j` is independent of the word or its context. In reality, projection errors can be highly context-dependent. For instance, a noun might be incorrectly projected as a verb only when it follows a certain type of auxiliary. While the current model is effective, its core assumption is a simplification, and acknowledging this limitation and discussing potential future work (e.g., context-dependent noise models) would make the paper more complete.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more detail on the two-stage training process? Did you experiment with training the full model from scratch, and if so, what were the results (e.g., instability, poor performance)? How sensitive is the final accuracy to the duration of the pre-training phase on the small annotated dataset?

2.  To better disentangle the benefits of the BiLSTM architecture from the noise layer, have you considered implementing a baseline that incorporates the projected tags in a manner more analogous to Duong et al. (2014a), for instance by concatenating an embedding of the projected tag with the word embedding as input to the BiLSTM? How does your proposed noise layer compare to such a baseline?
