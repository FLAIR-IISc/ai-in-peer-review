### Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

#### Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) model that incorporates an additional "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation matrix—to explicitly model the systematic errors and inconsistencies present in the projected labels. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that explicitly modeling the noise from cross-lingual projection, rather than treating it as gold data, leads to significant performance improvements in a low-resource setting. The results are shown to be effective across a range of simulated and two real-world low-resource languages.
2.  The introduction of a simple and interpretable method (the "noise layer") for integrating this noise modeling into a standard neural sequence tagging architecture.

#### Strengths
1.  **Well-motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem: making effective use of abundant but noisy parallel data for low-resource NLP. The preliminary experiments (Table 1, top section) clearly demonstrate that naively combining clean and noisy data is suboptimal, providing a strong motivation for a more nuanced approach like the one proposed.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method (BiLSTM+noise layer) achieves state-of-the-art results on the chosen benchmarks, consistently outperforming the prior work of Duong et al. (2014a) across all eight simulated languages and two real-world languages. The improvements are particularly notable on Kinyarwanda, suggesting the method is robust even when parallel data is limited and of lower quality.
3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Interpretability:** The core idea of adding a trainable K×K matrix to model label confusion is straightforward to implement and adds minimal computational overhead. Furthermore, the visualization of the learned matrix `A` (Figures 3 and 4) provides valuable qualitative insight into the types of projection errors the model is learning to correct, enhancing the interpretability of the model's behavior.

#### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** The central idea of using a confusion matrix to model label noise is a well-established technique in the broader machine learning literature on learning from noisy labels, where it is often referred to as a "noise transition matrix" or "label corruption matrix." While its application within a BiLSTM for this specific NLP task is new, the paper presents the "noise layer" as a novel invention without adequately situating it within this existing body of work. This overstates the conceptual novelty of the method, which is more of an effective application of an established idea.
2.  **Weak Justification for BiLSTM in a Low-Resource Context:** The choice of a BiLSTM as the base model for a low-resource task is not sufficiently justified. LSTMs are notoriously data-hungry, and the paper's own baseline results show that a BiLSTM trained on the small annotated set ("BiLSTM Annotated") often performs worse than the simpler "MaxEnt Supervised" baseline from prior work. This undermines the claim that the deep learning model itself is superior. The performance gains appear to stem primarily from the noise modeling strategy, which is not inherently tied to the BiLSTM architecture. It is plausible that applying the same noise modeling technique to a more traditional and less parameter-heavy model, such as a Conditional Random Field (CRF), could yield similar or even better results in this data-scarce regime.
3.  **Conflation of Architectural and Methodological Improvements:** The proposed model differs from the primary baseline (Duong et al., 2014a) in two key aspects: the underlying classifier (BiLSTM vs. MaxEnt) and the mechanism for handling noisy data (explicit noise layer vs. joint features). The experiments do not disentangle these two factors. A more rigorous evaluation would have included a baseline where the proposed noise layer is applied to a MaxEnt classifier. This would have isolated the contribution of the noise modeling strategy from the contribution of changing the base model, providing a clearer picture of what drives the performance improvements.
4.  **Ad-hoc Training Procedure:** The paper mandates a two-stage training process: pre-training the BiLSTM on the gold data before jointly training the full model. This is justified by the need to avoid "poor local optima," which suggests the joint training objective may be unstable or difficult to optimize. This reliance on a specific initialization scheme feels like a workaround rather than a principled solution and raises questions about the model's robustness. The sensitivity of the final performance to this pre-training step is not explored.

#### Questions to Authors
1.  The concept of your "noise layer" is functionally equivalent to the noise transition matrices used in the broader literature on learning with corrupted labels. Can you please clarify the novelty of your method in the context of this prior work and justify why it was not cited or compared against?
2.  Given that your BiLSTM baseline did not consistently outperform the MaxEnt baseline on the small annotated datasets, what is the primary motivation for using a complex, high-variance model like an LSTM in this extremely low-resource setting? Have you considered integrating your noise-modeling approach with a more established low-resource model, such as a CRF, which might be more appropriate for the data conditions?
3.  Your paper states that pre-training on the gold dataset is a necessary step. Could you provide an ablation study showing the performance of the model when trained end-to-end from a random initialization? (For example, by initializing the noise matrix `A` as a near-identity matrix). This would help assess the stability of the training process and the importance of the pre-training stage.
