### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to explicitly model the systematic noise introduced by the projection process. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger with two output heads: a standard output for training on clean data, and a "noised" output for training on projected data. This noised output is generated by applying a learned linear transformation (a "noise layer") to the clean output probabilities. By jointly training on both data sources, the model learns to predict clean tags while simultaneously learning a transformation that accounts for the discrepancies in the projected labels. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and elegant method for modeling label noise in distant supervision.** The proposed "noise layer" is a simple, parameter-efficient (a single K×K matrix) mechanism to explicitly model the transformation between a model's latent "clean" predictions and the observed noisy labels. This is a generalizable technique applicable to other tasks that combine clean and distantly-supervised data.
2.  **Strong empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art method, across ten different languages. The use of both simulated and authentic low-resource settings strengthens the experimental results.
3.  **Insightful analysis of cross-lingual projection noise.** The visualization and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices provide a clear and interpretable view of the types of errors and confusions inherent in the projection process for different language pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Novelty:** The core idea of adding a trainable noise layer is clever and well-motivated. Instead of relying on heuristics to filter noisy data or treating it as equally valid as gold data, this approach learns to "de-noise" the supervision signal during backpropagation. It provides a principled way to integrate two data sources of differing quality within a single end-to-end trainable model. This is a more sophisticated approach than simply using projected tags as features or mixing datasets naively, as demonstrated by the baseline results.

2.  **Convincing and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The paper presents a strong empirical case. The model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on its own evaluation setting for the eight simulated languages, and also surpasses it on two real low-resource languages. The gains are not marginal; for example, the accuracy on Kinyarwanda improves from 78.3% to 82.5%, a substantial gain. The inclusion of multiple baselines, including BiLSTMs trained on different data combinations, clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise-aware training method.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, particularly by the sobering results in Tables 1 and 2 showing that simply adding projected data (Ann+Proj) can harm performance. The model architecture is explained well and supported by a clear diagram (Figure 2). The analysis of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is an excellent addition, lending interpretability to the model and providing qualitative evidence that it is learning meaningful patterns of tag confusion.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplifications in the Projection Process:** The method for generating the projected labels seems overly simplistic and could be a source of un-modeled noise. For tokens aligned to multiple words or NULL, the paper states that a distribution over the *entire English sentence's tag frequency* is used. This is a very weak heuristic that discards local alignment information. A more natural approach for multi-aligned tokens would be to use a distribution over the tags of the specific words they are aligned to. The decision to discard all but one-to-one alignments also throws away a potentially large amount of data. While these are common practices, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the impact of these choices or compared them to more sophisticated projection schemes.

2.  **Lack of Ablation on the Training Strategy:** The authors state that jointly training the model from scratch is "impractical" and thus adopt a two-stage process (pre-train on gold data, then fine-tune jointly). This is a strong claim that requires empirical backing. An ablation study showing the performance of a model trained jointly from a random initialization would be necessary to justify this two-stage curriculum. Without it, it is unclear how critical this pre-training step is to the model's success.

3.  **Limited Discussion of the Base Model's Contribution:** The underlying tagger is a BiLSTM, which is a much more powerful sequence model than the MaxEnt classifier used by the primary baseline (Duong et al., 2014a). While the `BiLSTM Annotated` baseline shows that the BiLSTM alone is not sufficient, it's difficult to fully disentangle the gains from using a better base model versus the gains from the novel training objective. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on this point, acknowledging that part of the performance lift relative to prior work may come from the stronger inductive bias of the RNN architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the projection method: Could you clarify the motivation for using the entire source sentence's tag distribution for multi-aligned/NULL-aligned words, rather than a more localized distribution (e.g., from the specific aligned words)? Have you experimented with other projection heuristics, and if so, how did they impact the final performance and the learned noise matrix `A`?

2.  Regarding the two-stage training: The paper claims direct joint training from scratch is "impractical" due to poor local optima. Could you please provide quantitative results from an experiment that attempts this? This would help clarify how sensitive the model is to initialization and justify the necessity of the pre-training stage.

3.  The noise matrix `A` has dimensions K×K, assuming the source and target tagsets (after mapping to Universal Tags) are identical. Your footnote 3 mentions the possibility of a rectangular matrix for mismatching tagsets. Have you considered experiments where you project from a richer, non-universal English tagset directly to the universal target tagset? This seems like a potentially powerful use case for your model that avoids information loss from mapping the source tags to a universal set before projection.
