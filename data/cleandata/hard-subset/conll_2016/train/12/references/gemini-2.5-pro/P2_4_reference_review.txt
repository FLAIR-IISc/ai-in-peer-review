### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English).

The core idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in the projection process. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that has two output paths. When training on clean, annotated data, it uses a standard softmax output layer. When training on the noisy projected data, the output of the BiLSTM is passed through an additional linear transformation layer—the "noise layer"—before the final softmax. This layer, represented by a trainable matrix *A*, learns a transformation from the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution to a distribution that matches the noisy projected labels. The model is trained jointly on both data sources, allowing the error signal from the noisy data to be "de-noised" by the inverse of the learned transformation before being backpropagated to the underlying BiLSTM.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling label noise:** The primary contribution is the "noise layer," an elegant and explicit mechanism to learn the systematic errors (a confusion matrix) introduced by a noisy labeling process like cross-lingual projection. This is a more direct approach than implicitly modeling the noise by using projected tags as features.
2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages) and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The proposed model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art method on the same data splits, demonstrating its practical value.
3.  **Insightful analysis through visualization:** The visualization of the learned noise matrix *A* provides a clear and interpretable view of which POS tags are reliably projected and which are sources of confusion for different language pairs, adding a valuable analytical component to the work.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model:** The core problem—that projected annotations are useful but noisy—is well-established. The proposed solution is intuitive and technically simple, yet powerful. Instead of treating the noisy labels as true targets, the model learns to predict them via a transformation of its "true" internal predictions. This allows the model to leverage the large-scale signal from projected data without being corrupted by its noise. The use of a single K×K matrix is a parameter-efficient way to capture this complex relationship.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on a relevant task:** The paper demonstrates clear and consistent performance gains over several baselines, including a standard BiLSTM and the previous SOTA method from Duong et al. (2014a). Achieving improvements on 10 different languages, particularly the +1.0% absolute gain for Malagasy and a significant +4.2% for Kinyarwanda over a strong competitor, is a compelling result that validates the effectiveness of the approach.
3.  **Thorough and well-designed experiments:** The evaluation methodology is sound. Using both simulated (Europarl) and real-world low-resource scenarios provides comprehensive evidence of the method's robustness. The comparison is fair, as the authors use the same data splits as the main prior work they compare against. The inclusion of simple but important baselines (e.g., BiLSTM trained on annotated, projected, and combined data) clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer.
4.  **Clarity of presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly, and Figure 2 is an excellent illustration of the dual training process on annotated and projected data. The motivation, methodology, and results are logically connected, making the paper a strong and self-contained contribution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on a two-stage training process:** The authors state that they first pre-train the network on the small clean dataset and then fine-tune it jointly with the projected data, as direct joint training is "impractical". This is a critical detail that lacks empirical justification. How sensitive is the final performance to this pre-training? What happens if training is done jointly from scratch? It is possible that with a different learning rate schedule or optimizer, joint training could work. Without an ablation study, it is unclear if this two-stage process is a strict requirement or simply a helpful heuristic, which impacts the generality and robustness of the training recipe.
2.  **Limited technical novelty of the core component:** While the *application* is novel and effective, the core mechanism—a linear layer to model label confusion—is not in itself a major technical innovation. The idea of learning a confusion matrix to handle noisy labels has been explored in other machine learning contexts. The paper would be stronger if it better situated its approach within the broader literature on learning from noisy labels, beyond the specific domain of cross-lingual projection.
3.  **The analysis could be deepened:** The visualization of the noise matrix *A* is a strength, but the accompanying analysis is somewhat superficial. For example, the authors observe confusion between tags but do not attempt to connect these specific confusions to known linguistic divergences between English and the target language (e.g., differences in article systems causing DET->NOUN confusion, or pro-drop phenomena). Doing so would provide deeper linguistic insights and strengthen the claim that the model is learning meaningful patterns of syntactic divergence.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide more justification for the necessity of the two-stage training process? Have you experimented with joint training from a random initialization (perhaps with a weighted loss term to balance the small gold set and the large noisy set)? Understanding the sensitivity to this choice is important for reproducibility and application to new languages.
2.  A strong alternative baseline would be to incorporate the projected tags as features into the BiLSTM model (e.g., as an additional embedding concatenated with the word embedding), which is more in line with how Duong et al. (2014a) use the projected information. Have you considered this comparison? It would help to directly demonstrate the superiority of explicitly modeling the noise in the output space versus using the noisy signal in the input space.
3.  The paper states that only one-to-one alignments are used to reduce noise. Could you quantify how much of the parallel data this filtering step discards? Is it possible that a less strict filtering combined with the noise layer could yield even better results by leveraging more (albeit noisier) data?
