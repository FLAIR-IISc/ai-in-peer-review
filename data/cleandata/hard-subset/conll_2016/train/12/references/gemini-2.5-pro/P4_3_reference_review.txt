### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors observe that naively combining these two data sources can be ineffective. To address this, they propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that is jointly trained on both data sources. The key idea is to explicitly model the systematic errors in the projected data by introducing a "noise layer"—a linear transformation matrix—that learns to map the model's internal "clean" tag predictions to the observed noisy projected tags. This allows the model to learn from the projected data while accounting for its specific noise characteristics. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines and achieving new state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel method for incorporating distant supervision into a neural network by explicitly modeling the noise process. The proposed "noise layer" is a simple yet effective mechanism for learning the systematic confusion patterns between clean labels and noisy, projected labels, allowing the model to de-noise the training signal from the distant data.
2.  A strong empirical validation of the proposed method on low-resource POS tagging. The paper provides a thorough evaluation on 10 languages, showing that the model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art system on the same data splits.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Method:** The core contribution—the noise layer—is a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a common problem in NLP. Instead of treating noisy labels as ground truth, the model learns a transformation that characterizes the noise. This allows the shared underlying model (the BiLSTM) to learn a cleaner representation of the task, as the error signal from the noisy data is effectively "de-noised" via backpropagation through the learned transformation matrix. This is an elegant way to disentangle the main task from the artifacts of the distant supervision process.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate their model on a diverse set of 10 languages, covering both simulated low-resource settings (for direct comparison with prior work) and genuine low-resource languages. The inclusion of multiple baselines, including BiLSTMs trained on different subsets of the data (annotated, projected, combined) and the previous state-of-the-art model from Duong et al. (2014a), clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer. The consistent improvements shown in Table 1 and Table 2 strongly support the paper's claims.

3.  **Clarity and Interpretability:** The paper is well-written and the proposed architecture is explained clearly, aided by the diagram in Figure 2. The decision to visualize the learned noise matrices (A) in Figures 3 and 4 is a major strength. These visualizations provide valuable insight into what the model is learning, confirming that it captures both expected correlations (strong diagonal elements) and language-specific noise patterns (off-diagonal elements). This analysis adds a layer of interpretability and reinforces the technical soundness of the approach.

4.  **Potential for Generalization:** Although the method is presented in the context of low-resource POS tagging, the core idea of an explicit noise transformation layer is broadly applicable. It could be adapted to other tasks and other forms of distant or weak supervision where the noise is expected to be systematic (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations, rule-based labelers). This generality increases the potential impact of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplicity of the Noise Model:** The noise process is modeled by a single, token-independent linear transformation matrix `A`. This assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being projected as a noisy tag `j` is constant across all words and contexts. This is a strong assumption, as projection errors are often context-dependent (e.g., a noun-verb ambiguity might be more likely for certain lexical items). The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore more expressive, context-dependent noise models (e.g., where the transformation depends on the BiLSTM hidden state).

2.  **Lack of Detail on Training Procedure:** The paper omits several key details required for reproducibility. The joint loss function adds two unweighted cross-entropy terms. Given that the projected dataset is much larger than the annotated dataset, the loss will be heavily dominated by the noisy data term. The paper does not state whether a weighting hyperparameter was used to balance the two terms. Furthermore, details regarding the two-stage training process are sparse: there is no information on the number of epochs for pre-training versus joint training, the criteria for switching between stages, or whether a learning rate schedule was used. Details on word embedding initialization (e.g., random vs. pre-trained) are also missing.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Alternative Noise-Handling Techniques:** The paper frames the noise layer as a novel way to explicitly model noise but does not situate it with respect to other potential approaches. For example, a simple baseline could treat the projected annotations as soft labels (using the tag distributions the authors already compute for unaligned words) rather than one-hot targets. The proposed model is architecturally very similar to a multi-task learning setup, and the paper could be strengthened by clarifying the conceptual and practical differences. The claim of being the "first to explicitly model the noise" may be too strong without a more thorough discussion of related ideas, such as the use of an output confusion matrix in other domains.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The joint loss function combines terms for the annotated data (`T_t`) and the much larger projected data (`T_p`). Did you experiment with a weighting hyperparameter to balance the influence of these two losses? If not, can you comment on how the dominance of the projected data term affects the learning dynamics and the final performance?
2.  The noise transformation matrix `A` is static and context-independent. Have you considered or do you have intuitions about a dynamic noise model where the transformation could be conditioned on the token or its context (e.g., generated from the BiLSTM's hidden state)?
3.  For the purposes of reproducibility, could you please clarify the following training details: (a) How were word embeddings initialized? (b) How many epochs were used for the pre-training and joint-training stages, respectively? (c) Was a learning rate decay schedule employed?
