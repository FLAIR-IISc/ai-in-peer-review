**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural sequence tagging model based on a BiLSTM, which is augmented with a "noise layer"—a linear transformation matrix—that explicitly models the confusion between the model's predicted "clean" tags and the noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both the gold and projected data. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a simple and effective method for incorporating noisy, distantly-supervised labels into a neural sequence model. The method learns an explicit linear transformation (a confusion matrix) to map the model's clean predictions to the noisy labels, allowing the model to learn from the noisy data without being corrupted by it.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical validation of this method, establishing a new state-of-the-art for POS tagging on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda and showing consistent gains over strong baselines across eight other languages in a simulated low-resource setting.

**Strengths**
1.  **Simple and Intuitive Model:** The core idea of adding a trainable noise transformation matrix is elegant and easy to understand. It directly confronts the well-known issue of noise in projected annotations without resorting to complex, heuristic-based filtering or feature engineering. This simplicity makes the approach generalizable to other tasks and types of distant supervision.
2.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed model ("BiLSTM+noise layer") achieves superior performance across all ten languages tested. It convincingly outperforms several well-chosen baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on various data combinations and the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a). The performance gains on the two real low-resource languages are particularly noteworthy.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is a valuable addition. It offers qualitative insights into the types of projection errors the model learns to compensate for and how the nature of this noise differs across languages. This analysis strengthens the paper's claim that it is explicitly modeling the noise.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Serious Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The manuscript is marred by a lack of attention to detail, which is concerning. The bibliography is improperly formatted, containing stray question marks (e.g., "Ammar et al\\.,?"), inconsistent citation styles, and apparent LaTeX errors (e.g., `\Q2016\E`). This is unacceptable for a publication-ready paper. Furthermore, there are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings throughout the text (e.g., "wildly successful", "are likely be of considerable use", "may misdirect training result in a poor local optima"). Footnote 1 appears awkwardly in the middle of a sentence, which is poor typesetting.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail for Reproducibility:** The paper omits critical details required to reproduce the experiments. Key hyperparameters for the BiLSTM are missing, such as the specific optimizer used (vanilla SGD, Adam, Adagrad?), batch size, learning rate schedule or decay, and any form of regularization (e.g., dropout). A learning rate of "1.0" for SGD seems exceptionally high and warrants further explanation. The claim that direct end-to-end training is "impractical" is presented without evidence; an empirical comparison showing this to be the case would be necessary to justify the two-stage training regimen.
3.  **Unjustified or Unexamined Modeling Choices:** Several crucial methodological decisions are not adequately justified or analyzed.
    *   The strategy for handling non-singly-aligned tokens (assigning a "distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence") is a non-standard heuristic. Its impact on the model is not discussed, nor is it compared to other potential strategies (e.g., using corpus-level frequencies, or simply discarding these tokens).
    *   Following prior work, the authors retain only one-to-one alignments. This is a significant simplification that likely discards a large amount of potentially useful parallel data. The paper should at least report the proportion of data that was discarded and acknowledge the potential impact of this choice.
4.  **Framing of Novelty:** While the application to this specific problem is effective, the core technique of learning a confusion matrix to handle label noise is not entirely novel in the broader machine learning literature. The claim that this work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations" may be narrowly true but could be better contextualized with respect to related work on learning from noisy labels. The term "noise generating process" also seems to overstate the complexity of what is ultimately a single linear transformation.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please provide the full set of hyperparameters used for training, including the optimizer, batch size, learning rate decay schedule, and any regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, weight decay)?
2.  In Figures 3 and 4, which axis (rows or columns) corresponds to the model's internal "clean" tag distribution (`o_t`) and which corresponds to the projected "noisy" tags (`ỹ_t`)?
3.  You claim that initializing the network and training end-to-end is "impractical". Have you performed experiments to confirm this? If so, could you share results (e.g., learning curves or final performance) comparing the two-stage approach to a direct joint training approach? This would substantially strengthen the motivation for your training procedure.
4.  What percentage of the parallel data was discarded by only using one-to-one alignments? Have you investigated the impact of using more complex alignments (e.g., many-to-one) on the model's performance?
