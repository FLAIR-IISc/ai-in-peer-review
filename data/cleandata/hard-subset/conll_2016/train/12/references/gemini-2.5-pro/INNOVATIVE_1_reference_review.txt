### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, proposing a method to leverage noisy distant supervision from cross-lingual projection alongside a small amount of gold-standard data. The core of the work is a novel neural architecture, built on a BiLSTM, that explicitly models the systematic errors inherent in the projection process. This is achieved via a trainable "noise transformation" layer—a matrix that learns to map the model's 'clean' predictions to the observed noisy labels from the projected data. The model is trained jointly on both data sources and evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, where it is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  The primary contribution is the introduction of a simple, explicit, and learnable noise transformation layer to model systematic errors in distant supervision. This is an elegant and powerful concept that reframes noise not as a random nuisance to be filtered, but as a structured process that can be learned and compensated for. The idea is highly generalizable beyond this specific application.
2.  The paper presents a successful and compelling application of this concept to low-resource POS tagging. By achieving new state-of-the-art results, it demonstrates the practical utility of the approach and provides a strong new baseline for this important problem.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's standout feature is its bold approach to handling noisy data. The idea of an explicit, trainable transformation matrix (A) to model the label confusion process is a refreshing departure from common heuristics like filtering or simply combining data. It provides a principled way to let the model learn *how* to distrust the distant supervision. This is a significant conceptual contribution with the potential to influence how the field approaches a wide range of distant supervision and learning-with-noisy-labels problems.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves consistent and often substantial improvements over all baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art on this task (Duong et al., 2014a). The evaluation is commendably thorough, covering eight simulated low-resource languages and two real ones (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). This robustly demonstrates the method's effectiveness across different linguistic settings and varying qualities of parallel data, validating its practical value.

3.  **Excellent Potential for Generalizability:** While demonstrated on POS tagging, the noise layer is fundamentally model- and task-agnostic. This simple matrix transformation could be integrated into nearly any neural classifier and applied to other tasks where distant supervision creates systematic noise patterns (e.g., relation extraction from knowledge bases, NER using gazetteers, or classification using labels from crowd-sourcing). This potential for broad impact makes the work particularly exciting.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) is a key strength. It provides compelling, interpretable evidence that the model is not just a black box but is learning plausible patterns of tag confusion. The strong diagonals confirm that the projected labels are often correct, while the off-diagonal weights reveal systematic errors (e.g., Noun-Adjective confusion in Malagasy) that the model learns to account for. This analysis greatly enhances the credibility of the proposed mechanism.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas that could be strengthened. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws, given the novelty of the core idea.

1.  **Comparison to Modern Baselines is Somewhat Limited:** The primary point of comparison is Duong et al. (2014a), a strong but non-neural predecessor. The main neural baselines are ablations of the proposed model. While understandable, the paper would be more compelling if it compared against or at least discussed other modern techniques for learning from noisy labels within a neural framework, even if they required some adaptation. For instance, how would this method compare to more complex label correction models or semi-supervised approaches like co-training adapted for sequence tagging?

2.  **The Two-Stage Training Process Feels Heuristic:** The paper states that pre-training on clean data before joint training is necessary to avoid poor local optima. While this is a practical and common strategy, it feels somewhat ad-hoc. A deeper exploration of the training dynamics would be beneficial. For example, does training from scratch completely fail? Have the authors considered alternative strategies like curriculum learning, where the weight of the noisy data loss is gradually increased? A more rigorous justification or analysis of this design choice would strengthen the methodology.

3.  **Analysis of the Noise Layer Could Be Deeper:** The visualizations of the confusion matrices are excellent, but the accompanying analysis is a bit brief. It would be fascinating to see a more qualitative analysis linking specific off-diagonal weights to linguistic phenomena or common alignment errors. For example, showing a specific sentence where a projected tag is wrong (e.g., a projected `VERB` that should be a `NOUN` in Malagasy), and explaining how the model, guided by the learned matrix A, correctly predicts `NOUN`, would make the model's mechanism much more tangible.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the two-stage training: could you elaborate on the results of a single-stage joint training from a random initialization? Does the model fail to converge, or does it converge to a trivial solution where it effectively ignores the projected data?
2.  The noise matrix A has K^2 parameters. This is small for the 12 universal POS tags, but could become very large for tasks with hundreds of labels (e.g., fine-grained entity typing). Do you have thoughts on how this approach might scale? Would techniques like low-rank factorization of the A matrix be a viable path forward?
3.  The method for handling unaligned or multi-aligned words is to assign a tag distribution based on the sentence-level frequency. This seems like a reasonable but simple heuristic. Did you experiment with other strategies, such as treating these as a special `UNALIGNED` token or using a more sophisticated aggregation of aligned tags? How sensitive is the model's performance to this choice?
