**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The central proposal is a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that explicitly models the systematic noise inherent in the projected labels. This is achieved by introducing a "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation—that learns to map the model's internal "clean" tag predictions to the observed noisy tag distribution from the projected data. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective method for combining clean and noisy supervision by explicitly modeling the noise process. The proposed noise layer is a simple, well-motivated, and interpretable addition to a standard neural tagger that learns a confusion matrix between true and projected tags.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on POS tagging for two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) and show consistent, significant gains over strong baselines across eight simulated low-resource languages.
3.  An insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix, which provides interpretability into the model's behavior and reveals language-specific patterns of projection errors.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach to Noise Modeling:** The core idea of using a trainable transformation matrix to explicitly model the noise in distant supervision is elegant and well-motivated. It moves beyond common heuristics like filtering noisy instances or simply mixing data with different weights. Instead, it treats the noisy labels as a structured signal that can be modeled, allowing the model to learn *how* to de-noise the supervisory signal from the projected data during backpropagation. This is a principled way to leverage the information in the large projected corpus without being misled by its systematic errors.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The evaluation is thorough and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors test on a wide range of languages (8 simulated, 2 real) and show consistent improvements. Crucially, they compare against a strong state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a) designed for the same task setting and demonstrate superior performance. The average accuracy improvement of +0.4% on the simulated languages and significant gains of +1.0% on Malagasy and +4.2% on Kinyarwanda over this SOTA baseline are impressive.

3.  **High-Quality Analysis and Interpretability:** The visualization and discussion of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 are a major strength. This analysis provides valuable insight into what the model has learned, confirming that it captures meaningful noise patterns. For example, the strong diagonals show that projected tags are often correct, while specific off-diagonal weights highlight known cross-lingual divergences or alignment issues (e.g., the higher noise level in Kinyarwanda, which has less parallel data). This level of analysis adds confidence that the performance gains are due to the intended mechanism and makes the model less of a black box.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Related Work on Learning with Noisy Labels:** While the application to cross-lingual projection is novel, the core technique of using a trainable confusion matrix to model label noise has precedents in the broader machine learning literature (e.g., in computer vision). The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this context and positioning its contribution more precisely. The claim of being "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations" is likely true for this specific subfield, but the underlying mechanism is not entirely de novo.

2.  **Lack of Ablation on the Two-Stage Training Procedure:** The authors state that jointly training the model from scratch is "impractical" and thus adopt a two-stage approach: pre-training on the small gold dataset, then fine-tuning on the combined data. This is a critical design choice, but its impact is not quantified. An ablation study showing the performance of a model trained jointly from a random initialization is needed. Without it, it is unclear whether pre-training is essential for convergence or simply a helpful trick to achieve slightly better results. This detail is important for understanding the model's training dynamics and robustness.

3.  **Missing Comparison to Simpler Noise-Handling Baselines:** The paper compares against training on the union of annotated and projected data (`BiLSTM Ann+Proj`), which serves as a basic baseline. However, other simple yet stronger baselines for handling noisy data are not explored. For instance, one could simply apply a different weight to the loss term for the projected data (i.e., `L = L_gold + λ * L_projected`), treating it as less reliable. Another approach would be self-training, where the model trained on gold data is used to re-label the projected data before fine-tuning. Comparing against such methods would better isolate the benefits of explicitly modeling the noise structure versus simply down-weighting the noisy signal.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the two-stage training process, could you please provide results for a model trained jointly from a random initialization? Does this model fail to converge, converge to a poor local optimum, or simply perform slightly worse than the pre-trained version? This would help clarify how critical the pre-training step is.

2.  The noise layer is parameterized by a full K×K matrix. For the universal POS tagset (K=12), this is manageable. However, for tasks with larger label sets, this K^2 scaling could become problematic. Did you consider or experiment with alternative, more constrained parameterizations of the noise matrix, such as a low-rank decomposition, to improve scalability?

3.  How does the proposed noise layer compare to a simpler approach of weighting the loss from the projected data by a constant factor λ < 1? This would help disentangle the benefit of learning a structured noise transformation from the simpler benefit of just trusting the noisy data less than the gold data.
