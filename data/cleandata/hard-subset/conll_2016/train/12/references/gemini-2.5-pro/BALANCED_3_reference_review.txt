### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of low-resource Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a large corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger augmented with a "noise layer". This layer is a trainable linear transformation that explicitly models the systematic errors (or "noise patterns") between the model's internal predictions and the noisy projected labels. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources using a combined objective function. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated through extensive experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), where it achieves state-of-the-art results.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and generalizable method for learning from distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a simple yet effective "noise layer" that explicitly models the confusion between true labels and noisy labels from a distant source. This allows the model to learn to "trust" the distant supervision selectively, effectively de-noising the training signal during backpropagation. This idea is well-motivated and potentially applicable to other tasks and sources of noisy data.
2.  **Strong empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, outperforming strong baselines and the previous state-of-the-art on a wide range of languages. The consistent improvements across 10 different languages demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed method in a realistic low-resource setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea of adding a learnable noise transformation matrix (`A`) is intuitive and directly tackles the central challenge of using distant supervision: label noise. Instead of relying on complex heuristics for data filtering or implicitly hoping a powerful model learns the distinction, this work explicitly parameterizes the noise distribution. This is an elegant solution that is cleanly integrated into the neural architecture and trained end-to-end.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. By evaluating on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, the authors make a strong case for the general applicability of their model. Crucially, they compare against the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) using the exact same data splits, which makes the reported performance gains highly credible. The inclusion of several BiLSTM baselines clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting accuracy scores. The visualization of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 provides excellent qualitative insight into the model's behavior. It confirms that the model is indeed learning meaningful, language-specific noise patterns, such as the strong diagonal for more reliable projections and specific off-diagonal weights for common confusions. This analysis substantially strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Two-Stage Training Regimen:** The authors state that training the full model from scratch is "impractical" and thus propose a two-stage process: pre-train on clean data, then fine-tune jointly. This is a critical design choice that is asserted without empirical evidence. A direct comparison to a model trained jointly from a random initialization is missing. Without this ablation, it is unclear if the two-stage process is a necessary component for success or simply a heuristic that could be simplified. This weakens the reproducibility and understanding of the training dynamics.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Relationship to Broader "Learning with Noisy Labels" Literature:** While the paper is well-situated within the low-resource NLP literature, it misses an opportunity to connect its contribution to the broader machine learning field of learning with noisy labels. Many techniques exist for training deep models with label noise (e.g., loss correction, label smoothing, co-teaching). A discussion of how the proposed noise layer relates to, or differs from, these general-purpose methods would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding and highlight its novelty more broadly.
3.  **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis:** The joint loss function implicitly weights the clean and projected data based on the size of the respective datasets (`|Tt|` and `|Tp|`). The effect of this implicit weighting is not discussed. An analysis of the model's sensitivity to the amount of projected data or an experiment exploring an explicit weighting hyperparameter between the two loss terms would provide a more complete picture of the model's behavior and offer practical guidance for applying it in new settings.
4.  **Clarity on Pre-filtering of Projected Data:** The paper follows prior work in using only one-to-one word alignments to generate the projected data, noting that this reduces noise. While this is a reasonable choice, it means the noise layer is operating on data that has already been significantly cleaned. It would be interesting to know how the model performs if it is fed more, but much noisier, data (e.g., from many-to-one alignments). This would be a more stringent test of the noise layer's capabilities and could potentially unlock even more supervisory signal from parallel corpora.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the two-stage training process, could you please clarify the initialization of the noise matrix `A` before the joint training stage begins? Is it initialized as an identity matrix (reflecting an initial assumption of no noise) or randomly?
2.  In your rebuttal, could you provide results or at least a more detailed justification for why training the model jointly from scratch is "impractical"? What failure mode did you observe? This would greatly strengthen the motivation for the proposed two-stage approach.
3.  The combined loss function weights each token's contribution equally, regardless of whether it comes from the small, high-quality annotated set or the large, noisy projected set. Did you experiment with a hyperparameter to control the relative influence of the two loss components? How does the model's performance vary with the ratio of projected to annotated data?
