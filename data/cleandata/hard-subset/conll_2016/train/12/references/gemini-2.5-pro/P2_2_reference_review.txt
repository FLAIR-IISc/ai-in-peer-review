### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel method for modeling noise in distant supervision.** The core idea is the introduction of a "noise layer," a trainable linear transformation matrix (A), that explicitly models the confusion between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the observed noisy projected tags. This is an elegant way to allow the model to learn which projection patterns to trust and which to discount, directly within the learning objective.
2.  **Strong empirical validation and new state-of-the-art results.** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its method through a comprehensive evaluation on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The proposed model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a) on the same data splits.
3.  **Insightful analysis of the learned noise patterns.** The authors visualize the learned noise transformation matrix `A` for each language, providing a clear and interpretable view of the systematic errors in the cross-lingual projection process that the model has captured (e.g., confusion between PRT and X in Swedish).

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach to Noise Modeling:** The main strength of the paper is its method for handling noisy labels. Instead of treating projected tags as imperfect ground truth or simply as features, the model explicitly assumes they are a noisy transformation of the true latent tags. The noise layer is a simple (a single K×K matrix, where K is the tagset size) yet powerful mechanism to capture this transformation. This approach is more principled than ad-hoc heuristics for cleaning projected data and allows the model to learn from the noise patterns end-to-end. The joint objective function, which combines a standard cross-entropy loss for gold data and a "noised" cross-entropy loss for projected data, is clear and well-motivated.

2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experimental Results:** The evaluation is thorough and leaves little doubt about the method's effectiveness.
    *   The use of both simulated and real low-resource settings provides robust evidence.
    *   The authors compare against the correct and highly relevant state-of-the-art baseline (Duong et al., 2014a), using the same experimental setup for a fair comparison. The proposed `BiLSTM+noise layer` model achieves superior or equal performance on all 8 simulated languages and establishes new SOTA results on Malagasy (+1.0%) and Kinyarwanda (+4.2%).
    *   The inclusion of several BiLSTM baselines (trained on annotated, projected, and their union) clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer, demonstrating that simply mixing the data is suboptimal.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand. The motivation for the problem is clearly laid out in the introduction, with sobering initial results (Table 1, BiLSTM Ann+Proj) that effectively justify the need for a more sophisticated approach. The model architecture is explained well and effectively illustrated in Figure 2. The analysis in Section 4.3, particularly the visualization of the noise matrices, adds significant value by making the model's inner workings interpretable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion on the Scalability of the Noise Layer:** The noise layer is parameterized by a K×K matrix, where K is the size of the tagset. The experiments use the Universal POS tagset, which is small (K=12). The authors briefly mention that the approach supports mismatching tagsets but do not discuss how the `K^2` parameter growth would affect performance with much larger, language-specific tagsets (e.g., K > 50). With very little gold data to ground the "clean" output distribution `o`, training a large `A` matrix could become unstable or prone to overfitting. A discussion of this potential limitation is missing.

2.  **Lack of Detail on the Two-Stage Training Procedure:** The authors state that they first pre-train the network on clean data and then train jointly, as direct joint training leads to poor local optima. This is a critical implementation detail, but the paper provides no specifics. How many epochs is the model pre-trained for? What is the learning rate schedule? Is there a criterion for switching from stage one to stage two? This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and makes it unclear how sensitive the model is to these training hyperparameters.

3.  **Conflation of Heuristic Filtering and Model-Based Noise Handling:** The paper follows prior work in applying several heuristics to filter the projected data *before* training (e.g., using only one-to-one alignments, selecting sentences with high alignment scores). While this is a fair comparison to the baseline, it makes it harder to assess the full power of the noise layer. An interesting ablation would have been to use a much noisier set of projected labels (e.g., including many-to-one alignments) to see if the noise layer alone is capable of handling the increased noise, or if it relies heavily on this pre-filtering.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the specifics of the two-stage training? For instance, how many epochs did you pre-train on the gold data, and did you use any form of early stopping or other criteria to decide when to begin the joint training stage?

2.  Have you considered how the model would perform with larger, language-specific tagsets where the number of parameters in the noise matrix (`K^2`) could be substantial relative to the amount of gold training data? Do you foresee any potential optimization or regularization challenges?

3.  The loss function effectively gives equal weight to the contribution from the gold data and the projected data (by normalizing each term by its respective corpus size). Did you experiment with a hyperparameter `λ` to control the relative importance of the noisy data loss term? It seems plausible that down-weighting the noisy term might be beneficial, especially early in training.
