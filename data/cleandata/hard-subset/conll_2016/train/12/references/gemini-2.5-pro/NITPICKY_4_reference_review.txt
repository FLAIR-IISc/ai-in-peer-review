**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**
This paper proposes a method for low-resource POS tagging that leverages a small amount of gold-annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors' primary contribution is a novel neural architecture that explicitly models the noise inherent in the projected labels. This is achieved by adding a trainable linear transformation layer (a "noise layer" parameterized by a matrix A) on top of a standard BiLSTM tagger. This layer learns to map the model's predicted "clean" tag distributions to the observed noisy ones. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation of this method on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, where it is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Elegant and Principled Method:** The core idea of adding a trainable confusion matrix to explicitly model the noise from distant supervision is intuitive, simple, and well-motivated. It provides a more principled approach to handling noisy labels than simply mixing data or using the projected tags as features, as it attempts to learn the systematic error patterns of the projection process.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), on all 10 languages tested. The improvements are consistent and, in the case of the two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), quite substantial. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed technique.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix A provides valuable qualitative insights. It confirms that the model learns both to trust correct projections (the strong diagonal) and to model specific confusions between tags (the off-diagonal elements). This analysis strengthens the paper's claims by showing not just *that* the model works, but offering a glimpse into *how* it works.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Detail:** Several key methodological choices are presented as given without proper justification or analysis.
    *   The two-stage training process (pre-training on clean data, then joint training) is claimed to be necessary because direct training is "impractical." This is a strong claim that requires empirical evidence. A comparison with a single-stage joint training from random initialization should be included to justify this design choice.
    *   The heuristic for labeling unaligned or multi-aligned tokens (using the tag distribution of the entire source sentence) is questionable and could introduce non-local noise. The impact of this choice is not discussed or ablated. Why not a uniform distribution, or a corpus-level prior?
    *   Crucial details regarding data filtering are missing. The paper states it uses high-scoring sentence alignments, but provides no thresholds or statistics on how much data was filtered out. This hinders reproducibility.

2.  **Technical Imprecision and Unclear Presentation:** The paper suffers from several instances of technical sloppiness.
    *   Equation (2) is written in a non-standard and ambiguous way. The summation `∑ i ai,jot,i` is unclear. This should be expressed using standard matrix-vector notation (e.g., `softmax(A^T o_t)` or similar) for clarity. This notational imprecision is concerning in a technical paper.
    *   The hyperparameters are under-specified. A learning rate of 1.0 for vanilla SGD is exceptionally high. Was a decay schedule used? What was the batch size? How were word embeddings initialized? These details are essential for reproducibility.
    *   The term "noise layer" is used to describe matrix A. However, during backpropagation from the projected data, this layer effectively acts as a *de-noising* transformation on the error signal, as the authors themselves note. This terminology is confusing.

3.  **Insufficiently Controlled Baseline Comparisons:** While the results are strong, the comparison to the key baseline, Duong et al. (2014a), is not ideal. The authors compare their BiLSTM-based model to the published results of a MaxEnt-based model. A more direct and compelling comparison would involve implementing the Duong et al. method (using projected tags as features) within the same BiLSTM framework. This would isolate the contribution of the proposed noise layer from the architectural difference between a BiLSTM and a MaxEnt classifier.

4.  **Poor Formatting and Numerous Language Errors:** The paper is rife with formatting errors, typos, and grammatical mistakes, suggesting a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   The entire reference section is poorly formatted, with incorrect BibTeX processing evident (e.g., "Ammar et al\.,? 2016", "\Q2016\E"). This is unacceptable for a formal submission.
    *   Footnotes are improperly placed directly in the text body (e.g., footnote 1 after "the two.") rather than at the bottom of the column.
    *   There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings throughout (e.g., "wildly successful", "our contributions fall in three aspects", "misdirect training result in a poor local optima"). While minor individually, their high frequency detracts significantly from the paper's professionalism and clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you please clarify the exact matrix-vector operation intended by Equation (2)?
2.  Can you provide empirical evidence to support your claim that the two-stage training process is necessary? What happens if you train the entire model jointly from a random initialization?
3.  Have you considered or experimented with alternative heuristics for handling NULL-aligned or multi-aligned tokens, instead of using the source sentence's tag distribution? How sensitive is the model to this choice?
