### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for low-resource Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging that effectively combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a large amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core of the proposed method is a BiLSTM sequence tagger augmented with a "noise layer"—an additional linear transformation—that explicitly models the systematic errors and transformations between the true (gold) tags and the noisy projected tags. This allows the model to be trained jointly on both data sources, learning to "trust" the distant supervision appropriately.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and principled method for integrating distant supervision:** The key contribution is the "noise layer," a trainable K×K matrix that models the confusion between clean and noisy labels. This is an elegant way to handle distant supervision, moving beyond treating noisy labels as either ground truth or simple features. The idea of learning a de-noising transformation for the backpropagated error signal is particularly insightful.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on low-resource POS tagging:** The paper provides a strong and comprehensive evaluation, demonstrating that the proposed model consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on ten languages (eight simulated low-resource and two genuinely low-resource). This robust empirical validation is a significant contribution in itself.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The proposed architecture directly addresses a well-known problem with distant supervision: the data is noisy and cannot be trusted completely. The paper clearly demonstrates this problem with its baseline experiments (e.g., "BiLSTM Ann+Proj"), which show that naively mixing the data can degrade performance. The noise layer is a simple (only K² additional parameters) and effective solution that separates the primary task of prediction from the secondary task of modeling the noise process, while allowing them to be learned jointly.
2.  **Comprehensive and Strong Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. The use of eight simulated low-resource languages from Europarl allows for a controlled comparison with prior work (Duong et al., 2014a), while the experiments on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda demonstrate the method's effectiveness in a real-world, challenging scenario with smaller and lower-quality parallel data. The consistent improvements over all baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art, are compelling.
3.  **Good Analysis and Interpretability:** The authors include visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrix A (Figures 3 and 4). This is a major strength, as it provides insight into what the model is learning. The analysis correctly points out the strong diagonals (indicating general trust in the projection) as well as meaningful off-diagonal elements that capture systematic cross-lingual divergences or alignment errors (e.g., noun/adjective confusion). This interpretability adds confidence that the model is not just a black box but is capturing plausible linguistic phenomena.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion of Connection to Broader "Learning with Noisy Labels" Literature:** While the approach is novel in the context of cross-lingual projection for NLP, the core idea of using a matrix to model label corruption has been explored in the broader machine learning literature on learning with noisy labels. This matrix is essentially a label transition or confusion matrix. Situating the work within this context would strengthen its theoretical grounding and clarify its relationship to other noise-robust methods. The current framing presents the idea as somewhat more isolated than it is.
2.  **Sensitivity to Projection Heuristics is Unexplored:** The paper follows prior work by using only one-to-one alignments and filtering sentences by alignment score. This is a conservative strategy designed to reduce noise at the source. However, the proposed model is specifically designed to *handle* noise. An important question left unanswered is how the model would perform with more data that is also noisier—for instance, by including many-to-one alignments or using less aggressive filtering. A key advantage of a noise-robust model should be its ability to leverage larger but lower-quality data sources, and this aspect was not investigated.
3.  **Lack of Detail on Joint Training Procedure:** The paper mentions a two-stage training process: pre-training on clean data, followed by joint training. This is a crucial implementation detail, but specifics are sparse. For instance, it's unclear how the two loss terms are weighted relative to each other during joint training. The objective function implies weighting by the inverse of the dataset size, but it's common to use a hyperparameter to balance the influence of the gold and distant data. The absence of these details impacts reproducibility and leaves open questions about the sensitivity of the model to these training choices.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The noise matrix `A` scales quadratically with the size of the tagset (`K²`). While this is manageable for the 12-tag Universal POS tagset, how do you see this approach scaling to tasks with much larger label spaces, such as fine-grained NER (with >50 tags) or full morphosyntactic tagging (with hundreds of tags)?
2.  Could you elaborate on the joint training phase? Did you experiment with a hyperparameter to control the relative weight of the loss from the gold annotated data versus the noisy projected data? Or is the balance simply determined by the relative sizes of the two datasets and batch composition?
3.  Given that your model is designed to be robust to noise, have you considered its performance under a more aggressive projection strategy (e.g., including many-to-one alignments)? It seems plausible that your model could extract a stronger signal from more (but noisier) data than models that rely on heavily pre-filtered projections.
