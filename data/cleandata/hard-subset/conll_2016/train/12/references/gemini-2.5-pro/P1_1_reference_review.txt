### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger, noisier corpus created via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that explicitly models the noise inherent in the projected data. This is achieved by introducing a "noise layer"—a linear transformation matrix—that learns the systematic mapping between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the observed noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both data sources, allowing the error signal from the noisy data to be "de-noised" before updating the core tagger's parameters. The method is evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and elegant method for explicitly modeling label noise in a distant supervision setting. The addition of a trainable noise transformation matrix is an intuitive and generalizable approach for combining small, clean datasets with large, noisy ones, allowing the model to learn *how* to distrust the noisy labels.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method. The paper shows consistent and significant improvements over well-chosen baselines, including previous state-of-the-art systems, across a wide variety of languages in both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Principled Model:** The paper tackles the well-known problem of noise in cross-lingual projection. Instead of relying on complex filtering heuristics, the proposed solution—learning the noise patterns directly via a dedicated layer—is principled and elegant. The architectural change is minimal (a single K×K matrix), making it efficient, easy to implement, and likely applicable to other tasks that use distant supervision.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental setup is excellent. Using eight simulated languages allows for controlled comparison against prior work (Duong et al., 2014a) on the same data splits, while the experiments on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda demonstrate the method's effectiveness on genuine low-resource languages. The inclusion of multiple BiLSTM baselines clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer over naive ways of combining the data.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Interpretability:** The visualization of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is a key strength. It provides valuable insight into the model's behavior and confirms that it is learning meaningful linguistic patterns. For example, the strong diagonal shows that most projected tags are trusted, while specific off-diagonal values highlight common confusions. The comparison between the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda matrices, which correlates the level of noise with the quantity of parallel data, further strengthens the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem, the details of the proposed model, and the experimental results are presented logically and effectively. The illustration in Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the dual training process.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Questionable Handling of Unaligned Tokens:** In Section 3.1, the paper states that tokens with NULL or many-to-one alignments are assigned a supervisory signal based on the tag frequency distribution over the entire source sentence. This seems like a crude heuristic that discards valuable local information and could introduce unhelpful noise. A token's true tag is highly dependent on its local context, not the global tag distribution of a potentially long and complex sentence. The impact of this specific design choice is not analyzed, and it is unclear if a simpler method (e.g., ignoring these tokens or using a uniform distribution) might work better.

2.  **Lack of Ablation on the Training Strategy:** The paper proposes a two-stage training process: pre-training the BiLSTM on the small clean dataset, followed by joint training of the full model. While this is a sensible strategy to find a good initialization, its necessity is not empirically justified. It would be important to know how the model performs if trained jointly from scratch. This would help clarify whether the two-stage process is a crucial component for success or simply a helpful heuristic. The robustness of the model depends on such implementation details.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameters and Reproducibility:** While some model details are provided (embedding/hidden size), crucial details for reproducibility are missing. For example, were word embeddings initialized randomly or from pre-trained vectors? Was any form of regularization like dropout or weight decay used? A learning rate of 1.0 for standard SGD is unusually high and can be unstable; were techniques like gradient clipping employed? The process for determining the number of training epochs for both pre-training and joint training is also not described.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use the sentence-level tag frequency distribution for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens? Have you experimented with alternative strategies, such as simply excluding these tokens from the projected training set, and how does this affect performance?

2.  How critical is the two-stage pre-training procedure to the final performance? Have you experimented with training the entire model jointly from a random initialization?

3.  The noise matrix `A` appears to be unregularized. Have you considered applying any form of regularization to this matrix, for instance, an L2 penalty or a soft constraint to encourage diagonal dominance? This might help prevent overfitting on the projected data, especially when the amount of clean data for pre-training is extremely small.
