### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The central idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in the projected labels within a neural sequence tagging framework.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Noise-Modelling Architecture:** The primary contribution is a simple yet effective "noise layer" added to a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger. This layer, represented by a trainable matrix `A`, learns a transformation from the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution to the observed noisy distribution of projected tags. This allows the model to be trained jointly on both clean and noisy data in an end-to-end fashion, effectively learning *when* and *how* to trust the distant supervision signal.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) and consistently outperforms strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art method, across eight simulated low-resource languages. The comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real-world settings provides robust evidence for the method's effectiveness.
3.  **Analysis of Projection Noise:** The paper provides an insightful analysis by visualizing the learned noise transformation matrix `A`. This offers a degree of interpretability, revealing systematic confusions and trustworthy mappings between the projected English tags and the target language tags, which vary depending on the language pair and the quality of the parallel data.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed noise layer is an elegant solution to a common problem in distant supervision. Instead of relying on complex heuristics for data filtering or feature engineering to implicitly capture noise, the model explicitly parameterizes the noise process with a single KxK matrix. This is not only conceptually clean but also parameter-efficient and straightforward to integrate into existing neural architectures.
2.  **Robust and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors evaluate on a wide range of languages (8 simulated, 2 real), which demonstrates the general applicability of their approach. Crucially, they compare directly against the previous state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a) using the exact same data splits, which makes their claims of improved performance highly credible. The inclusion of multiple BiLSTM baselines clearly isolates the contribution of the noise layer, showing that simply mixing projected and annotated data is suboptimal.
3.  **High Potential for Generalization:** While applied to POS tagging via cross-lingual projection, the core idea of learning a noise transformation is broadly applicable. This technique could be readily adapted to other tasks that use distant supervision (e.g., relation extraction, named entity recognition) or other sources of noisy labels (e.g., crowd-sourcing, heuristic-based labeling). This significantly increases the potential impact of the work beyond the specific application demonstrated.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is explained well with both equations and a helpful diagram (Figure 2), and the experimental results are presented clearly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplistic Handling of Alignments and Projections:** The paper follows prior work in using only one-to-one word alignments for projection, discarding a potentially large amount of data from many-to-one or one-to-many alignments. While this simplifies the process, it is a significant limitation as these complex alignments are common. The authors briefly mention a strategy for unaligned tokens (using a sentence-level tag distribution) but do not elaborate on how this distribution is used as a supervision signal in the cross-entropy loss. A more sophisticated treatment of the projection process itself could potentially yield further gains.
2.  **Limited Analysis of the Noise Matrix:** The visualization and analysis of the noise matrix `A` are a good start but feel somewhat superficial. The authors point out general trends (e.g., the strong diagonal, specific confusions like PRT/X in Swedish) but miss an opportunity for deeper linguistic analysis. For instance, the introduction claims the model can learn to map projected determiners to nouns for languages without determiners, but the analysis does not confirm if this specific phenomenon was actually learned for Malagasy or Kinyarwanda. A more detailed case study connecting the learned matrix parameters to known typological differences would have been more powerful.
3.  **Lack of Ablation on the Training Strategy:** The paper describes a two-stage training process: pre-training the BiLSTM on the small clean dataset, followed by joint training on both clean and projected data. The authors justify this by stating that joint training from scratch is "impractical" due to the risk of poor local optima. While plausible, this claim is not substantiated with experimental evidence. An ablation study comparing this two-stage approach with direct joint training from a random initialization would be necessary to validate this choice and understand its impact on final performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your method for handling projected labels relies on a single tag being projected from a one-to-one alignment. How would your framework, specifically the loss function for the projected data `⟨ỹt, log softmax(Aot)⟩`, be adapted to handle more complex cases, such as many-to-one alignments where a single target word might receive multiple, conflicting projected tags? Would `ỹt` become a probability distribution, and if so, how would you justify the choice of loss function (e.g., cross-entropy vs. KL divergence)?
2.  Could you provide results for an ablation experiment where the model is trained jointly from scratch, without the pre-training stage on the annotated data? This would help quantify the importance of the proposed two-stage training regime.
3.  In the joint objective function, the loss terms for the projected and annotated data are normalized by the size of their respective datasets and then summed. Did you experiment with a hyperparameter to control the relative weight of the two loss terms (e.g., `L = α * L_annotated + (1-α) * L_projected`)? It seems that the relative trust between the small gold set and the large noisy set would be a critical factor for the model's learning process.
