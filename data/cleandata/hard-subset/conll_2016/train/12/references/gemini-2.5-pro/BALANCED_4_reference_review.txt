### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in the projected labels. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that is jointly trained on both data sources. For the noisy projected data, the model's output is passed through an additional linear transformation layer, parameterized by a "noise matrix", before the loss is calculated. This layer is designed to learn the systematic confusion patterns between the true POS tags and the noisy projected tags, effectively allowing the model to learn when and how to trust the distant supervision. The model is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over several baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple yet effective method for integrating noisy, distantly supervised labels into a neural sequence model by explicitly learning a noise transformation matrix. This provides a principled way to de-noise the supervisory signal from the projected data during training.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method for low-resource POS tagging. The experiments cover ten languages and show consistent improvements over well-chosen and competitive baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art on identical data splits.
3.  An insightful analysis of the learned noise matrices, which provides interpretability regarding the nature of projection errors between different language pairs and demonstrates that the model is learning meaningful linguistic patterns.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed "noise layer" is an intuitive and elegant solution to the common problem of learning from noisy data. Instead of treating projected labels as ground truth or using complex heuristics to filter them, the model explicitly learns a transformation that captures the noise distribution. This approach is more principled and is likely generalizable to other tasks and sources of distant supervision (e.g., crowd-sourcing, rule-based systems).

2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The paper's claims are backed by a thorough empirical evaluation. The authors test their model on a wide variety of languages (eight simulated and two real low-resource settings). The results are consistently strong, showing improvements over multiple baselines: a standard BiLSTM trained on only gold data, only projected data, and a naive union of both. Most importantly, the model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art system of Duong et al. (2014a) on the same evaluation splits, making the comparison direct and compelling. The reported gains, especially on the real low-resource languages Malagasy (+1.0%) and Kinyarwanda (+4.2%), are significant.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed model is explained clearly, aided by the helpful architecture diagram in Figure 2. A particular strength is the qualitative analysis of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4. These visualizations provide compelling evidence that the model is not just a black box but is learning interpretable patterns of tag confusion (e.g., the strong diagonal indicating trust, and specific off-diagonal elements indicating common projection errors). This analysis adds significant value and confidence in the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Detail on Training Procedure and Hyperparameters:** The paper lacks crucial details that are necessary for reproducibility. The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, followed by joint training) is mentioned as being critical, but key details are omitted. For instance, what were the stopping criteria for pre-training? In the joint training objective, how are the two loss terms (from gold and projected data) weighted? The equation suggests a simple sum, but a weighting hyperparameter is common and its absence or value should be justified. Furthermore, details on hyperparameter tuning (e.g., how the learning rate of 1.0 was chosen, optimizer details, dropout) are missing. This makes it difficult to assess the model's sensitivity to these choices and to reproduce the results.

2.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The authors claim that directly training the model from random initialization is "impractical" due to poor local optima. While plausible, this crucial claim is not supported by empirical evidence. An ablation study comparing the proposed two-stage training with a one-stage approach (jointly training from scratch) would have been highly valuable. Quantifying the performance drop would strengthen the paper's argument about the necessity of the pre-training step.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Related Work on Learning with Noisy Labels:** While the paper situates itself well within the low-resource NLP literature, the core technical idea of modeling label noise with a confusion matrix has precedents in the broader machine learning community. Acknowledging and briefly discussing this line of work (e.g., from the "learning with noisy labels" literature) would provide a more complete picture of the technical contribution and better frame the novelty of its application to this specific NLP problem.

4.  **The Comparison to Duong et al. (2014a) Could Be Sharper:** The proposed model outperforms the MaxEnt model of Duong et al. (2014a). However, the improvement comes from two sources: a stronger base model (BiLSTM vs. MaxEnt) and the explicit noise modeling mechanism. It is difficult to disentangle these effects. A more direct comparison would involve a BiLSTM baseline that incorporates projected tags in a manner analogous to Duong et al. (e.g., as features concatenated to the word embedding), which would help isolate the contribution of the proposed noise layer itself over implicit modeling via features within the same neural architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the joint training objective function `L(θ, A)`, were the two cross-entropy terms for the annotated and projected data given equal weight? Have you experimented with a hyperparameter to balance their contributions, and if so, how did it affect performance?

2.  Could you provide results for an ablation where the model is trained jointly on both data sources from a random initialization (i.e., a one-stage process), to quantify how "impractical" this is compared to your proposed two-stage training?

3.  The noise layer is an elegant way to model label confusion. How does it compare to a simpler approach within the same BiLSTM framework, where the projected tag for a token is treated as an input feature (e.g., via an embedding that is concatenated with the word embedding)? This seems like a strong baseline that is more analogous to prior feature-based work.
