### Summary of the paper
This paper proposes a method for low-resource Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging that effectively combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a larger, noisier dataset created via distant supervision (cross-lingual projection). The proposed model is based on a standard Bi-directional LSTM (BiLSTM) sequence tagger. The key innovation is the addition of a "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation matrix—which is applied to the BiLSTM's output probabilities when training on the noisy projected data. This layer explicitly models the systematic errors and confusions introduced by the projection process. The model is trained jointly using a combined objective function: one term for the clean data that trains the BiLSTM directly, and another for the noisy data that trains the BiLSTM through the noise layer. This allows the shared BiLSTM to learn a cleaner tagging model by effectively "de-noising" the training signal from the projected data. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines and achieving new state-of-the-art results.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel and generalizable method for integrating distant supervision with clean data.** The primary contribution is an architecture that explicitly models label noise from a distant source. By isolating the noise modeling into a dedicated transformation layer, the model can leverage large amounts of noisy data without corrupting the underlying sequence model, which is trained on a "de-noised" error signal. This technique is applicable not only to cross-lingual projection for POS tagging but potentially to other NLP tasks and sources of distant supervision.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical validation establishing a new state-of-the-art for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a strong set of experiments on ten languages, including two genuinely low-resource ones. The method is shown to consistently outperform previous state-of-the-art results on the same data splits, providing convincing evidence of its effectiveness.
3.  **An interpretable analysis of the noise patterns in cross-lingual projection.** Through visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix, the paper offers insights into the specific types of errors introduced by the projection process for different language pairs. This analysis validates that the model learns meaningful error patterns and adds a layer of interpretability to the approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity.** The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and clearly described. The idea of adding a trainable noise transformation layer to model systematic errors in distant supervision is intuitive. The joint training objective is straightforward, and the two-stage training strategy (pre-training on clean data, then joint training) is a sensible heuristic to guide the model towards a good solution. The architecture is clearly illustrated in Figure 2.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation.** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors evaluate on a diverse set of languages under both simulated and real low-resource conditions. Crucially, they compare against the most relevant prior state-of-the-art work (Duong et al., 2014a) using the identical data splits, which allows for a direct and fair comparison. The inclusion of multiple BiLSTM baselines (trained on annotated only, projected only, or their union) effectively isolates and demonstrates the significant benefit of the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results.** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance across all tested conditions. It surpasses the previous SOTA on both Malagasy (86.3% vs 85.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% vs 78.3%), with the improvement on Kinyarwanda being particularly substantial. It also achieves the highest average accuracy on the eight simulated languages. The consistency of these gains across different languages and data conditions underscores the robustness of the method.
4.  **Good Model Interpretability.** The paper provides a qualitative analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4). This analysis is insightful, revealing that the model learns strong self-transition probabilities (the diagonal) while also capturing specific, plausible confusions between tag pairs (off-diagonal elements). This confirms that the noise layer is not just an arbitrary set of parameters but is learning a meaningful model of projection errors, which strengthens confidence in the approach.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Detail for Reproducibility.** The experimental setup section is missing key details required for reproducibility. Information regarding hyperparameters such as batch size, the number of training epochs for the pre-training and joint training stages, regularization methods (e.g., dropout), and the optimization specifics beyond "stochastic gradient descent" is absent. While the paper mentions the use of the `clab/cnn` library, more specific parameters are needed for others to faithfully reimplement the work.
2.  **Limited Discussion of the Noise Model's Simplicity.** The noise model is a single, static linear transformation matrix `A`. This assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being mislabeled as a projected tag `j` is constant and independent of the word or its context. However, projection errors are often context-dependent. The paper does not acknowledge this limitation or discuss potential extensions, such as a dynamic noise model where the transformation could be conditioned on the BiLSTM's hidden state. While the simple model proves effective, a discussion of its inherent assumptions would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Novelty Could Be Better Contextualized.** While the application of this technique to cross-lingual projection in a neural model appears novel, the core idea of using a confusion matrix or a linear transformation to model label noise has precedents in the broader machine learning literature (e.g., learning from noisy labels). The paper could be strengthened by briefly situating its noise modeling approach within this wider context to better delineate its specific contribution. The claim of being "the first to explicitly model the noise" is strong and may be more accurately scoped to this specific NLP application.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide the missing hyperparameters and details of the training procedure to ensure reproducibility? Specifically, what were the batch sizes, number of training epochs (for both pre-training and joint training), and criteria for stopping training (e.g., performance on a development set)?
2.  The two-stage training process is presented as an important component. Could you provide an ablation study that quantifies its impact? For instance, what is the performance of the model if it is trained jointly from a random initialization without the pre-training step on the clean annotated data?
3.  The noise matrix `A` models a global, context-independent mapping between true and noisy tags. Have you considered or experimented with a more dynamic noise model where the transformation might be conditioned on the local context (e.g., by making `A` a function of the BiLSTM's hidden state)? What are your thoughts on the potential benefits or challenges of such an extension?
