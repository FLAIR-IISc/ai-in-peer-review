### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by proposing a novel method to combine a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a larger, noisier corpus generated via cross-lingual projection. The core of the contribution is a new neural architecture component, a "noise layer," which is explicitly designed to model the transformation between the model's 'clean' tag predictions and the noisy projected labels.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and conceptually elegant framework for learning from distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the idea of an explicit, learnable noise layer (parameterized by a matrix A) within a neural network. This reframes the problem from one of data cleaning or feature engineering to one of end-to-end noise modeling. This is a significant and bold conceptual leap that could have implications far beyond POS tagging, applicable to any domain using weak or noisy labels.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of the proposed method.** The paper demonstrates that this new approach achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong baselines on a wide array of ten languages (eight simulated low-resource and two genuinely low-resource). The visualizations of the learned noise matrix provide compelling evidence that the model is capturing meaningful linguistic and projection-related phenomena.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Novelty and A Bold New Direction.** This work's standout quality is its novelty. Instead of treating projected annotations as noisy features or attempting to filter them with heuristics, the authors propose to learn the noise process itself. The introduction of a dedicated noise layer that transforms the model's output distribution to match the noisy supervision is a powerful and elegant idea. It is a fundamentally new way of thinking about how to integrate distant supervision into deep learning models. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward conceptual work that pushes the field forward.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results.** The proposed model isn't just a neat idea; it works very well in practice. It consistently outperforms the prior state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) across all evaluated languages. The fact that the method holds up not only in simulated settings with high-quality parallel data (Europarl) but also on real, more challenging low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) speaks to its robustness and practical value. The consistent gains over simply training on the union of annotated and projected data (the `BiLSTM Ann+Proj` baseline) clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Interpretability.** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix A in Figures 3 and 4 is a major strength. It provides a rare and valuable window into what the model is learning. We can see the strong diagonal, indicating that most projected tags are trusted, but we also see meaningful off-diagonal weights that correspond to systematic confusions (e.g., NOUN mapping to ADJ/NUM in Malagasy). This analysis elevates the paper from a simple presentation of results to a more insightful exploration of the problem, substantiating the claim that the model learns to "trust" the supervision appropriately.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas where the exploration could have been deeper. These are not fatal flaws but rather opportunities for future work that would have made an already strong paper even stronger.

1.  **Limited Technical Exploration of the Noise Model.** The noise layer is implemented as a simple, global linear transformation. This is a great first step, but it feels like a simplification of a more complex process. Is projection noise really uniform across all contexts? It seems more likely that the noise patterns are context-dependent. For instance, an English verb aligning to a Malagasy word is more likely to be a projection error if that Malagasy word follows a determiner. The paper does not explore more expressive, non-linear, or context-aware noise transformations, which seems like a natural and important next step for this line of research.
2.  **Necessity of Two-Stage Training.** The authors state that direct end-to-end training is "impractical" and that pre-training on the small clean dataset is required. This suggests a potential optimization instability. While the proposed two-stage solution is pragmatic, it slightly detracts from the elegance of a fully joint model. A more thorough investigation into why this is the case (e.g., analyzing gradients, exploring alternative initializations like initializing A as a near-identity matrix) would provide deeper insight and potentially lead to a more robust single-stage training procedure.
3.  **Oversimplified Projection Method.** The method for generating the noisy labels is standard but simplistic, especially for unaligned or multi-aligned words, where it falls back to a sentence-level tag distribution. This is a coarse heuristic that likely discards useful token-level signals. While the paper's focus is on modeling the noise *after* projection, the quality of the noisy signal itself is a confounding variable. It would be interesting to see how the noise layer performs when coupled with a more sophisticated projection scheme.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Following on from Weakness #1, have you considered making the noise transformation dynamic or context-sensitive? For instance, could the values in matrix A be modulated by the token's embedding or the BiLSTM's hidden state? This would allow the model to learn, for example, that it should trust a projected 'VERB' tag more for some words than for others.
2.  Regarding the two-stage training: could you elaborate on the failure modes of direct joint training from a random initialization? Does the model fail to learn anything useful, or does the noise from the projected data overwhelm the signal from the small clean set, leading to a degenerate solution for the BiLSTM parameters? Did you experiment with weighting the two loss terms differently to mitigate this?
3.  The noise matrix A is learned jointly with the rest of the network. Did you consider the effect of regularization on A? For example, an L1 penalty might encourage a sparse matrix, highlighting only the most frequent noise patterns, while an L2 penalty encouraging A to stay close to the identity matrix could serve as a useful prior that the projected labels are mostly correct.
