### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a larger corpus of noisy data generated through distant supervision. The distant supervision signal is obtained by projecting POS tags from a high-resource language (English) to the low-resource language via word alignments in a parallel corpus. The central proposal is a neural sequence tagging model, based on a bidirectional LSTM, which incorporates a novel "noise layer." This layer is a trainable linear transformation matrix that explicitly models the systematic errors and confusion patterns between the model's internal "clean" tag predictions and the observed noisy projected tags. The model is trained end-to-end using a joint objective function that combines a standard cross-entropy loss on the small clean dataset and a "noised" cross-entropy loss on the large distant supervision dataset. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating that their approach achieves state-of-the-art accuracy by effectively learning to leverage the noisy projected data.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Architecture for Modeling Distant Supervision Noise:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a dedicated "noise layer" within a neural sequence model. This layer, parameterized by a confusion matrix, explicitly learns a transformation from the model's predicted clean label distribution to the observed noisy label distribution. This provides a principled and interpretable method for integrating distant supervision, allowing the model to learn from noisy data without being misled by its systematic errors.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on Low-Resource POS Tagging:** The paper presents a strong empirical contribution by substantially improving performance on low-resource POS tagging across ten languages. The method consistently outperforms strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art model that also uses projected annotations, validating the effectiveness of explicitly modeling the projection noise.

### Strengths

1.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise from distant supervision is elegant and directly addresses a fundamental challenge in the field. Instead of treating noisy labels as ground truth or using complex heuristics, the proposed noise layer offers a clean, learnable mechanism to account for label corruption. The joint training objective is well-formulated, allowing the model to leverage both clean and noisy data sources effectively.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate on a diverse set of ten languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios. Crucially, they compare against the relevant state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on the exact same data splits, providing a direct and fair comparison. The inclusion of several BiLSTM baselines trained on different combinations of data effectively isolates the performance gain attributable to the proposed noise layer.

3.  **High Clarity and Good Interpretability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model architecture is clearly described and illustrated (Figure 2). A significant strength is the visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4). These visualizations offer valuable insight into the model's behavior, showing that it learns plausible and language-specific noise patterns (e.g., a strong diagonal indicating high trust, with specific off-diagonal weights for common confusions). This interpretability adds a layer of confidence in the model's mechanism.

4.  **Potential for Generalization:** Although the method is presented in the context of POS tagging via cross-lingual projection, the underlying concept is highly generalizable. The noise layer framework could be applied to any task that combines a small clean dataset with a large, noisily-labeled dataset from sources like other forms of distant supervision or crowdsourcing. This broad applicability increases the potential impact of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on a Two-Stage Training Process:** The authors state that the model must be pre-trained on the small annotated dataset before joint training to avoid poor local optima. While this is a practical solution, it suggests potential instability in the joint learning objective when starting from a random initialization. The paper lacks an ablation study that demonstrates the impact of this pre-training step (i.e., performance of a model trained jointly from scratch), which would provide a clearer understanding of the model's training dynamics and robustness.

2.  **Limited Analysis of the Projection Method's Impact:** The projected data is generated using a standard but relatively simple method (Giza++ with only one-to-one alignments). The quality and nature of noise in projected data are highly dependent on the word alignment quality and the projection strategy. The paper does not investigate how sensitive the proposed model is to these factors. An analysis of how performance changes with different alignment qualities or more sophisticated projection techniques (e.g., using many-to-one alignments) would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the model's ability to handle noise.

3.  **Potential Scalability Concerns for Large Label Sets:** The noise layer is parameterized by a K×K matrix, where K is the size of the POS tagset. This is computationally feasible for the universal POS tagset (K=12) used in the experiments. However, the number of parameters in this layer grows quadratically with the number of tags. The paper does not discuss how this approach would scale to tasks with much larger label sets (e.g., fine-grained entity typing or slot filling, where K could be over 100). This may limit the direct applicability of the proposed method to such tasks without modifications.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The two-stage training procedure is presented as a practical necessity. Could you provide results for a model trained on the joint objective from a random initialization? This would help quantify the importance of the pre-training stage and assess the stability of the joint loss function.

2.  The noise layer's parameterization (a K×K matrix) scales quadratically with the number of classes, K. Have you considered the applicability of this method to tasks with significantly larger label spaces? Would a more parameter-efficient formulation, such as a low-rank approximation of the transformation matrix, be a viable alternative in those settings?
