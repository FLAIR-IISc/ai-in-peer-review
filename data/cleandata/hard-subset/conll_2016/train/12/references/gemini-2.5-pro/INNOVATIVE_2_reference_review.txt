### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold-standard data with a large amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The central and most compelling contribution is a novel neural architecture that explicitly models the noise inherent in the distant supervision process. Instead of simply training on the noisy labels, the authors introduce a "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation—that maps the model's predicted 'clean' tag distribution to the observed noisy distribution from the projected data. This allows the model to learn systematic biases and errors in the projection process (e.g., when a determiner in English consistently projects to a noun in a language that lacks determiners). The model is trained jointly on both the gold data (supervising the base model) and the projected data (supervising the noised output), effectively learning to de-noise the projected signal. This core idea represents a significant conceptual advance in how to leverage distant supervision. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this model's effectiveness, achieving state-of-the-art results on a suite of eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and insightful reframing of how to use noisy data. The idea of learning an explicit transformation that models the noise-generating process, rather than just filtering the data or treating it as ground truth, is highly novel and compelling. The proposed "noise layer" is a simple, elegant, and powerful mechanism to achieve this. It moves beyond heuristics and provides a principled way to learn *how much* and *in what ways* to trust the distant supervision signal. This is exactly the kind of creative, high-impact idea that the field needs.

2.  **Generality and Potential for Broad Impact:** While applied here to POS tagging, the core concept of a learnable noise transformation is broadly applicable. This technique could be readily adapted to many other NLP tasks that rely on distant or weak supervision, such as named entity recognition, relation extraction, or semantic parsing. Furthermore, it could be applied to other sources of noisy labels, like crowdsourced annotations. The simplicity of the approach—adding a single matrix multiplication—makes it easy to integrate into various existing neural architectures, suggesting a high potential for future impact and follow-up work.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The paper's novel idea is backed by solid empirical results. The proposed model consistently outperforms several well-chosen baselines, including a strong state-of-the-art system from prior work (Duong et al., 2014a), across ten different languages. The fact that the approach works well on both simulated settings (with high-quality parallel data) and real, challenging low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, with much less and noisier parallel data) demonstrates its robustness and practical utility.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise matrices (Figure 3 and 4) is an excellent piece of analysis that provides qualitative evidence for the paper's claims. These figures clearly show the model is not just learning the identity matrix; it captures meaningful cross-tag confusions and systematic differences between the source and target languages. The strong diagonal confirms that the projected signal is often trustworthy, while the prominent off-diagonal elements reveal exactly where the model has learned to correct for projection errors. This analysis greatly strengthens the paper by making the model's inner workings more transparent and intuitive.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Oversimplified Noise Model:** The main weakness, which is also a byproduct of the model's strength (simplicity), is that the noise transformation is token-independent. The matrix `A` applies the same tag-to-tag confusion mapping for every word in the corpus. It is highly plausible that projection errors are context-dependent. For example, an English verb might be misaligned to a noun in the target language, but this error might be much more likely if the target word is preceded by an article. A more sophisticated model could condition the noise transformation on the local context (e.g., the BiLSTM's hidden state). While the current approach is clearly effective, it leaves this interesting direction unexplored. I view this as a minor limitation and more of an opportunity for future work than a fatal flaw.

2.  **Limited Justification for Training Strategy:** The authors state that jointly training the entire model from a random initialization is "impractical" and thus opt for a two-stage process (pre-train on gold, then fine-tune on both). This is a common and reasonable heuristic, but the claim would be much stronger if supported by an ablation study. Showing that a one-stage training process either fails to converge or leads to significantly worse performance would provide a more rigorous justification for the necessity of the pre-training step.

3.  **Heuristics in Handling Alignment Noise:** The paper relies on standard but potentially suboptimal heuristics for handling alignment uncertainty. First, it discards all but one-to-one alignments, which throws away a substantial amount of potential (though noisy) signal. Second, for unaligned tokens, it uses a sentence-level tag frequency distribution as the target. This seems like a weak signal that may not be particularly helpful for the model. While these are common practices, the paper's core theme is about explicitly modeling noise, and it would be interesting to see if this philosophy could also be applied more directly to the alignment process itself, rather than relying on a pre-processing/filtering step.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the two-stage training procedure: Could you provide more detail on why a single-stage training from scratch is "impractical"? Did you run experiments with this setup, and if so, how did the results compare to the two-stage approach in terms of final performance and training stability?

2.  The noise matrix `A` models a global, token-independent noise distribution. Have you considered extensions where the noise transformation is context-dependent, for instance, by making `A` a function of the BiLSTM hidden state `h_t`? What would you see as the primary challenges in learning such a model, especially in a low-resource setting?

3.  Your method for handling unaligned or multi-aligned words involves assigning a sentence-level tag distribution as the supervision signal. How sensitive is the model's performance to this choice? Have you explored simpler alternatives, such as completely ignoring these tokens during the projected-data training step?
