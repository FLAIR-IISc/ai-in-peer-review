### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by effectively combining a small set of gold-annotated data with a larger, noisier set of data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural sequence model (a BiLSTM) with a novel "noise layer." This layer is a learnable linear transformation that explicitly models the systematic errors and confusions between the true POS tags and the noisy projected tags. The entire model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and principled method for modeling noise in distant supervision.** The core contribution is the introduction of a learnable "noise layer" (parameterized by a matrix A) that explicitly captures the confusion matrix between the model's internal predictions and the noisy labels. This is a more elegant and potentially more effective approach than simply treating noisy labels as features or mixing them naively with gold data.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a strong and thorough evaluation on ten languages (eight simulated low-resource and two genuinely low-resource). The proposed model consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods, including the strong baseline from Duong et al. (2014a), on the same data splits, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Interpretable Model.** The proposed noise layer is a simple yet powerful idea. It provides a clean, parameterized way to model the relationship between the clean and noisy label spaces. By learning a confusion matrix `A`, the model can discover systematic projection errors (e.g., a source-language determiner consistently projecting to a target-language noun) and learn to "de-noise" the training signal from the projected data during backpropagation. The visualizations of the learned `A` matrices in Figures 3 and 4 are a significant strength, providing valuable insight into the types of noise present in different language pairs and lending credibility to the approach.

2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Evaluation.** The experimental setup is rigorous. The use of both simulated low-resource settings (for direct comparison with prior work) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrates the method's robustness. The baselines are well-chosen and include not only the previous SOTA but also important ablations of their own model (e.g., `BiLSTM Annotated`, `BiLSTM Projected`). The consistent and significant improvements across all languages strongly support the paper's claims.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem. The introductory experiments in Section 3.1, which show that naively mixing projected and annotated data can harm performance, clearly establish the need for a more sophisticated approach. This "sobering result" effectively sets the stage for the proposed solution.

4.  **Generality of the Approach.** Although applied to POS tagging with cross-lingual projection, the core idea of an explicit noise layer is applicable to many other tasks that rely on distant or weak supervision (e.g., relation extraction, sentiment analysis from crowd-sourced data). The authors rightly point this out in the conclusion, and this broad potential impact increases the significance of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflation of Model Architecture and Noise Modeling in SOTA Comparison.** The main comparison is between the proposed `BiLSTM+noise layer` and the `MaxEnt` model of Duong et al. (2014a). While the proposed model is superior, the performance gain comes from two sources: (1) the stronger BiLSTM base architecture compared to MaxEnt, and (2) the novel noise layer. The paper lacks a crucial baseline: a BiLSTM that incorporates the projected tags as features (e.g., as part of the input embedding), which would be the modern neural equivalent of Duong et al.'s feature-based approach. Without this, it is difficult to fully disentangle the benefit of the noise layer itself from the general superiority of BiLSTMs for sequence tagging. The improvement of `BiLSTM+noise layer` over `BiLSTM Ann+Proj` gives some indication, but the latter is a naive data-mixing strategy, not a feature-based one.

2.  **Simplistic Model of Projection Noise.** The noise is modeled by a single, global transformation matrix `A`. This assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being projected as a noisy tag `j` is constant, regardless of the word, its morphology, or its local context. In reality, projection errors are often context-dependent. For example, a verb might be misaligned when followed by a preposition but not otherwise. A single confusion matrix cannot capture such nuanced error patterns. While the simplicity is a strength, it is also a limitation that is not discussed.

3.  **Heuristic for Unaligned/Multi-aligned Tokens.** The method for assigning a label to tokens with non-one-to-one alignments (using the tag frequency distribution of the entire source sentence) is a rather weak heuristic. This approach injects non-local information that may be entirely unrelated to the target token, potentially adding more noise than signal. It would be beneficial to know how many tokens fall into this category and how sensitive the model is to this specific choice. An alternative, like using a special `UNALIGNED` tag or simply discarding these tokens, could have been explored and compared.

### Questions to Authors

1.  To better isolate the contribution of your proposed noise layer, could you comment on how you would expect it to perform against a strong baseline that uses a BiLSTM but incorporates the projected tag (or its distribution) as an input feature, concatenated with the word embedding? This seems like a more direct comparison to the spirit of Duong et al. (2014a)'s methodology, updated for a neural architecture.

2.  The learned matrix `A` models a global, context-independent tag confusion. Have you considered or do you have thoughts on extending this to a context-dependent noise model? For instance, `A` could be dynamically generated based on the BiLSTM's hidden state `h_t`, allowing the model to learn that projection noise patterns differ for different lexical or syntactic contexts.

3.  Could you provide some statistics on the prevalence of non-one-to-one alignments in your projected data? How much of the projected training data is labeled using the sentence-level tag distribution heuristic, and have you explored the impact of simply discarding these tokens instead?
