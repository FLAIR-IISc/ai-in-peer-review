### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The authors propose a method to leverage a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language.

The primary contribution is a neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise inherent in the projected annotations. The model is based on a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger, but incorporates an additional linear transformation layer—termed a "noise layer"—which is only applied when training on the noisy projected data. This layer is parameterized by a matrix `A` that is learned jointly with the rest of the network and is intended to capture the systematic confusions between the true tag distribution and the noisy projected tag distribution.

A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this method. The authors conduct experiments on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating that their proposed model consistently outperforms several baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art method on the same data splits.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its experimental results. The proposed model ("BiLSTM+noise layer") achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming the previous best result from Duong et al. (2014a) on both the simulated low-resource languages (Table 1) and the two real-world low-resource languages (Table 2). The improvements are consistent across all ten languages evaluated, which provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed technique.

2.  **Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The experimental design is rigorous. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines, including the direct predecessor on this task. By including ablations like training a BiLSTM only on annotated data, only on projected data, and on their simple union, they effectively demonstrate the limitations of naive data combination strategies and highlight the value of their noise-modeling approach. The use of both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios further strengthens the evaluation.

3.  **Simplicity and Interpretability of the Proposed Method:** The core architectural innovation—the noise layer—is a simple linear transformation. This is a strength, as it does not introduce excessive complexity or a large number of parameters to the model. Furthermore, the learned transformation matrix `A` is interpretable, as shown in Figures 3 and 4. This allows for qualitative analysis of the types of noise the model learns to correct for, providing insight into the cross-lingual projection process for different language pairs.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** The central idea of modeling label noise with a trainable confusion matrix is not entirely new. Similar concepts have been explored in various machine learning contexts, including learning from noisy labels and distant supervision. The "noise layer" is functionally a linear layer applied to the output distribution, which is a standard component in neural networks. While its application to cross-lingual projection in a neural POS tagger appears to be novel, the paper overstates its contribution by claiming to be the "first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations". The contribution seems more like a successful and well-executed application of an existing concept rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.

2.  **Dependence on a Two-Stage Training Heuristic:** The authors state that "Directly training the whole deep neural network with random initialization is impractical" and thus resort to a two-stage training process: first pre-training on the clean data, and then joint training on both clean and noisy data. This reliance on a specific training curriculum is a practical weakness. It introduces an extra degree of complexity and sensitivity to the training setup. The paper provides no analysis of this dependency. For instance, how much clean data is required for the pre-training to be effective? If the annotated corpus is extremely small (e.g., <500 tokens), the pre-trained model may be of very low quality, potentially harming the subsequent joint training phase. The robustness of the method to the size and quality of the pre-training corpus is not explored.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of the Learned Noise Matrix:** While the visualizations of the matrix `A` are a welcome addition, the accompanying analysis is largely descriptive and does not rigorously validate what the model has learned. For example, the authors claim the model can "learn to effectively ignore the projected verb tag," but the visualizations do not clearly show this. A more compelling analysis would involve quantitatively comparing the learned `A` matrix to an empirical confusion matrix calculated from a development set where both gold and projected tags are available. This would directly verify whether the model is learning the true noise distribution or simply finding a parameterization that improves the final objective function. Without this validation, the claims about what the noise layer captures remain speculative.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the novelty of the proposed noise layer in the broader context of learning from noisy labels? How does your method differ from, for example, techniques in relation extraction that model noisy labels from distant supervision, or general approaches for label noise correction in classification?

2.  The paper states that the two-stage training process is necessary. Could you provide more detail on the sensitivity of your model to this setup? Specifically, how does performance degrade as the amount of annotated data for pre-training is reduced? Is there a minimum threshold of clean data below which this method is no longer effective?

3.  To provide stronger evidence for the claim that the matrix `A` models the true projection noise, have you considered comparing the learned `A` with an empirical confusion matrix derived from a held-out set containing both gold and projected labels? Such an analysis would provide more direct validation of your model's mechanism.
