### Summary & Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of low-resource Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The central idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in the projection process. The authors propose a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) model with two output paths: one for standard supervised training on clean data, and another that passes the model's output distribution through a learned linear transformation (a "noise layer") before being supervised by the noisy projected labels. This noise layer, represented by a KxK matrix, learns the confusion patterns between true tags and projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both data sources. The authors evaluate this approach on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating that their method outperforms previous state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  A novel and architecturally simple method for integrating distant supervision into a neural sequence model. The explicit modeling of label noise via a learnable transformation matrix is an elegant way to allow the model to learn *how* to trust the noisy data.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness, showing consistent improvements over several baselines, including prior state-of-the-art, on a total of 10 languages. The analysis and visualization of the learned noise matrix provide useful insights into the cross-lingual projection process.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Interpretable Model:** The core idea of adding a single linear transformation layer to model the noise is simple, powerful, and easy to integrate into a standard neural architecture. It requires very few additional parameters (K*K) but directly addresses the central challenge of distant supervision: label noise. The subsequent visualization of this transformation matrix (Figures 3 and 4) adds a valuable layer of interpretability, allowing for analysis of which tag confusions are most common for a given language pair.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Results:** The paper presents a convincing case for the model's effectiveness. It achieves state-of-the-art results on both simulated and real-world low-resource languages, outperforming the previous best method of Duong et al. (2014a). The inclusion of multiple baselines, including ablations of the proposed model (e.g., training only on projected data, training on the union of data), clearly demonstrates the value added by the noise layer. The average improvement of +0.4% on the simulated languages and significant gains of +1.0% on Malagasy and +4.2% on Kinyarwanda are compelling.
3.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a well-established and important problem in NLP. The motivation for using distant supervision for low-resource languages is clear, and the explicit acknowledgment and handling of the noise from cross-lingual projection is a well-grounded approach to improving upon prior work.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws in its methodology, experimental description, and presentation. The accumulation of these issues undermines the paper's quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Methodological and Training Detail:** The paper omits several crucial details required for reproducibility and for fully understanding the model's behavior.
    *   **Heuristic for Unaligned Words:** The method for handling tokens aligned to NULL or multiple English words is described as assigning "a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." This is a crude heuristic that lacks justification. Why should the tag distribution of an entire, potentially unrelated sentence, be a good prior for a single unaligned word? This choice is not motivated or evaluated against alternatives.
    *   **Training Procedure:** The training process is insufficiently described. The authors mention a two-stage process (pre-training on clean data, then joint training) because direct training is "impractical," but this claim is not substantiated with experimental evidence. Key hyperparameters are missing: What was the batch size? Was any regularization (e.g., dropout, weight decay) used? What were the criteria for switching from stage one to stage two, and for stopping training? The learning rate of 1.0 is mentioned, but for which optimizer? Vanilla SGD? Adam? This detail is critical.
2.  **Questionable Experimental Choices:**
    *   **Alignment Filtering:** The authors follow prior work in retaining only one-to-one word alignments to reduce noise. However, the entire premise of their model is to be robust to noise. This decision seems to undercut the model's potential. A key experiment would be to include many-to-one/one-to-many alignments to see if the noise layer can successfully exploit this larger, noisier data source. Without this, it is unclear if the model is truly more noise-robust or if it simply benefits from the same data filtering as previous methods.
    *   **Citation of Software:** The implementation is based on "clab/cnn," which is an old Lua/Torch-based library. Citing the specific framework (e.g., PyTorch, TensorFlow) and version would be more standard and helpful for reproducibility. Furthermore, footnote 5 ("The dataset was provided directly by Noah Smith.") is not a valid, permanent link to a data source.
3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and formatting inconsistencies.
    *   **Grammar/Typos:** Minor errors are pervasive (e.g., "wildly successful" instead of "widely", "better supervisions", "latent variable" instead of "a latent variable", "a poor local optima").
    *   **Figure Clarity:** In Figure 2, the text labels are too small to be easily legible. The caption for Figure 3 fails to explicitly state which axis corresponds to the gold tags and which to the projected tags, hindering interpretation.
    *   **References:** The reference section is exceptionally sloppy. It contains formatting errors ("Ammar et al\.,? 2016, Ammar et al\.,"), parsing artifacts ("\Q2016\E"), incomplete entries, and inconsistent formatting throughout. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier publication and reflects poorly on the submission as a whole.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more thorough justification for using the entire English sentence's tag distribution for unaligned words? Have you considered or experimented with more context-sensitive heuristics (e.g., using the distribution of tags of neighboring aligned words)?
2.  The paper claims direct end-to-end training is "impractical." Could you please provide results from an attempt at direct training to substantiate this claim and strengthen the motivation for the two-stage process?
3.  Given that your model is designed to explicitly handle noise, have you performed experiments that include the noisier (but more plentiful) many-to-one and one-to-many alignments? This seems like a critical test of the noise layer's capabilities.
