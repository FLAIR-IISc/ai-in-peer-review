**Summary**
This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The authors propose using a combination of a small annotated corpus and a larger, noisy corpus created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The core of their proposed method is a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) tagger augmented with a "noise layer"—a linear transformation matrix—which is trained to model the discrepancies between the model's predictions and the noisy projected labels. This model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), reporting state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that a BiLSTM tagger, when augmented with a simple linear transformation to explicitly model noise from distant supervision, can outperform a previous state-of-the-art maximum entropy model for low-resource POS tagging.
2.  A secondary contribution is the analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, which provides some qualitative insight into the types of errors introduced by the cross-lingual projection process for different language pairs.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves consistent improvements over the main baseline (Duong et al., 2014a) across all ten languages tested. The performance gains, while modest in some of the simulated settings (e.g., +0.0% for German, +0.3% for Italian), are more substantial for the real low-resource languages, particularly Kinyarwanda (+4.2%), demonstrating the potential real-world utility of the approach.
2.  **Sound Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a well-established and practical problem in NLP: how to best utilize large but noisy data sources to supplement small amounts of high-quality annotated data. The use of cross-lingual projection is a standard and realistic scenario for low-resource settings.
3.  **Clear Presentation:** The model architecture and training objective are described clearly and are easy to follow. The motivation for treating the projected labels differently from the gold labels is well-argued.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed "Noise Layer":** The central technical contribution, the "noise layer," is a single K×K linear transformation matrix applied to the model's output distribution. While the framing is interesting, this is a well-established mechanism, functionally equivalent to learning a confusion matrix or adding a task-specific linear output layer in a multi-task setting. The claim that this is the "first [work] to explicitly model the noise" in this manner seems overstated, as learning label confusion is a classical problem. The contribution is more an application of a known technique to a new architecture and task, rather than a novel modeling innovation.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The paper effectively demonstrates that naively mixing clean and noisy data (`BiLSTM Ann+Proj`) is suboptimal. However, it fails to compare against other established, simpler methods for handling noisy labels. For instance, a common baseline would be to apply a simple scalar weight to the loss term for the noisy data (i.e., `L_total = L_annotated + λ * L_projected`), where λ is a hyperparameter tuned on a development set. It is not clear from the paper whether the complexity of learning a K×K matrix is superior to such a straightforward approach. Without this comparison, the justification for the proposed layer is weakened.
3.  **Inadequate Ablation and Analysis:** The authors state that the two-stage training process (pre-training on clean data first) is necessary to avoid poor local optima. This is a critical implementation detail, yet it is presented without empirical evidence. An ablation study comparing this two-stage process to joint training from a random initialization is required to substantiate this claim. Furthermore, the analysis of the learned noise matrix `A` is purely qualitative and superficial. A more rigorous analysis would be beneficial, for instance, by correlating properties of the matrix (e.g., the entropy of its rows/columns) with language-pair characteristics or final performance.
4.  **Conflation of Architectural and Methodological Gains:** The primary baseline, Duong et al. (2014a), uses a maximum entropy model. The proposed model is based on a BiLSTM, which is known to be a much stronger sequence model. The results in Table 1 show that a simple `BiLSTM Annotated` model is already competitive with or superior to the `MaxEnt Supervised` model. Therefore, a significant portion of the performance gain over Duong et al. likely comes from the stronger BiLSTM architecture itself, rather than solely from the noise layer. A more compelling baseline would have been a BiLSTM that incorporates projected tags as features, directly mirroring the feature-based approach of the MaxEnt model it aims to beat. This would better isolate the benefit of the proposed noise-modeling approach.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Can you provide results for a baseline where the model is trained jointly from a random initialization, without the pre-training stage on annotated data? This would validate your claim that the two-stage process is critical for good performance.
2.  How does your proposed model compare to a simpler approach for handling noisy data, such as weighting the cross-entropy loss from the projected corpus by a single, tunable hyperparameter? This would help clarify the value added by the K×K noise matrix over a much simpler regularization technique.
3.  The paper's framing suggests the novelty lies in explicitly modeling the noise. Could you elaborate on how your method is fundamentally different from a standard multi-task learning setup where the BiLSTM has two separate linear output layers: one for predicting the "clean" tag (trained on gold data) and one for predicting the "projected" tag (trained on projected data), with a shared underlying representation?
