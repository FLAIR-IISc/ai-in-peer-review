### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a much larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection. The central and most significant contribution is a novel framework for explicitly modeling the noise inherent in distant supervision. Instead of attempting to heuristically filter the noisy projected labels, the authors augment a BiLSTM tagger with a learnable "noise layer"—a linear transformation that maps the model's 'clean' tag predictions to the noisy label distribution. This allows the model to learn the systematic error patterns of the projection process and effectively "de-noise" the training signal during backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

As I see them, the key contributions are:

1.  **A novel and elegant method for modeling systematic noise in distant supervision.** The idea of introducing a dedicated, learnable layer to transform a model's clean output space to a noisy one is a powerful conceptual leap. It reframes the problem from one of data cleaning to one of noise modeling, which is a far more principled approach. This is a significant contribution that could inspire similar models for other tasks and other sources of noisy data.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of the proposed technique for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a convincing set of experiments showing that this noise-modeling approach consistently outperforms strong baselines and previous state-of-the-art methods across a diverse set of languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and a Bold Conceptual Shift:** The paper's core idea is its greatest strength. Moving away from treating noisy labels as a nuisance to be filtered and towards actively modeling the noise-generating process is a fundamentally better way to think about distant supervision. The implementation as a single, interpretable transformation matrix is both simple and powerful. This kind of bold, conceptually clean idea is precisely what moves the field forward and opens up new avenues of research.

2.  **High Potential for Generalization:** While demonstrated on POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, the "noise layer" framework is a general tool. It could readily be adapted to handle noise from other sources, such as crowd-sourced annotations with systematic annotator biases, or heuristic-based labeling for tasks like relation extraction. The paper provides a blueprint for how to better exploit large but imperfect data sources in a variety of low-data settings.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors validate their method not only on a wide range of simulated low-resource languages (which allows for direct comparison with prior work) but also on two genuinely low-resource languages. The fact that their method surpasses the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on its own evaluation setting is a clear sign of a significant technical advance.

4.  **Excellent Interpretability and Analysis:** The visualizations of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 are extremely compelling. They provide direct, interpretable evidence for the paper's central claim: that the model is learning systematic patterns of confusion in the projected data. Seeing the strong diagonals (indicating trust) alongside specific, high-weight off-diagonals (indicating learned confusions like NOUN -> ADJ) transforms the noise layer from a black box into a transparent and insightful analytical tool.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas where its claims could be strengthened or its limitations explored further. These are not reasons for rejection but rather opportunities for improvement and future work.

1.  **The Noise Model's Simplicity May Be a Limitation:** The proposed noise layer is a single `KxK` matrix, which assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being projected as a noisy tag `j` is independent of the token itself or its context. This is a strong assumption. It is plausible that the projection noise is lexicalized (e.g., specific words are consistently misaligned) or context-dependent. While the simplicity is elegant, the paper would benefit from a discussion of this limitation and the potential for more expressive noise models (e.g., conditioning the transformation `A` on the BiLSTM's hidden state).

2.  **Unexplored Sensitivity to Training Regimen:** The authors state that a two-stage training process is "impractical" to avoid without pre-training on the clean data. This suggests the joint training objective may be difficult to optimize and sensitive to initialization. A deeper analysis of this sensitivity would be valuable. For instance, how much clean data is required for the pre-training to be effective? Can alternative optimization strategies (e.g., a staged learning rate) achieve similar results without a hard two-stage process? This is a minor methodological point but is important for understanding the robustness of the approach.

3.  **Reliance on Systematic Noise:** The method's success is predicated on the noise from projection being systematic enough to be modeled by a compact transformation matrix. The paper shows this holds for their data, but the noise from word alignment can be far more chaotic for typologically distant language pairs or out-of-domain parallel corpora. The analysis hints at this, as Kinyarwanda—with less parallel data—has a more diffuse noise matrix and sees a smaller relative improvement. A more explicit discussion of the point at which noise becomes too random for this method to be effective would be a welcome addition.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The two-stage training process is presented as a practical necessity. Have you experimented with joint training from scratch, perhaps by using a much lower learning rate for the noise matrix `A` in the initial epochs, to see if a good solution can be found without a separate pre-training phase?

2.  Your current noise model is token-independent. Have you considered a more dynamic noise model, for example, by predicting the transformation `A` (or a low-rank update to it) from the BiLSTM hidden state `h_t`? Do you foresee this being a fruitful direction, or would it likely overfit in a low-resource setting?

3.  The paper follows prior work in using only one-to-one alignments to reduce noise. It seems your framework is perfectly suited to handle the noisier, but more plentiful, data from many-to-one or one-to-many alignments. Could the model learn to handle this by treating the projected label `ỹ` as a multi-label distribution? This seems like a very promising application of your core idea.
