### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core problem is that projected data is inherently noisy due to alignment errors and linguistic divergence. The authors propose a novel method to explicitly model this noise. Their model consists of a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger which is trained jointly on both data sources. For the gold-annotated data, it uses a standard cross-entropy loss. For the noisy projected data, the BiLSTM's output probabilities are passed through an additional linear transformation layer (a "noise layer," parameterized by a matrix A) before the loss is calculated against the projected labels. This noise layer is trained to learn the systematic confusion patterns between the true tags and the noisy projected tags. The entire model is trained end-to-end, allowing the error signal from the projected data to be "de-noised" before updating the underlying BiLSTM tagger. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A principled method for modeling label noise in distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a trainable "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation from a model's predicted clean label distribution to an observed noisy label distribution. This is an elegant and generalizable idea that moves beyond heuristic filtering of noisy data, instead learning to leverage the noisy data by understanding its error patterns.
2.  **Strong empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, showing consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art model, across ten different languages. The combination of simulated and real-world low-resource settings demonstrates the robustness and practical utility of the proposed method.
3.  **Insightful analysis of cross-lingual projection noise.** The visualization and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix provide valuable insights into the nature of projection errors for different language pairs, adding a degree of interpretability to the model.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegance and Simplicity of the Proposed Model:** The core idea of adding a single linear layer to model the noise is simple, efficient (adding only KxK parameters, where K is the tagset size), and effective. It provides a principled way to handle label noise that could be applied to other sequence labeling tasks and other forms of distant supervision (e.g., crowd-sourcing) beyond cross-lingual projection.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem. The baseline results in Tables 1 and 2 clearly show that naively combining annotated and projected data (`BiLSTM Ann+Proj`) can harm performance, highlighting the need for a more sophisticated approach. The proposed `BiLSTM+noise layer` model consistently outperforms all baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a), often by a significant margin. The gains on the real low-resource languages are particularly impressive (+1.0% on Malagasy and +4.2% on Kinyarwanda over the SOTA).
3.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation setup is strong. By using 8 simulated low-resource languages from Europarl, the authors can directly compare to prior work on the same data splits. By also testing on two genuine low-resource languages with much smaller and noisier parallel corpora, they demonstrate that the approach is effective in a realistic setting.
4.  **Good Analysis and Interpretability:** The visualization of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is a compelling part of the paper. It confirms the model is learning sensible patterns: strong diagonals indicate that most projections are trusted, while specific off-diagonal weights reveal systematic confusions (e.g., Noun -> Adjective/Number for Malagasy). This analysis helps build confidence that the model is not just a black box but is learning meaningful properties of the data.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Handling of Different Projection Scenarios:** Section 3.1 states that for tokens aligned to multiple words or NULL, a distribution over tags from the source sentence is used. However, it's not specified how this distributional label `ỹ` is used in the cross-entropy objective. Is it a soft label? More importantly, the paper doesn't report the frequency of these cases versus the simpler one-to-one alignments. If the majority of projected labels are one-hot vectors from 1-to-1 alignments, the mechanism for handling these other cases might be less critical. But if they are a substantial portion, this detail is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the learning dynamics.
2.  **Missing Details on the Training Procedure:** The paper mentions a two-stage training process: pre-training on the small annotated data, followed by joint training. Several key details are missing. How was the noise matrix `A` initialized for the second stage? An identity matrix would be a logical choice, assuming projections are mostly correct, but this is not stated. During joint training, are the parameters of the base BiLSTM model updated along with the noise matrix `A`, or are they frozen? The phrase "continue training" suggests all parameters are updated, but this should be made explicit as it has a significant impact on the model.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Heuristic Filtering Baselines:** The introduction argues that prior work uses "heuristics or constraints to clean the projected tag." However, the experiments do not include a baseline that implements such a strategy. A strong baseline would be to train the same BiLSTM architecture on the annotated data plus a version of the projected data that has been filtered using a standard heuristic (e.g., only keeping projections from high-confidence alignments, or removing typologically unlikely projections). Without this comparison, it is difficult to definitively conclude that *modeling* the noise is superior to simply *removing* it.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact form of the projected label vector `ỹt` used in the loss function? Is it a one-hot vector for 1-to-1 alignments and a dense probability distribution for NULL/many-aligned tokens? What proportion of the projected data tokens fall into the latter category for the different language pairs?
2.  Regarding the two-stage training process: How was the noise matrix `A` initialized before the joint training stage? Were the BiLSTM parameters fine-tuned during this stage, or were they kept frozen?
3.  The proposed method learns to model noise, whereas a common alternative is to heuristically filter it. Have you considered a baseline where the projected data is first filtered (e.g., using Giza++ alignment scores) and then used to train a standard BiLSTM? This would provide a more direct comparison between the "modeling" vs. "filtering" paradigms for handling noisy data.
