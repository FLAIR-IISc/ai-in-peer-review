**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, where only a small amount of gold-standard annotated data is available alongside a larger, noisier corpus created via distant supervision. The distant supervision is generated by projecting POS tags from a high-resource language (English) to the low-resource target language using word alignments from a parallel corpus. The authors propose a neural sequence tagging model based on a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) that is designed to jointly learn from both the small "clean" annotated dataset and the large "noisy" projected dataset. The core of their contribution is an additional "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation matrix—that explicitly models the systematic errors and confusions between the true POS tags and the projected POS tags. The model is trained with a composite objective function: a standard cross-entropy loss for the clean data and a modified cross-entropy loss that passes through the noise layer for the noisy data. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through experiments on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A method to explicitly model label noise in distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a simple yet effective mechanism implemented as a trainable transformation matrix. This layer models the conditional probability of an observed noisy label given a true latent label. By integrating this layer into a joint training objective, the model learns to "de-noise" the training signal from the distant supervision data, allowing the shared underlying tagger to benefit from the large-scale noisy resource without being corrupted by it.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the proposed method's effectiveness for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across ten languages (eight simulated and two real-world). The proposed model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art method, establishing new benchmark results for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Strengths**

1.  **The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.** The core problem with using projected data is the systematic noise introduced by alignment errors and linguistic divergence. The authors' approach of explicitly modeling this noise as a learnable transformation is a direct and elegant solution. The mathematical formulation of the joint loss function is clear, and the intuition that the error signal from the noisy data is effectively "cleaned" by backpropagating through the inverse of the noise model is compelling. This provides a principled way to combine supervision signals of varying quality.
2.  **The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.** The paper evaluates the model in two distinct settings: simulated low-resource scenarios using eight European languages and real-world scenarios with Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. This demonstrates the method's robustness across different language families and data conditions. Crucially, the authors compare against the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on the same data splits, making the claims of improvement directly verifiable and significant. The inclusion of several BiLSTM baselines clearly isolates the performance gain attributable to the noise layer itself.
3.  **The paper is clearly written and includes insightful analysis.** The model architecture is well-explained and illustrated effectively in Figure 2. The analysis and visualization of the learned noise matrices (A) in Figures 3 and 4 are a significant strength. They provide qualitative evidence that the model is learning meaningful and interpretable patterns of tag confusion, such as the strong diagonal (most tags are projected correctly) and specific off-diagonal hotspots corresponding to systematic errors. This analysis reinforces the validity of the modeling approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The justification for the two-stage training process is insufficient.** The authors claim that directly training the model jointly from a random initialization is "impractical" as noise might "misdirect training." However, this claim is not substantiated with empirical evidence. A baseline comparing the proposed two-stage training (pre-train on clean data, then fine-tune jointly) with a single-stage joint training from scratch is missing. Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the pre-training step is a critical component or merely a heuristic. It is possible that with appropriate regularization or learning rate schedules, a simpler single-stage training regime would be equally effective.
2.  **The exploration of the noise model's architecture is limited.** The paper models the noise process as a single, static linear transformation (the matrix A). This assumes that the noise distribution is independent of the input token or its context. This assumption may not hold in all cases; for example, the likelihood of a projection error for a specific word might depend on its polysemy or syntactic context. The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore more expressive alternatives, such as a non-linear transformation (e.g., a small MLP) or a dynamic noise model conditioned on the BiLSTM's hidden state.
3.  **The reporting of experimental results lacks detail on hyperparameter tuning and model stability.** The paper specifies the core model dimensions but omits details on the hyperparameter selection process (e.g., use of a development set, range of values tested). More importantly, for a task involving very small annotated training sets (1000 tokens), model performance can be highly sensitive to factors like random weight initialization. The paper reports a single accuracy score for each experiment, which may not be representative. Reporting the mean and standard deviation over multiple runs with different random seeds would be necessary to establish the statistical significance and robustness of the claimed improvements.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the two-stage training process: have you experimented with a single-stage joint training approach where all parameters (BiLSTM and the noise matrix A) are learned simultaneously from a random initialization? If so, how did its performance compare to the pre-training approach described in the paper?
2.  The noise model assumes a context-independent linear transformation between true and projected tags. Did you consider or experiment with more complex noise models where the transformation could be conditioned on the input features or the hidden state of the BiLSTM to capture context-dependent noise patterns?
3.  Given the small size of the annotated training data, how sensitive are the final results to the random initialization of the model weights? Could you provide the mean and standard deviation of accuracy over multiple runs to demonstrate the stability of your model's performance?
