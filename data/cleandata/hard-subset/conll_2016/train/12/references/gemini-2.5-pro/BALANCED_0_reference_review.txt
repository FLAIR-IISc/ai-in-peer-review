### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a large amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural model based on a BiLSTM, augmented with a novel "noise layer." This layer, a learnable linear transformation, explicitly models the systematic errors and confusions between the true POS tags and the noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both data sources: it learns to predict clean tags from the annotated data, and simultaneously learns to transform its clean predictions to match the noisy projected labels. Experiments on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages demonstrate that this approach consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling label noise in distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the "noise layer," an elegant mechanism to explicitly learn a confusion matrix between clean and noisy labels. This allows the model to leverage the signal from a large, noisy dataset without being misled by its errors, effectively learning to "de-noise" the training signal during backpropagation. The approach is general and could be applied to other NLP tasks using distant supervision.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on a challenging low-resource task.** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, demonstrating significant improvements over strong baselines, including prior work that also utilizes projected data. The consistent gains across ten different languages (both simulated and real) provide strong evidence for the model's effectiveness and robustness.
3.  **Insightful analysis of the learned noise patterns.** The visualization of the learned transformation matrix provides valuable, interpretable insights into the cross-lingual projection process. It confirms that the model captures meaningful, language-specific error patterns, which strengthens confidence in the proposed method beyond just accuracy scores.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model design.** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise from distant supervision is highly compelling. Instead of treating projected labels as flawed ground truth or relying on complex heuristics for data filtering, the proposed model learns to account for the noise as part of the end-to-end training process. The joint loss function provides a principled way to combine the strong but scarce signal from gold data with the weak but abundant signal from projected data.
2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental validation.** The experimental setup is a major strength. The use of both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages) and true low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrates the method's wide applicability. Comparisons to a comprehensive set of baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on different data combinations and the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a), clearly isolate and quantify the benefits of the proposed noise layer. The reported results are consistently strong and establish a new state of the art on this task.
3.  **Clarity of presentation and analysis.** The paper is well-written, and the methodology is explained clearly with helpful illustrations (Figure 2). The analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) is particularly strong. It provides qualitative evidence that the model is not a black box but is learning interpretable phenomena, such as the high degree of trust along the diagonal and specific off-diagonal confusions that vary by language. This analysis significantly enhances the paper's contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited discussion of related work on learning from noisy labels.** While the application to cross-lingual projection in NLP is novel, the core idea of learning a label confusion/transition matrix has been explored in the broader machine learning literature, particularly for computer vision. Placing the work in this wider context and discussing similarities and differences with methods like "Training Deep Neural Networks on Noisy Labels with Bootstrapping" (Reed et al., 2015) or other noise-robust learning techniques would strengthen the paper's positioning and clarify its theoretical novelty.
2.  **The necessity of the two-stage training process is not fully explored.** The authors state that they first pre-train the BiLSTM on clean data before commencing joint training, as direct joint training is "impractical." This suggests a potential sensitivity to initialization and a risk of the model collapsing to a poor local minimum if the noisy data dominates early in training. This is a critical practical detail. The paper would be more complete with an ablation study that investigates the impact of this pre-training step. For instance, what is the performance if trained jointly from scratch? How does performance vary with the amount of clean data used for pre-training?
3.  **The strategy for handling non-one-to-one alignments seems overly simplistic.** For words that are unaligned or aligned to multiple source words, the paper assigns a tag distribution based on the tag frequencies of the entire source sentence. This heuristic may introduce uninformative noise, as the tag distribution of a whole sentence is a weak proxy for the tag of a specific unaligned word. While the paper follows prior work in focusing on one-to-one alignments for the main projection, it's unclear if a more sophisticated handling of other alignment types could yield further improvements or allow more of the parallel data to be used.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your "noise layer" and existing work on learning with noisy labels that models a label transition matrix? Highlighting the specific adaptations made for the cross-lingual projection task would help clarify the paper's contribution.
2.  Could you provide results or discussion on the importance of the two-stage training process? Specifically, how does a model trained jointly from a random initialization perform compared to the pre-trained model? This would help readers understand the practical robustness of the proposed training procedure.
3.  The noise layer is a full KxK matrix, which is feasible for the universal POS tagset (K=12). Have you considered how this approach might scale to tasks with much larger label sets (e.g., fine-grained NER with hundreds of types), and whether techniques like low-rank factorization of the matrix A would be necessary?
