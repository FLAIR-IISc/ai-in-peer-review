### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger augmented with a "noise layer." This layer is a linear transformation matrix that explicitly models the relationship between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the observed noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources. The primary contribution is this explicit noise modeling layer, which is evaluated on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, showing improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The paper's evaluation is thorough. It compares against a strong and relevant state-of-the-art method (Duong et al., 2014a) on the same data splits for the simulated low-resource languages. The inclusion of two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) is a significant strength, as it demonstrates the method's applicability in a realistic setting, moving beyond the often-idealized conditions of simulated experiments using high-resource European languages.

2.  **Intuitive Approach:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise process is sensible. Rather than treating projected labels as ground truth or applying complex heuristics to filter them, the model attempts to learn the systematic errors inherent in the projection process. This is a principled way to incorporate information from a noisy source without being entirely misled by it. The results, particularly the notable improvement on Kinyarwanda, suggest this approach is effective.

3.  **Good Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix `A` (Figures 3 and 4) provides valuable insight into what the model learns. The strong diagonals confirm that most projected tags are trusted, while the off-diagonal elements highlight systematic confusions between tags, which vary across languages. This analysis helps to make the model less of a black box and provides evidence that it is learning plausible cross-lingual divergences or projection errors.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The central contribution, the "noise layer," is essentially a linear transformation applied to the output logits of a standard classifier. This architectural component is not new. The idea of learning a confusion matrix or a noise-channel model to handle label noise is a well-established concept in machine learning. While its application to cross-lingual projection for POS tagging may be new, the underlying mechanism lacks fundamental novelty. The authors' claim to be the "first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations" seems overly strong without a more thorough discussion of prior work on learning from noisy labels in other domains.

2.  **Marginal Gains on Simulated Languages:** While the proposed model shows consistent improvements, the gains over the MaxEnt-based model of Duong et al. (2014a) on the eight simulated languages are quite small (an average of +0.4% accuracy). This raises the question of whether the added complexity of a BiLSTM model and a specialized training procedure is justified for such a modest improvement. Established, simpler models like Maximum Entropy classifiers are well-understood and computationally less demanding. The paper does not make a compelling case that this new neural approach is significantly superior in settings where the baseline is already strong.

3.  **Simplistic Noise Model:** The noise model is a single K×K matrix `A`, which assumes that the noise distribution is independent of the specific token being tagged. This is a strong simplification. In reality, projection noise is highly context-dependent: errors are more likely for certain words, for words involved in complex alignments (e.g., idiomatic expressions), or for words whose syntactic function differs greatly between the source and target language. A token-independent noise model cannot capture these nuances and may be a limiting factor.

4.  **Insufficient Justification for Training Procedure:** The authors state that pre-training the BiLSTM on the clean annotated data is necessary to avoid poor local optima. This is a plausible heuristic, but it is presented without empirical evidence. An ablation study comparing the proposed two-stage training with a standard end-to-end training from a random initialization is needed to substantiate this claim and understand the sensitivity of the model to initialization. Without this comparison, it is unclear if the two-stage process is a critical component or merely a convention.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please position the "noise layer" more clearly with respect to existing literature on learning from noisy labels? How does your approach differ conceptually from methods that learn a label confusion matrix to correct the loss function during training?

2.  Given the marginal improvements on the eight simulated languages, under what conditions do you expect your more complex BiLSTM-based model to provide a substantial benefit over a simpler, well-tuned MaxEnt model like that of Duong et al. (2014a)? The large gain on Kinyarwanda is interesting; what properties of this language or its parallel data might explain this?

3.  The noise model assumes the transformation from clean to noisy tags is token-independent. Have you considered the limitations of this assumption? Do you have any thoughts on how the model could be extended to incorporate token- or context-dependent noise?
