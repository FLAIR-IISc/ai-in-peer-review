### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The authors leverage a common distant supervision technique—projecting POS tags from a high-resource language (English) across word alignments in a parallel corpus. Recognizing that these projected tags are inherently noisy, the paper's core proposal is a neural network architecture that explicitly models this noise. The model uses a standard BiLSTM for sequence tagging, but augments it with a "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation matrix—that maps the model's internal "clean" tag predictions to the observed noisy projected tags. This allows the model to be trained jointly on a small set of gold-standard annotated data and a large set of noisy projected data, effectively learning to de-noise the projected supervision signal. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach with strong empirical results on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and elegant method for explicitly modeling projection noise.** The key contribution is the introduction of a learnable noise transformation layer (the matrix A). This is a more principled approach than heuristics or treating projected labels as simple features, as it directly learns the systematic confusion patterns between the true tag distribution and the noisy projected one.
2.  **A robust joint training framework.** The paper effectively combines a small, clean dataset with a large, noisy one. The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, then fine-tuning jointly) is a sensible strategy to guide the model towards a good solution space before exposing it to the noisy supervision.
3.  **Comprehensive and convincing empirical evaluation.** The experiments are extensive, covering ten languages in both simulated and real low-resource settings. The authors compare against strong and relevant baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art, and demonstrate consistent and significant improvements, lending strong support to their claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Intuitive Solution:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem in NLP. The core idea of learning a confusion matrix to model the noise from distant supervision is highly intuitive and elegant. Rather than attempting to filter noise heuristically, the model learns to exploit its statistical patterns. The visualization of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is a major strength, providing interpretability and insight into the model's behavior and the nature of projection errors for different language pairs.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Results:** The evaluation is a clear highlight. Using 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages demonstrates the method's generalizability. The authors show that their approach (BiLSTM+noise layer) consistently outperforms not only naive ways of combining the data (e.g., `BiLSTM Ann+Proj`) but also the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a) on the same data splits. The gains on the real low-resource languages Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly impressive (+1.0% and +4.2% absolute accuracy over the SOTA, respectively).

3.  **Well-Written and Clearly Presented:** The paper is very well-written, with a clear narrative. The motivation is established early on with a sobering experiment showing that naively adding projected data can harm performance (Table 1, `BiLSTM Ann+Proj`). The model architecture is explained well and illustrated effectively in Figure 2, clearly distinguishing between the training paths for annotated and projected data. This clarity makes the paper easy to follow and its contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Architecture:** The underlying sequence tagging model is a standard BiLSTM, which is not novel. The novelty rests almost entirely on the addition of the linear noise transformation layer. While this is an effective idea, it can be viewed as an incremental improvement. The concept of modeling label noise is not new in the broader machine learning literature, though its application in this specific context of cross-lingual projection for POS tagging is well-executed.

2.  **Lack of Detail Regarding Training and Hyperparameters:** The paper lacks sufficient detail for full reproducibility. The authors state they use "stochastic gradient descent model" with a learning rate of 1.0, which is unusually high for vanilla SGD and suggests a more advanced optimizer like Adadelta or Adagrad might have been used. Details on the optimizer, batch size, dropout or other regularization methods, and the specific schedule for the two-stage training (e.g., number of pre-training epochs) are missing. This makes it difficult for other researchers to precisely replicate the results.

3.  **Potential Scalability Issues with the Noise Layer:** The proposed noise layer is parameterized by a `K x K` matrix, where `K` is the number of POS tags. The experiments use the universal POS tagset, where `K` is small (12). The approach is well-suited for this setting. However, the paper does not discuss how this would scale to language-specific, fine-grained tagsets where `K` could be much larger (e.g., 40-50 for the Penn Treebank tagset, or even more for morphologically rich languages). The number of parameters in the noise layer (`K^2`) grows quadratically, which could lead to overfitting when only a small amount of gold data is available to ground the "clean" side of the transformation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the training details? Specifically, which optimizer was used (e.g., vanilla SGD, Adam, Adadelta)? What was the batch size and were any regularization techniques like dropout applied? How did you decide when to switch from the pre-training stage to the joint training stage?

2.  Your method for handling unaligned or multi-aligned tokens is to assign a sentence-level tag frequency distribution from the English side. This seems like a potentially weak heuristic that could introduce uniformative noise. Have you experimented with alternative strategies, such as introducing a special `UNKNOWN_PROJECTION` token/distribution, or simply ignoring these tokens during training on the projected data?

3.  The noise matrix `A` has `K^2` parameters. Have you considered the scalability of this approach to larger, language-specific tagsets where `K` might be 50 or more? Do you foresee issues with overfitting the noise layer in such a scenario, and have you considered any alternative, more parameter-efficient formulations for the noise transformation (e.g., a low-rank factorization of `A`)?
