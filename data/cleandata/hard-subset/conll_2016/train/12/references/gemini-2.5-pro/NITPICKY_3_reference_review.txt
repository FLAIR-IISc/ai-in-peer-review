### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small set of gold-annotated data and a larger, noisier set of data created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The authors propose a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) model that is jointly trained on both data sources. The key novelty is the introduction of a "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation matrix—that explicitly models the systematic errors and discrepancies between the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution and the noisy projected tags. The authors evaluate their model on eight simulated low-resource languages (from Europarl) and two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over several baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art method.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Strong empirical results:** The proposed BiLSTM with a noise layer achieves state-of-the-art performance on two real-world low-resource languages and consistently outperforms strong baselines on eight simulated low-resource scenarios.
2.  **An effective method for modeling noisy supervision:** The paper presents a simple and intuitive method for integrating distant supervision by explicitly modeling the noise as a single K×K transformation matrix. This approach is shown to be more effective than naively combining the clean and noisy datasets.
3.  **Analysis of projection noise:** The authors provide visualizations of the learned noise matrices, offering some interpretability and insight into the types of errors that occur during cross-lingual POS tag projection for different language pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and impactful problem:** The task of developing NLP tools for low-resource languages is of significant importance. The paper's approach of combining small gold corpora with more plentiful but noisy parallel text is a practical and well-established paradigm. The work provides a clear and effective solution within this paradigm.
2.  **Impressive empirical performance:** The proposed model demonstrates substantial and consistent accuracy gains over multiple baselines across all ten languages tested. Outperforming the prior state-of-the-art by 1.0% absolute on Malagasy and a striking 4.2% on Kinyarwanda is a significant result. The "sobering results" presented in Section 3.1 effectively demonstrate that naively using projected data is suboptimal, motivating the need for the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Elegant and simple model:** The core idea of adding a learnable linear transformation (the matrix A) to model the noise is simple, adds very few parameters (K²), and is well-integrated into the end-to-end training framework. This elegance makes the approach appealing and likely easy to reproduce and adapt to other tasks suffering from noisy labels.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong results, the paper is undermined by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, missing experimental details, and a general lack of polish, which collectively call its conclusions into question and make reproducibility difficult.

1.  **Crucial methodological details are missing or unclear.**
    *   **Handling of alignment noise:** The paper states that for tokens aligned to multiple words or NULL, "we assign a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." This is a critical detail that is left frustratingly vague. Is this distribution from the entire English side of the parallel corpus? Or just the source sentence? More importantly, how is a target distribution `y_tilde` used in a standard cross-entropy loss function, which typically expects a one-hot vector? This non-standard objective function is not defined.
    *   **Training procedure:** The paper describes a two-stage training process: pre-training on clean data, then joint training. This is a reasonable heuristic, but no details are provided. How many epochs was the model pre-trained for? What was the criterion to stop pre-training? How was the joint training implemented—by alternating batches, mixing data within batches, or some other method? What was the sampling ratio between clean and projected data? These choices can have a dramatic impact on final performance, and their omission is a major flaw.
    *   **Hyperparameters:** Key details are missing. The learning rate is set to 1.0 with SGD, which seems exceptionally high and potentially unstable. There is no mention of a development set, gradient clipping, regularization, or how hyperparameters were selected.

2.  **Inadequate baselines and comparisons.**
    *   The primary state-of-the-art comparison is with Duong et al. (2014a), which uses a Maximum Entropy classifier. While this is the correct paper to compare against for the task, the architectural differences are vast. A significant portion of the performance gain likely comes from using a BiLSTM over a feature-based linear model.
    *   A much more compelling baseline would be a BiLSTM that is first trained on the small gold dataset and then simply **fine-tuned** on the large projected dataset (treating the noisy labels as gold). This is a standard and powerful transfer learning technique, and its absence makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed noise layer is truly superior to simpler, well-established methods for leveraging out-of-domain or noisy data. The "BiLSTM Ann+Proj" baseline is weak, as simply mixing data of vastly different quality and size is known to be problematic without careful sampling.

3.  **Numerous formatting and writing errors.** The paper reads like an early draft and lacks the professional polish expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Citations and References:** The reference section is deeply flawed. Many entries are malformed with stray characters (e.g., `Ammar et al\.,? 2016`, `Duong et al\.,? 2014a`, `Lafferty et al\.,? \Q2001\E`). The same paper (Duong et al., "What can we get from 1000 tokens?") is cited twice with different keys (2014a and 2014b). In-text citations are inconsistent.
    *   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** The text is rife with minor errors, such as "wildly successful" (p.1), "aside from simple annotated text" (p.2), "to to inform" (p.2), "likely be of considerable use" (p.3), and "may misdirect training result in a poor local optima" (p.4). While individually minor, their sheer number is distracting and suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Figure Clarity:** The caption for Figure 2 is imprecise. The diagram shows `y_tilde` as a single projected label, but the caption mentions a "label distribution," which is not visualized. The data flow from `o` to the noised output `õ` is also not explicitly drawn.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how a target "distribution over tags" (for NULL or many-aligned words) is used as the supervision signal `y_tilde` in the cross-entropy loss? Please provide the exact mathematical formulation for this part of the objective function.
2.  Please provide the missing details of your two-stage training procedure: (a) What was the stopping criterion for the pre-training stage? (b) During joint training, how were batches from the annotated and projected corpora sampled and combined?
3.  Have you considered a simpler and more standard baseline of pre-training the BiLSTM on the 1000 gold tokens and then fine-tuning it directly on the projected data? How does your proposed model compare to this approach? This seems like a critical comparison to justify the additional complexity of the noise layer.
