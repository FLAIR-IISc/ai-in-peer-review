### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The core problem is how to effectively leverage a large amount of noisy, "distantly supervised" data (generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language) in conjunction with a very small set of high-quality, gold-annotated data.

The main contributions of this work, in my view, are:

1.  **A Novel and Elegant Model for Handling Projection Noise:** The primary contribution is the proposed "noise layer," a trainable linear transformation (matrix `A`) applied to the output of a standard BiLSTM tagger. This layer explicitly models the systematic errors and confusions that arise from the cross-lingual projection process. By training this layer to predict the noisy projected labels from the model's internal "clean" predictions, the error signal backpropagated to the underlying BiLSTM is effectively "de-noised." This provides a principled way to learn from noisy labels without corrupting the main tagging model.
2.  **A Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper presents a strong empirical case for its method. The evaluation is not limited to one or two languages but spans eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The authors compare against relevant and strong baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art method (Duong et al., 2014a) on identical data splits, which makes for a direct and convincing comparison.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Projection Noise:** Beyond just reporting higher accuracy scores, the authors provide qualitative analysis by visualizing the learned noise transformation matrices. This offers valuable insights into the types of errors common in cross-lingual projection for different language pairs and demonstrates that the model is indeed learning meaningful patterns about the noise, as hypothesized.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Method:** The central idea of explicitly modeling the noise-generating process is very well-motivated. It is a known issue that direct projection is noisy due to alignment errors and linguistic divergence. Rather than using heuristics to filter the data, this paper proposes learning a model of that noise. The architecture, which separates the core tagging model from the noise model, is clean and intuitive.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method (BiLSTM+noise layer) consistently outperforms all baselines across all ten languages tested. It achieves new state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, demonstrating its effectiveness in a real-world low-resource scenario. The gains over naively combining the annotated and projected data ("BiLSTM Ann+Proj") are particularly telling, proving the value of the noise-handling component.
3.  **Excellent Analysis and Interpretability:** The visualizations of the learned `A` matrix (Figures 3 and 4) are a significant strength of this paper. They provide compelling evidence that the model is not just a black box. The matrices reveal understandable linguistic patterns, such as the strong diagonal (most tags project to themselves) and specific off-diagonal confusions. This analysis validates the authors' hypothesis and deepens the reader's understanding of the problem.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated, the proposed model is explained well (Figure 2 is particularly effective), and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to be reproducible. The motivation for the two-stage training process (pre-training on clean data first) is also clearly justified.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** While the application is novel and effective for this NLP task, the underlying machine learning concept of adding a layer to model label noise is not entirely new. The noise layer is essentially a single, task-specific linear layer. The contribution lies more in the clever and successful application of this idea to cross-lingual projection rather than in a fundamental deep learning innovation.
2.  **Dependence on a Two-Stage Training Process:** The authors state that the model must be pre-trained on the clean annotated data before being jointly trained, as random initialization leads to poor local optima. This is a practical limitation. It raises questions about the model's robustness when the clean dataset is extremely small (e.g., <500 tokens), as the pre-training phase might not be sufficient to provide a good initialization for the BiLSTM. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the model to the size of this initial clean dataset.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Alternative Noise-Handling Techniques:** The related work section focuses on prior methods for low-resource POS tagging. However, it could be strengthened by a discussion of more general machine learning methods for learning from noisy labels (e.g., label correction models, modeling label transition matrices, co-teaching). While the authors claim their work is the "first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," positioning it within this broader context would provide a clearer picture of its novelty and relationship to other noise-robust learning paradigms.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The two-stage training process appears crucial. How sensitive is the final performance to the amount of annotated data used in the pre-training stage? For example, if you were to halve the annotated training data from 1000 to 500 tokens for the simulated experiments, how much does the performance of your method degrade compared to the baselines?
2.  Could you clarify the mechanism for handling tokens aligned to multiple English words or none at all? The paper states these are assigned a "distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." How is this soft label distribution used as the supervision signal `ỹ` in the cross-entropy loss function for the projected data?
3.  Have you experimented with initializing the noise matrix `A` in a more informed way, rather than learning it from scratch (presumably after an identity initialization)? For instance, one could compute an empirical confusion matrix on a small held-out set where both projected and gold labels are available. Could such an initialization scheme potentially speed up convergence or even remove the need for a separate pre-training stage?
