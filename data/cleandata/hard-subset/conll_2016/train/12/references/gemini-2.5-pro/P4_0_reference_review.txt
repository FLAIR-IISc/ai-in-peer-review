### Summary
This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, where only a small amount of gold-annotated data is available. The authors propose to leverage larger, but noisier, datasets created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The core of their proposal is a neural sequence model (a BiLSTM) augmented with a "noise layer". This layer is a learnable linear transformation that explicitly models the systematic errors (or "noise") introduced by the projection process. The model is trained jointly on the small, clean dataset to predict true POS tags and on the large, noisy dataset to predict the projected tags via the noise layer. This joint objective allows the model to learn a robust underlying tagger by effectively "de-noising" the training signal from the projected data. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating that their approach outperforms several baselines and previous state-of-the-art methods.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel neural architecture for learning from distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a simple yet effective mechanism for explicitly modeling the transformation between clean labels and noisy, distantly-supervised labels. By parameterizing this transformation as a learnable matrix, the model can adapt to the specific error patterns in the distant data, allowing the main model to benefit from this additional supervision without being corrupted by the noise. This method provides a principled way to integrate data from clean and noisy sources.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on low-resource POS tagging.** The paper presents a strong empirical case for the proposed model. It achieves new state-of-the-art accuracy on two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. Furthermore, on a set of eight simulated low-resource languages, the model consistently matches or exceeds the performance of a strong baseline from prior work, demonstrating the robustness and effectiveness of the approach across different language pairs.

### Strengths
1.  **High degree of novelty and technical soundness.** The proposed method of adding a learnable noise transformation layer is an elegant and novel way to tackle the problem of label noise in distant supervision. It moves beyond heuristic filtering or naive data mixing by directly modeling the noise-generating process. The joint training objective is well-motivated, and the architecture allows error signals from the noisy data to be "cleaned" before updating the shared model parameters. This is a technically sound approach to a common problem in NLP.
2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental evaluation.** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive. The use of both simulated and real-world low-resource settings validates the method's effectiveness in different scenarios. The authors compare against a strong set of baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art on the same data splits, which makes the reported performance gains credible. The inclusion of results from naively mixing the datasets (e.g., "BiLSTM Ann+Proj") clearly demonstrates the value added by the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to understand. The motivation for the problem is well-established, and the proposed model is described clearly with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The results are presented transparently, and the analysis, including the visualization of the learned noise matrices, provides useful insight into what the model is learning.
4.  **Generality of the approach.** While the method is applied to low-resource POS tagging, its core mechanism is task-agnostic. The idea of learning a transformation from a model's clean predictions to a set of noisy labels could be readily applied to other tasks that rely on distant supervision, such as relation extraction, named entity recognition, or tasks using crowd-sourced annotations. This broad potential impact is a significant strength.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited analysis of the model's properties and training.** The paper proposes a two-stage training process (pre-training on clean data, then joint training) to avoid poor local optima, but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim over a simpler one-stage joint training. Furthermore, while the learned noise matrices are visualized, the analysis is brief. An investigation into how the matrix `A` changes with varying amounts of clean or noisy data, or its sensitivity to initialization, would provide a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.
2.  **The model assumes a simple, global noise process.** The noise layer is parameterized by a single `K x K` matrix `A`, which assumes that the probability of a true tag `i` being projected as a noisy tag `j` is constant across all words and contexts. However, projection errors are often context-dependent (e.g., dependent on surrounding words or the ambiguity of the source word). The model cannot capture such complex noise patterns, which may limit its effectiveness in scenarios with highly varied or non-systematic noise.
3.  **Potential scalability issues are not discussed.** The noise layer introduces `K^2` parameters, where `K` is the size of the tagset. This is manageable for POS tagging with a universal tagset (`K=12`). However, for tasks with a very large label space (e.g., fine-grained entity typing with hundreds of classes), this quadratic growth in parameters could make the noise layer difficult to train effectively without a very large amount of data. The paper does not address this potential limitation or suggest ways to mitigate it.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The two-stage training regimen is justified by the need to avoid poor local optima. Could you provide results from an experiment where the entire model is trained jointly from a random initialization in a single stage? This would help quantify the empirical necessity of the pre-training step.
2.  The noise model is currently a static, token-independent transformation. Have you considered making the noise transformation dynamic, for example by conditioning the matrix `A` on the BiLSTM's hidden state `h_t`? This could potentially capture more complex, context-dependent noise patterns.
3.  How does the performance gain from your method vary with the amount of available clean annotated data? It would be instructive to see an analysis showing the accuracy improvement over a supervised-only baseline as the amount of annotated data is varied (e.g., from 100 to 5000 tokens), to better understand the data-efficiency of the approach.
