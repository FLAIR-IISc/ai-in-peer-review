Summary and contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by jointly learning from a tiny gold-annotated corpus and noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is to explicitly model the noise in projected tags via a learned linear “noise layer” (a K×K confusion matrix A) applied to the BiLSTM’s clean tag distribution. Training proceeds in two stages: (i) pretrain on gold data; (ii) jointly fine-tune on gold and projected data, with the noise layer guiding learning from noisy supervision. The method is evaluated on 8 simulated European languages (Europarl) and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent gains over strong baselines and prior work.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective noise-aware output layer that learns when/how to trust cross-lingual projections by modeling a confusion mapping between clean and projected tags, integrated into a BiLSTM tagger and trained end-to-end.
2) Empirical validation across both simulated and genuinely low-resource settings, including analysis of learned confusion matrices that offers interpretable insights into projection noise.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) that consistently improves over naïve uses of projected data and prior state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: The noise layer is a clear, principled way to reconcile small clean supervision with noisy distant supervision. It aligns well with known issues in cross-lingual projection (alignment errors, syntactic divergence) and is easy to implement and plug into existing neural taggers. This simplicity increases the likelihood of adoption.
- Solid empirical gains where it matters: On two real low-resource languages, the method achieves new state-of-the-art accuracies (86.3% Malagasy, 82.5% Kinyarwanda), outperforming both Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013). Improvements are also consistent across eight simulated languages. These results strongly support the central claim that explicitly modeling noise makes projections more useful.
- Sensible training and evaluation setup: The paper uses widely accepted low-resource settings (1k gold tokens for simulated languages; small gold sets for real languages), leverages parallel data that is realistic for low-resource scenarios, and compares against relevant baselines (MaxEnt, Duong et al., BiLSTM variants). The decision to retain only 1-1 alignments and to filter sentences by alignment scores is defensible and common practice.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of the learned A matrices are instructive, showing when tags are trusted (strong diagonals) and where systematic confusion occurs (off-diagonal structure), differing by language and data quality. This supports the intended role of the noise layer and offers qualitative insight beyond aggregate accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Modeling a label-noise/confusion channel atop a classifier is a straightforward idea with precedent in noisy supervision. The novelty here is its application to cross-lingual POS projection and its integration with a BiLSTM, rather than a fundamentally new learning principle. This limits the conceptual depth of the contribution.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity studies: Key design choices are not systematically evaluated. Examples: (i) the necessity and effect size of gold-only pretraining; (ii) the impact of the projected data weighting vs. gold data (the loss sums appear unweighted aside from normalization); (iii) initialization of A (identity vs. random) and stability across runs; (iv) handling of multi/NULL alignments via sentence-level tag distributions. These experiments would clarify what drives gains.
- Comparisons to stronger sequence taggers and alternatives: The baseline neural tagger is a BiLSTM with token-wise softmax. A BiLSTM-CRF or character-aware models could be stronger baselines for POS tagging, and prior cross-lingual methods (e.g., constraint-based or propagation-based) could be combined with the proposed approach. Without these, the margin over the best prior method in simulated settings is modest, and it is unclear how the proposed idea fares against stronger neural baselines.
- Reproducibility and statistical reporting: Important training details are sparse (epochs, batch size, regularization/dropout, gradient clipping, early stopping). No variance or statistical significance is reported, and results appear to be single-run. Given small improvements on some languages, significance testing or multiple seeds would bolster confidence.
- Noise model simplicity: The confusion matrix A is global and context-agnostic. It does not leverage alignment confidence, alignment type (1-1 vs. many-to-one), or word-specific features. This may limit its ability to model heterogeneous noise patterns, especially in truly low-resource settings with noisier alignments (as hinted by Kinyarwanda’s higher off-diagonal mass).

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized? Did you try identity or near-identity initialization to encourage trust in projections? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Did you tune the relative weighting of the gold and projected loss terms? If not, why not, and what happens if you vary this trade-off?
- How critical is the two-stage training? Please provide an ablation where you (a) train jointly from scratch and (b) train gold-only without projected fine-tuning.
- Do results change with a BiLSTM-CRF decoder or character-level embeddings? Even a small study on 1–2 languages would contextualize the gains versus a stronger architecture.
- How many runs per configuration did you perform? Can you report mean/std over multiple seeds and/or significance tests?
- For the real languages, how was the mapping to universal tags done on the gold sets, and is there any chance of mismatch with the English-side tagger’s mapping?
- Did you explore incorporating alignment probabilities or NULL/multi-alignment indicators as features or conditioning signals for A (e.g., multiple A’s or a gating function)?

Overall evaluation
This paper addresses a real and important problem with a simple, effective idea, and demonstrates meaningful empirical gains in genuinely low-resource settings. The methodological novelty is moderate, and additional ablations/stronger baselines would strengthen the case. Nevertheless, the approach is well-motivated, interpretable, and practically useful. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying and, if possible, expanding empirical evidence around the training scheme and robustness.
