- Summary: tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining tiny gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projections via a learned K×K noise layer on top of a BiLSTM.

- Contribution 1: proposes a simple, end-to-end learnable noise-aware output layer that models confusion between clean and projected tags.

- Contribution 2: validates the method empirically on 8 simulated European languages and two real low-resource languages, analyzing learned confusion matrices for interpretability.

- Contribution 3: offers a practical training recipe—gold pretraining followed by joint training with projected data—that consistently improves performance.

- Strength: conceptually clear and practical—easy to implement and addresses known projection errors (alignment, divergence).

- Strength: strong empirical gains on real low-resource languages (new SOTA for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) and consistent improvements across simulated languages.

- Strength: sensible experimental setup using accepted low-resource settings, realistic parallel data, and relevant baselines.

- Strength: interpretable analysis—visualized A matrices show when projections are trusted and systematic confusions.

- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—label-noise/confusion modeling is straightforward and not fundamentally new.

- Weakness: lacks key ablations and sensitivity studies (pretraining necessity, loss weighting, A initialization, multi/NULL alignment handling).

- Weakness: comparisons omit stronger sequence-tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, char-level models) and other cross-lingual methods.

- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—missing training hyperparameters, single-run results, no variance or significance testing.

- Weakness: noise model is global and context-agnostic, not leveraging alignment confidence, alignment type, or token-specific signals.

- Question: how is A initialized and how sensitive are results to initialization (identity vs random)?

- Question: was the relative weighting of gold vs projected loss tuned, and what is the effect of varying this trade-off?

- Question: how critical is two-stage training—results for joint-from-scratch and gold-only ablations?

- Question: do results hold with stronger decoders or features (BiLSTM-CRF, character embeddings)?

- Question: how many runs per configuration, and can the authors report mean/std and significance tests?

- Question: for real languages, how was mapping to universal tags done and could there be mismatches with English-side tags?

- Question: did the authors try conditioning A on alignment probabilities, alignment types, or token-specific signals (multiple A’s or gating)?

- Overall evaluation: well-motivated, interpretable, and practically useful approach with meaningful real-world gains, but moderate novelty and needs additional ablations, stronger baselines, and robustness reporting—leaning toward acceptance if clarified.
