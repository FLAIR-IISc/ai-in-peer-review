Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a simple neural architecture for low-resource POS tagging that jointly trains on a small gold corpus and noisy cross-lingual projections. The key component is a learned “noise layer,” a linear transformation on the model’s predicted tag distribution, intended to model systematic distortions introduced by projection/alignment noise.
- The method is evaluated on 8 simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing small average gains over the strongest prior projection-based baseline on the simulations, and larger gains on the two real languages. The learned noise matrices are visualized and interpreted.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical instantiation of label-noise modeling for cross-lingual POS projection: a global confusion matrix atop a BiLSTM to capture mapping from clean to projected tags, trained jointly with gold and projected data.
2) An empirical study across 10 languages showing that simple explicit noise modeling can match or slightly exceed the best prior projection-based system, with larger gains in a truly low-resource scenario.

Strengths
- Relevance and simplicity: The problem is important and the proposed modification is minimalistic, easy to implement, and compatible with standard neural taggers. This lowers the barrier for practitioners to better exploit noisy projection signals.
- Empirical coverage: The paper reports results on 8 languages plus 2 genuinely low-resource languages, with consistent improvements over a strong prior baseline (Duong et al., 2014a) on average, and a notable gain on Kinyarwanda (+4.2 points vs Duong et al.).
- Clear ablation against naïve uses of projection: The comparisons BiLSTM Annotated vs Projected vs Ann+Proj convincingly show that naïve incorporation of projected labels can hurt, motivating the need for explicit noise handling.
- Interpretability: Visualizations of the learned noise matrices are informative and help validate that the model captures plausible cross-tag confusions and alignment-induced biases.
- Training practicality: The two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint) is a sensible compromise and likely necessary in practice; the method appears robust in diverse settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established label-noise modeling: The approach amounts to learning a global confusion matrix on outputs, a well-known technique in learning with noisy labels and distant supervision. While applying it to cross-lingual POS projection is reasonable, the conceptual advance is modest, and related work on explicit noise modeling in NLP/distant supervision is not adequately discussed or contrasted.
- Small average gains on simulations; lack of significance testing: On the 8-language suite, the average improvement over the strongest prior method is 0.4 accuracy (91.7 vs 91.3), with several per-language differences being very small. No statistical significance or variance across runs is reported. This undermines the strength of the claims in the higher-resource simulation condition.
- Potential mismatch and clarity issues in the real low-resource setup: The paper emphasizes “tiny” gold data but uses ~5k gold tokens for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. It is unclear whether the baselines (Duong et al., Garrette & Baldridge) used the same amount of gold supervision and exactly the same splits. Without a controlled 1k-token setting for these languages (to match the paper’s framing and prior work), it is hard to attribute the improvements solely to the method rather than more supervision or corpus differences.
- Inconsistencies/insufficient detail on projection procedure: Early sections describe handling many-to-one/unaligned tokens by assigning sentence-level tag distributions, but Section 4.1.2 says only one-to-one alignments are retained. It is unclear which labeling regime actually produced the projected labels used for training, and how much projected data was discarded by the one-to-one constraint. The weight between gold and projected losses also appears un-tuned (simple averaging), which may not be optimal across languages.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines: No ablation on the noise layer (e.g., identity initialization vs learned; learning without pretraining), loss weighting, or alternative regularization (label smoothing, confidence weighting) is provided. Modern POS taggers often include character-level features or CRF decoding; these are not considered. It would be useful to compare against a BiLSTM-CRF with char embeddings trained with simple confidence weighting of projected labels to better isolate the benefit of the proposed noise modeling.

Questions to authors
- Real low-resource setup: Exactly how many gold tokens were used for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda training, and do the reported Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge numbers correspond to the same splits and supervision size? Can you report results with only 1k gold tokens to align with the paper’s stated scenario?
- Projection details: Did you in fact use only one-to-one alignments (discarding others), or did you supply sentence-level tag distributions for multiply aligned/unaligned tokens as earlier described? Please quantify coverage: what fraction of tokens in the parallel corpora contributed projected labels per language?
- Loss weighting and optimization: How are the supervised and projected losses weighted? Were these weights tuned on dev data per language? How sensitive are results to this weighting and to the pretraining duration?
- Initialization of the noise matrix A: How is A initialized (e.g., identity, small random, or from confusion estimates)? Does initializing near identity help stability and final accuracy?
- Robustness and significance: Can you report variance over multiple random seeds and statistical tests for the 8-language suite? On which languages are gains consistently significant?
- Alternative baselines: Have you tried character-level embeddings and/or CRF decoding, and confidence weighting of projected labels? How does the proposed noise layer compare to these more established improvements?

Additional comments
- The method is practically useful, but the paper would benefit from clearer experimental controls and stronger evidence of generality beyond modest average gains. Clarifying the real low-resource setup and adding ablations/significance tests would substantially strengthen the case.
