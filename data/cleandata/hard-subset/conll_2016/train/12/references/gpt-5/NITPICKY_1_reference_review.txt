Summary and contributions
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small set of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The core idea is to train a BiLSTM tagger jointly on gold data and projected data via an explicit noise layer: a learned K×K transformation A maps the clean output distribution o to the projected/noisy distribution before computing loss on projected labels. Training proceeds in two stages (pretrain on gold; then joint training), and experiments on 8 simulated languages (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show improvements over prior work (Duong et al., 2014a) and simple BiLSTM baselines.

My view of main contributions (in order):
1) Method: A simple, explicit label-noise modeling layer (confusion-matrix-like A) integrated into a sequence tagger to better exploit cross-lingual projected POS tags.
2) Empirical results: Consistent gains over prior projection-based methods across 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages, with analyses of learned A matrices.
3) Practical training recipe: Two-stage training (gold pretraining + joint) for stabilizing learning with noisy labels.

Strengths
- Clear, useful idea with good bias-variance tradeoff: The A matrix is an interpretable, low-parameter mechanism to correct systematic cross-lingual projection noise. It naturally handles both hard projected tags and soft “sentence-level” distributions and supports mismatched tagsets. The method is easy to implement and broadly applicable.
- Strong empirical performance: On both simulated (Europarl) and real low-resource settings (Malagasy/Kinyarwanda), the approach outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) and supervised-only baselines. The improvements are consistent and practically meaningful (e.g., 86.3%/82.5% on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda).
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on clean data before joint training is reasonable and likely essential; the paper articulates the motivation clearly.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of A reveal language-specific noise patterns (e.g., diagonals vs off-diagonals, Swedish peculiarities, more noise in Kinyarwanda), offering insight and aiding trust in the approach.
- Focused on a real need: The setting (1000-token gold sets + parallel corpora) matches realistic constraints in low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity gaps and inconsistencies:
  - Alignment protocol inconsistency: You state retaining only 1-1 alignments, yet for tokens aligned to many or none you assign sentence-level tag distributions. If many-to-one/one-to-many/NULL cases are excluded, how do these arise? If they are included, how are they filtered vs used? Please reconcile this.
  - Projected labeling for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens uses the “tag frequency distribution over the English sentence,” which is a very crude prior. No justification, ablation, or comparison to standard alternatives (e.g., discarding these tokens, marginalization over aligned sets, alignment posteriors) is provided.
  - Initialization/regularization of A is underspecified. The text hints random A is harmful; was A initialized to identity or zeros? Any penalty encouraging diagonality or sparsity? Any temperature/scaling? The figures imply values in [−1,1], but training constraints/regularization are not detailed.
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient:
  - No BiLSTM-CRF baseline (now standard for POS tagging), no comparison to other noise-robust learning strategies (e.g., EM with confusion matrices, posterior regularization, label-noise correction literature). Even a CRF layer atop the BiLSTM could be a strong and relevant baseline.
  - Missing ablations: (i) two-stage vs single-stage training, (ii) learning A vs fixing A=I, (iii) hard vs soft projected labels, (iv) varying amounts/quality of projected data, (v) effect of 1-1 restriction vs using many-to-one/one-to-many.
- Experimental rigor and reporting:
  - No variance, confidence intervals, or significance testing; runs appear single-seed. With small training sets (1000 tokens), variance can be large.
  - Hyperparameters are minimal (LR=1.0 SGD), with no discussion of batch size, epochs, early stopping, gradient clipping, dropout/regularization, or dev set tuning. This undermines reproducibility and comparability.
  - Data selection: “first 1,000 tagged tokens” may bias the training distribution. Random sampling and multiple random splits would be more robust.
  - No details on the number of projected tokens retained after filtering, nor alignment quality diagnostics or filtering thresholds (for the IBM Model 3 sentence selection step).
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing label-noise modeling: Using a learnable confusion matrix to map clean to noisy labels is known in the broader ML/NLP literature; the contribution here is its application to cross-lingual POS with a BiLSTM. The paper does not situate itself properly w.r.t. label-noise correction literature.
- Presentation and formatting quality issues:
  - References are in poor shape: placeholders (“?,” “\Q2016\E”), duplicated entries, incomplete metadata, inconsistent formatting/escaping, and erratic diacritics (e.g., Täckström). This is below venue standards.
  - Several typos and grammatical issues (“wildly successful” likely “widely”; “to to inform”; others).
  - Some figure/text mismatches and notational sloppiness (binary vs one-hot/distributional ỹ; õ vs o; Eq. 2 notation could be cleaner). Thresholds for filtering are not reported. Footnotes are dangling and hard to follow.

Questions to authors
- Alignment/labeling: Do you truly restrict to 1-1 alignments? If so, which tokens receive sentence-level tag distributions and why? If not, how are multi-alignments handled and how many such instances exist after filtering?
- Projected-label assignment: Why use sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens? Did you try discarding them, or leveraging alignment posterior probabilities? Any ablation?
- A initialization/regularization: How do you initialize A in stage 2? Any diagonality/sparsity constraints or priors? Any temperature scaling? Did you try tying A to be near identity?
- Baselines/ablations: Results with a BiLSTM-CRF baseline? Single-stage training? A fixed to identity? Different amounts of projected data? Hard vs soft projected labels?
- Experimental robustness: How many runs per setting? Any confidence intervals? How were hyperparameters tuned? Any dropout or gradient clipping?
- Data/Code: Will you release code, data splits, and scripts for projection and filtering?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is appealing and results are promising, but methodological inconsistencies, weak baselines/ablations, missing details on A and alignment handling, lack of statistical rigor, and poor presentation (especially references) collectively lower confidence. With stronger experimental baselines/ablations, clarified methodology, and polished writing, this could be a solid contribution.
