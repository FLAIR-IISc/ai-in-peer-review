Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small gold corpus with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger augmented with a learned “noise layer” (a K×K transformation A) that maps the model’s clean output distribution to match the distribution of projected tags, enabling joint training on gold and projected data and learning when/how to trust projections. Experiments on eight simulated European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a).

Primary contributions (as I see them):
1) Method: A simple, effective noise-adaptation layer atop a neural sequence tagger to explicitly model projection-induced label noise and jointly train on gold and projected data.
2) Empirical evaluation: Broad evaluation across 8 simulated and 2 genuinely low-resource languages, with consistent gains and interpretable analyses of learned noise matrices.
3) Analysis/interpretability: Visualization of A revealing cross-lingual tag confusions and noise structure, offering insight into projection reliability across languages.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of projection noise: The use of a dedicated confusion-like matrix A to transform clean tag distributions into projected-tag distributions is principled, lightweight (only K^2 parameters), and integrates seamlessly with backprop. It directly tackles systematic cross-lingual divergences and alignment errors without brittle heuristics.

- Consistent improvements with small gold data: On average over 8 languages, the BiLSTM+noise layer modestly but consistently outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) (91.7 vs 91.3), and yields the best reported results on Malagasy (86.3) and Kinyarwanda (82.5). The pattern that “BiLSTM Projected” underperforms but “BiLSTM+noise” recovers and improves reinforces the contribution.

- Generality and simplicity: The approach is model-agnostic at the output level and should transfer to other tasks with distant supervision (NER, dependency labels). Computational overhead is negligible, making the method practical for truly low-resource settings.

- Interpretability and qualitative insight: Heatmaps of A across languages reveal meaningful diagonals and plausible confusions (e.g., determiners vs nouns or pronouns), and greater noise for Kinyarwanda than Malagasy, aligning with data quality/size differences. This provides convincing evidence that the method learns to calibrate trust in projected labels.

- Sensible training protocol: Two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint) is appropriate given the risk of noisy supervision dominating early optimization. The design choice is reasonable and likely important in practice.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to label-noise adaptation literature: The core idea—learning a noise/confusion layer to model label corruption—has precedents in learning with noisy labels and distant supervision. While applying it explicitly to cross-lingual projection for POS is useful, the methodological novelty within the broader ML/NLP literature is moderate. A discussion situating this work among noise-adaptation approaches (e.g., learning a noise transition matrix) is missing.

- Global, context-independent noise model: A is shared across all tokens and contexts, which cannot capture context- or lexeme-specific projection errors (e.g., alignment quality, word order changes, lexicalized idiosyncrasies). This undermines the “learning when to trust” claim: the method learns which gold tag pairs are confusable on average, not when a specific projection should be down-weighted. Incorporating alignment confidence, source-side features, or conditioning A on context could strengthen the approach.

- Incomplete comparison and ablations: The gains over Duong et al. are modest on average (0.4 points), and significance is not reported. There is no ablation on the pretraining stage, initialization/regularization of A (e.g., identity prior), or weighting between gold and projected losses. Since performance depends on these choices, more analysis is needed to attribute improvements to the noise layer versus training protocol.

- Projection labeling and alignment inconsistencies: The paper first describes handling many-to-one alignments and NULLs by using sentence-level tag distributions, but later states that only one-to-one alignments are retained. It remains unclear how NULLs are treated in the final setup and whether multi-aligned tokens are fully discarded. Using sentence-level tag distributions for NULLs seems crude; more principled soft labeling (e.g., alignment posteriors, lexical translation probabilities) could reduce noise further. An ablation on alignment choices (1-1 vs including 1-N/M-1) and label smoothing would be informative.

- Missing regularization and hyperparameter details: No mention of dropout, early stopping criteria, epochs, or development set tuning; given the small gold set (1k tokens), overfitting is a risk. Also, the loss balances gold and projected terms by averaging, effectively giving them equal weight regardless of projected corpus size; sensitivity to this balance is not explored.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Did you try identity initialization and/or a penalty toward I to prevent degenerate mappings? Any observations on training stability?

- How is the relative weighting between gold and projected losses chosen? Your objective averages each term; did you sweep a weight to trade off the two supervision sources? Sensitivity analysis?

- What is the final treatment of NULL alignments and many-to-one/one-to-many cases? You state retaining only one-to-one alignments; do NULLs receive sentence-level tag distributions, and if so, how much do they contribute to training? Any ablation results?

- Did you conduct statistical significance testing against Duong et al. (2014a), especially given modest average improvements across the 8 languages?

- Could you report results with additional baselines that incorporate alignment/posterior confidence (e.g., weighting projected tokens by alignment probability) or soft labels derived from multiple aligned source tokens?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider conditioning the noise model on simple features (e.g., alignment score, presence of many-to-one links, source-side POS n-grams) to capture context-dependent projection reliability.

- Reporting training/regularization details (dropout, epochs, early stopping) and releasing code would substantially improve reproducibility.

- Since you mention rectangular A for mismatched tagsets, a small experiment demonstrating robustness to tagset mismatches would strengthen generality claims.

Overall, this is a clear, useful contribution with solid empirical results and good interpretability. The methodological novelty is incremental but well-executed; clarifying and strengthening the evaluation and ablations would make the case more compelling.
