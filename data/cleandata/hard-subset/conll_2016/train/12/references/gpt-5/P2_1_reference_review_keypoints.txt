- Novelty: Moderate — sensible instantiation of learned label-noise/confusion matrix for cross-lingual POS projection, but similar ideas exist in noisy-label and crowd-annotation literature.
- Significance: Good — consistent gains across 8 simulated and 2 true low-resource languages, especially Kinyarwanda.
- Clarity: Fair–good — main model is clear and intuitive, but important details about projected label construction and hyperparameters are missing or inconsistent.
- Empirical soundness: Solid but could be stronger — fair comparisons and informative analyses, but missing ablations and significance tests weaken causal claims.
- Reproducibility: Limited — key implementation and data-processing choices are underspecified and no code is provided.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a simple neural architecture (BiLSTM + K×K noise layer) to learn a mapping between clean and projected/noisy tag distributions, trained jointly with a two-stage schedule.
- Contribution 2: Empirically matches or outperforms prior distant-supervision methods across multiple languages, showing the noise layer prevents degradation from naïvely mixing projected and gold data.
- Contribution 3: Analyzes learned noise matrices for interpretable, language- and pipeline-specific projection errors.

- Strength: Principled handling of projection noise via a learned confusion matrix that integrates easily into neural taggers with low parameter cost.
- Strength: Consistent improvements across languages/settings, with notable gains in truly low-resource scenarios.
- Strength: Sound experimental setup with fair comparisons to prior work and demonstration that naïve mixing can hurt performance.
- Strength: Interpretability — learned A matrices provide qualitative evidence of plausible confusions and alignment artifacts.
- Strength: Simplicity and generality — plug-and-play for sequence taggers and applicable to other noisy distant-supervision tasks.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — insufficient engagement with broader noisy-label and crowd-annotation literature.
- Weakness: Inconsistent and unclear description of how projected labels are constructed (one-to-one vs many/NULL alignments, distribution weighting).
- Weakness: Missing training/model details (batch size, epochs, dropout, optimizer, A initialization/constraints) harming reproducibility.
- Weakness: Missing ablations and robustness studies (pretrain vs joint training, A initialization/regularization, varying projected-data amount/quality, alternative baselines, stronger taggers).
- Weakness: No significance testing and potential sampling bias from using the “first 1,000 tokens” rather than multiple random samples.

- Question: Do you include many-to-one or NULL-aligned tokens in training, and how are their label distributions defined/weighted given the one-to-one claim?
- Question: How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized/constrained, and is pretraining required for stability?
- Question: What are batch size, epochs/early stopping, dropout/weight decay, optimizer details, dev selection, and any subsampling or loss weighting between gold and projected data?
- Question: Are improvements over Duong et al. statistically significant across multiple random 1k-token samples, and how sensitive are results to parallel corpus size/quality and alignment thresholds?
- Question: Have you tried the noise layer with a CRF decoder or character-aware taggers, and do gains persist for morphologically rich languages?
- Question: How would the approach handle mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) in practice?
