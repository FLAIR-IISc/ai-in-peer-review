Summary and contributions
- Summary: The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by leveraging both a small amount of gold annotation and noisy cross-lingual projections from English. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger augmented with a learned linear “noise layer” (a K×K transformation) that maps the model’s clean tag distribution to the distribution of projected (noisy) tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretraining on gold data, then joint training on gold and projected data. Experiments on eight simulated Europarl languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show improvements over prior projection-based baselines.
- Contributions (as I see them):
  1) A simple, explicit label-noise modeling layer for projection-based POS tagging, trained jointly with a BiLSTM, to learn when/how much to trust projected labels.
  2) Empirical evaluation on multiple languages (8 simulated + 2 real), showing consistent, if sometimes modest, gains over strong older baselines (Duong et al. 2014) and simple BiLSTM variants.
  3) Qualitative analysis of learned noise matrices that reveals plausible cross-tag confusions and provides interpretability.

Strengths
- Clear and pragmatic idea: The linear noise layer is a minimal, interpretable addition that directly targets a known problem in projection (label noise). It integrates smoothly with standard sequence models and can be trained end-to-end.
- Consistent empirical improvements: Across eight Europarl languages, the method edges out Duong et al. (average +0.4 accuracy) and substantially improves over naïve use of projected data. On real low-resource data, improvements are meaningful for Kinyarwanda (+4.2 over Duong) and modest for Malagasy (+1.0), suggesting practical value.
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on gold then joint training is a reasonable way to stabilize learning with noisy supervision; this mirrors best practices in label-noise settings.
- Interpretability: Visualization of the learned A matrices is informative and shows that the model captures plausible cross-tag confusions (e.g., NOUN→ADJ/NUM), lending credibility to the approach and its intended behavior.
- Broad applicability: While only POS is tested, the formulation is generic and could transfer to other distant supervision settings; the parameter overhead is small.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established label-noise literature: Modeling a confusion/transition matrix between clean and noisy labels is well-known (e.g., in crowdsourcing and distant supervision). The contribution is primarily an application to cross-lingual POS projection with a BiLSTM, rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea. The paper does not position itself against this broader literature nor compare against alternative noise-aware approaches (e.g., instance reweighting, EM over latent clean labels).
- Coarse noise model: The noise layer is global and context-independent, yet projection errors depend on alignment quality, syntactic divergence, and token-specific properties. A static K×K matrix may capture priors but not conditional noise patterns. The paper lacks ablations to test whether A is doing more than simple reweighting (e.g., A fixed to identity, A diagonal-only, or per-tag biases).
- Baselines are dated/incomplete: No comparisons to stronger modern POS architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character-level features), more sophisticated projection heuristics (confidence-weighted projection, posterior constraints), or multilingual transfer methods (multilingual embeddings, multi-source transfer). This weakens claims of state-of-the-art performance.
- Evaluation and comparability concerns: 
  - The “low-resource” real-language setting uses ~5k gold tokens for training, which is much larger than the 1k tokens repeatedly motivated. This undermines the core premise and complicates comparisons to prior work claiming 1k-token regimes.
  - It is unclear whether data splits for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda exactly match those of Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge; the latter appears to use a different setup. Without strict comparability or statistical significance testing, gains—especially small ones on Europarl—are hard to interpret.
  - The projected labeling scheme for unaligned/many-to-one tokens uses sentence-level tag distributions, which is ad hoc and potentially harmful; no ablation contrasts simpler filters or confidence thresholds.
- Claims outpace evidence: The paper claims support for mismatched tagsets but only evaluates universal tags on both sides. It also suggests broad applicability beyond POS without experimental support. No analysis is provided on sensitivity to the quantity/quality of parallel data or to alignment noise.

Questions to authors
- Data and splits: For Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, how many gold tokens are used for training, dev, and test? Are your splits identical to Duong et al. (2014) and to Garrette & Baldridge (2013)? If not, please clarify differences and justify the comparisons.
- Initialization/regularization of A: How is A initialized (identity, random, diagonal + small noise)? Are there constraints or priors (e.g., encouraging diagonality) or any regularization applied to A? Did you try constraining rows to be stochastic?
- Ablations: 
  - Performance with A fixed to identity (i.e., no noise layer) but with the same two-stage training.
  - A diagonal-only A (per-tag bias) vs full A.
  - Joint training from scratch vs your two-stage regimen.
  - Filtering projected labels by alignment confidence vs your sentence-level selection; using many-to-one alignments with the noise layer.
  - Statistical significance of reported gains.
- Sensitivity: How does performance vary with the amount of projected data? Please include learning curves for parallel data size and for gold data size (particularly in the truly 1k-token regime).
- Tagset mismatch: Do you have any experiments where English uses a richer tagset (e.g., PTB) and the target uses universal tags to substantiate the “rectangular A” claim?
- Implementation details: Were hyperparameters tuned on dev sets? Any use of dropout/early stopping? Will code and data splits be released?

Overall, the paper targets an important problem and presents a sensible, interpretable solution with encouraging results. However, the methodological novelty is limited, baselines are not fully up to current standards, and several evaluation details need clarification and ablation to solidify the claims.
