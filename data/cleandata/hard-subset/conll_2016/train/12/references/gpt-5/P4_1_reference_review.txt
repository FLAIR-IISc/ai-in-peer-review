Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold-annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected annotations. The core idea is to explicitly model the systematic noise in projected labels via a learnable “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger. Training proceeds in two phases: pretraining on gold data, then joint training on gold and projected data where the noise layer transforms clean predictions to match the distribution of projected tags. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and 2 genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work.

Contributions, in order of strength:
1) A noise-aware sequence tagging model that explicitly learns a mapping between clean POS tags and noisy projected tags, enabling effective joint training on gold and distant supervision.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages, including two truly low-resource languages, with consistent improvements over strong baselines and prior state-of-the-art.
3) Qualitative analysis of learned noise transformations, providing interpretable insight into projection noise patterns across languages.

Strengths
1) Addresses a central limitation of cross-lingual projection—label noise—in a principled and simple way. The learnable noise transformation allows the model to exploit projected data without being misled by systematic alignment/tagging errors. The two-stage training (pretrain on gold; then joint) is a sound mitigation of identifiability/local optimum issues and is justified by empirical gains over “Annotated,” “Projected,” and “Ann+Proj” baselines.
2) Strong empirical results and breadth of evaluation. The method matches or exceeds prior work (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a) on 8 simulated settings and achieves new reported results on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), improving over both projection-based and resource-light baselines. The consistency of improvements supports the generality of the approach.
3) Clear demonstration that naïvely mixing projected with gold data can hurt, and that the proposed noise-aware mechanism recovers and surpasses performance. The controlled comparisons (BiLSTM Annotated vs Projected vs Ann+Proj vs +noise layer) substantiate the claim about the necessity of noise modeling.
4) Interpretability via visualization of the learned noise matrices. The diagonals and specific off-diagonal patterns (e.g., determiner/noun relations, PRT/X confusion) provide evidence that the model captures realistic projection errors and cross-lingual divergences rather than overfitting idiosyncrasies.
5) Practicality. The approach uses standard components (BiLSTM, softmax, a small matrix A), straightforward backpropagation, and requires only modest architectural changes, making it easily adoptable.

Weaknesses
1) Noise model formulation and identifiability. The mapping p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o) with o = p(y|x) differs from the standard class-conditional noise model (ỹ ~ T·y with T stochastic). As defined, A operates on soft probabilities through a softmax, which reduces direct probabilistic interpretability as a confusion matrix. Without constraints (e.g., stochasticity, diagonal dominance, regularization), there is potential parameter non-identifiability between the base classifier and A. The paper mitigates this with pretraining but does not analyze stability, sensitivity to initialization, or add structural constraints on A.
2) Limited ablations on modeling choices. The paper does not report: (a) training from scratch vs two-stage; (b) fixing A=I vs learning A; (c) alternative noise parameterizations (e.g., linear T over logits or probabilities); (d) weighting the relative contributions of projected vs gold losses; or (e) regularization of A. Such ablations would clarify which components are essential and how robust the approach is.
3) Projection heuristics are ad hoc and under-analyzed. Assigning sentence-level tag-frequency distributions for multi- or NULL alignments is a coarse heuristic and may introduce bias. The decision to retain only 1-to-1 alignments discards substantial data. There is no comparison to alternative projection clean-up strategies (e.g., marginalizing over alignments, posterior weighting, or graph propagation) or to using all alignments with noise modeling.
4) Data and sensitivity analysis. While real-language parallel data sizes are reported (100k sentences for Malagasy, 11k for Kinyarwanda), there is no sensitivity analysis on the amount/quality of parallel data, alignment error rates, or the proportion of projected vs gold data. It remains unclear how performance scales in truly scarce-parallel scenarios or with noisier alignments.
5) Reporting and clarity details. Some references and formatting are inconsistent; training details (dev-based early stopping, dropout/regularization, number of runs, variance, and statistical significance) are missing. The claim that the approach supports mismatched tagsets via rectangular A is not empirically validated.

Questions to authors
- Why choose the mapping p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o) instead of a standard class-conditional noise model with a stochastic transition matrix T (i.e., p(ỹ=j|y=i)=Tij and p(ỹ|x)=T^T o)? Did you try alternatives (e.g., transforming logits) and, if so, how did they compare?
- How is A initialized, and is any constraint or regularization applied (e.g., diagonal bias, L2, temperature)? How sensitive are results to these choices and to random seeds?
- How is the relative weighting between the gold and projected losses chosen? Given the much larger projected dataset, did you explore down/up-weighting or batching strategies to avoid dominance by distant supervision?
- Can you provide ablations for (a) training without the pretraining stage; (b) A fixed to identity; and (c) different treatments of multi/NULL alignments (e.g., excluding them, marginalizing alignments, or using alignment posteriors)?
- Do results change if you include many-to-one and one-to-many alignments while relying on the noise layer to absorb the additional noise? A sensitivity curve vs the quantity of projected tokens would be informative.
- Have you tested the rectangular A setting for mismatched tagsets in practice, or is this a theoretical extension? If tested, please report results.
