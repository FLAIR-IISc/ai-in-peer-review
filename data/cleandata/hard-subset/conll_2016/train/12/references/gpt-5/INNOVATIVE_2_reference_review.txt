Summary and contributions
The paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by leveraging both a tiny amount of gold annotations and noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is to explicitly model projection noise via a learnable “noise layer”: a linear transformation A that maps the model’s clean tag distribution to the observed projected tag distribution, trained jointly with a BiLSTM tagger. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold data, then continue jointly on gold and projected data. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013), achieving 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit, and learnable model of projection noise via a confusion-like transformation A applied to the tag distribution, enabling the model to “learn when to trust” projected labels.
2) A practical joint training framework (with pretraining) that integrates gold and projected supervision end-to-end and yields consistent improvements across 10 languages.
3) Interpretable analyses of learned A matrices that reveal cross-lingual divergences and error patterns, supporting the method’s intended behavior.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: Explicitly modeling projection noise with a learnable transformation is a clean, bold idea. Prior low-resource POS work often uses heuristics or indirect joint features; here the paper introduces a direct, parameter-efficient mechanism that is easy to drop into neural taggers. The approach feels general and applicable beyond POS/projection (e.g., other distant/crowd-supervised settings).

- Consistent, meaningful empirical gains: Across 8 simulated languages, the BiLSTM+noise layer matches or exceeds Duong et al. (2014a) with an average accuracy of 91.7% (vs 91.3%). On the real low-resource setting, the model achieves new SOTA: 86.3% on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda, outperforming Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013). Naive use of projected data hurts or helps only slightly, while the noise layer turns projections into clear gains, validating the central premise.

- Interpretability and diagnostic value: The learned A matrices are informative. Strong diagonals show that projections are often trustworthy, but off-diagonals capture systematic divergences (e.g., DET↔NOUN, NOUN↔ADJ/NUM for Malagasy; PRT↔X for Swedish). These visualizations substantiate the claim that the method learns tag-specific trust and transformations, not just a black-box improvement.

- Practicality for low-resource settings: The method uses widely available resources (parallel corpora + word alignments) and a standard BiLSTM architecture without heavy feature engineering. The two-stage training is simple. This makes the approach attractive for many languages where gold data remain scarce.

- Generality and extensibility: The paper notes rectangular A for mismatched tagsets and suggests applicability to other noisy supervision regimes. The mechanism should integrate well with richer encoders (char/subword) or better aligners in future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to broader label-noise literature: While likely novel in cross-lingual POS projection, the idea of learning a noise/transition matrix between clean and noisy labels has prior art in distant supervision and label-noise modeling. The paper would benefit from positioning against that line of work and clarifying the specific advance here (e.g., operating in logit space, joint training dynamics with sequence models).

- Global, context-independent noise model: A is shared across all tokens and contexts. Projection noise depends on alignment type, alignment confidence, and lexical/morphological properties. The current model cannot adjust trust by context (e.g., downweight verbs only when alignment is NULL or many-to-one). Even a simple conditioning on alignment features or a gating scalar could improve fidelity.

- Crude handling of alignment edge cases: For tokens aligned to many words or NULL, the label is replaced by the sentence-level tag distribution, which seems ad hoc and potentially harmful. A comparison with standard alternatives (e.g., majority over aligned words, NULL masking with loss downweighting, alignment-probability-weighted mixtures) is missing.

- Baselines and ablations could be stronger: The main neural baseline is a BiLSTM without character/subword features and without CRF inference. Given modern POS baselines, character-level modeling often provides large gains in low-resource settings. Ablations that isolate the effect of pretraining, compare different initializations/constraints for A (e.g., identity init), or evaluate alternatives to softmax(A o) (e.g., a proper stochastic transition T) would help establish robustness and design choices.

- Reporting details and statistical rigor: Statistical significance is not reported for improvements on the real low-resource datasets (e.g., +0.6 on Kinyarwanda over Garrette & Baldridge). Training details about A (initialization, regularization/constraints to avoid degenerate solutions) are sparse. Sensitivity to the size/quality of parallel data (Malagasy vs Kinyarwanda) is only discussed qualitatively.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How do you initialize and constrain A? Do you use identity initialization, any regularization (e.g., L2, entropy), or constraints to make it a valid transition matrix? Did you observe degenerate solutions (e.g., collapsing to majority tags)?

- Why choose sentence-level tag distribution for multiply aligned/NULL cases? Can you report results comparing against (a) majority vote among aligned words, (b) alignment-probability-weighted mixtures, or (c) ignoring those tokens (masking) or downweighting their loss?

- Can you report ablations on: (i) removing the pretraining stage; (ii) training with A fixed to identity vs learned; (iii) conditioning noise on simple alignment features (one-to-one vs many-to-one vs NULL) via separate A’s or a gating scalar?

- Do you have significance tests for the real low-resource gains, particularly on Kinyarwanda? Also, can you provide learning curves vs amount of parallel data to quantify data-quality sensitivity?

- You mention support for mismatched tagsets (rectangular A). Did you try this setting? If not, could you comment on practical considerations (e.g., regularization to avoid overfitting when tagsets differ)?

Overall, I find the core idea elegant and impactful: a small, learnable component that turns noisy projections into useful supervision. Despite some methodological simplifications and missing ablations, the conceptual contribution and empirical gains justify acceptance.
