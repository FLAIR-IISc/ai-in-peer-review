Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projections from English. The core idea is to model the systematic noise in projected tags via a learnable transformation (“noise layer”) on top of a BiLSTM tagger. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold only, then jointly train with gold and projected data while learning the noise transformation. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work and stronger BiLSTM baselines.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, effective noise-aware learning framework that leverages projected supervision by learning a small confusion-like transformation between clean and projected tag spaces, integrated end-to-end atop a BiLSTM tagger.
2) Empirical demonstration that explicitly modeling projection noise outperforms both naïve use of projected data and prior cross-lingual projection methods under comparable supervision budgets, including on two genuinely low-resource languages.
3) Analysis of the learned noise matrices that provides interpretable insights into cross-lingual projection errors.

Strengths
- Clear, practically useful idea with strong empirical payoff: The proposed “noise layer” operationalizes a widely recognized need (projection noise) in a minimal, end-to-end way. It yields consistent improvements over (i) BiLSTM trained on gold only, (ii) BiLSTM using projected data naively, and (iii) Duong et al. (2014a), with especially meaningful gains on real low-resource languages (e.g., +4.2 points on Kinyarwanda vs. Duong et al.). This addresses a persistent stumbling block in cross-lingual supervision.

- Sensible training procedure and architecture: The two-stage training (pretrain on clean, then jointly with projected) is well-motivated to avoid early overpowering by noisy supervision. The architectural choice (BiLSTM) is appropriate and widely adopted for tagging, easing reproducibility and extension.

- Interpretable diagnostics: Visualizations of the learned transformation matrices are insightful. They verify that the method indeed learns to “downweight” unreliable projections and highlight language-specific divergences (e.g., determiners, pronouns), which is valuable for practitioners.

- Realistic evaluation setting: Beyond simulated low-resource experiments, the paper evaluates on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda with small parallel corpora and small gold datasets, aligning with realistic constraints.

- Generality: The framing is broadly applicable to other distant supervision settings (e.g., NER, parsing) and other sources of noisy labels (e.g., crowd annotations), which increases the potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Questionable parameterization of the noise model: The paper calls A a confusion matrix, but the likelihood uses softmax(A o), where o is a probability vector from the base softmax. This is not equivalent to a standard confusion/noise channel p(ỹ|y) with p(ỹ|x)=C^T o. The proposed parameterization mixes probabilities via a linear transform followed by softmax, making A difficult to interpret as p(ỹ|y), and potentially conflating model calibration with noise. A clearer probabilistic formulation with a row-stochastic C (and possibly a prior favoring near-identity) would be preferable. An ablation comparing softmax(A o) vs. C^T o would be important.

- Limited sequence modeling on the output: The model uses per-token softmax rather than a CRF layer with transition modeling. Sequence-level constraints are standard in POS tagging and can especially help under noisy supervision. It is unclear whether the proposed gains persist once a CRF is included. Including a BiLSTM-CRF baseline with/without the noise layer would strengthen the case.

- Projected label construction is crude for non-1:1 alignments: Tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments are assigned sentence-level tag distributions, which is ad hoc and likely to introduce avoidable noise. Stronger alternatives (e.g., using alignment posteriors, fractional labels from neighboring aligned words, or graph-based propagation) could materially change results. An ablation on this choice is needed.

- Missing regularization/identifiability discussion: The model learns both base classifier and noise layer jointly without constraints. There is a risk of identifiability issues or degenerate solutions (e.g., A absorbing what should be in o). No priors, regularizers, or constraints (e.g., diagonal dominance, near-identity penalty) are described, nor is initialization of A clarified. This is important for stability and interpretability.

- Incomplete experimental controls and reporting: 
  • No variance or significance analysis; some gains on Europarl are small (~0.4 average). Report seed variability. 
  • Architectural details are sparse: no character/subword features, dropout, or pretrained embeddings discussed—choices that materially affect low-resource POS. 
  • Fairness of comparisons: Duong et al. (2014a) is a MaxEnt-based method; showing a stronger neural baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, with chars) would make the incremental value of the noise layer more compelling.
  • The claim that the approach handles mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) is not evaluated.

Questions to authors
- Noise model: Why use softmax(A o) instead of a standard confusion channel C with p(ỹ|x)=C^T o? Can you report an ablation comparing these parameterizations and discuss interpretability and training stability?

- Constraints/priors on A: How is A initialized? Did you try regularizers (e.g., L2, diagonal dominance, identity prior) or constraints (row-stochastic) to improve identifiability and prevent degenerate solutions?

- Output layer: Did you try a CRF layer and, if so, how do the results change with/without the noise layer?

- Projection details: What proportion of target-side tokens are 1:1 aligned? How sensitive are results to your handling of NULL/many-to-one aligned tokens (e.g., using sentence-level tag distributions vs. alternative strategies)?

- Robustness and variance: Please report mean±std over multiple runs and statistical significance of improvements, especially on simulated languages.

- Features and resources: Are embeddings randomly initialized? Any use of character/subword modeling? Do your conclusions hold with stronger baselines that include these features?

- Mismatched tagsets: Can you demonstrate the rectangular A use case empirically (e.g., source-side fine-grained tags to target-side universal tags)?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The idea is simple, novel in this specific setting, and practically useful, with clear empirical benefits on realistic low-resource languages. However, the probabilistic grounding of the noise layer, missing ablations/variance, and baseline strength need clarification. Addressing these questions would solidify the contribution.
