Summary and contributions
- Primary: Introduces a simple yet principled “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger that explicitly models the transformation between clean gold POS tags and noisy cross-lingual projected tags via a learnable K×K matrix. This lets the model learn when and how much to trust distant supervision.
- Secondary: Proposes joint training on small gold data and large projected data with a two-stage schedule (pretrain on gold, then continue with both), enabling end-to-end de-noising through backpropagation.
- Tertiary: Provides empirical evidence on 8 simulated (Europarl) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) with consistent gains over strong prior methods, plus interpretable analyses of the learned noise matrices.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and generality: Explicitly learning a noise transformation for projected labels is a clean, bold idea that squarely tackles a long-standing pain point in projection-based supervision. The approach is broadly applicable (other tasks, other noisy signals such as crowdsourcing) and integrates seamlessly with neural sequence models.
- Meaningful improvements on true low-resource settings: The gains on Malagasy (86.3 vs 85.3) and especially Kinyarwanda (82.5 vs 78.3; also > Garrette & Baldridge’s 81.9) are practically significant and aligned with the paper’s motivation. Improvements on simulated languages are small but consistent.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The method adds only a small number of parameters (K×K) and produces interpretable matrices that reveal cross-lingual divergences and alignment artifacts. Figures 3–4 convincingly show the model learning plausible mappings (e.g., determiner/number/adj tendencies, PRT/X confusions).
- Sound training design: The two-stage training (gold pretraining, then joint) is sensible and appears crucial; the paper anticipates optimization pitfalls and addresses them without overengineering.
- Thorough evaluation breadth: Evaluated on 10 languages, with realistic parallel corpora sizes (not just Europarl). The projection pipeline and data choices are clearly stated, making the scenario credible for low-resource work.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses: While there are reasonable baselines (BiLSTM on annotated, projected, union; MaxEnt; prior SOTA), important ablations are missing: (a) noise layer vs identity A (i.e., noising disabled) under the same joint training; (b) alternative treatments of NULL/multi-alignments (e.g., drop, majority tag, confidence-weighted training); (c) impact of sentence filtering and 1–1 alignment restriction; (d) sensitivity to size/quality of projected data. These would strengthen the causal claim that A is the key factor.
- Modeling choice of A is under-specified: p(ŷ|x)=softmax(A o) treats A as a linear transformation in logit space over the model’s posterior o. This is not equivalent to a proper class-conditional confusion matrix, nor is it constrained to be stochastic or nonnegative. The paper should clarify the probabilistic interpretation, initialization (e.g., identity), and whether any constraints/regularization are used to avoid degenerate solutions.
- Baseline strength and fairness: The BiLSTM baseline appears relatively bare-bones (no character/subword features, no CRF layer, no pretrained embeddings, no dropout). Stronger neural baselines might reduce absolute margins on simulated languages. Although the target is low-resource, it would help to show that gains persist when the base tagger is stronger.
- Statistical significance and error analysis: No significance tests are reported, and error analyses beyond matrix visualizations are limited. Per-tag breakdowns, examples of corrected errors, and significance on real languages would bolster the empirical case.
- Exposition and references: There are noticeable typos (“wildly” vs “widely”), incomplete/duplicated citations, and stray footnote markers. Some details are buried (e.g., projected-label distributions for NULL/multi-alignments) and deserve clearer, earlier exposition.

Questions to Authors
- Noise layer specifics: How is A initialized? Identity, near-identity, or random? Any constraints (e.g., diagonal bias, L2/L1 regularization, temperature scaling) to keep it close to a confusion-like mapping or prevent overfitting?
- Probabilistic interpretation: Do you view A as acting in logit space or as a learned “label-noise adapter”? Did you experiment with a true confusion matrix parameterization (e.g., column-stochastic Q with p(ŷ|y)=Q) and p(ŷ|x)=Q^T o? If so, how did it compare?
- Confidence and alignment uncertainty: Did you try weighting projected instances by alignment quality or IBM model scores, or passing alignment features into the noise layer? How robust is the approach when many tokens are NULL or multi-aligned?
- Ablations: What is the performance if you train jointly on gold+projected without A (i.e., A=I) but keep the same schedule? And what if you drop the sentence filtering heuristic?
- Stronger baselines: Do the gains hold with a stronger BiLSTM (char/subword, CRF decoding, dropout, pretrained embeddings)? Even small-scale tests on 1–2 languages would be informative.
- Significance: Are the improvements on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda statistically significant over Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013)?

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a clear, novel, and broadly useful idea—learning when to trust projected labels via an explicit noise layer—that yields meaningful gains in genuinely low-resource settings. While additional ablations and clarifications would improve the work, the conceptual contribution and practical impact justify acceptance.
