Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging with distant supervision from cross-lingual projection. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger with an additional linear “noise layer” (a K×K matrix A) applied to the model’s output distribution when learning from projected labels, aiming to learn a mapping between clean and noisy tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold data, then jointly train on gold and projected data. Experiments on 8 Europarl languages (simulated low-resource) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show improvements over prior work (Duong et al., 2014a) and baseline BiLSTMs.

Contributions as I see them (in order of significance)
1) A simple and effective label-noise adaptation layer for projected supervision in a neural POS tagger, yielding consistent improvements over strong baselines across languages.
2) An empirical study showing that naïvely mixing projected and gold data can hurt, and that explicitly modeling projection noise helps, with visualizations of learned confusion matrices.
3) Results on two genuine low-resource languages that are competitive or slightly better than prior work.

Strengths
- Practical, conceptually clean modeling of projection noise: The noise layer is a minimal addition (one linear transform) that makes intuitive sense for cross-lingual projection, is easy to train with backprop, and is likely portable to other sequence labeling tasks. The two-stage training is reasonable.
- Solid empirical gains across multiple languages: On 8 Europarl languages, the proposed method edges out Duong et al. (2014a) on average and on most languages; on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda it improves over both Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge (though the margins are modest). The naïve BiLSTM controls help motivate the approach.
- Analysis of learned noise: The A matrices provide interpretable evidence that the model learns plausible confusions. The observation that noisier Kinyarwanda yields a noisier A is consistent with the performance pattern.
- Focus on realistic supervision: The scenario of very limited gold labels and noisy projection is important and relevant. The design choices (e.g., avoiding many-to-one alignments) reflect awareness of projection pitfalls.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and insufficiently situated: Modeling noisy labels via a learned linear “confusion”/adaptation layer is a known idea in learning from noisy supervision. The paper does not position itself against general label-noise correction methods (e.g., noise adaptation/confusion-matrix layers, multi-head supervision) nor convincingly differentiate from Duong et al.’s two-output model beyond architecture choice. A deeper comparison or ablation (e.g., separate output head vs linear noise layer, bias term, constrained/initialized A) is needed.
- Missing and inconsistent methodological details:
  - In §3.1, the projected label for many-to-one/NULL is described as a distribution, yet §4.1.2 states only one-to-one alignments are retained. If many-to-one and NULL are excluded, the distribution case is moot; if not excluded, how frequently are distributions used, and how are they represented in the loss?
  - The text alternates between ỹ as a one-hot “binary random variable” and as a “distribution,” and Eq. (2) uses Ỹ while Eq. (1) uses y; sampling yt ~ Multinomial(ot) is not part of training and is confusing.
  - Orientation and parameterization of A are unclear/inconsistent: Eq. (2) defines logits via ∑i ai,j ot,i, which corresponds to õ = softmax(Aᵀ o), but the loss uses softmax(A o). Please clarify shape, orientation, and whether a bias term is used.
  - Training: No details on initialization of A (identity vs random), gradient clipping, dropout/regularization, early stopping, batch composition (mixing gold/projected), number of epochs, or seed averaging. With LR=1.0 and vanilla SGD, stability is questionable.
  - Loss weighting: The joint objective normalizes by corpus sizes but has no tunable λ between gold and projected losses. With 200k projected sentences vs 1k gold tokens, how is the gold signal not swamped? This is critical for reproducibility and fairness.
- Evaluation issues and limited analysis:
  - Significance/variance is not reported. Many gains over Duong et al. (2014a) are small (often ≤0.6). No multiple runs/seed variance are given.
  - No ablations on: (i) one-stage vs two-stage training, (ii) effect of the amount/quality of projected data, (iii) adding a bias term or constraints to A, (iv) using many-to-one alignments, (v) annealing the projected loss.
  - The “rectangular A for mismatched tagsets” is mentioned but not evaluated; all experiments are on universal tagset.
- Real low-resource setup inconsistencies:
  - The introduction emphasizes “only 1000 tokens,” but Malagasy/Kinyarwanda use ~5k gold tokens; please justify and reconcile with related works’ setups. Are Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge numbers truly on the same splits and training sizes? Table 2 claims “same data split,” but the training size appears different from the 1k-token framing.
  - Parallel data sizes are tiny for Kinyarwanda (52k tokens). Report actual count of aligned tokens and coverage. Are all English tags from Stanford POS reliable in this domain?
- Numerous formatting, citation, and clarity issues:
  - Many references are incomplete, duplicated, truncated, or with TeX artifacts (e.g., “\Q2016\E”, “?”, duplicated Duong et al. 2014a/2014b entries with identical titles, truncated proceedings). Names/diacritics inconsistent (Täckström vs Täckström).
  - Typos and word choice: “wildly successful”→“widely,” “correspondance,” “noised output,” etc. Figures are referenced (Figure 1) but not shown; axis labels and scaling of A matrices ([-1,1]) are not explained.
  - Footnotes are embedded in text and indexing is off.

Questions to authors
1) How exactly is A initialized? Did you try identity initialization and/or adding a bias term? Any constraints (e.g., row-stochastic, non-negativity)?
2) How are batches formed and losses weighted between gold and projected examples? Did you tune a λ to balance them? How many projected tokens were used per epoch relative to gold?
3) Do you actually use distributional projected labels in training, or did you restrict to one-to-one alignments only? If both, what fraction are distributions and how are they incorporated in the loss?
4) Can you report results averaged over multiple random seeds and significance tests vs Duong et al. (2014a)? Also provide an ablation of the two-stage training vs training from scratch jointly.
5) For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, what is the exact size of gold training data used for each method and whether the splits match Duong et al./Garrette & Baldridge? If you use ~5k tokens, can you report the 1k-token setting for comparability?

Additional comments
- Please clarify Eq. (2) orientation and explicitly define õ = softmax(B o + c) (naming B for clarity), and specify whether c=0. The current notation is ambiguous.
- Include hyperparameters (dropout, epochs, clipping), early stopping criterion, and code to ensure reproducibility.
- Consider reporting coverage of projected labels (percentage of aligned tokens) and an analysis of error types reduced by the noise layer.
