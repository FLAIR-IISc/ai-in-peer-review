Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected labels. The core idea is to explicitly model the systematic noise in projected labels via a learnable “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger. The model is trained in two stages (pretrain on gold, then joint training with gold and projected data), with separate losses for clean and projected supervision. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit noise-adaptation layer for handling projected label noise in sequence tagging, trained jointly with a BiLSTM backbone and leveraging both clean and projected supervision.
2) An empirical study showing that explicit noise modeling leads to consistent improvements over strong prior projection-based baselines across 10 languages, with clear visualizations of learned confusion structure.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) demonstrating robustness in realistic low-resource scenarios.

Strengths
- Clear, effective modeling of projection noise: The K×K noise layer is a principled way to capture systematic mismatches between true and projected tags (e.g., determiners misprojecting to nouns, verb misalignments). The formulation is compact (on the order of 100 parameters) and easy to optimize, yet expressive enough to capture recurrent projection biases. The visualization of learned matrices offers interpretable evidence that the model learns sensible confusions.

- Consistent empirical gains across settings: On average, the method improves over Duong et al. (2014) on 8 Europarl languages (91.7 vs 91.3) and shows stronger improvements on the real low-resource languages (e.g., +4.2 absolute on Kinyarwanda over Duong; +0.6 over Garrette & Baldridge). Even where gains are modest, they are consistent and come with a simple architectural change.

- Sensible training procedure and loss design: Separating losses for gold and projected data, and pretraining on gold to anchor the clean prediction head, addresses identifiability concerns and prevents the noise layer from absorbing genuine signal. Using soft labels for ambiguous or unaligned projections is also a reasonable step.

- Well-scoped and relevant problem: Cross-lingual projection remains an important avenue for low-resource NLP. A practical method to “learn when to trust” distant supervision is valuable and likely portable to other tasks and annotation sources.

- Strong baselines and matched setup: Comparisons include supervised baselines (MaxEnt, BiLSTM) and the prior state of the art using projected tags as auxiliary outputs. The evaluation follows a widely accepted low-resource protocol and uses the universal tagset for comparability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to label-noise literature and alternative formulations: The idea of a learnable noise adaptation/confusion layer is well-known in learning with noisy labels (e.g., transition matrices applied to predictions). The specific formulation p(ỹ|x)=softmax(A o) works, but is somewhat ad hoc compared to the standard probabilistic model ỹ ~ T^T y, i.e., ỹ probabilities as T^T o with a row-stochastic T. A direct comparison to that formulation (and to initializing T from counts) would clarify whether the softmax(A o) mapping is necessary or optimal.

- Missing ablations: Key components lack analysis: (i) effect of pretraining vs joint training from scratch; (ii) initialization of A (identity vs zeros) and its regularization; (iii) weighting between gold and projected losses (curriculum or λ-sweep); (iv) impact of using sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens; (v) how much of the gain comes from noise modeling vs simply more projected data. Ablations would make the causal story more convincing.

- Baselines could be stronger/more diverse: A BiLSTM-CRF decoder is a standard POS baseline that typically improves over per-token softmax; comparing against it (with and without noise layer) would better contextualize the gains. Also, a multitask BiLSTM with an auxiliary projected-label head (Duong-style but neural) would be an informative baseline. Finally, a baseline using all alignments (with symmetrization heuristics) plus the noise layer would test the method’s robustness to more noise.

- Projection and alignment choices appear heuristic and under-explored: Restricting to 1-to-1 alignments discards a lot of data; for unaligned tokens using the sentence-level tag distribution is very crude. No analysis of alternative alignment symmetrization, alignment confidence thresholds, or sentence selection thresholds is provided. Since the paper’s contribution centers on handling noise, showing robustness to these upstream choices is important.

- Reporting and reproducibility gaps: No variance or statistical significance is reported; given small training sizes, variance can be considerable. Hyperparameters (epochs, batch size, dropout/regularization) and early-stopping criteria are not detailed. Code/data release is not mentioned, and some reference formatting is inconsistent. These issues hinder reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Why choose p(ỹ)=softmax(A o) rather than the more standard ỹ=T^T o with a row-stochastic T (or logits-space adaptation)? Did you try such alternatives, and how did they compare?

- How is A initialized and regularized? Identity/near-identity initialization often stabilizes noise-adaptation layers; did you explore this or add priors (e.g., L2 toward identity, sparsity)?

- How do you balance the gold vs projected losses during joint training? Did you try scaling or curriculum strategies to mitigate the large size imbalance? Any sensitivity analysis?

- Can you report variance over multiple random seeds and significance testing on key languages?

- What is the impact of using all alignments (with grow-diag-final) versus only 1-to-1? Does the noise layer recover from the added noise if you include more projected tokens?

- For unaligned/multi-aligned tokens, why use sentence-level tag distributions rather than uniform or lexical priors (e.g., dictionaries)? Any comparison?

- Did you try a BiLSTM-CRF decoder and/or a neural multitask baseline (auxiliary projected head) to isolate the value of explicit noise modeling vs architectural capacity?

Overall recommendation
This is a solid, practical contribution with clear motivation, a simple method, and consistent improvements on relevant benchmarks. The novelty is incremental relative to general noisy-label modeling, and the empirical section would benefit from stronger ablations and baselines. With these clarifications and additions, I’d be inclined to accept.
