Summary
The paper proposes a simple yet effective way to exploit noisy cross-lingual POS tag projections together with a small set of gold annotations for low-resource POS tagging. The core idea is to append a learned “noise layer” (a K×K linear transformation in the logits/probabilities space) on top of a BiLSTM tagger, so that the model can explicitly adapt its clean tag predictions to match noisy projected labels during training, while still learning to predict clean tags at test time. Training is staged: pretrain on clean gold data, then jointly train on both gold and projected data. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong prior work based on maxent and projected-label multi-tasking.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit label-noise adaptation layer for cross-lingual projection: modeling a tag-to-tag confusion matrix between clean and projected labels integrated into a neural sequence tagger, trained end-to-end.
2) A practical training recipe for combining tiny gold data with large noisy projections (pretrain on gold, then joint fine-tuning), with empirical evidence across 10 languages, including two real low-resource settings.
3) Analysis of learned confusion matrices that provides interpretable insights about projection noise patterns across languages.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea with consistent empirical benefits: The proposed noise layer improves over (a) BiLSTM trained on gold only, (b) BiLSTM trained directly on projected labels, and (c) the state-of-the-art Duong et al. (2014a) in 9/10 reported settings. The improvements are modest in simulations (avg 91.7 vs 91.3) but consistent, and larger in real low-resource settings (e.g., +4.2 points on Kinyarwanda over Duong et al., and +0.6 over Garrette & Baldridge).
- Sound modeling intuition and simplicity: Treating projection errors as label noise with a global confusion matrix is principled, easy to implement, and should transfer to other tasks involving distant supervision or crowdsourcing.
- Careful setup for projection noise: The paper acknowledges alignment errors and syntactic divergences, uses one-to-one alignments and sentence filtering, and introduces soft labeling for multiply aligned/unaligned tokens. This grounding makes the empirical comparison meaningful.
- Useful qualitative analysis: Visualizations of the learned noise matrices show plausible cross-tag confusions and language-specific patterns, helping justify the approach beyond raw numbers.
- Low-resource relevance and practicality: Using ~1k gold tokens and readily available parallel data is realistic and aligns with prior low-resource literature.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to general label-noise literature: While explicit noise modeling in cross-lingual projection is a valuable application, the core mechanism (a learned noise/adaptation layer between clean and noisy labels) is known in learning-with-noisy-labels. The paper would benefit from positioning against that body of work and clarifying what is new here beyond the application domain and sequence tagging instantiation.
- Global, context-independent noise modeling: The confusion matrix A is shared across all tokens and contexts. Projection reliability varies with alignment quality, source/target words, and local syntax. Not conditioning on alignment scores, alignment type (1–1, 1–N, NULL), or lexical/morphological context likely leaves performance on the table. An ablation with per-instance weighting or conditioning A on alignment confidence would strengthen the contribution.
- Sparse ablations and missing training details: Important aspects are under-specified: initialization of A (identity vs random), regularization (dropout/weight decay), batch size, early stopping criteria, and dev-set tuning are not described. There are no ablations for two-stage training, fixing vs learning A, or using rectangular A for mismatched tagsets. No variance/SE or significance testing is reported, and results with neural models can vary with random seed.
- Choice of soft labels for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens: Assigning the sentence-level tag frequency as the label distribution seems ad hoc and could bias learning towards frequent tags. A comparison against skipping such tokens, marginalizing over alignments, or weighting by alignment probabilities would clarify the impact.
- Baseline coverage: While comparisons to Duong et al. (2014a) and a BiLSTM are fair, additional baselines could better contextualize gains (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF; self-training/EM; graph-based label propagation; multilingual pretraining). Even brief experiments on one or two languages would strengthen the empirical case.

Questions to Authors
1) How is the noise matrix A initialized? Identity, small random around identity, or otherwise? Did you constrain/regularize A (e.g., with L2 or row-stochasticity) to avoid degenerate solutions?
2) How do you set training hyperparameters (dropout, batch size, early stopping)? Was model selection done on a dev set? Please report sensitivity and whether results are averaged over multiple seeds.
3) For unaligned or multiply-aligned tokens, why choose the sentence-level tag frequency distribution? Did you try skipping those tokens, using uniform over aligned tags, or weighting by alignment confidence?
4) Did you evaluate the two-stage training vs. joint training from scratch? How critical is pretraining on gold?
5) Can you report per-tag F1 and error analyses to understand which tags benefit most from the noise layer, especially in Malagasy/Kinyarwanda?
6) How many projected tokens end up used per language after filtering, and how sensitive are results to the number of projected sentences (e.g., 50k/100k/200k)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the methodological positioning by discussing related noisy-label adaptation approaches and clarifying your technical differences.
- Add ablations: (i) no noise layer; (ii) A fixed to identity; (iii) A learned but initialized differently; (iv) without two-stage training; (v) skipping weakly aligned tokens.
- Incorporate alignment-aware conditioning (e.g., gates or instance weights using alignment scores), or at least analyze performance stratified by alignment confidence.
- Report training details, seed variability, and significance testing; include per-tag metrics and qualitative error analysis on the real languages.
- Explore projected data size sensitivity and multi-source projections, which are common in low-resource scenarios.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is simple, well-motivated, and shows consistent benefits in realistic settings. Addressing the ablations and training detail gaps would make the paper stronger and more reproducible.
