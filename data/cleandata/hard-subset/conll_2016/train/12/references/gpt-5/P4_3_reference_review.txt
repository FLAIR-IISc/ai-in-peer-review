Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold-labeled data with noisy tags projected from parallel text. The core idea is to train a BiLSTM tagger jointly on gold data and projected data while explicitly modeling a learnable noise transformation between the model’s “clean” output distribution and the projected labels. Concretely, a K×K parameter matrix A maps the model’s tag distribution o to a “noised” distribution used to match projected labels; training alternates between pretraining on gold data and joint training on both sources. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two true low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over baselines, including prior state-of-the-art.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit noise-aware training objective for cross-lingual projection that learns a label-noise transformation jointly with a neural tagger, improving robustness to projection errors.
2) Empirical evidence across 10 languages, including two genuinely low-resource languages, with improved accuracy over strong prior work, and analysis of learned noise matrices that align with linguistic/aligner intuitions.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint learning) that consistently leverages noisy supervision when naïve mixing does not.

Strengths
- Clear and focused problem formulation with a simple, implementable solution. The proposed “noise layer” neatly captures systematic discrepancies between projected and target tags without complex heuristics or constraints. The method integrates naturally with standard BiLSTM taggers and backpropagation.

- Consistent and meaningful empirical gains. On 8 Europarl languages, the approach improves over Duong et al. (2014a) on average (91.7 vs. 91.3), and shows larger gains in the real low-resource settings (86.3 vs. 85.3 for Malagasy, 82.5 vs. 78.3 for Kinyarwanda). The method also outperforms training on projected data directly and naïve mixing, demonstrating that the noise modeling is doing substantive work.

- Strong real-world evaluation. The inclusion of Malagasy and Kinyarwanda with limited and noisier parallel data is valuable and supports the method’s practical utility beyond simulations.

- Informative analysis via learned transformation matrices. Visualizations of A reveal intuitive patterns (e.g., strong diagonals, plausible off-diagonal confusions), differing noise levels across languages, and a qualitative link between noise severity and performance. This adds interpretability to the approach.

- Sensible training strategy. Pretraining on gold before joint optimization is a practical solution to avoid poor local optima when the noise model is uninitialized; this is supported by discussion and results.

Weaknesses
- Limited probabilistic grounding of the noise model. The formulation p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o) applies a linear transform to the probability vector o followed by softmax. This is not equivalent to marginalizing a stochastic confusion matrix p(ỹ|y) against p(y|x), which would be C^T o (optionally in log space with normalization). The current design is effective empirically but ad hoc; the paper does not justify why this form is preferable, nor compare to a constrained stochastic/confusion-matrix alternative.

- Missing comparisons to standard neural tagging baselines and alternative noisy-label approaches. There is no BiLSTM-CRF baseline, which is a common, stronger sequence tagging architecture. Nor are there comparisons to other ways to use projected tags (e.g., multi-task auxiliary losses without a noise layer, EM/marginalization with a constrained noise channel, or label propagation). This limits assessing whether the gains come from the noise model specifically, rather than from using a BiLSTM.

- Incomplete training and reproducibility details. Important hyperparameters and procedures are underspecified: initialization of A (identity/near-identity vs. random), constraints or regularization on A (to avoid degenerate solutions), optimization schedule and sampling ratio between gold and projected data, early stopping criteria, epochs, dropout/regularization. Without these details, reproducing the results is difficult.

- Scope of projection choices and ablations. The method restricts to one-to-one alignments and sentence filtering; it would be informative to test whether the noise layer can absorb additional noise from many-to-one/one-to-many alignments and whether performance degrades gracefully. Ablations on (i) pretraining necessity, (ii) diagonal-only or low-rank A, (iii) initializing A to identity, and (iv) varying the quantity/quality of projected data would strengthen the causal claims.

- Novelty is moderate. Explicitly modeling label noise with a learned transformation has prior art in noisy-label learning; here it is adapted to cross-lingual projection and sequence tagging. The contribution is practical and useful but incremental relative to both noisy-label literature and prior cross-lingual projection work that addressed noise via heuristics/constraints.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Noise model design: Why choose softmax(A o) over a probabilistically grounded confusion channel C with rows summing to 1 (õ = C^T o)? Did you try constraining A (e.g., row-normalized, diagonal bias) or alternative parameterizations (logits mapping)? Any empirical comparison?

- Initialization and regularization of A: How is A initialized (identity, small noise, random)? Are there priors or penalties (e.g., L2, diagonal dominance) to prevent degenerate mappings? Sensitivity to these choices?

- Training details: What is the batching/sampling ratio between gold and projected data during joint training? Number of epochs, early stopping criteria, dropout/regularization settings, and dev set usage?

- Baselines: Did you evaluate a BiLSTM-CRF and/or a multi-task baseline that predicts both gold tags and projected tags without a noise layer? If so, how do they compare?

- Projection scope: Have you tried including many-to-one/one-to-many alignments and relying on the noise model to cope? How does performance change as more (noisier) projected labels are added?

- Tagset mismatch: You mention support for mismatched tagsets via a rectangular A. Do you have experiments demonstrating this scenario?

- Reproducibility: Will code, data splits, and configuration be released?

Overall, this is a clear and technically sound application of a simple noise-aware layer that yields consistent improvements, with room to strengthen probabilistic grounding and comparative evaluation.
