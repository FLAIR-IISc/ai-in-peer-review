Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by exploiting noisy cross-lingual projections together with a small gold corpus. The core idea is to insert a learnable “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger that maps clean tag distributions to projected (noisy) tag distributions via a K×K transformation matrix A, trained jointly on gold and projected data (with pretraining on gold). The approach is evaluated on 8 simulated low-resource European languages (Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over strong prior work (Duong et al., 2014a) and other baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit label-noise modeling layer for learning from cross-lingual projected tags, integrated into a neural sequence tagger and trained end-to-end, yielding consistent improvements over strong baselines.
2) Empirical demonstration across 10 languages, including two truly low-resource settings, with interpretable analyses of the learned noise matrices that reveal plausible cross-lingual/projection confusions.
3) Practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint learning) that stabilizes learning with noisy supervision and enables gains with minimal added parameters.

Strengths
- Sound and simple idea with clear motivation. Modeling projection noise explicitly via a learnable confusion matrix directly addresses well-known issues in cross-lingual projection. The formulation is lightweight (few parameters) and slots cleanly on top of a standard BiLSTM, making it broadly applicable to other tasks and supervision sources.
- Consistent empirical gains, particularly in truly low-resource settings. The method improves over Duong et al. (2014a) on average across 8 simulated languages (91.7 vs 91.3) and more substantially on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy 86.3 vs 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs 78.3; also surpassing Garrette & Baldridge on both), which is where it matters most. The baselines further show that naively mixing projected and gold data can hurt, underscoring the importance of the proposed noise modeling.
- Interpretability and analysis. Visualizations of A show strong diagonals with plausible off-diagonal structure (e.g., Malagasy nouns mapping to NOUN/ADJ/NUM, PRT vs X confusions in Swedish), providing evidence that the model captures realistic, language-dependent projection artifacts.
- Realistic data setup and scale. The paper leverages substantial parallel corpora when available (Europarl) and much smaller and noisier bilingual data for the real low-resource languages (100k and 11k sentence pairs), which strengthens the practical relevance of the findings.
- Training practicality. The two-stage training (gold pretraining then joint) addresses optimization difficulties when projected labels are very noisy, and is simple to replicate.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to known label-noise models. The noise layer is essentially a learnable class-conditional confusion matrix, a concept with prior art in noisy-label learning and calibration. The contribution lies more in application to cross-lingual projection within a neural tagger, rather than in advancing the modeling of noise per se. This should be acknowledged and positioned more clearly.
- Baseline coverage and architecture choices. The comparisons omit strong sequence tagging variants (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and other projection strategies (e.g., constraint-based or graph propagation methods like Täckström et al., 2013; Das & Petrov, 2011) implemented under comparable conditions. Given the modest average gains over Duong et al. on Europarl (0.4 points), it would be important to show the approach remains competitive against a BiLSTM-CRF baseline, and preferably against an implementation of a graph/constraint projection method.
- Heuristic handling of alignments and projected labels. Only 1-to-1 alignments are used and multi-aligned/unaligned cases are assigned sentence-level tag distributions, which is quite coarse. Modern alignment posteriors or alignment confidence weighting could be exploited; alternatively, marginalizing over alignment choices could reduce heuristic bias. An ablation showing the impact of these choices would strengthen the claims.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses. The paper motivates gold pretraining but does not quantify its effect (e.g., training from scratch with different A initializations; fixing A to identity; varying the amount of gold and projected data; conditioning A on alignment confidence). Sensitivity to the size/quality of parallel corpora is especially relevant for low-resource settings.
- Reporting and reproducibility details. Important training details are sparse (dropout/regularization, early stopping, epochs, batch size, initialization of A, whether A is constrained/regularized). No significance testing or variance across runs is reported; several improvements (especially on Europarl) are small enough that significance is uncertain.

Questions to authors
- How critical is the two-stage training? Please provide an ablation comparing: (a) joint training from scratch with different A initializations (identity vs random), (b) pretraining but freezing A, and (c) learning A only (freezing the base tagger).
- What is the impact of replacing the softmax tagger with a BiLSTM-CRF? Does the noise layer still help, and by how much?
- How sensitive are results to the amount of gold data (e.g., 1k vs 5k tokens) and to the size/quality of the parallel corpus? A learning curve would be informative, especially for Kinyarwanda.
- Can you weight projected examples by alignment confidence or sentence alignment score, and does that further improve performance?
- For multi-aligned/unaligned tokens, how many tokens are affected, and how does the sentence-level prior compare to other heuristics (e.g., majority over aligned English words, uniform over POS, or dropping such tokens)?

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated, simple, and practical method that delivers consistent gains, with clearer benefits in truly low-resource settings. While the modeling novelty is incremental, the empirical value and interpretability are strong. Addressing the baseline and ablation gaps would solidify the contribution. I lean toward acceptance, especially given the low-resource focus and portability of the approach.
