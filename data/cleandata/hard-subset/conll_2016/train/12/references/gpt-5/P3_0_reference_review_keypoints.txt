- Paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining small gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected labels via a learnable noise layer on a BiLSTM tagger.

- Core method: explicit K×K noise-adaptation layer to model systematic projection errors, trained in two stages (gold pretraining then joint training with separate losses).

- Empirical claim: consistent improvements over prior projection-based baselines across 8 simulated Europarl languages and 2 real low-resource languages.

- Contribution: simple, explicit noise-adaptation layer for sequence tagging that leverages both clean and projected supervision.

- Contribution: empirical study showing consistent gains and interpretable confusion-matrix visualizations across 10 languages.

- Contribution: practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) claimed to be robust in low-resource scenarios.

- Strength: principled, compact noise layer captures systematic tag mismatches and yields interpretable learned confusions.

- Strength: consistent empirical gains, with notable improvements on real low-resource languages (e.g., +4.2 on Kinyarwanda).

- Strength: sensible loss design separating gold and projected supervision and using soft labels for ambiguous/unaligned projections.

- Strength: well-scoped, relevant problem with a portable approach to handling noisy distant supervision.

- Strength: strong baselines and matched experimental setup using universal tagset and accepted low-resource protocol.

- Weakness: limited novelty relative to existing label-noise literature; formulation differs from standard probabilistic transition-matrix approaches without direct comparison.

- Weakness: missing ablations (pretraining vs scratch, A initialization/regularization, loss weighting, sentence-level distributions, contribution of extra projected data).

- Weakness: baselines could be stronger (BiLSTM-CRF, neural multitask auxiliary head, symmetrized alignments with noise layer).

- Weakness: projection/alignment choices (restricting to 1-to-1, crude handling of unaligned tokens) are heuristic and under-explored.

- Weakness: reporting/reproducibility gaps—no variance/significance, missing hyperparameter/early-stopping details, and no mention of code release.

- Question: why use p(ỹ)=softmax(A o) instead of the standard ỹ = T^T o with a row-stochastic T, and were alternatives tried?

- Question: how is A initialized and regularized; were identity or priors explored to stabilize learning?

- Question: how are gold vs projected losses balanced during joint training; any scaling or curriculum strategies and sensitivity analysis?

- Question: can authors report variance over seeds and significance tests for key languages?

- Question: what is the impact of using all alignments (e.g., grow-diag-final) versus only 1-to-1; can the noise layer handle added noise?

- Question: why use sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens instead of uniform or lexical priors; any comparisons?

- Question: were BiLSTM-CRF or neural multitask (auxiliary projected head) baselines tried to isolate the value of explicit noise modeling?

- Overall recommendation: solid, practical contribution with consistent gains but incremental novelty and needing stronger ablations and baselines; likely acceptable with revisions.
