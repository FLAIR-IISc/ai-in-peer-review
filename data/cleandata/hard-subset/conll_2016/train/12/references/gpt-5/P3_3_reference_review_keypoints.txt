- Summary: Models projection noise for low-resource POS tagging by adding a learnable "noise layer" on top of a BiLSTM, trained first on gold then jointly with projected data; shows gains on simulated and two real low-resource languages.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a simple, end-to-end noise-aware framework that learns a small transformation between clean and projected tag spaces on top of a BiLSTM.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows modeling projection noise outperforms naive projected-data use and prior cross-lingual methods under comparable supervision budgets, including Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

- Contribution 3: Analyzes learned noise matrices to provide interpretable insights into projection errors.

- Strength: Clear, practically useful idea with consistent empirical improvements over several baselines and prior work.

- Strength: Sensible two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint training) and widely adopted BiLSTM architecture facilitate reproducibility.

- Strength: Learned transformation matrices yield interpretable diagnostics that reveal language-specific projection issues.

- Strength: Evaluation includes realistic low-resource settings (small parallel corpora and small gold sets) beyond simulations.

- Strength: Method is broadly applicable to other distant supervision/noisy-label scenarios.

- Weakness: Noise model parameterization is questionable—using softmax(A o) is not equivalent to a standard confusion channel and may conflate calibration and noise.

- Weakness: Output modeling is token-wise softmax rather than sequence-level CRF; gains may not persist with a BiLSTM-CRF baseline.

- Weakness: Projected-label construction for non-1:1 or NULL alignments is ad hoc (sentence-level distributions) and may introduce avoidable noise.

- Weakness: No discussion of regularization/identifiability for A; risks degenerate solutions and unclear initialization/constraints.

- Weakness: Missing variance/significance analysis; some gains are small and seed variability is not reported.

- Weakness: Sparse architectural details (char/subword features, dropout, pretrained embeddings) that could materially affect low-resource POS results.

- Weakness: Comparisons may be unfair—stronger neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char features) are not included.

- Weakness: Claim about handling mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) is not empirically evaluated.

- Question: Why use softmax(A o) instead of a standard confusion channel C with p(ỹ|x)=C^T o, and can you ablate these parameterizations?

- Question: How is A initialized, and did you try regularizers or constraints (L2, identity prior, row-stochastic) to improve identifiability?

- Question: Did you try a CRF output layer, and how do results change with/without the noise layer when using a CRF?

- Question: What proportion of target tokens are 1:1 aligned, and how sensitive are results to handling NULL/many-to-one alignments?

- Question: Please report mean±std over multiple runs and statistical significance, especially for simulated languages.

- Question: Are embeddings randomly initialized or pretrained, and do you use character/subword modeling; do conclusions hold with stronger feature-rich baselines?

- Question: Can you empirically demonstrate the rectangular A case (mismatched tagsets), e.g., source fine-grained to target universal tags?

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept leaning accept—idea is useful and empirically promising but needs clearer probabilistic grounding, ablations, variance reporting, and stronger baselines.
