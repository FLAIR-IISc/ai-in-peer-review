Summary
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key idea is to train a BiLSTM tagger augmented with a learnable “noise layer” that maps the model’s clean tag distribution to the distribution of projected tags, thereby learning when and how to trust the distant supervision. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold, then jointly train on gold and projected labels. Experiments on eight simulated European languages (CoNLL/Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective noise-adaptation layer on top of a BiLSTM that explicitly models the mapping between clean and projected tag distributions, enabling joint training on gold and noisy projected labels.
2) Strong empirical results across 10 languages, including two genuinely low-resource settings, demonstrating robustness and practical utility of the approach.
3) Analysis of learned noise matrices, offering interpretable insights into projection errors and cross-lingual tag confusions.

Strengths
- Clear, targeted modeling of projection noise: The linear transformation A over tag distributions operationalizes “when to trust” projected tags. This explicit modeling is conceptually clean and yields consistent gains over using projected data directly or naively mixing it with gold data.
- Solid and broad empirical evaluation: Results on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, with consistent improvements over prior work (average +0.4 accuracy over Duong et al. in simulations; +1.0 and +4.2 on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda over Duong et al., and improvements over Garrette & Baldridge on both languages) support the method’s generality.
- Pragmatic training protocol: The two-stage training (gold pretrain, then joint) is sensible for identifiability and stability, and the ablation versus BiLSTM Annotated / Projected / Ann+Proj baselines convincingly shows that the noise layer is the main driver of gains.
- Interpretability: Visualizations of A reveal plausible cross-tag relationships (e.g., determiner/noun interactions, noun/adj/num patterns), grounding the approach and aiding error analysis.

Weaknesses
- Modeling formulation and identifiability: The noise layer is defined as softmax(A o), where o is a probability vector. This yields valid outputs but does not correspond to a proper conditional noise channel p(ỹ|y) (e.g., row-stochastic matrix). The unconstrained A can have negative entries, making interpretability as “confusion probabilities” questionable, and raises identifiability concerns (A and the base classifier can trade off). A comparison to a constrained stochastic noise model (e.g., learn a row-stochastic T with ỹ ~ T^T y, or operate in logit space) is missing.
- Inconsistencies in projection labeling vs. alignment filtering: Section 3.1 proposes assigning distributions when tokens are multi-aligned or unaligned, yet Section 4.1.2 states only one-to-one alignments are retained. It is unclear what supervision is actually used (hard labels vs. distributions) and what proportion of tokens receive projected labels in each condition. This affects both the training signal and the interpretation of A.
- Baselines could be stronger/more modern: A BiLSTM-CRF, character/subword features, or multilingual pretraining (e.g., polyglot embeddings) are standard for POS and could change the gap. The method’s benefit relative to such stronger baselines is unknown. Also, comparison to alternative noisy-label approaches (e.g., noise adaptation layers, label-noise correction) would clarify novelty.
- Scope of claims vs. evidence: The paper claims support for mismatched tagsets via rectangular A, but all experiments use universal tagsets; no empirical demonstration of tagset mismatch handling is provided. Similarly, the amount of parallel data in simulation (200k sentences) is large for “low resource,” potentially overstating generality.
- Missing details impacting reproducibility: Limited hyperparameter/regularization details, early stopping criteria, dev set usage, and code/resource availability. The filtering thresholds for sentence selection and the alignment coverage statistics are not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Can you clarify the exact supervision used for projected data in experiments? Do you use only one-to-one alignments (hard labels), or do you also use sentence-level distributions for multi/NULL alignments as described earlier? Please provide coverage statistics (percent of target tokens with projected labels) per language.
- Why choose softmax(A o) rather than a row-stochastic noise channel T operating as p(ỹ|y) = T, with marginalization p(ỹ|x) = T^T p(y|x)? Did you try constraining A (e.g., via softmax rows) or operating in logit space? Any empirical comparisons?
- How sensitive are results to the size/quality of parallel data? Can you report performance as a function of parallel corpus size, especially for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda?
- What regularization and early stopping were used? Any issues with overfitting A on tiny gold data? Did you try freezing A after some epochs or adding priors toward identity?
- Have you evaluated with a BiLSTM-CRF or with character-level features? Does the noise layer still bring gains over those stronger baselines?

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Consider an ablation study: (i) gold-only, (ii) projected-only, (iii) naive union, (iv) union with identity A, (v) learned A without gold pretraining. This would more sharply isolate the contribution of the noise layer and the pretraining step.
- Evaluate tagset-mismatch capability with a concrete experiment (e.g., train with projected PTB tags, evaluate on universal tags) to substantiate the rectangular A claim.
- Provide error analysis by tag (especially for verbs/determiners and pronouns) and correlation with alignment quality. This can guide when the approach is most beneficial.
- Tighten related work on noisy-label modeling and distant supervision; situate the approach relative to existing noise adaptation frameworks.
- Clean up citations and references (several are duplicated/partial).

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple, and effective idea with solid empirical support. Addressing the modeling clarity and baseline comparisons would strengthen the contribution and its positioning.
