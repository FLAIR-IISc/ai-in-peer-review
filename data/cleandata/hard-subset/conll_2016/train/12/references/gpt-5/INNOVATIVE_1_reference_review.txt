Summary
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a tiny gold-annotated set with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key idea is to explicitly model projection noise via a learnable transformation (“noise layer”) applied to the BiLSTM tagger’s output, mapping clean tag distributions to the distribution observed in projected labels. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold, then jointly train on gold and projected data with the noise layer. Experiments on eight simulated European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work and strong new SOTA on the real low-resource scenarios. The learned noise matrices are interpretable and provide insight into cross-lingual tag confusions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Explicit noise modeling for cross-lingual projection: a simple, learnable confusion/transformation layer that maps clean tag probabilities to noisy projected labels, trained end-to-end with gold and projected supervision.
- Strong empirical validation in low-resource settings, including two genuinely low-resource languages, with consistent improvements over prior state of the art and sensible analysis/visualization of the learned noise.
- Practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) demonstrating how to reliably leverage noisy projection without carefully engineered heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear, novel formulation of projection noise: Rather than relying on ad hoc heuristics, the paper introduces a principled, compact parameterization (K×K matrix) that learns when to trust or ignore projected tags. This is a bold, general idea likely applicable beyond POS tagging and beyond projection (e.g., other forms of distant/crowd supervision).
- Consistent and meaningful gains on real low-resource languages: Achieving 86.3% on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda improves over both Duong et al. (2014) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013). Given the small gold sets and modest parallel corpora, these improvements are substantial in practice.
- Thorough baselines and ablations for the core claim: The comparisons against (i) gold-only, (ii) projected-only, and (iii) naive union (Ann+Proj) BiLSTM clarify that the improvement comes from the noise layer rather than architecture or data quantity alone.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of the learned A highlight language-specific confusions (e.g., NUM↔ADJ in Malagasy, PRT↔X in Swedish) and align with linguistic intuitions and alignment noise patterns, lending credibility to the mechanism.
- Simple, general, low-parameter addition: The approach adds minimal complexity over a standard BiLSTM tagger, making it easy to adopt and potentially robust, with favorable compute/parameter trade-offs for low-resource contexts.

Weaknesses
- Limited expressivity of the noise model: A single, global tag–tag matrix A assumes stationary, context-independent noise. It cannot condition on alignment type (1–1 vs. NULL/many), alignment confidence, source-side tagger confidence, or lexical/morphological cues. This may underfit cases where projection errors are highly context-dependent. An ablation showing performance by alignment type or confidence weighting would strengthen the case.
- Incomplete exploration of training and initialization: The paper states that direct joint training is “impractical” without good A but does not detail initialization (e.g., identity) or constrain A (e.g., row-stochastic). More analysis on sensitivity to pretraining, learning rate, and regularization of A (to avoid degenerate solutions) is needed.
- Projection labeling choices may be suboptimal: For NULL or many-to-one alignments, using sentence-level tag frequency distributions is a crude heuristic. Alternatives (e.g., alignment posterior weighting, uniform/uninformative priors, or source-context-informed distributions) are not compared, leaving uncertainty about how much of the gain comes from the noise layer vs. this heuristic.
- Scope of comparisons: While the paper compares to Duong et al. (2014) and supervised baselines, it omits comparisons to other strategies for noisy supervision (e.g., confidence-calibrated weighting, self-training, label propagation (Das & Petrov, 2011) in the same setup) and to sequence-level decoding (CRF layer) on top of the BiLSTM, which is a common POS improvement.
- Generalization beyond English source and matching tagsets is asserted but not demonstrated: The method claims to support mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), yet no experiments test this or multi-source projection, limiting evidence for broader applicability.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How is A initialized and constrained? Do you enforce any normalization (e.g., softmax rows/columns) or regularization (e.g., L2, entropy) to avoid degenerate mappings? Did you try identity initialization, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- Can you report ablations on (a) training without gold pretraining, (b) varying the amount of gold data, and (c) varying the amount/quality of projected data? Learning curves would help quantify data-efficiency.
- How does performance vary by alignment type and confidence? For example, results when including many-to-one alignments with the noise layer vs. excluding them; or weighting projected instances by IBM posteriors.
- Did you try conditioning the noise transformation on simple features (e.g., alignment type, source tag, or a small MLP over alignment confidences)? Even a block-diagonal or mixture-of-experts A might capture richer structure.
- Any comparisons with a BiLSTM-CRF? If not, do you expect additive gains from a CRF layer, and would the noise layer still help?
- Do you plan to release code and the processed projection data to aid reproducibility?

Overall, this paper proposes a simple but powerful idea—explicitly learn when to trust distant supervision—and demonstrates clear gains in realistic settings. Despite some missing ablations and broader comparisons, the conceptual contribution is strong and broadly useful. I lean toward acceptance.
