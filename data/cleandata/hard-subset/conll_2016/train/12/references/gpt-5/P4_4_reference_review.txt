Summary and contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM tagger trained jointly on a small gold-annotated corpus and noisy projected labels from parallel data. The key idea is an explicit “noise layer”: a learnable K×K linear transformation applied to the model’s clean tag distribution before comparing to projected labels. Training proceeds in two stages—pretrain on gold data, then jointly train with both gold and projected data—so the model learns when to trust (or ignore) projections. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over prior work.

Main contributions (as assessed here), in order of strength:
1) A simple, explicit noise modeling layer that maps clean tag distributions to projected-label distributions, enabling principled joint training on clean and noisy supervision for sequence tagging.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages with consistent gains over strong projection-based baselines, plus interpretable analyses of learned confusion matrices that reveal cross-lingual/projection noise patterns.
3) Practical training protocol and projection labeling strategy (handling NULL/many-to-one alignments via sentence-level tag distributions) that make the approach workable in realistic low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of projection noise: The formulation p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o(x)) provides a compact, interpretable mechanism to account for systematic errors introduced by alignments and cross-lingual divergences. Figures 3–4 show meaningful diagonals and plausible off-diagonal patterns (e.g., DET/NOUN, PRT/X confusions), supporting the claim that the layer learns when to trust projection.

- Technically sound joint objective and training: The objective combines cross-entropy on gold labels (using the base output o) with cross-entropy on projected labels (using the noised output softmax(A o)). The two-stage training (gold pretraining followed by joint optimization) is a reasonable safeguard against early drift from noisy supervision, aligning with best practices for learning with label noise.

- Consistent improvements over relevant baselines: On 8 simulated languages, the proposed model averages 91.7% vs. 91.3% for Duong et al. (2014a), outperforming in most languages (Table 1). On real low-resource languages, it achieves 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda), exceeding both Duong et al. (85.3/78.3) and Garrette & Baldridge (81.2/81.9) (Table 2). The gains on Kinyarwanda are particularly substantial.

- Interpretable error diagnosis: Heatmaps of A provide insight into projection reliability across tags and languages, and correlate with performance differences (e.g., more noise in Kinyarwanda projections, larger benefit from modeling).

- Simplicity and generality: The approach adds a small matrix to a standard BiLSTM tagger, is easy to implement, and is applicable beyond POS tagging to other tasks with noisy distant supervision.

Weaknesses
- Global, context-agnostic noise model: The K×K matrix A is shared across all tokens and contexts. Projection noise depends on alignment properties (NULL vs. one-to-many), alignment confidence, and lexical/morphological cues. A single global matrix may underfit these structured noise sources. No comparison is provided to conditioning A on alignment features or to more principled confusion models (e.g., row-stochastic matrices with direct marginalization ỹ = C^T y).

- Limited baselines and ablations: The neural baselines exclude commonly used extensions (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF sequence decoding, character/subword features) that often yield stronger POS performance, including in low-resource settings. Ablations are missing on: (i) training from scratch vs. two-stage pretraining, (ii) using different projected-label heuristics (e.g., alignment posterior weighting instead of sentence-level tag distributions for NULL/multi-alignments), and (iii) sensitivity to the proportion of gold tokens. This limits the isolation of the noise layer’s contribution and the generality claims.

- Modest average gains on simulated languages and no significance testing: Improvements over the strongest prior (Duong et al.) are small on average (0.4 points; Table 1). The paper does not report statistical significance or confidence intervals, making it hard to assess robustness, especially where differences are within typical variance.

- Incomplete training details: Some implementation details that affect reproducibility and stability are unspecified (initialization of A—identity/zero/random?; regularization such as dropout/weight decay; early stopping criteria; number of epochs; batching). Given the known sensitivity of label-noise learning to initialization and optimization, these details matter.

- Ad hoc handling of NULL/many-to-one cases: Assigning sentence-level tag frequency distributions to such tokens is a coarse heuristic and may introduce bias. There is no analysis of its impact relative to alternatives (e.g., skipping, confidence weighting, or using alignment posteriors).

Questions to authors
- How is the noise matrix A initialized (identity, zero, random)? Did you impose any constraints (e.g., row/column normalization) or regularization on A, and did you observe degenerate solutions without them?

- Did you try a probabilistic confusion model ỹ = C^T o with C row-stochastic (learned via softmax over rows) instead of softmax(A o)? If so, how did it compare?

- What is the sensitivity to the size of gold supervision (e.g., 500, 1000, 2000 tokens) in the simulated setting? Does the benefit of the noise layer diminish with more gold data?

- Did you evaluate variants that condition the noise transformation on alignment features (e.g., NULL vs. one-to-one, alignment score) or lexical cues? If not, do you see systematic failure modes in A that suggest such conditioning would help?

- Are the gains preserved when using stronger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character embeddings)? If tested, please report.

- For the two-stage training, what are the stopping criteria for each stage, and what is the relative weighting of gold vs. projected losses during joint training?
