Summary and contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by leveraging noisy cross-lingual projections together with a small gold corpus. The core idea is to explicitly model projection noise with a simple learnable transformation between latent “clean” tag probabilities and observed projected tags, integrated into a BiLSTM sequence tagger and trained jointly on gold and projected data (with gold-only pretraining).

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, principled noise-aware training objective for distant supervision: a learnable K×K “confusion” layer on top of the BiLSTM outputs that maps clean tag distributions to projected-label distributions and allows end-to-end learning that “decides when to trust” projected tags.
2) Empirical evidence across 8 simulated and 2 true low-resource languages showing consistent gains over strong prior work (Duong et al., 2014) and over naïve uses of projection, plus qualitative analyses of learned noise matrices.
3) A practical training recipe for combining tiny gold data and large noisy projections (gold pretraining followed by joint training), which appears robust across settings.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and practicality: The noise layer is a minimal, interpretable addition to a standard BiLSTM. It directly addresses the main challenge in projection-based supervision (label noise and biases) and can be dropped into other sequence models. The joint loss is straightforward and the two-stage training mitigates bad local optima.
- Consistent empirical improvements with appropriate baselines: The model outperforms the prior state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014) on 8 Europarl languages (average 91.7 vs. 91.3) and on two true low-resource languages (Malagasy 86.3 vs. 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs. 78.3), while demonstrating that naïvely mixing projected and gold data can hurt. This supports the central claim that modeling projection noise matters.
- Interpretability of the learned noise: Visualizations of the A matrices are informative; they capture plausible divergences (e.g., determiners vs. nouns in determiner-less languages, NOUN/ADJ/NUM interplay), and also reveal language-specific alignment issues, aligning well with performance differences (e.g., noisier Kinyarwanda).
- Realistic evaluation: The paper includes both simulated low-resource experiments (standard in the literature) and truly low-resource settings with real parallel data. The latter is important to substantiate relevance.
- Generality beyond POS: The formulation naturally extends to other distant supervision settings (e.g., NER, parsing) or mismatched tagsets, which broadens impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing label-noise modeling: The approach is essentially a linear confusion/noise adaptation layer trained jointly with the classifier—an idea known in supervised learning with noisy labels. While applying it to cross-lingual projection for POS tagging is useful, the conceptual contribution is incremental. Comparing against other noise-robust learning strategies (e.g., EM over latent clean labels, loss correction, or co-teaching-like approaches) would strengthen the novelty claim.
- Missing/underpowered baselines: 
  - A BiLSTM-CRF decoder is a standard stronger POS baseline; including it (with and without the noise layer) would clarify whether gains persist with stronger sequence modeling.
  - Character/subword features are crucial for low-resource POS. It is unclear whether the reported gains hold when char-level models are used.
  - A multi-task setup that predicts both gold and projected tags without an explicit noise layer (beyond Duong et al.’s MaxEnt) in a neural architecture would be a fairer comparison.
- Statistical rigor and run variability: No statistical significance testing or multiple runs are reported. Gains on Europarl are sometimes modest; LSTM variance can be large. Confidence intervals or significance tests are needed, especially for the small improvements in Table 1.
- Reproducibility and training details: Important details are missing: initialization of A (identity/zeros?), regularization of A (e.g., L2, bias towards identity), early stopping criteria, number of epochs, seed, dropout/other regularization, and whether soft labels are used when projections yield distributions. Code/data for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda parallel corpora are not publicly referenced in a reproducible form.
- Projection labeling heuristics could bias results: 
  - Only 1-to-1 alignments are used; this reduces coverage. An ablation comparing 1-to-1 vs. all alignments (with the noise layer) would show robustness.
  - For NULL/multi-alignments, assigning the English sentence-level tag frequency as supervision is ad hoc; alternative formulations (e.g., marginalization over alignments or word-level priors) might yield different behavior. An ablation of this choice would be informative.
  - The annotated training size differs between simulated (1k tokens) and real languages (~5k tokens), which complicates claims about “tiny” annotation; clarifying this and discussing scalability to truly 1k-token settings in real languages would help.

Questions to authors
- How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized? Did you try biasing A towards the identity (e.g., A = I + small noise) or adding an explicit penalty ||A−I||? How sensitive are results to this?
- Do you use soft labels in the projected loss when the projection yields a distribution (e.g., NULL/multi-alignments)? If so, is the loss cross-entropy with soft targets? If not, why?
- How sensitive is performance to the amount/quality of parallel data? Can you plot accuracy vs. number of parallel sentences (especially for the low-resource languages)?
- Would the gains hold with a BiLSTM-CRF and/or with character-level embeddings? Any preliminary results?
- Training details: epochs, early stopping on dev, dropout/regularization, and random seed settings?
- Do you actually evaluate the “mismatched tagset” scenario claimed as supported (rectangular A), or is this just a theoretical possibility?

Additional comments
- Please report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or approximate randomization) or multiple runs with mean±std.
- Consider releasing code and preprocessed projection data to facilitate reproduction.
- Minor: Clean up references (years/venues, duplicates) and fix a few typos.
