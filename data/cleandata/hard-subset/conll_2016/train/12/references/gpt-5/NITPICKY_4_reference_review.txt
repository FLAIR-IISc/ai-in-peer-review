Summary
The paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small gold-annotated corpus with noisy cross-lingual projected labels. The core idea is to train a BiLSTM tagger on gold data while, for projected data, inserting a learnable “noise layer” (a K×K linear transform followed by softmax) that maps the model’s clean tag distribution to the distribution observed in projection. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold, then jointly train on gold and projected data. Experiments on 8 Europarl languages (simulating low-resource with 1k gold tokens) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show improvements over previous projection-based baselines and over training on gold or projected data alone. Visualization of learned matrices suggests interpretable cross-tag confusions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, integrated label-noise modeling layer for learning from projected tags in a neural sequence tagger, with joint training on gold and noisy data.
2) Empirical evidence across 10 languages (8 simulated, 2 real) that this approach yields consistent gains over strong prior projection methods and over naïve use of projected data.
3) Analysis via learned confusion matrices that gives intuition about how projection noise differs across languages.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: Modeling projection noise via a small K×K transform is simple, parameter-efficient, and easy to integrate with standard BiLSTMs. It aligns well with the observed mismatch between projected and true tags and is likely useful beyond POS tagging.
- Consistent empirical gains: Across 8 simulated languages, the proposed model matches or improves upon Duong et al. (2014a) and clearly beats naïve BiLSTM training on annotations, projections, or their union. On Malagasy and Kinyarwanda it achieves 86.3% and 82.5% accuracy, respectively, surpassing reported baselines.
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on clean data before introducing noisy supervision is a reasonable and often necessary choice to avoid poor local optima when injecting noise.
- Interpretability: The visualized A matrices reveal plausible patterns (e.g., determiner/noun or adjective/noun interactions, pronouns less confused), giving qualitative support that the layer captures systematized projection errors.
- Focus on realistic low-resource settings: The paper uses small gold sets (∼1k tokens) and real parallel corpora of varying quality, which strengthens the applied relevance.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established label-noise modeling: Learning a confusion matrix/noise layer and training with cross-entropy on noisy labels has been studied broadly in label-noise literature; in projection settings, prior work has modeled alignment/projection noise via constraints or EM-like approaches. The paper positions itself as first to “explicitly model noise” in projection, but does not sufficiently differentiate from existing noise-aware methods nor cite broader label-noise work. Novelty is thus incremental.
- Probabilistic formulation and identifiability are under-specified: The model sets p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o), where o is already a probability vector. This mixes spaces (linear map on probabilities then softmax) without justification. Why not operate on logits or parameterize a conditional confusion p(ỹ|y)? Without constraints or priors, A and the base classifier may be non-identifiable; the paper does not discuss regularization (e.g., encouraging near-diagonality) or initialization (identity vs random). The statement “ai,j denotes the confusion score” is intuitive but not formally grounded here.
- Inconsistency in projection protocol: Section 3.1 assigns distributions to tokens with many/NULL alignments based on sentence-level tag frequencies; Section 4.1.2 says only one-to-one alignments are retained (following Duong et al., 2014b). It is unclear whether multi/NULL cases were used (as soft labels) or filtered out. This affects the loss definition (ỹ as one-hot vs distribution) and data volume.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines: No ablation of the noise layer vs. simpler alternatives (e.g., sample reweighting of projected data, temperature-scaled calibration, marginalization with an identity-initialized A, or EM over latent clean tags). A CRF sequence layer or char-level features could be strong baselines in low-resource tagging. It’s also important to test initialization of A (identity vs random), freezing vs fine-tuning A, and sensitivity to the amount/quality of projection.
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps: No statistical significance or confidence intervals; some gains are small (e.g., average +0.4 over Duong et al.). Hyperparameters and training details are sparse: no batch size, epochs, early stopping, dropout or gradient clipping, embedding initialization (pretrained vs random), tokenization/segmentation, tag mapping procedure details, or exact filtering threshold for IBM Model 3 sentence selection (just “200k sentences”). Learning rate 1.0 with vanilla SGD is unusual without decay/clipping details.
- Presentation issues: Numerous typos and formatting issues (“wildly” vs “widely”, duplicated/garbled references with placeholders, inconsistent citation formatting, inline footnote artifacts). Figures are referenced but not clearly presented in the text provided. These detract from polish and raise concerns about carefulness.

Questions to Authors
1) Projection protocol: Did you actually include tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments using sentence-level tag distributions, or did you filter to one-to-one only? If both, how were they combined in training, and what proportion of projected tokens used soft vs one-hot labels?
2) Noise layer parameterization: Why apply a linear map to probabilities followed by softmax, rather than modeling p(ỹ|y) directly (i.e., a row-stochastic confusion matrix) or operating on logits? How is A initialized (identity vs random), and is any regularization used (e.g., L2, diagonal prior, or constraint to be row-stochastic)?
3) Ablations: What is the performance of (i) BiLSTM with projected data only but filtered via confidence thresholds; (ii) reweighting projected instances; (iii) identity-initialized A with/without fine-tuning; (iv) replacing softmax(A o) with a learned bias per projected tag; (v) adding a CRF layer or character embeddings?
4) Significance and variance: Are the reported improvements statistically significant across multiple runs? Please provide standard deviations or confidence intervals.
5) Reproducibility details: Please provide tokenization, universal tag mapping procedure, batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria, dropout/regularization, gradient clipping, and the precise rule for selecting the 200k Europarl sentences (threshold or top-N by alignment score).

Overall, the idea is practical and the results are promising, but the methodological clarity, novelty positioning, ablations, and presentation need strengthening. Given the number of technical and reporting issues, I lean toward rejection in the current form despite the potential utility of the approach.
