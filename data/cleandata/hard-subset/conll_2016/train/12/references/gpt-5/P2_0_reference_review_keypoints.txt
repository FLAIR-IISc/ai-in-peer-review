- Problem: Improve low-resource POS tagging by combining small gold data with noisy cross-lingual projected tags.  
- Core idea: Learn a linear "noise layer" (confusion matrix) on top of a BiLSTM so base model predicts clean tags and noise layer maps to projected-tag distribution.  
- Training: Two-stage recipe — pretrain on gold, then joint training on gold + projected data.  
- Experiments: Evaluated on 8 simulated European languages and 2 truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) with consistent gains.  
- Key contribution 1: Explicit noise modeling for cross-lingual projection via a learnable confusion matrix trained end-to-end.  
- Key contribution 2: Empirical validation across languages, including real low-resource cases, with visualization-based interpretability of learned noise matrices.  
- Key contribution 3: Practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) to stabilize learning with noisy labels.  
- Strength: Principled approach that directly models projection noise sources (alignment/syntactic divergence) rather than heuristics.  
- Strength: Measurable gains, especially substantial improvement on Kinyarwanda and meaningful gains on Malagasy.  
- Strength: Interpretability — learned confusion matrices reveal sensible error patterns and trust weighting.  
- Strength: Strong, fair baselines and consistent experimental setup (universal tagset, prior filtering).  
- Strength: Simple and general — adds only a K×K matrix and is easy to integrate into neural taggers.  
- Weakness: Incremental novelty — confusion-matrix noise modeling is well-known; novelty is mainly application to POS projection.  
- Weakness: Limited ablations/sensitivity analyses (no tests w/o pretraining, different A initializations, regularization, logits vs probabilities).  
- Weakness: Heuristic projection and filtering choices under-explored (treatment of unaligned/multi-aligned tokens, sentence-selection bias).  
- Weakness: Claim of handling mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) not demonstrated — all experiments use square A on universal tags.  
- Weakness: Sparse reporting of reproducibility details (training schedule, early stopping, regularization, A init) and no significance testing for small gains.  
- Question: How was noise matrix A initialized and was identity/regularization toward identity tried?  
- Question: Were variants without pretraining or different curricula evaluated and how sensitive are results?  
- Question: Why apply A to post-softmax probabilities rather than logits, and was this compared?  
- Question: Why use sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens and were alternatives tried?  
- Question: Can you report variance across runs and significance tests, especially for simulated languages?  
- Question: Can you provide an experiment with mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) to support that claim?  
- Question: What were exact early stopping, dropout/L2, and hyperparameter-tuning procedures given tiny gold data?
