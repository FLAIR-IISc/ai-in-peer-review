Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small set of gold annotations with noisy tags projected from a high-resource language via word alignments. The core idea is to train a BiLSTM tagger with an explicit “noise layer” that maps predicted clean tag probabilities to the distribution of noisy projected tags through a learned K×K transformation matrix. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold data; then jointly train on gold and projected data, where the projected loss is applied after the noise layer. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior projection-based methods.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An explicit, trainable noise model for distant supervision that learns a global confusion mapping between true tags and noisy projected tags, integrated into a neural sequence tagger and trained end-to-end.
2) An empirical demonstration that explicit noise modeling yields consistent improvements over strong projection baselines and prior work across 10 languages, with analysis visualizing learned noise matrices.
3) A practical training recipe (pretraining + joint training) that stabilizes learning with noisy labels and leverages small gold datasets effectively.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and general mechanism to handle label noise: The K×K noise layer is conceptually clean, easy to integrate with existing neural taggers, and broadly applicable to distant supervision beyond POS. Unlike heuristic filtering alone, it explicitly models the mapping from clean to noisy labels and lets projected data contribute informative gradients. This addresses a pervasive issue in cross-lingual projection.

- Consistent empirical gains across diverse settings: On eight simulated settings, the proposed model improves average accuracy over Duong et al. (2014a) (91.7 vs. 91.3 avg), and on real low-resource languages it achieves 86.3 (Malagasy) and 82.5 (Kinyarwanda), outperforming both Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge. The consistency across ten languages and two resource conditions supports robustness.

- Sensible training strategy and ablation-like comparisons: The paper compares training on annotated only, projected only, and their union; these show that naïvely mixing gold and projected can hurt, motivating the noise layer. The two-stage training (pretrain then joint) is reasonable and appears to mitigate instability.

- Insightful analysis of the learned noise: Visualizations of A reveal language-specific confusions (e.g., PRT vs. X in Swedish, NOUN leaking into ADJ/NUM in Malagasy), validating the intended behavior and offering linguistic insight into projection noise and alignment artifacts.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to general noisy-label modeling: While the application to cross-lingual POS projection is valuable, the core idea (a linear label-noise transition layer trained jointly) is a known pattern in learning with noisy labels. The paper would be strengthened by positioning against broader noisy-label literature and by demonstrating advantages over alternative noise models (e.g., logit-space transition, EM-style estimation, regularized/structured A).

- Global, context-independent noise matrix: A single A shared across all tokens cannot capture context-dependent noise (e.g., varying alignment quality by POS, morphology, position, or alignment cardinality). The method gives solid gains, but a more expressive noise model (conditioned on alignment type, alignment scores, or token features) could further improve performance; lack of such exploration limits the contribution.

- Projected data labeling heuristics are ad hoc and under-explored: For NULL or multi-alignments, assigning the sentence-level tag distribution is crude and may inject substantial noise. The paper doesn’t compare against common alternatives (ignore such tokens, uniform over plausible tags, alignment-score-weighted mixtures), so it’s unclear how much the method’s gains depend on this choice versus the noise layer’s robustness.

- Experimental baselines and reporting could be stronger: The BiLSTM baseline lacks common enhancements (character/subword features, CRF layer), and there is no comparison to BiLSTM-CRF or char-aware taggers, which are standard for POS and may change the margin. No statistical significance tests are reported. Details on optimization (epochs, early stopping, A initialization, regularization/constraints on A) are sparse, affecting reproducibility and interpretability.

- Data usage and weighting choices not fully justified: The joint objective averages the gold and projected losses regardless of large size differences; this may implicitly overweight scarce gold data. An investigation of loss weighting, sensitivity to parallel corpus size/quality, and learning curves would better support the methodological claims.

Questions to Authors
1) How is the noise matrix A initialized? Identity, near-identity, or random? Did you try regularizing A toward identity or constraining it to be (row/column) stochastic? Any sensitivity analysis?

2) Why apply A to probability outputs (softmax(A o)) instead of to logits (e.g., softmax(B z) where z are pre-softmax logits)? Did you compare these formulations in practice?

3) For NULL or many-to-one/one-to-many alignments, why choose sentence-level tag frequency as the supervision target? Did you try ignoring such tokens, distributing mass uniformly over top-k tags, or weighting by alignment/translation probabilities? How sensitive are results to this design?

4) How many tokens in each setting end up with one-to-one alignments vs. NULL/multi? Please provide counts or proportions; this would contextualize the contribution from projected data and the difficulty of the noise modeling.

5) Loss weighting: you normalize by dataset sizes and sum the two losses. Did you sweep the relative weight between gold and projected losses? Any evidence that alternative weights help, especially when projected data are scarce (Kinyarwanda) vs. plentiful?

6) Pretraining schedule and early stopping: How many epochs on gold before joint training, and what validation criterion is used? Did you observe instability when training jointly from scratch?

7) Baselines: Did you try a BiLSTM-CRF and/or character embeddings for the base tagger? If so, how do gains from the noise layer compare? If not, could you comment on expected interactions?

8) Significance: Are the improvements over Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge statistically significant across languages?

Overall, this is a solid and practical contribution with consistent empirical benefits and clear analysis. Addressing the above concerns—especially stronger baselines, ablations on A and projection heuristics, and more detailed training/reporting—would substantially strengthen the paper.
