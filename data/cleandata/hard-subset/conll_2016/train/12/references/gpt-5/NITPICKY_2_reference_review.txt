Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small gold-annotated corpus with noisy cross-lingual label projection. The key idea is to explicitly model projection noise with a learned linear “noise layer” (a K×K confusion matrix A) on top of a BiLSTM tagger: the supervised output o = p(y|x) is transformed to match the projected label distribution via softmax(A o), and training jointly optimizes cross-entropy for gold data (directly on o) and projected data (on softmax(A o)). A two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint) is used. Experiments on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages show gains over prior work.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, explicit noise-adaptation layer for cross-lingual projection, integrated into sequence tagging and trained jointly on gold and projected data.
2) An empirical study across 10 languages (8 simulated, 2 real) showing consistent gains over strong baselines and prior work, with qualitative analysis via learned A matrices.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretrain + joint training) for stabilizing learning with noisy distant supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, effective idea with small footprint: Modeling projection noise via a linear confusion layer is conceptually clean, easy to implement, and computationally light. The visualization of A helps interpret what the model learns (e.g., systematic PRON/NOUN confusions).
- Consistent empirical improvements: On 8 Europarl languages, the proposed model improves over both a supervised BiLSTM and the prior two-output maxent model (Duong et al., 2014a), with average gains (~0.4 over Duong et al. and larger over simple BiLSTM baselines). On Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, gains over prior work are meaningful (e.g., 86.3 vs 85.3 for Malagasy; 82.5 vs 78.3 for Kinyarwanda).
- Joint training on hard labels and distributions: The method supports projected hard labels and soft distributions (for NULL/multi-alignments), which is appropriate for noisy supervision; the loss handles both naturally.
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on gold before exposing the model to noisy signals is a pragmatic choice to avoid degenerate solutions.
- Reproducibility indicators: Some implementation and hyperparameter details (word/hidden size, LR, toolkit) are given; datasets are standard (Europarl, CoNLL-X, G&B 2013), with universal tagset mapping.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing noise-label literature and insufficient citation: The noise layer is essentially a label-noise adaptation/confusion model, long studied in ML and NLP (e.g., Dawid–Skene; crowd annotation models; and neural “noise adaptation layers” such as Sukhbaatar et al., 2015; Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, 2016). The paper claims to be the first to explicitly model projection noise but does not situate the approach with respect to these lines of work or adapt known regularizers/constraints (e.g., row-stochasticity, priors toward identity). This undermines the novelty claim.
- Alignment strategy inconsistency and projection labeling details: Section 4.1.2 says only one-to-one alignments are retained, yet Section 3.1 defines handling of many-to-one/un-aligned tokens by assigning sentence-level tag distributions. These are contradictory. If only 1–1 alignments are retained, when and how are soft distributions used? Moreover, using sentence-level tag histograms as supervision for unaligned tokens seems ad hoc and potentially very noisy; no ablation is provided to justify this choice or its impact.
- Lack of key ablations and sensitivity analyses: No studies on (i) effect of pretraining vs training from scratch; (ii) impact of varying the weight between gold and projected losses (currently an unweighted average; a λ is standard); (iii) size/quality of projected data; (iv) initialization/regularization of A (identity vs zeros vs learned prior; diagonal bias); (v) using CRF decoding or character/subword features, important for low-resource OOVs. The observed gains, while real, could be orthogonal to the noise layer (e.g., from the switch to BiLSTMs).
- Methodological under-specification: Training details are sparse (epochs, batch size, early stopping, dev tuning, LR schedule, dropout/regularization). It is unclear how A is initialized and whether any constraints are imposed (e.g., simplex constraints). The combined loss averages over token counts of two datasets with very different sizes without any balancing—this is unusual and may bias optimization.
- Real low-resource setup and fairness: For simulated languages, training uses 1,000 tokens, but for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda the gold training sets are ~5k tokens each. This weakens the “tiny gold” narrative and complicates comparison to prior work that often uses 1k tokens. Although the paper claims “same data split” as Duong et al., 2014a, it is unclear if that work used the same 5k-token training for these two languages. Clarity is needed to ensure fairness.
- Reference and formatting issues: Numerous reference entries are malformed/incomplete (e.g., “Ammar et al.,? 2016”, “Guo et al.,? \Q2016\E”), duplicate/partial titles, and encoding artifacts (Täckström). Typos (“wildly successful” vs “widely”), footnotes inline disrupting flow, inconsistent figure/table cross-references. For a camera-ready standard, this needs attention.

Questions to Authors
1) Alignment and supervision: Do you truly retain only one-to-one alignments? If so, when exactly are soft sentence-level tag distributions used? Please reconcile Sections 3.1 and 4.1.2 and provide statistics on the proportion of tokens supervised by hard vs soft labels.
2) Noise layer specifics: How is A initialized (identity, zeros, random)? Any regularization (L2, diagonal prior) or constraints (row-stochastic)? Did you try a λ to weight the projected loss vs gold loss? Sensitivity?
3) Training details: What are batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, LR schedule, dropout? Are word embeddings randomly initialized or pretrained? Any character/subword features?
4) Real low-resource data: Exactly how many gold training tokens are used for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, and what splits are used for dev/test? Do your baselines (Duong et al., Garrette & Baldridge) use identical splits and gold sizes? If not, please re-run or clarify.
5) Ablations: Please report (i) no pretrain; (ii) identity-initialized A vs random; (iii) removing sentence-level distributions; (iv) varying projected data size; (v) CRF decoding; (vi) character embeddings.
6) Related work: Please discuss and compare to neural noise-adaptation layers and classic annotation-noise models (crowdsourcing/DS), and clarify novelty relative to these.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sound and results promising, but the novelty is limited relative to existing label-noise modeling, and there are important inconsistencies, missing ablations, and methodological omissions. With stronger positioning, clearer alignment/projection handling, ablations/regularization of A, and tightened presentation, this could become a solid contribution.
