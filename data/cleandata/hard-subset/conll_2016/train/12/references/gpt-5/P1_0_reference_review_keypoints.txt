- Problem & approach: Tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining noisy cross-lingual projection with a small amount of gold data via a learnable K×K noise layer on top of a BiLSTM tagger.  
- Training protocol: Pretrain on gold annotations, then joint-train on gold and projected data to model noise explicitly.  
- Core contribution: Introduces a simple, explicit, and effective class-conditional confusion matrix to map clean tag distributions to noisy projected tags.  
- Empirical validation: Evaluated on eight simulated languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) with consistent gains.  
- SOTA results: Achieves state-of-the-art on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), with meaningful improvements over prior work.  
- Interpretability: Learned noise matrices provide linguistically informative insights about projection biases.  
- Practicality: Low-parameter, easy to implement, and relies on widely available resources (parallel text + English tagger).  
- Robust baselines: Compares against reasonable baselines (Duong et al., supervised MaxEnt, BiLSTM variants) and shows why naïve use of projected data can hurt.  
- Novelty caveat: Methodological novelty is modest—confusion-matrix noise modeling is known in noisy-label literature; novelty is mainly the application to cross-lingual POS projection.  
- Projection ambiguity: Inconsistencies in description of alignment handling (sentence-level soft labels vs. retaining only 1–1 alignments) make supervision used unclear.  
- Limited ablations: Missing comparisons to down-weighting projected examples, BiLSTM-CRF with char embeddings, calibration methods, and more expressive/conditional noise models.  
- Global noise limitation: A single, context-independent noise matrix cannot capture alignment-dependent or context-dependent projection errors.  
- Reproducibility gaps: Lacks details on A initialization/regularization, training epochs, early stopping, variance across runs, and loss weighting between datasets.  
- Significance/variance missing: No statistical significance tests or mean±std reported to assess robustness of reported gains.  
- Practical suggestion: Conditioning A on alignment features or using multiple A matrices per alignment type could improve modeling of projection noise.  
- Key author questions requested: Clarify use of 1–1 alignments vs. soft labels, A initialization/regularization, loss weighting/sampling strategy, significance/variance, stronger tagger baselines, and conditioning on alignment quality.  
- Overall verdict: Solid, practical, and effective approach with modest novelty; recommended acceptance pending clarification, stronger ablations, and more experimental details.
