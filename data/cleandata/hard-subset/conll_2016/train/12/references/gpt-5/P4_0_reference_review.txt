Summary
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projections from parallel corpora. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an output “noise layer” that models the mapping between clean tags and noisy projected tags via a learnable matrix. Training proceeds in two stages: pretraining on gold data, then joint training on gold and projected data, with the projected branch passing through the noise layer. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over strong prior work, including Duong et al. (2014).

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Explicit modeling of projection noise in a neural tagger via a learned output transformation, enabling joint learning from clean and projected labels.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages, including two truly low-resource settings, demonstrating consistent gains over prior methods and reasonable analysis of the learned noise transformations.
3) A simple, practical training recipe (two-stage training) for effectively leveraging projected supervision.

Strengths
- Effective and simple approach: Introducing a small learned transformation (“noise layer”) to adapt the tag distribution to projected labels yields consistent, non-trivial gains over both (i) a BiLSTM trained on gold or projected data alone or their naive union, and (ii) the prior two-output MaxEnt approach (Duong et al., 2014). On average across eight Europarl languages, the method improves from 91.3% (Duong et al.) to 91.7%, and on real low-resource languages achieves 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda), surpassing prior reported results in this setting.
- Broad evaluation with appropriate setting: The simulated low-resource protocol (1k gold tokens) is standard and enables direct comparison with prior work. The inclusion of Malagasy and Kinyarwanda with real parallel data adds credibility that the method transfers beyond Europarl.
- Clear utility of projections despite noise: The paper empirically shows that naive use of projected data can hurt performance (BiLSTM Projected and Ann+Proj baselines), whereas the proposed noise modeling allows projected data to be exploited beneficially. This supports the central claim.
- Insightful analysis of learned noise: Visualizations of the learned transformation matrices reveal language- and tag-specific patterns (e.g., Swedish PRT/X confusability, Malagasy noun-to-adjective/number mappings), aligning with known projection challenges and providing qualitative evidence that the layer is learning meaningful structure.
- Practicality: The model is lightweight (a K×K matrix on top of a standard BiLSTM), easy to train end-to-end with a straightforward two-stage schedule, and does not require complex constraints or external resources beyond alignments and a source POS tagger.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to known label-noise modeling: The idea of learning a label-noise/confusion transformation on top of a classifier is established in the noisy-label literature. The novelty here is its application to cross-lingual POS projection within a BiLSTM. This is an incremental methodological contribution; a discussion and comparison to standard probabilistic noise-transition formulations is missing.
- Questionable noise formulation: The paper models p(ỹ|x) as softmax(A o), where o is the model’s predicted p(y|x). A more canonical formulation is p(ỹ=j|x) = sum_i T_{j,i} p(y=i|x) with T row-stochastic. The proposed softmax over A o introduces an extra nonlinearity and makes A depend on o in a way that departs from a straightforward class-conditional confusion matrix. The paper lacks theoretical motivation, identifiability discussion, or comparison to the linear mixture alternative. This affects technical soundness and interpretability.
- Baseline coverage: Important neural baselines are missing, such as BiLSTM-CRF and character-level features, both known to improve POS tagging, especially in morphologically rich low-resource languages. Comparisons to other projection-cleaning approaches (e.g., graph-based propagation, constraint-based methods) within the same data regime are not reported, limiting the strength of the empirical claim.
- Ablations and sensitivity: The paper fixes equal weighting between gold and projected losses (via separate averaging) but does not study the effect of varying this weight, initializations of A (e.g., identity vs random), regularization of A, or training schedules. No ablation demonstrates the specific contribution of the noise layer relative to joint training without it. Sensitivity to the amount/quality of projected data is discussed qualitatively but not quantified.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted; footnotes and figures/tables are referenced in-line in a way that hampers readability; details such as A’s initialization, regularization, and the exact sentence-selection thresholds for high-confidence parallel data are missing. No statistical significance testing is reported.

Questions to authors
- Noise model: Why use p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o) rather than a probabilistic transition matrix T with p(ỹ|x) = T o and constraints ensuring rows sum to 1? Did you compare the two formulations empirically? How is A initialized and regularized to avoid degenerate solutions?
- Loss weighting and schedule: Did you tune a weighting coefficient between the gold and projected objectives? How sensitive are results to this weight and to the two-stage training schedule versus joint training from scratch?
- Projection labeling: For NULL or many-to-one alignments, you assign the sentence-level tag-frequency distribution. What motivated this choice versus, e.g., uniform or marginal distributions from a source tagger? Any ablation on this design?
- Model capacity: Did you experiment with BiLSTM-CRF or character/subword embeddings? If so, does the noise layer still yield gains? If not, can you justify omitting these widely used components?
- Data selection: For Europarl you select 200k highest-scoring sentence pairs. What thresholding method and alignment scoring were used? How do results vary with the amount of parallel data included?
- Significance: Are improvements over Duong et al. (2014) statistically significant across languages?
