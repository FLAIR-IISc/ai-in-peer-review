- Paper proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with a learned K×K noise-adaptation layer A to map clean tag distributions to noisy cross-lingual projected tags and enable joint training on gold and projected data.

- Evaluated on 8 simulated European languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements and best reported results on the two real languages.

- Contribution: simple, effective noise-adaptation layer to explicitly model projection-induced label noise and jointly train on gold and projected data.

- Contribution: broad empirical evaluation with interpretable analyses of learned noise matrices.

- Contribution: analysis/visualization of A revealing plausible cross-lingual tag confusions and projection reliability differences across languages.

- Strength: principled, lightweight approach (only K^2 parameters) that integrates with backprop and addresses systematic cross-lingual divergences and alignment errors.

- Strength: consistent gains with small gold data; BiLSTM+noise outperforms baselines including Duong et al. (2014a) on average and on specific low-resource languages.

- Strength: model-agnostic output-level method likely transferable to other distant-supervision tasks with negligible computational overhead.

- Strength: interpretability—heatmaps of A show meaningful diagonals and confusions, aligning with data quality differences (e.g., Kinyarwanda vs Malagasy).

- Strength: sensible two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint) to avoid noisy supervision dominating early optimization.

- Weakness: limited novelty relative to existing label-noise adaptation literature; lacks discussion situating work among noise-transition methods.

- Weakness: A is global and context-independent, so it cannot capture token- or context-specific projection errors (alignment confidence, lexical idiosyncrasies).

- Weakness: incomplete comparisons and ablations — modest average gains, no significance testing, no ablation on pretraining, initialization/regularization of A, or loss weighting.

- Weakness: unclear handling of NULLs and many-to-one/one-to-many alignments; inconsistencies between sentence-level tag distribution description and later statement that only 1-1 alignments are retained.

- Weakness: missing training/regularization and hyperparameter details (dropout, early stopping, epochs); no sensitivity analysis on balancing gold vs projected loss.

- Questions for authors: how is A initialized and regularized; any identity prior or penalty toward I; training stability observations?

- Questions for authors: how is the relative weighting between gold and projected losses chosen; was a weight sweep or sensitivity analysis performed?

- Questions for authors: final treatment of NULL alignments and many-to-one cases; contribution and ablations of sentence-level tag distributions if used?

- Questions for authors: were results statistically significant against Duong et al. (2014a) given modest improvements?

- Questions for authors: results with baselines that use alignment/posterior confidence or soft labels from multiple aligned source tokens?

- Suggestions: condition the noise model on simple features (alignment score, many-to-one links, source-side POS n-grams) to capture context-dependent reliability.

- Suggestions: report training/regularization details and release code for reproducibility.

- Suggestions: experiment with rectangular A or tagset mismatch robustness to strengthen generality claims.
