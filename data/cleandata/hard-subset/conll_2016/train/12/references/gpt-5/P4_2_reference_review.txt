Summary
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small set of gold annotations with noisy distant supervision obtained via cross-lingual projection from English. It introduces a BiLSTM tagger augmented with a learnable “noise layer” that maps the model’s clean tag distribution to match the distribution of projected (noisy) tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretraining on gold data, followed by joint training on both gold and projected data, allowing the model to learn when and how to trust projected labels. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work and baselines; the learned noise matrices are analyzed to illustrate typical projection errors.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, explicit noise-modeling layer on top of a BiLSTM tagger to account for systematic errors in cross-lingual POS tag projection, trained jointly with gold and projected data.
2) Empirical evidence across 10 languages (8 simulated, 2 real low-resource) that this approach outperforms prior methods leveraging projection, with consistent improvements and particularly strong gains in Kinyarwanda.
3) Qualitative analysis of learned noise transformations that sheds light on cross-lingual projection errors and their language-specific structure.

Strengths
- Effective, general mechanism for noisy labels: The linear noise transformation applied to the model’s outputs and trained jointly with the base tagger is a principled way to reconcile clean and noisy supervision. It requires few extra parameters, integrates with standard backpropagation, and is applicable beyond POS/tag projection.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong baselines: On eight simulated languages, the method improves average accuracy (91.7%) over Duong et al. (91.3%) and substantially outperforms naïve uses of projected data. On real low-resource languages, it reaches 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda), exceeding prior results, with a notable +4.2 points over Duong et al. in Kinyarwanda.
- Sensible training strategy: Pretraining on gold data followed by joint training mitigates the risk of the noise layer absorbing errors early and stabilizes optimization. This design choice is appropriate given the high noise in projections.
- Clear articulation of projection noise and analysis: The paper explains main noise sources (alignment issues, syntactic divergence, null/many-to-one alignments) and visualizes learned A matrices, showing meaningful diagonals and plausible off-diagonal confusions differing by language, which supports the method’s interpretability.
- Realistic evaluation setup: The study includes both simulated low-resource conditions (standard for comparability) and two truly low-resource languages with limited, noisier parallel data, demonstrating robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to known noisy-label modeling: While novel in this POS projection context, the core idea (a learnable linear mapping from clean to noisy labels) is closely related to prior work on learning noise/confusion layers for multiclass classification with noisy labels. The contribution is more in application and empirical demonstration than in modeling innovation.
- Probabilistic formulation of the noise layer is under-specified: The paper uses softmax(A o_t) where o_t is a probability vector. This departs from a standard stochastic noise channel p(y~|y) = T^T o_t with T row-stochastic, or a logit-space additive model. A is unconstrained and operates on probabilities, which may blur interpretability as a confusion matrix. The paper does not justify this choice, discuss alternative parameterizations (e.g., constrained T), or analyze stability/identifiability.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: Key design choices are not dissected, including (i) two-stage training vs. joint from scratch; (ii) using sentence-level tag distributions for null/many-to-one alignments; (iii) impact of filtering to one-to-one alignments; (iv) effect of parallel data size/quality; and (v) regularization or constraints on A. Such ablations would clarify where gains come from and how robust the approach is.
- Baseline coverage and decoding choices: The BiLSTM is used without a CRF layer or structured decoding, which is a common enhancement for sequence tagging. Comparisons to BiLSTM-CRF (with/without projection) or to graph-based projection methods are absent. Statistical significance of the (often modest) gains on simulated languages is not reported.
- Clarity and reproducibility details: Some notation inconsistencies (two equivalent but differently transposed formulations of the noise mapping), broken footnotes, and sparse hyperparameter/regularization details limit reproducibility. Availability of the specific parallel data (especially Malagasy/Kinyarwanda) is unclear.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Noise layer parameterization: Why apply a free A to probability vectors followed by softmax rather than a row-stochastic confusion matrix T with y~ ∼ Cat(T^T o_t) (or a logit-space additive model)? Did you try constraining A/T to be stochastic, and how did that affect performance and interpretability?
- Initialization and regularization: How is A initialized before joint training? Do you regularize A (e.g., towards identity or sparsity) to avoid degeneracy? What happens if training starts jointly from scratch?
- Alignment treatment: How important is the use of sentence-level tag distributions for null/many-to-one alignments? Please provide an ablation using only one-to-one alignments without this heuristic.
- Structured decoding: Did you evaluate a BiLSTM-CRF backbone (with and without the noise layer)? If not, can you comment on expected effects and whether your approach is compatible?
- Data sensitivity: How does performance vary with the amount/quality of parallel data and with the strictness of alignment filtering? Any learning curves or robustness checks?
- Significance testing: Are the improvements over Duong et al. statistically significant across the simulated languages?
- Tagset mismatch claim: You state the approach supports mismatched tagsets; did you evaluate this setting, and if so, what were the results? If not, please clarify scope.
