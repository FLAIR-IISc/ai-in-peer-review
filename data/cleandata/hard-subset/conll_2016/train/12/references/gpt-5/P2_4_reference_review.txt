Summary and contributions
- The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging with cross-lingual projection by explicitly modeling the noise in projected labels. It augments a BiLSTM tagger with a learned linear “noise layer” (matrix A) that maps the clean tag distribution to the projected/noisy distribution, and jointly trains on a small gold corpus and large projected data (with two-stage training: gold pretraining, then joint training).
- Contributions (as I see them):
  1) A simple, effective noise-adaptation layer to learn when/how to trust projected tags in low-resource POS tagging, integrated into a neural tagger and trained end-to-end.
  2) Empirical validation on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages with consistent gains over strong prior work, including new SOTA on Malagasy (86.3) and Kinyarwanda (82.5).
  3) Qualitative analysis via learned confusion matrices that provide interpretable insights into projection noise.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful idea with minimal complexity that addresses a central pain point in projection: The noise layer adds only K×K parameters yet consistently improves over using projected data directly or simple union with gold. The improvements are tangible on real low-resource languages (e.g., +4.2 accuracy on Kinyarwanda over Duong et al. 2014a; +0.6 over Garrette & Baldridge 2013), indicating practical utility beyond simulations.
- Strong experimental design and comparability: The work adopts the same data splits and universal tagset mapping as Duong et al. (2014a), includes appropriate baselines (gold-only, projected-only, union), and demonstrates that naïvely adding projected data can hurt—while the noise layer recovers and improves performance.
- Insightful analysis of the learned noise: Visualizations of A reveal plausible cross-tag confusions and language-specific phenomena (e.g., determiner vs noun, pronoun vs noun), reinforcing the claim that the model learns to discount systematic projection errors and align structural divergences.
- Generality and applicability: The modeling approach is easy to port to other sequence labelling tasks and other sources of noisy supervision (distant supervision, crowd labels), and fits standard neural training pipelines (backprop with pretraining).
- Robustness across settings: Gains are observed in both high-quality Europarl projections and noisier real-language scenarios, suggesting the method is not overly tuned to one resource regime.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and modeling rigor: The core idea—an output-layer noise adaptation—is closely related to known label-noise modeling techniques. While its application to projection in low-resource POS is valuable, the formulation p(y~|x)=softmax(A o(x)) is not a proper noise channel (i.e., not constrained to a row-stochastic confusion matrix C with p(y~|x)=C^T o(x)). The interpretation of A as “confusion scores” is thus loose. A discussion or ablation of constrained vs unconstrained A would strengthen the contribution.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity studies: The paper asserts two-stage training is necessary but provides no empirical ablation (e.g., random init vs identity init of A; with/without pretraining; effect of A regularization). Similarly, no analysis of the impact of (i) the amount/quality of parallel data, (ii) the sentence-selection filter, or (iii) the NULL/many-to-one heuristic (using sentence-level tag frequencies), which seems crude and could bias learning.
- Realism of data scenarios: Simulations use 200k Europarl sentences—much more bitext than many truly low-resource cases. For the real languages, the gold training sets are ~5k tokens, which is larger than the “1000-token” narrative emphasized in the introduction and prior work; it would be important to report results at 1k to validate the benefit in truly tiny-gold regimes.
- Baselines and architecture: The neural baseline lacks common modern enhancements (e.g., character-level embeddings, BiLSTM-CRF decoding). It remains unclear whether the noise layer still yields similar gains when paired with stronger encoders/decoders trained only on gold. Statistical significance tests are not reported.
- Reproducibility details: Training details are sparse (epochs, early stopping, regularization, initialization of A), and there is no mention of code release. Given the small deltas in the 8-language average (+0.4 points over Duong et al.), clarity on tuning and stability would help.

Questions to authors
- Initialization/constraints: How is A initialized (identity vs random)? Did you try constraining A to be a (row-)stochastic confusion matrix or using p(y~|x)=softmax(log(C^T o(x))) with C≥0, rows summing to 1? Any regularization on A to avoid degenerate solutions?
- Necessity of pretraining: What happens if you train jointly from scratch? Please report a small ablation on pretraining vs no pretraining, and on initializing A as identity vs random.
- Heuristics for NULL/multi-alignments: Using sentence-level tag-frequency distributions seems coarse. Did you try alternatives (e.g., uniform over sentence-aligned tags, alignment confidence-weighted mixtures, dropping these tokens)? How sensitive are results?
- Data sensitivity: How do results vary with the amount of parallel data? For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, can you report performance with only 1k gold tokens to match the “tiny” setting emphasized in the paper and prior work?
- Stronger baselines: Do the gains persist with character-level features and/or a CRF layer? If not tested, please discuss expected interactions.
- Training details: Please provide details on optimization (epochs, early stopping, dev selection, gradient clipping, regularization) and whether any hyperparameters were tuned on dev sets.
