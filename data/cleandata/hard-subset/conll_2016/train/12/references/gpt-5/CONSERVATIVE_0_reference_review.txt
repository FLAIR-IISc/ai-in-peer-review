Summary and contributions
- A simple noise-aware sequence tagging model: the authors add a learned linear “noise” (confusion) layer on top of a BiLSTM tagger to map predicted clean tag distributions to noisy projected labels. Training is two-stage (pretrain on gold, then joint training on gold and projected data), with the noise layer intended to learn when/how to trust projections.
- Empirical evaluation on 8 simulated languages (Europarl) and 2 low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing small but mostly consistent gains over a prior projection-aware maxent model (Duong et al., 2014a), and larger gains on Kinyarwanda.
- Qualitative analysis via learned confusion matrices that offer some interpretability of cross-lingual projection noise.

Strengths
- Importance and practicality: Learning from small gold data plus noisy bilingual projections is a well-motivated, established setting. The proposed module is simple, easy to integrate, and adds few parameters, which is attractive in low-resource scenarios.
- Consistency of improvements: Across 10 languages, the model equals or surpasses baselines; notably, on Kinyarwanda the gain over Duong et al. (2014a) is sizable (+4.2 absolute), and the method outperforms Garrette and Baldridge on both languages. These are meaningful improvements in a difficult setting.
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on clean labels followed by joint training is standard practice for noise-robust learning and is appropriate here; it likely stabilizes learning of the noise layer.
- Interpretability: The learned A matrices plausibly capture cross-lingual divergences and alignment errors, which is a useful diagnostic and suggests the method is learning non-trivial structure.
- Use of universal tagset and widely used corpora/tools facilitates comparison and potential reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Learning a global confusion/noise layer to correct for label noise is a well-known idea in ML and NLP (e.g., learning with noisy labels, distant supervision, crowd annotation models). Framing it as a linear layer after a BiLSTM is a straightforward application rather than a conceptual advance. The paper should more thoroughly position itself against prior noise modeling approaches beyond Duong et al. (2014a).
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines: 
  - No ablation isolating the contribution of the noise layer vs. simply training with projected data as soft targets or with confidence weighting. A variant where projected labels supervise the base output (without A) would clarify the incremental value of A.
  - No comparison to stronger, established taggers (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character features), nor to noise-robust training baselines (bootstrapping, loss correction). Given small average gains in Europarl (0.4 absolute), significance and competitiveness vs contemporary architectures are unclear.
  - No statistical significance or variance across runs; results may be within noise on several languages.
- Inconsistencies/underspecified projection pipeline: 
  - Section 4.1.2 states only 1-to-1 alignments are retained, yet Section 3.1 discusses handling multi-/NULL alignments by assigning sentence-level tag distributions. If only 1-to-1 links are used, when do these soft distributions arise? Clarification is needed to assess how much noise the method actually faces.
  - The heuristic of using sentence-level tag frequency for multi-/NULL alignment is very crude; no analysis compares alternative projection strategies (e.g., alignment probabilities, neighborhood voting).
- Questionable generality to truly low-resource settings:
  - Europarl experiments rely on abundant parallel data and further sentence-level filtering; the noise layer’s benefit may be understated or overstated under these favorable conditions.
  - For the “real” languages, the gold training sets are 5k+ tokens, not the 1k tokens used to motivate the low-resource setting; it is unclear how performance scales with less gold supervision (e.g., 200/500/1000 tokens). Fairness to prior work and practical relevance would be better demonstrated by learning curves.
- Modeling limitations: The noise matrix A is global and context-independent. Many projection errors depend on context, morphology, and alignment uncertainty; a single global confusion matrix may be too coarse. No analysis of failure cases or contexts where the model still over-trusts projections.

Questions to authors
- Initialization and regularization of A: How is A initialized (identity, small noise, random)? Is there any regularization or prior encouraging near-identity (e.g., diagonal dominance) to prevent degenerate solutions?
- Projection details: Do you actually include multi-/NULL-aligned tokens in training, given you “retain only one-to-one alignments”? If yes, how are these tokens obtained? If no, when are sentence-level tag distributions used?
- Significance and stability: How many random seeds were run, and are improvements statistically significant? Please report means/standard deviations.
- Gold supervision size: Can you report learning curves on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda (e.g., 200/500/1000/2000 tokens) to substantiate the low-resource claim?
- Baselines/ablations: 
  - Compare to a BiLSTM(-CRF) with character features trained on Annotated and Ann+Proj (without noise), and to a simple soft-label or confidence-weighted projection baseline.
  - Provide an ablation where projected labels supervise the base output directly (no A), and one where A is fixed to identity during joint training.
- Robustness to projection noise: What happens without sentence-level filtering in Europarl? Does the noise layer deliver larger gains when projection noise is higher?

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a clean, practical idea and shows promising results, especially on Kinyarwanda. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and the empirical validation lacks key ablations, stronger baselines, and statistical rigor needed to confidently endorse the approach over established alternatives. Clarifying the projection pipeline and strengthening experiments would significantly improve the submission.
