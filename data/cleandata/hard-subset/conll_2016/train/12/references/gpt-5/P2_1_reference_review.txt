Overall assessment with respect to CoNLL criteria
- Novelty: Moderate. The core idea—learning a label-noise/confusion transformation between clean tags and noisy projected tags—is sensible and well-motivated for cross-lingual projection, but the general concept of modeling label noise via a learned confusion matrix has prior art in noisy-label learning and crowd annotation modeling. The paper’s contribution is to instantiate this idea for POS projection within a BiLSTM and show its practical utility.
- Significance: Good. Consistent gains across 8 simulated and 2 true low-resource languages, with particularly notable improvements on Kinyarwanda, suggest practical value for low-resource tagging.
- Clarity: Fair to good. The main model is clearly described and intuitive; however, some important details about how projected labels are constructed and training hyperparameters are missing or inconsistent.
- Empirical soundness: Solid but could be stronger. The comparisons to strong prior work are appropriate and on matched splits; the analyses of learned noise matrices are informative. Missing ablations and significance tests weaken the causal claims.
- Reproducibility: Limited. Key implementation choices and data processing details are underspecified; no code is provided.

Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, general neural architecture for learning when to trust distant supervision: a BiLSTM POS tagger with a learned K×K “noise layer” that maps clean tag probabilities to projected/noisy tag distributions, trained jointly on small gold data and large projected data with a two-stage training schedule. Primary contribution.
2) Empirical demonstration that this approach equals or outperforms prior state-of-the-art distant supervision methods on 8 simulated and 2 true low-resource languages, with clear evidence that naïvely mixing projected and gold data can hurt whereas the noise layer reliably helps. Secondary contribution.
3) Analysis of learned noise matrices across languages, offering interpretable insights into language- and pipeline-specific projection errors (e.g., PRON/NOUN confusions, language-specific determiners). Tertiary contribution.

Strengths
- Clear, principled handling of projection noise: Instead of heuristics or ad hoc filtering, the model explicitly learns a confusion mapping (matrix A). This directly targets the core weakness of annotation projection and is easy to integrate into standard neural taggers. The parameter cost is tiny (K×K), yet the gains are consistent.
- Consistent improvements across languages and settings: The method improves or matches the best baseline in all 10 cases, with notable gains in truly low-resource settings (e.g., +4.2 points on Kinyarwanda over Duong et al.). This supports the claim that the approach is robust where projection is the noisiest and annotated data is scarce.
- Sound experimental setup and fair comparisons: The simulated low-resource setting follows prior work; comparisons include Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013) where appropriate. The paper also shows that naïvely adding projected data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj) can degrade performance, underscoring the need for the proposed method.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of the learned A matrices give concrete evidence that the model captures plausible cross-lingual/tagging confusions and alignment artifacts. This provides qualitative validation beyond accuracy numbers.
- Simplicity and generality: The approach is plug-and-play for sequence taggers, does not depend on language similarity, and could be reused for other tasks with noisy distant supervision.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to broader literature on noisy labels: Learning a confusion/noise transition between latent clean labels and observed noisy labels is a well-established pattern (e.g., crowd annotations, EM with noise channels, neural noisy-label models). The paper does not engage with that literature or position its contribution beyond applying it to cross-lingual projection. This weakens the novelty claim.
- Inconsistencies and missing details on projection labels: The paper alternately says it retains only one-to-one alignments (4.1.2) and also describes assigning sentence-level tag distributions to tokens with many/NULL alignments (3.1). It is unclear which labels actually train the distant objective. Clarification is needed on: whether tokens with NULL/many alignments are included; how distributions are constructed/weighted; and how this interacts with the one-to-one restriction and IBM3 sentence filtering.
- Sparse training and model details hinder reproducibility: Beyond embedding/hidden sizes and a learning rate, key choices are missing: batch size, epochs/early stopping, dropout/regularization, optimizer variants, initialization of A (identity vs random), and whether A is constrained/regularized. Without these, reproducing exact results is difficult.
- Missing ablations and robustness studies: The core claims would be stronger with (i) ablation on pretrain vs joint training only; (ii) initialization/regularization of A (identity, scaled identity, random); (iii) effect of varying amount/quality of projected data; (iv) comparison to alternative uncertainty-aware baselines (e.g., weighting by alignment confidence, label smoothing, temperature scaling); (v) BiLSTM-CRF or char-aware taggers for morphologically rich languages. These would substantiate that the gains are due to the noise layer per se.
- Statistical significance and sampling: The average improvements over Duong et al. in simulated languages are small (~0.4 points). No significance tests are reported, and the training sample is the “first 1,000 tokens” rather than a random sample, which can bias results. Reporting variance over multiple random 1k-token samples would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
- Projected labels: Do you include tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments in training? If so, how exactly are their label distributions defined and weighted? How does this reconcile with retaining only one-to-one alignments?
- Noise matrix A: How is A initialized (identity, random)? Any regularization or constraints (e.g., diagonal dominance, sparsity)? Did you observe instability without pretraining? Please provide an ablation.
- Training details: Batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria, use of dropout/weight decay, selection via dev set, and whether you subsample projected data. Any curriculum or weighting between gold and projected losses?
- Significance and robustness: Are the improvements over Duong et al. statistically significant across multiple random 1k-token samples? How sensitive are results to the size/quality of the parallel corpus and to alignment filtering thresholds?
- Generality: Have you tried the noise layer with a CRF decoder or character embeddings? Do gains persist? Could you comment on extending to mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) in practice?

Overall, this paper addresses an important problem with a simple, effective idea and provides compelling empirical evidence, but would benefit from clearer methodological details, stronger positioning wrt the noisy-label literature, and ablation/robustness analyses.
