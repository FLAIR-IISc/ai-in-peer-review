Summary
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold data with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The core idea is to explicitly model the noise in projected labels via a learned linear transformation (“noise layer”) on top of a BiLSTM tagger: the base model predicts a clean tag distribution, which is then transformed to match the distribution of projected labels. Training proceeds in two stages (pretrain on gold, then joint training on gold and projected). Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and 2 truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), with particularly notable improvement on Kinyarwanda.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Explicit noise modeling for cross-lingual projection: a simple, learnable confusion matrix connecting clean and projected tag distributions within a neural tagger, trained end-to-end. This is the primary novelty in the POS projection setting.
- Empirical validation across multiple languages, including two real low-resource cases, with consistent improvements over strong baselines and interpretability via visualizations of the learned noise matrices.
- Practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) that stabilizes learning with noisy labels.

Strengths
- Clear, principled way to exploit projected data: The noise layer directly models the mismatch between true and projected tags, rather than relying on heuristics or implicit correlations. This aligns well with the known sources of projection noise (alignment errors and syntactic divergence) and explains why simply mixing projected data degrades performance (Tables 1–2, “BiLSTM Ann+Proj” vs proposed).
- Consistent, meaningful gains, especially on real low-resource languages: While gains on the 8 simulated languages are modest but consistent (average 91.7 vs 91.3), the improvements on Malagasy (86.3 vs 85.3) and particularly Kinyarwanda (82.5 vs 78.3) are substantial, supporting the claim that explicit noise modeling pays off when projections are noisier and parallel data is smaller.
- Interpretability and analysis: The visualization of the learned confusion matrices reveals intuitive patterns (e.g., determiners vs nouns, PRT/X ambiguity), showing the model learns when to trust or downweight projected tags. This adds credibility and provides insight beyond aggregate scores.
- Sound experimental setup and fair comparisons: The paper compares to strong baselines (Duong et al., MaxEnt, pure BiLSTM with various supervision sources), uses the universal tagset, and applies projection filtering consistent with prior work. The two-stage training rationale is well-motivated.
- Simplicity and generality: The approach adds few parameters (a K×K matrix) and is easy to integrate into standard neural sequence taggers, promising applicability to other tasks with distant supervision or noisy labels.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: Modeling label noise via a confusion matrix is a well-known idea in learning with noisy labels and crowd annotations. The main novelty here is its application to cross-lingual projection within a neural POS tagger. While effective, the conceptual advance is modest.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analysis: The paper lacks deeper ablations to isolate design choices, e.g., (i) what happens without the pretraining stage, (ii) initializing A to identity vs random, (iii) constraining/regularizing A (e.g., towards identity) to prevent overfitting to noisy projections, or (iv) attaching the noise layer to logits instead of probabilities. Such analyses would strengthen the methodological claims.
- Heuristic projection and filtering choices are under-explored: For unaligned or multiply-aligned tokens, using sentence-level tag frequency distributions is simplistic; alternative heuristics (e.g., local alignment confidence, neighborhood-based distributions, or ignoring those tokens) aren’t compared. The sentence-selection filtering (top IBM3 scores) may also bias the projected dataset; the impact of this filtering and the quantity/quality trade-off are not analyzed.
- Scope of “mismatched tagsets” claim not demonstrated: The method is said to support rectangular A for mismatched tagsets, but all experiments use the universal tagset with a square A. A demonstration with true tagset mismatch would substantiate the generality claim.
- Reporting and reproducibility details: Important training details are sparse (e.g., exact schedule/epochs, early stopping criteria, dropout/regularization, A initialization, optimization settings beyond lr=1.0/SGD). For the simulated setting, significance testing is not reported; given small average gains, significance would be informative.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How is the noise matrix A initialized? Did you try identity initialization or regularizing A towards identity? Any constraints to ensure identifiability/stability?
- Did you evaluate variants without the pretraining stage, or with different training curricula? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Why apply A to the post-softmax probabilities rather than to the logits? Did you compare these two formulations?
- For unaligned/multi-aligned tokens, why choose sentence-level tag distributions? Did you try alternatives (e.g., ignoring these tokens, alignment-confidence-weighted mixtures)? How does performance change?
- Can you report variance across runs and significance tests, especially for the simulated languages where gains are small?
- Can you provide a concrete experiment with mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) to support that claim?
- What early stopping/regularization settings did you use (dropout, L2)? How were hyperparameters tuned given tiny gold data?

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated application of noise modeling to cross-lingual projection with good empirical benefits on truly low-resource languages. Despite moderate novelty, the approach is simple, effective, and insightful. I lean positive.
