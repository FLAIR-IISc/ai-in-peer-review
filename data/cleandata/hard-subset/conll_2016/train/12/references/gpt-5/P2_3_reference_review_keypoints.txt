- Paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining tiny gold annotation sets with noisy cross-lingual projections using a learnable noise layer on top of a BiLSTM tagger.
- Main contribution: a learnable K×K transformation that maps clean tag distributions to projected-tag distributions to model projection noise.
- Empirical contribution: consistent or superior results across 8 simulated languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) versus strong baselines.
- Analysis contribution: visualization of learned noise matrices offers interpretable insights into projection errors and linguistic divergences.
- Strength: principled, explicit modeling of noisy distant supervision that is conceptually clean, end-to-end trainable, and broadly applicable.
- Strength: strong empirical gains on genuinely low-resource languages, notably +5.1 over a resource-light method for Malagasy.
- Strength: consistent, if modest, improvements across simulation languages with average accuracy gains and uniform behavior.
- Strength: learned noise matrices enable useful qualitative diagnostics of cross-lingual tag correspondences.
- Strength: sensible training protocol (pretrain on gold, then joint training) and standard projection procedures.
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to established label-noise literature; needs discussion of connections to standard transition/ noise models.
- Weakness: modest average gains in simulation (~0.4 absolute) and absence of statistical significance testing or variance estimates.
- Weakness: missing key ablations and sensitivity analyses (two-stage training effect, noise layer vs. regularized union, alignment/parallel size sensitivity).
- Weakness: baselines could be stronger (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, character/subword encoders) to contextualize gains from the noise layer.
- Weakness: unclear identifiability and regularization of the noise matrix A; lack of constraints or discussion of failure modes with very little gold data.
- Question: why parameterize p(ỹ|x)=softmax(A o) instead of standard p(ỹ|y)=T and p(y|x)=o with p(ỹ|x)=T^T o, and did they try constraining A to be stochastic?
- Question: how critical is the two-stage training—provide ablations for joint-from-scratch and trains that continue on only projected or only gold data.
- Question: sensitivity to parallel corpus size/quality and to one-to-one alignment restriction—do gains persist under varied projection sizes/filters?
- Question: impact of labeling NULL/many-to-one tokens via sentence-level tag distributions—report ablations where they are ignored or handled differently.
- Question: are improvements statistically significant across languages—provide per-language tests or confidence intervals.
- Question: were stronger supervised baselines (BiLSTM-CRF or character-aware models) tried, and would gains persist with them?
- Overall assessment: clear, practical, and interpretable contribution with meaningful real-world gains, but needs additional ablations, stronger baselines, and clearer discussion of noise-modeling choices for stronger acceptance case.
