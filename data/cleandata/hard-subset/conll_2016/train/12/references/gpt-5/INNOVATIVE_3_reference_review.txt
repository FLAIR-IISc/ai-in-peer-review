Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small gold-annotated corpus with noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is to explicitly learn a noise transformation between clean tags and projected tags within a BiLSTM tagger: the model produces a distribution over gold tags, then applies a learnable confusion matrix to match the distribution observed in projected labels. Training is done in two stages (gold pretraining, then joint training with projected data). Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, general, and interpretable “noise layer” that explicitly models projection noise between clean and projected tag spaces, integrated end-to-end within a neural sequence tagger.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages, including two truly low-resource cases, with consistent improvements over prior work, and insightful analysis via learned confusion matrices.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training with projected data) that lets the model learn when/how much to trust noisy projections without heavy heuristic filtering.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and generality: Explicitly learning a confusion matrix on top of neural tag probabilities is a clean and widely applicable way to exploit distant supervision. It neatly separates modeling the task (gold-tag predictor) from modeling supervision noise (projection map), and naturally supports mismatched tagsets. The idea is simple yet powerful.
- Solid empirical gains where it matters: The approach outperforms the prior state of the art on both simulated low-resource settings (average 91.7 vs 91.3) and, more importantly, on real low-resource languages: 86.3 on Malagasy and 82.5 on Kinyarwanda, improving over Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013). These results substantiate that learning the noise pays off, especially under noisier projection.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizing the learned A matrices provides linguistic insight (e.g., determiner/noun and pronoun/noun confusions, language-specific divergences). This is a rare advantage in neural approaches and supports the method’s plausibility.
- Practicality and simplicity: The method adds very few parameters (K×K) and is easy to implement on top of any sequence tagger. The two-stage training is straightforward and likely to transfer to other tasks that use distant supervision or crowd-sourced labels.
- Clear ablation contrasts: The BiLSTM Annotated vs Projected vs Ann+Proj vs +Noise comparisons clearly show that naive use of projected data is harmful/limited, while the noise layer unlocks consistent gains.

Weaknesses
- Positioning w.r.t. noisy-label literature: The paper does not sufficiently connect to general label-noise modeling (e.g., noise layers/confusion matrices as in Sukhbaatar et al., Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, and related distant supervision work). While applying this to cross-lingual projection in POS is valuable, the methodological core is close to known ideas; a deeper discussion and possibly comparisons would strengthen novelty claims.
- Limited scope of validation: The claim that the approach supports mismatched tagsets is not empirically validated; all experiments use universal tags on both sides. No experiments beyond POS tagging (e.g., NER, parsing) are provided, which would help demonstrate generality.
- Baselines and ablations could be broader: Missing comparisons to LSTM-CRF with type constraints or projection-cleaning constraints (e.g., Täckström et al.), and to more recent cross-lingual neural transfer methods. Ablations on the necessity of pretraining, initialization/regularization of A, and data quality/quantity (sensitivity to alignment filtering, parallel corpus size) are absent.
- Identifiability and regularization of A: There is potential confounding between θ (the tagger) and A (the noise map). The paper does not discuss constraints or priors on A (e.g., diagonal dominance, initialization at identity), nor calibration, which could affect stability/interpretability and robustness.
- Heuristic for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens: Assigning sentence-level tag distributions to such tokens is very coarse and may bias learning. Some analysis of its prevalence and impact would strengthen the story.

Questions to Authors
- How does your noise-layer formulation relate to prior work on learning with noisy labels (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., NIPS 2015; Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, ICLR 2017) and to distant supervision noise modeling in NLP? What are key differences/advantages in your setup?
- Did you evaluate rectangular A with mismatched tagsets (e.g., Penn tags in English to Universal tags in the target)? Any results to confirm this claimed flexibility?
- How sensitive are results to pretraining duration and to the amount/quality of projected data? Can you provide curves vs. number of parallel sentences or alignment filtering thresholds?
- How is A initialized and is it regularized (e.g., towards identity or diagonal dominance)? What happens if A is fixed to identity, or initialized randomly vs identity?
- Do you foresee sequence-level extensions (e.g., to NER with span-level projection or to parsing with tree projections)? Any preliminary experiments?

Overall, this is a compelling, clean idea with encouraging results and interpretability. Despite some missing connections and ablations, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to inspire follow-up work beyond POS tagging.
