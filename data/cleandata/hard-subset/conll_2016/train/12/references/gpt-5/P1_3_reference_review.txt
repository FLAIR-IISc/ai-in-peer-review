Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold-labeled data with noisy cross-lingual projections. It proposes a BiLSTM sequence tagger augmented with an explicit label-noise transformation (“noise layer”) that maps clean tag distributions to the distribution of projected/noisy tags. Training is two-stage: pretrain on gold data, then jointly train on gold and projected data, with the noise layer applied only to projected instances. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A simple, principled, and effective modeling of projection noise via a learned linear transformation in tag space, enabling end-to-end training that learns when to trust projected labels.
2) Empirical evidence that this approach yields consistent improvements over strong prior cross-lingual projection methods across 10 languages, with interpretable analyses of the learned noise matrices.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) that stabilizes learning with noisy distant supervision.

Strengths
- Clear and effective idea with good empirical support: Modeling projected-label noise via a learned tag-transition/confusion matrix is conceptually sound, easy to implement, and empirically beneficial. The improvements are consistent across 8 simulation languages (average 91.7 vs. 91.3 for Duong et al.) and sizable on the real low-resource languages (Malagasy: 86.3 vs. 85.3; Kinyarwanda: 82.5 vs. 78.3), indicating the approach travels beyond Europarl conditions.
- Interpretable analysis: Visualizations of the learned A matrices provide insight into cross-lingual/projection error patterns (e.g., Swedish PRT/X confusions; Malagasy nouns mapping to adjectives/numbers). This strengthens the case that the model captures systematic projection artifacts rather than overfitting idiosyncrasies.
- Sensible training strategy: The two-stage procedure (pretrain on clean; joint training with noise layer) addresses instability that often plagues learning with noisy labels, a practical contribution others can adopt.
- Lightweight and general: The noise layer adds only K×K parameters, integrates with standard sequence models (BiLSTM), and is applicable to mismatched tagsets. The idea should transfer to other tasks with projected or distantly supervised labels.
- Fair and relevant evaluation setting: Low-resource regimes are simulated with 1k tokens of gold data; real Malagasy and Kinyarwanda results use established datasets/splits. Comparisons include both projection-based SOTA (Duong et al., 2014a) and resource-light methods (Garrette & Baldridge, 2013).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to label-noise literature: The explicit label-transition/noise layer is closely related to long-standing approaches for learning with noisy labels (e.g., confusion-matrix modeling, Dawid–Skene style label transitions; more recent neural label-noise transition layers). The contribution is adapting this to cross-lingual POS projection with a BiLSTM, rather than a fundamentally new learning principle. This should be acknowledged and positioned more carefully.
- Baselines could be stronger: The supervised baselines are MaxEnt and a plain BiLSTM without CRF decoding, character/subword features, or pre-trained embeddings. In low-resource tagging, char-level modeling often yields large gains. It remains unclear whether the proposed gains would hold relative to a BiLSTM-CRF + char model, with or without the noise layer. This limits the strength of the empirical claims.
- Inconsistent/unclear projection labeling details: The paper states they retain only one-to-one alignments (to reduce noise) yet earlier also describes handling many-to-one/unaligned cases by using sentence-level tag distributions for the target token. It’s unclear which protocol was used in the final experiments. The choice strongly affects the noise characteristics and comparability.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: Key questions remain unanswered: (a) effect of the amount of gold data (beyond 1k tokens); (b) effect of the amount/quality of parallel data; (c) contribution of the pretraining stage vs. joint training from scratch; (d) performance if A is constrained to identity or learned with regularization to favor diagonality; (e) whether A is initialized to identity or zeros, and whether regularization to prevent degenerate scaling is used. Such ablations would clarify when and why the method helps.
- Reproducibility and setup details: Important details are sparse (GIZA++ settings, alignment symmetrization, criteria for selecting the 200k sentences, hyperparameter tuning, initialization/regularization of A, early stopping). The Kinyarwanda data source is referenced but not readily accessible. Code/data release would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to authors
- Projection protocol: Do you truly “retain only one-to-one alignments” in all experiments? If so, how do you generate sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/many-aligned tokens, or are such tokens excluded? Please clarify the exact labeling pipeline used in the reported results.
- Initialization and regularization of A: How is A initialized (identity, zeros, random)? Do you use any regularization (e.g., L2, diagonal prior) on A to encourage near-identity mappings or prevent scaling/identifiability issues with softmax(A o)?
- Sensitivity to supervision size: How do results vary with 500/1k/2k/5k gold tokens (especially in the real languages where you have ~5k gold tokens)? Does the benefit of the noise layer diminish as gold data grows?
- Stronger baselines: Have you tried BiLSTM-CRF and/or character-level features? How do your gains compare to a stronger supervised baseline, and can the noise layer further improve such a model?
- Parallel data quality: For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, what is the impact of parallel data size/quality? Do performance and the learned A matrices change as you vary the subset of parallel data or alignment filtering thresholds?

Additional comments
- Position the work more clearly relative to prior noisy-label modeling in NLP/ML, and cite relevant literature on label transition/noise layers.
- Consider releasing code and preprocessing scripts for transparency.
- Exploring tagset mismatch scenarios would further demonstrate generality.
