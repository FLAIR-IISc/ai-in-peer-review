Summary and contributions
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small gold corpus with noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is a BiLSTM tagger augmented with a learned “noise layer”: a K×K linear transformation applied to the model’s output distribution and followed by a softmax to better match the distribution of projected tags. Training proceeds in two stages (pretrain on gold; then joint training on gold and projected). Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work, including Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette and Baldridge (2013).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit label-noise adaptation layer to model cross-lingual projection noise, jointly trained with a sequence model and shown to improve POS tagging in low-resource settings.
2) A thorough empirical evaluation across 10 languages with analyses of learned noise transformations, demonstrating the method’s utility and interpretability.
3) Practical training protocol (pretraining, then joint learning) for stabilizing learning with highly noisy supervision.

Strengths
- Addresses a real pain point with projection: The paper focuses on a pervasive issue—noisy, biased projected labels due to alignment errors and structural divergences—and proposes a targeted remedy that integrates well with neural sequence tagging.
- Simple and effective modeling: The noise layer adds very few parameters, is easy to implement, and yields consistent improvements over strong baselines. Average gains over Duong et al. are modest but consistent on the 8 Europarl languages; the gains in Kinyarwanda are notably larger (+4.2 points over Duong et al.).
- Joint learning from gold and projected data is well motivated: Pretraining on gold to avoid bad local optima and then continuing with projected data makes practical sense and is validated empirically.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: Visualizations of the learned A matrices reveal plausible cross-tag confusions per language and offer diagnostic value (e.g., greater noise for Kinyarwanda aligns with its smaller parallel data).
- Evaluation breadth and realism: The paper balances simulated and true low-resource settings and reports on two genuinely low-resource languages with publicly known datasets.

Weaknesses
- Modeling choice and identifiability of the noise layer: The parameterization p(ỹ|x) = softmax(A o), with o = p(y|x), does not correspond to a standard class-conditional noise model (which typically uses õ = T o with T row-stochastic). As defined, A operates on probabilities inside a softmax, making it harder to interpret as a confusion/transition matrix and raising identifiability concerns between A and the base classifier. No constraints or regularization (e.g., diagonal dominance, row-normalization, priors) are described. An ablation comparing this to a standard transition matrix (õ = T o), diagonal priors, or initializing A to identity would clarify whether the proposed form is necessary and robust.
- Projection heuristics seem crude and untested: When tokens are unaligned or multiply aligned, the paper assigns the English sentence-level tag frequency distribution as supervision. This is likely to inject strong frequency bias and weak signal. Alternatives (uniform distribution, alignment posterior-weighted tag distributions, marginalizing over soft alignments, or pruning low-confidence tokens) are not explored. An ablation here would be important, especially in low-resource settings where each noisy supervision step matters.
- Baselines and ablations are limited: Modern sequence taggers often include CRF decoding and character-level representations; neither appears in the baselines. A BiLSTM-CRF or BiLSTM+char vs. BiLSTM+noise-layer comparison would strengthen claims. There is also no ablation on: (i) using gold only; (ii) adding projected data without noise but with instance reweighting or confidence filtering; (iii) different joint training schedules.
- Experimental clarity and fairness: For simulated settings, training on only 1k gold tokens is appropriate; for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, the training sets are ~5k tokens, while the narrative repeatedly invokes the “1000 tokens” scenario. Please clarify the exact gold sizes used for each model and whether baselines used identical sizes. Statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported. Hyperparameter choices (dropout, epochs, early stopping) are under-specified, and no development-set tuning details are given.
- Scope vs. claims: The paper states that the approach supports mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), but experiments only use universal tagsets. A small controlled experiment with mismatched tagsets or language-specific-to-universal mappings would back up this claim.

Questions to authors
- Why use softmax(A o) rather than a row-stochastic transition matrix T with õ = T o? Did you try constraining A or using logits (i.e., A applied to pre-softmax scores) instead of probabilities? Any diagonal regularization or initialization to identity?
- How sensitive are results to the projection labeling heuristics for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens? Please provide an ablation (sentence-level tag prior vs. uniform vs. alignment posterior-based distributions).
- What are the exact gold training sizes for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda for your method and for each baseline? If they differ from the often-cited 1k tokens, please clarify.
- Do results hold when adding a CRF layer and/or character-level features to all neural baselines? If not tested, can you discuss expected interactions?
- Can you report variance across multiple random seeds and significance tests? Also provide coverage statistics: proportion of one-to-one aligned target tokens retained, before/after filtering.
- Did you try initializing A to identity and/or encouraging diagonal dominance? How does this affect learning speed and final accuracy?

Overall evaluation
The paper offers a practical and interpretable way to leverage noisy projections and shows consistent gains across many languages, including two genuine low-resource cases. However, the noise model’s formulation is unconventional and insufficiently justified, baselines/ablations are limited, and some experimental details are under-specified. With clarifications and stronger ablations—especially around the noise layer parameterization and projection heuristics—this would be a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward borderline accept, contingent on addressing the core methodological and empirical questions.
