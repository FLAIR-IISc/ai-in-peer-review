Recommendation: Weak Accept

Summary
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by leveraging both a small set of gold annotations and noisy cross-lingual projections. The key idea is to augment a BiLSTM tagger with a learned “noise layer,” a linear transformation (A) over the model’s output distribution, to map clean tag predictions to the distribution of noisy projected tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold data, then jointly train on gold and projected data with the noise layer active. Experiments on eight Europarl languages in a simulated low-resource setting and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a). Visualizations of A provide interpretable insights into projection noise patterns.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Explicit noise modeling for cross-lingual projection: A simple, global confusion-matrix layer that learns when/how to trust projected tags and enables joint training on gold + noisy data.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages, including improvements over prior state of the art in Malagasy and Kinyarwanda under realistic data constraints.
3) Analysis of learned noise patterns via A, offering interpretable diagnostics about projection bias and alignment noise.

Strengths
- Clear, principled approach with minimal complexity: The noise layer is a compact, differentiable way to reconcile clean and noisy supervision, requiring few extra parameters and integrating seamlessly with end-to-end training. The two-stage training is sensible and practical.
- Strong, consistent empirical results: The model outperforms relevant baselines in 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource settings, including prior SOTA on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. Baselines are appropriate and include both neural and non-neural comparators and naive uses of projected data.
- Interpretability and analysis: The visualizations of A reveal meaningful cross-lingual confusion patterns (e.g., DET–NOUN, PRT–X), supporting the core claim that projection noise is systematic and learnable.
- Relevance and scope: The setting (tiny gold + noisy projection) matches practical low-resource scenarios, and the method is general enough to be applied to other tasks relying on distant supervision.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling of heterogeneity in noise: The noise layer is a single global K×K matrix, independent of token/context, alignment type, or confidence. Many projection errors are context-, lexical-, or alignment-specific (e.g., many-to-one alignments, NULL alignments, syntactic divergence). The paper would be stronger with variants that condition on alignment features (1-1 vs many-to-one/NULL), alignment scores, or source-side tags, or with per-tag or per-alignment-type A matrices. An ablation or comparison to alternatives (e.g., instance weighting by alignment confidence, EM with latent gold y) is missing.
- Experimental clarity/inconsistencies around projection labeling: Section 3.1 states that for many-to-one or NULL-aligned tokens, the projected supervision is a sentence-level tag distribution; Section 4.1.2 says only one-to-one alignments are retained. This appears contradictory. It is unclear what exact supervision is used per token in each setting and how often distributional labels vs one-hot are employed. This ambiguity affects reproducibility and interpretation of results.
- Limited ablations and significance analysis: No statistical significance tests are reported. Key ablations are missing: (i) joint training without pretraining, (ii) training on projected-only with the noise layer, (iii) the impact of sentence filtering and parallel data size/quality, (iv) CRF top layer vs softmax, (v) character/subword features that are known to help POS tagging, especially in morphologically rich languages. These would help isolate the contribution of the noise layer and assess robustness.
- “Simulated low-resource” may overstate practicality: Europarl experiments rely on abundant, high-quality bitext (200k selected sentences per language). The two truly low-resource settings are more convincing; however, there is no systematic sensitivity analysis to parallel size/quality or domain mismatch, which is critical for deployment.
- Missing details for reproducibility: Initialization of A (identity vs random), regularization/constraints on A, early stopping criteria, number of epochs, dropout, vocabulary handling, OOV treatment, and whether embeddings are pretrained or randomly initialized are not clearly specified. The claim that A can handle mismatched tagsets is not exercised (both sides use the universal tagset).

Questions to Authors
- Projection labeling: Do you retain only one-to-one alignments (as in §4.1.2), or do you also supply distributional labels for many/NULL alignments (as in §3.1)? If both, how are they combined? What fraction of tokens receives each type of supervision per language?
- A initialization and regularization: How is A initialized (identity bias vs random)? Are there priors/penalties to encourage diagonality or prevent degenerate scales? Did you observe instability without constraints?
- Sensitivity analyses: How does performance vary with (i) the amount and quality of parallel data, (ii) the proportion of gold tokens, and (iii) the sentence filtering threshold? Any results on domain mismatch?
- Alternatives to a global A: Did you try conditioning A on alignment type (1-1 vs NULL/many), alignment score, or source tag? If so, how did they compare?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a CRF decoding layer and character/subword features; while orthogonal to your contribution, they would provide stronger baselines and could interact synergistically with the noise layer.
- Report significance tests and learning curves. Include ablations isolating pretraining and the sentence filtering step.
- Clarify training details (epochs, early stopping, dropout, embedding initialization) and release code to improve reproducibility.
- Explore confidence-weighted losses (e.g., scale the projected loss by alignment probability) or context-sensitive noise layers as future work.

Overall, this is a neat, well-motivated idea with solid empirical gains. Addressing clarity issues around projection labeling and adding key ablations/sensitivity analyses would further strengthen the paper.
