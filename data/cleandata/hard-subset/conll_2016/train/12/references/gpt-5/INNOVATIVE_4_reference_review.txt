Summary and contributions
- Primary: Introduces an explicit, learnable noise transformation (“output noising”) on top of a BiLSTM tagger to model the mapping between true POS tags and noisy cross-lingually projected tags. This learns when and how much to trust distant supervision.
- Secondary: Joint training regime combining a tiny amount of gold supervision with large noisy projected data, with a pragmatic two-stage schedule (pretrain on gold, then continue jointly).
- Tertiary: Broad empirical study on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages showing consistent gains over strong baselines, with interpretable analyses of the learned noise matrices.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and novel framing for cross-lingual projection: The idea of treating projected labels as the output of a noise channel and learning a small confusion matrix A that adapts model outputs to the projection space is simple, elegant, and impactful. It directly addresses a core pain-point in projection (alignment/tagset noise) rather than only filtering or heuristic weighting. The rectangular A extension for mismatched tagsets increases applicability.
- Practical gains where it matters: The approach yields notable improvements, especially for truly low-resource settings (e.g., +4.2 points over Duong et al. on Kinyarwanda, +0.9 over Duong et al. and +5.1 over BiLSTM Annotated on Malagasy). Even in simulated settings, it slightly but consistently outperforms the best prior work. The improvements are achieved with a tiny number of additional parameters, suggesting robustness.
- Interpretability and diagnostics: Visualizations of A are insightful, revealing language-specific confusions (e.g., DET↔NOUN or PRT↔X) and giving users a way to inspect when projection is trusted or ignored. This is valuable for error analysis and could guide annotation or alignment improvements.
- Generalizable recipe: The architecture is straightforward to implement atop standard sequence models and is task-agnostic; the approach plausibly extends to other distant supervision scenarios (NER, parsing) and to crowdsourced/weak labels. The training schedule is practical for low-resource workflows.
- Solid empirical setup: Evaluations cover both simulated (Europarl) and real-world low-resource languages with realistic small gold sets, and comparisons include strong baselines (Duong et al., Garrette & Baldridge) and naive BiLSTM configurations. The results clearly demonstrate that naively mixing projected labels can hurt, while the noise layer unlocks their value.

Weaknesses
- Limited positioning vs. prior noise-modeling literature: The paper frames the approach as the first to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projection. However, learning label-noise/confusion layers is a known idea in supervised and distant supervision settings (e.g., noise-adaptation/annotator models, confusion matrices for crowd labels). The novelty here is its targeted application to cross-lingual POS projection and the specific training regime; this distinction should be clarified and prior art discussed more deeply.
- Global, context-independent noise assumption: Using a single matrix A for all tokens ignores that projection noise varies with alignment confidence, token type, morphology, and syntactic position. This may limit performance, particularly in noisier corpora (as hinted by Swedish and Kinyarwanda). Conditioning A on features (or learning a gating over A, or per-tag temperature scaling) could be more faithful to the data. Some analysis of failure cases or calibration vs. alignment confidence would strengthen the paper.
- Projection labeling choices are heuristic and under-analyzed: For many-to-one/NULL alignments, using the sentence-level tag distribution seems crude. Alternatives (alignment posteriors, marginalization over aligned English tags, or soft constraints) are not compared. An ablation isolating the contribution of: (i) one-to-one restriction, (ii) sentence-level distributions, (iii) alignment quality filtering (IBM3 sentence score), would clarify where gains come from.
- Missing ablations and baselines: Important ablations are absent: effect of the noise layer without gold pretraining; sensitivity to the amount of gold data; regularization on A; learning-rate schedules; proportion of projected vs. gold examples per batch. Baseline gaps include a BiLSTM-CRF, confidence-weighted training, and constraint-driven projection cleaning (e.g., Täckström et al.) applied atop BiLSTM. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Reproducibility/details: While implementation framework is stated, details on data filtering thresholds (e.g., IBM3 score cutoff), batching ratios, A initialization, and any constraints/regularization on A are missing. Code release and alignment configurations (e.g., symmetrization) are not specified.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Did you consider encouraging a near-diagonal structure (e.g., L2 to identity) or temperature scaling to avoid degenerate solutions?
- How do you mix projected vs. gold examples during joint training (batching ratio, curriculum)? How sensitive are results to that ratio?
- Did you experiment with conditioning the noise on alignment confidence (e.g., using posterior from Giza++ or alignment type)? If so, what was the effect?
- For NULL/many-to-many alignments, why choose sentence-level tag distributions over alignment posteriors or marginalization over aligned English tags? Any ablations?
- Did you try BiLSTM-CRF baselines, and would the noise layer still help on top of a CRF? Similarly, how does your method compare to confidence-weighted loss on projected labels?
- In any experiment did you use a rectangular A with mismatched tagsets? If yes, please report; if not, what do you anticipate would be needed in practice?
- Are the improvements statistically significant across runs? What is variance across seeds?

Overall, I find the central idea novel for the cross-lingual projection setting, conceptually appealing, and practically useful. Despite some missing ablations and limited discussion of related noise-modeling work, the paper opens a promising direction with clear gains on genuinely low-resource languages. I lean toward acceptance.
