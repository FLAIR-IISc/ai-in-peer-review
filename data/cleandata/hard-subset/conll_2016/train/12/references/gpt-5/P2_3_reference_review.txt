Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by jointly leveraging a tiny amount of gold annotations and noisy cross-lingual projections from parallel text. The core idea is to explicitly model projection noise with a learnable transformation (“noise layer”) on top of a BiLSTM tagger, trained end-to-end with gold and projected data.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) A simple, effective noise-aware training objective for distant supervision: a learnable K×K transformation that maps clean tag distributions to projected-tag distributions, enabling the model to “learn when to trust” projected labels.
  2) Empirical evidence across 8 simulated languages and 2 genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) that the approach consistently matches or outperforms strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013).
  3) Analysis/interpretability: visualization of the learned noise matrices provides insight into cross-lingual projection errors and linguistic divergences.

Strengths
- Explicit and principled handling of noisy distant supervision. Prior cross-lingual POS projection work often relied on heuristics, jointly engineered features, or implicit modeling. Here, the noise is modeled as a parameterized transformation trained end-to-end. This is conceptually clean, easy to implement, and broadly applicable to other settings with noisy labels (crowd labels, weak supervision).
- Strong empirical results on truly low-resource languages. On Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), the method surpasses both the state-of-the-art projection-based approach (Duong et al., 2014a) and the resource-light approach (Garrette & Baldridge, 2013), with notably large gains for Malagasy (+5.1 over Garrette, +1.0 over Duong). This demonstrates practical value under realistic constraints.
- Consistent gains over solid baselines in simulations. Across 8 Europarl languages, the average accuracy (91.7) improves over Duong et al. (91.3) and several reasonable BiLSTM baselines (trained on gold only, projected only, and union). While the average gain is modest, it is uniform and coupled with interpretability.
- Interpretability and qualitative analysis. The learned noise matrices make visible which tag correspondences are reliable or noisy per language (e.g., pronoun vs noun, determiner vs numeral). This is useful both scientifically and practically for diagnosing projection issues.
- Sensible training protocol and realistic data usage. The two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint training) addresses identifiability/optimization issues common with noisy-label training. The projection choices (GIZA++ IBM3, one-to-one alignments, sentence filtering) are standard and reasonable.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to broader “learning with noisy labels” literature and prior projection work. While applying a noise layer to cross-lingual POS is valuable and seems new in this context, the idea of learning a confusion/noise transformation is well-established. The paper would benefit from discussing connections to standard label-noise transition models (T y) and clarifying why the chosen parameterization softmax(A o) is preferable (e.g., identifiability, stability).
- Modest average gains in simulation and no statistical significance testing. The improvement over the strongest prior method is ~0.4 absolute on average across 8 languages. Absent statistical tests or variance estimates, it is unclear if the gains are significant per language. This matters for CoNLL acceptance where incremental gains should be supported by robust evidence.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses. Important questions are left unanswered: 
  - Effect of the two-stage training (does the method fail without gold pretraining?).
  - Impact of the noise layer vs. simple union training under stronger regularization.
  - Sensitivity to alignment quality/size of parallel data (beyond the two real cases).
  - Contribution of the “sentence-level tag distribution” heuristic for NULL/many-to-one alignments.
- Baseline scope could be stronger. Modern POS taggers commonly include sequence-level decoding (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and character/subword encoders, which often help in low-resource morphology-rich settings. While the goal is to test distant supervision, a stronger supervised baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF on gold-only) and/or character features would contextualize the gain due to the noise layer more convincingly.
- The noise model’s identifiability and regularization are unclear. Without constraints (e.g., stochasticity of a transition matrix, priors), A may overfit or be hard to interpret. The reported diagonals look reasonable, but the paper does not discuss constraints, regularization, or failure modes, especially with very little gold data.

Questions to authors
- Why use p(ỹ|x)=softmax(A o) instead of a standard label-noise transition p(ỹ|y)=T and p(y|x)=o, leading to p(ỹ|x)=T^T o? Does your parameterization have optimization or expressiveness advantages? Did you try constraining A or using a stochastic T?
- How critical is the two-stage training? Please provide an ablation where you train jointly from scratch and another where you continue training only on projected data (or only on gold) to quantify the role of each component.
- How sensitive are results to the size/quality of parallel corpora and to the one-to-one alignment restriction? Do gains persist if you vary the number of projected sentences or alignment filtering thresholds?
- What is the impact of labeling NULL/many-to-one tokens via sentence-level tag distributions? Please report an ablation where such tokens are ignored or handled differently.
- Are improvements statistically significant across the simulated languages? Please provide per-language significance tests or confidence intervals.
- Did you try a BiLSTM-CRF or character-aware baselines on the gold-only and Ann+Proj settings? If not, can you comment on whether you expect your gains to persist with stronger supervised architectures?

Overall assessment
A clear, well-motivated, and practically useful contribution for low-resource POS tagging with noisy projections. The approach is simple, general, and shows meaningful gains on real low-resource languages, with interpretable analyses. The main limitations are modest average gains in simulations, limited ablations, and incomplete discussion of the noise modeling choice. With clarifications and additional experiments, I would lean toward acceptance.
