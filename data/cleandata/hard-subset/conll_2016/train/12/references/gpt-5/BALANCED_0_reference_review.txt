Summary
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a tiny amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected labels. The core idea is to explicitly model the projection noise via a learnable transformation (“noise layer”) atop a BiLSTM tagger. The base BiLSTM predicts a clean tag distribution; a learned matrix A then maps this distribution into the projected-label space, allowing the model to learn when and how to trust projected tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold data, then jointly train on both gold and projected data with the noise layer active. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages (Europarl) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a). Analyses of learned A matrices provide interpretable insights into projection noise across languages.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective noise-adaptation layer that explicitly models projection-induced tag confusions and enables joint training on gold and noisy projected data.
2) Empirical evidence across 10 languages that this approach improves over prior projection-based methods and naive mixing of projected and gold data.
3) Qualitative analysis of learned noise matrices that elucidates cross-lingual projection errors and supports the method’s interpretability.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling of label noise: The learnable matrix A gives a direct, compact parameterization of projection noise at the tag level. It is simple to implement (a linear transformation before softmax), readily trainable end-to-end, and general (applicable to other noisy supervision settings). The approach improves average accuracy over Duong et al. (2014a) on the 8-language simulation (91.7 vs 91.3) and on both real low-resource languages (Malagasy 86.3 vs 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs 78.3), supporting its effectiveness.
- Strong empirical support and consistent gains: The paper demonstrates that naive use of projected labels degrades performance (e.g., average 78.0 with BiLSTM Projected vs 88.1 with BiLSTM Annotated), while the proposed noise layer reliably leverages projection to surpass both maxent and BiLSTM baselines, and the prior state of the art. The consistency across diverse languages (German, Greek, Italian, etc.) strengthens the case for generality.
- Interpretable analysis: Visualizations of A highlight language-specific confusion patterns (e.g., DET/NUM in Italian, PRT/X in Swedish; broader noise in Kinyarwanda vs Malagasy). This interpretability is valuable for diagnosing projection pipelines and may guide future filtering heuristics or alignment improvements.
- Sensible training protocol: Pretraining on gold, then joint training, is a pragmatic strategy to stabilize learning and avoid being misled by early noise. The method also supports training with hard projected labels and soft distributions (for NULL/multi-aligned cases), which is a practical extension.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to label-noise adaptation literature: Modeling label noise via a learnable confusion/transition matrix after the classifier is a known strategy in supervised learning with noisy labels. The novelty here is the application to cross-lingual projection for POS tagging and the empirical study, rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea. This should be better situated relative to general noise-adaptation approaches.
- Global, context-independent noise model: A single matrix A assumes tag confusions are stationary across tokens, alignment types, and confidence levels. In projection, noise often depends on alignment quality (1–1 vs 1–n/NULL), sentence/word-level alignment scores, or syntactic divergence. The paper does not condition A on these signals, nor ablate the effect of using a single A vs more structured/gated variants. This may leave performance on harder languages on the table.
- Baselines and ablations are thin: 
  - No comparison to simple strong alternatives, e.g., instance weighting by alignment confidence, temperature/label smoothing, or marginalizing alignments while down-weighting projected examples.
  - No BiLSTM-CRF baseline, which is standard for sequence tagging and may provide stronger performance than the plain BiLSTM used here.
  - No ablations on pretraining vs random initialization, or on the contribution of using soft distributions for NULL/multi-alignments vs ignoring them.
  - No analysis of sample efficiency (performance vs size of projected corpus) or sensitivity to A’s initialization/regularization.
- Heuristic handling of unaligned/multi-aligned tokens: Assigning a sentence-level tag frequency distribution to such tokens is quite coarse and could inject bias. It would be informative to compare to ignoring these tokens, using alignment confidences, or simple heuristics (e.g., copying nearest aligned neighbor).
- Missing details and statistical rigor: The paper does not specify how A is initialized (identity? zeros?), whether any regularization or constraints (e.g., diagonal bias) are used, or whether improvements are statistically significant. For reproducibility, more hyperparameter details (e.g., batch sizes, epochs, early stopping) and release plans for code/data splits would help.

Questions to authors
- How is the noise matrix A initialized, and do you apply any regularization (e.g., L2, diagonal bias, temperature scaling) or constraints (e.g., row/column normalization)? How sensitive are results to this?
- Did you try conditioning the noise on alignment properties (1–1 vs 1–n/NULL, alignment score bins), e.g., via multiple A matrices or a gated mixture? If so, what was the outcome?
- Why choose sentence-level tag frequency distributions for NULL/multi-aligned tokens? How do results change if these tokens are ignored, or if you use alignment confidence to weight their loss?
- Can you report ablations: (i) without pretraining, (ii) BiLSTM Ann+Proj with instance weighting by alignment score, and (iii) BiLSTM-CRF variants with/without the noise layer?
- Are the improvements in Tables 1–2 statistically significant? Please include significance testing across runs or seeds.
- How does performance vary with the amount of projected data (e.g., 50k/100k/200k sentences) and with different alignment filters (e.g., including 1–n alignments)?
- Do you plan to release code and preprocessing scripts (including the sentence selection based on IBM Model 3 scores) to facilitate replication?
