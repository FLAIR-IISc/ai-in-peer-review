- Paper proposes modeling projection noise for low-resource POS tagging via a learnable K×K confusion layer mapping clean tag distributions to projected tags, integrated into a BiLSTM and trained on gold + projected data with gold pretraining.
- Contribution: a simple, principled noise-aware training objective using a learnable confusion layer that decides when to trust projected tags.
- Contribution: empirical gains across 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages versus prior work and naïve projection, plus qualitative analyses of learned noise matrices.
- Contribution: a practical two-stage training recipe (gold pretraining then joint training) for combining tiny gold data with large noisy projections.
- Strength: method is minimal, interpretable, easily added to standard BiLSTMs, and directly addresses projection label noise and bias.
- Strength: consistent empirical improvements over prior SOTA (Duong et al., 2014) and demonstration that naïvely mixing projected and gold data can hurt.
- Strength: learned noise matrices are interpretable and capture plausible language-specific divergences and alignment issues.
- Strength: evaluation includes both simulated low-resource experiments and truly low-resource settings with real parallel data.
- Strength: approach generalizes naturally to other distant supervision settings and mismatched tagsets.
- Weakness: limited novelty—approach is essentially a linear confusion/noise layer, similar to prior noisy-label methods; lacks comparison to other noise-robust strategies (EM, loss correction, co-teaching).
- Weakness: missing/underpowered baselines—no BiLSTM-CRF comparison, unclear handling of character/subword features, and no neural multi-task baseline predicting both gold and projected tags.
- Weakness: no statistical significance testing or multiple runs reported; small improvements may be within LSTM variance.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—missing details about A initialization/regularization, early stopping, epochs, seeds, dropout, and whether soft labels are used; parallel data/code not clearly referenced.
- Weakness: projection-labeling heuristics may bias results—only 1-to-1 alignments used, ad hoc handling of NULL/multi-alignments, and inconsistent annotated training sizes between simulated and real experiments.
- Question: how is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized; were identity priors or penalties like ||A−I|| tried and how sensitive are results?
- Question: are soft labels used for projected distributions (e.g., NULL/multi-alignments) and what loss is used with them?
- Question: how sensitive is performance to amount/quality of parallel data—can accuracy vs. number of parallel sentences be shown?
- Question: do gains persist with BiLSTM-CRF decoders and/or character-level embeddings; any preliminary results?
- Question: request for training details—epochs, early stopping, dropout, regularization, and random seed settings.
- Question: was the mismatched tagset (rectangular A) actually evaluated or only proposed as a theoretical possibility?
- Additional request: report statistical significance or multiple runs with mean±std.
- Additional request: consider releasing code and preprocessed projection data to facilitate reproduction.
- Minor: clean up references (years/venues, duplicates) and fix typos.
