- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger for low-resource languages that learns a label-noise linear transformation to map clean tags to noisy projected tags, training first on gold data then jointly with projected data.
- Contribution 1: Learns a simple, principled linear noise/transition matrix in tag space to model projection noise and enable end-to-end training.
- Contribution 2: Provides empirical evidence of consistent improvements over prior projection methods across 10 languages and interpretable analyses of learned noise matrices.
- Contribution 3: Offers a practical two-stage training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) to stabilize learning with noisy distant supervision.
- Strength: Conceptually clear, easy-to-implement approach with consistent empirical gains, including on real low-resource languages.
- Strength: Interpretable A matrices reveal systematic projection error patterns rather than overfitting.
- Strength: Two-stage training strategy plausibly mitigates instability from noisy labels and is practically useful.
- Strength: Lightweight and general — adds only K×K parameters and can integrate with other sequence models and mismatched tagsets.
- Strength: Evaluation is fair and relevant, using 1k-token low-resource simulations and real-language datasets with comparisons to prior projection and resource-light baselines.
- Weakness: Limited novelty — approach closely mirrors established label-noise/confusion-matrix methods and should be better positioned relative to that literature.
- Weakness: Baselines are weak (no BiLSTM-CRF, char/subword features, or pretrained embeddings), so gains may not hold against stronger supervised models.
- Weakness: Unclear/ inconsistent description of projection labeling protocol (one-to-one alignments vs. sentence-level distributions).
- Weakness: Missing key ablations/sensitivity analyses (varying gold data size, parallel data size/quality, pretraining vs. joint training, constraints/regularization and initialization of A).
- Weakness: Reproducibility details lacking (GIZA++ settings, alignment symmetrization, parallel data selection, hyperparameters, A initialization/regularization, Kinyarwanda data access); code/data release recommended.
- Question: Clarify the projection protocol — do you retain only one-to-one alignments, and how are unaligned/multi-aligned tokens handled in experiments?
- Question: How is A initialized and regularized (identity/zeros/random, L2 or diagonal priors) to avoid identifiability or scaling issues?
- Question: How sensitive are results to the amount of gold supervision (e.g., 500/1k/2k/5k tokens), especially in real languages?
- Question: Have you tried stronger baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, character features); does the noise layer still improve those models?
- Question: How does parallel data size/quality and alignment filtering affect performance and the learned A matrices for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda?
- Additional comment: Better position the work relative to noisy-label literature and cite relevant prior methods (Dawid–Skene style, neural noise layers).
- Additional comment: Release code and preprocessing scripts to improve transparency and reproducibility.
- Additional comment: Evaluate tagset-mismatch scenarios to further demonstrate generality.
