Summary and contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by leveraging noisy cross-lingual projection alongside a small amount of gold annotations. The core idea is to explicitly model the mapping between clean tags and noisy projected tags using a learned “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger. Training proceeds by pretraining on gold data and then joint training on gold and projected data. Experiments on eight simulated languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent improvements over prior work.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, explicit, and effective noise model for projected labels: a learnable K×K confusion matrix that maps clean tag distributions to projected tag distributions, trained jointly with a BiLSTM tagger. This directly tackles the well-known label noise in cross-lingual projection.
2) Empirical validation on both simulated and real low-resource settings with consistent gains, including state-of-the-art results on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), and insightful analyses of learned noise matrices that illustrate language-specific projection biases.
3) A practical training protocol (gold pretraining + joint training) demonstrating that naïvely mixing noisy projected data can hurt, while explicit noise modeling enables consistent improvements.

Strengths
- Clear performance improvements where it matters: The method improves substantially on true low-resource languages (Malagasy +1.0 over Duong et al. and +5.6 over BiLSTM Projected; Kinyarwanda +4.2 over Duong et al. and +20.6 over BiLSTM Projected). These are practically meaningful improvements under realistic data constraints. On the eight simulated languages, the approach is consistently at or above the state of the art (average 91.7 vs. 91.3), showing robustness across typologically diverse settings.
- Conceptually clean, low-parameter addition with interpretability: The K×K noise layer is easy to implement, cheap to train, and integrates seamlessly with standard sequence taggers. Visualizations of the learned A matrices provide interpretable insights (e.g., pronouns rarely confused with nouns in certain languages; PRT vs. X confusion in Swedish). This makes the approach attractive for practitioners and for diagnosing projection issues.
- Strong experimental framing and baselines: The paper demonstrates why direct use of projected data can be harmful and shows that joint training without noise modeling can underperform. Comparisons include prior SOTA (Duong et al.), supervised MaxEnt, and several BiLSTM baselines, which collectively support the central claim: explicit noise modeling helps.
- Practical relevance and portability: The method relies on widely available resources (parallel text + English tagger), not closely related languages or large lexicons. The real-world evaluations (Malagasy/Kinyarwanda) use publicly discussed datasets and realistic amounts of parallel data, underscoring applicability.

Weaknesses
- Modest methodological novelty relative to broader literature on learning with noisy labels: Modeling label noise with a learnable confusion matrix is a known strategy in ML and distant supervision. The main novelty here is its application to cross-lingual POS projection within a BiLSTM tagger. The paper would benefit from acknowledging and situating within prior noisy label modeling work (e.g., class-conditional noise models, label transition layers), and clarifying what is new beyond the application and thorough evaluation.
- Inconsistencies/ambiguity in the projection setup: Section 3.1 describes assigning a sentence-level tag distribution for multi-aligned or unaligned tokens, but Section 4.1.2 states that only one-to-one alignments are retained, which would preclude many-to-one/one-to-many and thus remove those cases. It is unclear what supervision ỹ is actually used in training (hard vs. soft labels) and for which tokens. This affects the interpretation of the noise model and the generality of the approach.
- Limited ablations and stronger baselines: There is no comparison to (i) simply down-weighting projected examples, (ii) BiLSTM-CRF with character embeddings (a strong POS baseline), or (iii) alternative/no-parameter noise handling (e.g., calibration, temperature scaling) or more expressive noise models (conditioning A on alignment features). The gains on Europarl are small; without statistical tests and stronger baselines it is hard to assess their robustness.
- Global, context-independent noise matrix: Projection errors plausibly depend on alignment type (NULL/1:1/1:many), alignment score, and local context. A single global A cannot capture such structure. This might explain the limited gains on large clean parallel corpora. A small extension to condition A on alignment features or to separate A matrices per alignment type could be informative.
- Reproducibility/details: Training specifics are sparse: initialization and regularization of A, number of epochs, early stopping, variance across runs, and loss weighting between gold and projected datasets are not specified. Selection of “top 200k sentences” by IBM Model 3 is mentioned but details and thresholds are not fully described, especially for the low-resource settings.

Questions to authors
- Projection supervision: Do you actually use only one-to-one alignments in training? If so, how are ỹ constructed for tokens without 1–1 alignments? If not, how are soft labels formed for multi/unaligned tokens, and are they used in the loss?
- Noise layer details: How is A initialized (identity, random, diagonal + small noise)? Any regularization (e.g., L2, diagonal bias) to encourage near-identity mappings? Did you observe sensitivity to initialization?
- Loss weighting and sampling: What is the relative weight of the projected-data loss vs. gold loss? Do you balance minibatches or subsample projected data? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Significance and variance: Are the improvements in Table 1 statistically significant? How many runs with different seeds were averaged? Please report mean±std or paired tests.
- Stronger tagger baselines: Did you try a BiLSTM-CRF and/or character-level embeddings? Does the noise layer still help in that setting?
- Conditioning on alignment quality: Did you experiment with conditioning A or loss weights on alignment scores or NULL indicators? If so, what was the effect?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-motivated paper with a simple and effective idea that yields consistent gains, especially in truly low-resource settings. Novelty is modest but the contribution is practical and relevant, and the analyses are insightful. Addressing the projection setup ambiguity, adding ablations/stronger baselines, and clarifying training details would strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
