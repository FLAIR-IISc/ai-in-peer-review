Summary
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small gold-annotated corpus with noisy cross-lingual projections from English. The key idea is to add an output “noise layer” over a BiLSTM tagger: the clean tag distribution o is transformed by a K×K matrix A and a softmax to match projected labels, while the model is trained jointly on gold labels (directly from o) and projected labels (from softmax(Ao)). Training is staged: pretrain on gold, then jointly train with gold+projected. Experiments on 8 simulated Europarl languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show small but consistent improvements over a strong prior method (Duong et al., 2014a), with best reported accuracies for the two real languages. Visualizations of A illustrate language-specific confusion patterns.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, explicit label-noise model (linear confusion layer) for cross-lingual projection integrated into a neural tagger, trained end-to-end with both clean and projected supervision.
2) Empirical evaluation across 10 languages showing consistent gains over prior work and visual analysis of learned confusion matrices.
3) Practical training recipe (gold pretraining then joint training) for stabilizing learning with noisy supervision.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The linear noise layer is an intuitive and principled way to model projection noise; it adds very few parameters and integrates cleanly with the BiLSTM. The objective combines clean and noisy supervision in a way that is easy to optimize.
- Consistent empirical gains: On 8 simulated languages, the method slightly but consistently outperforms Duong et al. (average +0.4% absolute). On real low-resource data, it improves over both Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge, reaching 86.3 (Malagasy) and 82.5 (Kinyarwanda).
- Informative analysis: The learned A matrices are interpretable and align with linguistic expectations (e.g., determiners vs nouns), offering diagnostic insight into projection errors and cross-lingual divergences.
- Sensible use of limited supervision: The two-stage training and equal-weighted loss terms (averaged per dataset) are reasonable choices to prevent the large projected set from swamping the small gold set.
- Relevance: The approach is general and potentially applicable to other distant supervision scenarios and tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to known label-noise modeling: Modeling annotation noise via a learned transition/confusion matrix after a classifier is established in the broader ML literature; several NLP works have also considered projection noise heuristics/constraints. The contribution feels incremental (apply a known noise-channel idea to cross-lingual POS with a BiLSTM).
- Inconsistent and underspecified projection protocol: Section 3.1 assigns sentence-level tag distributions to NULL or many-to-one alignments, yet Section 4.1.2 states only 1–1 alignments are retained. These conflict. If many-to-one/NULL cases are discarded (1–1 only), when are sentence-level distributions used? If not discarded, how are conflicts handled? This ambiguity directly affects how ỹ is formed and the loss is computed.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: No ablation on (i) the noise layer vs. simple reweighting of projected loss, (ii) initialization of A (e.g., identity/zeros/confusion from a dev set), (iii) single-stage vs two-stage training, (iv) alternative forms (A before/after softmax, temperature scaling), (v) different mixing weights between gold and projected losses. Improvements over Duong et al. are small; without ablations, it’s hard to attribute gains to the noise layer rather than implementation choices.
- Baselines are weak for modern sequence tagging: A BiLSTM-CRF and/or character-level features are standard for POS, especially for morphologically rich, low-resource settings. The paper uses a plain BiLSTM with 128-d embeddings/hidden and no char model or CRF, and does not compare to stronger neural baselines. This undermines claims of state-of-the-art, especially when gains are modest.
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps: No statistical significance, no multiple runs/seeds, no variance. Hyperparameters are sparse (LR=1.0, SGD, but no epochs, batch size, dropout, clipping, optimizer variants). No details on A initialization, early stopping, selection criteria. Not clear whether code/data will be released. Domain mismatch (Europarl vs CoNLL-X) is not discussed, nor is the universal tagset mapping procedure for each language.
- Technical/formal issues:
  - The mapping softmax(Ao) vs. Equation (2) (∑i ai,j ot,i) implies a transpose; please fix and clarify dimensions/orientation.
  - ỹ is described as “binary” but can be a distribution; clarify (it seems used as a soft label in the loss).
  - “Multinomial” for a categorical draw is odd; better “Categorical.”
  - Loss weights: adding two averaged losses implicitly gives equal dataset weight; justify or explore alternatives.
- Projection and alignment details are thin: “Highest sentence alignment scores” selection is underspecified; IBM Model 3 details (thresholds? bidirectional symmetrization?) are missing. Tokenization, casing, OOV handling, and handling of punctuation/NUM are not described.
- Numerous formatting and citation issues: Many malformed references (e.g., “Ammar et al.,? 2016”; stray \Q \E markers), typos (“wildly” vs “widely”), broken footnotes, figure text bleeding into body (“raha ny marina ... misaotra”), and incomplete bibliographic entries. This detracts from clarity and polish.

Questions to Authors
1) Precisely define how projected labels ỹ are constructed. Do you keep only 1–1 alignments or also use many-to-one/NULL with sentence-level distributions? If both appear in the pipeline, when and how?
2) How is A initialized for the joint phase? Identity, zeros, or from an empirical confusion matrix? Did you try initializing A to identity and freezing/unfreezing schedules?
3) How are batches mixed between gold and projected data? Are the two losses weighted equally by construction (as per Equation) or tuned? Did you try different weights and what is the sensitivity?
4) Did you run multiple seeds and test for statistical significance? What is the variance across runs, particularly on the 0.2–0.7% gains in Table 1?
5) Ablations: performance without the two-stage training; with a temperature on the projected softmax; with A applied before softmax (i.e., linear logits transform) vs after.
6) Why not a BiLSTM-CRF and/or character features? Do the same conclusions hold with stronger baselines?
7) For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda: details on tokenization, tagset mapping to Universal POS, and coverage stats of projected tokens after filtering. How much of each corpus received projected labels?
8) Can you release code, processed data, and scripts to fully reproduce Tables 1–2?
9) Could you clarify the exact matrix orientation in Equation (2) vs “softmax(Ao)” and ensure consistency throughout?
