Summary and contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold data with noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is to insert a learnable “noise layer” (a K×K linear transformation over the BiLSTM output probabilities) to model the mapping from clean tags to noisy projected tags, trained jointly with gold and projected data in a two-stage procedure (pretrain on gold, then joint).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple label-noise modeling layer integrated into a BiLSTM tagger to learn when/how to trust projected POS labels, trained end-to-end on gold and projected data.
2) An empirical study over 8 “simulated low-resource” European languages and 2 truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over the prior projection-based method of Duong et al. (2014a) and several baselines.
3) Qualitative analysis of learned confusion matrices, offering some interpretability of projection noise patterns.

Strengths
- Clear, simple mechanism to handle noisy distant supervision. Modeling a confusion/noise matrix on top of the classifier output is principled and aligns with established approaches to label noise. The two-stage training (gold pretraining, then joint) is sensible and avoids obvious pitfalls of directly optimizing on noisy labels.

- Consistent improvements across languages, including low-resource settings. The method generally equals or outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) on 8 simulated languages and yields new SOTA for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda in the reported setup. The gains, while modest in some cases, are systematic.

- Interpretability of the noise layer. Visualizations of A show plausible patterns (e.g., determiners/NUM relationships, confusion in Swedish PRT/X), lending credibility that the model is indeed learning projection noise rather than overfitting idiosyncratically.

- Pragmatic setup with standard resources. Using GIZA++, universal tagset mapping, and a basic BiLSTM makes replication plausible, and the approach should be adaptable to other distant supervision contexts.

- Real low-resource evaluation. Including Malagasy and Kinyarwanda (with much smaller parallel corpora) is important and more realistic than only European simulations; the method still helps, especially where projection noise is higher.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing label-noise modeling. The core technique—a learnable confusion transformation on outputs—has been explored in the label-noise literature and in various NLP projection and domain adaptation settings (e.g., learning class-conditional noise matrices). The paper claims to be first to “explicitly” model projection noise as a layer, but this feels incremental; the contribution is more in applying a known idea in this context than in a fundamentally new modeling approach.

- Missing strong neural baselines and ablations. The baselines lack a BiLSTM-CRF, character/subword features, or common regularization (dropout), which are known to matter for POS, especially with morphologically rich languages. No ablations on: initialization/regularization of A, weighting between gold and projected losses, effect of the two-stage schedule, or replacing the noise layer by simpler alternatives (e.g., temperature scaling, class priors, instance weighting/confidence filtering). It is therefore hard to attribute gains specifically to the proposed layer vs. other training choices.

- Projection and alignment choices are simplistic and may bias results. Restricting to 1-1 alignments discards many tokens; for multi- or unaligned tokens, assigning the sentence-level tag frequency distribution is crude. More principled alternatives (marginalizing over alignment posteriors, using alignment confidences, modeling null/multi-alignments explicitly) are not compared. It is unclear how much the proposed layer compensates vs. how much is due to aggressive filtering and heuristics.

- Evaluation design concerns. The simulated setting uses 200k parallel sentences (high-resource projection) plus 1k gold tokens; this may not reflect many truly low-resource scenarios. For the real languages, the improvements over prior work are not accompanied by significance testing. There is no sensitivity analysis to the size/quality of parallel data, nor to using all alignments vs. 1-1. Reported hyperparameters are minimal (no dropout/early stopping details), and code/data availability is not stated.

- Claims on tagset mismatch not validated. The method mentions support for rectangular A (mismatched tagsets) but all experiments use universal tagsets. Without evidence, this remains a claim rather than a demonstrated capability.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Did you try identity initialization with small noise vs. random? Any constraints (e.g., row-stochastic, sparsity) or priors to prevent pathological solutions?

- How is the joint loss weighted? You average each term by corpus size and sum. Did you try varying the relative weight between gold and projected terms (α)? Sensitivity results would help.

- What is the impact of the two-stage schedule? If you train from scratch jointly, how much does performance drop? If you freeze A or freeze the base tagger during joint training, what happens?

- Can you provide a BiLSTM-CRF and/or char-subword baseline, and show results with standard regularization (dropout) and early stopping? This would strengthen the empirical case.

- For multi- or unaligned tokens, why use sentence-level tag frequency rather than English token-level marginal distributions or alignment posteriors? Did you try leveraging alignment confidence to weight projected instances?

- Are the improvements statistically significant on the test sets, especially in Table 1 where margins over Duong et al. are small for several languages?

- Can you release code and exact data splits to ensure reproducibility?
