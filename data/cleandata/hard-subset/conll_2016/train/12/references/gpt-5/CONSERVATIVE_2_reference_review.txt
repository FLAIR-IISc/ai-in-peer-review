Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an “output noising” layer: a learned K×K linear transformation A applied to the model’s predicted tag distribution before a softmax, intended to map clean tag probabilities to the projected/noisy label distribution. Training proceeds in two stages: (i) pretrain on a small gold set; (ii) jointly train on gold and projected data, with projected labels supervising the noised output and gold labels supervising the clean output.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A simple discriminative noise channel on top of a BiLSTM for learning from projected labels together with a small gold set, with joint training.
2) A broad empirical evaluation on eight simulated Europarl languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing improvements over previous projection-based methods in this setup.

Strengths
1) Clear, practical objective: The paper targets a well-motivated and important setting (POS tagging with very limited annotation plus projections). It reuses a standard BiLSTM backbone and augments it minimally, making the idea easy to implement and potentially reusable.

2) Empirical gains across multiple languages: On the eight simulated languages, the proposed method slightly but consistently outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) on average (91.7 vs 91.3). On the two low-resource languages, it reports 86.3 on Malagasy and 82.5 on Kinyarwanda, with a notable improvement on Kinyarwanda over Duong et al. (78.3). While absolute gains are modest in the simulated setting, they are consistent.

3) Sensible training procedure: Pretraining on gold labels before joint training with projection noise is a reasonable, established practice to avoid degenerate solutions when learning with noisy labels.

4) Diagnostics for the noise layer: Visualizations of the learned A matrices are helpful and provide some qualitative insight into the types of confusion the model captures (e.g., diagonal dominance with some interpretable off-diagonal mass).

Weaknesses
1) Questionable probabilistic formulation and lack of constraints on A: A is treated as an unconstrained weight matrix mapping o to logits before softmax. This undermines the interpretation of A as a confusion/noise matrix (values can be negative; rows/columns need not be stochastic), and mixes representational capacity between the base classifier and the noise layer. Without constraints (e.g., row-stochastic transition matrix) or regularization, identifiability is problematic. The paper neither justifies this choice nor compares against a constrained/noise-transition alternative.

2) Fairness and clarity of the real low-resource comparisons: For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, the paper uses training sets with ~5k gold tokens (from Garrette & Baldridge, 2013), whereas earlier parts of the paper and related work (including Duong et al., 2014a) emphasize the 1k-token setting. It is unclear whether the reported Duong et al. baselines in Table 2 used the same data size/split. If not, the comparison is not apples-to-apples. This is a major concern since the strongest claimed gains are on Kinyarwanda.

3) Ambiguity/inconsistency in projection data handling: Section 3.1 proposes assigning sentence-level tag-frequency distributions to multiply aligned or NULL-aligned tokens; Section 4.1.2 then states that only 1-to-1 alignments are retained, which would eliminate many-to-one/one-to-many cases. It is unclear which labeling scheme is actually used in the experiments, and to what extent “distributional” supervision is used. An ablation here is essential.

4) Missing baselines and ablations: 
- No comparison to a fixed confusion matrix estimated from English→target projected label pairs (e.g., from a small dev set), or to a constrained/stochastic noise transition matrix learned jointly.
- No sensitivity study on weighting between gold and projected losses; the current normalization equalizes the average loss per set, regardless of data volume, which is not obviously optimal.
- No ablation of the two-stage training (from-scratch joint training vs pretraining), or initialization of A (e.g., identity vs learned).
- No report of variance across runs or statistical significance.

5) Limited modeling and relatively weak baselines for POS: The BiLSTM uses only word embeddings (no character/subword or CRF layer). For morphologically rich or truly low-resource settings, character features are standard and strong. It is unclear whether the gains survive against stronger, established POS taggers (BiLSTM-CRF with char CNN/LSTM). This weakens the empirical rigor.

6) External resource and preprocessing details are under-specified: The choice to filter by sentence alignment scores (Yarowsky et al.) is mentioned, but not ablated; the impact of keeping only 1-1 alignments vs broader coverage is not quantified. There are few details on tokenization, alignment parameters, tagger versions, early stopping, regularization, or hyperparameter selection, and no significance testing.

Questions to Authors
1) For Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, what are the exact sizes/splits of gold training/dev/test used for all systems in Table 2? Did Duong et al. (2014a) use the same training data and splits? If not, please re-run baselines with identical data or clarify the comparison.

2) Why is A unconstrained? Did you try a row-stochastic transition matrix (e.g., A = log T with T≥0, rows sum to 1) to better match a noise-channel interpretation? How do results compare?

3) Which projection labeling strategy was used in practice: only 1-to-1 alignments (discarding others) or inclusion of many-to-one/NULL using sentence-level tag-frequency distributions? Please provide an ablation showing the impact of these choices.

4) How sensitive are results to the relative weighting of gold vs projected losses? Did you try tuning a scalar to interpolate between the two terms?

5) Did you evaluate stronger tagger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character features)? If not, can you provide results or justification?

6) What is the effect of pretraining vs training from scratch jointly? How is A initialized (identity, zero, random), and does that matter?

7) Can you report variance across multiple runs and statistical significance for key comparisons?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and presents a simple, potentially useful idea. However, the probabilistic soundness of the noise layer, inconsistencies in projection labeling, and possible unfairness in the low-resource comparisons need to be addressed. I would be more confident with clearer experimental controls, stronger baselines, and a principled treatment (or at least ablation) of the noise matrix.
