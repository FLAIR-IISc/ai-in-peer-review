Summary and main contributions
- The paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key idea is to explicitly model the noise in projected labels via a learnable linear “noise” transformation applied to the model’s outputs when training on projected data.
- Methodologically, the model is a BiLSTM tagger trained with two supervision channels: (i) clean gold tags with a standard cross-entropy loss, and (ii) projected tags with a cross-entropy loss after passing the predicted tag distribution through a learnable K×K matrix A that maps true tags to projected/noisy observations. Training proceeds by pretraining on gold, then joint training on both sources.
- Empirically, the approach outperforms strong baselines on eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), with state-of-the-art results and informative analyses of the learned noise matrices.

Strengths
- Clear, principled handling of projection noise. Modeling the projection process as a learnable confusion/noise layer is well-motivated and aligns with known issues in projection (alignment errors, syntactic divergence). It provides a coherent probabilistic story (latent true tag, observed noisy tag), and the end-to-end learning integrates naturally with neural sequence tagging.
- Consistent empirical gains. The method improves over Duong et al. (2014a) in average accuracy on eight Europarl languages (91.7 vs 91.3) and more substantially on Kinyarwanda (+4.2 points) and Malagasy (+1.0). The baselines include competitive neural (BiLSTM) and non-neural (MaxEnt) models, and naive uses of projected data perform worse, underscoring the value of noise modeling.
- Interpretability and analysis. Visualizations of the learned A matrices show plausible patterns (strong diagonals, language-specific confusions such as NUM/DET in Italian, PRON/NOUN separation), offering insight into projection error structure and language divergence. This is a nice ancillary contribution.
- Simple and general. The approach is minimalistic (a single linear layer with few parameters) and should transfer to other tasks/settings with noisy supervision (e.g., crowd labels, distant supervision for NER/RE), without heavy task-specific engineering.
- Sensible training protocol. Pretraining on gold followed by joint training is practical and addresses optimization pitfalls given label noise.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to label-noise modeling literature. The learned confusion/noise matrix is closely related to established approaches for learning with noisy labels (e.g., EM/Dawid–Skene-style confusion matrices; end-to-end models with learnable noise transitions in vision/NLP). The contribution is largely in applying this idea to cross-lingual projection with a BiLSTM; the paper could better position itself against this body of work and clarify what is novel beyond the application context and empirical validation.
- Under-specified and unconstrained noise layer. A is not constrained to be a stochastic matrix nor regularized to favor identity; identifiability issues between A and the base classifier are possible. The method relies on pretraining to anchor the solution, but the paper lacks ablations on: (i) A initialization (identity vs random), (ii) regularization (e.g., diagonal prior or L2), (iii) weighting between gold and projected losses. These details could materially affect robustness, especially with very small gold sets.
- Projection heuristics may be suboptimal. Using only 1-1 alignments discards substantial supervision; mapping many-to-one/unaligned tokens to a sentence-level tag distribution is a very coarse heuristic that can inject unstructured noise. Prior work leverages posterior alignment confidences or agreement-based constraints. It would strengthen the claims to compare with stronger projection pipelines (beyond sentence filtering) or to incorporate alignment uncertainty directly.
- Baseline coverage and fairness. While Duong et al. (2014a) is an appropriate baseline, important alternatives are missing: graph-based label propagation (Das & Petrov, 2011), posterior regularization/agreement, or multi-source projection. Also, the BiLSTM baseline lacks standard enhancements (e.g., character/subword features, CRF layer), which might change the relative gains. Statistical significance and variance are not reported.
- Low-resource realism and data dependence. For “real” low-resource settings, the gold training sets are ~5k tokens (not 1k), and the amount/quality of parallel data varies drastically (Malagasy 100k sents vs Kinyarwanda 11k). The paper suggests more noise for Kinyarwanda via A but does not disentangle the effect of parallel corpus size/quality from the model’s benefits. A sensitivity analysis over (gold, projected) data sizes would improve the practical guidance.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized? Did you try identity initialization or a diagonal-biased prior/regularizer? Any constraints (e.g., row-stochastic) tried, and with what effect?
- How do you balance losses from gold vs projected data? The current objective normalizes by token counts; did you tune a weighting coefficient? Does performance change if projected data dominates?
- Ablations: What happens without gold pretraining? How sensitive is the method to the amount of gold data (e.g., 500 vs 1k vs 5k tokens)?
- Did you evaluate the rectangular A case (mismatched tagsets) in practice? If not, what prevents this, and do you foresee stability issues?
- What proportion of tokens receive projected labels under 1-1 alignment filtering, and what is the accuracy of the English tagger + mapping? Any evidence that handling many-to-one alignments more carefully improves results?
- Are the improvements statistically significant across runs? Please report mean/std over multiple random seeds and/or significance tests.

Additional comments
- Please clarify dev set usage and early stopping; report key optimization hyperparameters (batch size, epochs, dropout).
- Consider reporting performance with character features; low-resource POS often benefits, and it may interact with the utility of projection.
- Releasing code and alignments/projection scripts would enhance reproducibility.
