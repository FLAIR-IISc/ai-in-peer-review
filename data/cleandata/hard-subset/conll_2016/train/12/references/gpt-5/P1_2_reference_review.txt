Summary
This paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by combining a small amount of gold annotations with noisy projected tags from parallel corpora. The core idea is to explicitly model the noise in projected tags via a learnable confusion transformation applied to the outputs of a BiLSTM tagger. Training proceeds in two stages: pretraining on gold, then joint training on gold and projected data with the noise layer. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work and several baselines; the approach also yields interpretable confusion matrices characterizing projection noise.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective label-noise modeling layer for cross-lingual projection that learns when to trust projected tags. The learnable K×K matrix applied to model outputs is an explicit, end-to-end way to absorb systematic projection errors.
2) Empirical evidence that this noise-aware training improves POS tagging in low-resource settings, with consistent gains over strong baselines on 8 simulated and 2 truly low-resource languages.
3) Qualitative analysis of the learned confusion matrices, offering interpretable insights into projection errors and cross-lingual divergences.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of projection noise: Instead of heuristically filtering or implicitly handling noise, the paper introduces an explicit, differentiable confusion transformation bridging clean and projected labels. This targets a known pain point in projection (alignment errors, structural divergences) and leverages rich projection data without overwhelming the model.
- Consistent improvements across diverse settings: On 8 Europarl languages, the proposed method slightly but consistently outperforms the prior state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a), and shows larger gains on real low-resource languages (Malagasy: 86.3% vs 85.3%; Kinyarwanda: 82.5% vs 78.3%). These results support the practicality of the approach where projection is noisier.
- Strong, relevant baselines and ablative comparisons: The paper contrasts supervised-only, projected-only, and naïve union training using the same BiLSTM, demonstrating the pitfalls of using projected labels directly and the benefits of noise modeling. This isolates the contribution of the noise layer beyond just having a neural tagger.
- Interpretability: Visualizations of the learned A matrices provide intuitive diagnostics (e.g., mapping projected determiners to nouns where determiners are less grammaticalized; confusion between PRT/X), which is valuable for error analysis and for understanding cross-lingual phenomena.
- Simplicity and generality: The noise layer is model-agnostic and could be used with other architectures or tasks involving noisy supervision (e.g., distant supervision, crowdsourcing). The training recipe (gold pretrain + joint fine-tuning) is straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Modest gains on simulated languages and lack of significance testing: Improvements over Duong et al. (2014a) on Europarl are small (average 91.7 vs 91.3). Statistical significance is not reported. While gains are more substantial on real low-resource languages, establishing significance and variance would strengthen the claims.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses: Key design choices are not probed. Examples: initialization and regularization of A (identity vs zero; L2 or temperature constraints), effect of varying the weighting between gold and projected losses (the objective normalizes by set size and sums; is that optimal?), and necessity of the two-stage training (no comparison to training from scratch or curriculum variants).
- Projection pipeline details and consistency: There is some tension between Sections 3.1 and 4.1.2 regarding alignment usage. The method description assigns tag distributions to tokens aligned to multiple or NULL words, but the experiments state only one-to-one alignments are retained. Clarifying whether distributional labels for unaligned tokens are used in practice (and their effect) is important, as these labels could be extremely noisy.
- Fairness and scope of baselines: The strongest prior (Duong et al., 2014a) is a MaxEnt model; a more apples-to-apples comparison would include a BiLSTM variant of their auxiliary-output approach or other modern sequence tagging baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, char/subword features) trained with projected labels but without the noise layer. This would isolate the benefit of the noise layer over other reasonable neural strategies.
- Reproducibility and resource dependence: Some resources (e.g., Kinyarwanda parallel data from ARL MURI) are not public, and hyperparameter/search details are sparse (e.g., batch size, early stopping, regularization, number of epochs, selection of 200k Europarl sentences threshold). Without released code, replicability may be limited.

Questions to Authors
- How is the A matrix initialized and regularized? Did you try identity initialization or constraints (e.g., diagonal dominance, temperature scaling) to encourage stability? Any evidence that A avoids degenerate solutions (e.g., collapsing probability mass)?
- How is the joint loss weighted in practice? Since each term is averaged over its dataset size, the two datasets are effectively equally weighted regardless of size. Did you try tuning a scalar to up/down-weight projected data?
- Do you actually use distributional labels for tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments, given that later you say only one-to-one alignments are retained? If yes, what proportion of supervision comes from such distributional labels, and how important are they?
- Is two-stage training necessary? What happens if you train end-to-end from scratch, or use a curriculum that gradually increases the projected-data weight?
- How sensitive are results to the amount/quality of parallel data (e.g., subsampling Malagasy parallel sentences) and to the amount of gold data (e.g., 200/500/1000 tokens)?
- Did you test mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), as mentioned? If not, can you comment on expected behavior?

Overall, this submission introduces a simple, principled mechanism to learn when to trust projected supervision, with consistent empirical benefits and good interpretability. Despite limited ablations and small gains on simulated data, the approach is practical and likely to transfer to other noisy-supervision scenarios.
