- Proposes a neural POS tagger for low-resource languages that jointly uses small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projected data via a learnable noise layer.  
- Core innovation: a noise layer (matrix A) that models transformation from noisy projected tags to true tags.  
- Claims: (1) study and model noise patterns in projected data, (2) integrate noise modeling into joint neural training, (3) show improved performance on simulated and real low-resource languages.  

- Novelty: explicit, learnable noise transformation is a different approach to handling distant supervision noise.  
- Evaluation: comprehensive experiments on 8 simulated European languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) showing consistent gains.  
- Analysis: visualizations of learned noise matrices provide interpretability about trusted tag mappings.  
- Experimental design: separates simulated and real low-resource setups for robust validation.  

- Strength: strong theoretical motivation for learning the noise transformation instead of cleaning or ignoring noisy projections.  
- Strength: consistent and meaningful accuracy improvements on real low-resource languages (e.g., Malagasy 86.3% vs 85.3%, Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs 78.3%).  
- Strength: clear visualization and analysis of which projected tag mappings are trusted by the model.  
- Strength: well-structured experiments covering multiple languages and settings.  

- Weakness: limited novelty at the architecture level (BiLSTM + extra layer) and insufficient contrast with recent neural transfer or attention-based methods.  
- Weakness: lacks ablation studies isolating the noise layer’s contribution versus the BiLSTM backbone.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of failure cases or when the noise layer does not help.  
- Weakness: real-language parallel corpora are small (100k and 11k sentences) and the effect of parallel data size is not analyzed.  

- Question: How does the noise layer compare or combine with attention mechanisms for handling noisy data?  
- Question: Why use a two-stage training (pretrain on annotated data then joint training) and what if trained end-to-end from scratch?  
- Question: Can you provide ablation comparing full model to standard BiLSTM trained on the same data to quantify the noise layer’s effect?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to parallel corpus size and what is the minimum parallel data needed?  

- Overall: Strong, well-motivated contribution with solid empirical results and insightful analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing novelty comparison and ablation studies.
