

# Review for CONLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected annotations through a noise transformation layer in a BiLSTM architecture. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) studying the noise of projected data in word alignments and describing it with an additional layer model, (2) integrating this model into a deep neural network for joint training on annotated and projected data, and (3) demonstrating superior performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty**: This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision in cross-lingual POS tagging. Unlike previous work that either ignored the noise in projected tags or attempted to clean them before training, the authors explicitly model the noise as a transformation matrix within the neural network. This "noise layer" is a bold conceptual contribution that directly addresses a fundamental limitation in cross-lingual projection methods. As the authors state, "we believe that our work is the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," and I find this claim well-supported by the related work review.

2. **Clear Demonstration of the Approach**: The paper effectively demonstrates how the noise matrix A learns language-specific patterns of noise (Figures 3 and 4). This provides valuable insight into *why* the approach works, showing that the model learns to trust certain tags more than others and how it handles specific noise patterns (e.g., NUM vs. DET confusion in Italian). This interpretability significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

3. **Consistent and Meaningful Improvements**: The approach consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods across both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The improvements are not marginal but meaningful (up to 1.6% absolute improvement on some languages), and the results are statistically significant. The paper's claim that "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods" is well-supported by the data.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The noise layer is a simple but effective modification to standard BiLSTM architectures (just adding a linear transformation layer), making it easy to implement and adopt in practice. This practicality increases the paper's impact and accessibility to the broader NLP community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Mechanisms**: While the paper shows the learned noise matrices, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* certain noise patterns exist. For example, why does Swedish have so many weak diagonal values (indicating high noise in tag projections)? Understanding these language-specific noise patterns could provide additional insights into cross-lingual transfer learning.

2. **Lack of Ablation Study on Noise Layer**: The paper compares their full model (BiLSTM+noise layer) to a BiLSTM trained on the union of annotated and projected data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj), but doesn't provide an ablation study showing the specific contribution of the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture. While the comparison is valid, a more detailed ablation would strengthen the argument for the noise layer's necessity.

3. **Small Parallel Corpora for Real Low-Resource Languages**: The parallel data for Malagasy (100k sentences) and Kinyarwanda (11k sentences) is relatively small, which might limit the generalizability of the results to even more extreme low-resource scenarios. The paper could have included a more challenging low-resource language with even less parallel data to demonstrate the approach's robustness.

4. **Comparison to Other Noise Modeling Approaches**: The paper doesn't compare to other noise modeling techniques that might be relevant, such as noise-robust loss functions or separate noise estimation models. While this isn't a fatal flaw, a brief comparison would help position the contribution more clearly within the broader literature on noisy label learning.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the noise matrix A is initialized with a random matrix during the two-stage training process. Could the authors elaborate on how they initialize A in the first stage, and whether they've experimented with different initialization strategies?

2. In Figure 4, the noise matrix for Kinyarwanda shows significantly more noise than for Malagasy. Could the authors explain why Kinyarwanda appears to have more noise in the projected tags, and whether this correlates with the quality or quantity of the parallel data?

3. The paper states that their approach is generalizable to other forms of noisy annotations. Could the authors provide a brief example of how this might work for a different NLP task with noisy supervision, such as named entity recognition with crowd-sourced annotations?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision in low-resource POS tagging. The concept of explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags as a transformation layer within a neural network is bold, well-motivated, and effectively validated through comprehensive experiments. While there are minor weaknesses in the analysis and comparison, these do not detract from the paper's core novelty and significance.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The approach is both conceptually interesting and practically valuable, with clear potential for broader application beyond POS tagging. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.
