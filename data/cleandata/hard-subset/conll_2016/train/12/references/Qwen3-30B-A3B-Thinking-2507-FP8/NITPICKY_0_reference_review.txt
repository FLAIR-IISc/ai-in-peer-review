

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly trains on gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected tags from cross-lingual projection. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that models the transformation between projected tags and gold tags, allowing the model to learn when to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their approach on 8 simulated languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), reporting state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural network architecture with an explicit noise layer to model the transformation between projected and gold tags.
2. A method for jointly training on both gold and projected data to better leverage noisy distant supervision.
3. Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource language datasets.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP: low-resource POS tagging, which is crucial for many NLP applications. The approach of explicitly modeling noise in projected data is conceptually sound and addresses a real limitation in existing methods.

2. The experimental results are generally strong, showing consistent improvements over baselines across multiple languages. The results on real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) are particularly compelling, with the authors achieving 86.3% and 82.5% accuracy respectively.

3. The noise transformation matrices (Figures 3-4) provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages, which is an important aspect of understanding the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses
1. **Extensive formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting issues that undermine professionalism and readability. These include inconsistent use of "et al." vs "et al\." (with backslash), inconsistent notation for variables (e.g., |T p| vs |T_p|), inconsistent use of commas as thousand separators (e.g., "1000" vs "1,000"), and misplaced footnote markers (e.g., "text.4" instead of "text (4)."). These issues appear throughout the paper, from the introduction to the references, suggesting a lack of proofreading.

2. **Technical inconsistencies in equations and notation**: In the noise layer equation (Equation 2), the sum should be over i (i.e., ∑_i) for clarity, but it's written as "∑ i" without the subscript. The training objective equation uses inconsistent notation (|T p| vs |T_p|), and the model description in Section 3.2 contains confusing phrasing about "adjusting tag probabilities from the supervised model."

3. **Inconsistent reference formatting**: The reference list contains numerous formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent use of parentheses around years, inconsistent "et al." formatting, and punctuation errors. For example, "Ammar et al\.,? 2016" should be "Ammar et al. (2016)" throughout. These issues make the references appear unprofessional.

4. **Minor grammatical and phrasing errors**: The paper contains several minor grammatical errors that affect readability, such as "a small 1000 token dataset" (should be "1,000"), "We set learning rate as 1.0" (should be "We set the learning rate to 1.0"), and inconsistent use of abbreviations (e.g., "Swedish (sv)" vs "sv" in tables).

5. **Lack of clarity in method description**: The paper states that the noise layer is "parameterized by a K × K matrix A" but doesn't clearly explain how this matrix is learned or initialized. The claim that "the matrix A parameterizes the final layer" is confusing since A is applied to the output of the BiLSTM, not to the layer itself.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the initialization procedure for matrix A? The paper mentions that direct training with random initialization is impractical, but doesn't specify how A is initialized during the two-stage training process.

2. The paper mentions that the noise layer supports mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide any examples or discussion of how this works in practice. Could you elaborate on this point?

3. The paper states that the noise transformation matrices show "strong diagonal" patterns, but in Figure 4 for Kinyarwanda, the diagonal appears quite weak. How does the model handle cases where the diagonal is weak?

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem in low-resource POS tagging and presents a conceptually sound approach with strong empirical results, the numerous minor formatting inconsistencies, technical notations, and grammatical errors throughout the paper significantly undermine its professionalism. As a perfectionist reviewer who meticulously examines every aspect of the paper, I find these issues to be substantial enough to recommend rejection, even though the core technical contributions appear valid.

The paper would benefit from thorough proofreading to address the consistent formatting issues, technical notation inconsistencies, and grammatical errors that appear throughout the manuscript. Without these corrections, the paper's presentation fails to meet the high standards expected at CONLL.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor flaws that collectively diminish the paper's credibility and professionalism.
