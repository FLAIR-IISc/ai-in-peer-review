

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly utilizes gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected annotations from cross-lingual projection. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that models the transformation between projected tags and gold tags using a K×K matrix A. The authors evaluate on eight simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural network architecture that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and gold tags through a learnable matrix A
2. An evaluation framework demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines on both simulated and real low-resource languages
3. A demonstration of how to effectively integrate noisy projected data with limited gold data for POS tagging

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant problem in NLP: low-resource POS tagging. The motivation is clear, and the approach has practical value for real-world applications where annotated data is scarce.

2. The evaluation on real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) is valuable and demonstrates the approach's practical utility, with improvements over state-of-the-art methods (86.3% vs 85.3% for Malagasy, 82.5% vs 78.3% for Kinyarwanda).

3. The noise layer concept is intuitive and fits naturally into the neural network framework. The transformation matrix A provides a clear mechanism for the model to learn when and how to trust projected tags.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and problematic formatting**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors that undermine credibility. References include backslashes (e.g., "Duong et al\.,? 2014a") and strange formatting like "\Q2013\E" in McDonald et al. (2013). The mathematical notation is messy (e.g., "W→ −→ h t" instead of proper formatting), and the paper contains grammatical errors like "wildly successful" instead of "widely successful." These are unacceptable in a high-quality NLP paper.

2. **Lack of clear novelty**: The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this is not convincingly demonstrated. The noise layer is simply a transformation matrix applied to the output, which is a common technique in neural networks for handling noisy labels. The paper fails to properly differentiate this approach from previous work on handling noisy data (e.g., methods for learning with noisy labels in machine learning).

3. **Insufficient justification for methodology**: The paper states "direct projection has several issues and results in very noisy and unreliable annotations" but fails to quantify the noise level or provide concrete evidence of how their approach specifically addresses the identified problems. The claim that their approach "explicitly models the transformation between tagsets" is vague - the matrix A is just a simple linear transformation, not a detailed model of the noise process.

4. **Flawed experimental design**: The use of European languages as "simulated low-resource languages" by taking only 1,000 tokens is problematic. These languages are not low-resource, and their linguistic properties may not generalize to truly low-resource languages. The paper doesn't justify why these specific languages were chosen or why they're representative.

5. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims improvements over baselines but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the results. The differences in accuracy (e.g., 91.7% vs 91.3% for Spanish) may not be statistically significant, which is a serious omission in a paper claiming to present a new method with superior results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "explicitly model the noise" but doesn't define what constitutes "noise" in their framework. Can you clarify how your approach differs from standard techniques for handling noisy labels in neural networks?

2. The noise layer is described as a K×K matrix A, but the paper doesn't explain why this specific form is sufficient. How would your approach handle cases where the projected tagset is different from the gold tagset (e.g., different number of tags)?

3. The paper uses European languages as a simulation of low-resource languages by taking only 1,000 tokens. Why are these languages appropriate for this simulation, and how do they compare to truly low-resource languages in terms of linguistic properties?

## Final Recommendation

This paper presents a useful approach for low-resource POS tagging, but the numerous formatting errors, lack of clear novelty, and methodological shortcomings outweigh its contributions. The paper's claims about explicitly modeling noise are overstated, and the experimental design has significant flaws. The formatting issues alone (backslashes in references, inconsistent citation styles) are unacceptable for a conference submission.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial issues with presentation and methodology that undermine the paper's credibility and contribution.
