

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy distant supervision from cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a novel neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix, rather than using the projected tags directly or applying heuristic cleaning methods. The model uses a BiLSTM with an additional "noise layer" that learns to map projected tags to the true tags. The approach is evaluated on 8 simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over previous methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **The noise transformation layer as a principled way to model the mapping between projected and true tags**: This is the most significant contribution, offering a novel approach to handling the inherent noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags.

2. **Comprehensive empirical validation on both simulated and real low-resource languages**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple evaluation settings, including both simulated low-resource scenarios and two real low-resource languages.

3. **Insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices**: The paper provides valuable visualizations of the noise matrices, revealing patterns of tag confusion and demonstrating how the model learns to "trust" certain projections while ignoring others.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to handling noisy projected data**: The authors' key insight is that the noise in projected tags is not random but follows systematic patterns that can be modeled. This is a significant advance over previous work that either used heuristics to clean the data or tried to implicitly capture the noise through joint feature sets. The noise layer provides a principled way to incorporate the projected data into the learning process, rather than treating it as a separate source of information.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple languages**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The improvements are consistent and meaningful - for example, 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy (vs. 85.3% for the previous state-of-the-art) and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda (vs. 78.3%). This demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

3. **Insightful analysis of the noise transformation**: The authors provide compelling visualizations of the learned noise matrices (Figure 3 and 4), which reveal patterns of tag confusion (e.g., NOUN vs. PRON in Spanish, PRT vs. X in Swedish) and show how the model learns to "trust" certain projections while ignoring others. This analysis goes beyond just reporting accuracy and provides valuable insights into how the model handles noise.

4. **Practical implementation**: The approach is relatively simple to implement and can be applied to other tasks beyond POS tagging that use distant supervision. The authors note this in their conclusion, suggesting the method could be useful for other noisy annotations like crowd-sourced labels.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of thorough ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive ablation study to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. While they compare BiLSTM Annotated, Projected, and Ann+Proj, they don't explicitly show how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the joint training of both datasets. A more detailed ablation would strengthen the claims about the noise layer's contribution.

2. **Limited comparison to other noise modeling approaches**: The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise," but doesn't compare with other noise-robust learning approaches that could be relevant. For example, there are methods like noise-robust loss functions (e.g., symmetric cross-entropy, co-teaching), label correction methods, or Bayesian approaches that could be compared against. This makes it difficult to assess how much of the improvement is due to the specific noise layer design versus general noise handling techniques.

3. **Theoretical justification for the noise layer is lacking**: The paper presents the noise layer as an effective heuristic but doesn't provide a strong theoretical foundation for why this particular form of noise modeling is appropriate for the problem. The formulation seems somewhat ad-hoc, and a more principled explanation would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages**: While the paper evaluates on two real low-resource languages, this is a relatively small number. Given the challenges of obtaining data for low-resource languages, this is understandable, but it limits the generalizability of the results. More languages would strengthen the claim that the approach is broadly applicable to low-resource POS tagging.

5. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the model fails or where the noise layer doesn't help as much. Understanding these failure cases would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on how the noise transformation matrix A is initialized? The paper mentions a two-stage training process (pretraining on gold data, then joint training), but doesn't explain how A is initialized. Does the initialization method affect the final results?

2. How does your model compare to other noise-robust learning approaches for handling noisy labels (e.g., symmetric cross-entropy, co-teaching, etc.)? Have you considered these methods as baselines?

3. The paper uses Giza++ with IBM model 3 for word alignments. How sensitive are the results to the alignment method? Would using a different alignment model (e.g., FastAlign) change the results significantly?

4. For the real low-resource languages, the parallel data for Kinyarwanda is much smaller than for Malagasy (11k sentences vs 100k sentences). The paper notes that Kinyarwanda has more noise, but could you elaborate on how the size of the parallel data affects the quality of the projected tags and the model's performance?

5. The paper uses the universal POS tagset from Petrov et al. (2011). How does this affect the results compared to using language-specific tagsets? Would the results be different if they used language-specific tagsets, which might be more appropriate for low-resource languages?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some weaknesses in the evaluation and analysis, the paper makes a significant step forward in how we can effectively use cross-lingual projection for low-resource POS tagging. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the ablation study and comparison to other noise modeling approaches.
