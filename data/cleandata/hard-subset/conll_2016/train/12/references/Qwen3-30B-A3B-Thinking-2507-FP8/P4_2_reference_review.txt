

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining small amounts of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data from English. The authors propose a neural network model with a novel "noise transformation" layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected (noisy) tags and true tags. The main contributions are:

1. **Explicit modeling of noise in projected tags**: The authors propose a learnable noise transformation matrix (A) that maps projected tags to the true tags, rather than using projected tags directly or implicitly modeling the relationship through features.

2. **Joint training framework**: The model is trained jointly on both gold-standard and projected data, with the noise layer enabling the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.

3. **Empirical validation on real low-resource languages**: The method demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art approaches on both simulated low-resource settings (using 8 European languages with small gold datasets) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem formulation and strong motivation**: The paper effectively identifies the core problem with current cross-lingual POS projection approaches (noise from word alignments and syntactic divergence) and demonstrates through preliminary experiments that direct use of projected tags leads to poor performance. This provides a strong justification for the proposed noise modeling approach.

2. **Novel and technically sound contribution**: The noise transformation layer (matrix A) is a clean, simple yet effective solution that is well-formulated mathematically. The approach differs significantly from prior work that either used projected tags directly or tried to implicitly model the relationship through features. The model can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation, making it practical and easy to implement.

3. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The results consistently show improvements over previous methods across both simulated and real low-resource languages. The performance gains are significant (e.g., 86.3% vs 85.3% on Malagasy, 82.5% vs 78.3% on Kinyarwanda) and consistent across languages. The analysis of the learned transformation matrices provides valuable insights into how the model learns to handle different types of noise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to related work**: While the authors claim their approach is the first to explicitly model noise, there are related techniques in the literature that achieve similar goals. For example, label noise modeling has been explored in various contexts (e.g., in neural machine translation for handling noisy inputs). The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these techniques to better establish the novelty of the proposed approach.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. A critical experiment would be to compare the performance of the model using projected data without the noise layer (treating projected tags as additional training examples) versus the proposed approach. This would help determine whether the improvement comes from the joint training framework or specifically from the noise modeling.

3. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases**: The paper shows strong average performance but does not analyze cases where the method fails. For example, the authors note that Swedish (sv) has "many weak values on the diagonal" in the noise matrix, but they don't explain why this occurs or how it affects performance. Understanding failure modes would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Simulated low-resource setting limitations**: While using European languages with small gold datasets is a common practice, these languages are not truly low-resource. The results might not generalize as well to languages with very different structures from English, which are often the most challenging low-resource cases. The paper could have included a more diverse set of languages or discussed the limitations of the simulation approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your noise modeling approach compare to techniques that use language-specific features (e.g., morphological features) in addition to cross-lingual projection? Could your method be combined with such features to further improve performance?

2. The paper mentions that the noise transformation matrix can handle mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide examples of how this would work in practice. Could you provide a concrete example of how your approach would handle different tagsets between languages?

3. The authors state that their method is generalizable to other noisy annotations like distant supervision. What specific modifications would be needed to apply this approach to these other domains, and what challenges might arise?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a clear, well-motivated approach to low-resource POS tagging that makes a technically sound contribution to the field. The explicit modeling of noise in projected tags is a novel and effective solution that consistently outperforms previous methods. While the novelty claim could be strengthened with a more thorough comparison to related work, and additional ablation studies would provide more insight into the contribution of the noise layer, the empirical results are strong and the approach is well-executed. The method's simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable contribution to low-resource NLP.
