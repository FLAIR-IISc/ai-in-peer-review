

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by proposing a novel neural architecture that effectively combines small amounts of gold-standard annotated data with noisy projected data from cross-lingual projection. The authors introduce a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from a high-resource language) and true tags in the low-resource language. The model is a BiLSTM with an additional transformation matrix A that adjusts tag probabilities to better match the noisy projected data. The approach is evaluated on 8 simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over strong baselines.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel explicit modeling of projection noise**: The paper's most significant contribution is the formalization and modeling of the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags as a learnable transformation matrix A, rather than treating it as an unmodeled nuisance.
2. **Effective integration of gold and projected data**: The authors demonstrate how to effectively combine noisy distant supervision with gold-standard data in a unified neural architecture, showing consistent improvements across multiple languages.
3. **Strong empirical validation on real low-resource languages**: The paper provides compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness on real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is a significant contribution given the scarcity of such evaluations.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and effective demonstration of the problem**: The paper establishes a compelling need for their approach through Table 1, which clearly shows that directly using projected tags (BiLSTM Projected) performs worse than using gold data alone (BiLSTM Annotated), despite the larger size of the projected dataset. This is a strong foundation for the paper's contribution, as it demonstrates the fundamental problem that their approach solves.

2. **Novel technical contribution with sound theoretical foundation**: The noise layer that models the transformation between projected and true tags is a genuinely novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision. The mathematical formulation (Equation 2) and the visualization of the learned transformation matrix A (Figures 3-4) provide clear evidence that the model learns meaningful patterns in the noise, rather than simply memorizing it. This is a significant improvement over previous approaches that either used the projected tags directly or attempted to implicitly model the relationship between tag sets.

3. **Strong empirical results on real low-resource languages**: The paper's evaluation on Malagasy (86.3% accuracy) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% accuracy) is particularly impressive and demonstrates the practical value of the approach. The results exceed the state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) by 1.0% and 4.2% respectively, which is meaningful in the context of low-resource POS tagging. The fact that the approach works well on real low-resource languages, not just simulated ones, is a major strength.

4. **Insightful analysis of the noise layer**: The paper provides valuable insights through the visualization of the noise transformation matrix A (Figures 3-4). These visualizations show how the model learns to trust certain tag mappings (strong diagonal) and ignore others (white cells), which helps understand how the model handles different types of noise. This analysis goes beyond simply reporting accuracy numbers and provides deeper understanding of the model's behavior.

5. **Appropriate experimental design and baselines**: The authors use a well-established evaluation framework with a small amount of gold data (1,000 tokens) for all languages, which is consistent with previous work (Duong et al., 2014a). The comparison to strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art method, provides a fair and meaningful evaluation of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient ablation studies**: The paper does not provide adequate ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For example, it doesn't show how much of the improvement over Duong et al. (2014a) is due to the noise layer versus the use of BiLSTM instead of the MaxEnt classifier. A critical ablation would be comparing the noise layer with a standard BiLSTM trained on the union of gold and projected data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj), which is already shown in Table 1. The improvement from BiLSTM Ann+Proj to BiLSTM+noise layer is marginal for some languages (e.g., German: 90.2% vs 92.5%), but the paper doesn't analyze why this is the case or what specific aspects of the noise layer are responsible for the improvement.

2. **Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations**: The paper does not analyze cases where the model fails or where the noise layer doesn't work well. For example, it would be valuable to understand why the noise layer performs less effectively on Swedish (sv) compared to other languages (as shown in Figure 3). Understanding the limitations of the approach would be important for future work and for assessing the approach's generalizability.

3. **Comparison to more recent methods**: The paper compares to Duong et al. (2014a) as the state-of-the-art, but doesn't compare to more recent methods in the field. For instance, there have been significant advances in multilingual transfer learning with models like mBERT that could potentially outperform the approach described here. The authors should at least acknowledge these more recent developments and discuss how their approach might complement or differ from them.

4. **Insufficient discussion of the noise layer's sensitivity to initialization**: The paper notes that "Directly training the whole deep neural network with random initialization is impractical" and uses a two-stage training process. However, it doesn't discuss how sensitive the model is to the initialization of the noise matrix A, or how the two-stage training process affects the final performance. The paper should provide more details on how the noise matrix is initialized in the first stage, and whether different initialization strategies yield different results.

5. **Potential overfitting concerns**: Given the small amount of gold data (only 1,000 tokens), there's a risk of overfitting to the gold dataset. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model isn't overfitting, such as showing development set performance or using cross-validation. This is a significant concern in low-resource settings where the gold data is extremely limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about how you determined the optimal size of the noise matrix A (K×K) and whether you considered alternative parameterizations (e.g., using a smaller matrix with a reduced number of parameters)?

2. In the two-stage training process, how did you determine the point at which to switch from pretraining on gold data to joint training? Was this based on development set performance, or was it fixed?

3. How does your approach compare to simply using a weighted combination of the gold and projected data (e.g., using a learnable weight for each type of supervision)? The noise layer seems to be a more complex way to achieve what could potentially be done with a simple weighting scheme.

4. Could you discuss how your approach might generalize to languages with very different syntactic structures from English (the source language used in projection)? For example, how would the approach perform when projecting from English to a language with a radically different word order?

5. In the real low-resource language experiments, the parallel data for Kinyarwanda is much smaller (52k tokens) than for Malagasy (1,231k tokens). How did the size of the parallel data affect the performance of your approach, and what is the minimum amount of parallel data required for the approach to be effective?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to handling noisy distant supervision in low-resource POS tagging. The core contribution of explicitly modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected tags is significant and well-executed, with strong empirical results on both simulated and real low-resource languages. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of ablation studies and discussion of limitations, the overall contribution is valuable and well-supported by the evidence presented. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
