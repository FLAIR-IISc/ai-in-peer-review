

# Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that combines gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that learns a transformation matrix between projected tags (from high-resource language) and true tags, allowing the model to learn "when to trust" the projected data. The authors claim three contributions: (1) studying noise in projected data and modeling it with an additional layer, (2) integrating this into a deep neural network for joint training, and (3) showing superior performance on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages.

## Strengths
The paper makes a reasonable attempt to address a genuine problem in low-resource NLP. The experimental setup is appropriate for the task, and the results on the two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) are promising. The noise transformation matrices shown in Figures 3 and 4 provide some interesting insights into the nature of the noise in projected tags.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient novelty and overstatement of contribution**: The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this is a misrepresentation of the field. The noise layer is essentially a learned confusion matrix that could be implemented as a simple linear transformation layer in any standard neural network. This approach is not fundamentally novel; it's a standard technique used in various forms (e.g., label smoothing, confusion matrices in noisy label learning). The paper fails to demonstrate how this differs significantly from prior work that has used similar techniques to handle noisy labels.

2. **Inadequate validation against state-of-the-art methods**: The paper only compares against Duong et al. (2014a) and a few naive baselines, but fails to include more recent state-of-the-art methods for low-resource POS tagging. For example, the paper doesn't compare against modern neural approaches like those using transfer learning, multi-task learning, or data augmentation techniques that have been shown to be effective for low-resource settings. The reported improvements (e.g., 91.7% vs. 91.3% on the 8 languages) are marginal and may not be statistically significant, yet the paper presents them as a major contribution.

3. **Lack of ablation studies and justification for the noise layer**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer specifically. It's unclear whether the noise layer is necessary or whether the marginal improvements could be achieved simply by using more data (the union of annotated and projected data). The paper claims that "the ultimate goal is to predict the POS tag yt" but doesn't demonstrate why a standard BiLSTM couldn't learn this transformation implicitly rather than through an explicit noise layer. The training process description (two-stage training) also lacks justification for why random initialization would be problematic.

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to just 8 European languages (simulated low-resource) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The paper doesn't evaluate on more diverse low-resource languages or in more challenging settings (e.g., languages with very limited parallel data or languages with significant syntactic divergence from English). The results on the simulated languages (which use Europarl, a high-quality parallel corpus) may not generalize to more challenging low-resource scenarios where parallel data is scarce and noisy.

5. **Overstated claims about generalizability**: The paper claims their technique "is general, and is likely to prove useful for exploiting other noisy annotations such as distant supervision and crowd-sourced annotations." However, the paper provides no evidence or analysis to support this claim. The noise layer appears to be specific to the cross-lingual POS projection setting, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how it would generalize to other types of noisy annotations.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why is an explicit noise layer necessary when the BiLSTM could learn the transformation implicitly? What evidence is there that the noise layer provides a significant advantage over simply using the projected data with standard neural network training?

2. The paper reports marginal improvements over the state-of-the-art (e.g., 0.4% absolute improvement on the 8 languages). Were these improvements statistically significant? What is the confidence interval for these results?

3. Why not compare against more recent methods for low-resource POS tagging, such as those using transfer learning (e.g., from multilingual models) or data augmentation techniques that have been shown to be effective in recent literature?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in low-resource POS tagging, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed approach offers a significant methodological advance. The claimed novelty is overstated, the validation is inadequate, and the improvements over existing methods are marginal. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison against recent state-of-the-art methods, ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer, and a more nuanced discussion of how this work differs from existing techniques for handling noisy labels. Without these, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims of novelty and significance.

This work would be more appropriate for a venue focused on incremental improvements rather than a major conference like CONLL, which emphasizes significant methodological advances.
