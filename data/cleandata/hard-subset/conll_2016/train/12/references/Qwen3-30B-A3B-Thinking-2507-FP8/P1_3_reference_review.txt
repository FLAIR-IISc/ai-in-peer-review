

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that models the transformation between projected tags (from a high-resource language) and true tags in the low-resource language. The authors argue that this allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data, rather than treating it as direct supervision. They evaluate their approach on 8 European languages (simulating low-resource settings) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over previous methods.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer)
1. **Explicit modeling of noise in projected POS tags** through a transformation matrix that captures the mapping between projected and true tags
2. **Practical integration of this noise modeling** into a BiLSTM architecture for joint training on both gold and projected data
3. **Strong empirical validation** across multiple languages demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a significant problem in NLP**: Low-resource POS tagging is a well-known challenge in NLP, and the paper directly tackles this problem with a practical approach that leverages widely available parallel corpora. The authors provide a compelling argument for why cross-lingual projection is valuable despite its noise, which is a critical insight for the field.

2. **Well-motivated technical contribution**: The noise layer concept is logically developed from the observation that projected tags are often unreliable but contain useful information. The authors provide a clear explanation of why direct use of projected tags is suboptimal (Tables 1-2 show significant performance degradation when using projected data alone). The transformation matrix A is a simple yet effective way to model the noise, with minimal additional parameters (K×K matrix).

3. **Strong and consistent empirical results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple languages (Table 1 shows the approach outperforming all baselines in 7 out of 8 languages, with the best results in 6 languages). The results on real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) are particularly compelling, achieving 86.3% and 82.5% accuracy respectively, outperforming previous state-of-the-art. The analysis of the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how the model handles different types of noise.

4. **Practical implementation**: The approach is straightforward to implement within existing neural network architectures, adding only a single matrix parameter to the model. The two-stage training procedure (pretraining on gold data then joint training) is practical and avoids the pitfalls of random initialization for the noise layer.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the noise modeling approach**: While the application to POS tagging is novel, the concept of modeling noise as a transformation matrix has appeared in other contexts (e.g., in multi-task learning, domain adaptation, and robust learning). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this approach from existing noise modeling techniques. For instance, the authors cite related work but don't explicitly compare their noise layer to other noise modeling approaches in the literature.

2. **Insufficient evaluation on real low-resource languages**: The paper only evaluates on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to establish generalizability. The authors claim "two real-world low-resource languages" as a contribution, but this limited scope makes it difficult to assess how well the approach would work across a broader range of languages with varying characteristics.

3. **Comparison to more recent methods**: The paper compares to Duong et al. (2014a), which is appropriate for the evaluation setting, but doesn't compare to more recent deep learning approaches for low-resource POS tagging that might have been published after their work. For example, recent Transformer-based approaches for cross-lingual transfer might provide better baselines.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the noise layer. For instance, it would be valuable to see how performance changes when the noise layer is removed or when the transformation matrix A is constrained to be identity.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding languages with very different syntactic structures from the source language. The authors note that cross-lingual syntactic divergence is a problem (citing Duong et al., 2013), but don't discuss how this affects their model's performance or how it might be mitigated.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the noise layer approach generalize to other NLP tasks beyond POS tagging (e.g., named entity recognition or dependency parsing), and what modifications might be needed?

2. The paper uses a two-stage training procedure (pretrain on gold data, then joint training with projected data). Why not train end-to-end from the beginning, and what are the theoretical advantages of the two-stage approach?

3. The authors claim this is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations." Could you provide a more detailed comparison to similar noise modeling approaches in the literature?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem in NLP with a practical and effective solution. The core contribution of explicitly modeling the noise in projected POS tags is reasonable and well-executed, and the empirical results are strong. However, the novelty is somewhat limited compared to the impact of the contribution, and the evaluation on real low-resource languages is too limited to fully validate the approach's generalizability.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP, particularly for POS tagging, and should be accepted with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above. The authors should strengthen the discussion of novelty, provide more comprehensive evaluation on real languages, and include ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of their noise layer.
