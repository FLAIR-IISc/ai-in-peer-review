

# Review for CONLL

## Summary
The paper proposes a new approach for low-resource POS tagging that combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with distant supervision from cross-lingual projection (using parallel data with a resource-rich language). The authors introduce a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a transformation matrix A. The model uses a BiLSTM with an additional noise layer that transforms the projected tags to better match the true tags. The authors evaluate their approach on 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing improvements over existing methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Explicit modeling of noise in projected tags through a transformation matrix A** - This is the most significant contribution, directly addressing a key challenge in using distant supervision for low-resource POS tagging.
2. **Joint training framework for gold and projected data** - The paper effectively integrates both data sources into a single training process.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages** - The evaluation demonstrates the approach's applicability across different scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated approach to handling noise in distant supervision:** The paper provides a clear and compelling justification for why direct use of projected tags is problematic (Table 1 clearly shows training on projected data alone leads to poor results). The authors effectively demonstrate that the noise in projected tags is not random but follows patterns that can be learned, as evidenced by the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4). The noise layer is simple yet effective, and the model's ability to learn that certain tags like "NUM" in Italian are rarely projected as "DET" shows the approach captures meaningful patterns in the noise.

2. **Strong and consistent experimental results:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines across both simulated and real low-resource languages. On the 8 simulated languages (Table 1), their approach achieves 91.7% accuracy compared to the previous state-of-the-art (91.3%). On real low-resource languages, it achieves 86.3% for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, outperforming previous state-of-the-art results (85.3% and 78.3% respectively). The consistency of results across different languages and evaluation settings strongly supports the validity of their approach.

3. **Clear problem statement and effective demonstration of the problem:** The paper provides a strong motivation for the problem, clearly explaining why low-resource POS tagging is challenging and why cross-lingual projection is promising but problematic. The authors effectively demonstrate the limitations of direct use of projected tags (Table 1), which justifies their approach. The connection between the noise in projected tags and the need for a transformation layer is well-justified, and the paper provides evidence that the noise is structured rather than random (Figure 3).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach:** While the explicit modeling of noise is a good idea, the core approach (using a BiLSTM with an additional noise layer) is not particularly novel. The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with other noise-handling techniques in the literature. For example, there are other approaches to handling noise in distant supervision, such as using noise-robust loss functions or training a separate noise model. The claim that this is "the first to explicitly model the noise" is overstated given existing work on noise modeling in machine learning. A more thorough comparison with these alternative approaches would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For instance, the authors don't compare the performance of their model with a random transformation matrix versus a learned one, or with different noise modeling approaches. The paper also doesn't show how the noise layer performs when the projected data quality varies (e.g., using different alignment methods). Without these ablation studies, it's difficult to determine how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture itself.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't address how the method performs when parallel data is extremely limited or of very poor quality (beyond the two real languages tested). The paper mentions that Kinyarwanda has more noise than Malagasy (Figure 4), but doesn't discuss how this affects performance or what could be done to mitigate it. The sensitivity of the model to the quality of word alignments is also not addressed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations." However, there are other approaches in the literature that handle noise in distant supervision (e.g., noise-robust loss functions, or using a separate classifier to predict noise). How does your approach differ from these, and why is explicit modeling of the transformation matrix A better than these alternatives?

2. The paper demonstrates that the noise layer improves performance, but doesn't provide ablation studies to show why the specific architecture is necessary. What if you used a different noise model (e.g., a simple dropout layer or a random noise matrix)? How much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture itself?

3. The paper uses a fixed-size transformation matrix A, but tagset sizes vary across languages. How would your approach scale to languages with very different tagsets (e.g., languages with more fine-grained POS tags)? The paper mentions support for mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide implementation details.

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in low-resource POS tagging with strong experimental results. While the core idea of explicitly modeling noise is not radically novel, the implementation is clean and effective, and the results are compelling. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with existing noise-handling techniques and additional ablation studies. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
