

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a novel neural network model with a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags. The key contributions of this work are:

1. A novel neural network architecture with a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the noisy projected data.
2. A comprehensive evaluation on both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over previous methods.
3. Insightful analysis of the noise transformation matrix A, revealing patterns of tag confusion that help explain why certain tags are more noisy than others.

## Strengths

**1. Novel and well-motivated approach to handling noisy distant supervision**  
The paper identifies a critical limitation in existing approaches to cross-lingual POS projection: directly using projected tags without accounting for their noise. The authors correctly point out that "direct projection has several issues and results in very noisy and unreliable annotations." Their solution—explicitly modeling the noise transformation with a parameterized matrix A—is both novel and well-justified. The results (Table 1) clearly demonstrate the superiority of their approach over baselines that use projected data directly (BiLSTM Projected), with their method achieving 91.7% accuracy compared to Duong et al.'s 91.3% on the 8 simulated languages. The noise layer effectively addresses the core problem the authors identify, making this a strong contribution.

**2. Comprehensive and well-designed experimental evaluation**  
The authors provide a thorough evaluation across both simulated and real low-resource settings. For the simulated experiments, they use 8 European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens to simulate low-resource conditions, following established practice in the field. For real-world evaluation, they use two diverse low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which are well-chosen to demonstrate the practical applicability of their approach. The results consistently show that their method outperforms all baselines, with statistically significant improvements in both settings. For example, they achieve 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy (vs. 85.3% for Duong et al.) and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda (vs. 78.3% for Duong et al.), demonstrating the practical value of their approach.

**3. Insightful analysis of the noise transformation matrix**  
The authors provide a valuable analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix A (Figures 3 and 4), which reveals concrete patterns of tag confusion. For example, they show that in Swedish, PRT and X are confused with each other, while in Italian, NUM and DET are rarely confused. This analysis not only validates their approach but also provides meaningful insights into the nature of the noise in projected tags, which is particularly valuable for understanding how the model handles different languages. This level of analysis goes beyond typical results reporting and adds significant value to the paper.

## Weaknesses

**1. Insufficient justification for neural network approach**  
The paper claims the neural network component is important, but doesn't convincingly argue why a neural approach is necessary. The authors compare their BiLSTM+noise layer to Duong et al.'s maximum entropy classifier, but don't provide an ablation study that isolates the contribution of the noise layer from the neural architecture. It's possible that the noise layer alone (without the BiLSTM) would provide similar benefits, and the authors don't explore this. A more thorough comparison between the noise layer applied to simpler models (e.g., CRFs) and neural models would strengthen the paper's claim that the neural approach is essential.

**2. Limited real-world evaluation**  
While the authors evaluate on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), this is insufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach. The paper claims their technique is "general" and "likely to prove useful for exploiting other noisy annotations," but they provide no evidence beyond these two examples. For a paper focused on low-resource languages, more diverse real-world evaluation would be valuable. The authors should provide additional examples from other language families or with different types of noise to strengthen their claim of general applicability.

**3. Lack of discussion on computational cost**  
The paper doesn't address the computational cost of their approach compared to previous methods. Training a BiLSTM with a noise layer is likely more computationally intensive than the maximum entropy approach used by Duong et al. (2014a), but this isn't discussed. For low-resource language applications, computational efficiency is often an important consideration, especially when working with limited resources. The authors should provide a brief comparison of training time and resource requirements between their method and baselines.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 1, your method achieves 91.7% accuracy on the 8 simulated languages, while Duong et al. achieves 91.3%. Is this difference statistically significant? If not, how do you explain the consistent improvement across languages?

2. The paper mentions that the noise transformation matrix A can handle mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide an example. Could you provide a specific example where this capability was particularly useful in your experiments?

3. For the real low-resource languages, you used parallel data from web sources (Malagasy) and the ARL MURI project (Kinyarwanda). How much of this data was actually used for training after filtering with the sentence alignment score criterion? The paper states "we use the whole parallel data because of limited bilingual text," but doesn't specify the effective size of the filtered datasets.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated and effective approach to handling noisy distant supervision in low-resource POS tagging. The noise layer is a novel and valuable contribution that addresses a real problem in the field, and the results consistently demonstrate its effectiveness. The comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource settings provides strong evidence for the approach's practical value.

The main weaknesses are the insufficient justification for using a neural network (rather than a simpler model) and the limited real-world evaluation. However, these do not undermine the core contribution of the noise layer, which is the most significant part of the paper.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above, particularly regarding the justification of the neural approach and the computational cost of the method. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of low-resource NLP, and I believe it would be valuable to the ACL community.
