

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining small amounts of gold-standard annotated data with noisy projected data from a high-resource language. The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly models the transformation between projected (noisy) tags and true tags using a learnable noise matrix. The model uses a BiLSTM with an additional noise layer that adjusts the tag probabilities to better match the noisy projected POS tags. The main contributions, assessed objectively, are:

1. A novel noise transformation layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected tags and true tags in a deep learning framework (primary contribution)
2. A joint training approach that effectively combines gold-standard and projected data in a single neural network (secondary contribution)
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation across 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrating consistent improvements over prior methods (tertiary contribution)

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness of the Noise Layer**: The paper presents a clear and well-motivated technical contribution in the form of the noise layer. The transformation matrix A provides a simple yet effective way to model the relationship between projected and true tags. The model's architecture is well-justified, with the noise layer allowing for error backpropagation through the transformation, which is a technically sound approach to handling noisy labels. The visualization of the noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) demonstrates that the model learns meaningful patterns of tag confusion rather than random noise.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper provides robust experimental results across multiple languages and settings. The method consistently outperforms baselines across 8 simulated languages (with improvements of 1-3% over the state-of-the-art) and achieves 86.3% accuracy for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, exceeding previous results. The evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines including Duong et al. (2014a), the previous state-of-the-art.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of previous work that used direct projection (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a) and clearly demonstrates through ablation studies that using projected data directly harms performance. The paper establishes a compelling case for why an explicit noise modeling approach is needed, supported by the poor results of the BiLSTM Projected model (Table 1).

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Novelty Claim**: The paper's claim of novelty is overstated. While the noise layer is a specific implementation, the concept of modeling noise in projected labels through a transformation matrix is not entirely new. Previous work in noisy label learning (e.g., using confusion matrices for error correction) shares conceptual similarities. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from existing techniques for handling noisy labels, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Questionable Simulation Setup**: The use of European languages with abundant parallel data to simulate low-resource scenarios is problematic. The parallel data for these languages (Europarl corpus) is high-quality and massive (1.85 million sentences on average), which is not representative of truly low-resource languages. This simulation may overestimate the benefits of the approach, as real low-resource languages often have limited, lower-quality parallel data. The paper lacks justification for why these particular languages and corpora are appropriate for simulating low-resource settings.

3. **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the approach to the amount of parallel data. While they evaluate on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda with limited parallel data (100k and 11k sentences respectively), they don't test the method on even smaller parallel corpora (e.g., <5k sentences) to demonstrate its limits. This is particularly important since the paper's core contribution is for truly low-resource settings where parallel data may be extremely limited.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims their approach is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations." However, error-corrected training using confusion matrices has been used in other contexts for handling noisy labels. How does your noise layer approach specifically differ from these existing methods, and why is your formulation more suitable for cross-lingual projection?

2. The simulation experiments use European languages with abundant parallel data (Europarl corpus). How do you justify that this setup accurately reflects the challenges of real low-resource languages, which typically have much smaller and lower-quality parallel resources? Could you provide additional experiments with smaller parallel corpora to better simulate extreme low-resource scenarios?

3. The noise matrix A is learned during training. How sensitive is the performance to the initial values of A, and how does the two-stage training process (pretraining on gold data followed by joint training) affect the final results? The paper mentions using a random initialization for A, but doesn't discuss how different initializations might impact the model's ability to learn meaningful transformations.
