

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture for POS tagging in low-resource languages that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags as a learnable transformation. The key contribution is the "noise layer" - a transformation matrix A that adjusts tag probabilities from a base BiLSTM model to better match the noisy projected tags. The authors claim this is the first approach to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations, rather than implicitly capturing correlations through joint features.

## Strengths
1. **Novel conceptual approach**: The core contribution of explicitly modeling noise as a learnable transformation (matrix A) represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work. Previous approaches either tried to clean the projected data before training or implicitly captured tagset correlations through joint features. This paper's approach of embedding the noise transformation directly into the neural network architecture is both elegant and conceptually fresh. The authors' framing of "learning when to trust distant supervision" is particularly compelling.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The approach demonstrates consistent, meaningful improvements across multiple languages (both simulated low-resource settings and two real-world low-resource languages). The results show not just incremental gains but significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art (86.3% accuracy for Malagasy vs. 85.3% for Duong et al. 2014a). The paper also provides valuable insights through the visualization of learned noise matrices, showing how the model learns to handle specific noise patterns (e.g., nouns mapping to adjectives in Malagasy).

3. **Broad applicability**: The authors correctly note that their technique is general and could be applied to other forms of noisy supervision beyond cross-lingual projection (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations). This broader potential significantly elevates the contribution beyond just POS tagging in low-resource languages.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty claim justification**: While the authors claim this is the first to explicitly model noise, they don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from related work in noisy label learning (e.g., noise-contrastive estimation, robust loss functions). A more thorough discussion of how this differs from existing techniques would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer from other aspects of the model (e.g., the BiLSTM architecture itself). Without these, it's difficult to determine exactly how much of the improvement comes from the core innovation versus other components.

3. **Limited exploration of noise patterns**: While the paper visualizes the noise matrices, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* certain noise patterns emerge (e.g., why NUM and DET are confused in Italian but not in Spanish). A deeper exploration of these language-specific noise characteristics would add significant value.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. Despite minor methodological limitations, the paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision that has clear conceptual value and demonstrable empirical impact. The core idea of explicitly modeling noise as a learnable transformation represents a bold new direction in how we handle unreliable data sources in NLP.

The paper's contribution is not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but a fundamental shift in how we think about and incorporate noisy data into neural models. The authors' framing of "learning when to trust distant supervision" is particularly compelling and could influence how we approach similar problems in other NLP tasks.

While the paper could benefit from more thorough ablation studies and a more nuanced discussion of related work on noise handling, these are minor issues that don't undermine the paper's significant contribution. The novelty and potential impact of the core idea outweigh these limitations, especially given the strong empirical results.

This paper represents the kind of bold, conceptually fresh approach that advances the field beyond incremental improvements. It's precisely the kind of work that should be encouraged at a conference like CoNLL.
