

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a method for low-resource POS tagging that combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The key innovation is a noise layer that models the transformation between projected tags (from English-to-target language projection) and true tags. The authors claim this approach "unifromly equals or exceeds existing methods" on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. A noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags
2. Application of this technique to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection
3. Comprehensive evaluation on 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real low-resource languages

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness of Noise Modeling**: The paper correctly identifies that directly using projected tags is problematic (as shown in Table 1), and the noise layer (matrix A) is a principled approach to model the transformation between projected and true tags. The approach to handle the uncertainty in projected tags is theoretically well-motivated.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation on both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results consistently show improvements over baselines across multiple languages, demonstrating the approach's generalizability.

3. **Insightful Noise Transformation Analysis**: The noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. This analysis helps understand the model's behavior and the nature of the noise in projected tags.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper fails to provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. Without these, it's impossible to determine whether the improvements come from the noise layer, the BiLSTM architecture, or some combination. For instance, how much would the performance improve if they used a simpler noise model (e.g., label smoothing) or fixed transformation matrix?

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares primarily with Duong et al. (2014a), but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods for low-resource POS tagging that have emerged since 2014. Given the rapid advances in neural NLP, this is a significant oversight that weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. **Overstated Claims of Uniform Improvement**: The authors claim "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods" (Section 1), but the results in Table 1 show only marginal improvements in some cases (e.g., Swedish: 89.1% vs. 88.7% for Duong et al.). The word "uniformly" is not supported by the data.

4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the method fails or where the noise layer doesn't provide significant improvement. Understanding the limitations would be valuable for the community and would strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Lack of Justification for Neural Architecture Choice**: The paper uses a BiLSTM without justification for why this specific architecture is better suited for this task than other approaches. Previous work (Duong et al., 2014a) used a maximum entropy classifier, and the paper doesn't adequately explain why a neural approach is necessary.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide ablation studies demonstrating that the noise layer (matrix A) is responsible for the improvements, rather than the BiLSTM architecture alone?

2. How does your noise layer compare to simpler noise modeling techniques such as label smoothing or using a fixed transformation matrix?

3. Have you compared your method with more recent state-of-the-art approaches for low-resource POS tagging (post-2016)?

4. Why do you claim "uniformly exceeds existing methods" when the improvements are marginal in some cases (e.g., Swedish)?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to handling noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags, the lack of ablation studies, insufficient comparison with more recent work, and overstated claims of uniform improvement undermine the contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened with more thorough validation and a clearer demonstration of the novelty and necessity of the proposed noise layer.

The results are promising, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the noise layer is the key innovation rather than a natural extension of existing neural modeling techniques. Without more rigorous validation, it's difficult to justify the claim that this represents a significant advance over established methods.

I recommend rejection, but would consider acceptance if the authors could provide the requested ablation studies and comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods.
