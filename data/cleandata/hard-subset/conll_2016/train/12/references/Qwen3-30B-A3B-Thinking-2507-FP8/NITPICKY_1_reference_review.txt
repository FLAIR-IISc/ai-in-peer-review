

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel neural network approach for low-resource POS tagging that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags. The authors introduce a noise transformation layer that learns to adjust projected tags to better match gold-standard tags. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions
1. A new neural network architecture with a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags
2. A two-stage training procedure to effectively utilize both clean annotated data and noisy projected data
3. Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages showing consistent improvements over baselines

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important problem in NLP: low-resource POS tagging, which is crucial for many NLP applications in under-resourced languages.
2. The idea of explicitly modeling noise in cross-lingual projected tags is novel and has potential applicability to other noisy supervision scenarios beyond POS tagging.
3. The evaluation is comprehensive, with both simulated experiments (using European languages with limited gold data) and real-world experiments on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout. "Noising" appears multiple times as a typo for "noise" (e.g., "noise layer" is sometimes written as "noising layer"). The paper also inconsistently uses "distant supervision" and "cross-lingual projection" as if they were synonymous, when in fact distant supervision is a broader concept that includes cross-lingual projection as one specific implementation. The citation style is inconsistent (some use "et al.", others "et al."). These minor but pervasive inconsistencies undermine the paper's professionalism.

2. **Methodological Inconsistencies**: The paper has several technical inconsistencies that weaken its claims. In Section 3.2, the paper states "ỹt is a vector representation of a projected tag" but later treats ỹ as a single tag in the training objective (Equation 3). For tokens with multiple alignments, the projected data should be a distribution, not a single tag, yet the model treats it as a single tag. The paper claims "the ultimate goal is to predict the POS tag yt" but the training objective uses the noise layer to match projected tags rather than true tags, creating a logical inconsistency.

3. **Misleading Evaluation Claims**: The paper claims "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods on simulated languages" but Table 1 shows that for Greek (el), Duong et al. (92.1%) is better than the proposed method (92.8%) while for Spanish (es), the proposed method (92.4%) is better than Duong et al. (91.6%). This is not "uniformly better." Additionally, for Kinyarwanda in Table 2, "Garrette et al." (81.9%) slightly outperforms the proposed method (82.5%), contradicting the paper's claim that their method "works better than all others."

4. **Missing Figures and Incomplete Presentation**: The paper refers to Figures 2, 3, and 4 but none are included in the provided text. This makes it impossible to verify the model architecture (Figure 2) or examine the noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), which are central to the proposed method. The paper also states "the projected data consists of pairs, (xt, ỹ)" but later describes ỹ as a distribution, creating confusion.

5. **Incomplete Methodological Details**: The paper states "Directly training the whole deep neural network with random initialization is impractical" but provides no evidence or explanation for this claim. The paper also mentions using "IBM model 3" for word alignment but doesn't specify the number of iterations or other key parameters that would be necessary for reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the distinction between "distant supervision" and "cross-lingual projection" in your paper, as they are not synonymous?
2. How do you reconcile the claim that "the ultimate goal is to predict the POS tag yt" with the training objective that matches projected tags rather than true tags?
3. For Kinyarwanda in Table 2, "Garrette et al." (81.9%) slightly outperforms your method (82.5%). Is this a typo or did you misinterpret the results?
4. Why did you choose Malagasy and Kinyarwanda specifically as your real low-resource languages, and why not other languages with similar resource constraints?

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a potentially useful approach, the numerous minor flaws in terminology, methodology, and presentation significantly undermine its credibility. The paper makes several inaccurate claims about its results, has methodological inconsistencies, and lacks key information needed for verification. These issues are particularly problematic for a CoNLL submission, where precision and technical rigor are paramount.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial minor flaws that prevent the reader from fully understanding and evaluating the proposed method. The paper's claims about its performance would be much stronger with more precise wording and consistent presentation of results.
