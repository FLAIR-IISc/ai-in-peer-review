

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data from parallel corpora. The authors propose a neural network model with a "noise transformation" layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected tags (from high-resource languages) and true tags in the low-resource language. The main contributions are:

1. A novel neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags through a transformation layer, rather than treating them as direct supervision.
2. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines on both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).
3. Insightful visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices, revealing language-specific patterns of tag confusion.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and well-motivated problem**: The paper tackles a significant challenge in NLP—POS tagging for low-resource languages—which has important real-world implications. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of current approaches and provide a compelling case for their method.

2. **Novel contribution to noise modeling**: The core contribution of explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags through a transformation layer is genuinely novel. Previous work either filtered or ignored noise, while this approach learns when and how much to trust projected data. The visualization of the noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into how the model handles language-specific noise patterns.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The method consistently outperforms baselines across both simulated and real low-resource languages. The results show clear improvements over the state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a), particularly in languages where projection noise is significant (e.g., 91.7% vs 91.3% for Swedish).

4. **Clear methodology**: The paper provides a detailed description of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The two-stage training approach (pretraining on gold data, then joint training with projected data) is well-justified and addresses practical training challenges.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with simpler alternatives**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare the proposed noise layer with simpler approaches to handling noisy data, such as using weighted combinations of gold and projected data. It's unclear whether the added complexity of the noise layer provides significant benefits over these more straightforward methods.

2. **Limited discussion of computational cost**: The noise transformation layer adds a K×K matrix (K being the tagset size), which could be computationally expensive for large tagsets. The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead compared to standard BiLSTM taggers, which is particularly important for resource-constrained settings.

3. **Insufficient exploration of failure cases**: The paper doesn't provide enough analysis of when the approach fails or where it provides minimal improvement. For example, in the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda results (Table 2), the improvement over the state-of-the-art is significant (86.3% vs 85.3% and 82.5% vs 78.3%), but the paper doesn't discuss why the method works particularly well for these languages compared to others.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent neural transfer methods**: The paper doesn't compare with recent neural approaches to cross-lingual transfer, such as those using multilingual embeddings or transfer learning with large pre-trained models (e.g., XLM-R). This is a notable omission given the rapid advances in this area.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your noise transformation approach compare to simply using a weighted combination of gold and projected data (e.g., with weights learned from validation data)? Is the added complexity of the noise layer justified?

2. Can you provide more details about the computational complexity of your approach compared to standard BiLSTM POS taggers, particularly regarding the size of the noise transformation matrix?

3. Have you considered the impact of using different word alignment methods (e.g., IBM models 4-5 or neural alignment) on the quality of the projected tags, and how this might affect your noise transformation layer?

4. How does your method perform when the parallel corpus is extremely small (e.g., less than 5k sentences), which is often the case for truly low-resource languages?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages. The core idea of explicitly modeling noise in cross-lingual projected tags is novel and well-motivated, and the results are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of comparing with simpler alternatives and discussing computational costs, these are not fatal flaws. The work is clearly relevant to the CONLL community and makes a solid contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, particularly in addressing the comparison with simpler noise-handling approaches and providing more detailed computational analysis, this paper would be an excellent fit for CONLL.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions addressing the questions above)
