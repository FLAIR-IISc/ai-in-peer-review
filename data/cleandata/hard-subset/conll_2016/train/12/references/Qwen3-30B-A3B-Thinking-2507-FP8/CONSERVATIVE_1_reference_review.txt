

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that learns to transform the projected tags into a more accurate representation through a matrix A that models the mapping between projected tags and true tags. The authors claim their method outperforms existing approaches on both simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The authors present a method that incorporates a noise transformation layer into a BiLSTM model to handle noisy cross-lingual projected POS tags.
2. They provide experimental results demonstrating modest improvements over existing approaches on both simulated and real low-resource languages.
3. They offer interpretability through the noise transformation matrix A, revealing patterns of tag confusion in the projected data.

## Strengths
1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper correctly identifies the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages and the limitations of cross-lingual projection, which is a well-motivated research problem.

2. **Experimental Results**: The authors provide results on both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is a reasonable experimental setup for evaluating low-resource approaches.

3. **Model Interpretability**: The noise transformation matrix A provides valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise in projected tags, as visualized in Figures 3 and 4. This interpretability is a significant strength of the work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Novelty Justification**: The authors claim their work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but they don't provide sufficient evidence for this claim. The noise layer they propose is a simple linear transformation (matrix A), which bears strong resemblance to standard techniques for handling noisy labels. The paper doesn't adequately differentiate their approach from existing methods for dealing with noisy labels in machine learning.

2. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The reported improvements over the state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) are marginal (typically 0.2-1.2% absolute improvement on the eight simulated languages, and 1.0% on Malagasy, 4.2% on Kinyarwanda). The authors claim their approach "uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods," but the results in Table 1 show only minor improvements in most cases. These marginal gains don't justify the claim of "uniformly exceeds" and raise questions about the practical significance of the proposed method.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the reported improvements are meaningful. The small absolute gains (0.2-1.2%) could easily be within the margin of error for these types of experiments. Without statistical testing, it's impossible to determine whether the improvements are truly significant.

4. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare against Duong et al. (2014a) as the state-of-the-art, but this paper is from 2014, while the authors' own references include works from 2016. The paper doesn't compare against more recent methods that might have improved upon Duong et al.'s approach. This limits the significance of their claims about being "state-of-the-art."

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture itself. Without this, it's impossible to assess the true value of the proposed noise transformation.

6. **Questionable Methodological Rigor**: The training process involves pretraining on annotated data followed by joint training on both annotated and projected data. However, the paper doesn't provide justification for why this two-stage training is necessary or how it compares to alternative training approaches. The paper also doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the approach to the size of the annotated dataset or the quality of the projected data.

## Questions to Authors
1. The authors claim their approach is the first to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. Can they provide a more detailed comparison with previous work that might have addressed similar noise issues?

2. The reported improvements over Duong et al. (2014a) are marginal in most cases. Could the authors provide statistical significance tests to demonstrate that these improvements are meaningful?

3. The authors mention that their method works well on both simulated and real low-resource languages. However, the real low-resource languages have very limited parallel data (100k sentences for Malagasy, 11k for Kinyarwanda). How do they expect their approach to scale to languages with even less parallel data?

4. The noise transformation matrix A is a K×K matrix. How does the model handle cases where the tagsets of the source and target languages are mismatched (as mentioned in the paper)? What happens when the tagset of the source language has more tags than the target language?

## Overall Recommendation
While the problem addressed is important and the experimental results show modest improvements over existing approaches, the paper falls short of the methodological rigor expected for a top-tier NLP conference. The marginal gains, lack of statistical significance testing, and insufficient novelty justification make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The approach appears to be a reasonable extension of existing techniques rather than a significant advance. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims with more rigorous validation, statistical significance testing, and a clearer demonstration of novelty to justify publication at a conference like CoNLL.

The paper demonstrates a useful application of neural networks to a practical problem, but the contributions are incremental rather than transformative. Given the skeptical stance of the reviewer toward unproven approaches, the paper does not meet the high standards required for acceptance.
