

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly utilizes a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and noisy cross-lingual projected data. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that learns to transform projected tags to better match true tags, which the authors claim is the first explicit modeling of the noise in cross-lingual projection.

As I see it, the paper's main contributions are:
1. A method for incorporating noisy projected POS tags into POS tagging through a learnable transformation layer
2. Experimental validation showing consistent improvements over previous methods on both simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda)
3. Analysis of the noise transformation matrix across different languages

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a genuine and important problem in NLP: POS tagging for low-resource languages where annotated data is scarce. The authors correctly identify that directly using projected tags leads to poor performance, which is a well-established challenge in the field.

2. The experimental setup is reasonably thorough, with evaluation on both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results show consistent improvements over baselines on these languages.

3. The paper provides useful visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and Figure 4), which help understand how the model "learns to trust" the projected data differently across languages and tag types.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The proposed "noise layer" is not a truly novel contribution. The transformation matrix A is essentially a linear transformation of the output probabilities, which is conceptually similar to techniques used in error-correcting models or simple semi-supervised learning approaches. The authors claim this is the "first" to explicitly model the noise, but this is an overstatement given existing work on handling noisy labels in machine learning.

2. **Insufficient comparison with stronger baselines**: The paper only compares against Duong et al. (2014a), a method from 2014, and does not include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art approaches. For example, the paper fails to compare with methods using multilingual BERT or other modern transfer learning techniques that have become standard for low-resource NLP tasks. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed approach is genuinely competitive with current best practices.

3. **Lack of ablation studies and justification for the design**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that their specific approach (the noise layer) is better than simpler alternatives. For example, why not use a simple weighted combination of gold and projected data, or a simpler transformation? The authors don't provide ablation studies showing the relative contribution of the noise layer versus other components.

4. **Overstated claims about uniform superiority**: The paper claims "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods," but the results in Table 1 show that for Greek (el), the improvement over Duong et al. (2014a) is marginal (92.1% vs 92.8%), and for Swedish (sv), the improvement is only 0.4%. The paper also doesn't report statistical significance tests for these improvements, which weakens their claim of "uniform" superiority.

5. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation on real low-resource languages is limited to just two languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to claim general applicability. The paper doesn't discuss whether the approach would work for languages with different linguistic structures or more severe alignment challenges.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods for low-resource POS tagging, such as those using multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R), which have become standard in the field?

2. What is the computational cost of your noise layer compared to simpler approaches like direct combination of gold and projected data? How does this affect scalability to larger datasets?

3. Can you provide statistical significance tests for your improvements over the baselines to support your claim of "uniform" superiority?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable approach to a well-known problem in low-resource POS tagging, but the novelty of the core contribution is questionable. While the experimental results are promising, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate that their specific method offers a significant improvement over simpler alternatives or more recent baselines. The lack of comparison with current state-of-the-art methods is particularly concerning, as it makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and inadequate comparison with established baselines. The paper would need to provide stronger evidence of the value of their specific approach through more comprehensive comparisons with recent methods and thorough ablation studies before it would be suitable for publication.
