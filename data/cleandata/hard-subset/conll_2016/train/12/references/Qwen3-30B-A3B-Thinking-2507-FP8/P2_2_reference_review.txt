

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for low-resource POS tagging by effectively leveraging noisy cross-lingual projected annotations from high-resource languages. The authors address the challenge of using distant supervision from parallel data (English to low-resource languages) by introducing a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags. Their BiLSTM-based model jointly trains on both gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected data, with the noise layer learning when and how much to trust the projected data. The approach is evaluated on both simulated low-resource scenarios (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions
1. **Explicit modeling of noise in projected tags**: The authors propose a novel noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags, rather than treating projected tags as direct supervision.
2. **Joint training on clean and noisy data**: The model jointly trains on gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected data, with the noise layer learning to adapt the projected tags to the target language's tagset.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation on simulated and real low-resource languages**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across both simulated low-resource scenarios and real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods.

## Strengths

**1. Novel and well-motivated approach to handling noisy distant supervision**  
The paper's key strength lies in its novel approach to handling noisy projected tags. The authors clearly articulate the problem with direct use of projected tags (Table 1 shows BiLSTM Projected performs much worse than BiLSTM Annotated), and their solution—explicitly modeling the noise transformation—is well-justified. The noise layer (matrix A) is a simple yet effective addition to the standard BiLSTM architecture, with minimal additional parameters (K×K, where K is the tagset size). The authors provide compelling evidence that this approach works better than previous methods (Table 1 shows their model outperforms Duong et al.'s state-of-the-art by 0.4-3.2% across languages), and the analysis of the noise transformation matrix (Figure 3) reveals valuable insights about how the model handles different types of noise.

**2. Comprehensive and well-designed evaluation**  
The evaluation strategy is strong and well-justified. The authors use both simulated low-resource settings (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which provides a robust validation of their approach. The simulation experiments allow for fair comparison with prior work (Duong et al., 2014a), while the real-world experiments demonstrate practical applicability. The results consistently show improvements across both settings (Table 1 and Table 2), with the noise layer model achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda, outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (85.3% and 78.3% respectively).

**3. Clear and insightful analysis of the noise transformation**  
The analysis of the noise transformation matrix (Figures 3 and 4) is a significant contribution in itself. The authors provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. For example, they show that for Swedish (sv), PRT and X tags are confused with each other, while for Italian (it), NUM and DET tags are rarely confused. This analysis validates their approach's ability to learn meaningful noise patterns, rather than simply memorizing the projected data. The comparison between Malagasy and Kinyarwanda (Figure 4) also reveals that the amount of noise correlates with the quality/quantity of parallel data, which is a meaningful observation.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited novelty compared to related work on label noise**  
While the authors claim their approach is the first to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projected annotations, the concept of modeling label noise is not entirely new. The approach resembles techniques used in standard supervised learning for handling label noise (e.g., the work of Goldberger and Ben-Reuven on learning with noisy labels). The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss how their approach differs from these existing methods or why they are applicable to the cross-lingual projection context. A comparison with these related techniques would strengthen the novelty claim.

**2. Limited exploration of the method's limitations and failure cases**  
The paper demonstrates strong results but doesn't thoroughly explore the boundaries of the method. For instance, the authors don't investigate how the model performs with extremely poor parallel data or when the source and target languages are very dissimilar. The evaluation on the 8 simulated languages uses relatively high-quality parallel data (Europarl v7), but real low-resource scenarios often involve much noisier or smaller parallel corpora. The authors should provide more insight into when this approach might fail or when it might not be applicable.

**3. Insufficient detail on the training process**  
The paper lacks important details about the training process that would help reproduce the results. For example, how many epochs were used for pretraining? How was the noise matrix A initialized? How was the balance between the annotated and projected data determined during joint training? The paper mentions a two-stage training process but doesn't provide enough detail about the hyperparameters used. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the approach.

**4. Limited discussion of computational complexity**  
The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of adding the noise layer. While the matrix A is small (K×K), the paper doesn't provide information about how this affects training time or model size compared to the baseline models. For a practical application in low-resource settings, computational efficiency is important, and this should be addressed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their approach is the first to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. However, similar noise modeling techniques have been used in standard supervised learning for handling label noise (e.g., Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, 2016). Can you elaborate on how your approach differs from these techniques and why they're not directly applicable to the cross-lingual projection context?

2. In Figure 4, the noise transformation matrix for Kinyarwanda shows more noise than for Malagasy, which correlates with the performance difference (82.5% vs. 86.3% accuracy). How does the model handle cases where the parallel data is extremely limited or of very poor quality (e.g., less than 5k tokens)? Would the performance degrade significantly in such cases?

3. The paper mentions that they use Giza++ with IBM model 3 for word alignment, but they filter to only one-to-one alignments. What happens if they use all alignments (including many-to-one and one-to-many)? Would this increase the amount of projected data but also introduce more noise? Have you experimented with this?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated and effective approach to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection. The key contribution of explicitly modeling the noise transformation between projected and true tags is novel and well-justified, and the experimental results are strong across both simulated and real low-resource languages. While the novelty claim could be strengthened by better comparison with related work on label noise, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The evaluation is comprehensive, and the insights from the noise transformation analysis are valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
