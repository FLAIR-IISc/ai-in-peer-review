

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining small amounts of gold-standard annotated data with noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a neural model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags as a transformation layer, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel noise transformation layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected tags and true tags, trained end-to-end with the BiLSTM model.
2. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines on both simulated (8 European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens) and real-world (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) low-resource languages.
3. Insightful analysis of the noise transformation matrices that reveals language-specific patterns of tag confusion.

## Strengths

1. **Technical novelty in noise modeling**: The authors present a clear and simple yet effective approach to handling noisy distant supervision in POS tagging. The noise layer (matrix A) is a straightforward addition to the standard BiLSTM architecture that can be trained end-to-end. This differs from previous approaches that either used heuristic cleaning of projected tags or implicitly captured tag correlations through joint feature sets. The paper provides strong evidence that this explicit noise modeling leads to significant performance improvements (e.g., 91.7% average accuracy vs. 91.3% for previous state-of-the-art on simulated languages).

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple languages and settings. The results on both simulated and real low-resource languages are statistically significant, with the approach outperforming all baselines including the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) by up to 1.4% on the simulated languages and 1.0% on Malagasy and 4.2% on Kinyarwanda. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the noise matrices (Figure 3 and 4), revealing language-specific patterns of tag confusion that validate the model's behavior.

3. **Clear presentation of the noise analysis**: The paper's visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices provide valuable insights into the nature of noise in projected tags. For example, the matrices show which tag pairs are frequently confused (e.g., PRT and X in Swedish) and which tags are rarely confused (e.g., NUM vs DET in Italian). This analysis not only supports the model's effectiveness but also provides practical insights for future work on cross-lingual projection.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty claim**: The authors claim their work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this is not fully supported by the literature. Previous work by Das and Petrov (2011) used graph-based label propagation to handle noisy labels, which can be interpreted as modeling noise. Duong et al. (2014a) also used a joint model with projected data, though they did not explicitly model the noise transformation. The paper would benefit from a more precise positioning of their contribution against these prior works.

2. **Lack of thorough ablation studies**: The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For example, it does not compare the performance of the noise layer with different model architectures (e.g., CRF instead of softmax output), nor does it explore the impact of different initialization strategies for the noise matrix A. The authors state they pretrain the network on gold data before jointly training with projected data, but don't discuss how different pretraining strategies affect the final performance.

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't adequately address cases where the approach underperforms. For instance, Swedish (sv) shows a smaller improvement (92.4% vs. 91.6% for Duong et al.) compared to other languages. The authors could provide more insight into why their approach works less effectively for certain languages (e.g., word alignment quality, tagset differences) to help understand the limitations of their method.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you distinguish your explicit noise modeling approach from the graph-based label propagation used by Das and Petrov (2011), which also handles noisy labels?

2. What happens if you initialize the noise matrix A randomly instead of starting with A ∝ I? Would this affect the performance or convergence speed?

3. The results for Swedish show a smaller improvement compared to other languages. What specific characteristics of Swedish (e.g., word alignment quality, tagset differences) might explain this?

4. The paper states that the noise layer "supports mismatching tagsets" but doesn't provide an example of this. Could you elaborate on how the model handles tagset mismatches in practice?
