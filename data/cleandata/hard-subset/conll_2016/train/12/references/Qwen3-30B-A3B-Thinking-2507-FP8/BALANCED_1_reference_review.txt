

# Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging for low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose a neural network model with a noise transformation layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected tags and true tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. The approach is evaluated on 8 simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited training data) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural network layer that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags, enabling the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple languages.
3. Analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, revealing meaningful patterns of tag confusion that align with linguistic expectations.

## Strengths
1. **Novel and well-motivated approach**: The paper identifies a key limitation of existing methods (treating projected tags as reliable) and proposes a clean solution by explicitly modeling the noise through a transformation layer. The noise layer is a natural extension to the standard neural tagger architecture, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it works and why it's beneficial.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves consistent improvements over strong baselines (including the state-of-the-art Duong et al. 2014a) across all 8 simulated languages and both real low-resource languages. The results are statistically significant (with p<0.05 in most cases), and the authors provide detailed analysis of the learned noise matrices, showing the model learns meaningful patterns of tag confusion.

3. **Clear analysis of noise patterns**: The paper's Figure 3 and Figure 4 provide valuable insights into how the model learns to handle different types of noise across languages. For example, the matrices reveal that certain tag pairs (like NUM vs DET in Italian) are rarely confused, while others (like PRT and X in Swedish) are frequently confused. This analysis goes beyond simple accuracy metrics to provide deeper understanding of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unrealistic low-resource simulation**: The paper uses 8 European languages (like Danish, German, and Spanish) with only 1,000 tokens of gold training data to simulate low-resource settings. However, these languages are not truly low-resource - they have large amounts of available text and annotation resources. This makes it questionable whether the results generalize to truly low-resource languages (like Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The paper should clarify the gap between this simulation and real low-resource scenarios.

2. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate on only two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to establish the generalizability of their approach. While the results are good, the paper doesn't address whether the method would work for other language families or with even less parallel data.

3. **Overstated novelty claim**: The authors claim their work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations." However, previous work (e.g., Duong et al. 2014a) already attempted to model tagset correlations implicitly. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their explicit modeling approach from previous implicit approaches in a way that establishes true novelty.

4. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the noise layer compared to simpler approaches. For low-resource applications where resources may be limited, understanding the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost is crucial.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the noise layer perform when the target language has significantly different syntax from English (e.g., languages with SOV word order)? The current evaluation only includes languages with relatively similar syntax to English.

2. The authors claim their approach works for languages with mismatching tagsets. Could they provide an example of how the noise matrix handles this, and what the performance impact is?

3. Given the limited evaluation on real low-resource languages, what additional languages would the authors recommend for future evaluation to better establish the generalizability of their approach?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a well-motivated approach to a significant problem in NLP for low-resource languages. The noise transformation layer is a clever extension to standard neural taggers that addresses a real limitation of existing methods. The empirical results are strong, and the analysis of the learned noise matrices provides valuable insights.

However, the paper's simulation of low-resource settings using European languages with only 1,000 tokens of training data is problematic, as these are not truly low-resource languages. The limited evaluation on only two real low-resource languages also makes it difficult to assess the approach's generalizability. The novelty claim could also be more carefully qualified.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about the low-resource simulation and provide additional evaluation on more languages in the revision. The paper has strong potential but requires more convincing evidence of its applicability to truly low-resource languages.
