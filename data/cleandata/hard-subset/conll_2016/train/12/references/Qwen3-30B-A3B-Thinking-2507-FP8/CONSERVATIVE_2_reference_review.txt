

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that combines gold-standard annotated data with cross-lingual projected tags from English. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from English) and actual tags in the target language. The authors claim this approach outperforms previous methods on both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions
The authors claim three main contributions:
1. A new neural model that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags
2. A joint training approach using both gold and projected data
3. Superior performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a genuine problem in NLP: the scarcity of annotated data for low-resource languages. This is a significant practical challenge with real-world implications.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both simulated low-resource scenarios (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The authors provide detailed results across multiple metrics.

3. The paper includes insightful analysis of the noise transformation matrices, showing how the model learns to handle different types of noise in different languages. This provides valuable diagnostic information about the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core methodological contribution**: The "noise layer" is a simple linear transformation matrix that does not represent a significant methodological advance. Similar approaches have been used in previous work on handling noisy data in NLP (e.g., noise-robust training, error-correcting codes, or weighted averaging of weakly supervised signals). The paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from these established techniques, making the claimed novelty questionable.

2. **Insufficient validation against relevant baselines**: The paper primarily compares against Duong et al. (2014a), but fails to include more recent state-of-the-art methods for cross-lingual POS tagging. Since 2014, there have been significant advances in transfer learning, multilingual models, and low-resource learning that should be compared against. The authors' comparison to "MaxEnt Supervised" as a baseline is particularly weak given the dominance of neural approaches in recent years.

3. **Inadequate demonstration of generalizability**: The evaluation only includes 8 European languages (all closely related to English) and 2 African languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). This limited scope does not sufficiently demonstrate the method's effectiveness across diverse language families and resource scenarios. The paper claims "uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods" but provides no evidence for languages outside this narrow scope.

4. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper does not provide a compelling theoretical argument for why a linear noise transformation is the right approach to handle projection noise. It's unclear why this specific modeling choice is better than simpler alternatives like using the projected tags as a weakly supervised signal without explicit noise modeling, or using language-specific heuristics to clean the projected tags.

5. **Overstated claims without proper evidence**: The paper claims their method "is the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this is contradicted by previous work on noise modeling in distant supervision. The authors also claim "uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods" without demonstrating consistent improvement across all languages or providing statistical significance testing.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide a more thorough comparison against recent state-of-the-art methods for low-resource POS tagging (post-2014), particularly those using multilingual transfer learning or contextualized embeddings?

2. How would your method perform on languages with significantly different syntactic structures from English (e.g., SOV languages like Japanese or Korean), where cross-lingual projection noise might be substantially higher?

3. Can you provide evidence that the noise layer is necessary, or that simpler approaches (e.g., weighted averaging of projected tags, or using the projected tags as a weakly supervised signal without explicit noise modeling) would not achieve comparable results?

4. What is the theoretical justification for modeling the noise as a linear transformation matrix rather than using more complex approaches (e.g., neural noise models or language-specific heuristics)?

## Overall Assessment
This paper addresses an important problem in NLP but fails to make a compelling methodological contribution. The proposed "noise layer" is a minor variation of established techniques rather than a novel approach. The experimental validation is limited in scope and does not adequately compare against relevant baselines. While the results are promising for the specific languages tested, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that this approach represents a significant advance over established methods for low-resource POS tagging. I recommend rejection for lack of sufficient methodological novelty and insufficient validation against appropriate baselines.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent literature, a clearer articulation of the specific novelty of their approach, and a more comprehensive evaluation across diverse language families. Without these improvements, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.
