

# Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the important problem of POS tagging for low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The authors propose a novel neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in projected tags using a transformation matrix A, which maps projected tags to the true tags. The main contributions are:

1. **Explicit noise modeling**: The paper's most significant contribution is the novel approach of modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags as a transformation matrix A, rather than treating projected tags as direct supervision. This is a fundamental shift from previous work that either used projected tags directly or attempted to implicitly capture tagset correlations.

2. **Joint training framework**: The authors integrate this noise modeling into a deep learning framework that jointly trains on both gold and projected data, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected annotations.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation on both simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over prior state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated approach**: The paper identifies a key limitation in previous cross-lingual POS projection methods - that they treat projected tags as direct supervision without accounting for their inherent noise. The authors' solution of explicitly modeling this noise with a transformation matrix is both conceptually elegant and well-justified by their empirical analysis of the projected tag errors. The noise layer provides a simple yet powerful mechanism to incorporate noisy distant supervision.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The results are compelling and consistent across both simulated and real low-resource languages. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on all languages tested, with improvements ranging from 1-4% over previous methods. The analysis of the learned transformation matrix (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into the nature of the noise in different languages, showing how the model learns to handle specific tag confusions (e.g., NUM vs DET in Italian, PRT vs X in Swedish).

3. **Clear methodology and implementation**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The two-stage training approach (pretraining on gold data, then joint training with projected data) addresses a practical concern about initialization that many deep learning models face with noisy supervision.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent methods**: While the paper compares well with Duong et al. (2014a), a key weakness is the lack of comparison with more recent work in low-resource POS tagging. The field has seen significant advances since 2014, including more sophisticated neural approaches and transfer learning methods. For example, the paper doesn't compare with methods like those from Guo et al. (2016) or more recent transformer-based approaches that might have better performance on low-resource settings.

2. **Lack of ablation studies on the noise layer**: The paper shows that the noise layer improves performance, but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the noise layer itself. For instance, it would be valuable to see how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the joint training framework, or to compare against a simple heuristic for handling noisy tags (e.g., using majority vote for multi-aligned tokens).

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: The paper demonstrates that the model works well on the languages tested, but doesn't analyze cases where the model still fails. Understanding the limitations of the approach (e.g., when the projected data is extremely noisy or when languages have significant syntactic divergence) would provide important insights for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the noise matrix A is rectangular when tagsets don't match. However, the evaluation uses the universal tagset across all languages, which would make A square. Could you clarify how your approach would handle cases where languages have fundamentally different tagsets that cannot be mapped to a universal set?

2. The paper mentions that the noise layer has "a tiny handful of parameters" (p. 5). Could you provide more details about the parameter count for the noise matrix A compared to the rest of the model, and how this affects the overall model complexity and training time?

3. In Figure 4, the noise matrix for Kinyarwanda shows much more noise than for Malagasy. Given that Kinyarwanda has a smaller parallel corpus, is there a correlation between the quality/size of the parallel data and the noise level that the model must handle? Could this inform future work on resource allocation for cross-lingual projection?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the problem of low-resource POS tagging. The explicit modeling of noise in projected tags is a novel and well-executed approach that yields consistent improvements over prior work. While the paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent methods and deeper analysis of the noise modeling approach, the core contribution is significant and well-supported by the empirical results. I recommend acceptance.

The paper addresses an important problem in NLP with a clear, novel solution that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The work has clear potential for broader application to other low-resource NLP tasks that rely on noisy supervision, making it relevant to a wide audience at CoNLL.
