

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected tags rather than attempting to clean the noisy data beforehand. The key contribution is a noise layer that learns the transformation between projected tags (from high-resource language) and true tags (for low-resource language), allowing the model to learn when to trust the projected data. The authors integrate this noise layer into a BiLSTM architecture and jointly train on both gold-standard and projected data.

## Primary Contributions

1. **Explicit modeling of projection noise**: The paper introduces a novel neural architecture that explicitly models the transformation between noisy projected tags and true tags through a learnable noise matrix A. This is a fundamental shift from previous approaches that either used projected tags directly or implicitly modeled the noise through feature engineering.

2. **Simple yet effective framework**: The authors demonstrate that adding a single noise transformation layer to a standard BiLSTM can significantly improve performance without substantially increasing model complexity.

3. **Consistent empirical validation**: The approach shows consistent improvements across both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual approach**: The paper's core idea—treating noisy projected tags as a form of "noised" supervision to be modeled explicitly rather than cleaned—represents a significant conceptual shift in how we think about distant supervision. This is precisely the kind of bold, novel approach I value highly in NLP research. Previous work has either tried to clean the noise or ignored it, but this work embraces and models the noise as part of the learning process.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple languages, with the noise layer model outperforming state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The improvement on Malagasy (86.3% vs. 85.3% for the previous state-of-the-art) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% vs. 78.3%) is particularly compelling for real-world applications.

3. **Generalizability**: The authors correctly note that this approach is generalizable to other noisy supervision scenarios beyond cross-lingual projection, including crowd-sourced annotations and other forms of distant supervision. This broad applicability significantly increases the paper's impact.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of the noise transformation**: While the paper shows the noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* the noise layer works or how the learned matrix A correlates with the actual noise patterns. Understanding these relationships would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the approach.

2. **Limited exploration of failure cases**: The paper doesn't investigate scenarios where the approach might fail, such as when the parallel data is extremely poor quality or when the languages are very dissimilar. A deeper analysis of the approach's limitations would make the contribution more robust.

3. **Small real-world validation**: While the two real-world languages are meaningful, the paper's validation on real low-resource languages is limited to just two examples. More extensive validation would better demonstrate the approach's practical utility.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core contribution—explicitly modeling the noise transformation between projected tags and true tags—is novel, conceptually elegant, and has significant potential to influence how we handle noisy supervision in NLP. While the paper could benefit from deeper analysis of the noise transformation and more extensive validation on real-world low-resource languages, the conceptual novelty and consistent empirical results outweigh these limitations.

The paper represents exactly the kind of bold, novel approach I prioritize in my review process. It doesn't just incrementally improve existing methods but rethinks how we should handle noisy supervision data. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP by introducing a simple yet powerful framework that could be applied to many other tasks beyond POS tagging.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the CONLL proceedings, offering both a concrete technical contribution and a new conceptual direction for handling noisy data in NLP. The approach is well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over existing methods.
