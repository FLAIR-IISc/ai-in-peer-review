

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
The paper addresses POS tagging for low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags using a learnable transformation matrix, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust projected data. The model is a BiLSTM with an additional "noise layer" that transforms projected tags into a more accurate representation for training.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)
1. A novel approach to explicitly modeling noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags through a learnable transformation matrix.
2. A practical neural network framework that jointly trains on both gold-standard and noisy projected data.
3. Strong empirical validation across both simulated low-resource languages (eight European languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Strengths

1. **Novel noise modeling approach**: The paper makes a clear conceptual contribution by explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags rather than attempting to clean the data before training. The transformation matrix A provides a simple yet effective way to learn the mapping between projected tags and true tags. This is a significant methodological advance over previous approaches that either ignored the noise or attempted to filter it out before training. The authors provide visualizations of the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3-4) that offer valuable insights into the noise patterns across different languages.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over all baselines across multiple evaluation settings. On the eight simulated low-resource languages, the proposed method achieves 91.7% average accuracy compared to Duong et al.'s 91.3% (Table 1). On real low-resource languages, it achieves 86.3% for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, outperforming previous state-of-the-art results (Table 2). The results are robust across languages with varying levels of noise, and the improvements are consistent across both simulated and real-world settings.

3. **Clear evaluation methodology**: The paper uses a well-established experimental setup that allows for fair comparison with prior work. For simulated low-resource languages, the authors use the same data split as Duong et al. (2014a), ensuring direct comparability. For real low-resource languages, they use standard datasets from Garrette and Baldridge (2013), with detailed descriptions of the parallel and annotated data. The evaluation metrics (per-token accuracy) are standard for POS tagging tasks, and the use of universal POS tags enables cross-lingual comparison.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of the noise layer's contribution**: While the paper demonstrates that the noise layer improves performance, it lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of this component. For example, the authors don't compare the performance when the noise layer is frozen versus when it's trained jointly. It's unclear whether the noise layer is learning meaningful patterns or simply acting as a regularizer. The paper would benefit from experiments that quantify the noise layer's specific contribution to the overall performance gain.

2. **Comparison with more recent approaches**: The paper compares with Duong et al. (2014a), which was the state-of-the-art at the time of writing, but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to low-resource POS tagging. For instance, there are recent methods using multilingual BERT or other transfer learning techniques that might be relevant. The absence of these comparisons makes it difficult to assess the current relevance and competitiveness of the proposed method in the rapidly evolving field of low-resource NLP.

3. **Ambiguity in model implementation details**: The paper describes the noise layer as a K×K transformation matrix A, but lacks clarity on how this matrix is parameterized and trained. It's unclear whether A is shared across languages or learned separately for each language. The authors provide visualizations of A for different languages (Figures 3-4), but don't discuss whether the learned matrices are interpretable or consistent across languages, which would provide valuable insights into the method's behavior.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of your method vary when the transformation matrix A is initialized with different values (e.g., identity matrix vs. random matrix)? This would help understand the importance of the two-stage training process.

2. Have you considered using a more complex noise model (e.g., a neural network instead of a simple matrix transformation) to capture more complex noise patterns? If not, what are the limitations of the current approach?

3. Is the transformation matrix A shared across all languages or learned separately for each language? If learned separately, how consistent are the patterns across different languages?

4. How does your method compare with more recent approaches to low-resource POS tagging, such as those using multilingual BERT or other transfer learning techniques?
