

# Peer Review for CONLL

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by proposing a method to effectively leverage both a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors introduce a neural network model based on bidirectional LSTMs with an additional "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (which contain errors due to imperfect word alignments) and true POS tags. The model is trained jointly on both gold-standard data and the noisy projected data, learning when and how much to trust the projected labels.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Explicit noise modeling for cross-lingual projected POS tags**: The most significant contribution is the method of explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix A, rather than treating projected tags as reliable labels. This is a practical approach to handling the inherent noise in cross-lingual projection.

2. **Joint training framework for gold and projected data**: The paper demonstrates a simple but effective framework for training a model on both types of data simultaneously, which outperforms methods that use only gold data or only projected data.

3. **Empirical validation on both simulated and real low-resource languages**: The comprehensive evaluation on 8 simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) provides strong evidence of the approach's practical utility.

## Strengths

1. **High practical relevance**: The problem addressed is critically important for NLP, as most languages in the world lack sufficient annotated resources for supervised learning. The paper directly tackles this challenge with a method that could be immediately useful for practitioners working on low-resource languages. The demonstration of 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda (outperforming prior state-of-the-art) is particularly compelling for real-world impact.

2. **Clear and well-motivated methodological contribution**: The noise layer approach is simple, intuitive, and well-explained. The authors effectively demonstrate how the transformation matrix A captures meaningful patterns of noise (e.g., "nouns map to not just noun, but also adjective and number" in Malagasy, as shown in Figure 4). This is a practical solution to a common problem in cross-lingual transfer learning.

3. **Thorough evaluation with insightful analysis**: The paper provides comprehensive results across multiple languages and settings, with the noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) offering valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise for different languages. The comparison to multiple baselines, including the state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a), is well-executed.

4. **Strong empirical results**: The method consistently outperforms all baselines across both simulated and real low-resource language settings, with particularly significant improvements on the real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results are statistically meaningful, with the proposed method achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy compared to 85.3% for the previous state-of-the-art.

5. **Clear demonstration of the value of the noise layer**: The paper effectively shows that simply combining projected and gold data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj) provides only marginal improvements, while the noise layer approach (BiLSTM+noise layer) delivers substantial gains, highlighting the importance of explicitly modeling the noise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty claim**: The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this is not entirely accurate. Similar noise modeling techniques have been used in other NLP contexts, such as semi-supervised learning (e.g., using noise models for unlabeled data) and error-correcting output codes. The connection to these prior works is not sufficiently explored, making the novelty claim overstated.

2. **Lack of detailed ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why the noise layer approach works better than simply using the projected data as additional training examples. For instance, it would be valuable to see how the model's performance changes when the noise layer is removed or when the transformation matrix A is fixed to the identity matrix. The paper shows that using projected data directly (BiLSTM Projected) is worse than gold data, but doesn't explain why the noise layer specifically improves upon the union of both data sources.

3. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages**: While the evaluation on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is valuable, it's limited to just two languages. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of low-resource languages would strengthen the claims about the generalizability of the approach. The paper states that "real low-resource languages" are "Malagasy and Kinyarwanda," but doesn't provide justification for why these two were chosen over others.

4. **No comparison to more recent methods**: The paper compares to Duong et al. (2014a), which is a good baseline, but doesn't compare to more recent methods for low-resource POS tagging (e.g., from 2015-2023). For example, there have been significant advances in transfer learning and multilingual models since 2014, such as those using BERT or other transformer-based architectures. The paper's claim that it "exceeds the state-of-the-art results" is questionable given that it doesn't compare to these newer approaches.

5. **Lack of computational efficiency analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed method compared to baselines. It's not clear if the noise layer significantly increases training or inference time, which would be an important consideration for practical applications, especially in low-resource settings where computational resources might be limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to explicitly model noise in projected tags. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with related work that uses noise modeling in other NLP contexts (e.g., semi-supervised learning, error-correcting output codes), and clarify the specific novelty of your approach?

2. The results in Table 1 show that the noise layer approach consistently outperforms the simple union of gold and projected data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj). Could you provide more insight into why the noise layer is so effective, rather than just stating that it "encodes the mapping between 'clean' tags and the kinds of noisy tags produced from projection"?

3. The evaluation on real low-resource languages is limited to just two languages. Could you discuss why these two were chosen, and whether the approach would generalize to other low-resource languages with different typological features?

4. The paper doesn't compare to more recent methods for low-resource POS tagging (e.g., using multilingual BERT or other transformer-based approaches). Given that these methods have become state-of-the-art for many low-resource NLP tasks, could you provide a comparison with these newer approaches?

5. Could you provide information on the computational cost of your method compared to the baselines (e.g., training time, memory requirements)? This would be valuable for practitioners considering implementing your approach.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a practical and effective solution to a significant problem in NLP: POS tagging for low-resource languages. The method is well-motivated, well-executed, and produces strong empirical results. While the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, the practical contributions are clear and valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the need for a more accurate novelty claim and a more comprehensive comparison to recent methods.
