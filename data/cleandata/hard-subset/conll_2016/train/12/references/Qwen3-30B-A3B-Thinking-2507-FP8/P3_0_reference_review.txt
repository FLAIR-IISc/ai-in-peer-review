

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by proposing a neural network model that effectively combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The authors' key contribution is a novel "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (which are noisy) and true tags, rather than treating projected tags as reliable supervision.

As a reviewer, I see the main contributions as follows (in order of importance):

1. **Explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected tags**: The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction of a learnable noise transformation matrix that explicitly models how projected tags differ from true tags. This is a fundamentally different approach from previous work, which either tried to clean the projected data before training or treated it as reliable supervision.

2. **Strong empirical validation across multiple settings**: The authors provide comprehensive evaluation across 8 simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Insightful analysis of noise patterns**: The paper includes detailed visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices, revealing language-specific noise patterns (e.g., which tags are confused with which others), which provides valuable insights into how the model adapts to different languages.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision**: The paper's core contribution is a significant methodological advance. Previous work on cross-lingual POS projection has treated the projected tags as either reliable or required external cleaning procedures. The authors' insight that we should "learn when and how much to trust the projected data" is powerful and directly addresses a fundamental limitation of existing approaches. The noise layer is elegantly simple yet effective, requiring only a K×K matrix to model the transformation between projected and true tags.

2. **Strong empirical results with comprehensive evaluation**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource languages. In the 8 simulated languages, their method achieves 91.7% accuracy compared to Duong et al.'s 91.3%. For real low-resource languages, they achieve 86.3% for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, which significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (85.3% and 78.3%, respectively). The results are not just marginally better but represent a clear and consistent improvement across multiple languages.

3. **Detailed analysis of noise patterns**: Figure 3 and Figure 4 provide valuable insights into how the model learns to handle specific language-specific noise. The authors show that the model learns to trust certain tags (strong diagonal) while learning to ignore or adjust for specific confusions (off-diagonal elements). For example, in Kinyarwanda, the model learns to map nouns to adjectives and numbers but not to pronouns, which is consistent with the language's characteristics. This analysis goes beyond just reporting accuracy and provides meaningful insights into the model's behavior.

4. **Practical implementation and training procedure**: The paper details a two-stage training process that addresses a practical challenge in training such models (the need to initialize the noise matrix). First, they pretrain on gold data, then jointly train on both gold and projected data. This is a pragmatic approach that makes the method more accessible to practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares with Duong et al. (2014a) as the state-of-the-art, but there have been significant advances in low-resource NLP since 2014. Modern approaches like multilingual transfer learning (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) and more sophisticated cross-lingual transfer methods have since emerged. For example, the recent XLM-R model has shown strong performance on low-resource POS tagging tasks. The authors should compare with these more recent methods to better establish the novelty and relative contribution of their approach.

2. **Limited analysis of failure modes and language-specific limitations**: While the paper provides good analysis of noise patterns, it lacks a thorough examination of when the approach fails or where it struggles. For example, the paper notes that Kinyarwanda has more noise than Malagasy, but doesn't explore why this is the case or what specific linguistic properties of Kinyarwanda make it more challenging. A deeper analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Insufficient justification for why the noise layer is necessary**: The paper claims that their approach "explicitly models the noise," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to show why this is better than simpler alternatives. For instance, they could have compared their noise layer with a model that simply uses a different loss function for projected data, or a model that uses a more sophisticated architecture to handle noisy data. Ablation studies showing how much the noise layer contributes to the performance gain would strengthen their argument.

4. **Limited discussion of scalability to languages without parallel data**: The paper assumes the availability of parallel data between the low-resource language and English, which is "more plentiful" but not always available. For truly low-resource languages with no parallel data at all, this approach wouldn't work. The paper doesn't discuss this limitation or how the approach might be extended to languages without parallel data, which is a significant gap for a paper addressing low-resource NLP.

5. **The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the noise layer**: While the noise layer is simple (a K×K matrix), it adds a small but non-trivial amount of parameters and computation. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the noise layer or how it might scale to larger tagsets or more complex tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare with more recent multilingual transfer learning methods like XLM-R or mBERT, which have shown strong performance on low-resource tasks? Have you considered using these as baselines, and if not, why not?

2. The paper mentions that for languages with mismatched tagsets, the noise transformation matrix A would be rectangular. How do you handle this case in practice, and what are the limitations of this approach when the tagsets are significantly different between languages?

3. In the real-world experiments, you use Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. Can you provide more details about the quality of the parallel data for these languages? How does the quality of the parallel data (e.g., sentence alignment accuracy, translation quality) affect the performance of your model?

4. The paper states that "the model can learn to effectively ignore the projected verb tag, through associating all tags with verbs." Can you provide a specific example from your results showing this behavior, and how it compares to other methods that might handle this issue differently?

5. The paper claims that "our approach is general, and is likely to prove useful for exploiting other noisy annotations such as distant supervision and crowd-sourced annotations." Can you provide preliminary evidence or examples of how this approach might be applied to other types of noisy supervision?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a thoughtful and well-executed approach to handling noisy cross-lingual projected data for low-resource POS tagging. The core contribution of explicitly modeling the noise through a learnable transformation matrix is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are strong across multiple languages.

However, the paper's main weakness is the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods in low-resource NLP. The authors should address this in their revision by comparing with modern transfer learning approaches. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of failure modes and limitations of the approach.

With these revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP and would be suitable for publication at CONLL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
