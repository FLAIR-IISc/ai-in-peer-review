

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both small amounts of gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected data from a high-resource language. The key innovation is a BiLSTM-based model with an additional "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (which are noisy) and true gold tags. The model learns a K×K transformation matrix A that encodes how to map the projected tags to the correct tags, allowing the model to "learn when and how much to trust" the projected data. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), reporting consistent improvements over previous methods.

## Main Contributions
1. **Explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags** through a learnable transformation matrix, rather than treating projected tags as clean data or applying heuristics to clean them.
2. **Integration of this noise modeling into a deep neural network framework** that can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation on both gold and projected data.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation** demonstrating consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource language settings.

## Strengths
1. **Novel and well-motivated approach to noise modeling**: The paper correctly identifies that cross-lingual projection produces noisy tags with predictable patterns (e.g., certain tags being consistently misprojected), and the authors propose a principled way to learn these patterns rather than using ad-hoc cleaning heuristics. The noise layer is a simple but effective addition to the standard BiLSTM architecture, with minimal additional parameters (a single K×K matrix) that provide significant performance gains.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation shows consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource languages. The results on Malagasy (86.3% accuracy) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% accuracy) exceed previous state-of-the-art results by a notable margin (3-4% absolute improvement). The analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into the nature of the noise patterns across different languages.

3. **Clear theoretical justification**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of previous approaches (treating projected tags as clean data or using implicit feature sets to capture tag correlations) and provides a compelling argument for why explicit noise modeling is superior. The training objective and model architecture are well-motivated and properly grounded in the problem.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited evaluation on truly divergent language pairs**: The simulated low-resource evaluation uses European languages (Danish, Dutch, German, etc.) that share significant linguistic similarities with English. The real-world evaluation covers only two languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which are both relatively close to English in terms of linguistic structure. The paper doesn't evaluate on languages with dramatically different grammatical structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Japanese), where the noise patterns might be substantially different.

2. **Lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares primarily with Duong et al. (2014), which is now outdated. Since 2014, there have been significant advances in low-resource NLP, including methods using multilingual BERT, transfer learning, and more sophisticated cross-lingual projection techniques. The authors should have compared with these more recent approaches to establish the current relevance of their contribution.

3. **Insufficient explanation of "trust" learning**: While the paper claims the model learns "when and how much to trust the projected data," the mechanism for this is not clearly explained. The noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) show strong diagonal elements, suggesting the model largely trusts the projected tags, but doesn't explain how the model learns to distrust certain tags (e.g., why some off-diagonal elements are large). The paper should clarify how the model distinguishes between reliable and unreliable projected tags.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would your method perform if the high-resource language was not English (e.g., French for African languages)? Have you evaluated on language pairs where the high-resource language is not English?

2. Could you compare your approach with more recent low-resource POS tagging methods, such as those using multilingual BERT or transfer learning techniques?

3. The noise layer appears to primarily learn to trust the diagonal (i.e., assume projected tags are mostly correct), with some off-diagonal elements capturing specific errors. Could you elaborate on how the model would learn to completely ignore certain projected tags (e.g., if verbs are often misaligned)?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated, novel approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual projected data for low-resource POS tagging. The explicit noise modeling through a learnable transformation matrix is a significant contribution that addresses a real problem in the field. The empirical results are strong and well-documented, with clear improvements over previous methods.

However, the evaluation could be strengthened by including more diverse language pairs and comparing with more recent state-of-the-art methods. The explanation of how the model "learns to trust" the projected data also needs more detail.

Given the strong technical contribution and solid evaluation, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of low-resource NLP, particularly for POS tagging, and the approach could be extended to other NLP tasks with noisy supervision.
