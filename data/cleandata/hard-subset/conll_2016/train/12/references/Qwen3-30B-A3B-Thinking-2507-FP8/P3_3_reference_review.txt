

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for low-resource POS tagging that effectively leverages cross-lingual projection from high-resource languages while explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags. The authors make three key contributions: (1) a principled approach to modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags through a learnable transformation matrix; (2) a joint training framework that simultaneously optimizes for both gold-standard and projected data; and (3) comprehensive evaluation across 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual novelty in handling noisy distant supervision**: The paper's core contribution—explicitly modeling the noise in projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix—is both novel and conceptually sound. Previous work either treated projected tags as direct supervision (with poor results) or attempted to clean them before training (with limited success). The authors' approach recognizes that the noise is systematic rather than random, which is a significant methodological advance that could be applied to other noisy supervision scenarios beyond POS tagging.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The results demonstrate consistent improvements across all languages tested. The 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda represent significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art (85.3% and 78.3%, respectively). The performance gains are particularly pronounced on Kinyarwanda, where parallel data is more limited, suggesting the approach is especially valuable in the most challenging low-resource scenarios.

3. **Insightful noise pattern analysis**: The visualization of the noise transformation matrix A provides valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. For example, the analysis shows how the model learns to ignore certain tag combinations (like NUM vs DET in Italian) and how noise patterns differ between languages (greater noise in Kinyarwanda correlating with lower performance). This analysis goes beyond simple accuracy metrics to provide meaningful understanding of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited architectural innovation**: While the noise layer is novel, the base model is a standard BiLSTM, which is well-established for POS tagging. The authors don't sufficiently justify why BiLSTM is necessary for this specific problem rather than other sequence models (e.g., CRFs, transformers). The paper would be strengthened by a comparison with a simpler baseline model to isolate the contribution of the noise layer.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper demonstrates that the noise layer improves performance but lacks critical ablation studies to determine what aspects of the approach are most impactful. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer itself versus the joint training framework? Without these studies, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of the key innovation.

3. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address scenarios where the approach might fail. For instance, how would the method perform with extremely poor word alignments (e.g., from machine translation rather than human translation)? The authors note that performance correlates with parallel data quality (better results for Malagasy with more parallel data), but don't explore the threshold where the method might fail or how to handle such cases.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the noise layer perform if the parallel data was significantly noisier (e.g., using machine-translated parallel data instead of human-translated data)? Have you considered this scenario?

2. The paper mentions that the approach is generalizable to other noisy supervision scenarios, but doesn't provide examples. Could you elaborate on how this would work for other tasks, such as named entity recognition with noisy crowdsourced annotations?

3. Given the recent success of transformer-based models for POS tagging in high-resource settings, why did you choose to use a BiLSTM rather than a transformer architecture for your base model? How would the noise layer integrate with a transformer architecture?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful contribution to low-resource NLP by providing a principled method for handling noisy distant supervision. The paper is clearly written, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. While the architectural choice could be better justified and more ablation studies would strengthen the claims, the core idea of explicitly modeling noise in projected annotations is novel and valuable. The work provides a useful framework that could be extended to other low-resource NLP tasks and noisy supervision scenarios. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
