

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a neural model for low-resource POS tagging that incorporates both gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected data. The main contribution is a noise transformation layer that models the mapping between projected tags and true tags. The authors claim this approach outperforms prior methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in low-resource NLP, where cross-lingual projection is commonly used but suffers from noise issues.
2. The proposed noise layer conceptually makes sense for handling the mismatch between projected tags and true tags.
3. The evaluation on both simulated (8 European languages) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) provides a reasonable validation of the approach.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Technical Errors in Formulation (Major Issue)
The paper contains fundamental mathematical errors that undermine the core contribution. Equation (2) states:
"p(Ỹt = j|xt, θ, A) = softmax (∑ i ai,jot,i)"

This is mathematically incorrect. The correct formulation should be:
"p(Ỹt = j|xt, θ, A) = softmax(∑_i ai,j ot,i)"

The current notation implies softmax is applied to each term ai,jot,i, which doesn't make sense. The equation as written would be mathematically meaningless. This error appears in both the main text and the figure caption, suggesting a severe lack of attention to detail.

### 2. Implausibly High Learning Rate (Major Issue)
The paper states: "We set learning rate as 1.0 and use stochastic gradient descent model to learn the parameters." A learning rate of 1.0 is extremely high for neural network training and would typically cause divergence rather than convergence. This is a critical methodological flaw that invalidates the reported results. The authors should have used a standard learning rate (e.g., 0.001-0.1) and provided justification for their unusual choice.

### 3. Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting
Multiple inconsistent terms are used without explanation: "output noising" vs. "noise transformation" vs. "noising layer." The paper also contains numerous formatting errors:
- "wildly successful" (should be "widely successful")
- "to to inform" (typo)
- "NigerCongo" (should be "Niger-Congo")
- Inconsistent quotation marks ("distant" vs. 'distant')
- "labelling" vs. "labeling" (American vs. British English inconsistency)

### 4. Lack of Comparison with Recent Work
The paper only compares with Duong et al. (2014a), ignoring more recent approaches to cross-lingual POS tagging. For example, recent work using multilingual BERT or other transfer learning techniques would provide a more meaningful comparison. The claim that "our work is the first to explicitly model the noise" is unfounded given recent advances in noise-aware learning.

### 5. Insufficient Experimental Detail
Key implementation details are missing:
- No specific version of the Stanford POS tagger used
- No parameters for Giza++ alignment
- No explanation of how the 1,000-token gold datasets were selected (randomly? balanced across tags?)
- No statistical significance tests for the performance improvements

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide the correct mathematical formulation for the noise transformation layer, addressing the error in Equation (2)?
2. Why was a learning rate of 1.0 used, and how did you ensure convergence with this value?
3. Can you provide details about the specific implementation of the Stanford POS tagger used for English tagging?
4. Why weren't more recent methods (e.g., multilingual BERT-based approaches) included in the comparison?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem addressed is important and the core idea has potential, the paper contains critical mathematical errors, implausible methodological choices, and numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the work. The technical errors in the equations (particularly Equation 2) are severe enough to make the paper uninterpretable. The claim of being "the first to explicitly model the noise" is also unfounded given the current literature.

The paper's weaknesses significantly outweigh its strengths. The authors have not demonstrated a working implementation of their proposed method, as the mathematical formulation is incorrect and the learning rate choice is implausible. These are not minor issues but fundamental flaws that prevent the work from being taken seriously.

The paper would benefit from substantial revision, including correcting the mathematical formulation, using a standard learning rate, providing implementation details, and comparing with more recent approaches. However, as submitted, it lacks the technical rigor required for publication at CONLL.
