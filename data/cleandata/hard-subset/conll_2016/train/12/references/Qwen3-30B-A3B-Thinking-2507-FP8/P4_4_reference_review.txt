

# Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from English. The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a "noise transformation" layer. The main contributions are:

1. A novel neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags via a learnable transformation matrix, rather than treating projected tags as clean labels or using heuristics to clean them.
2. A two-stage training procedure that first pretrains on gold data before jointly training with projected data, addressing the issue of poor initialization when using noisy data.
3. Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated low-resource scenarios (8 European languages with only 1,000 tokens of gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Modeling of Projection Noise**: The paper's most significant contribution is the explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected tags as a learnable transformation matrix. Previous work either treated projected tags as clean labels (leading to poor performance) or used heuristics to clean them before training. The authors' approach of incorporating this noise modeling directly into the neural network architecture is novel and well-motivated. The results (Table 1) show that their method consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) across all eight simulated languages, with improvements of 0.2-1.6% in accuracy. This demonstrates the effectiveness of their noise modeling approach.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation on both simulated low-resource scenarios (using 8 European languages with only 1,000 tokens of gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The evaluation is well-designed, using standard datasets and metrics (per-token accuracy with universal POS tags). The results (Tables 1 and 2) show consistent improvements over all baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art. The analysis of the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provides additional insight into how the model handles noise, making the results more interpretable and convincing.

3. **Technical Soundness of the Approach**: The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their model (Equation 2) and training objective (Equation 3), which is well-grounded in standard machine learning principles. The two-stage training procedure addresses a practical issue (poor initialization with noisy data) and is a sensible approach. The model's architecture (BiLSTM with noise layer) is well-motivated for POS tagging tasks, and the implementation details are standard and appropriate for the task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Architecture**: While the noise transformation layer is a novel contribution, the core architecture (BiLSTM) is not new to POS tagging. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between the novelty of the noise layer versus the standard BiLSTM component. Many recent POS tagging papers use BiLSTMs, so the main innovation is the noise layer, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate why this is a significant departure from prior work beyond the specific application to cross-lingual projection.

2. **Lack of Ablation Study on Noise Layer**: The paper demonstrates that the noise layer improves performance over direct use of projected data, but doesn't provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the noise layer versus other components. For example, it's unclear whether the improvement comes primarily from the noise layer or from the two-stage training procedure. A controlled ablation study would strengthen the paper's claims about the necessity of the noise layer.

3. **Evaluation on Real Low-Resource Languages is Limited**: While the authors evaluate on two real low-resource languages, the datasets are small (Malagasy: 5,294 tokens, Kinyarwanda: 4,882 tokens), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient statistical significance tests for these results. The performance gains (Table 2) are modest (1-2% improvement), which might not be statistically significant given the small dataset sizes. The paper would be stronger with a more rigorous statistical analysis of the results on the real low-resource languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details on why the two-stage training procedure is necessary, and what happens if you train the full model end-to-end from random initialization? Is the improvement from the two-stage training significant compared to the noise layer?

2. In Figure 3, the noise transformation matrices show strong diagonals, suggesting that most tags are trusted. However, the authors claim their approach "learns when and how much to trust the projected data." Could you elaborate on how the model learns to trust some tags more than others, and what specific patterns of noise it learns to handle?

3. For the real low-resource languages, the authors report improvements over prior work, but the datasets are small. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda to confirm that the improvements are not due to random fluctuations?
