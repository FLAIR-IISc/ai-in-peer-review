

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging for low-resource languages by proposing a neural network model that jointly leverages small amounts of gold-standard annotated data and noisy cross-lingual projected data. The authors introduce a novel "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags through a learnable matrix, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. The explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected POS tags through a learnable transformation matrix, which represents a principled approach to handling noisy distant supervision in POS tagging.
2. The thorough experimental evaluation across both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
3. The insightful visualization and interpretation of the learned noise transformation matrices, which reveal patterns of tag confusion and trust that provide valuable linguistic insights.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and principled approach to handling noisy distant supervision**: The paper identifies a key limitation in existing cross-lingual POS tagging methods—treating projected tags as if they were reliable—and proposes a clean solution by explicitly modeling the noise. The noise layer is a simple yet powerful addition to the standard BiLSTM architecture that effectively learns the mapping between projected and true tags. This is a significant contribution to the literature on low-resource NLP, as it provides a generalizable approach to handling noisy supervision.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation settings**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods across both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results are statistically significant (e.g., 91.7% vs. 91.3% on the 8-language average), and the authors provide detailed error analysis through the noise transformation matrices, which helps interpret why the method works.

3. **Clear and insightful interpretation of the noise layer**: The visualization of the transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) is particularly valuable, as it reveals specific patterns of tag confusion (e.g., NUM vs. DET in Italian, PRT vs. X in Swedish). This goes beyond simply reporting accuracy numbers and provides meaningful insights into the model's behavior and the nature of the noise in projected tags.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate comparison with a fair baseline**: The paper compares against Duong et al. (2014a), but the baseline uses a maximum entropy classifier while the authors' model is a BiLSTM. A more meaningful comparison would be to compare the same neural architecture with and without the noise layer, rather than comparing different model types. The authors could have demonstrated that the noise layer provides improvements *within* the BiLSTM framework, which would strengthen their claim about the contribution of the noise layer itself.

2. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages**: The paper evaluates on only two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to establish broad applicability. The authors should have included more languages to better support their claim that the method generalizes well to diverse low-resource settings. The current evaluation is more convincing for the simulated languages than for the real-world application.

3. **Lack of discussion on computational cost**: The paper does not address the computational implications of adding the noise layer. While the matrix A has size K×K (which is small for POS tagsets), it's unclear how this affects training time, model size, or inference speed. For practical deployment in low-resource settings, these factors are important considerations that the authors should address.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to explicitly model the noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. However, there is prior work on handling noisy supervision in other NLP tasks (e.g., relation extraction with noisy distant supervision). How does this work differ from those approaches, and why is it novel specifically for POS tagging?

2. The authors report that their method achieves 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda. What is the absolute improvement over the previous state-of-the-art for these languages, and how much of this improvement is due to the noise layer specifically (vs. using a BiLSTM instead of a maxent model)?

3. The paper mentions that the noise layer can handle mismatching tagsets. Could the authors provide a specific example of how this works in practice, particularly when the tagsets differ significantly between languages?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to improving low-resource POS tagging through explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected data. The core idea is novel, the experimental results are strong, and the insights from the noise transformation matrices are valuable. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by including more low-resource languages and providing a fairer comparison with a baseline that uses the same neural architecture. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the fairer model comparison and expanded evaluation on low-resource languages. The paper's contribution is significant and addresses an important problem in low-resource NLP, and with the suggested improvements, it would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
