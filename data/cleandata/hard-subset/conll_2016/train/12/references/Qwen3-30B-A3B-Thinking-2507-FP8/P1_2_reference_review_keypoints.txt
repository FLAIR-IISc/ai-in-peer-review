- Proposes a neural POS tagger for low-resource languages that jointly trains on small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projections using an explicit “noise layer.”  
- Empirically demonstrates consistent improvements over prior methods on simulated and real low-resource languages.  
- Analyzes learned noise transformation matrices to reveal which projected tag transitions are trusted or distrusted.

- Strong empirical validation across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages with clear accuracy gains.  
- Clear, well-motivated problem statement showing direct use of projected data can be harmful.  
- Insightful visualizations of noise patterns that explain model behavior beyond numeric results.

- Novelty claim is questionable because similar error/noise models exist in other NLP contexts and need clearer differentiation.  
- Lacks ablation studies isolating the noise layer’s contribution versus other model components.  
- Limited evaluation using only English as the source language, leaving unclear performance with non-English or more distant source languages.  
- Does not discuss computational cost or resource requirements of the two-stage training relative to baselines.

- How does this noise-layer approach differ from error models in machine translation or other noise-robust NLP techniques, and could those be applied here?  
- What explains marginal improvements on some languages (e.g., Danish) and how does parallel corpus size affect gains?  
- How does the amount of parallel data influence the noise layer’s effectiveness and is there a minimum threshold for benefit?

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, pending clearer novelty positioning and more analysis of the noise layer’s contribution.
