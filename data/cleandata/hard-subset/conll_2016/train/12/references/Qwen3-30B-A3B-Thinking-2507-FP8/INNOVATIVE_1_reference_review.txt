

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture for low-resource POS tagging that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags. The key innovation is a "noise transformation" layer that learns how projected tags (which are often inaccurate due to alignment errors and syntactic divergence) map to true tags. This approach allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the noisy projected data, rather than attempting to clean it with heuristics as previous work did.

## Main Contributions
1. **Explicit noise modeling**: The first work to treat the noise in cross-lingual projected tags as a learnable transformation within a deep neural network architecture.
2. **Effective utilization of noisy data**: Demonstrates that this approach consistently outperforms previous methods across both simulated low-resource languages and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).
3. **Interpretable noise patterns**: Shows that the learned noise transformation matrix reveals meaningful patterns of confusion between tags, providing insights into the nature of projection noise.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual novelty and boldness**: This is the paper's strongest point. Rather than trying to fix noisy projected tags (as most prior work does), the authors propose to model the noise as an integral part of the learning process. This represents a fundamental shift in how we should think about distant supervision, moving from "how to clean noisy data" to "how to learn from noisy data." This conceptual innovation is rare in NLP and has potential applicability beyond POS tagging.

2. **Consistent empirical gains**: The approach consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods across multiple languages and settings. The results on real low-resource languages (Malagasy 86.3% vs. previous 85.3%, Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs. previous 78.3%) are particularly compelling, demonstrating practical value beyond simulation.

3. **Interpretability of noise patterns**: The visualizations of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3-4) reveal meaningful patterns of tag confusion (e.g., NUM vs. DET in Italian, NOUN vs. PRON in Spanish). This goes beyond mere performance gains to provide new insights into the nature of cross-lingual projection noise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of the noise layer specifically. How much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture? The paper compares the full model to previous methods but doesn't show what happens when the noise layer is removed or replaced with alternatives.

2. **Limited analysis of noise patterns**: While the noise matrices are visualized, there's no deeper analysis of *why* certain tags are confused (e.g., why nouns map to adjectives in Malagasy but not in other languages). This limits the paper's contribution to understanding the problem.

3. **No comparison with other noise modeling approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with alternative noise modeling techniques (e.g., using uncertainty estimates, different noise models, or Bayesian approaches). This makes it difficult to assess whether the authors' specific approach is truly novel or just one of many possible ways to model noise.

4. **Limited evidence for generalizability**: The paper claims the technique is "general, and is likely to prove useful for exploiting other noisy annotations" but provides no evidence beyond POS tagging. This is a significant claim that would be strengthened by at least one additional experiment on a different task.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture alone? How much does the noise layer specifically contribute to the performance gains?

2. The noise matrix for Swedish (Figure 3) shows strong confusion between PRT and X. Can you provide linguistic explanations for this particular confusion pattern?

3. Have you considered comparing your noise modeling approach with other probabilistic approaches to handling noisy labels (e.g., using label smoothing or uncertainty estimates)?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel conceptual approach to handling noisy supervision in low-resource NLP tasks. The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise transformation as a learnable parameter is bold, elegant, and conceptually significant. While the methodological execution is straightforward, the novelty of the approach outweighs the minor limitations in analysis and ablation studies. The consistent empirical gains across multiple languages, combined with the interpretability of the noise patterns, make this a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a significant new direction for handling noisy supervision in low-resource settings, which has broad implications for NLP beyond just POS tagging. The minor weaknesses noted above are common in early-stage novel work and can be addressed in future extensions.
