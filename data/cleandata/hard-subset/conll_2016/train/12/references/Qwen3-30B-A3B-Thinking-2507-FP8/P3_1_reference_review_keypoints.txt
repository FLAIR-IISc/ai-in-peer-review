- Proposes a neural BiLSTM model that combines small gold-standard POS data with noisy cross-lingual projected tags via a learnable noise transformation matrix A.

- Introduces explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable K×K transformation matrix rather than treating noisy tags as unmodeled nuisance.

- Demonstrates effective integration of gold and projected data in a unified architecture, yielding consistent improvements across languages.

- Provides strong empirical validation on 8 simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

- Clear problem statement showing projected tags alone can hurt performance compared to gold data despite larger size.

- Novel noise layer with sound mathematical formulation and visualizations that show meaningful learned noise patterns.

- Achieves competitive real-world results (Malagasy 86.3%, Kinyarwanda 82.5%), outperforming prior state-of-the-art by notable margins.

- Visualization and analysis of the transformation matrix A give insight into which tag mappings are trusted or ignored.

- Uses an appropriate experimental design and strong baselines with consistent small gold-data setup (1,000 tokens).

- Lacks sufficient ablation studies to isolate the specific contribution of the noise layer versus model architecture (e.g., BiLSTM vs. MaxEnt).

- Limited analysis of failure cases and when the noise layer underperforms (e.g., Swedish), reducing understanding of limitations.

- Does not compare to more recent multilingual transfer methods (e.g., mBERT) or discuss how the approach relates to such advances.

- Insufficient discussion on sensitivity to initialization and the effects of the two-stage training procedure on final performance.

- Potential overfitting concerns given only 1,000 gold tokens and no presented evidence (development curves or cross-validation).

- Unclear choices around the noise matrix size and whether alternative, more parameter-efficient parameterizations were considered.

- Asks for details on how the switch point in the two-stage training was determined and whether it was development-based or fixed.

- Requests comparison to simpler strategies like learnable weighting of gold vs. projected data to assess if the noise layer’s complexity is necessary.

- Seeks discussion on generalization when projecting from English to typologically different target languages (e.g., different word orders).

- Questions how parallel data size (e.g., 52k vs 1,231k tokens) impacted performance and what minimum parallel data is required for effectiveness.

- Overall recommendation: novel, well-motivated, and empirically strong approach; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address ablations and limitations.
