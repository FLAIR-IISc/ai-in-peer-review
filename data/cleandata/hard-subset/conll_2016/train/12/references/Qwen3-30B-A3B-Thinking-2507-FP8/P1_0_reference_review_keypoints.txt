- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger for low-resource languages that jointly trains on small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projected data using a learnable noise layer to model projection errors.

- Contribution 1: Introduces an explicit learnable noise transformation matrix A to model errors in projected POS tags.

- Contribution 2: Presents a simple joint training framework that leverages both gold-standard and projected noisy data.

- Contribution 3: Validates the approach on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

- Strength 1: Addresses a highly relevant problem for NLP with practical real-world impact and strong reported accuracies on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

- Strength 2: Presents a clear, intuitive, and well-explained noise-layer methodology that captures meaningful error patterns.

- Strength 3: Provides thorough evaluation across multiple languages with insightful analysis of noise matrices and comparisons to baselines.

- Strength 4: Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over baselines, including outperforming prior state-of-the-art on target languages.

- Strength 5: Clearly shows the added value of explicitly modeling noise versus naively combining projected and gold data.

- Weakness 1: Novelty claim is overstated; similar noise-modeling ideas exist in other NLP contexts and are not sufficiently cited or compared.

- Weakness 2: Lacks detailed ablation studies to explain precisely why the noise layer outperforms simpler approaches.

- Weakness 3: Evaluation on real low-resource languages is limited to only two languages, limiting claims of generalizability.

- Weakness 4: No comparison to more recent transfer-learning or multilingual transformer methods (e.g., multilingual BERT), making the state-of-the-art claim questionable.

- Weakness 5: Missing analysis of computational cost and efficiency (training time, memory) relative to baselines.

- Question 1: Please compare and contextualize your noise modeling with prior work in semi-supervised learning and error-correcting output codes to clarify novelty.

- Question 2: Can you provide deeper analysis or diagnostics explaining why the noise layer yields substantial gains over simply combining datasets?

- Question 3: Why were Malagasy and Kinyarwanda chosen, and how likely is the method to generalize across typologically diverse low-resource languages?

- Question 4: Please compare your method to more recent low-resource POS-tagging approaches, especially transformer-based multilingual models.

- Question 5: Provide details on computational costs (training/inference time, memory) to assess practicality for low-resource settings.

- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to temper novelty claims and add comparisons/analyses regarding related work, ablations, generalizability, and computational cost.
