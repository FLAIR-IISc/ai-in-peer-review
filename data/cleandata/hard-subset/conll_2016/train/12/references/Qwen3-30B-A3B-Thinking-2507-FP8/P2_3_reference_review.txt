

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that effectively combines small amounts of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The core innovation is a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (which are often inaccurate due to word alignment errors and syntactic divergence) and true tags. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) studying the noise patterns in projected data and modeling them with an additional layer, (2) integrating this into a deep neural network for joint training, and (3) demonstrating superior performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel noise modeling approach**: The most significant contribution is the explicit modeling of the noise transformation between projected tags and true tags through a learnable matrix A. This represents a fundamentally different approach to handling noisy distant supervision compared to previous work that either tried to clean the data before training or ignored the noise entirely.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across diverse languages**: The paper provides strong empirical validation across 8 European languages (simulated low-resource setting) and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned noise patterns**: The visualizations of the noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights into how the model learns to trust different tag mappings, which adds significant interpretability to the approach.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical motivation for the noise layer**: The authors convincingly argue that previous methods fail to adequately address the noise in projected data, either by treating it as clean data (leading to performance degradation) or by attempting to clean it before training (which is often difficult to do effectively). Their approach of learning the noise transformation as part of the model is theoretically sound and addresses a genuine gap in the literature.

2. **Consistent and meaningful improvements**: The results are impressive and consistent across both simulated and real low-resource languages. For instance, on the real low-resource languages, the authors achieve 86.3% accuracy for Malagasy (vs. 85.3% for the state-of-the-art) and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda (vs. 78.3%). These improvements are not marginal but represent significant gains in a challenging setting.

3. **Clear analysis of noise patterns**: The visualization of the noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) is particularly valuable. It shows how the model learns to trust certain tag mappings while ignoring others, providing concrete evidence that the noise layer is effectively learning to "trust" the projected data appropriately. For example, the matrices show that in Malagasy, nouns map not just to nouns but also to adjectives and numbers, but never to pronouns.

4. **Well-structured experimental design**: The paper appropriately separates simulated experiments (using European languages with limited gold data) from real low-resource experiments (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), providing a more comprehensive evaluation than many previous works that only use one setting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to recent neural approaches**: While the noise layer is novel, the overall architecture is relatively straightforward (BiLSTM with an additional layer). The paper doesn't sufficiently contrast with recent neural approaches to low-resource NLP (e.g., transfer learning, multi-task learning, or approaches using attention mechanisms for noisy data). This weakens the claim of novelty.

2. **Lack of ablation studies on the noise layer**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies that isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement (e.g., 91.7% vs 91.3% on the 8 languages) comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture itself. A comparison between BiLSTM+noise layer and a standard BiLSTM trained on the same data would strengthen the argument for the noise layer's contribution.

3. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where the approach fails or where the noise layer doesn't provide significant improvement. Understanding these limitations would help clarify the scope and boundaries of the method's applicability.

4. **Small parallel corpora for real low-resource languages**: The parallel data for Malagasy (100k sentences) and Kinyarwanda (11k sentences) is quite limited. The paper doesn't discuss how the size of the parallel corpus affects the performance of their method, which is a crucial consideration for real-world low-resource applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your noise layer compare to recent approaches that use attention mechanisms to handle noisy data in low-resource settings? For example, could your approach be combined with attention mechanisms for potentially better performance?

2. Could you provide more details about the two-stage training process (pretraining on annotated data followed by joint training)? Why is this necessary, and what happens if you train the entire model end-to-end from random initialization?

3. The paper states that "the noise layer provides a better use of the noisy projected data," but it's not clear how much of the improvement is specifically due to the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture. Could you provide an ablation study comparing your full model with a standard BiLSTM trained on the same data?

4. For the real low-resource languages, the parallel data is much smaller than for the simulated languages. How does the size of the parallel corpus affect the performance of your model, and what is the minimum amount of parallel data needed for your method to be effective?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP by introducing a novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision. The noise layer is well-motivated, the results are impressive and consistent across multiple languages, and the analysis of the learned noise patterns provides valuable insights. While the paper has some weaknesses regarding novelty comparison and lack of ablation studies, these do not significantly undermine the core contribution.

The paper meets the CONLL review criteria well, with clear contributions, strong empirical validation, and insightful analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above, particularly regarding the novelty of the approach compared to recent neural methods and the ablation study for the noise layer. The work has clear potential for broader impact in low-resource NLP applications.
