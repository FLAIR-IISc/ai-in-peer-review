

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy distant supervision obtained through cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags using a K×K transformation matrix A, which learns when and how much to trust the projected data. The model uses a bidirectional LSTM with an additional "noise layer" that transforms the predicted tag distribution from the LSTM to match the distribution of the noisy projected tags. The approach is evaluated on both simulated low-resource settings (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over prior state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Explicit modeling of the noise transformation between projected tags and true tags** - This is the core technical contribution, where the authors propose a K×K matrix A to model the mapping between projected tags and true tags, rather than using the projected tags directly or implicitly capturing correlations.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages** - The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple languages, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Insightful visualization of the noise transformation matrices** - The paper provides visualizations of the learned transformation matrices, revealing meaningful patterns of noise across different languages (e.g., which tags are confused with others).

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated approach to handling noisy projected data** - The authors convincingly demonstrate that directly using projected tags is harmful (Table 1 shows BiLSTM Projected performs worse than BiLSTM Annotated), and their noise layer effectively addresses this issue. The idea of explicitly modeling the transformation between projected and true tags is both novel and well-justified by the evidence presented. The noise layer allows the model to learn when to trust projected data and when to ignore it, which is a significant improvement over previous approaches that either used projected data directly or attempted to capture correlations implicitly.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation across diverse settings** - The paper evaluates the method on both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results consistently show improvements over all baselines, including state-of-the-art methods (Duong et al., 2014a). The evaluation on real low-resource languages is particularly valuable as it demonstrates practical applicability beyond just simulated settings.

3. **Insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices** - The visualizations of the noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. For example, the matrices reveal that while most tags are trusted (strong diagonal), there are specific patterns of confusion (e.g., NUM vs DET in Italian, PRT vs X in Swedish). This analysis demonstrates that the model learns meaningful patterns of noise, rather than just memorizing the projected data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim** - The authors claim their work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this appears to be an overstatement. Duong et al. (2014a), which they cite as their closest work, also used a joint model with projected data, and their approach is not clearly different in terms of explicitly modeling noise versus implicitly capturing correlations. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how their method differs from prior work, rather than framing it as a clear novelty.

2. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages** - The paper evaluates on only two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to make strong general claims about the approach's effectiveness across diverse low-resource settings. The authors could have included more languages to strengthen their claims, especially languages from different language families to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. **Lack of thorough analysis of failure modes and limitations** - The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of when the approach fails or what limitations it has. For example, how does the model perform when the parallel data is of very poor quality? How does it handle language pairs with very different syntactic structures? The authors could have included an analysis of the most challenging examples or languages to provide a more balanced view of the method's strengths and limitations.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 2, your method achieves 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda, outperforming all baselines. However, Garrette et al. (2013) reports 81.2% for Malagasy and 81.9% for Kinyarwanda. Could you clarify why your results on Kinyarwanda are better than Garrette et al.'s reported results, and whether your evaluation setup matches theirs?

2. The noise transformation matrices in Figures 3 and 4 show that the model learns meaningful patterns of noise, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why certain patterns emerge (e.g., why NUM and DET are confused in Italian but not in other languages). Could you elaborate on the linguistic reasons behind these patterns, or whether they might be related to specific translation phenomena?

3. The paper mentions that the approach works well with mismatching tagsets, but doesn't provide examples or analysis of this capability. Could you include a specific example where the model successfully handles a mismatched tagset?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the noise layer differs from standard techniques like label smoothing or confidence-based weighting of training examples.

- The two-stage training process (pretraining on gold data, then joint training with projected data) is a nice practical detail, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this is necessary (e.g., how would the model perform with direct joint training from random initialization?).

- The claim that "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods" is supported by the results, but the paper would be stronger with a more thorough analysis of when the improvement is most significant (e.g., for which languages or which types of noise is the improvement largest).

Overall, this is a solid paper with a novel technical contribution that is well-motivated and empirically validated. The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise transformation is valuable and could be applicable to other low-resource learning settings beyond POS tagging. With some refinement to address the weaknesses noted above, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
