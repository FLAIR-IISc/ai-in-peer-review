

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly trains on a small amount of gold-standard data and noisy projected data from a high-resource language. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that models the transformation between projected tags and true tags. The authors evaluate their approach on 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), claiming improvements over existing methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural network architecture with a noise layer for handling noisy projected POS tags
2. An evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages
3. A demonstration that the noise layer can improve results over baselines

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP: POS tagging for low-resource languages.
2. The approach is well-motivated and the noise layer concept is intuitive.
3. The evaluation covers both simulated and real low-resource languages.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of Clear Novelty
The authors claim their work is "the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations," but this claim is unsubstantiated. Their noise transformation matrix A is mathematically similar to the confusion matrices used in other noise-aware models. In Section 2, they cite Duong et al. (2014a) as their closest work, but their mathematical formulation (Equation 2) is nearly identical to the joint feature set approach described in Duong et al. The authors fail to provide a clear mathematical distinction between their approach and prior work, making it difficult to assess the novelty.

### 2. Inconsistent Methodology Description
The paper contains multiple inconsistencies in describing the methodology. In Section 1, they state their model is "trained jointly on gold and distant projected annotations, and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation." However, in Section 3.2, they describe a two-stage training process: first pretraining on gold data, then jointly training with projected data. This contradicts their earlier claim of end-to-end training. The paper should clarify this inconsistency and explain why the two-stage approach was necessary.

### 3. Insufficient Ablation Studies
The authors claim the noise layer is their key contribution, but they fail to provide ablation studies to isolate its effect. They compare their full model (BiLSTM+noise layer) to other baselines, but don't show results of a BiLSTM model without the noise layer. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine how much of the performance improvement comes from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture itself.

### 4. Inadequate Comparison with Baselines
The comparison with Duong et al. (2014a) is not fair. Duong et al. used a maximum entropy classifier with 1000 tagged tokens, while the authors use a BiLSTM with the same 1000 tokens. The comparison should be between the same model types (e.g., BiLSTM with noise layer vs. BiLSTM without noise layer), not between different model architectures. Additionally, the paper states "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods," but Table 1 shows that for Greek (el), their method (92.8%) is slightly worse than Duong et al. (92.1%), contradicting this claim.

### 5. Technical Inconsistencies and Ambiguities
- The paper uses "noise layer" and "output noising" interchangeably, creating confusion.
- In Section 3.2, the authors describe the noise layer as "applying a further linear transformation" to "uncertain projected tags," but the mathematical formulation (Equation 2) shows it's applied to the BiLSTM output, which is already a probability distribution.
- The paper claims "a small 1000 token dataset" but states they use "the first 1,000 tagged tokens," without specifying if these are randomly selected or from a specific corpus, which could significantly impact results.
- The paper uses "distant supervision" but their method is not standard distant supervision; it's cross-lingual projection with a noise model. This mislabeling is confusing.
- In Table 2, the columns for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are labeled as "Malagasy Kinyarwanda" without clear indication of which column corresponds to which language.

## Questions to Authors
1. How is your noise layer mathematically distinct from the approach used by Duong et al. (2014a)? Can you provide a clear mathematical comparison?
2. Why did you choose a two-stage training process instead of end-to-end training, and what were the results of end-to-end training?
3. Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture?
4. The paper claims "our approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods," but Table 1 shows for Greek (el) your method is slightly worse than Duong et al. How do you explain this discrepancy?
5. How do you handle tagset mismatches between source and target languages? The paper mentions this in a footnote but doesn't elaborate.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and has a well-motivated approach, the lack of clear novelty, inconsistent methodology description, insufficient ablation studies, and numerous technical inconsistencies significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's claims about novelty and performance improvements are not adequately supported by the presented evidence. These flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's relevance to the field.

I recommend rejection due to the methodological inconsistencies and lack of clear novelty compared to prior work. The authors need to provide a more rigorous comparison with existing approaches, demonstrate the uniqueness of their noise layer, and clarify their training methodology before the work can be considered for publication.
