

# Review for CONLL

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by combining limited gold-standard annotations with noisy cross-lingual projected data from parallel corpora. The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix applied after the main BiLSTM layer.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel noise modeling approach**: The paper's most significant contribution is introducing a learnable noise transformation layer (matrix A) that explicitly models the mapping between projected (noisy) tags and true (gold) tags, rather than treating projected data as clean labels.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation across both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages with 1,000 gold tokens) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over prior work.

3. **Insightful visualization of noise patterns**: The paper includes compelling visualizations of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3-4), revealing how the model learns to handle different types of tag confusion across languages.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The noise transformation layer represents a meaningful advancement over prior work that either treated projected data as clean (Duong et al., 2014a) or handled noise implicitly through joint feature sets. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach (Equation 2) and demonstrate its effectiveness through strong empirical results across multiple languages.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation is well-designed and comprehensive. The results in Table 1 show consistent improvements over all baselines across 8 simulated languages, with the proposed method achieving 91.7% accuracy compared to the previous state-of-the-art of 91.3%. The real-world results on Malagasy (86.3% vs. 85.3% for Duong et al.) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% vs. 78.3% for Duong et al.) further validate the approach's practical utility.

3. **Valuable insights into noise patterns**: The noise matrices (Figures 3-4) provide important insights into how the model handles different types of tag confusion across languages. For example, the analysis reveals that the model learns to ignore certain tag combinations (like NUM vs DET in Italian) while recognizing others (like VERB vs NOUN in Malagasy), demonstrating the approach's ability to capture meaningful patterns in the noise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. While Table 1 compares the full model (BiLSTM+noise layer) against BiLSTM Ann+Proj, it doesn't show how much of the improvement comes specifically from the noise layer versus the BiLSTM architecture. A comparison between a standard BiLSTM trained on both data sources versus the noise layer variant would strengthen the argument for the novelty of the approach.

2. **Limited failure analysis**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the model fails or where the noise layer doesn't sufficiently correct for projection errors. Understanding the limitations of the approach (e.g., which specific tags or language features it struggles with) would provide valuable guidance for future work and help assess the method's practical utility.

3. **Comparison with alternative noise handling approaches**: While the paper claims to be the first to explicitly model noise, it doesn't compare with other noise-handling techniques that might be relevant, such as approaches using confidence scores from the projection process or methods that use multiple projections to reduce noise. A brief comparison with these alternatives would better establish the novelty and advantages of the proposed approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more detail on how the noise matrix A is regularized to avoid overfitting given the limited amount of gold data? The paper mentions pretraining on annotated data first, but doesn't discuss how this helps prevent A from learning noise patterns that don't generalize.

2. The paper states that the approach "is general, and is likely to prove useful for exploiting other noisy annotations." How might this approach be adapted for other noisy supervision scenarios beyond cross-lingual projection, such as crowd-sourced annotations or distant supervision from knowledge bases?

3. In Figure 4, the noise matrix for Kinyarwanda shows much more noise than Malagasy. Given that Kinyarwanda has substantially less parallel data (11k sentences vs. 100k for Malagasy), is the amount of noise directly related to the quality/quantity of the parallel data? If so, how does this impact the practical applicability of the approach for languages with very limited parallel resources?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to improving POS tagging for low-resource languages by explicitly modeling the noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. The method is novel, well-validated through comprehensive experiments, and provides valuable insights into the nature of noise in projected tags. While the lack of ablation studies and failure analysis are notable weaknesses, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the ablation study and failure analysis)

The paper's strengths in methodology, empirical validation, and insightful analysis of noise patterns outweigh the weaknesses. The authors should address the questions above and include a brief ablation study comparing the noise layer variant against a standard BiLSTM trained on both data sources. With these improvements, this work would make a valuable contribution to the CONLL conference.
