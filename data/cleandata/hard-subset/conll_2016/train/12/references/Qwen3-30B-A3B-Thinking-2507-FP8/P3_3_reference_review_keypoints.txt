- Proposes a framework for low-resource POS tagging that leverages cross-lingual projection and models noise via a learnable transformation matrix.  
- Uses joint training to optimize both gold-standard and projected data simultaneously.  
- Evaluates on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages, showing consistent improvements over prior work.  

- Novel idea: explicitly models systematic noise in projected tags with a learnable transformation matrix.  
- Strong empirical validation: consistent gains (e.g., Malagasy 86.3% vs 85.3%, Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs 78.3%), especially in very low-resource scenarios.  
- Provides interpretable analysis via the noise transformation matrix, revealing language-specific noise patterns.  

- Limited architectural novelty: base model is a standard BiLSTM with insufficient justification over alternatives (CRF, transformer).  
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate the contributions of the noise layer versus joint training.  
- Underdeveloped discussion of limitations and failure cases, e.g., performance with very poor word alignments or machine-translated parallel data.  

- How does the noise layer perform with significantly noisier parallel data (e.g., machine translation) and has this been tested?  
- How would the approach generalize to other noisy supervision tasks (e.g., NER with crowdsourced annotations)?  
- Why choose a BiLSTM over transformers, and how would the noise layer integrate with transformer architectures?  

- Overall: a well-executed, novel method for handling noisy distant supervision with compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address ablation, architecture justification, and limitations.
