

# Peer Review: "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy data obtained through cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The authors propose a neural network model that incorporates a "noise layer" to explicitly model the transformation between projected tags (which contain errors) and true tags. The model is trained jointly on both gold and projected data.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work (in order of significance) are:

1. **Empirical demonstration of a principled approach to handling noisy cross-lingual projections** - The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation showing consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages, which is valuable for the NLP community working on resource-poor languages.

2. **Insightful analysis of the noise transformation matrices** - The authors present detailed visualizations of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4), revealing which tag transitions the model trusts or distrusts, providing valuable insights into how the model handles different types of projection errors.

3. **Practical demonstration of effective resource utilization** - The paper shows how to effectively leverage existing bilingual resources (which are often available even for low-resource languages) to improve POS tagging performance with minimal additional annotation effort.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation across diverse settings**: The authors evaluate their approach on eight European languages (simulated low-resource settings) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over multiple baselines. Their results (Tables 1 and 2) show that their method achieves 91.7% average accuracy on the simulated languages and 86.3% and 82.5% accuracy on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, respectively, outperforming all previous approaches. This comprehensive evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.

2. **Clear and well-motivated problem statement**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing cross-lingual projection methods (noise from poor alignments, syntactic divergence) and positions their work as addressing these limitations by explicitly modeling the noise. The analysis of the projected data's quality (Table 1, top section) clearly shows that direct use of projected data is harmful, motivating their approach.

3. **Insightful visualization of learned noise patterns**: The noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of projection errors. For example, they show that the model learns to distrust certain tag transitions (like NUM vs DET in Italian) while trusting others (strong diagonals), which helps explain why the method works well. This analysis goes beyond simply reporting numbers to provide understanding of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty of the noise layer**: The paper claims this is the first work to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projected annotations, but similar concepts have been used in other NLP contexts. For example, error models have been used in machine translation to handle word alignment errors (e.g., in phrase-based MT), and noise-robust learning techniques have been applied to other NLP tasks. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from these prior works, which weakens the novelty claim. The authors should clarify how their specific noise layer formulation differs from existing error models.

2. **Lack of ablation studies to understand the noise layer's contribution**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies showing how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus other aspects of the model. For example, it would be valuable to see results with the noise layer removed but with the same model architecture, to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. Without these studies, it's unclear whether the noise layer is the key innovation or whether the improvements could be achieved with other techniques.

3. **Limited exploration of the method's boundaries**: The paper uses English as the source language for all experiments, but in many low-resource settings, the closest related language might not be English (e.g., for African languages, the closest might be another African language). The paper doesn't explore how well the method works when the source language is not English or when the source and target languages are not closely related. This is a significant limitation for a method that claims to be generally applicable to low-resource languages.

4. **Inadequate discussion of computational cost**: While the paper mentions the two-stage training process (pretraining on gold data, then joint training), it doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed method compared to baselines. For low-resource language applications where computational resources might be limited, this is an important consideration that should be addressed.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims this is the first work to explicitly model noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. How does your approach differ from error models used in machine translation (e.g., to handle word alignment errors) or other noise-robust learning techniques in NLP? Could these existing techniques be applied to the same problem?

2. In Table 1, your method achieves 92.3% accuracy on Danish (da), while Duong et al. (2014a) achieves 92.1%. Given that the difference is minimal, what factors contribute to the marginal improvement on some languages but not others? How does the size of the parallel corpus affect the performance difference?

3. The paper states that the noise layer is particularly beneficial for languages with more noise (e.g., Swedish, as shown in Figure 3). However, in the real low-resource languages, Malagasy has more parallel data than Kinyarwanda, and the performance gain is larger for Malagasy. Could you elaborate on how the amount of parallel data affects the noise layer's effectiveness, and is there a threshold below which the noise layer doesn't provide significant benefits?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The work addresses an important problem in NLP with a well-motivated approach that provides consistent empirical improvements over previous methods. While the novelty claim could be strengthened with better comparison to related work, the empirical results and insightful analysis of the noise patterns make this a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by clarifying the novelty of their approach and providing more detailed analysis of the noise layer's contribution.
