

# Peer Review for CONLL

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy projected data from cross-lingual projection. The key innovation is an explicit noise transformation layer that models the mapping between projected tags (from a high-resource language) and true tags in the low-resource language. The authors demonstrate that their method consistently outperforms existing approaches on both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel neural network architecture with an explicit noise transformation layer that models the mapping between projected tags and true tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.
2. A joint training procedure that effectively combines gold-standard annotations with noisy projected annotations, resulting in consistent performance improvements across multiple languages.
3. Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated low-resource scenarios (8 languages) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), with clear visualization of the noise transformation matrices.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to handling noisy data**: The paper's core contribution is modeling the noise in projected tags explicitly with a transformation matrix, which is a significant departure from previous work that either tried to clean the projected data before training or attempted to implicitly capture noise through joint feature sets. The authors demonstrate this approach leads to consistent improvements across multiple languages.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper shows statistically significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the real low-resource languages. For example, the method achieves 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda, substantially outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (85.3% and 78.3% respectively). The results are consistent across both simulated and real low-resource settings.

3. **Clear demonstration of the noise transformation**: The authors provide excellent visualizations of the noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), which help readers understand how the model learns to handle different types of noise in different languages. This is particularly valuable for understanding why the method works.

4. **Practical significance**: The approach addresses a critical challenge in NLP for low-resource languages, with clear potential for real-world application. The method could be extended to other tasks beyond POS tagging that rely on distant supervision.

5. **Well-structured evaluation**: The evaluation is comprehensive, using both simulated low-resource scenarios (with 8 European languages) and real low-resource languages. The authors also provide ablation studies showing the effectiveness of the noise layer.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with more recent methods**: While the paper compares with Duong et al. (2014a), the previous state-of-the-art, it doesn't compare with more recent approaches to low-resource POS tagging published since then. Given the rapid progress in NLP, this is a significant omission for a CONLL submission.

2. **Lack of analysis on varying noise levels**: The paper demonstrates the method works well with the given data but doesn't analyze how performance changes with different levels of noise in the projected data. For instance, what happens when using parallel data from a different domain or with higher alignment errors? This would strengthen the argument for the robustness of the approach.

3. **Insufficient detail on the noise layer implementation**: The paper states that the noise layer is "parameterized by a K×K matrix A" but doesn't provide enough detail on how this matrix is initialized or learned. The two-stage training process is mentioned, but it's unclear how the matrix A is initialized during the first stage, which could affect the model's ability to learn.

4. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: While the paper presents strong overall results, it doesn't analyze specific cases where the model performs poorly. Understanding the limitations of the approach would be valuable for future work.

5. **Computational complexity not addressed**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead of adding the noise layer to the BiLSTM model. For larger tagsets or more complex models, this could become a significant concern, especially in low-resource settings where computational resources may be limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method compare with more recent approaches to low-resource POS tagging published since Duong et al. (2014a), such as those using transfer learning or multilingual pretraining?

2. Have you analyzed the performance of your method when the quality of the parallel data varies (e.g., using different parallel corpora with different levels of noise)?

3. Can you provide more details on the initialization of the noise transformation matrix A and how it evolves during training, particularly during the first pretraining stage?

4. What are the most common types of tag confusion that your model handles well, and which types present the greatest challenges?

5. How does the computational complexity of your approach compare to the baseline BiLSTM models, especially considering the size of the noise transformation matrix?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper with a novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision in low-resource POS tagging. The empirical results are compelling and the approach has clear practical significance. However, the limited comparison with more recent methods and insufficient detail on the noise layer implementation are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these issues resolved, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
