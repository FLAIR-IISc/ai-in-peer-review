

# Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with noisy cross-lingual projected tags from parallel English data. The authors propose a neural network model with a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags. The main contributions as I see them are: (1) introducing an explicit noise transformation layer to model the relationship between projected and true tags, (2) demonstrating a joint training framework that effectively combines clean and noisy data, and (3) providing strong empirical results across both simulated low-resource languages (8 European languages with limited gold data) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

## Strengths
1. **Significant problem addressed with practical impact**: Low-resource POS tagging is a critical challenge in NLP, and the authors effectively address it with a method that could be widely applicable to many languages with limited resources. The paper's focus on real-world applicability is commendable, especially with the demonstration on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

2. **Novel and principled approach to noise modeling**: The noise layer is a clever and theoretically sound approach to handling the inherent noise in cross-lingual projected tags. Unlike previous work that either cleaned the projected data before training or used it as a weak signal, the authors explicitly model the transformation between projected and true tags, which is a more principled approach to leveraging this data.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across both simulated low-resource settings (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms all baselines, with particularly strong gains on the real low-resource languages (86.3% accuracy for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda).

4. **Insightful analysis of noise patterns**: The paper provides valuable visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), which reveal how the model learns to trust certain tags and ignore others. This analysis offers important insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages.

5. **Strong technical execution**: The model is well-implemented and the training procedure (two-stage training) is clearly explained. The comparison with relevant baselines (including the state-of-the-art from Duong et al.) is thorough and convincing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient theoretical justification for the noise layer approach**: While the authors claim their approach is "the first to explicitly model the noise," they don't provide a strong theoretical argument for why modeling the noise as a transformation layer is superior to other approaches (e.g., using a weighted combination of the two data sources). A more thorough comparison with alternative noise modeling approaches would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited evaluation on real low-resource languages**: The evaluation on real low-resource languages is restricted to only two languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), which is insufficient to establish broad applicability. The authors claim the method is "general" and "likely to prove useful for other noisy annotations," but the evidence for this claim is limited.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies that isolate the contribution of the noise layer. For example, it would be valuable to know how much the noise layer contributes to the overall performance gain compared to simply using a weighted combination of the two data sources.

4. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the approach fails or where the noise layer doesn't help as much. Understanding the limitations of the approach would make the paper more balanced and useful for future work.

5. **Underdeveloped generalization claims**: While the authors claim the technique is general and applicable to other noisy annotations, they don't provide any evidence or discussion of how it might work for other tasks beyond POS tagging. This claim needs stronger support.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that "our approach is the first to explicitly model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations." Could you provide a more thorough comparison with related work that might have implicitly modeled similar noise patterns (e.g., some of the graph-based approaches mentioned in the related work)?

2. The authors state that "the noise layer learns when and how much to trust the projected data." How does the noise layer differ from simply using a weighted combination of the two data sources (gold and projected)? Could you provide more details about the learning dynamics of the noise transformation matrix A?

3. The evaluation on real low-resource languages is limited to only two languages. Would you be able to provide results for additional languages to better demonstrate the broad applicability of your approach?

4. How does the performance of your approach scale with the amount of gold standard data? Is there a threshold below which the approach doesn't provide significant benefits, and if so, what is it?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to low-resource POS tagging. The proposed noise layer approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods. However, the limited evaluation on real low-resource languages and insufficient theoretical justification for the noise layer approach prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. With additional experiments on more languages and a more thorough comparison with alternative noise modeling approaches, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses identified above in a revised version. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to CONLL with appropriate revisions.
