

Summary: The paper proposes a neural network model for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly learns from a small amount of gold-standard data and noisy projected data from cross-lingual projection. The key contribution is an explicit noise layer that models the transformation between projected tags and gold standard tags, allowing the model to learn when to trust the projected data. The work is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

Main contributions: The primary contribution is the explicit noise layer that models the transformation between projected tags and gold standard tags. The second contribution is the evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource settings. The third contribution is the analysis of the noise transformation matrix, which provides insights into the nature of the noise in projected tags.

Strengths:
1. The approach addresses an important practical problem in NLP: effectively utilizing noisy distant supervision for low-resource languages. The explicit modeling of the noise transformation is a novel and elegant solution to the problem of unreliable projected tags.
2. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering both simulated low-resource settings (eight languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The consistent improvements across these diverse settings demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
3. The analysis of the noise transformation matrix provides valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. This helps understand the model's behavior and the nature of the noise in projected tags.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims novelty in explicitly modeling the noise transformation, but the distinction from existing joint training approaches is not sufficiently clarified. A more detailed comparison with methods that jointly train on multiple data sources (beyond just Duong et al.) would strengthen the novelty claim.
2. The evaluation uses only 1000 tokens of gold standard data, but it's unclear how representative this amount is for truly low-resource languages. For many low-resource languages, obtaining even 1000 tokens of gold data might be challenging, so the evaluation might not fully reflect the practical utility of the approach.
3. The paper doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the approach to the quality of word alignments. Since the projected tags depend on word alignments, it's important to understand how the method performs when alignment quality is poor, which is common in cross-lingual projection.
4. The computational complexity and training time compared to baseline methods are not discussed. For a method targeting low-resource settings, it's important to know whether the additional complexity is justified by the performance gains.

Questions to Authors:
1. How sensitive is the approach to the quality of word alignments, and what happens when alignment quality is poor (e.g., when using languages with very different word order)?
2. Could the approach be extended to handle cases where the tagsets are not aligned (e.g., different POS tagsets between languages)?
3. How does the performance scale when the amount of gold standard data is reduced below 1000 tokens, and what is the minimum amount of gold data required for the approach to be effective?
