

Summary: This paper proposes a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that explicitly models noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. The key contribution is a noise layer that learns to correct errors from projection, allowing the model to jointly learn from gold-standard data and noisy projected data. The authors demonstrate their approach on 8 simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

Main Contributions:
1. A noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected (noisy) tags and gold tags in a neural network framework.
2. A joint learning approach that effectively combines small amounts of gold-standard data with noisy projected data.
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing consistent improvements across multiple languages, with strong results on real low-resource languages.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important problem in NLP with a technically sound and novel approach. The noise layer concept is well-motivated and clearly explained, and the authors provide strong empirical evidence for its effectiveness.
2. The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, covering both simulated low-resource settings (using 8 European languages with limited gold data) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results consistently show improvements over existing methods, with the model achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda.
3. The visualization of the noise transformation matrix (Figures 3 and 4) is particularly valuable, as it provides concrete insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages. This adds significant interpretability to the approach.
4. The paper's claim that their approach is the first to explicitly model noise in projected annotations is well-supported by their comparison with related work, particularly Duong et al. (2014a), which implicitly handles tagset correlations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail on the initialization and training of the noise layer matrix A. How is A initialized before training? How does this initialization affect the final performance? The authors should clarify this in the revision.
2. The paper doesn't sufficiently address why the model performs better on some languages than others (e.g., why Swedish shows more noise in Figure 3). A more detailed analysis of language-specific characteristics that affect the noise layer's performance would strengthen the paper.
3. The paper assumes that word alignments are sufficiently reliable for projection, but doesn't discuss how alignment errors specifically impact the projected tags or the noise layer's effectiveness. A brief analysis of alignment quality and its relationship to performance would be valuable.
4. The paper claims "our approach is the first to explicitly model the noise," but doesn't adequately distinguish this from previous work on noisy label learning. While the application to cross-lingual projection is novel, the noise modeling itself is related to broader literature on handling noisy labels in machine learning.

Questions to Authors:
1. How is the noise layer matrix A initialized before training, and how does this initialization affect the final performance? Is there a specific initialization strategy that works best?
2. The paper mentions that the model learns to "map projected determiner tags to nouns" for languages without determiners. Could you provide specific examples from your results that illustrate this behavior?
3. In Table 2, your method outperforms Garrette et al. (2013) on Kinyarwanda but not Malagasy. Could you elaborate on why this difference might occur, given that Kinyarwanda has less parallel data than Malagasy?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to low-resource NLP. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits. With some additional details on the noise layer implementation and a more thorough analysis of language-specific performance differences, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
