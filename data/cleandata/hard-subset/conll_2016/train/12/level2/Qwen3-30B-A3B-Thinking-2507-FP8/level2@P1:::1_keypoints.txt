- Proposes a noise layer that models the transformation between noisy projected tags and gold tags in a neural POS tagger.  
- Introduces joint learning that combines small gold-standard datasets with noisy projected data.  
- Evaluates on 8 simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements.  
- Empirical results are strong and technically sound, with clear motivation and evidence for the noise layer’s effectiveness.  
- Thorough evaluation design across simulated and real low-resource scenarios; reports 86.3% on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda.  
- Visualization of the noise transformation matrix provides interpretable insights into how the model handles noise.  
- Claim of being the first to explicitly model projection noise is supported relative to prior cross-lingual tagging work (e.g., Duong et al. 2014a).  
- Missing details on initialization and training of the noise matrix A and how initialization affects final performance.  
- Lacks analysis explaining variation in performance across languages (e.g., why Swedish shows more noise).  
- Does not analyze the impact of word-alignment errors on projected tags and the noise layer’s effectiveness.  
- Overstates novelty by not fully distinguishing the noise modeling from broader noisy-label literature.  
- Asks authors to clarify initialization strategy for A and its effect on results.  
- Requests concrete examples showing the model mapping projected determiners to nouns in languages without determiners.  
- Asks for explanation why the method outperforms Garrette et al. (2013) on Kinyarwanda but not Malagasy, given different amounts of parallel data.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel contribution to low-resource NLP with recommendation for acceptance pending clarifications and deeper analysis.
