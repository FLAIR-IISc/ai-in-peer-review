

Summary: This paper addresses the challenge of low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection from a resource-rich language. The authors propose a novel neural network model that explicitly models noise in projected tags through a transformation layer, enabling joint learning from both gold standard data and noisy projected data. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), using only 1000 gold tokens.

Main Contributions: 
1. A noise transformation layer that explicitly models the mapping between projected tags and gold tags
2. A joint learning framework that combines gold standard and projected data without requiring additional constraints
3. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over prior work on both simulated and real low-resource languages

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a genuine problem in low-resource POS tagging - noisy cross-lingual projection - and proposes a well-motivated solution. The noise layer is a natural extension to the BiLSTM model that provides clear intuition about how the model "learns when to trust" projected tags.

2. The evaluation is thorough and well-justified. The use of both simulated low-resource settings (8 European languages) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) provides strong evidence of the approach's generalizability. The results consistently show improvements over baselines, with the noise layer providing significant gains.

3. The paper provides excellent visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), which help illustrate how the model handles different types of noise across languages. This adds significant value to the paper's empirical claims.

4. The approach is practical and could be applied to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging that use distant supervision. The authors correctly note the generalizability of their technique.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is overstated. While the paper presents a novel application to cross-lingual POS tagging, the core concept of modeling noise in label data is not new to machine learning. The paper should better contextualize this within the broader literature on handling noisy labels (e.g., work by Liu and Tao, 2015; Goldberger and Ben-Reuven, 2017). The authors cite only a few related machine learning papers, missing key works in this area.

2. The paper lacks sufficient discussion of practical limitations. How does the model perform when gold data is even more scarce (e.g., <500 tokens)? What happens when parallel data quality is extremely poor? The authors show results for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda but don't discuss failure cases or scenarios where the approach might not work.

3. The paper provides insufficient details about the noise layer's training process. How is the matrix A initialized? How does the model avoid overfitting to the noise? The two-stage training process is mentioned, but the paper doesn't explain why this is necessary or how the pretraining step affects the final model.

4. The paper claims to be "the first to explicitly model the noise" but doesn't adequately compare to related work on label noise correction. For example, there's work on learning to correct noisy labels through neural networks (e.g., the "noisy label learning" literature), which the authors should address to better position their contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the performance of your noise layer approach scale with decreasing amounts of gold data (e.g., 500 tokens instead of 1000)? What is the minimum amount of gold data required for the approach to be effective?

2. The noise transformation matrices (Figures 3-4) show considerable off-diagonal elements. Can you provide more insight into what these patterns reveal about the specific types of noise present in cross-lingual projection for different languages?

3. How would your approach perform if the parallel data contained significant translation errors or if the languages were very distantly related? Could you provide any examples where the approach might fail?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to low-resource POS tagging. The approach is novel in its specific application to cross-lingual projection, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are convincing. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by better contextualization within the broader machine learning literature on noisy labels and a more thorough discussion of practical limitations.
