

This paper introduces a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages by jointly learning from a small amount of gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected data. The main contribution is the explicit modeling of noise in projected tags through a noise transformation layer, which allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected annotations. The second contribution is the demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms baselines on both simulated low-resource settings (8 languages) and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The third contribution is the insightful analysis of language-specific noise patterns revealed through the learned transformation matrices.

Strengths:
1. The explicit noise modeling is a well-motivated and novel approach to a persistent challenge in cross-lingual projection. Previous work (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a) handled noise implicitly through joint features, while this paper provides a clean neural network solution that directly models the noise process.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource settings. The results on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%) significantly exceed prior state-of-the-art.
3. The analysis of noise transformation matrices provides valuable insights into how the model handles different types of noise across languages, revealing language-specific patterns that would be useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between the benefits of joint training versus the explicit noise layer. The BiLSTM Ann+Proj baseline (training on both data types without the noise layer) already shows improvement over Projected data, suggesting joint training might be the primary factor rather than the noise layer itself.
2. Critical implementation details about the noise transformation matrix A are missing. How is A initialized? How does its size affect performance? These details are essential for reproducibility and understanding the method's behavior.
3. The paper doesn't address limitations of the approach. How does it perform with extremely noisy parallel data or when languages have significant syntactic divergence? The authors acknowledge these challenges but don't demonstrate the method's boundaries.

The paper presents a promising approach with strong empirical results, but would benefit from clearer differentiation between joint training and the noise layer, more implementation details, and discussion of limitations. The insights into language-specific noise patterns are particularly valuable for the field.
