Summary / Contributions
- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit K×K label-noise transformation layer A to model noisy cross-lingual projected tags.
- Trains in two stages: pretraining on small gold data, then joint training on gold and projected data with separate loss terms.
- Empirical validation across 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages shows consistent gains and new SOTA on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.
- Claims interpretability via analysis of learned noise matrices revealing projection confusions.

Strengths
- Simple, well-motivated method that improves utility of distant supervision and outperforms prior work in most simulations and real low-resource cases.
- Principled modeling of systematic projection biases using an explicit confusion matrix and sensible two-stage loss weighting.
- Diagnostic interpretability: visualizations of A provide insights into when projection is trustworthy and common confusions.
- Thoughtful experimental setup: realistic ≈1k-token low-resource setting, universal tags, and multiple relevant baselines.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: confusion-matrix/noise-layer approaches are known; novelty is mainly in this application and would benefit from clearer positioning versus prior noisy-label methods.
- Heuristic treatment of non-1:1 alignments (assigning sentence-level English tag distributions) may bias results; no ablation of alternatives.
- Limited ablations/sensitivity analyses (pretraining effect, A initialization/regularization, loss weighting, performance across varying gold sizes).
- Baseline breadth limited: lacks BiLSTM-CRF and character/morphological features that are standard for POS and crucial for morphologically rich languages.
- Reproducibility/significance details sparse: no significance testing, and implementation details (A initialization, early stopping, filtering thresholds) and code availability are unclear.

Questions for authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Any identity initialization, diagonality penalties, or stochastic constraints used?
- How sensitive are results to weighting between gold and projected losses or to curriculum strategies?
- Can you provide ablations for omitting pretraining, varying gold token counts, and alternatives to the sentence-level tag-distribution heuristic?
- Did you try a CRF layer or character embeddings, and do noise-layer gains persist with stronger architectures?
- For real languages, what are the parallel corpora domains and alignment quality, and does the filtering threshold affect performance?

Additional comments / Suggestions
- Quantify sparsity/diagonality of A (e.g., trace or entropy) and correlate with alignment quality or parallel-data size.
- Clarify whether English tagging errors are absorbed into A and whether better English taggers reduce off-diagonal mass.
- Report confidence intervals or significance tests for main results, especially where gains are modest.
- Demonstrate generalization to mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) with a small experiment.
