Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by leveraging noisy cross-lingual projection from parallel data together with a small amount of gold annotations. The key idea is to explicitly learn a label-noise transformation between clean labels and projected labels via a linear “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger, and jointly train on gold and projected data. The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), and reported to match or surpass prior work.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A simple, explicit noise-aware training objective for sequence tagging that models the mapping from true tags to projected (noisy) tags via a learnable confusion matrix, jointly trained with a BiLSTM.
2) Empirical evidence that this noise-aware integration of projected data yields consistent gains over strong baselines (including prior SOTA) in both simulated and real low-resource setups.
3) Insightful analysis of learned noise matrices across languages, providing interpretable evidence about projection noise patterns.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling of projection noise: The learnable K×K transformation (A) directly accounts for systematic biases in cross-lingual projections. This is conceptually clean, easy to implement (softmax(A o)), and integrates seamlessly with end-to-end backpropagation. The approach is more transparent than prior methods that only implicitly capture tag correlations via features.

- Consistent empirical improvements across many settings: On eight simulated languages, the method improves average accuracy (91.7) over Duong et al. (91.3). On real low-resource languages, it outperforms prior work notably on Kinyarwanda (82.5 vs 78.3) and moderately on Malagasy (86.3 vs 85.3). The baselines include BiLSTM trained on gold, projected, and their union, as well as MaxEnt and prior SOTA, illustrating the method’s robustness.

- Realistic low-resource evaluation with parallel data: The paper evaluates not only on simulated low-resource settings but also on two genuinely low-resource languages with smaller and noisier parallel corpora, increasing the practical relevance of the results.

- Interpretability and analysis: Heatmaps of A offer an interpretable view into cross-lingual projection errors (e.g., noun/adj/num interactions, determiner mismatches), and correlate with performance differences across languages. This aids understanding and trust in the method.

- Sound training recipe: Pretraining on clean gold labels followed by joint training with projected data is sensible to avoid degenerate solutions, and aligns with standard practice in label-noise modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations and diagnostics: The paper asserts that end-to-end training from scratch is “impractical,” but provides no quantitative ablation showing (i) performance without pretraining, (ii) the effect of different weights/ratios between gold and projected data, or (iii) the benefit magnitude of the noise layer vs a simple additional linear layer trained only on projected data. Such ablations would clarify where the gains come from and the sensitivity to design choices.

- Fairness and realism of data conditions: For simulated languages, the method leverages up to 200k high-quality parallel sentences (filtered by IBM Model 3), which reduces projection noise (as seen in strong diagonals of A). This may overstate gains in truly low-resource settings where such parallel data is scarce or unfiltered. In contrast, the real low-resource experiments rely on 100k (Malagasy) and 11k (Kinyarwanda) sentences, but the gold training sets for these languages are 5k tokens, not the 1k-token scenario emphasized elsewhere. The discrepancy between the “1k tokens” framing and the 5k-token real experiments should be clarified, and results with 1k-gold-token subsets reported for completeness.

- Missing statistical significance and variance: Improvements over prior work on the simulated languages are small on average (e.g., +0.4 absolute). No statistical tests or variance across random seeds are reported. For a conference paper, this makes it hard to judge the reliability of the improvements, especially given the modest margins.

- Questionable handling of many-to-one/unaligned projections: For tokens with many or no alignments, the projected supervision is the sentence-level tag frequency distribution. This heuristic is very coarse and might inject systematic biases. More principled options (alignment posteriors, marginalization over aligned tags, or confidence-aware weighting) are not explored; consequently, it is unclear how much of the final gain is compensating for suboptimal projection choices.

- Incomplete comparison to related label-noise literature: The paper positions its novelty mainly within cross-lingual projection, but there is relevant work on neural networks with label-noise models (e.g., learnable noise/confusion layers) that should be cited and contrasted. Discussing identifiability and regularization of A (e.g., diagonal priors) would also strengthen the methodological framing.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are results to the relative weighting of gold vs projected corpora during joint training? Did you try sampling schemes (e.g., mixing ratios) or scaling the loss terms to reflect dataset sizes?
- Can you provide ablations showing: (a) no pretraining; (b) training with projected data but without the noise layer; (c) different initialization or regularization for A (e.g., diagonal bias)?
- For Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, can you report results with only 1k gold tokens to match the setting highlighted in the abstract? Conversely, for the simulated languages, what happens when you reduce parallel data or relax 1-1 alignment filtering?
- Did you run multiple seeds and/or significance tests for the reported gains, particularly in Table 1?

Additional comments (minor)
- Clarify the treatment of NULL/many-to-one cases; consider using alignment posteriors or soft projections instead of sentence-level tag distributions.
- Provide details on regularization (dropout, L2) and hyperparameter tuning on dev sets; a CRF output layer might further help sequence consistency—worth noting as future work or ablation.
- Some references and citations are inconsistently formatted; consider adding related work on learning with noisy labels in neural networks.
- If possible, release code and preprocessed resources to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A solid, practical contribution that explicitly models projection noise and delivers consistent empirical gains with good interpretability. With stronger ablations, clarified data conditions, and tighter positioning versus label-noise literature, this paper would be a strong accept. As is, it merits acceptance, especially given its simplicity, robustness, and relevance to low-resource NLP.
