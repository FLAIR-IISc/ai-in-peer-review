- Summary: Jointly trains a BiLSTM POS tagger on ~1k gold tokens and noisy cross-lingual projections using a learned confusion matrix to model label noise, yielding strong accuracy gains on simulated and real low-resource languages.

- Contribution: Introduces an explicit, trainable label-noise layer (confusion matrix A) to correct systematic projection biases during end-to-end training.

- Contribution: Proposes a simple two-stage training protocol (gold pretraining, then joint training with projections) to leverage minimal gold data and abundant noisy projections.

- Contribution: Provides empirical validation across 10 language settings and interpretable noise matrices that reveal projection error patterns.

- Strength: Strong empirical results, matching or exceeding prior work on 8 simulated languages and improving Malagasy and Kinyarwanda accuracies.

- Strength: Principled, compact noise modeling (confusion matrix) with interpretable heatmaps showing realistic tag confusions.

- Strength: Practical and realistic setup using parallel text plus ~1k gold tokens, applicable to true low-resource scenarios.

- Strength: Implementation simplicity—extends standard BiLSTMs with a linear noise layer and supports end-to-end training.

- Weakness: Incremental novelty—explicit noise modeling is established in noisy-label and crowdsourcing literature, with limited comparison to existing methods like EM or loss-correction.

- Weakness: Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses (A initialization/regularization, effect of pretraining, varying gold/projected data sizes, comparisons to confidence-weighted methods).

- Weakness: Dependence on 1–1 alignments reduces coverage and leaves alignment-quality sensitivity unquantified, a concern for truly low-resource settings.

- Weakness: Reporting gaps—insufficient hyperparameter details, no significance tests, unclear runtime/resources and code availability, hindering reproducibility.

- Question: How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized to prevent degenerate mappings?

- Question: How sensitive is performance to gold token count, projected data size/quality, and alignment strategies (many-to-one, symmetrized)?

- Question: How does the method compare to alternative noise-aware techniques (EM, loss correction, label smoothing, confidence filtering)?

- Question: Can the approach handle multi-source projections or mismatched tagsets in practice, and what are the expected gains?

- Question: Does fine-tuning without the noise layer at test time improve or degrade final performance?
