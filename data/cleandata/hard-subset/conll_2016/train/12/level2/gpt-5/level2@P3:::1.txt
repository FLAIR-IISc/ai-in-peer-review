Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a sequence tagging model for low-resource POS tagging that jointly learns from a small gold-annotated corpus and noisy cross-lingual projected labels. The key idea is an explicit “noise layer” — a K×K linear transformation on the model’s tag distribution — that models systematic distortions introduced by projection through word alignments and cross-lingual divergences.
- The model is a BiLSTM tagger trained in two stages: pretraining on gold data, followed by joint training on gold and projected data with the noise layer. The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- The work reports consistent improvements over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a), and provides interpretable analyses via the learned noise matrices.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) An explicit, learnable noise-adaptation layer for cross-lingual projection integrated into a neural sequence tagger, enabling the model to “learn when to trust” distant supervision. This is simple, low-parameter, and directly optimizes the end objective.  
2) A thorough empirical evaluation across 10 languages showing consistent gains over state-of-the-art methods in the standard 1k-token low-resource setting and on two real low-resource languages, with interpretable diagnostics of learned noise patterns.

Strengths
- Clear technical novelty in the application context: Modeling projection noise with an explicit confusion matrix on the output distribution is a principled and effective mechanism. While conceptually related to noise-adaptation layers, the integration for cross-lingual POS projection and joint training with minimal annotation is well-motivated.
- Strong and broad empirical results: On eight simulated languages, average accuracy improves to 91.7 from 91.3 for Duong et al. (Table 1), and on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda the model reaches 86.3/82.5 vs. 85.3/78.3 for Duong et al. and 81.2/81.9 for Garrette & Baldridge (Table 2). Direct training on projected data performs poorly, while the proposed approach extracts value from projection reliably.
- Interpretability and diagnostic value: Visualizations of the learned A matrices (Figures 3–4) show language-specific noise patterns (e.g., Swedish confusion between PRT and X; Malagasy nouns mapping to ADJ/NUM), supporting the claim that systematic projection errors are captured and mitigated.
- Practical and scalable: The added parameterization is small (K×K), training is straightforward, and the approach requires only standard resources (parallel data, word alignments, an English tagger). The two-stage training protocol addresses instability when relying on noisy labels.
- Well-contextualized evaluation: Comparisons include both neural and non-neural baselines, with consistent data splits and realistic low-resource constraints (1k tokens in simulations; limited parallel data in the real languages).

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity in projection setup: Section 3.1 describes assigning sentence-level tag distributions to tokens aligned to multiple or NULL words, but Section 4.1.2 states that only one-to-one alignments are retained. If only one-to-one alignments are used, the multi/NULL strategy is moot; if not, the relative prevalence and impact of these cases are unclear. This affects understanding of the supervision signal used by the noise layer.
- Limited analysis of significance and variance: Improvements over Duong et al. are sometimes modest (e.g., +0.4 average points in Table 1). The paper lacks confidence intervals, significance tests, or results across multiple random initializations, making it difficult to assess robustness.
- Real-language training sizes vs. abstract: The abstract emphasizes evaluation with only 1000 gold tokens, but Section 4.1.3 indicates 5–5.3k training tokens for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda. This discrepancy should be clarified, and results with 1k tokens on these languages would better align with the stated scenario.
- Noise model simplicity: The A matrix is global and context-independent. Projection noise is known to correlate with alignment quality and token properties. Without conditioning A on alignment confidence or token features, the model may underfit structured noise. A small extension (e.g., gating with alignment scores) could plausibly yield further gains.
- Reproducibility details: Training details are sparse (e.g., initialization of A, exact pretraining/finetuning schedules, number of epochs, early stopping, regularization/dropout). Given the sensitivity of neural taggers and the two-stage setup, more specifics would facilitate replication.

Questions to authors (for response period)
- Projection protocol: Were many-to-one/one-to-many/NULL alignments used in supervision, or were they discarded by retaining only one-to-one links? If discarded, what role does the sentence-level tag distribution play? Please quantify the proportion of tokens supervised via each case.
- Initialization of the noise matrix: How is A initialized (identity, zeros, random)? Are any constraints (e.g., diagonal bias) applied to stabilize learning?
- Training regimen: How many epochs for pretraining and joint training, and what early stopping criteria were used? Any dropout or regularization?
- Statistical reliability: Are the reported improvements averaged over multiple runs? If not, could variance or significance be reported?
- Real low-resource setup: What are the results when restricting Malagasy/Kinyarwanda gold training data to 1k tokens, to match the simulated setting and the abstract’s claim?

Suggestions for improvement (feasible within rebuttal/camera-ready)
- Clarify the projection pipeline and the role of multi/NULL alignments, and report proportions and their effect on performance.
- Provide training details (A initialization, schedules, early stopping, regularization) and, if possible, a small variance analysis across seeds for one or two languages.
- Add a brief ablation on alignment quality filtering (e.g., with/without IBM Model 3 sentence selection) or on conditioning by alignment confidence, to illuminate where the noise layer helps most.
- For transparency, reconcile the 1k-token narrative with the larger real-language training sets; optionally include 1k-token results for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda.

Overall assessment
A simple, well-motivated method that consistently improves low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling projection noise. The approach is practical and interpretable, with solid empirical coverage. Addressing the noted clarifications and adding minimal robustness reporting would significantly strengthen the submission.
