Summary and contributions
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, which is noisy due to alignment errors and cross-lingual divergences. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an explicit label-noise transformation layer (a K×K matrix A) applied to the predicted tag distribution when training on projected data. The model is trained in two stages: pretraining on a small gold corpus, then joint training on gold and projected data, with separate cross-entropy terms. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, explicit noise-modeling layer for projected labels integrated into a BiLSTM POS tagger, jointly trained on clean and projected supervision, that improves the utility of distant supervision.
2) Extensive empirical validation across 10 languages with consistent improvements, including new state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda given ~1k gold tokens.
3) Interpretable analysis of learned noise matrices that sheds light on projection errors and cross-lingual tag confusions.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated approach with strong empirical support. The method is conceptually simple (p(ỹ|x)=softmax(A o)) yet effective, improving over Duong et al. (2014) in 7–8/8 simulation languages (average 91.7 vs 91.3) and more notably on real low-resource languages (Malagasy 86.3 vs 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs 78.3 and 81.9). The gains on truly low-resource settings are especially compelling.
- Principled treatment of projected-label noise. Modeling a confusion matrix between clean and projected tags directly addresses systematic projection biases (e.g., determiners vs nouns), which prior work handled indirectly via features or heuristics. The two-stage training and separate loss averaging for gold vs projected data are sensible and likely contribute to stability.
- Diagnostic interpretability. The visualization of A across languages offers insight into when projection is trustworthy and where major confusions arise (e.g., Swedish PRT vs X), supporting the paper’s thesis and providing a useful tool for practitioners.
- Thoughtful experimental design. The low-resource setting (≈1000 gold tokens) is realistic; the use of universal tags allows cross-language comparison; baselines include supervised MaxEnt, Duong et al., and BiLSTM variants trained on annotated, projected, and combined data, highlighting why naïve use of projection can harm performance.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to general label-noise modeling. Explicit noise layers or confusion-matrix approaches to noisy supervision are known; the novelty here lies in applying them to cross-lingual projection for POS tagging. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning against prior noisy-label learning methods and a discussion of identifiability (A vs o) and regularization.
- Heuristic handling of non-1:1 alignments. Assigning sentence-level English tag distributions to tokens with many-to-one or NULL alignments is a coarse heuristic that may introduce bias. There is no ablation comparing alternatives (e.g., alignment-confidence-weighted mixtures, neighborhood/backoff with lexical features), nor analysis of its impact on the learned A.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses. Missing: (i) effect of pretraining vs joint-only training; (ii) initialization and regularization of A (e.g., towards identity); (iii) weighting between gold and projected losses; (iv) performance as a function of gold tokens (e.g., 100/500/1000/2000), which would strengthen the low-resource story.
- Baseline breadth. A BiLSTM-CRF decoder and character/morphological features are standard for POS and particularly relevant for morphologically rich low-resource languages (e.g., Kinyarwanda). It would be useful to know if the gains of the noise layer persist atop stronger architectures, or if they are complementary.
- Significance and reproducibility details. Improvements on the Europarl simulations are modest on average; no significance testing is reported. Some implementation details are sparse (initialization of A, early stopping, regularization, exact selection threshold of top alignment-score sentences), and code/data availability is not stated.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Did you use an identity or near-identity initialization or penalties to encourage diagonality? Any constraints (e.g., row- or column-stochasticity) considered?
- How sensitive are results to the weighting between the gold and projected losses? Did you try tuning or curriculum strategies as training progresses?
- Can you report ablations on: (i) omitting pretraining; (ii) varying the number of gold tokens; (iii) alternatives to the sentence-level tag distribution heuristic for non-1:1 alignments?
- Did you try a CRF layer or character embeddings; if so, do the noise-layer gains persist?
- For the real languages, can you provide more details about the parallel corpora (domains, alignment quality) and whether filtering threshold (200k for Europarl; all data for real languages) impacts performance?

Additional comments
- The analysis of learned A is a strong point; consider quantifying sparsity/diagonality (e.g., trace(A) or entropy) and correlating with alignment quality or size of parallel data.
- Please clarify whether English POS tagging errors are absorbed into A (they likely are), and whether better English taggers reduce the need for strong off-diagonal entries in A.
- Reporting confidence intervals or significance tests for the main results would strengthen claims, especially in the simulation setting where gains are small but consistent.
- The method should generalize to mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), which you mention; a small experiment demonstrating this would be valuable.
