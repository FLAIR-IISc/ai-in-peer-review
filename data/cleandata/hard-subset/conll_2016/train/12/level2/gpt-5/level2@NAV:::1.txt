Summary: The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by jointly training on a tiny gold corpus (~1k tokens) and noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an explicit label-noise transformation (a learned confusion matrix A) that maps clean tag distributions to projected ones, enabling end-to-end learning “when to trust” distant supervision. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains, achieving 86.3% and 82.5% accuracy respectively, surpassing prior work.

Contributions (as I see them, in order):
- An explicit, trainable label-noise layer for cross-lingual projections, jointly optimized with the tagger to account for systematic biases in alignments and cross-lingual divergences.
- A simple two-stage training protocol (gold pretraining, then joint training) that effectively leverages minimal gold data and abundant noisy projections.
- Empirical validation across 10 settings, with interpretable noise matrices that illuminate projection errors.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical support: equals or exceeds state-of-the-art across 8 simulated settings and improves on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%). Baselines (BiLSTM on projected only; union without noise) underperform, underscoring the utility of the noise layer.
- Principled modeling: learns a compact confusion matrix that corrects systematic projection errors; interpretable heatmaps corroborate learned patterns (e.g., DET↔NOUN issues).
- Practical relevance: relies on plausible resources (parallel text + ~1k gold tokens), making it useful for real low-resource scenarios.
- Implementation simplicity: the method extends standard BiLSTMs with a linear layer; training is end-to-end.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is somewhat incremental: explicit confusion/label-noise modeling is known in noisy-label and crowdsourcing literature; the paper does not compare to or position against EM-based noise models, loss-correction, or posterior regularization.
- Missing ablations: no analysis of A initialization/regularization, removal of pretraining, varying amounts of gold/projected data, or comparisons to confidence-weighted projection/self-training.
- Alignment dependence: restricting to 1–1 alignments lowers coverage; sensitivity to alignment quality/strategy is not quantified, which is critical in truly low-resource settings.
- Reporting gaps: limited hyperparameter details, no statistical significance tests, resource/runtime not discussed, and code availability unclear, affecting reproducibility.

Questions to Authors (optional):
- How is the noise matrix A initialized/regularized (e.g., diagonal prior)? Any constraints to avoid degenerate mappings?
- Sensitivity curves: performance vs. gold token count and projected data size/quality; impact of using many-to-one/one-to-many or symmetrized alignments?
- Comparisons to alternative noise-aware methods (EM for P(y|ỹ), loss correction, label smoothing, confidence filtering)?
- Can the approach exploit multi-source projections or mismatched tagsets in practice, and with what gains?
- Does fine-tuning without the noise layer at test time help or hurt?
