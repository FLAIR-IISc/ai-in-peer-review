This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, leveraging cross-lingual projection and addressing the inherent noise through an explicit noise layer within a BiLSTM framework. The primary contribution lies in modeling the transformation between projected and gold tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results. A third, less prominent, contribution is the empirical analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix, providing insights into tag confusion patterns.

Strengths:

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The explicit noise layer is a strong point. Addressing the inherent inaccuracies in cross-lingual projection is crucial for effective low-resource learning, and this approach offers a principled way to do so. The learned noise transformation matrix (A) provides valuable insights into the projection quality and the types of errors the model learns to correct. The approach of de-noising during backpropagation is well-motivated.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The consistently strong performance across eight simulated low-resource scenarios, and particularly the state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, provide substantial evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The fact that improvements are seen on both simulated *and* real low-resource languages is compelling.
3. **Well-Defined Experimental Setup:** The use of a consistent evaluation setup (CoNLL data, 1000-token training sets) allows for fair comparison with existing methods, as acknowledged by the authors referencing Duong et al. (2014a). The inclusion of both simulated and real low-resource languages adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
4. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, including the mathematical formulation of the noise layer and the training objective. The diagrams (Figure 2) are helpful in illustrating the model architecture.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Matrix:** While the paper presents the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4), the analysis of these matrices is relatively superficial. A more in-depth analysis, exploring specific patterns of tag confusion and correlating them with linguistic differences between the source and target languages, could provide valuable insights. What specific linguistic phenomena cause the observed noise? For example, are confusions driven by syntactic divergence, or lexical ambiguity?
2. **Dependence on Word Alignment Quality:** The method’s performance is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for cross-lingual projection. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't adequately discuss the sensitivity of the approach to different alignment methods or parameters. A brief investigation into the impact of alignment quality on the results would strengthen the paper.
3. **Pre-training Strategy:** The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data followed by joint training) is a pragmatic choice, but its necessity isn’t fully justified. The paper states it is impractical to train directly, but provides no ablation study demonstrating the performance difference between single-stage and two-stage training. Could the second stage benefit from a more sophisticated initialization strategy than simply using the pretrained weights?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on overall accuracy. A more detailed error analysis, examining specific types of tagging errors made by the model, could reveal further areas for improvement and provide insights into the limitations of the approach.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (A), relating the observed patterns of tag confusion to specific linguistic differences between the source and target languages?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or parameters, and if so, what impact did they have on the results?
3. Was an ablation study performed to determine the benefit of the two-stage training strategy? If so, what were the results?
