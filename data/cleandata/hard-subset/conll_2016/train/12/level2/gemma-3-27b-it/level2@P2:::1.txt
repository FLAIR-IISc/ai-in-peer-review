This paper tackles the challenging problem of low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in this process. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model with a novel “noise layer” designed to learn and compensate for errors introduced during tag projection. The core idea of learning *when* to trust the projected data is well-motivated and the experimental results across both simulated and real low-resource languages are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Explicit Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The most significant contribution is the introduction of the noise layer, which explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and ground truth tags. This allows the model to learn to discount unreliable projections, effectively mitigating the negative impact of noisy data.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate consistently strong performance across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods. This suggests the proposed approach is a robust and effective solution.
3. **Integration of Noise Modeling with Deep Learning:** The work successfully integrates the explicit noise modeling into a deep learning framework (BiLSTM), enabling end-to-end training and benefiting from the representational power of neural networks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of noisy cross-lingual projection and its impact on low-resource POS tagging. The authors effectively motivate the need for a method that can explicitly address this noise.
2. **Novelty of the Noise Layer:** The noise layer is a genuinely novel contribution. While prior work has addressed projection noise through heuristics or constraints, this approach offers a learnable and data-driven solution. The learned transformation matrices provide insights into the types of errors commonly encountered in the projection process (as shown in Figure 3 & 4).
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including both simulated experiments (allowing for controlled comparisons) and experiments on real low-resource languages. The consistent improvement over existing methods strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Strong Baselines:** The comparison against established baselines, including the work of Duong et al. (2014a), provides a clear benchmark for the proposed approach.
5. **Detailed Model Explanation:** The model architecture and training procedure are clearly explained, including the two-stage training process to address the initialization problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Learned Noise Transformation:** While the authors present the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4), a more in-depth analysis of these matrices could provide valuable insights. For example, what are the common patterns of tag confusion? Are there language-specific tendencies in the noise? Investigating these aspects could further refine the model and inform future work.
2. **Dependence on Word Alignment Quality:** The effectiveness of cross-lingual projection is inherently tied to the quality of word alignments. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not extensively explore the impact of different alignment tools or parameters on their results. A brief analysis of the sensitivity to alignment quality would be beneficial.
3. **Computational Cost:** Training deep learning models, particularly BiLSTMs, can be computationally expensive. The paper does not provide details on training time or resource requirements, which may be a limiting factor for some researchers.
4. **Tagset Dependency:** The noise layer relies on the assumption that the tagsets are comparable, potentially limiting its applicability to languages with drastically different tagging schemes. While the authors mention handling mismatching tagsets, the effectiveness of this approach is not thoroughly evaluated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 & 4)? Specifically, what are the most common types of tag confusion observed across different languages?
2. How sensitive are the results to the quality of word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or parameters?
3. What are the computational resources (e.g., GPU memory, training time) required to train the model on the datasets used in your experiments?
4. In cases where the tagsets are significantly different, how does the performance of your model compare to alternative approaches that do not rely on direct tag mapping?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential future work, such as extending the approach to other NLP tasks or exploring different noise modeling techniques.
*   The reference formatting is slightly inconsistent (e.g., some authors have initials, others do not). Ensure consistent formatting throughout.
