This paper proposes a neural network model for low-resource POS tagging that leverages cross-lingual projection and explicitly models the noise inherent in this process. The core idea – learning a “noise transformation” to map projected tags to more accurate representations – is a valuable contribution. The experimental results on both simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, consistently equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The most significant strength is the explicit modeling of projection noise. Previous work often treated projected tags as simply noisy data, but this paper takes a more sophisticated approach by learning a transformation to correct for systematic errors. This is a conceptually sound and practically effective idea. The noise layer, parameterized by matrix A, allows the model to learn which projected tags are reliable and which should be discounted or remapped.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse set of experiments. Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on eight simulated low-resource languages, as well as two real languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), provides strong evidence of the method’s generalizability and effectiveness.  The performance gains on the real languages are particularly noteworthy, as these are inherently more challenging than the simulated settings.
3. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup. The motivation for the work – the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing cross-lingual projection techniques – is well-established. The inclusion of Figure 2 illustrating the model architecture is helpful. 
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation, comparing their approach to several strong baselines, including the previously state-of-the-art method by Duong et al. (2014a). The ablation study with the different BiLSTM configurations (Annotated, Projected, and Ann+Proj) is particularly insightful, demonstrating the benefit of incorporating the noise layer.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Matrix (A):** While the authors present the learned noise transformation matrix A, the analysis is somewhat limited. Providing a more detailed qualitative analysis of the matrix – for example, identifying specific tag confusions that the model learns to correct – would strengthen the paper.  The current discussion focuses on general patterns (strong diagonal, ignoring certain combinations) but lacks specific examples.
2. **Dependency on Word Alignment Quality:** The performance of the entire system is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for projection. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of alignment errors, and perhaps some experimentation with different alignment tools or filtering strategies, would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, hidden layer size). It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of how performance varies with different hyperparameter settings. Although 128 dimensions for the embeddings and hidden layers is a reasonable starting point, knowing the sensitivity could inform future research.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A deeper dive into *what* errors the model still makes after applying the noise layer would be valuable.  For example, are there specific POS tags that are consistently mispredicted, even with the noise correction? Understanding the remaining error patterns could suggest avenues for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more specific examples of tag confusions that are corrected by the learned noise transformation matrix A?  For instance, what are the most frequent incorrect mappings learned by the model, and why do you think these errors occur?
2. Have you experimented with different word alignment tools or filtering strategies to assess the impact of alignment quality on the overall performance?
3. How sensitive is the model to the size of the initial gold standard annotated data? Would performance still improve with even fewer (e.g., 100) gold standard tokens?
4. Is the choice of using a linear transformation (matrix A) for modeling the noise justified, or have you explored more complex non-linear transformations?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The phrase "uniformly equals or exceeds" in the abstract feels slightly strong, as some results are tied. "Equals or exceeds" would be more accurate.
*  Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed model compared to other methods.
*  It would be beneficial to mention potential applications beyond POS tagging where this noise modeling technique could be applied.
