## Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection, a technique known to introduce noise. The core contribution is a neural network model incorporating an explicit "noise layer" to learn to correct errors arising from the projection process. This is framed as joint learning over gold-standard and projected data, allowing the model to dynamically assess the reliability of projected tags. The paper demonstrates strong performance, equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art results on simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

The strength of this work lies in its practical approach to a common problem in low-resource NLP. The explicit modeling of projection noise is a valuable contribution, offering a more nuanced way to integrate distant supervision than simply treating all projected data equally. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering both simulated and real-world scenarios, and the results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) offers insightful qualitative analysis of how the model learns to handle projection errors.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The introduction lacks sufficient motivation for *why* a noise layer is a better solution than existing heuristic approaches to cleaning projected data – a more thorough comparison would be valuable. While the model architecture is clearly presented, the choice of a BiLSTM seems somewhat conventional; justification for this choice over other sequence modeling techniques (e.g., Transformers) is missing. Furthermore, the experimental setup, while comprehensive, lacks detailed analysis of *where* the model succeeds and fails, and *what* types of projection errors are most effectively mitigated by the noise layer. Finally, while the paper acknowledges limitations, a discussion of the potential impact of word alignment quality on performance would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why a noise layer was chosen over more traditional methods for mitigating projection errors (e.g., filtering based on alignment scores)?
2.  What is the computational cost associated with the noise layer, and how does it impact training time compared to a standard BiLSTM model?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of projection errors that the model handles well versus those that remain challenging?
