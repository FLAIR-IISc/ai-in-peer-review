- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM sequence-tagging model that jointly learns from a tiny gold corpus and noisy cross-lingual projected tags using an explicit noise layer to correct projection errors.

- Contribution 1: New neural model for low-resource sequence tagging combining tiny gold annotations with distant supervision from cross-lingual projection.

- Contribution 2: BiLSTM with an explicit noise layer that models tagset transformation and learns when/how much to trust projected data.

- Contribution 3: Evaluation on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), reporting state-of-the-art results.

- Strength 1: Addresses a critical problem of scarce annotated data and noisy distant supervision in low-resource languages.

- Strength 2: Novel and effective method that corrects projection errors and achieves strong empirical performance.

- Strength 3: Thorough evaluation including multiple baselines and analysis of the noise layer.

- Strength 4: Model is generalizable to other sequence-tagging tasks and compatible with other architectures.

- Strength 5: Well-written paper with clear explanation of model and components.

- Weakness 1: No clear definition of what counts as a "small" gold corpus, so the assumption may not generalize.

- Weakness 2: Requires substantial parallel data, which may be unavailable for many low-resource languages.

- Weakness 3: Missing detailed analysis of computational resources needed to train the model.

- Weakness 4: Performance may depend heavily on the quality of potentially noisy word alignments.

- Question 1: What are the computational resource requirements and how can the method be adapted for limited-resource compute environments?

- Question 2: How does the model perform when the gold annotated corpus is moderate-sized or large?

- Question 3: Can you provide deeper analysis of the noise layer, including types of errors it corrects and per-language behavior?

- Question 4: Have you considered or compared transfer learning or multi-task learning alternatives to improve low-resource performance?
