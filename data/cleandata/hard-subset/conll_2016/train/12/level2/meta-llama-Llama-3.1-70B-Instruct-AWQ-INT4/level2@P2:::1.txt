**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) network, which produces two types of output: gold tags generated directly from the hidden state of the neural network, and uncertain projected tags generated after applying a further linear transformation. The transformation encodes the mapping between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, and learning when and how much to trust the projected data.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a new neural network model for sequence tagging in a low-resource language, suitable for training with both a tiny gold standard annotated corpus and distant supervision using cross-lingual tag projection.
2. The model uses a bidirectional LSTM network with a noise layer to explicitly model the transformation between tagsets, allowing for the correction of errors from cross-lingual projection.
3. The paper evaluates the approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant problem in low-resource languages, where annotated data is scarce, and distant supervision is often noisy and unreliable.
2. The proposed model is novel and effective in correcting errors from cross-lingual projection, achieving state-of-the-art results in several languages.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the approach, including comparisons with several baselines and an analysis of the noise layer's performance.
4. The model is generalizable to other sequence tagging tasks and can be used with other modeling approaches.
5. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that the gold standard annotated corpus is small, but it does not provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "small" corpus. This assumption may not hold for all low-resource languages.
2. The model requires a significant amount of parallel data, which may not be available for all low-resource languages.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train the model, which may be a concern for low-resource languages with limited computational resources.
4. The model's performance may be sensitive to the quality of the word alignments, which can be noisy and unreliable.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the computational resources required to train the model, and how it can be adapted to low-resource languages with limited computational resources?
2. How does the model perform when the gold standard annotated corpus is not small, but rather moderate-sized or large?
3. Can you provide more insights into the noise layer's performance, including an analysis of the types of errors it corrects and the languages it performs best on?
4. Have you considered using other modeling approaches, such as transfer learning or multi-task learning, to improve the model's performance on low-resource languages?
