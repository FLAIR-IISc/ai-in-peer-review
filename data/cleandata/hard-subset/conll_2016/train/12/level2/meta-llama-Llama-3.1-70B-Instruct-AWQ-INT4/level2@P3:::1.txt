**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages using cross-lingual projection. The authors introduce a neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The model is trained jointly on gold standard annotated data and projected data, and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to sequence tagging**: The authors propose a new neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer.
2. **Joint training on gold and projected data**: The model is trained jointly on gold standard annotated data and projected data, allowing it to learn from both sources of information.
3. **Evaluation on low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate its effectiveness in these settings.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of projected data**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can effectively use projected data to improve POS tagging accuracy, even in low-resource languages.
2. **Robustness to noise**: The explicit noise layer in the model allows it to learn to correct errors from cross-lingual projection, making it more robust to noise in the projected data.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: The authors demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: While the authors evaluate their approach on two real low-resource languages, the evaluation is limited to only two languages, and more extensive evaluation on other languages would be beneficial.
2. **Assumes availability of parallel data**: The approach assumes the availability of parallel data, which may not always be available for low-resource languages.
3. **Computational resources**: The approach requires significant computational resources, which may be a limitation for some researchers or organizations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other low-resource languages, and what challenges do they anticipate in doing so?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the noise transformation matrix A, and how it is learned during training?
3. How does the approach handle cases where the projected data is highly noisy or biased, and what strategies can be used to mitigate these issues?
