**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The main contributions of this work are: (1) the introduction of a noise layer to model the transformation between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, (2) the integration of this model into a deep neural network, and (3) the evaluation of the approach on simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of noisy projected data**: The proposed approach demonstrates a significant improvement in POS tagging accuracy by effectively utilizing noisy projected data. This is a crucial contribution, as it addresses the challenge of dealing with noisy annotations in low-resource languages.
2. **Novel noise layer**: The introduction of a noise layer to model the transformation between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags is a novel contribution. This layer allows the model to learn the patterns of noise in the projected data and improve the overall performance.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on both simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited analysis of the noise layer**: While the paper presents the noise layer as a key contribution, there is limited analysis of its behavior and impact on the overall performance. A more in-depth analysis of the noise layer's properties and its relationship with the projected data would strengthen the paper.
2. **Dependence on high-quality parallel data**: The approach relies heavily on high-quality parallel data, which may not always be available for low-resource languages. The paper could benefit from a discussion on how to adapt the approach to scenarios with limited or low-quality parallel data.
3. **Comparison to other approaches**: While the paper compares the proposed approach to several baselines, it would be beneficial to include a comparison to other state-of-the-art approaches that also utilize noisy projected data.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more insights into the behavior of the noise layer, such as its sensitivity to different types of noise in the projected data?
2. How do you plan to adapt the approach to scenarios with limited or low-quality parallel data?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed approach to other state-of-the-art approaches that utilize noisy projected data?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The experimental results are impressive, and the paper demonstrates a significant contribution to the field of low-resource language processing. However, addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.
