## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in this process. The authors propose a BiLSTM model with a novel “noise layer” that learns to correct errors from projected tags, jointly training on both gold-standard and projected data. The core idea of learning *when* to trust the projected data is compelling and well-motivated, given the known issues of word alignment errors and syntactic divergence.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of the noise layer and its integration into a deep learning framework for jointly learning from gold and projected data. This allows the model to effectively de-noise the projected data during training, leading to improved tagging accuracy. A second contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent performance gains over existing methods. Finally, the analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of errors present in the projected data and how the model learns to mitigate them.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages, demonstrating its effectiveness. The results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly noteworthy.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** Explicitly modeling projection noise as a learnable transformation is a novel and insightful approach. This moves beyond simply filtering or weighting projected data and allows the model to actively learn from the errors.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across a diverse set of languages and datasets strengthens the validity of the findings.
*   **Interpretability:** The visualization of the noise transformation matrices provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior and the nature of projection errors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Noise Matrix:** While the visualization of the noise matrix is helpful, a more in-depth analysis of the learned patterns would be beneficial. For example, are certain types of errors (e.g., alignment errors) consistently captured in the matrix?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices, particularly those related to the noise layer (e.g., the size of the A matrix).
*   **Data Preprocessing Details:** More details on the data preprocessing steps, especially for the real-world languages, would be helpful for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, focusing on specific error patterns and how they are captured?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the size of the A matrix in the noise layer? Did you experiment with different sizes?
*   What specific preprocessing steps were applied to the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda data, beyond the standard tokenization?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
