**Summary and Contributions:**

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by effectively combining a small set of gold-standard annotated data with a larger, noisy dataset created via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger augmented with a novel, explicit "noise layer." This layer is a trainable transformation matrix that models the systematic errors and confusion patterns between the true POS tags and the noisy projected tags. The entire model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and well-motivated neural architecture that explicitly models the noise inherent in distant supervision. The learnable noise layer provides an elegant mechanism for the model to "learn when to trust" the projected labels, moving beyond heuristic filtering or implicit feature engineering.
2.  A comprehensive and strong empirical evaluation on ten languages (eight simulated low-resource and two genuine low-resource). The proposed method is shown to consistently achieve state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong baselines and previous work on the same task.

**Strengths:**

1.  The core idea of the explicit noise layer is highly innovative and elegant. It directly tackles the central challenge of using projected data—its unreliability—by parameterizing the noise process itself. The visualization of the learned transformation matrix provides compelling qualitative evidence that the model is capturing meaningful cross-lingual divergences and alignment errors.
2.  The experimental results are robust and convincing. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over several baselines, including a standard BiLSTM and the previous state-of-the-art model from Duong et al. (2014a), across a wide array of languages. The inclusion of both simulated and real-world low-resource settings (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) strongly supports the general applicability of the method.
3.  The work has significant practical implications for the low-resource NLP community. It offers a principled and effective strategy for leveraging readily available parallel corpora to bootstrap high-quality models from minimal human annotation, a common scenario for thousands of languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The paper lacks a discussion of its potential limitations. The performance is likely dependent on the quality of the initial word alignments and the typological distance between the source and target languages. An analysis of how the model copes with lower-quality parallel data or more distant language pairs would make the contribution even stronger.
2.  While the results are strong, the analysis could be deepened. For example, an ablation study showing performance sensitivity to the amount of gold data (e.g., 100 vs. 500 vs. 1000 tokens) would better illustrate the data efficiency of the proposed technique and provide practical guidance for its application.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Your method effectively captures tag confusion. Have you analyzed how its performance correlates with the quality of the underlying word alignments? For instance, does the model's advantage over baselines increase when alignment quality is lower, suggesting it is more robust to noise?
2.  The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data first) is presented as a practical necessity. Did you experiment with a fully joint training from scratch, and if so, how significant was the performance degradation? This would help clarify the importance of the pre-training step.
