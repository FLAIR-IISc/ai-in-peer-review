**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a method for Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging in low-resource settings by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core of the contribution is a neural network architecture (a BiLSTM) that is jointly trained on both data sources. To handle the noisy labels from projection, the authors introduce an explicit "noise layer" — a linear transformation applied to the network's output probabilities before computing the loss for the projected data. This layer is designed to explicitly model the systematic errors and confusions inherent in the projection process, allowing the model to learn a "de-noised" representation from the noisy data.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A novel and intuitive architecture for jointly learning from clean and noisy supervision in a sequence tagging task. The explicit modeling of the noise via a learnable transformation matrix is a simple but powerful idea that allows for effective end-to-end training.
2.  Strong empirical results that establish a new state-of-the-art on two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) and demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines across eight simulated low-resource languages.
3.  An insightful analysis and visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix, which offers some interpretability of the types of errors introduced by the cross-lingual projection process for different language pairs.

**Strengths**
1.  **Elegant and effective modeling of noise:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel approach to handling noisy labels. Instead of relying on heuristics to filter the projected data or treating projected tags as features, the model learns to characterize the noise directly. The proposed noise layer (the matrix A) is a simple, low-parameter addition to a standard BiLSTM tagger that allows the model to effectively discount or transform the error signal from the noisy data. This is an elegant way to integrate distant supervision.
2.  **Comprehensive and convincing evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough and well-designed. The authors evaluate on a wide range of languages, including eight simulated low-resource settings (which allows for direct comparison with prior work) and two genuinely low-resource languages (which demonstrates real-world applicability). The performance gains are consistent and significant, particularly on the real low-resource languages, where the proposed model surpasses the previous state-of-the-art by a clear margin (e.g., 4.2% absolute improvement for Kinyarwanda over Duong et al.).
3.  **Strong set of baselines:** The paper includes a thoughtful set of baselines that help to isolate the contribution of the proposed method. By comparing against a BiLSTM trained only on annotated data, only on projected data, and on the simple union of the two, the authors clearly demonstrate that naive uses of projected data are suboptimal and that their joint modeling approach is critical to its success. The comparison to the state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a) further solidifies the paper's contribution.
4.  **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained well and supported by an effective diagram (Figure 2). The analysis of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 is a valuable addition that helps build intuition for what the model is learning.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Unclear sensitivity to the two-stage training procedure:** The authors state that the model is pretrained on the small clean dataset before being jointly trained on both data sources, as direct joint training is "impractical". This reliance on pre-training is a potential weakness. It would strengthen the paper to include an ablation study that quantifies the impact of this pre-training step. How much does performance degrade if the model is trained jointly from scratch? This would help clarify whether the joint objective is unstable or simply slow to converge, and it would give a better sense of the model's robustness.
2.  **Lack of comparison to related multi-task learning frameworks:** The proposed joint training scheme can be viewed as a form of multi-task learning, where predicting gold tags is the primary task and predicting projected tags is an auxiliary task. The noise layer acts as a task-specific final layer for the auxiliary task. It would be beneficial to frame the contribution in this context and potentially compare it to a more standard multi-task setup (e.g., simply having two separate softmax classifiers that share the BiLSTM layers). This would help to clarify whether the specific formulation of the noise layer (`A*o_t`) is superior to other ways of combining the two supervisory signals.
3.  **Ambiguous handling of alignment uncertainty:** The method for handling tokens that are unaligned or multi-aligned is to assign a "distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." This seems like a weak and potentially noisy heuristic, as the sentence-level distribution may not be relevant for a specific word. Given that alignment quality is a major source of noise in projection, the sensitivity of the model to this specific choice is an important factor. An analysis of alternative strategies (e.g., ignoring these tokens, or using a uniform distribution) would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please provide results for a version of your model trained jointly from a random initialization, without the pre-training stage on the 1000 annotated tokens? This would help in understanding the stability and robustness of the proposed joint loss function.
2.  The experiments are conducted with a fixed size of 1000 annotated tokens. How does the performance gain from your method change as the amount of available annotated data varies? For example, would the relative improvement be even larger with only 100 annotated tokens, or would it diminish as you approach 5000 annotated tokens?
