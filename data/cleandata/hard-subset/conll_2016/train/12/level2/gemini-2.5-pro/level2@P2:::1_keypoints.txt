- Tackles POS tagging for low-resource languages by combining small gold datasets with large noisy projected tags from English via alignments.  
- Proposes a trainable linear "noise layer" that models a confusion matrix between true tags and noisy projected tags to de-noise distant supervision.  
- Contribution: a principled method to integrate distant supervision with gold data for sequence tagging via an explicit noise transformation.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation across eight simulated European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).  
- Strength: elegant, interpretable noise-layer formulation (trainable KxK matrix) with few extra parameters and a natural joint loss.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation with diverse languages and strong baselines/ablations isolating the noise layer's effect.  
- Strength: state-of-the-art or competitive results, notably +1.0% on Malagasy and +4.2%/+0.6% improvements on Kinyarwanda.  
- Strength: good analysis and interpretability via visualized noise matrices showing learned systematic confusions and corpus-quality effects.  
- Weakness: relies on a two-stage training curriculum (pretrain on gold, then joint training), raising concerns about optimization and simplicity.  
- Weakness: limited discussion/analysis of projection heuristics, especially handling multi-aligned or unaligned tokens using sentence-level tag distributions.  
- Weakness: base architecture (BiLSTM) is standard; main novelty is the noise-handling method rather than the neural architecture itself.  
- Question: clarify necessity of two-stage training and whether alternative initializations (e.g., scaled identity for A) permit end-to-end training from scratch.  
- Question: report proportion of projected tokens using the sentence-level tag heuristic and effect on performance if those tokens are omitted.  
- Question: consider whether a non-linear noise model (e.g., MLP) could better capture noise than the proposed linear transformation.
