**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a larger amount of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to explicitly model the noise inherent in the projection process rather than treating the projected labels as ground truth.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel neural architecture for sequence tagging that incorporates an explicit "noise layer". This layer, parameterized by a transformation matrix, learns the systematic error patterns between the model's predictions and the noisy projected labels. This allows the model to be trained jointly on both a small clean dataset and a large noisy dataset in a principled manner.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method. The model is shown to achieve state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of languages, including eight simulated low-resource settings from Europarl and two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), outperforming previous methods that use the same data conditions.
3.  An effective analysis of the learned noise patterns. By visualizing the learned transformation matrix, the paper provides insight into the model's behavior and the types of noise it corrects for in different language pairs.

**Strengths**
1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model**: The proposed noise layer is an intuitive and elegant solution to the common problem of learning from noisy, distantly supervised data. Instead of relying on complex heuristics to filter the projected data, the model learns to characterize and adapt to the noise directly. The joint training objective, which simultaneously optimizes for correctness on gold data and for matching the noise distribution on projected data, is principled and compelling.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation**: The experimental setup is rigorous and extensive. Evaluating on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages demonstrates the general applicability of the approach. The work carefully follows the experimental design of prior work (Duong et al., 2014a), ensuring fair and direct comparisons. The inclusion of several baselines, including a strong BiLSTM trained on various data combinations and the previous state-of-the-art, clearly establishes the significant performance gains achieved by the proposed model.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Interpretability**: The paper goes beyond simply reporting accuracy scores by providing a qualitative analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4). This analysis gives valuable insight into what the model learns, showing that it captures both the strong signal in the data (the dominant diagonal) and systematic, language-specific confusion patterns (the off-diagonal elements). This interpretability strengthens the claims and makes the contribution more scientifically sound.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed method is explained lucidly, and the model architecture is effectively illustrated in Figure 2.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Ambiguity in the Projection Method for Unaligned Words**: Section 3.1 describes the method for handling tokens that are aligned to multiple English words or none at all. The strategy of assigning "a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence" seems to provide a very weak and potentially uninformative signal. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed justification for this choice over other common heuristics (e.g., using a special NULL tag, or ignoring such tokens). An analysis of the frequency of these cases and their impact on training would be beneficial.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on the Training Procedure**: The paper mentions a two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, followed by joint training) as essential for good performance, but provides few implementation details. For reproducibility and a full understanding of the method's stability, it would be helpful to know more about this procedure. For example, what were the criteria for stopping the pre-training phase? How sensitive is the final model's performance to the duration of this pre-training? Substantiating the claim that single-stage training is "impractical" with empirical results would also make the motivation for the two-stage approach more concrete.
3.  **Generalizability of the Noise Layer is Claimed but Not Shown**: The contribution is presented as a general technique for handling noisy labels, applicable to other classifiers. However, the experiments are limited to a BiLSTM base model. While the BiLSTM is a strong model, demonstrating that the noise layer also provides a significant boost when added to a simpler model (like the MaxEnt classifier used in the Duong et al. baseline) would decouple the gains of the noise layer from the gains of using a more powerful underlying model. This would more robustly support the claim of the noise layer's general utility.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could the paper provide more justification for the chosen projection strategy for unaligned/multi-aligned words? How does this strategy compare to alternatives, and what proportion of the projected data is labeled in this manner?
2.  Could more details be provided about the two-stage training regime? Specifically, what were the hyperparameters or convergence criteria for the pre-training stage, and were any experiments conducted to measure the performance impact of this stage?
3.  The paper mentions that the noise matrix `A` can be rectangular to handle mismatched tagsets. Given that even the universal tagset might not be exhaustive for all languages or annotation schemes, was this capability explored? It seems like a potentially valuable feature for real-world applications.
