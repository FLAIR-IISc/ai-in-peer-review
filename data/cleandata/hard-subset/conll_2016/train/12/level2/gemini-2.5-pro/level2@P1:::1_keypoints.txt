- Novel joint BiLSTM architecture that learns from small gold data and larger noisy projected data using a learnable linear "noise layer" to model projection errors.
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains, achieving new SOTA on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda and consistent improvements across eight simulated low-resource languages.
- Provides analysis and visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrix to interpret projection error patterns.
- Elegant and low-parameter modeling of noise via matrix A that integrates distant supervision without heuristic filtering.
- Comprehensive and convincing evaluation protocol covering multiple languages, simulated settings, and real low-resource cases with significant gains (e.g., +4.2% for Kinyarwanda).
- Uses a strong set of baselines (gold-only, projected-only, union, and prior SOTA) that isolate the contribution of the joint/noise approach.
- Clear writing, good presentation, and helpful figures explaining the model and noise analyses.
- Reliance on a two-stage pretraining (gold first) is unclear; an ablation is needed to quantify the necessity and impact of pretraining versus joint training from scratch.
- No comparison to standard multi-task learning baselines (e.g., separate softmax heads) to show whether the specific noise-layer formulation is superior.
- Handling of unaligned or multi-aligned tokens by using sentence-level tag distributions is a weak heuristic and needs analysis of alternatives or sensitivity.
- Request for results of training jointly from random initialization (no pretraining) to assess stability and robustness of the joint loss.
- Request for analysis of performance as the amount of annotated data varies (e.g., 100, 1000, 5000 tokens) to understand when the method provides the largest benefit.
