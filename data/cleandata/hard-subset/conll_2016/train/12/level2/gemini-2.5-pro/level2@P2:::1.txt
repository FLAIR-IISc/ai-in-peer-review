### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, where only a small amount of gold-standard annotated data is available, but larger parallel corpora exist. The authors propose a method to leverage noisy POS tags projected from a high-resource language (English) via word alignments. The core idea is to jointly train a BiLSTM sequence tagger on both the small clean dataset and the large noisy dataset. The key contribution is a novel "noise layer" — a trainable linear transformation — that explicitly models the systematic errors and biases present in the projected tags. This layer allows the model to learn a confusion matrix between the true tag distribution and the noisy projected tag distribution, effectively learning when and how to trust the distant supervision signal. The main contributions are:

1.  A novel and principled method for integrating distant supervision with gold-standard data for sequence tagging. The proposed noise layer explicitly models the transformation between clean and noisy label spaces, allowing the model to effectively "de-noise" the training signal from the projected data. This is a generalizable technique applicable to other tasks using noisy labels.
2.  A strong empirical validation of the proposed model. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive results on a comprehensive set of eight simulated low-resource European languages and, more importantly, on two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Intuitive Model:** The proposed noise layer is a simple yet powerful mechanism for handling noisy labels. Instead of treating projected tags as noisy versions of the true label and mixing them in the training data, or using them as features, the model explicitly learns the relationship between the two. The formulation of this as a trainable KxK matrix `A` that transforms the model's output probabilities to match the noisy distribution is clean, interpretable, and requires very few additional parameters. The joint loss function is a natural consequence of this modeling choice.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors test on a wide variety of languages (eight simulated, two real), demonstrating the general applicability of their approach. The comparisons are well-chosen and include crucial baselines: a strong supervised model (MaxEnt), the previous state-of-the-art on this task (Duong et al., 2014a), and important ablations of their own model (BiLSTM trained only on annotated data, only on projected data, and on a simple union of both). This clearly isolates the performance gain attributable to the proposed noise layer.

3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance in Realistic Scenarios:** The model consistently outperforms strong baselines across all languages. The improvements are not just marginal; the gains on the real low-resource languages Malagasy (+1.0% over SOTA) and Kinyarwanda (+4.2% over Duong et al. and +0.6% over Garrette et al.) are particularly significant. This demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method for the target application of low-resource NLP.

4.  **Good Analysis and Interpretability:** The authors provide visualizations of the learned noise matrix `A` for all languages (Figures 3 and 4). This analysis provides valuable insight into what the model is learning. The matrices confirm that the model learns a strong diagonal (projected tags are often correct) but also captures systematic confusions (e.g., between PRT and X in Swedish). Furthermore, the comparison between the matrices for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda persuasively links the higher degree of noise learned for Kinyarwanda to the smaller, lower-quality parallel corpus available for that language. This analysis strongly supports the claim that the model is indeed learning to characterize the noise.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on a Specific Training Curriculum:** The paper states that "Directly training the whole deep neural network with random initialization is impractical" and thus a two-stage training process is used (pre-training on gold data, then joint training). This is a crucial implementation detail that might limit the method's simplicity. It suggests that the joint objective function may have undesirable local minima. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion or analysis of this point. For example, how much pre-training is necessary? Have the authors experimented with other initialization strategies for the noise matrix `A` (e.g., initializing as an identity matrix) to enable end-to-end training from scratch?

2.  **Limited Discussion of the Projection Heuristics:** The method for generating the "distant" labels is a critical part of the pipeline. The paper states that for tokens aligned to many English words or none, "we assign a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." This seems like a very weak and potentially uninformative prior. It would be useful to know how sensitive the model is to this choice. An analysis comparing this heuristic to simply ignoring such tokens in the projected training set would help clarify how much signal is being extracted from these uncertain cases.

3.  **Novelty of the Base Architecture:** While the overall method is novel, the base model is a standard BiLSTM for sequence tagging, which was a well-established architecture by 2016. The paper's contribution lies almost entirely in the addition of the noise layer and the joint training objective. This is not a major weakness, as the contribution is significant, but the authors should ensure the framing of the paper clearly emphasizes that the novelty is in the *method for handling noisy supervision*, not in the neural architecture itself.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the necessity of the two-stage training process? Have you experimented with alternative initializations for the noise matrix `A`, such as a scaled identity matrix, to see if stable joint training from scratch is possible?

2.  The heuristic for handling unaligned or multi-aligned words (assigning a sentence-level tag frequency distribution) seems quite noisy. What proportion of the projected training data tokens fall into this category? How does the model's performance change if these tokens are simply omitted from the projected training data?

3.  The noise layer is a linear transformation. Have you considered whether a more complex, non-linear transformation (e.g., a small MLP) could model the noise more effectively, or do you believe a linear mapping is sufficient to capture the main error patterns in cross-lingual projection?
