

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel neural approach for low-resource POS tagging that jointly leverages small amounts of gold-standard data (1,000 tokens) and noisy cross-lingual projected annotations. The key contribution is a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from English parallel data) and true POS tags, learning when and how to trust the projected data. The authors frame this as joint learning over two corpora, with a bidirectional LSTM followed by a transformation matrix A that corrects projection errors. The second contribution is a comprehensive evaluation on eight simulated low-resource languages (using Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements over baselines. The third contribution is the demonstration of how the learned noise matrix reveals language-specific error patterns (e.g., confusion between PRT/X in Swedish).

**Strengths**  
1. The noise layer effectively leverages noisy projected data without requiring manual error correction, achieving state-of-the-art results on both simulated and real low-resource settings (86.3% on Malagasy, 82.5% on Kinyarwanda).  
2. The method is generalizable to other noisy supervision scenarios (e.g., crowdsourcing), with the noise matrix providing interpretable insights into projection errors.  
3. The evaluation design is rigorous, using standardized splits and comparing against multiple strong baselines (including Duong et al.'s SOTA), with ablation studies confirming the noise layer's necessity.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The claimed "novelty" of modeling noise explicitly is overstated; prior work (e.g., Das & Petrov, 2011) used graph-based label propagation to handle projection noise, though not via a neural noise layer.  
2. The parallel data quality for real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) is limited (100k/11k sentences), raising questions about scalability to languages with even sparser resources.  
3. The method assumes reliable word alignments (via Giza++), which may not hold for morphologically complex or low-resource languages with poor alignment tools.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does the noise layer perform when word alignments are generated via unsupervised methods (e.g., fast_align) rather than IBM model 3?  
2. Could the noise matrix A be initialized using linguistic knowledge (e.g., tagset mappings) to reduce training time for extremely low-resource scenarios?
