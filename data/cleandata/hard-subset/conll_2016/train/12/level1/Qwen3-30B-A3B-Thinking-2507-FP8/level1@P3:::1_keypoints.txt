- Proposes a novel neural POS-tagger that models noise in cross-lingual projected tags via a learnable transformation matrix A.
- Evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European-language settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), claiming state-of-the-art results.
- Provides detailed analysis of learned noise matrices to explain projection errors.

- Novel and well-motivated approach that directly learns noise transformations instead of heuristic cleaning.
- Strong empirical validation showing consistent improvements and good performance with only 1,000 gold tokens.
- Learned noise matrices give useful language-specific insights into projection errors.

- Missing comparisons to more recent neural sequence-tagging methods beyond Duong et al. (2014a).
- Two-stage training procedure is underspecified (initialization of A, hyperparameters, implementation details) hindering reproducibility.
- Limited discussion of method limitations and cases where the noise layer might fail or not generalize to other noisy supervision types.
- Claims about using "only 1,000 tokens" lack detail on token selection and representativeness.

- How does the noise layer compare to simpler confidence-weighted losses based on alignment quality?
- What happens if the model is trained end-to-end from scratch without pretraining—does the noise layer still help?
- Can the noise layer compensate for weaker alignment models (e.g., IBM Model 1 versus Model 3)?

- Overall: strong, novel contribution with convincing results, but needs more recent-baseline comparisons and clearer training/selection details.
