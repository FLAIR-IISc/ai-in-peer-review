- Proposes a neural method for low-resource POS tagging that jointly uses small gold data (1k tokens) and noisy cross-lingual projected annotations.  
- Introduces a noise layer (transformation matrix A) on top of a BiLSTM to model how projected tags map to true POS tags during joint training.  
- Evaluates on eight simulated low-resource languages (Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements.  
- Shows the learned noise matrix reveals language-specific projection error patterns (e.g., PRT/X confusions in Swedish).  
- Strength: Noise layer effectively leverages noisy projections without manual correction, achieving SOTA (86.3% Malagasy, 82.5% Kinyarwanda).  
- Strength: Method generalizes to other noisy supervision scenarios and provides interpretable error analyses via the noise matrix.  
- Strength: Rigorous evaluation with standardized splits, multiple strong baselines (including Duong et al.), and ablation studies confirming the noise layer’s necessity.  
- Weakness: Novelty claim is overstated given prior work on handling projection noise (e.g., Das & Petrov, 2011), though prior methods were non-neural.  
- Weakness: Real-language parallel data sizes (100k/11k sentences) are limited, raising scalability concerns for even lower-resource languages.  
- Weakness: Method relies on reliable word alignments (Giza++), which may fail for morphologically complex or poorly resourced languages.  
- Question: How does the noise layer perform with alignments from unsupervised tools (e.g., fast_align) instead of IBM model 3?  
- Question: Can the noise matrix A be initialized using linguistic/tagset mappings to speed training in extremely low-resource settings?
