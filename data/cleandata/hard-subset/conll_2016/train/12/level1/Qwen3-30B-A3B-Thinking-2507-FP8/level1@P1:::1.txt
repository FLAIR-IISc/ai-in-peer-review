

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English) using parallel data. The authors propose a novel neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a "noise layer" that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection. The model jointly trains on a small amount of gold-standard annotated data (1,000 tokens) and the noisy projected data, using a BiLSTM with an additional transformation matrix that maps projected tags to the true tag distribution.

The main contributions of this work, in order of significance, are:

1. **A novel noise modeling approach**: The paper introduces an explicit noise layer that models the transformation between projected tags and true tags, rather than relying on heuristics or implicit modeling as previous work did. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could generalize beyond POS tagging to other NLP tasks with noisy supervision.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The approach is rigorously evaluated on eight simulated low-resource settings (using European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), consistently outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Insightful analysis of noise patterns**: The learned noise matrices provide valuable insights into the specific patterns of error in cross-lingual projection, revealing which tag pairs are commonly confused (e.g., NOUN vs. PRON in Spanish) and which tags are reliably projected.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual novelty and practical impact**: The explicit noise layer represents a significant departure from previous approaches that either ignored the noise or attempted to implicitly model it through joint feature sets. This is not just a minor improvement but a fundamentally different approach to handling noisy distant supervision. The practical impact is substantial, as the method requires only 1,000 gold tokens and leverages widely available parallel data, making it highly applicable to real-world low-resource scenarios.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across both simulated low-resource settings (8 languages) and real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvement over all baselines. The results are convincing, with the proposed method achieving 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda, outperforming previous state-of-the-art by 1-3% in both cases.

3. **Insightful noise analysis**: The visualization of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into the nature of the noise in cross-lingual projection. These matrices reveal specific patterns of error (e.g., Swedish confusing PRT and X tags, Malagasy mapping nouns to adjectives) that would be difficult to discern through other means. This analysis not only validates the approach but also provides useful diagnostic information for future work.

4. **Clear technical contribution**: The paper presents a well-structured neural network architecture with a clear explanation of how the noise layer integrates with the BiLSTM. The mathematical formulation (Equations 1-3) is precise and the two-stage training procedure is well-motivated, addressing the practical challenge of training with noisy data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer. It would be valuable to see how much of the improvement comes from the noise layer versus the use of BiLSTM versus the joint training approach. For example, how does the model perform without the noise layer (i.e., just using the union of gold and projected data) versus with it?

2. **Limited discussion of failure modes**: The paper does not adequately discuss when the approach fails or what its limitations are. For instance, how does the model perform with extremely poor word alignments or when the source and target languages have substantial syntactic divergence? The analysis of noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 shows some patterns of error, but the paper doesn't explore how these patterns might affect model performance in different scenarios.

3. **Comparison with more recent work**: The paper compares primarily with Duong et al. (2014a), but doesn't address how the method might compare with more recent approaches to low-resource POS tagging (e.g., from 2015-2016) that might have addressed similar challenges. This limits the context of the contribution within the current state of the field.

4. **Insufficient implementation details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about the training procedure, particularly the two-stage training process. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to the choice of pretraining data size or the specific implementation of the noise layer. The paper states "we use the clean annotated data to pretrain the network" but doesn't specify how much of the gold data is used for pretraining versus the joint training phase.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the two-stage training process? Specifically, what proportion of the gold-standard data is used for pretraining versus joint training, and how sensitive are the results to this choice?

2. How does your approach compare with more recent methods for low-resource POS tagging, such as those using transfer learning from multilingual models or more sophisticated alignment techniques?

3. Can you provide more insight into the failure modes of your approach? For example, are there specific language pairs or tag types where the noise layer fails to improve performance, and why?
