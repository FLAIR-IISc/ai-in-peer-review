

# Review for "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by proposing a novel neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags. The authors introduce a noise layer that learns to transform projected tags into a more accurate representation, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. The main contributions are:

1. A novel noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and gold standard tags, enabling the model to learn when to trust projected data.
2. A comprehensive evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).
3. Insightful visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices, revealing patterns of error that the model learns to correct.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated approach to a significant problem**: The paper identifies a critical gap in existing cross-lingual POS projection methods—the assumption that projected tags can be used directly without accounting for their inherent noise. The authors' solution of explicitly modeling the noise transformation through a simple yet effective layer is both novel and well-justified. Their approach differs fundamentally from previous work that either ignored the noise or attempted to clean it before training.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation across diverse settings**: The paper's evaluation is thorough and convincing. The simulated low-resource setting with eight languages demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines, and the real-world evaluation on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda shows the approach's practical applicability. The results are clear (Table 1 and 2), with the proposed method consistently outperforming all baselines, including the state-of-the-art Duong et al. (2014a) method.

3. **Effective visualization of the noise layer**: The noise transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights into how the model handles different types of errors. These visualizations demonstrate the model's ability to learn specific patterns of noise (e.g., confusing NUM and DET in Italian, or handling the higher noise level in Kinyarwanda compared to Malagasy), which adds credibility to the approach.

4. **Practical relevance**: The paper demonstrates that only 1,000 tokens of gold data are sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance, which is highly relevant for low-resource language processing. This is particularly valuable given the authors' demonstration that their approach works well even with limited gold data and noisy projected data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of why the noise layer works**: While the paper presents a compelling empirical result, it lacks a detailed analysis of why the specific noise layer formulation works better than alternatives. The authors don't explore whether the noise layer's effectiveness stems from its specific architecture or whether other noise modeling approaches would yield similar results. This limits our understanding of the key innovation.

2. **Insufficient evaluation on real low-resource languages**: The evaluation on real low-resource languages is limited to just two languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). While these are valid examples, a more comprehensive evaluation across additional low-resource languages would strengthen the claim that the approach generalizes well to diverse linguistic settings.

3. **Comparison to other noise-handling methods**: The paper doesn't compare its noise layer approach to existing methods for handling noisy projected tags (e.g., heuristic filtering or other noise modeling techniques). This makes it difficult to assess whether the authors' approach represents a significant improvement over existing noise-handling strategies.

4. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead of adding the noise layer. For practical deployment, understanding the additional training time and model size would be important, particularly in resource-constrained settings where low-resource language processing is most needed.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the initialization of the noise transformation matrix A and how it evolves during training? Specifically, how does the model avoid getting stuck in poor local optima when initializing A randomly?

2. The paper states that the noise layer works well across different languages, but doesn't explain how it handles languages with very different syntactic structures. Could you elaborate on how the approach would perform on a language with significantly different word order or syntactic categories?

3. The authors claim to use "only 1000 tokens" of gold data, but the paper doesn't clearly specify how much of this data was used for training versus development. Could you clarify this to help interpret the results more accurately?

This paper presents a valuable contribution to low-resource POS tagging with a well-motivated approach that demonstrably improves performance over existing methods. While some additional analysis would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is significant and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
