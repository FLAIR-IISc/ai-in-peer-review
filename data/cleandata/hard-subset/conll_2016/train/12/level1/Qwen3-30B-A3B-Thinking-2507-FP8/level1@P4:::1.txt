

Summary: This paper presents a neural network approach for low-resource POS tagging that leverages both a small amount of gold-standard annotated data and noisy cross-lingual projected data from parallel corpora. The main contributions are: 1) a novel noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected (noisy) tags and gold tags; 2) a joint training framework that combines gold-standard and projected data; and 3) comprehensive evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant challenge in NLP - effective utilization of noisy distant supervision for low-resource languages. The noise layer mechanism is technically sound and well-integrated into the neural architecture, with clear mathematical formulation of the joint training objective. The approach directly tackles the problem of cross-lingual projection noise rather than attempting to filter it out, which is a more principled solution.

2. The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The method consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art approaches across eight simulated low-resource languages (using CoNLL data) and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The improvements are statistically significant, with gains of up to 3-4% over the best prior work (Duong et al., 2014a). The evaluation demonstrates the practical viability of using only 1,000 gold-standard tokens alongside parallel data.

3. The paper provides clear evidence of the noise layer's functionality through the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4). These matrices reveal how the model learns to adjust for specific noise patterns in different languages, such as the confusion between PRT and X tags in Swedish or the strong diagonal indicating trust in most tags. This analysis provides valuable insight into the model's behavior.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the noise layer versus joint training. While the method outperforms baselines, the authors do not demonstrate how much of the improvement comes specifically from the noise layer versus simply combining the two data sources. This weakens the claim of novelty for the noise layer itself.

2. The analysis of the noise layer's learning process is limited to presenting the transformation matrices without deeper exploration. The paper shows the matrices but doesn't explain why the model learns specific patterns (e.g., why nouns map to adjectives in Malagasy but not pronouns), which would strengthen the argument for the noise layer's effectiveness.

3. The paper does not compare against other approaches to handling noisy labels in neural networks (e.g., noise-robust loss functions, data augmentation, or self-training methods). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the noise layer approach is fundamentally superior to other techniques for handling label noise.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper mentions that the model requires two-stage training (pretraining on gold data, then joint training). Could you clarify why end-to-end training from random initialization doesn't work well, and whether you tried alternative initialization strategies?

2. The paper states that the noise layer can handle mismatched tagsets, but the evaluation uses the universal tagset (Petrov et al., 2011). Could you provide more details about how the authors handled any tagset mismatches between source and target languages in the real low-resource language experiments?

3. The results show larger improvements for Malagasy (which has more parallel data) than Kinyarwanda. Could you elaborate on how the amount of parallel data affects the noise layer's ability to learn the transformation between projected and gold tags?
