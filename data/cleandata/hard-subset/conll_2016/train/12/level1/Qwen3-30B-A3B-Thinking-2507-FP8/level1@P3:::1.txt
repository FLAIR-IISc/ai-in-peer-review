

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture for low-resource POS tagging that jointly learns from a small amount of gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key innovation is a "noise layer" that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from high-resource languages) and true tags through a learnable matrix A. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings (using European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), claiming state-of-the-art results.

My assessment of the primary contributions (in order of significance):
1. The explicit modeling of noise in cross-lingual projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix (A) represents a novel and theoretically sound approach to handling distant supervision in low-resource settings.
2. The demonstration of consistent improvements across both simulated and real low-resource language settings, with 86.3% accuracy on Malagasy and 82.5% on Kinyarwanda.
3. The detailed analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3-4) provides valuable insights into the nature of cross-lingual projection errors.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to handling noisy distant supervision**: The paper's core contribution - explicitly modeling the noise transformation between projected and true tags - is conceptually elegant and well-motivated. Unlike previous approaches that either ignore the noise or attempt to clean it heuristically, this method learns the noise patterns directly from data. The noise layer's ability to capture complex tag misalignments (e.g., NUM vs DET in Italian) while maintaining the strong diagonal (indicating most tags are trusted) is particularly compelling.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource languages provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The results (Table 1-2) consistently show improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods, including the Duong et al. (2014a) approach, which is significant since the authors share the same evaluation setup. The fact that the method works well even with minimal gold data (1,000 tokens) makes it highly practical for real-world low-resource scenarios.

3. **Theoretical and practical insights**: The paper provides valuable insights into the nature of cross-lingual projection noise through the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3-4). For example, the analysis reveals that Swedish (sv) has particularly high noise levels (many weak diagonal elements), which correlates with its performance results. This demonstrates the model's ability to capture language-specific noise characteristics, enhancing our understanding of cross-lingual projection limitations.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with more recent methods**: The paper cites Duong et al. (2014a) as the state-of-the-art, but since this is a 2016 conference submission, it misses the opportunity to compare with more recent approaches (e.g., neural methods published after 2014). This is particularly concerning given the rapid advancements in neural sequence tagging during that period.

2. **Insufficient detail on the two-stage training procedure**: The paper describes training in two stages (pretraining on gold data then joint training with projected data) but provides minimal details about the implementation. How was the noise matrix A initialized? What were the hyperparameters for the two stages? Without this information, it's difficult to replicate the results or understand the relative importance of each stage.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper claims the approach "uniquely" handles noise in cross-lingual projection, but doesn't discuss scenarios where this might fail. For example, how does the method perform when source and target languages have drastically different grammatical structures (e.g., Malagasy vs. English)? The paper also doesn't address whether the noise layer would generalize to other types of noisy supervision (beyond cross-lingual projection).

4. **Questionable claims about "only 1000 tokens"**: The paper repeatedly emphasizes "only 1000 tokens" of gold data, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how these tokens were selected or whether they're representative. In low-resource settings, the quality and representativeness of such a small sample is critical, yet the paper doesn't address this.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the noise layer compare to simpler approaches, such as using a confidence-weighted loss for the projected data (where confidence is based on alignment quality)?
2. Given the two-stage training procedure, what would happen if you trained the entire model end-to-end from scratch without pretraining? Would the noise layer still be beneficial?
3. How does the method perform when using weaker alignment models (e.g., IBM model 1 instead of model 3)? Would the noise layer be able to compensate for lower-quality projections?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the low-resource POS tagging problem. The noise layer concept is novel and well-executed, and the results are convincing. With some additional comparisons to more recent methods and clarification on the training procedure, this would be an excellent contribution to the field.
