- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit noise layer to correct noisy cross-lingual projected annotations, jointly trained with small gold datasets and evaluated on 10 languages, achieving state-of-the-art results.

Main contributions:
- Introduces an explicit noise layer in a BiLSTM to model and correct systematic errors in projected POS tags.
- Proposes joint training on small gold-standard data and large noisy projected data to improve low-resource POS tagging.
- Provides empirical validation across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, showing strong improvements (notably for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Strengths:
- Novel and practical noise layer that directly targets projection errors.
- Consistently strong empirical results outperforming baselines and prior methods.
- Method is generalizable to other noisy-annotation scenarios (e.g., distant supervision, crowdsourcing).
- Thorough evaluation including ablations and visualizations of learned noise matrices.

Weaknesses:
- Limited, shallow analysis of how the noise layer adapts across languages and noise patterns.
- No discussion of computational efficiency or scalability to larger datasets or complex tasks.
- Reliance on parallel corpora; alternatives (monolingual or related-language sources) are not explored.

Questions to authors:
- How does performance change as the amount of gold-standard data increases, and is the noise layer still beneficial in higher-resource settings?
- Can the noise layer be adapted to other noisy annotation types like crowdsourcing or distant supervision?
- What are the model’s computational requirements for training on larger datasets or morphologically complex languages?

Additional comment:
- Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to low-resource NLP, but deeper analysis of the noise layer and scalability considerations would strengthen the work.
