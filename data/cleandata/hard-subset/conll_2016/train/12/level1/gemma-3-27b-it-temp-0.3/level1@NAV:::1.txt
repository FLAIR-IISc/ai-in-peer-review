## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in this process. The authors propose a BiLSTM model with a novel “noise layer” designed to learn the transformation between projected and gold-standard tags, allowing the model to effectively weigh the reliability of different projected annotations. 

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of this noise layer and its integration into a deep learning framework for joint training on both gold and projected data. While the idea of accounting for projection noise isn’t entirely new, framing it as a learnable transformation within a BiLSTM architecture is a valuable contribution. A second, significant contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings *and* two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). This thorough empirical validation strengthens the findings and demonstrates the practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently equals or exceeds state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly noteworthy, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach in practical scenarios.
2. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise via the learnable transformation matrix is a clever and effective way to address a key limitation of cross-lingual projection. The visualizations of the learned matrices (Figures 3 & 4) provide valuable insights into the types of errors the model is learning to correct.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed method. The inclusion of real-world languages is a significant strength, as it moves beyond purely simulated settings.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The diagrams (Figure 2) are helpful in understanding the model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Architecture:** The use of a BiLSTM for sequence tagging is well-established. While the noise layer is novel, the core architectural components lack significant innovation.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for projection. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of alignment errors would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide much detail on hyperparameter tuning. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to different learning rates, embedding dimensions, or noise layer configurations.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contribution of the noise layer? For example, what is the performance gain achieved solely by adding the noise layer to a standard BiLSTM model, without any other modifications?
2.  How does the performance of your model vary with different word alignment tools or alignment quality thresholds?
3.  What is the computational cost of training the model with the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model?
