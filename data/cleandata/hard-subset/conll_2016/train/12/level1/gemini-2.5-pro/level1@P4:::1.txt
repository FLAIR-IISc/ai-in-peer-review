**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, a setting where gold-standard annotated data is scarce. The proposed method leverages a small amount of annotated data alongside a much larger, noisier corpus created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The core of the contribution is a neural sequence tagging model (a BiLSTM) augmented with an explicit "noise layer." This layer, parameterized by a transformation matrix, learns to model the systematic errors and biases present in the projected annotations. The model is trained jointly with two objectives: one to predict the correct tags on the clean data, and another to predict the noisy projected tags on the larger dataset by passing its clean predictions through the noise layer. This framework allows the model to learn from the abundant but noisy data without being corrupted by it.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel neural architecture for learning from distant supervision that explicitly models the noise process. The addition of a trainable noise transformation layer allows the model to jointly learn a "clean" tagging model and a "noise" model, effectively de-noising the supervisory signal from the projected data during backpropagation. This approach is generalizable to other tasks and sources of noisy labels.
2.  A comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method on low-resource POS tagging. The model is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines and the previous state-of-the-art results on the same data splits.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness of the Method:** The proposed method of explicitly modeling the noise in distant supervision via a trainable transformation layer is a novel and elegant solution. Instead of relying on heuristics to filter noisy data or treating noisy labels as ground truth, the model learns the statistical patterns of the noise. The joint training objective is well-motivated and allows the shared underlying model to benefit from large-scale noisy data while being regularized by a small set of clean data. The technical approach is sound and the two-stage training process is a sensible strategy to guide the optimization.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's claims are substantiated by an extensive set of experiments. The evaluation spans ten different languages, including both simulated and genuine low-resource settings, which strengthens the claim of the method's generalizability. The authors compare their model against several well-chosen baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on different data combinations and the previous state-of-the-art model (Duong et al., 2014a). The consistent improvements shown in Table 1 and Table 2 provide compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed noise layer.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition and Model Interpretability:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and presenting the proposed solution in an intuitive manner. The architecture diagram in Figure 2 is effective in illustrating the dual training process. A significant strength is the interpretability of the learned noise transformation matrix A. The visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 provide qualitative evidence that the model is learning meaningful confusion patterns, such as the strong diagonal (indicating trust in the projection for many tags) and specific off-diagonal weights (indicating systematic projection errors). This analysis offers insight into the model's behavior beyond simple accuracy metrics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Ablation and Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the model's components and their sensitivity to experimental conditions. The two-stage training procedure (pre-training on gold data, then joint training) is presented as a necessity, but no experiment is provided to quantify its benefit over a simpler joint training scheme from a random initialization. Furthermore, all simulated experiments are conducted with exactly 1000 gold-standard tokens. An analysis showing how performance varies with the amount of available gold data (e.g., 100, 500, 2000 tokens) would provide valuable insight into the trade-offs of the method and the conditions under which it is most effective.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in Handling Alignment Ambiguity:** The paper states that for tokens with many-to-one or null alignments, a "distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence" is assigned. This heuristic is not well-justified and seems likely to introduce uninformative noise. Crucially, the paper does not specify how this probability distribution is used as a supervisory signal within the cross-entropy loss function. This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully understand the training dynamics and to reproduce this aspect of the work.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Alternative Noise Models:** The noise layer is implemented as a single linear transformation matrix. While effective, this is a relatively simple model of noise. The paper does not discuss or compare this choice to other potential noise-handling techniques from the machine learning literature, such as noise-robust loss functions or more complex, context-dependent noise models. A discussion placing the proposed method in the broader context of learning from noisy labels would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Missing Training Details for Reproducibility:** The description of the experimental setup lacks several key details required for reproducibility. Information about batch size, regularization techniques (e.g., dropout), the number of training epochs for both pre-training and joint training phases, and the strategy for sampling from the annotated and projected datasets during joint training (e.g., batch mixing ratios) is not provided. These details can significantly impact the final performance of a neural network model.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the mechanism for incorporating the tag distribution for ambiguously aligned tokens into the training objective? Specifically, how is the cross-entropy loss calculated when the target label `ỹt` is a probability distribution rather than a one-hot vector?
2.  How critical is the two-stage training process? Have you performed an ablation study comparing the proposed pre-training plus joint-training approach to a direct joint-training approach from a random initialization?
3.  Have you considered the possibility of a context-dependent noise model, where the transformation from clean to noisy tags could be influenced by the word or its context (e.g., by conditioning the matrix A on the BiLSTM hidden state `h_t`)? This could potentially capture more nuanced projection errors.
