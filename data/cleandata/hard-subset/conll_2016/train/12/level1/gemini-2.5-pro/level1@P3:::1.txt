### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core idea is to explicitly model the systematic noise introduced by the projection process. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger trained jointly on both data sources. For the noisy projected data, the model's output is passed through an additional linear transformation layer—the "noise layer"—before the loss is calculated. This layer, represented by a trainable matrix A, learns a confusion matrix that maps the model's "clean" predicted tag distribution to the observed noisy distribution from projection, effectively learning to "de-noise" the training signal from the projected data.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and principled method for integrating distant supervision:** The primary contribution is the introduction of an explicit, learnable noise layer to model the transformation between a model's latent clean predictions and the observed noisy labels. This is a more elegant and direct approach than previous methods that treated noisy labels as imperfect ground truth or used them as features. It provides a general framework for learning from noisy sequence labels.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation:** The paper presents a robust evaluation across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The proposed model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on various data combinations and the prior state-of-the-art model on this task, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Novelty:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise-generating process is compelling. Rather than simply mixing clean and noisy data and hoping the model figures it out, the architecture directly addresses the known deficiency of the projected data. The noise layer (matrix A) is a simple yet powerful mechanism that allows the model to learn systematic projection errors (e.g., English determiners consistently mapping to nouns in a determiner-less language) while sharing the underlying sequence representation (the BiLSTM) with the high-quality gold data. This is a well-principled way to handle distant supervision.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is extensive and convincing. The use of eight simulated low-resource settings from Europarl allows for a controlled and direct comparison with the prior state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on the exact same data splits. Furthermore, testing on two real-world, non-European low-resource languages demonstrates that the method is not just an artifact of controlled simulation but is effective in a realistic setting with smaller and potentially lower-quality parallel corpora.
3.  **Clear and Consistent Performance Gains:** The results presented in Tables 1 and 2 are unambiguous. The proposed "BiLSTM+noise layer" model outperforms all baselines, including naive applications of a BiLSTM and the previous SOTA. The improvement is consistent across all ten languages. This demonstrates a clear and significant advance for this specific low-resource task. The poor performance of the "BiLSTM Ann+Proj" baseline, which simply unions the datasets, is particularly telling and highlights the importance of the proposed noise-aware training.
4.  **Insightful Analysis and Interpretability:** The visualization of the learned noise matrices (A) in Figures 3 and 4 is an excellent addition. It provides valuable insight into what the model learns about the projection process for different language pairs. The strong diagonals confirm that projections are often correct, while the off-diagonal weights reveal systematic confusions. For example, the analysis showing greater noise for Kinyarwanda (which has less parallel data) than for Malagasy aligns with intuition and lends credibility to the model's behavior. This interpretability is a significant advantage.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Ablation on the Training Strategy:** The paper states that a two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data, then joint training) is necessary to avoid poor local optima, claiming that direct joint training is "impractical". This is a critical design choice that is asserted but not empirically validated. Without a direct comparison to joint training from scratch, it is difficult to assess the sensitivity of the model to this initialization or the true impact of the pre-training step. An ablation study showing the performance of a randomly initialized, jointly trained model would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the training regime.
2.  **Limited Discussion on the Source of Noise:** The paper correctly identifies alignment errors and syntactic divergence as sources of noise. However, the analysis of the learned noise matrix A is not deeply connected back to these sources. It would be more insightful to provide concrete linguistic examples. For instance, if the matrix for German shows a high confusion score between projected DET and gold NOUN tags, is this due to a specific, recurring alignment error pattern or a known syntactic divergence related to German compound nouns? A more qualitative analysis linking the learned parameters back to linguistic phenomena would elevate the work.
3.  **Potential Scalability Concerns:** The noise layer is parameterized by a K×K matrix, where K is the size of the tagset. The universal POS tagset used here is small (K=12). This approach is perfectly feasible for this task, but it is not clear how well it would scale to problems with much larger label spaces, such as fine-grained named entity recognition or semantic role labeling, where K could be in the hundreds. Learning a full K×K matrix in such settings might become prohibitively data-intensive, especially given the low-resource context. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential solutions (e.g., a low-rank approximation of A) would be welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The two-stage training process is presented as a crucial component. Could you please provide quantitative results comparing this strategy to a single-stage joint training from a random initialization? This would help clarify how "impractical" the single-stage approach is and justify the necessity of pre-training.
2.  In Section 3.1, you describe the process for generating projected labels. You first state that you "retain only one-to-one alignments," but later mention that for tokens aligned to many English words or none, you "assign a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." These two statements seem to be in conflict. Could you clarify the exact procedure? For example, are you first filtering out sentences that contain anything other than one-to-one alignments?
3.  Your analysis of the noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 is very interesting. Have you investigated whether the most prominent off-diagonal values (i.e., the most systematic errors the model learns) correspond to the most frequent errors found by directly comparing the projected labels to a gold-standard development set? This would provide further validation that the noise layer is indeed capturing the true error patterns in the projected data.
