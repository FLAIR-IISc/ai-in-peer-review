**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside plentiful but noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that is jointly trained on both data sources.

The primary contribution is a simple yet effective method for integrating the noisy data: an explicit "noise layer." This is a trainable linear transformation matrix that models the systematic confusion between the model's predicted clean tags and the noisy projected tags. By learning this transformation, the model can effectively de-noise the error signal from the projected data during backpropagation. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation of this approach, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Interpretable Method:** The proposed noise layer is an elegant and principled way to handle distant supervision. Unlike prior work that uses heuristics or implicit feature modeling, this approach explicitly models the noise process. The resulting transformation matrix is interpretable (as shown in Figures 3 and 4), providing valuable insights into the types of errors introduced by cross-lingual projection for different languages.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across ten languages. The proposed model consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM and the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a). The gains are particularly notable on the real low-resource languages, highlighting the practical utility of the method.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Analysis:** The authors clearly motivate the need for their approach by showing that naively combining annotated and projected data can degrade performance. The analysis of the learned noise matrices effectively connects the model's behavior to the known challenges of projection, such as alignment errors and syntactic divergence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Two-Stage Training:** The paper states that pre-training the model on the small clean dataset is crucial to avoid poor local optima before joint training begins. This dependency suggests potential instability in the joint training objective and raises questions about the method's robustness. An ablation study on the impact of this pre-training step is missing.
2.  **Incremental Architectural Novelty:** While the noise layer is a clever addition, the underlying architecture is a standard BiLSTM. The core technical contribution is confined to this single component and the associated joint loss function, making the overall architectural novelty somewhat limited.
3.  **Simplified Noise Model:** The model assumes that projection noise can be captured by a single, static confusion matrix. However, projection errors can be context-dependent. For example, the likelihood of a mistagged noun might depend on the surrounding words or the ambiguity of the source-language alignment. The current model cannot capture these more complex, context-sensitive noise patterns.
