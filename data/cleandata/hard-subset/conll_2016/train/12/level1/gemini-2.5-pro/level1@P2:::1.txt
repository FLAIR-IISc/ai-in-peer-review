### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, where only a small amount of gold-annotated data is available, but larger parallel corpora exist. The authors propose a method to leverage noisy POS tags projected from a high-resource language (English) by explicitly modeling the noise process. The core of their approach is a Bi-directional LSTM tagger that is jointly trained on both the small gold corpus and the larger, noisily-labeled projected corpus. This joint training is facilitated by an additional "noise layer"—a learnable transformation matrix—that maps the model's internal "clean" tag predictions to the observed noisy tag distributions from the projection data.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and elegant method for modeling distant supervision noise:** The primary contribution is the introduction of an explicit, learnable noise layer within a neural sequence model. This layer, parameterized by a simple matrix `A`, provides a principled way to learn the systematic errors and biases (i.e., the "confusion profile") inherent in the cross-lingual projection process, allowing the model to effectively "de-noise" the training signal from the large but imperfectly labeled dataset.
2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging:** The paper provides convincing evidence of the model's effectiveness across a wide range of languages. The evaluation on eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines and the prior state-of-the-art, validating the utility of the proposed approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed noise layer is a simple yet powerful idea. Instead of treating projected tags as noisy features or using complex heuristics for data selection, the model directly learns the relationship between the latent true tags and the observed projected tags. This is an elegant way to incorporate knowledge about the noise-generating process into the model's architecture. The parameterization as a single `K x K` matrix is efficient, adding very few parameters while capturing first-order tag confusion effectively.
2.  **Excellent Experimental Design and Results:** The evaluation is thorough and compelling.
    *   **Baselines:** The authors compare against several well-chosen baselines, including models trained only on annotated data, only on projected data, and on their simple union. These comparisons (Table 1 and 2) clearly demonstrate that naive data combination is suboptimal and that the proposed joint model with the noise layer is crucial for success.
    *   **Breadth:** Testing on ten languages in total (8 simulated, 2 real) shows the general applicability of the method. The fact that it outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) on nearly every language is a very strong result.
    *   **Realistic Setting:** The setup, using only 1000 gold-standard tokens, aligns well with realistic low-resource scenarios, making the results highly relevant.
3.  **Clarity and Interpretability:** The paper is very well-written and the model is clearly explained, with Figure 2 being particularly helpful for understanding the dual training objectives. Furthermore, the visualization of the learned noise matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 is a major strength. It provides valuable insight into what the model learns about the cross-lingual projection process for different language pairs (e.g., the strong diagonal indicating general trust, but specific off-diagonal weights showing systematic confusion like PRT/X for Swedish). This analysis adds a layer of interpretability that strengthens the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Justification for Two-Stage Training:** The paper states that directly training the whole network is "impractical" and thus adopts a two-stage process: pre-training on the small gold dataset, then joint fine-tuning. This claim is not sufficiently justified or analyzed. It reads as a potential weakness in the model's training stability that is solved with a heuristic. It would be much stronger if the authors provided an experiment or analysis showing *why* end-to-end training fails (e.g., poor local optima, gradient instability) and demonstrated that their two-stage approach is a robust solution. Without this, it is unclear if this is a necessary step or simply an artifact of their implementation.
2.  **Limited Model of Noise:** The noise layer models a context-independent tag confusion: the probability of observing a projected tag `ỹ` depends only on the true latent tag `y`. However, projection errors are often context-dependent. For instance, a specific source-language word might be systematically mistranslated or misaligned, leading to a recurring error for a particular target word `x`. The current model cannot capture such `x`-dependent noise patterns. While the simplicity of the model is a strength, this is a key limitation that should be acknowledged and discussed.
3.  **Lack of Detail on Data Coverage:** The authors follow prior work in using only one-to-one word alignments to generate the projected data, stating this reduces noise. While a reasonable choice, the paper provides no statistics on the impact of this filtering. What percentage of tokens in the target-language side of the parallel corpus receive a projected tag under this constraint? If this coverage is very low, the model may only be leveraging a small fraction of the available parallel data, which would be an important detail for understanding the true utility of the method.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the two-stage training process: Could you elaborate on why direct end-to-end training is "impractical"? Did you experiment with joint training from a random initialization, and if so, what was the performance? Is it possible that with different hyperparameter settings (e.g., a lower learning rate for the projected data loss term initially), a single-stage training regime could succeed?
2.  Your noise model is context-independent. Do you have a sense from error analysis of how many projection errors are tied to specific lexical items versus general tag confusion? Could your model be extended to handle context-dependent noise, for example by making the noise matrix `A` a function of the BiLSTM's hidden state?
3.  Could you provide statistics on the coverage of your projection method? Specifically, for the parallel corpora used, what proportion of the target-language tokens were kept after filtering for one-to-one alignments?
