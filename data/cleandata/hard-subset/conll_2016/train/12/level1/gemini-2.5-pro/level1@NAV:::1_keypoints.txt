- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger for low-resource languages that jointly trains on small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projections using a trainable linear "noise layer" to denoise projected labels.
- The noise layer is a novel, interpretable method that explicitly models systematic confusion between clean and projected tags via a learned transformation matrix.
- Empirically strong: achieves state-of-the-art results across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, outperforming strong baselines including Duong et al. (2014a).
- Clear motivation and analysis: shows naive data combination can hurt performance and uses learned noise matrices to explain projection errors like alignment mistakes and syntactic divergence.
- Relies on two-stage training (pre-training on clean data) to avoid poor local optima, with no ablation study examining this dependency.
- Architectural novelty is incremental: the main model is a standard BiLSTM and the contribution is largely the single noise component and joint loss.
- Noise model is oversimplified as a single static confusion matrix and cannot capture context-dependent projection errors.
