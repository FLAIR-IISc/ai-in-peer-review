**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, a setting where only a small amount of gold-standard annotated data is available. The authors leverage a larger, but noisier, source of supervision: POS tags projected from a high-resource language (English) via word alignments in a parallel corpus. The core proposal is a BiLSTM-based sequence tagger that is jointly trained on both the small clean corpus and the large noisy corpus. To handle the errors inherent in projection, the model includes a novel "noise layer," which is a learnable linear transformation that explicitly models the systematic confusion between the model's predicted "clean" tags and the observed "noisy" projected tags. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments, achieving state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and generalizable method for learning from noisy sequence labels.** The introduction of an explicit, learnable noise transformation matrix is an elegant and principled way to integrate distant supervision. It allows the model to learn the patterns of error in the noisy data source, effectively "de-noising" the training signal during backpropagation. This technique is not limited to POS tagging and could be readily applied to other sequence labeling tasks that utilize distant supervision.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a strong and convincing empirical validation of the proposed method. By outperforming strong baselines, including a previous state-of-the-art model, across ten different languages, the authors clearly demonstrate the practical value and effectiveness of their approach in a realistic low-resource scenario.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the nature of cross-lingual projection noise.** The visualization of the learned noise matrices provides a compelling interpretation of the model's behavior. It offers a clear view of which tag confusions are common for different languages, linking model performance to the quality of the parallel data and inherent linguistic divergences.

**Strengths**

1.  **High degree of novelty and methodological elegance.** The paper's primary strength lies in its core idea: explicitly modeling the noise in the distant supervision source. This contrasts sharply with prior work that relies on heuristics to filter noisy data or complex feature sets to implicitly capture label correlations. The noise layer is a simple (a single matrix multiplication) yet powerful addition to a standard neural architecture, making the approach both effective and easy to interpret. It directly addresses the central challenge of using projected data: its unreliability.
2.  **Comprehensive and convincing experimental results.** The evaluation is thorough and robust. The authors test their model on a diverse set of ten languages, including both simulated and genuinely low-resource settings. Their inclusion of multiple strong baselines—particularly the BiLSTM variants trained on different combinations of data—serves to isolate the contribution of the noise layer itself. The reported improvements are consistent and significant (e.g., an absolute improvement of 4.2% on Kinyarwanda over the previous SOTA), providing strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed method.
3.  **Clarity of presentation and analysis.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained and illustrated in Figure 2. The motivation is well-grounded in the literature, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The analysis of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 is a major strength, as it provides valuable insight into what the model has learned and offers a qualitative explanation for the performance differences across languages.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of ablation on the training strategy.** The authors state that they employ a two-stage training process: pre-training the BiLSTM on the small gold dataset, and then fine-tuning the entire model (including the noise matrix) jointly on both datasets. They motivate this by claiming that training from scratch is "impractical" due to the risk of poor local optima. While this is a plausible claim, it is a crucial design choice that is not experimentally validated. An ablation study comparing this two-stage approach to a one-stage joint training from random initialization would be necessary to substantiate this claim and understand how critical the pre-training step is to the model's success.
2.  **Unclear handling of non-one-to-one alignments.** There appears to be a contradiction in the paper regarding the handling of words with complex alignments. In Section 3.1, the authors describe a strategy for tokens aligned to multiple words or NULL: "we assign a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." However, in Section 4.1.2, describing the experimental setup, they state: "we retain only one-to-one alignments." This is a critical detail of the data processing pipeline that needs to be clarified. The noise patterns for unaligned words are likely very different from those for misaligned one-to-one words, and the choice of which data to include could significantly impact the results.
3.  **Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning and model sensitivity.** The paper provides basic model dimensions but lacks discussion on how hyperparameters were selected. In a low-resource setting with only 1000 gold tokens for training, neural models are notoriously sensitive to choices like learning rate, hidden layer size, and regularization. A learning rate of 1.0 for SGD seems unusually high and warrants justification. Without information on the tuning process (e.g., use of a development set) or a sensitivity analysis, it is difficult to assess the robustness of the results or the effort required to reproduce them.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the training procedure? Specifically, how critical is the two-stage pre-training process for achieving the reported results? What is the performance of the model if it is trained jointly on both datasets from a random initialization?
2.  Could you resolve the apparent contradiction between Section 3.1 and Section 4.1.2 regarding the handling of word alignments? Did your final experiments use only one-to-one alignments, or did they also include data from many-to-one or unaligned words, and if so, how were their labels generated?
3.  The paper proposes a KxK matrix for the noise layer. Did you experiment with any constrained or simpler versions of this transformation, for example, by enforcing that the rows sum to one (to model a true conditional probability distribution p(noisy_tag | true_tag))? Could such constraints help prevent overfitting, especially for the real low-resource languages with smaller parallel corpora?
