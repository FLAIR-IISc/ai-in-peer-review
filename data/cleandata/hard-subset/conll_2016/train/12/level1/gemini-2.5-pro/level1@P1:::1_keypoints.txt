- Paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by jointly training a BiLSTM on small clean annotations and large noisy cross-lingual projected tags, introducing a learnable noise layer to model projection errors and achieving SOTA on 10 languages.
- Contribution: Proposes a general, learnable noise transformation matrix to model and correct systematic label noise from distant supervision.
- Contribution: Demonstrates strong empirical gains, outperforming strong baselines and prior SOTA across simulated and real low-resource languages.
- Contribution: Provides analysis of learned noise matrices, offering insights into common cross-lingual tag confusions and their relation to parallel data quality.
- Strength: Methodological novelty and elegance—simple, interpretable noise layer that directly addresses projected-data unreliability.
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental evaluation across ten languages with consistent, significant improvements.
- Strength: Clear presentation and insightful visual analysis of learned noise patterns.
- Weakness: No ablation study on the two-stage training strategy; unclear how critical pre-training is versus joint training from scratch.
- Weakness: Contradiction about handling complex alignments—paper claims both distributional labeling for multi/NULL alignments and retaining only one-to-one alignments.
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity (e.g., unusually high SGD lr=1.0) limiting reproducibility and robustness assessment.
- Question: How critical is the two-stage pre-training; what performance if trained jointly from random init?
- Question: Which alignment strategy was actually used in experiments—only one-to-one, or were many-to-one/unaligned tokens included and how were they labeled?
- Question: Did you try constrained/simpler noise matrices (e.g., rows summing to one) to model p(noisy|true) and reduce overfitting on small parallel corpora?
