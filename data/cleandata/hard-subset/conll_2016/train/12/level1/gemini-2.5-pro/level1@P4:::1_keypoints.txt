- Problem & approach: POS tagging for low-resource languages using small gold data plus large cross-lingual projected noisy corpus, with a BiLSTM augmented by a trainable noise transformation layer and joint clean/noisy objectives.
- Main contributions: (1) novel neural architecture that explicitly models noise via a learnable transformation layer to de-noise projected labels, (2) comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent improvements on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages.

- Strength 1: Novelty & soundness — explicit, trainable noise layer and two-stage/joint training elegantly model systematic projection errors instead of heuristically filtering noise.
- Strength 2: Strong empirical evaluation — experiments across ten languages, multiple baselines, and consistent gains including prior SOTA improvements.
- Strength 3: Clarity & interpretability — clear exposition, helpful architecture diagram, and interpretable noise matrix visualizations revealing meaningful confusion patterns.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient ablation/sensitivity analysis — no experiments varying pre-training vs. joint-only training or amount of gold data (only 1,000 tokens tested).
- Weakness 2: Ambiguous handling of alignment uncertainty — heuristic tag-distribution for many-to-one/null alignments is unexplained and its use in cross-entropy is unspecified.
- Weakness 3: Limited discussion of alternative noise models — no comparison to noise-robust losses or context-dependent/complex noise models from related literature.
- Weakness 4: Missing training details for reproducibility — batch size, dropout/regularization, epoch counts, and sampling/mixing strategy during joint training are not reported.

- Question 1: How is the cross-entropy computed when the target for ambiguously aligned tokens is a probability distribution rather than a one-hot label?
- Question 2: How critical is the two-stage (pre-train then joint-train) procedure versus training jointly from random initialization — any ablation results?
- Question 3: Have you considered or tested context-dependent noise models (e.g., conditioning the noise transform on the BiLSTM hidden state) to capture more nuanced projection errors?
