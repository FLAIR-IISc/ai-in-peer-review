- Tackles POS tagging for low-resource languages by combining small gold annotations with larger, noisily projected tags.  
- Proposes a BiLSTM jointly trained on gold and projected data with a learnable noise layer (KxK matrix A) that maps clean tag predictions to observed noisy projections.  
- Main contribution: an explicit, learnable noise layer that models systematic cross-lingual projection errors.  
- Main contribution: comprehensive empirical validation across 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages showing consistent improvements and beating prior SOTA.  
- Strength: methodological novelty and simplicity—single matrix captures first-order tag confusion with few parameters.  
- Strength: excellent experimental design—strong baselines, breadth across languages, and realistic low-resource setup (1000 gold tokens).  
- Strength: clarity and interpretability—clear exposition and informative visualizations of the learned noise matrix A.  
- Weakness: two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint fine-tune) is asserted as necessary but not justified or analyzed empirically.  
- Weakness: noise model is context-independent and cannot capture lexical/context-dependent projection errors.  
- Weakness: missing statistics on projection coverage after filtering for one-to-one alignments; unclear what fraction of parallel tokens are used.  
- Question: why is end-to-end joint training "impractical"? were experiments with random init/joint training or different hyperparameters tried?  
- Question: how many projection errors are tied to specific lexical items vs. general tag confusion, and could A be made context-dependent (e.g., function of BiLSTM state)?  
- Question: what proportion of target-language tokens remained after one-to-one alignment filtering for the parallel corpora?
