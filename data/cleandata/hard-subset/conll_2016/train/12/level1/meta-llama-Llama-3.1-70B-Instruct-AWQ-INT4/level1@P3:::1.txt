**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) and a noise transformation layer to encode the mapping between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags. The model is trained jointly on gold and distant projected annotations and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and show that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art on all settings.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to sequence tagging**: The authors propose a new approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer.
2. **Joint learning of gold and projected annotations**: The authors propose a joint learning framework that combines gold standard and projected annotations to improve the accuracy of POS tagging.
3. **Noise transformation layer**: The authors propose a noise transformation layer that encodes the mapping between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of projected annotations**: The authors show that their approach can effectively use projected annotations to improve the accuracy of POS tagging, even in low-resource settings.
2. **Robustness to noise**: The authors show that their approach is robust to noise in the projected annotations and can learn to correct errors.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: The authors show that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art on all settings, including eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and it is unclear how well their approach will generalize to other languages.
2. **Dependence on word alignments**: The authors' approach relies on word alignments, which can be noisy and may not always be accurate.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors' approach requires joint learning of gold and projected annotations, which can be computationally expensive.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other languages and domains?
2. Can you provide more details on how you handle word alignments and how you deal with noisy alignments?
3. How do you plan to reduce the computational complexity of your approach to make it more practical for real-world applications?
