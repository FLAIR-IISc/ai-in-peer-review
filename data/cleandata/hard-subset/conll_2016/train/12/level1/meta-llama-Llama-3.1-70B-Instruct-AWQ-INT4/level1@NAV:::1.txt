**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) with a noise layer to encode the mapping between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and achieve state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Noise layer for cross-lingual projection**: The authors propose a novel approach to model the noise in cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This layer is learned jointly with the BiLSTM model and allows the model to correct errors in the projected tags.
2. **Joint learning of gold and projected data**: The authors propose a joint learning framework that uses both gold standard and projected data to train the model. This approach allows the model to leverage the strengths of both types of data.
3. **State-of-the-art results on low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and achieve state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of noisy projected data**: The authors' approach effectively uses noisy projected data to improve the performance of the model. This is particularly useful in low-resource settings where high-quality annotated data is scarce.
2. **Robustness to noise**: The authors' approach is robust to noise in the projected data, which is a common problem in cross-lingual projection.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a limited number of languages and datasets. Further evaluation on more languages and datasets is needed to confirm the effectiveness of their approach.
2. **Dependence on high-quality parallel data**: The authors' approach relies on high-quality parallel data, which may not always be available. Further research is needed to develop approaches that can work with lower-quality parallel data.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors' approach requires joint learning of gold and projected data, which can be computationally expensive. Further research is needed to develop more efficient algorithms.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to work with lower-quality parallel data?
2. Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of your approach and how it compares to other state-of-the-art approaches?
3. How do you plan to evaluate your approach on more languages and datasets?
