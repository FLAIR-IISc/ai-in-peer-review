**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The method is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The authors evaluate their system on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and achieve state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. **Modeling noise in cross-lingual projection**: The authors propose a deep learning model that explicitly models the transformation between tagsets as part of a more expressive neural network.
2. **Joint learning of gold and projected annotations**: The model is trained jointly on gold and projected annotations, allowing it to learn from both sources of information.
3. **Effective use of parallel data**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in utilizing parallel data to improve POS tagging accuracy in low-resource languages.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel approach to modeling noise**: The authors' approach to modeling noise in cross-lingual projection is novel and effective, allowing the model to learn from both gold and projected annotations.
2. **State-of-the-art results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Well-written and clear presentation**: The paper is well-written and clearly presents the authors' approach, making it easy to follow and understand.
4. **Strong experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation of their approach, including a comparison to several baselines and an analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited analysis of the noise transformation matrices**: While the authors provide some analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, a more in-depth analysis could provide further insights into the strengths and limitations of their approach.
2. **Assumes availability of parallel data**: The authors' approach assumes the availability of parallel data, which may not always be the case for low-resource languages.
3. **Limited comparison to other approaches**: The authors primarily compare their approach to a maximum entropy classifier and a supervised BiLSTM tagger, but a comparison to other approaches, such as those using language relatedness information, could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, including a discussion of the strengths and limitations of your approach?
2. How does your approach perform when parallel data is limited or unavailable?
3. Can you compare your approach to other methods that utilize language relatedness information, such as those using dependency parsing or lexical resources?
