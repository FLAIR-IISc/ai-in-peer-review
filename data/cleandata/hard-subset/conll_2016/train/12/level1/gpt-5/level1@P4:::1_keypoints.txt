- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger jointly trained on small gold data and large noisy projected data with an explicit learnable noise layer (matrix A) modeling clean→noisy tag mapping.
- Training uses a two-stage schedule: pretrain on gold labels, then jointly train on gold and projected data to stabilize learning of A.
- Evaluated on 8 simulated European languages and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing small but consistent improvements over prior baselines.
- Main contribution: explicit learnable noise transformation layer that captures systematic projection confusions.
- Main contribution: joint training framework treating projected labels as hard or soft distributions and using per-token averaging to balance datasets.
- Main contribution: empirical evaluation and visualization of learned noise matrices supporting qualitative interpretability.
- Strength: simple, well-motivated noise modeling with visualizations showing plausible language-specific mappings.
- Strength: consistent empirical gains, especially on real low-resource languages where projection noise is higher.
- Strength: technically sound joint objective and two-stage training to mitigate identifiability/optimization issues.
- Strength: model-agnostic encoder side and conceptual extensibility to other distant supervision or mismatched tagset scenarios.
- Strength: transparent reporting of projection pipeline choices (GIZA++, IBM3, filtering), aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited ablations on training dynamics—no quantification of two-stage necessity, sensitivity to A initialization, or loss weighting.
- Weakness: unclear gold data size for "real" experiments (abstract claims 1k tokens but real experiments use ~5k), needing clarification.
- Weakness: baselines may be weak or insufficiently tuned (no BiLSTM-CRF, minimal regularization), raising fairness concerns.
- Weakness: modeling choice of applying softmax(A·o) on probabilities (not logits) is ad hoc; identifiability, constraints, or regularization on A are not discussed.
- Weakness: limited exploration of projection variants and no experiments on mismatched tagsets despite claims of applicability.
- Questions: how is A initialized and was regularization (e.g., L2 to identity, sparsity) tried?
- Questions: were projected and gold losses weighted (λ) and how sensitive are results to that hyperparameter?
- Questions: exact number of gold tokens used in real-language experiments and whether a 1k-token comparability run was done?
- Questions: what happens with a stronger base tagger (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF); does the noise layer still help?
- Questions: why use sentence-level tag distributions for NULL/multi-alignments instead of alignment posterior weighting, and any comparisons?
- Additional: report variance/statistical significance over multiple runs would strengthen claims about small improvements.
- Additional: a targeted error analysis showing which tags or examples benefit most would improve clarity.
