Summary and claimed contributions (as assessed)
The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, which yields noisy labels due to alignment errors and cross-lingual divergences. It proposes a BiLSTM tagger jointly trained on a small gold-annotated corpus and a large projected corpus, with an explicit noise layer that models the transformation from true tags to noisy projected tags. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold labels, then jointly train with both sources. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show improvements over prior work.

Main contributions (in order of strength)
- An explicit, learnable noise transformation layer (matrix A) on top of a BiLSTM tagger to model the mapping between clean tags and noisy projected tags, enabling effective joint training on gold and projected data.
- A joint training framework that leverages projected labels treated as hard or soft distributions, with a two-stage training procedure (gold pretraining followed by joint training) to stabilize learning.
- Empirical evaluation across 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource settings, with competitive or superior results to prior state-of-the-art baselines, plus analysis via visualization of the learned noise matrices.

Strengths
- Clear, simple modeling of projection noise: The linear transformation A applied to the model’s output before a projection softmax is well-motivated for capturing systematic confusion patterns between gold and projected tags. Visualizations corroborate that A learns plausible language-specific mappings (e.g., NOUN→ADJ/NUM; diagonal dominance when projection is reliable).
- Consistent gains over strong baselines: On 8 simulated languages, average accuracy (91.7) slightly exceeds the published state-of-the-art baseline (91.3) with per-language improvements. On real low-resource languages, gains are more pronounced (Malagasy 86.3 vs 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs 78.3 and 81.9), indicating the method’s utility when projection noise is higher.
- Joint training design is technically sound: The objective combines cross-entropy for gold labels and projected labels (hard or soft), and the per-token averaging normalizes contributions from datasets of different sizes. The two-stage training mitigates identifiability and optimization issues when learning A.
- Broad applicability: The approach is model-agnostic on the encoder side and conceptually extends to other distant supervision settings and mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), increasing potential impact beyond POS tagging.
- Empirical transparency about projection setup: The paper details alignment choices (GIZA++, IBM Model 3, one-to-one alignments), sentence filtering, and how distributions are assigned when alignments are ambiguous, allowing reproducibility of the projection pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of training dynamics and ablations: While a two-stage schedule is proposed, there is no ablation quantifying its necessity or the sensitivity to initialization of A (e.g., identity vs random), nor to the weighting between gold and projected losses. An explicit hyperparameter trade-off or schedule exploration would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Unclear gold data size for “real” low-resource experiments: The abstract claims evaluation with only 1000 gold tokens, but the real-language experiments use ∼5k gold tokens. This discrepancy should be clarified, as it affects the claimed low-resource regime and comparability to prior work that used exactly 1k tokens.
- Baseline breadth and fairness: The neural baseline is a locally normalized BiLSTM without a CRF layer or modern regularization (e.g., dropout), and hyperparameter tuning appears minimal. It is unclear whether competing baselines (e.g., Duong et al.) were re-run under identical preprocessing and data selection for the real languages. Including a stronger neural baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and reporting tuned settings would better isolate the contribution of the noise layer.
- Modeling choice and identifiability: The noise layer computes softmax(A o), where o are probabilities, not logits. This may be ad hoc and can blur interpretability; a logit-space confusion model or a stochastic noise channel (e.g., conditional confusion matrix) could be more principled. Constraints or regularization on A (e.g., diagonal bias, row-stochasticity) are not discussed; without them, A and the base classifier may be underdetermined.
- Limited exploration of projection variants: The method is said to handle mismatched tagsets, but no such experiments are presented. Choices such as restricting to one-to-one alignments and using sentence-level tag distributions for NULL/multi-alignments are reasonable but likely suboptimal; alternatives (alignment posterior weighting, better aggregation) are not compared.

Questions to Authors
- How is A initialized before joint training (identity, random, or derived from confusion statistics)? Did you try constraining or regularizing A (e.g., L2 to identity, sparsity) and what was the effect?
- Did you experiment with weighting the projected loss relative to the gold loss (a tunable λ)? If so, how sensitive are results to λ?
- For the real-language experiments, how many gold tokens were used for training exactly, and is there a 1k-token setting for comparability with simulated experiments?
- How do results change if a BiLSTM-CRF is used as the base tagger? Does the noise layer still yield gains?
- For NULL/multi-aligned tokens, why use sentence-level tag distributions rather than alignment posterior-weighted tag distributions? Any empirical comparison?

Additional comments
- Reporting variance (multiple runs) or statistical significance would increase confidence in the small improvements on simulated languages.
- A brief error analysis beyond A visualizations (e.g., which tags benefit most; examples) would improve clarity.
