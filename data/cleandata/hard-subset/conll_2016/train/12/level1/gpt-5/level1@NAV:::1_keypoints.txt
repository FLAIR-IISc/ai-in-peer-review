- Summary: BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit learnable “noise layer” modeling confusion between clean and projected labels, trained via pretraining on tiny gold data then joint training with noisy projections; evaluated on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages with consistent gains.

Contributions:
- Introduces an explicit, learnable noise transformation for projected tags integrated into a neural sequence tagger to learn when to trust distant supervision.
- Proposes a simple two-stage training protocol (pretrain on 1k gold tokens, then joint training with projections) that yields consistent improvements and interpretable confusion matrices.
- Provides a multilingual evaluation showing small but consistent SOTA improvements in simulations and notable gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

Strengths:
- Clear empirical value: reaches or exceeds prior SOTA in most settings (avg 91.7 vs. 91.3 over 8 languages; Malagasy 86.3 vs. 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5 vs. 78.3).
- Principled handling of projection noise: learned confusion (A) matrices are interpretable and align with linguistic intuitions.
- Low annotation requirement: competitive performance using only 1k gold tokens, suitable for realistic low-resource pipelines.
- Simplicity and portability: few extra parameters, fits standard BiLSTM taggers, potentially transferable to other distant supervision settings.

Weaknesses:
- Modest average gains and no significance testing: +0.4 average accuracy may not be statistically significant; variance/tests are missing.
- Baselines could be stronger: lacks CRF decoding, comparisons to other noise-robust label-noise models, and recent cross-lingual transfer methods.
- Limited ablations: unclear contributions of pretraining vs. joint training vs. A initialization; no regularization toward identity discussed.
- Ad hoc projection heuristics: 1-1 alignments and sentence-level heuristics for un/multi-aligned tokens may be brittle; sensitivity unclear.
- Reliance on parallel data: assumes parallel corpora availability; robustness to small or domain-mismatched parallel data not evaluated.

Questions to authors:
- How sensitive are results to parallel data size/quality and alignment choices; any learning curves vs. number of parallel sentences?
- Did you try initializing A near identity or regularizing A toward identity, and does that stabilize training?
- What is the impact of adding a CRF layer or character/morphological features in truly low-resource settings?
