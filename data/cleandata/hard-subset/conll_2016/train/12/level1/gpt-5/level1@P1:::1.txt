Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a simple, effective way to exploit noisy cross-lingual POS projections alongside a small amount of gold annotations. The core idea is to add an explicit “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger: the model predicts clean tag distributions from contextual features, and a learned linear transformation maps them to the noisy projected label space during training on projected data. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold-only, then jointly train with both gold and projected data. Experiments on 8 simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit noise-transition layer for learning with cross-lingual projected tags, enabling the model to learn “when to trust” projections and how to correct them (primary).
2) An empirical study across 10 languages showing this approach outperforms prior state of the art (Duong et al., 2014a) and naive uses of projection, with insightful analyses of learned confusion matrices (secondary).
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) for stable optimization in low-resource settings (secondary).

Strengths
- Clear, principled treatment of label noise in projection. The linear noise transformation between clean and projected tag distributions is a natural, well-motivated way to model systematic projection errors (alignment noise, cross-lingual divergences). The approach is simple, adds few parameters, and integrates seamlessly with a standard BiLSTM. The learned A matrices are interpretable (figures 3–4), validating that the model captures plausible cross-lingual mappings (e.g., determiner↔noun in determiner-poor languages).

- Consistent empirical gains across languages, including real low-resource settings. On 8 simulated languages, the proposed model averages 91.7 vs 91.3 for Duong et al. (Table 1), and on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda achieves 86.3/82.5 (Table 2), outperforming prior work. Especially on Malagasy, the gain over Garrette & Baldridge (81.2→86.3) and over naive BiLSTM training shows the method unlocks substantial value from projection.

- Strong baselines and careful comparisons. The paper includes: BiLSTM trained on gold-only, projected-only, and union; a supervised MaxEnt baseline; and the most relevant prior method (Duong et al., two-output MaxEnt). The BiLSTM Ann+Proj baseline underperforming demonstrates that naïvely mixing noisy projections can hurt; the noise layer recovers and improves performance, directly supporting the paper’s thesis.

- Practical training strategy. The two-stage training (gold pretraining followed by joint optimization) is reasonable and likely necessary for stability; it is explained clearly and appears effective in practice. The approach seems easy to reproduce and extend.

- Informative analysis. The visualization of A across languages provides qualitative evidence about projection noise patterns and connects them to performance differences (e.g., more noise in Kinyarwanda aligns with smaller gains), making the work more insightful than a black-box improvement.

Weaknesses
- Mismatch between “1000 tokens” claim and actual real-language setup. The abstract and introduction emphasize learning with only 1000 gold tokens, but the real Malagasy/Kinyarwanda experiments use 5,294/4,882 gold tokens for training (§4.1.3), which is substantially larger. This weakens the central low-resource claim and makes comparisons to prior work less clean. Please either limit training to 1k tokens in real languages or clarify and correct the claims.

- Incremental novelty relative to existing label-noise modeling. While the application to cross-lingual projection is valuable, the idea of learning a label-transition matrix on top of a classifier is known in the supervised-with-noise literature. The methodological addition is conceptually straightforward (a linear layer on logits). Given the modest average gains over Duong et al. (0.4 points in Table 1), the paper’s novelty and impact hinge on stronger evidence and broader analysis.

- Lack of statistical testing and variance reporting. Improvements are small in several settings (e.g., equal on de; +0.2 to +0.6 on others). No significance tests or multiple-run variances are reported, making it difficult to assess robustness. This is particularly important for the simulated languages and for the modest Kinyarwanda gain over Garrette & Baldridge (+0.6).

- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses. Key factors are not disentangled: effect of pretraining vs joint training, initialization and constraints on A, amount/quality of parallel data, alignment filtering thresholds, use of only 1–1 alignments, and the heuristic for many-to-one/unaligned tokens (sentence-level tag distributions, which seems very coarse). Ablations would strengthen causal claims.

- Global, context-independent noise model. A is a single global matrix, independent of context, alignment confidence, or alignment type (NULL/1–1/many). Projection noise is often context- and structure-dependent. A more nuanced model (e.g., conditioning on alignment features or confidence) may better capture systematic errors. At minimum, an empirical check (performance by alignment type; learning separate A for aligned vs NULL) would help.

- Missing or underspecified details that affect reproducibility. Important training details are sparse: dropout/regularization, early stopping, batch size, A initialization (identity/zeros?), whether embeddings are pretrained or updated, learning rate schedule, number of epochs, and selection criteria on dev. Mapping from Penn tags to Universal Tags for projections is mentioned but not fully described. These omissions hinder replication.

Questions to authors
- Real low-resource setup: Why use 5k gold tokens for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda when the paper’s premise is 1k? Can you provide results restricted to 1k tokens for those languages?

- Noise layer specifics: How is A initialized and are there any constraints (e.g., row-normalization, diagonal bias)? Did you try identity initialization and/or regularization to prevent degenerate solutions?

- Mapping details: Are English projected tags mapped to the Universal tagset before projection, and how is this mapping handled? Is the target-language gold also mapped with the same scheme?

- Ablations: What is the effect of (a) removing gold pretraining, (b) training with only projected data + noise layer, (c) varying the amount of projected data, (d) including many-to-one alignments, and (e) conditioning the noise model on alignment confidence?

- Robustness: Do results persist across random seeds? Are improvements statistically significant on the simulated and real languages?

Additional comments
- The heuristic of using sentence-level tag distributions for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens is very coarse; consider conditioning on local context or alignment probabilities.

- Reference formatting needs cleanup (duplicated entries; dangling “?” and “\Q2016\E” artifacts).

- Releasing code and data splits would aid adoption.
