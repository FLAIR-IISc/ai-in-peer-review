- Paper proposes modeling noisy cross-lingual POS projection via a learned “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger, trained jointly on small gold data and noisy projected data with a two-stage schedule.

- Main contribution: a simple parameterized confusion/noise layer that learns when to trust projected tags and reconciles systematic projection errors.

- Empirical contribution: consistent improvements across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, outperforming Duong et al. (2014) and Garrette & Baldridge (2013).

- Practical contribution: a training recipe (pretrain on gold, then joint training) to stabilize learning with noisy distant supervision.

- Strength: explicit, compact noise parameterization addresses alignment errors and syntactic divergence instead of heuristic filtering.

- Strength: strong empirical baselines and evidence, showing naïve aggregation of noisy labels is harmful while the proposed method retains benefits.

- Strength: learned noise matrices are interpretable, revealing language-specific confusion patterns and correlating with projection quality.

- Strength: realistic low-resource setup (1k gold tokens, modest parallel corpora) and reasonable alignment filtering choices.

- Strength: method is simple, adds few parameters (K×K), end-to-end trainable, and requires only standard resources.

- Weakness: noise-layer parameterization (softmax(A o)) may mix calibration with noise and lacks identifiability compared to a stochastic transition matrix T applied linearly to probabilities.

- Weakness: limited ablations on training choices and regularization (no experiments on A initialization, L2/KL priors, or alternative pretraining strategies).

- Weakness: underutilization of alignment and source-tagger uncertainty—sentence-level heuristics replace richer token-level soft targets and many-to-one alignments are discarded.

- Weakness: narrow neural architecture (word embeddings only); no char/subword modeling or comparisons to stronger neural baselines like BiLSTM-CRF with char features.

- Weakness: incomplete experimental probing—only 1k gold-data budget tested, no sensitivity analysis to gold/projected amounts, and no significance testing or seed variance reporting.

- Question: How is A initialized, and does regularizing A toward identity (L2 or KL) affect performance or stability?

- Question: Why use softmax(A o) instead of a stochastic transition matrix T with q = T^T o, and were the two compared?

- Question: How are gold vs projected examples balanced during joint training (batch composition, loss weights), and were curriculum schedules tried?

- Question: Did you try using alignment posteriors or source tagger confidences to create token-level soft targets for ambiguous/NULL cases?

- Question: How sensitive are results to the quantity/quality of projected data (fewer parallel sentences, inclusion of many-to-one alignments with uncertainty)?

- Question: Would adding character-level features to the BiLSTM change the relative gains provided by the noise layer?

- Additional request: Clarify early stopping criteria and use of development sets for hyperparameter/epoch selection.

- Additional request: Report training variance over multiple seeds and statistical significance for key comparisons.

- Additional request: Provide an experiment or discussion on the rectangular A case for mismatched tagsets, since this is claimed as a benefit.
