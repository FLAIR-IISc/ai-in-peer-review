Summary: The paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by jointly learning from a tiny gold corpus and noisy cross-lingual projections. The core idea is a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an explicit “noise layer” that models a confusion matrix between clean and projected labels, trained end-to-end with a two-stage procedure (pretrain on gold, then joint training). Experiments on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over prior work.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) An explicit, learnable noise transformation for projected tags integrated into a neural sequence tagger, enabling the model to learn when to trust or ignore distant supervision.
2) A simple, effective joint training protocol using minimal gold data (1k tokens) plus parallel text, yielding consistent improvements and interpretable confusion matrices.
3) A thorough multilingual evaluation demonstrating small but consistent SOTA improvements in simulated settings and nontrivial gains on two genuinely low-resource languages.

Strengths:
1) Clear empirical value: The method reaches or exceeds SOTA in most settings (average 91.7 vs. 91.3 on 8 languages; 86.3 vs. 85.3 for Malagasy; 82.5 vs. 78.3 for Kinyarwanda; also surpassing Garrette & Baldridge on Kinyarwanda). The improvements are consistent and practically meaningful for low-resource contexts.
2) Principled handling of projection noise: Modeling label corruption explicitly via a learned confusion matrix is a strong, general idea; the learned A matrices are interpretable and align with linguistic intuitions (e.g., determiner/number/noun confusions).
3) Low annotation requirement: Demonstrating competitive performance with only 1k gold tokens supports the method’s suitability for realistic low-resource pipelines.
4) Simplicity and portability: The approach adds few parameters and fits standard BiLSTM taggers; it should transfer to other distant supervision scenarios.

Weaknesses:
1) Modest average gains in simulations and lack of significance testing: The +0.4 average accuracy over the prior SOTA may not be statistically significant; variance estimates or tests are missing.
2) Baselines could be stronger: No CRF decoding atop BiLSTM, no comparisons to alternative noise-robust training (e.g., label-noise models) or more recent cross-lingual transfer methods (embeddings, graph propagation).
3) Limited ablations: The contribution of pretraining vs. joint training vs. initialization of A is not disentangled; no regularization toward identity for A is discussed.
4) Projection heuristics: Using only 1-1 alignments and sentence-level tag distributions for un/multi-aligned tokens is ad hoc; the sensitivity of results to these choices is unclear.
5) Reliance on parallel data: The approach presumes availability of parallel text; robustness under very small or domain-mismatched parallel corpora is not evaluated.

Questions to Authors:
- How sensitive are results to the size/quality of parallel data and alignment choices? Any curves vs. number of parallel sentences?
- Did you try initializing A near identity and/or regularizing toward identity? Does this stabilize training?
- What is the impact of adding a CRF layer or character/morphological features in truly low-resource settings?
