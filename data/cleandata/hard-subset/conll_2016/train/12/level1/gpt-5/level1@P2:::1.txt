Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple, principled approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual projections for POS tagging in low-resource settings. A BiLSTM tagger is trained jointly on a tiny amount of gold-labeled target-language data (1k tokens) and a large amount of projected labels obtained via word alignments from English. The key idea is an explicit “noise layer”: a learned linear transformation (A) applied to the clean model outputs to match the distribution of projected labels. Training proceeds in two stages: pretrain on gold, then jointly fine-tune with both gold and projected data. The method outperforms prior work on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Explicit label-noise modeling for cross-lingual projections via a learned confusion matrix layered on top of a sequence tagger, enabling the model to learn when and how to trust projected supervision.
2) Strong empirical validation across 10 languages, including two genuinely low-resource languages, with consistent gains over robust baselines and prior state-of-the-art.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) and qualitative analyses (visualizing A) that make the approach interpretable and easy to adopt.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: The explicit noise layer is a minimal, well-motivated addition to a standard BiLSTM tagger. It directly captures systematic biases from projection (alignment errors, structural divergences). This is preferable to ad hoc filtering or indirect feature-based coupling, and it integrates seamlessly into end-to-end training. The two-stage training is sensible (prevents the noise from dominating early learning).

- Strong and broad empirical gains: On the 8 simulated languages, the proposed model achieves average 91.7% vs 91.3% for Duong et al. (2014a) and clearly outperforms naïve BiLSTM baselines, especially vs Projected and Annotated+Projected (which demonstrate that using noisy projections without denoising can harm). On real low-resource data, gains are larger and more meaningful: 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda), surpassing both Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge. These results convincingly demonstrate that explicitly modeling projection noise is beneficial, particularly when parallel data are smaller or noisier.

- Evidence the model learned the intended behavior: Visualizations of A show strong diagonals punctuated by plausible off-diagonal structure (e.g., NOUN mapping to ADJ/NUM; confusion between PRT and X in Swedish), indicating that the model captures consistent projection artifacts rather than memorizing noise arbitrarily. This supports the paper’s central hypothesis and adds interpretability.

- Sound experimental setup and fair baselines relative to prior work: The evaluation matches the low-resource scenario (1k gold tokens), uses standard resources (Europarl, Stanford tagger, Giza++), the universal tagset, and compares against the strongest comparable prior system (Duong et al.). The inclusion of both simulated and real settings strengthens the claim of practical utility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a well-known idea of learning a label-noise/confusion layer: While applying a learned confusion matrix to cross-lingual projection is a valuable instantiation, the general technique of modeling annotation noise with a learned transformation has precedents in distant supervision and weak supervision. The claim of being the first to explicitly model projection noise is plausible within POS tagging, but the overall technical novelty is modest.

- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses: The paper asserts that pretraining is necessary, but does not show results without pretraining, nor the effect of different initializations/regularizers for A (e.g., identity bias). It would be important to see sensitivity to the size/quality of parallel data, the proportion of projected vs gold tokens per batch, and the benefit of using distributions for multi/NULL alignments vs hard labels. An ablation where A is fixed to identity (i.e., no noise layer) during joint training would isolate the gain due specifically to denoising.

- Incomplete reproducibility details: Training details are sparse (epochs/early stopping, batch sizes, regularization, initialization of A, sampling schedule for gold vs projected data, dev-set tuning). For real low-resource settings, details on the projected token counts and alignment quality are crucial to contextualize results. Without these, reproducing or extending the work is harder.

- Comparisons to stronger neural baselines: The baselines do not include common augmentations for low-resource POS (e.g., character-level features, CRF decoding, multilingual embeddings), which could close the gap. While it is fair to compare to Duong et al., it would strengthen the case to show that adding the noise layer on top of a stronger tagger continues to yield gains, or at least to discuss this expectation.

Questions to authors
- How is A initialized and regularized? Do you bias it toward the identity (e.g., diagonal initialization or L2 toward I) to reflect a prior that projections are often correct? Did you observe instability without such bias?

- What is the mixing strategy between gold and projected data during joint training (batch composition, sampling ratio)? How sensitive are results to this ratio and to the learning rate schedule?

- Can you provide ablations on: (a) no pretraining; (b) A fixed to identity; (c) hard projected labels vs distributions for multi/NULL alignments; (d) using many-to-one and one-to-many alignments? These would clarify which design choices are essential.

- How many projected tokens per language were used after filtering? For the real languages, can you quantify alignment quality (e.g., alignment error rate) and show how performance scales with parallel data size?

- Did you run statistical significance tests for the reported improvements? If so, please report them.

Overall assessment
The paper meets the criteria well: it is clear, empirically strong, and addresses an important problem with a simple, effective idea. Novelty is moderate but impact and practicality are high. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying reproducibility and providing key ablations.
