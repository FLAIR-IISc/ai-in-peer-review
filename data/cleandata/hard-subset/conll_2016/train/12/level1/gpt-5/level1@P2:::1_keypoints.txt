- Proposed method: learn a linear noise layer (confusion matrix A) on top of a BiLSTM tagger, pretrain on 1k gold tokens then jointly train with noisy projected labels.

- Contribution 1: explicit label-noise modeling for cross-lingual projection via a learned confusion matrix that lets the model learn when to trust projections.

- Contribution 2: strong empirical validation across 10 languages (8 simulated, 2 real) with consistent gains over baselines and prior work.

- Contribution 3: a practical two-stage training recipe and interpretable analyses (visualizations of A) that aid adoption.

- Strength: conceptual clarity and practicality—minimal, well-motivated addition that captures systematic projection biases and integrates into end-to-end training.

- Strength: strong and broad empirical gains, notably larger improvements on genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

- Strength: evidence that A captures meaningful projection artifacts (strong diagonals with plausible off-diagonal confusions).

- Strength: sound experimental setup and fair baselines appropriate to the low-resource scenario.

- Weakness: limited novelty—label-noise/confusion-layer idea has precedents in distant/weak supervision.

- Weakness: missing ablations and sensitivity analyses (necessity of pretraining, initialization/regularization of A, mixing ratios, hard vs distributional labels).

- Weakness: incomplete reproducibility details (epochs, batch sizes, A initialization, sampling schedule, projected token counts and alignment quality).

- Weakness: comparisons omit stronger neural baselines (char features, CRF, multilingual embeddings), so unclear if gains hold on stronger taggers.

- Question: how is A initialized/regularized and is it biased toward identity to reflect typically-correct projections?

- Question: what is the gold vs projected mixing strategy during joint training and how sensitive are results to this ratio and learning schedule?

- Question: please provide ablations for no pretraining; A fixed to identity; hard vs distributional projected labels; handling many-to-one/one-to-many alignments.

- Question: how many projected tokens per language were used and what is the alignment quality; how does performance scale with parallel data size?

- Question: were statistical significance tests run for reported improvements; if so, please report them.

- Overall assessment: clear, practical, and empirically strong paper with moderate novelty; recommend acceptance conditional on improved reproducibility details and key ablations.
