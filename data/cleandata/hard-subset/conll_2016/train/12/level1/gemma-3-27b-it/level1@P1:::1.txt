This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in the projection process. The core idea – learning a "noise layer" to map projected tags to the target language’s tagset – is well-motivated and implemented within a BiLSTM framework. The results across both simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrate a consistent performance improvement over strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Noise-Aware Cross-Lingual Projection:** The most significant contribution is the introduction of the noise layer, a learnable transformation matrix that accounts for errors and biases in cross-lingual tag projection. This is a crucial step beyond simply treating projected data as another source of (potentially unreliable) supervision. This contribution directly addresses a well-known weakness of cross-lingual projection and provides a practical mechanism for mitigating its effects.
2. **Joint Training Framework:** The framework effectively integrates gold-standard data and noisy projected data within a single BiLSTM model, enabling joint learning and leveraging the strengths of both resources. The joint optimization allows the model to learn which projected tags to trust and which to disregard, resulting in more robust performance.
3. **Empirical Validation on Multiple Languages:** The thorough evaluation across eight simulated low-resource scenarios *and* two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) strengthens the claim of generalizability. The consistent improvements demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach across diverse linguistic contexts.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the inherent noise in cross-lingual projection. This strong motivation provides a solid foundation for the proposed solution.
2. **Novelty of the Noise Layer:**  The noise layer is a genuinely novel approach to address a practical problem in cross-lingual learning.  While previous work has attempted to filter or correct projected data, learning a transformation to explicitly model the noise is a unique and effective strategy.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance improvements over established baselines are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets demonstrates the practical value of the approach. The results on the real languages are particularly convincing, as these are not easily simulated.
4. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The experimental setup, including the use of both simulated and real low-resource languages, is well-thought-out. The comparison to relevant baselines is fair and comprehensive.
5. **Analysis of the Learned Noise Matrix:** Presenting and discussing the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insight into the types of errors the model is learning to correct and offers qualitative validation of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Quality:** While the paper acknowledges that word alignment errors contribute to noise, there’s limited discussion about the *quality* of the Giza++ alignments used. The impact of alignment quality on the model's performance isn’t thoroughly explored.  A brief analysis of alignment precision/recall, or the use of different alignment tools, would strengthen the work.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper lacks details regarding hyperparameter tuning. While the core parameters are mentioned, details on the search process, validation set size, and sensitivity to different hyperparameter values are missing.
3. **Complexity of Implementation:** The method introduces extra parameters (the noise matrix A) which increases the complexity of the model. There isn’t a comprehensive analysis of the computational cost associated with training and inference compared to simpler approaches, especially considering the benefit gained.
4. **Tagset Specificity:** The assumption of a universal tagset, while allowing for comparison, might not be optimal for all languages. The model's performance with drastically different tagset structures is not addressed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the word alignments used for cross-lingual projection? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or strategies for filtering low-confidence alignments?
2.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, validation set size, and sensitivity analysis?
3.  What is the computational overhead associated with the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model without it? (e.g., training time, inference time, memory usage).
4. How well does the model generalise to languages with very different tagset structures than the universal tagset used during training?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is clear and concise, though some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations (e.g., the exact formulation of the loss function).
*   The code availability would greatly enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.
*   Consider mentioning potential applications of this approach beyond POS tagging.
