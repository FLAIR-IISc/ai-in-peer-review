- Novel method: introduces a learnable “noise layer” to model and correct cross-lingual projection errors for low-resource POS tagging.  
- Architecture: integrates the noise layer into a BiLSTM for joint end-to-end training.  
- Empirical strength: achieves state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages.  
- Notable results: Malagasy accuracy 86.3% and Kinyarwanda 82.5% demonstrate strong real-world performance.  
- Evaluation breadth: tested across eight simulated settings and two real languages, supporting generalizability.  
- Presentation: paper is clear, well-written, and includes helpful figures illustrating the model and noise matrices.  
- Related work: thorough literature review that situates and differentiates the contribution.  
- Practicality: uses standard tools (Giza++, Stanford POS tagger) and modest BiLSTM compute, making it accessible.  
- Limited analysis: insufficient in-depth investigation of the learned noise transformation matrices and common mis-mappings.  
- Alignment sensitivity: paper does not analyze how results vary with word alignment quality or different alignment methods.  
- Tagset mismatch: lacking explicit evaluation of performance degradation as source/target tagsets diverge.  
- Reproducibility: hyperparameter tuning details (learning rate, hidden size) are underreported.  
- Questions for authors: request detailed analysis of A matrices, experiments on alignment sensitivity, tagset-divergence impact, and hyperparameter selection.  
- Minor fixes: clarify label “Ann+Proj”, add motivating language examples (e.g., absent determiners), and standardize citation formatting.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
