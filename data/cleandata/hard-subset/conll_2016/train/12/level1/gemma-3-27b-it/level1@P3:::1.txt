This paper presents a compelling approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The core idea of learning a “noise layer” to correct projection errors is novel and effectively implemented within a BiLSTM framework. The experimental results, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, strongly support the efficacy of the proposed method.

**Contributions:**

1. **Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The most significant contribution is the explicit modeling of projection noise as a learned transformation between tagsets. This moves beyond simple heuristics or filtering and allows the model to learn *how* and *when* to trust projected data, adapting to the specific characteristics of the source and target languages. This is a fundamentally valuable insight for anyone working with cross-lingual transfer.
2. **Joint Learning Framework:** The integration of the noise layer into a deep learning architecture (BiLSTM) and the resulting joint training objective are well-executed.  This allows for end-to-end optimization and avoids the need for separate, potentially sub-optimal, pre-processing or post-processing steps.
3. **Empirical Validation on Diverse Datasets:** The thorough evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings *and* two real languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of real-world languages is particularly important, as simulated data can sometimes mask practical challenges.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:**  The concept of a learnable noise transformation is genuinely novel. Previous work has largely focused on *reducing* projection noise through better alignments or heuristics. This work cleverly embraces the noise and learns to compensate for it, offering a more robust solution.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistent outperformance of existing state-of-the-art methods across a diverse set of languages is a powerful demonstration of the method’s effectiveness. Achieving SOTA on both simulated *and* real data builds considerable confidence in the approach. The accuracy numbers for Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%) are impressive given the low-resource setting.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The illustrations (e.g., Figure 2) are helpful in understanding the model architecture. The discussion of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable qualitative insights.
4. **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of relevant literature, effectively positioning their work within the broader context of low-resource NLP and cross-lingual transfer. They clearly articulate how their approach differs from and builds upon previous research.
5. **Practicality:** The use of readily available tools like Giza++ and the Stanford POS tagger, coupled with the relatively modest computational requirements of the BiLSTM model, suggests that this approach is practical for researchers with limited resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Transformation:** While the authors *show* the learned noise transformation matrices (A), the analysis of these matrices is somewhat limited. A more detailed investigation into the patterns observed in A could provide deeper insights into the types of errors that are being corrected and the cross-lingual relationships that the model is learning. For example, quantifying the frequency of specific mis-mappings would be valuable.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** Although the method is designed to be robust to noisy projections, the quality of the initial word alignments still plays a crucial role. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different alignment algorithms or parameters. A small ablation study varying alignment quality would be informative.
3. **Tagset Mismatch Handling:** The paper mentions that the method also supports mismatching tagsets but doesn't detail how performance changes as tagset divergence increases.  A more explicit investigation of the impact of tagset differences on performance would strengthen the claims of generality.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper briefly mentions the hyperparameter settings but lacks detail on the tuning process. Providing more information about how the learning rate and hidden layer size were selected would increase reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (A)? Specifically, what are the most common types of mis-mappings observed, and can these be related to known linguistic differences between the source and target languages?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment algorithms or parameters, and what impact did this have on performance?
3.  How does performance degrade as the tagsets of the source and target languages become more dissimilar?
4.  Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process?  How were the learning rate and hidden layer size selected?

**Additional Comments:**

* The phrase "Ann+Proj" is a bit cryptic; consider using a more descriptive label like "Gold+Projected".
* In the introduction, mentioning some specific examples of languages where determiners are absent or verb alignment is poor would further motivate the need for the proposed approach.
* The citation style is a little inconsistent (e.g., "Ammar et al.,? 2016" vs. "Duong et al.,? 2014a"). Please standardize the citation format.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
