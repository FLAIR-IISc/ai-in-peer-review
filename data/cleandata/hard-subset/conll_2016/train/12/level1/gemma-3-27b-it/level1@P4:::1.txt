This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in the projected data via a “noise layer” within a BiLSTM network. The core idea – to learn the transformation between noisy projected tags and clean gold tags – is a strong contribution. The experimental results, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, provide further evidence of the effectiveness of this approach. A secondary, but valuable contribution is the comprehensive evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings alongside two real languages, strengthening the generalizability claims.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is the most significant contribution. Previous work often relies on heuristics to filter or correct projected data; this method learns to adapt to the noise directly within the model, which is a more principled and potentially powerful approach. The noise layer allows the model to essentially ‘de-noise’ the projected data during training.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive performance across a diverse set of experiments. The results on the real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) are particularly compelling, showing significant improvements over previous methods. The consistent outperformance across eight simulated languages further strengthens the robustness of the method.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The inclusion of both simulated and real low-resource languages provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The use of a well-established evaluation setting (following Duong et al., 2014a) facilitates comparison with existing work and strengthens the validity of the results. The presentation of the transformation matrices A (Figures 3 & 4) offers valuable insight into the learned noise patterns.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of low-resource POS tagging and the limitations of existing approaches. The motivation for addressing the noise in cross-lingual projection is well-defined and justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Layer:** While the paper presents the learned transformation matrices (A), a more in-depth analysis of what the model *learns* about the noise would be beneficial. For example, are specific types of alignment errors consistently modeled by the noise layer? A more qualitative analysis could offer insights into the nature of the projection noise and how the model adapts to it.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The performance of the method relies heavily on the quality of the word alignments generated by Giza++. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore the impact of different alignment models or pre-processing techniques. Investigating the robustness of the approach to variations in alignment quality would strengthen the findings.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions the learning rate and network architecture, but lacks details regarding the tuning process for other hyperparameters (e.g., regularization, noise layer parameters). Providing more details on the hyperparameter optimization process would increase reproducibility and provide a more complete picture of the model's performance.
4. **Complexity Added:** Introducing the noise layer adds a degree of complexity to the model. While the performance gains justify this complexity, it would be valuable to discuss the computational cost associated with training and inference.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (A), specifically identifying common patterns or alignment errors that the model learns to correct?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or pre-processing techniques?
3. What was the process for tuning the hyperparameters of the model, particularly those related to the noise layer? Were any specific strategies employed to prevent overfitting?
4. Can you comment on the computational cost (training time, inference time) associated with the addition of the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The referencing is generally good, but consider adding citations to recent work on neural machine translation and alignment, as those advancements could potentially impact the quality of word alignments used in this study.
*   The figures are helpful, but the font size in Figure 3 and 4 is quite small. Increasing the font size would improve readability.
