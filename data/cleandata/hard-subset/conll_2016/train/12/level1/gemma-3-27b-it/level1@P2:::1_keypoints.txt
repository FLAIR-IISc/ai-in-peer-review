- Introduces a learnable “noise layer” to model and correct projection noise in cross-lingual POS tagging.  
- Integrates the noise layer into a BiLSTM and jointly trains on gold and projected data for end-to-end learning.  
- Strong empirical validation: state-of-the-art results on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages.  
- Novelty: moves beyond filtering noisy projections to learning a corrective transformation, with interpretable transformation matrices.  
- Robust experimental comparisons and ablations show consistent performance gains over established baselines.  
- Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful illustrative examples.  
- High practical relevance for low-resource language scenarios and limited annotated data.  
- Thorough evaluation across simulated and real datasets demonstrating generalizability.  
- Limited analysis of what the noise layer specifically learns; more interpretability needed (error types, language patterns).  
- Method depends on word alignment quality; lacks sensitivity analysis across alignment tools/parameters.  
- Adds parameters and complexity—computational overhead compared to simpler BiLSTM taggers is not discussed.  
- Missing hyperparameter details (e.g., dimensions of the A matrix) beyond learning rate and hidden size.  
- Asks for a deeper analysis of learned transformation matrices and language-specific error corrections.  
- Requests experiments on sensitivity to different alignment tools/parameters.  
- Requests clarification of computational overhead of the noise layer versus standard BiLSTM.  
- Notes: clab/cnn reference aids reproducibility; use of universal tagset is appropriate; suggest adding limitations and future work.
