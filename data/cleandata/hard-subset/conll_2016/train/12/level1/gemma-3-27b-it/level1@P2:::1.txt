This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in the projection process. The core idea – introducing a “noise layer” to learn the transformation between projected and true tags – is a solid contribution. The experimental results, showing improvements over existing methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Contributions:**

1. **Noise Modeling for Cross-Lingual Projection:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is the most significant contribution. Previous work largely treated projection errors as a fixed problem, addressed through heuristics or constraints. This work frames the issue as a learning problem, allowing the model to adapt to the specific noise characteristics of the projection process.
2. **Joint Training Framework:** The integration of the noise layer into a BiLSTM network and the joint training scheme (gold data + projected data) constitute a substantial contribution. This allows for end-to-end learning and enables the model to leverage both high-quality and noisy data effectively.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The comprehensive evaluation on eight simulated languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art results on these datasets adds significant value to the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The noise layer is a genuinely new idea in the context of cross-lingual projection. It moves beyond simply trying to filter noisy data and instead learns to *correct* for it. The learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) provide insights into the types of errors the model is identifying and mitigating.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistently strong performance across diverse datasets is convincing. The comparisons to established baselines, including the strong results from Duong et al. (2014a), demonstrate a clear improvement. The ablation studies implicitly provided by comparing different training configurations (BiLSTM Annotated, Projected, Ann+Proj) clearly show the benefits of the noise layer.
3. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, method, and results. The inclusion of illustrative examples (e.g., Figure 1) helps to understand the challenges of cross-lingual projection.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The focus on low-resource languages is highly relevant, and the method’s ability to work with limited annotated data is a significant advantage in real-world scenarios.
5. **Thorough Evaluation:** Evaluating on both simulated and real datasets demonstrates generalizability and provides valuable insights into the method's performance in different settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of the Noise Layer:** While the learned transformation matrices are presented, a deeper analysis of *what* the model is learning would be beneficial. For example, are specific types of errors consistently corrected? Are there language-specific patterns in the noise? More detailed interpretability of the noise layer could further strengthen the paper.
2. **Sensitivity to Alignment Quality:** The method relies heavily on the quality of word alignments. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't thoroughly investigate the impact of different alignment tools or alignment parameters. A brief sensitivity analysis could demonstrate the robustness of the method to alignment errors.
3. **Computational Cost:**  Adding the noise layer introduces additional parameters and computational complexity. A discussion of the computational cost compared to simpler approaches would be helpful.
4. **Hyperparameter tuning details**: The paper states the learning rate and the hidden layer size, but does not mention other hyperparameters. For example, what were the dimensions of the A matrix?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned transformation matrices (A) in Figure 3 and 4? What specific types of projection errors are being corrected, and are there any language-specific patterns?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality of word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or parameters?
3. What is the computational overhead of the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM tagger?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to clab/cnn is helpful for reproducibility.
*   The choice of using the universal tagset is a good one for comparability.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential limitations and future research directions in the conclusion.
