## Review of “Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection”

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in the projected data. The core idea is to augment a BiLSTM tagger with a “noise layer” that learns to correct errors from projection, effectively learning when and how much to trust the projected tags. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance. 

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a trainable “noise layer” within a neural network architecture to explicitly model and correct for errors introduced during cross-lingual projection. While noise modeling in machine translation and related tasks is not novel, applying it specifically to correct projected POS tags within a sequence tagging framework is a valuable contribution.  A secondary, though substantial, contribution is the comprehensive evaluation on a diverse set of languages, including two real-world low-resource settings. This provides strong empirical support for the effectiveness of the proposed method. Finally, the analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) offers insight into the types of errors the model learns to mitigate.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Layer:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a trainable layer is a clever and effective approach. This moves beyond simple filtering or heuristic adjustments of projected data.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across a wide range of languages.  The consistent improvements observed in both simulated and real-world scenarios demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough experimental setup, including both simulated and real low-resource settings, strengthens the credibility of the results and facilitates comparison with existing work.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrices provides valuable insight into the model's behavior and the types of projection errors it learns to correct. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Quality:** The paper briefly mentions alignment errors as a source of noise, but lacks a detailed analysis of the relationship between alignment quality and performance. A more thorough investigation of this relationship would be beneficial.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, noise layer dimensions) is not adequately addressed. It is unclear how much tuning was required to achieve the reported results.
3. **Complexity:** The addition of the noise layer introduces additional parameters and complexity to the model. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail about the sensitivity of your model to different hyperparameters, particularly those related to the noise layer?
2.  Did you explore the impact of alignment quality (e.g., using different alignment tools or filtering alignments based on confidence scores) on the performance of your method?
3.  How does the computational cost of your model compare to other state-of-the-art POS taggers for low-resource languages?
