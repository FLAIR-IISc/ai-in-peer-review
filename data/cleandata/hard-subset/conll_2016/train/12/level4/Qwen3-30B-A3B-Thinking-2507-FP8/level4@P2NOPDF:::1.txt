

This paper has undergone significant revision since its initial submission as an ACL 2016 short paper draft. The critical flaw in the experimental setup identified during that review has been adequately addressed, aligning with my prior suggestion that acceptance would be possible upon correction. I now find no substantive objections to the revised work.  

A secondary concern raised by another reviewer—that the experimental design may oversimplify real-world constraints for extremely low-resource languages, particularly regarding annotation cost trade-offs (e.g., acquiring vs. compensating annotators)—should be explicitly acknowledged in the manuscript. While the method is technically sound, the discussion should temper claims about broad applicability by clarifying the artificiality of the setup relative to practical deployment scenarios. This transparency is essential to avoid overstating the contribution.  

The related work section would benefit from a more comprehensive engagement with parallel efforts in annotation projection for under-resourced settings. Specifically, the work of Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agic et al. (ACL 2015), which directly address similar challenges in extremely low-resource contexts, should be cited and contrasted with this paper’s approach. This would strengthen the positioning of the contribution and ensure the literature review meets the standard of completeness expected at CONLL.  

The core technical contribution remains valuable: a well-motivated, empirically validated approach to leveraging parallel data for low-resource NLP tasks. The revised experiments now convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness, and the writing is clear and focused. Given the resolution of the initial flaw and the paper’s overall rigor, I strongly support acceptance.  

Finally, the authors should explicitly state whether the dataset(s) used in the experiments will be publicly released. As a reviewer, I have evaluated the paper under the assumption that research data will be made available to the community, consistent with CONLL’s expectations for reproducibility. If data availability is confirmed in the final version, this will further enhance the paper’s utility.  

In summary, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in low-resource NLP. With minor revisions to address the points above—particularly the experimental realism discussion and related work—the paper is ready for publication. I recommend acceptance.
