

The paper introduces a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, enabling parameter learning from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource settings. The traditional softmax output layer—which defines a distribution over possible labels—is extended by a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, yielding an output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels.  

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is well-suited, simple, and elegant. The authors report compelling results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, utilizing a small set of gold annotations alongside a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The method’s modularity ensures it may be readily adopted by researchers addressing diverse NLP challenges in low-resource scenarios.  

However, the experimental setup warrants critique. While scenarios requiring as few as 1,000 gold tokens (e.g., certain DARPA-sponsored evaluations) exist, they are uncommon. A more robust empirical validation would have included accuracy plots against varying gold annotation sizes. Such plots would clarify critical questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? What is the approximate threshold of gold annotations below which the method becomes beneficial? And does this threshold vary across target languages?  

Beyond cross-lingual projection, noisy labels could derive from alternative sources (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations) or differ in tag sets from gold data. While the broader applicability is promising, the paper only validates results using cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets.  

It is surprising that the training objective assigns equal weights to gold and noisy labels. Given the assumption of limited gold annotations, it would have been informative to report whether tuning the relative contributions of these terms in the objective function improves performance.  

In line 357, the projected data is described as pairs of word tokens $(x_t)$ and their vector representations $\tilde{y}$, but the nature of $\tilde{y}$ is not explicitly defined (e.g., whether it represents a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for a word type). A natural inquiry is whether the approach remains effective when $\tilde{y}$ is constructed from token-level projected POS tags (rather than aggregating predictions per word type). Additionally, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how $\tilde{y}$ is constructed for words without alignments.
