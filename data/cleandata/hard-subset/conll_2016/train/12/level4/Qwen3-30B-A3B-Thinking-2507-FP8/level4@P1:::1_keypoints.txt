- Summary: Proposes a neural sequence tagger that jointly trains on 1,000 gold tokens and noisy cross-lingual projected tags using an explicit noise layer; evaluated on 8 simulated languages and Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, achieving SOTA.

- Strength: Noise layer effectively models projection noise, enabling better use of projected tags and avoiding performance drops from naively using projections.

- Strength: Strong empirical results—outperforms all baselines across eight simulated languages and sets new SOTA for Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%).

- Strength: Method is generalizable; the noise-modeling approach could benefit other tasks using noisy distant supervision (e.g., crowdsourced labels).

- Weakness: Applicability may be overstated because the cost/availability of obtaining 1,000 gold tokens isn’t compared to the cost/availability of parallel data in true low-resource settings.

- Weakness: Related work omissions—does not discuss Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agić et al. (ACL 2015), which address similar minimal-gold projection problems.

- Weakness: Interpretation of the noise layer relies on visualizations but lacks statistical analysis linking observed noise patterns to accuracy gains.

- Question: How do you quantify the relative costs (time/resources) of obtaining 1,000 gold tokens versus acquiring parallel data for projection?

- Question: Can the noise layer be adapted to languages with no parallel data (e.g., via monolingual transfer), and if not, what are the limitations?

- Question: Please clarify data and code availability to ensure reproducibility.
