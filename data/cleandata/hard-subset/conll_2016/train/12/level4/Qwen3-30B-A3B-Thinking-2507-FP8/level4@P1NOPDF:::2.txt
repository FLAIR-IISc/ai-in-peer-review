

The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, enabling parameter learning from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource settings. The traditional softmax output layer—which defines a label distribution—is augmented with a fully connected layer modeling noise generation, yielding a new output layer representing a distribution over noisy labels. Overall, this is a solid contribution: the method is well-motivated, simple, and elegant. The authors demonstrate strong results for POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, leveraging minimal gold annotations alongside extensive cross-lingually projected data. Its modularity suggests broad applicability for researchers tackling diverse NLP tasks in low-resource scenarios.  

However, the experimental setup warrants critical consideration. While building a POS tagger with as few as 1,000 gold tokens may occur in niche contexts (e.g., certain DARPA-funded projects), this scenario is uncommon. A more compelling validation would have included a plot showing tagging accuracy as gold annotation size varies. Such a plot would clarify key practical questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? What is the approximate threshold below which it becomes beneficial? And does this threshold depend on the target language?  

The paper also limits its noise source analysis to cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets, overlooking broader potential. Noisy labels could also originate from alternative sources (e.g., crowd-sourcing) or mismatched tag sets, representing a promising extension. While the current results are valid, exploring these avenues would strengthen the work’s impact.  

Another concern is the equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the training objective. Given the assumption of limited gold data, it would be valuable to report whether tuning the relative weights of these terms improves performance. This could reveal whether the method’s efficacy is sensitive to the gold/noisy data balance.  

Regarding technical details: Line 357 describes projected data as pairs of word tokens $(x_t)$ and vector representations $\tilde{y}$, but fails to specify the nature of $\tilde{y}$ (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for that word type). This raises a natural question: Would the approach remain effective if $\tilde{y}$ were constructed using token-level projected tags (rather than aggregating predictions across identical word types)? Additionally, since the method preserves only one-to-one word alignments, it is unclear how $\tilde{y}$ would be defined for unaligned words.  

Finally, Line 267 contains a typo: replace one closing bracket with an opening bracket.
