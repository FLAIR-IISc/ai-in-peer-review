- Formalizes projection noise as a learnable transformation matrix and jointly trains a model on gold and projected data with an explicit "noise layer."  
- Proposes a joint training framework that dynamically weights projected data based on learned noise patterns.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical validation across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world cases (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Main novelty: explicit noise-layer mechanism that models projection errors rather than implicitly correcting them.

- Novel noise modeling: learning matrix A to correct projected tags yields interpretable, language-specific correction patterns and tangible accuracy gains.  
- Strong empirical validation: consistent improvements over baselines in both simulated and real-language settings (e.g., Malagasy 86.3% vs. 85.3%; Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs. 78.3%).  
- Practical relevance: requires only ~1,000 gold tokens and parallel data, making it applicable to many low-resource scenarios and language families.

- Simulation concern: using small gold sets from resource-rich languages may misrepresent truly low-resource scenarios that also lack parallel data.  
- Incomplete related work: omits prior minimal-resource/unsupervised projection studies (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agic et al. 2015), leaving comparisons incomplete.  
- Data availability unclear: paper does not state whether parallel corpora and gold annotations will be released, hurting reproducibility.

- How does the approach perform when no parallel data is available—does it degrade or need adaptation?  
- How does the learned noise matrix A correlate with the amount of parallel data (e.g., large vs. small parallel corpora)?  
- Can the noise layer be extended to multiple source languages simultaneously, and how would that affect matrix A?

- Overall recommendation: strong, well-motivated contribution with compelling results; recommend acceptance pending minor revisions on simulation realism, related work, and data release.
