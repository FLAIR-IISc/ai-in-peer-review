

This paper introduces a novel modification to the output layer of RNNs for low-resource NLP, enabling joint learning from limited gold annotations and cross-lingually projected labels. The core innovation replaces the standard softmax layer with a two-stage process: a fully connected layer models noise generation, yielding a distribution over noisy labels. This approach is well-motivated, elegant, and demonstrates strong empirical results on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world low-resource languages (e.g., Burmese, Yoruba), using only 1,000 gold tokens and large-scale projected data. The method’s modularity makes it broadly applicable to other low-resource NLP tasks, representing a significant contribution to the field.

The work’s primary strength lies in its simplicity and effectiveness. The proposed architecture requires minimal changes to standard RNN pipelines while achieving state-of-the-art results under extreme data scarcity. The experimental validation is thorough for the target setting, convincingly showing that projected annotations substantially improve performance when gold data is scarce. The results on truly low-resource languages are particularly compelling, demonstrating practical viability beyond simulated scenarios.

However, the experimental design warrants refinement to strengthen the paper’s broader claims. The focus on a fixed 1,000-gold-token setting, while relevant for specific contexts (e.g., DARPA projects), lacks generalizability. A critical gap is the absence of a systematic analysis of how performance scales with varying gold annotation sizes. A plot showing tagging accuracy against increasing gold token counts (e.g., 500–10,000 tokens) would clarify: (1) whether the method degrades when ample gold data is available (e.g., does it harm performance at 5,000 tokens?), (2) the threshold below which the approach becomes beneficial, and (3) whether this threshold varies across target languages. Such analysis would transform the paper from a "proof of concept" into a method with clear, actionable guidelines for practitioners.

The scope of label noise sources also feels underexplored. While the paper focuses exclusively on cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets, noisy labels could arise from other sources (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations) or even different tag sets (e.g., coarse-grained labels for projection). The authors briefly acknowledge this potential but provide no empirical validation. Including even a single experiment with a non-cross-lingual noise source (e.g., crowd-sourced tags) would significantly broaden the method’s perceived utility and address a natural reader question.

A technical concern pertains to the training objective’s equal weighting of gold and noisy labels. Given the setup assumes a *small* gold corpus, it is counterintuitive that both terms receive identical weight. The paper should investigate whether tuning the relative contribution of the two loss components (e.g., via a hyperparameter) yields further gains. Reporting the optimal weight ratio or sensitivity analysis would provide crucial insight into the method’s robustness and adaptability.

The description of the projected labels requires greater precision. Line 357 refers to \tilde{y} as "vector representations" but fails to specify their nature (e.g., whether \tilde{y} is a distribution over projected POS tags for a word type, or a token-level tag). This ambiguity raises a key question: does the method require aggregating predictions across all occurrences of a word (e.g., for "run" → [VB, NN]), or would token-level projected tags suffice? Clarifying this is essential for reproducibility. Additionally, the method relies on one-to-one word alignments, yet it does not address how \tilde{y} is constructed for unaligned words (e.g., loanwords, morphologically complex terms). A brief discussion of this edge case would strengthen the technical foundation.

Minor issues include a typo in line 267 (a misplaced closing bracket requiring an opening bracket) and the need for slightly tighter phrasing in the abstract (e.g., "models the noise generation process" → "models noise in projected labels").

Overall, this is a strong submission with a clear, impactful contribution to low-resource NLP. The method is simple, effective, and broadly applicable. Addressing the experimental design gaps (especially the gold data size analysis) and technical clarifications would elevate the paper to a top-tier result. The current work provides a valuable tool for practitioners, but the proposed extension to varying gold data regimes would make it indispensable. With these revisions, the paper would make a compelling contribution to CONLL.
