- Summary: Proposes a noise-layer neural model that jointly learns from small gold POS annotations and cross-lingually projected noisy tags; evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages with 1000 gold tokens.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a noise layer that models mapping between projected tags and true tags to learn when to trust noisy projected data.

- Contribution 2: Achieves state-of-the-art results on simulated and real low-resource languages using only 1000 gold tokens.

- Contribution 3: Presents a modular approach that can be applied to other low-resource sequence labeling tasks.

- Strength 1: Effectively addresses leveraging noisy distant supervision, showing strong empirical gains with a simple, practical method.

- Strength 2: Method is generalizable—noise layer can be added to any sequence tagger with softmax output.

- Strength 3: Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering simulated and real settings and comparing to strong baselines.

- Weakness 1: Does not explore trade-offs between amounts of gold vs. projected data (e.g., performance at 100, 500, 5000 tokens).

- Weakness 2: Evaluation only uses the same tag set for projection; lacks experiments with differing tag sets or other noisy label sources.

- Weakness 3: Training objective weights gold and projected data equally; no investigation of tuning relative loss weights when gold is scarce.

- Weakness 4: Projected data representation is unclear—paper omits explicit construction/details of the vector \tilde{y} and handling of unaligned words.

- Question 1: How does performance scale when increasing gold data beyond 1000 tokens, and when does the noise layer become less beneficial?

- Question 2: Can the approach handle different tag sets between source and target languages, and what is the impact?

- Question 3: How sensitive is performance to weighting between gold and projected loss terms, and what weights were optimal?

- Question 4: How is \tilde{y} constructed for words without alignments, and does the method work when only one-to-one alignments are preserved?

- Overall: Valuable, well-motivated contribution with clear implementation and strong results, but needs clearer projected-data details and broader experimental analysis.
