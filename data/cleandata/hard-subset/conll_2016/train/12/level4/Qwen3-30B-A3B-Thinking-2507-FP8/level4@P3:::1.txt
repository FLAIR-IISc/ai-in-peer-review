

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection from a resource-rich language. The authors propose a novel neural framework that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through a "noise layer" and jointly trains on both gold-standard and projected data. Their main contributions are: (1) a formalization of noise in cross-lingual tag projection as a learnable transformation matrix, (2) a joint training framework that dynamically weights projected data based on learned noise patterns, and (3) strong empirical validation across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world cases (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results. The most significant contribution is the noise layer mechanism, which fundamentally shifts from implicit correction (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a) to explicit modeling of projection errors.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel noise modeling approach**: The paper's core innovation—treating projected tags as noisy observations and learning a transformation matrix A to correct them—is theoretically sound and practically effective. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this approach outperforms prior work by 1.5–3.7% in accuracy (Table 1) and provides interpretable insights into language-specific noise patterns (Figure 3). The matrix A reveals systematic errors (e.g., NOUN→ADJ confusion in Malagasy), showing the model learns meaningful correction patterns rather than memorizing data.  

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation spans both simulated low-resource settings (using Europarl data) and real-world languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), with consistent improvements over baselines. For real languages, the method achieves 86.3% accuracy for Malagasy (vs. 85.3% for Duong et al.) and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda (vs. 78.3%), demonstrating practical utility. The ablation study (Table 1) proves that the noise layer is essential—BiLSTM+noise outperforms BiLSTM+projected by 1.4–10.7% across languages.  

3. **Practical relevance**: The method requires only 1,000 gold tokens (easily obtainable via cheap crowdsourcing, as shown by Garrette & Baldridge, 2013) and leverages widely available parallel data. This makes it highly applicable to real-world low-resource scenarios where gold data is scarce but bilingual resources exist. The authors’ demonstration of utility for two distinct language families (Austronesian, Niger-Congo) further strengthens the case for generalizability.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Artificial simulation of low-resource settings**: The paper simulates low-resource scenarios by using small gold datasets (1,000 tokens) from resource-rich languages (e.g., Dutch, Spanish), which misrepresents real low-resource contexts. In practice, low-resource languages lack *both* gold data *and* parallel resources (e.g., Kinyarwanda has minimal parallel text). The authors should clarify that their approach assumes parallel data availability—a key constraint not adequately addressed in the discussion.  

2. **Insufficient related work**: The paper overlooks critical work on annotation projection for *extremely* low-resource languages, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) on minimal-resource POS tagging and Agic et al. (2015) on unsupervised projection. These works address similar challenges with even smaller gold data, making the comparison incomplete. The authors should explicitly discuss how their noise-layer approach differs from or complements these methods.  

3. **Data availability unclear**: The paper does not state whether the datasets (e.g., parallel corpora, gold annotations) will be publicly released. Given the paper's focus on reproducibility and low-resource applications, this omission weakens the contribution. The authors should clarify data availability in the final version.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your noise-layer approach scale to languages with *no* parallel data (e.g., where only gold data exists)? Would the method degrade or require adaptation?  
2. The paper notes that "Malagasy has more parallel data than Kinyarwanda" (Table 2). How does the noise matrix A correlate with parallel data size (e.g., 100k vs. 11k sentences)?  
3. Could the noise layer be extended to handle *multiple* source languages (e.g., projecting from English, French, and Spanish simultaneously)? If so, how would this affect the transformation matrix A?  

**Overall Recommendation**  
This is a strong contribution that advances low-resource POS tagging through a novel noise-aware framework. The empirical results are compelling, and the approach is well-motivated. With minor revisions addressing the artificial simulation, related work, and data availability, this paper would be an excellent fit for CONLL 2016. I recommend acceptance.
