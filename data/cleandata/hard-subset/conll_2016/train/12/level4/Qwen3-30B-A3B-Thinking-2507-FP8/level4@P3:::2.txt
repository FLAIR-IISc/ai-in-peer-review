

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a novel neural model for low-resource POS tagging that jointly learns from a small gold-standard corpus and noisy cross-lingual projected annotations. The primary contribution is a *noise layer* that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from high-resource language projections) and true tags, enabling the model to learn *when and how much to trust* the noisy data. The authors integrate this layer into a BiLSTM framework, training end-to-end to jointly optimize both gold and projected supervision. Secondary contributions include a rigorous evaluation across eight simulated low-resource languages (using Europarl) and two real-world languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent state-of-the-art results.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel noise modeling approach**: The core contribution—explicitly learning a transformation matrix *A* to correct projection errors—is conceptually simple yet highly effective. Unlike prior work that implicitly handles noise (e.g., via joint features), this method *directly models the noise process*, enabling the model to adapt to language-specific biases (e.g., ignoring verb tags in languages without determiners). The noise layer’s interpretability (evident in Figure 3) validates its practical utility.  
2. **Strong empirical results**: The method uniformly outperforms all baselines (including Duong et al.’s state-of-the-art) across 8 simulated languages (e.g., +1.6% accuracy over Duong on German) and both real low-resource languages (Malagasy: 86.3% vs. 85.3% for Duong; Kinyarwanda: 82.5% vs. 78.3%). Crucially, it leverages *only 1,000 gold tokens*—a realistic low-resource constraint—proving the viability of the approach.  
3. **Modularity and generalizability**: The noise layer is agnostic to the source of noise (e.g., cross-lingual projection, crowdsourcing), making it broadly applicable. The authors’ demonstration of tagset mismatch handling (Section 3.2) and clean integration into standard BiLSTM architectures provide a clear path for adoption in other low-resource NLP tasks.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unrealistic gold data constraint**: The paper evaluates *only* on a fixed 1,000-token gold set, ignoring the practical question of *how much gold data is needed for this method to be beneficial*. A more compelling validation would include ablation studies varying gold data size (e.g., 500 vs. 5,000 tokens), addressing whether the method *only* helps when gold data is extremely scarce. Without this, the claim of "low-resource" applicability lacks nuance.  
2. **Lack of noise-weight tuning**: The training objective treats gold and projected data equally (Equation 3), yet the paper assumes *minimal* gold data. It is unclear why the authors did not explore tuning the relative weights of the two loss terms (e.g., weighting gold data higher). The results could be further improved if the model prioritizes gold data when available, and this hyperparameter should be addressed.  
3. **Ambiguity in projected label construction**: The paper states projected labels are derived from "tag frequency distributions over English sentences" (Section 3.1.2), but does not clarify how *word-level* projected tags are constructed (e.g., for unaligned tokens). Section 3.1.1 notes "NULL-aligned tokens" are handled via sentence-level distributions, but it remains unclear how this affects the noise layer’s learning. A concrete example (e.g., handling *unaligned tokens in Kinyarwanda*) would strengthen the methodology.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper evaluates *only* on 1,000 gold tokens. How does performance change when using *larger* gold datasets (e.g., 5,000 tokens)? Does the noise layer’s benefit diminish as gold data increases, or is it universally advantageous?  
2. In Equation 3, gold and projected losses are weighted equally. Did you experiment with varying these weights (e.g., λ ∈ [0.1, 1.0])? If so, what was the optimal λ for each language, and how did it impact results?  
3. For words with no English alignment (e.g., "NULL" tokens), the projected label is derived from the *entire English sentence’s tag distribution*. How does this compare to alternative approaches (e.g., using the most frequent tag in the sentence)? Could this introduce bias, and if so, how does the noise layer mitigate it?
