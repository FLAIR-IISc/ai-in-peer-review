- Proposed model: a BiLSTM POS tagger with a learned noise layer that models transformation from noisy cross-lingual projected tags to true tags, trained end-to-end.  
- Evaluation: rigorous tests on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages, claiming state-of-the-art using only 1,000 gold tokens.

- Strength: novel noise modeling—learns a transformation matrix A to correct projection errors and adapt to language-specific biases; interpretable.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains across languages (e.g., German +1.6% over prior SOTA; Malagasy 86.3% vs 85.3%; Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs 78.3%) with 1,000 gold tokens.  
- Strength: modular and generalizable noise layer—agnostic to noise source, handles tagset mismatch, easily integrates into standard BiLSTM architectures.

- Weakness: evaluation uses only a fixed 1,000-token gold set—no analysis of how performance varies with different amounts of gold data.  
- Weakness: training treats gold and projected losses equally; no experiments tuning relative loss weights to prioritize gold data.  
- Weakness: unclear projected-label construction for unaligned/NULL tokens—insufficient detail and no concrete examples of its effect on noise learning.

- Q1: How does performance change with larger gold datasets (e.g., 5,000 tokens); does the noise layer’s benefit persist or diminish?  
- Q2: Did you vary the weight between gold and projected losses (λ) and what were the optimal settings per language?  
- Q3: For NULL-aligned tokens, why use sentence-level tag distributions instead of alternatives (e.g., most frequent sentence tag), and could this introduce bias?
