

The paper introduces a novel noise layer for sequence tagging models that explicitly models the transformation between gold-standard tags and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. This enables joint training on both gold-annotated and projected data without requiring manual cleaning of the projected annotations. The primary contribution is the formulation of the noise transformation as a learnable parameter matrix within a neural architecture, rather than treating projected tags as directly usable supervision. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that this approach consistently outperforms baselines across both simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) using only 1,000 gold tokens.

Strengths: The proposed noise layer is conceptually simple and elegant, providing a modular solution that can be integrated into existing sequence tagging frameworks without complex modifications. The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world languages with minimal gold annotation (1,000 tokens), demonstrating practical utility for low-resource NLP. The method's generality is well-justified through the demonstration that it handles tagset mismatches and learns language-specific noise patterns, as evidenced by the learned transformation matrices.

Weaknesses: The experimental design is limited to a single fixed amount of gold annotation (1,000 tokens), omitting a critical analysis of how performance scales with varying amounts of gold data. A more informative validation would include accuracy curves showing performance as gold annotation size increases, which would clarify the method's utility threshold for different languages. The evaluation is restricted to cross-lingual projection with identical tagsets, without exploring whether the approach generalizes to other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations or different tagset mappings). The training objective assigns equal weight to gold and projected data without investigating whether optimal weighting depends on language or data quality.

Questions to Authors: How would the method perform if the projected labels were constructed at the token level (rather than aggregated per-word type)? For words that never align in the one-to-one alignment setup, how is the projected label vector $\tilde{y}$ constructed?
