

Summary: This paper introduces a novel neural approach for low-resource POS tagging by jointly training on small gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The primary contribution is an explicit noise layer modeling the transformation between projected and true tags, addressing a core limitation in prior work. Secondary contributions include joint training of gold and projected data within a BiLSTM framework, and robust empirical validation across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world cases (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results (86.3% and 82.5% accuracy).

Strengths:
1. **Corrected experimental flaw and strong empirical validation**: The authors addressed a critical flaw in the prior ACL draft (inconsistent data splits), leading to reliable results. Their method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines (Duong et al., 2014a) on both simulated (8 languages, Table 1) and real low-resource settings (Malagasy: +1.0% over Duong; Kinyarwanda: +4.2% over Duong), demonstrating clear utility. The noise layer’s effectiveness is visually corroborated in Figure 3 (diagonal dominance with language-specific noise patterns).
2. **Novel noise modeling approach**: Unlike prior work that implicitly handles projection noise (e.g., Duong et al., 2014a), this paper explicitly models the tag transformation via a learnable matrix *A*. This directly addresses the core problem of unreliable projected tags (Section 3.2), enabling the model to "learn when to trust" projected data (e.g., ignoring misaligned verb tags as shown in Figure 2).
3. **Practical applicability for low-resource settings**: The method requires only 1,000 gold tokens (matching Garrette & Baldridge, 2013’s cost estimate of <2 hours of annotation), making it highly feasible for real-world deployment. The results on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda (Table 2) prove effectiveness where prior methods (Garrette et al., 2013) fall short.

Weaknesses:
1. **Artificiality of the real-world evaluation**: The paper overstates applicability to *truly* low-resource languages by using parallel data from web sources (e.g., Malagasy: 100k sentences), which is not representative of the most resource-poor scenarios where such data is scarce. The authors should clarify that this setup assumes *some* parallel data exists, and explicitly discuss limitations when parallel resources are unavailable (e.g., for languages with no English-aligned texts).
2. **Incomplete related work**: The related work (Section 2) omits key recent advances in low-resource annotation projection, including Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) on unsupervised tag projection and Agic et al. (2015, ACL) on multilingual transfer. This undermines the paper’s claim of novelty in "exploiting noisy projections" and should be addressed.
3. **Data availability unconfirmed**: The review assumes data will be made public (as stated in the draft), but this is not explicitly stated in the paper. Authors must confirm public release of datasets (e.g., Malagasy/Kinyarwanda parallel data) to ensure reproducibility.

Questions to Authors:
1. Will the parallel data (Malagasy: 100k sentences; Kinyarwanda: 11k sentences) and annotated datasets be publicly released to enable reproducibility?
2. How does the method handle cases where *no* parallel data exists (e.g., for languages with no English-aligned resources), given the paper’s focus on "low-resource" settings where parallel data is often unavailable? Please clarify this limitation in the discussion.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with a clear, novel solution to a significant challenge in low-resource POS tagging. The corrected experiments and empirical results are compelling, and the method’s efficiency (1,000 gold tokens) is highly practical. With minor revisions addressing the artificiality concern, related work, and data availability, this paper merits acceptance. I recommend acceptance.
