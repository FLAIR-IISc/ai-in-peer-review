- Summary: Proposes a neural sequence-tagging model with an explicit "noise layer" and joint training on gold and projected data, showing consistent gains across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages.

- Strength: Noise layer is elegant and well-motivated, explicitly modeling projection noise and yielding consistent performance gains.

- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation on multiple simulated and real low-resource settings, achieving strong results with only 1,000 gold tokens.

- Strength: Method is modular and generalizable beyond POS tagging to other noisy supervision scenarios.

- Weakness: Evaluation focuses only on the artificial 1,000-token gold scenario; lacks results across varying amounts of gold data.

- Weakness: Generalization beyond cross-lingual projection with identical tag sets is not empirically explored despite claims of broader applicability.

- Weakness: Training treats gold and projected data equally; no exploration of weighting the two loss terms given limited gold data.

- Weakness: Insufficient detail about the projected-data representation (per-word distributions vs. aggregated types) and handling of unaligned words.

- Question: Please analyze which language-specific factors (e.g., syntactic divergence, alignment quality) drive the variation in the learned noise matrices.

- Question: Please provide preliminary results for mismatched tag sets to support the claimed applicability beyond identical tag sets.

- Question: How does using a different alignment model (e.g., neural aligner vs. IBM Model 3) affect projection noise and final performance?
