

The paper introduces a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, enabling parameter learning from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource language scenarios. The traditional softmax output layer, which defines a label distribution, is augmented by a fully connected layer modeling the noise generation process, yielding an output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels.  

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is well-motivated, straightforward, and elegant. The paper demonstrates effective results on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, leveraging a small gold annotation set and a large cross-lingually projected annotation set. Its modularity ensures broad applicability to diverse NLP tasks in low-resource settings.  

However, the experimental design is atypical. While scenarios with as few as 1,000 token gold annotations exist (e.g., certain DARPA projects), they are uncommon. A more robust validation would involve plotting tagging accuracy against varying gold annotation sizes, addressing key questions: (1) Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? (2) What is the approximate threshold of gold annotations below which the method becomes advantageous? (3) How does this threshold vary across target languages?  

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could derive from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or different tag sets. The paper’s focus solely on cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets limits the demonstrated scope.  

Additionally, the training objective assigns equal weight to gold and noisy labels—a surprising choice given the small gold corpus. It would be valuable to investigate whether tuning the relative weights in the objective improves performance.  

Line 357 describes projected data as (x_t, \tilde{y}) without clarifying \tilde{y}'s nature (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags per word type). This raises questions: (1) Would the approach remain effective using token-level projected POS tags instead of aggregated word-type predictions? (2) How is \tilde{y} constructed for unaligned words, given the reliance on one-to-one word alignments?
