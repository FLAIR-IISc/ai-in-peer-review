

The paper introduces a novel modification to recurrent neural network output layers, enabling joint learning from gold and cross-lingually projected annotations in low-resource settings. The method replaces the standard softmax output with a noise-aware layer: the original label distribution is multiplied by a fully connected layer modeling noise generation, yielding a distribution over noisy labels. This approach is well-motivated, elegant, and practical, with strong empirical results on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real-world languages (e.g., Somali, Swahili). The authors demonstrate significant gains using minimal gold annotations (1,000 tokens) alongside large-scale projected data, and the modular design ensures broad applicability to other low-resource NLP tasks.  

The primary strength lies in the method’s simplicity and empirical effectiveness. The results convincingly show that projected annotations—when combined with sparse gold data—can substantially improve performance, addressing a critical bottleneck in low-resource NLP. The paper’s clarity in framing the noise model and its implementation further enhance its contribution.  

However, three key limitations warrant attention. First, the experimental design is atypical: the use of exactly 1,000 gold tokens is uncommon outside niche contexts (e.g., specific DARPA projects) and does not reflect the typical low-resource scenario where gold data ranges from 5,000–20,000 tokens. A more rigorous validation would involve plotting tagging accuracy against varying gold annotation sizes (e.g., 100–20,000 tokens) to answer critical questions: (1) Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? (2) What is the threshold below which it becomes beneficial? (3) How does this threshold vary across languages? Such an analysis would better establish the method’s practical utility.  

Second, the paper narrowly focuses on cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets, ignoring broader applications of noisy labels. For instance, noisy labels could originate from crowdsourcing (with potentially mismatched tag sets) or weak supervision. The authors should address whether their approach generalizes to these scenarios, as this would significantly expand the method’s relevance.  

Third, the objective function assigns equal weight to gold and noisy labels—a choice that seems suboptimal given the scarcity of gold data. The authors should investigate whether tuning this weight (e.g., via validation) improves performance. For example, could a higher weight on gold labels mitigate noise when gold data is available? A sensitivity analysis here would strengthen the method’s practicality.  

Technical clarifications are also needed. Line 357 describes $\tilde{y}$ as "a vector representation" without specifying its form (e.g., a distribution over projected POS tags per word type). This ambiguity raises a key question: would the method work if $\tilde{y}$ were derived from token-level projected tags (rather than aggregated per word type)? Additionally, the current approach relies solely on one-to-one word alignments, making $\tilde{y}$ undefined for unaligned words. The authors should discuss how to handle such cases (e.g., via back-translation or alignment heuristics).  

Finally, Line 267 contains a typo: one closing bracket should be replaced with an opening bracket.  

Overall, this is a compelling contribution with clear potential for low-resource NLP. Addressing the above points—particularly the experimental validation strategy and generalizability to non-cross-lingual noisy labels—would elevate the work to a strong CONLL 2016 submission. The paper’s core idea is promising, but deeper empirical exploration and technical refinement are needed to fully demonstrate its impact.
