

Summary: This paper proposes a novel neural sequence tagging model for low-resource languages that jointly learns from a small gold-standard corpus and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The core contribution is an explicit "noise layer" that models the transformation between projected tags and true tags during joint training. The authors evaluate on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art results with only 1,000 gold tokens.

Strengths:
1. The noise layer effectively addresses a critical limitation in cross-lingual projection: directly using projected tags harms performance (Table 1, "BiLSTM Projected" vs. "BiLSTM Annotated"). Their joint training framework explicitly models projection noise, enabling better utilization of distant supervision.
2. The method achieves strong empirical results: it outperforms all baselines (including Duong et al. 2014a) across all eight simulated languages (Table 1) and sets new SOTA for Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), significantly exceeding prior work (Table 2).
3. The approach is generalizable beyond POS tagging—its noise modeling framework could improve other tasks using noisy distant supervision (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations), as noted in the conclusion.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation setup risks overstating real-world applicability. While the paper uses small gold datasets (1,000 tokens), the *cost* of obtaining these annotations (e.g., "less than 2 hours of non-expert time" for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda) is not contrasted with the *cost* of parallel data acquisition. For many low-resource languages, parallel data may be as scarce (or scarcer) than gold annotations, making the "low-resource" scenario artificial. The authors should clarify this limitation.
2. The related work section omits key prior work on annotation projection for extremely low-resource settings, specifically Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agić et al. (ACL 2015). These papers address similar problems with minimal gold data and should be discussed to position the contribution accurately.
3. The noise layer’s interpretation relies on visualizations (Figure 3, 4), but the paper does not provide statistical evidence for *why* the noise patterns (e.g., "NUM vs DET in Italian") improve performance. A brief analysis of how specific noise patterns correlate with accuracy gains would strengthen the argument.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you quantify the relative costs (time, resources) of obtaining 1,000 gold tokens versus the parallel data required for projection? This is critical for assessing the method’s practicality in *true* low-resource settings.
2. Could the noise layer be adapted for languages with no parallel data (e.g., via monolingual transfer)? If not, what are the fundamental limitations?
3. Please clarify the data availability statement. The review assumes public release of code/data; confirmation is needed for reproducibility.
