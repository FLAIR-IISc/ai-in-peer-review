

Summary: This paper proposes a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected annotations. The authors frame this as joint learning over two corpora: one with gold-standard tags and one with projected tags. Their model uses a BiLSTM with an additional noise layer that learns the mapping between projected tags and gold tags. Evaluated with only 1000 gold-standard tokens, the system achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Main contributions:
1. The authors introduce an explicit noise layer to model the transformation between projected and gold tags, rather than treating projected tags as clean data.
2. They propose a joint training approach that effectively combines both gold-standard and projected data to improve POS tagging performance.
3. The method demonstrates consistent improvements over previous approaches on both simulated low-resource settings and real-world low-resource languages.

Strengths:
1. The noise layer effectively captures the mapping between projected tags and gold tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. This is a significant improvement over previous methods that treated projected tags as clean or used implicit correction.
2. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results with minimal gold-standard data (only 1000 tokens), which is highly relevant for low-resource language applications where annotated data is scarce.
3. The joint training framework successfully leverages both gold-standard and projected data, outperforming methods that either ignored the noise in projected data or handled it implicitly through feature engineering.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately address the practical limitations of the approach for real-world applications, particularly regarding the cost comparison between obtaining parallel data versus annotating small datasets. This is important for evaluating the practicality of the method.
2. The related work section should incorporate recent advances in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), to provide a more complete context for the contribution.
3. The experimental setup for real low-resource languages could be better justified in terms of practical applicability, as the costs of finding parallel data versus paying annotators should be discussed.

Questions to Authors:
1. Will the datasets and code be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility?
