

The paper introduces a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, enabling parameter learning from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource languages. The standard softmax output layer—which defines a label distribution—is multiplied by a fully connected layer modeling the noise generation process, yielding an output layer representing a distribution over noisy labels.  

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant. The paper reports robust results for POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, leveraging a small gold-annotated corpus and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. Its modularity ensures broad applicability for researchers addressing diverse NLP tasks in low-resource settings.  

However, the experimental setup is unconventional. While scenarios requiring as few as 1,000 gold tokens (e.g., certain DARPA projects) exist, this is uncommon. A more rigorous validation would have included a plot of tagging accuracy against gold annotation size, addressing key questions: (1) Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? (2) What is the approximate threshold of gold annotations below which this approach becomes beneficial? (3) How does this threshold vary across target languages?  

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could derive from alternative sources (e.g., crowd-sourcing) or different tag sets. Though this broader applicability is promising, the paper only validates results using cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets.  

It is notable that the training objective assigns equal weights to gold and noisy labels. Given the small gold corpus assumption, it would have been informative to report whether tuning the relative contribution of these terms improves performance.  

Line 357 describes projected data as pairs (x_t, \tilde{y}) without clarifying \tilde{y}'s structure (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for a word type). This raises two questions: (1) Would the method remain effective using token-level projected POS tags (rather than word-type aggregations)? (2) How is \tilde{y} constructed for words with no one-to-one alignments?
