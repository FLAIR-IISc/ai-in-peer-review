

Summary: This paper presents a novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages by learning to correct errors from cross-lingual projection. The primary contribution is a neural network model that explicitly models the noise in projected tags through an additional "noise layer" that transforms between projected and gold tags. The second contribution is the joint learning framework that trains on both gold-standard and projected data. The third contribution is the thorough evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths:
1. The proposed noise layer mechanism is elegant and well-motivated. It directly addresses a fundamental problem in cross-lingual projection—noise in projected tags—by modeling the transformation between projected and gold tags as part of the learning process. This approach is conceptually simple yet effective, as evidenced by the consistent performance gains across multiple languages.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors demonstrate strong results on both simulated low-resource settings (eight European languages) and real-world low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing that their method equals or exceeds state-of-the-art performance with only 1,000 gold-standard tokens. The results are particularly impressive given the limited annotated data.

3. The method is modular and generalizable beyond the specific POS tagging task. The authors explicitly note that their approach could be applied to other noisy supervision scenarios, such as crowd-sourced annotations or other distant supervision methods, making it potentially valuable for a broader range of low-resource NLP problems.

Weaknesses:
1. The experimental setup is somewhat artificial, as the authors focus exclusively on the 1,000-token gold annotation scenario. A more convincing validation would include results across varying amounts of gold data, which would help answer important questions about when this method is most beneficial (e.g., how much gold data is needed before the method becomes unnecessary, and whether it degrades performance when ample gold data is available).

2. The paper does not explore the generalization of the approach beyond cross-lingual projection with identical tag sets. While the authors mention potential applications to other noisy labeling sources (e.g., crowdsourcing), they only evaluate the method using cross-lingual projection with the same tag set. This limits the perceived scope of the contribution.

3. The training objective treats gold and projected data equally, which seems counterintuitive given the small size of the gold dataset. It would be valuable to investigate whether tuning the relative weights of the two loss terms in the objective function could further improve performance, particularly since the authors assume the availability of limited gold annotations.

4. The description of the projected data representation in Section 3.2 is insufficiently detailed. The authors refer to "vector representations" of projected tags without clarifying whether these are per-word distributions or aggregated across word types. It's unclear how the method handles words that are never aligned (since the authors only preserve one-to-one alignments), which could be problematic in practice.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple languages, but the noise transformation matrices (Figure 3) show significant variation between languages. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which language-specific characteristics (e.g., syntactic divergence, alignment quality) influence the noise patterns?

2. The authors mention that their method works with mismatched tag sets, but the paper only evaluates with identical tag sets. Could you share preliminary results with mismatched tag sets to demonstrate the full potential of this capability?

3. How would the performance change if you used a different alignment model (e.g., neural alignment) instead of IBM model 3, which might produce more accurate alignments and potentially less noisy projections?
