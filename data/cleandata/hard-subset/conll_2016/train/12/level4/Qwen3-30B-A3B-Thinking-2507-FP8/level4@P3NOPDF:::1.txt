

Review:  
This paper has been substantially improved since my earlier review of its ACL 2016 short paper draft. The critical flaw in the experimental setup identified at that time has been corrected, and I confirm that this resolves the primary concern I raised. Having previously indicated my willingness to recommend acceptance for an ACL venue upon correction of this issue, I now see no remaining barriers to supporting acceptance.  

A valid concern raised by another reviewer warrants attention: the experimental setup assumes an artificial scenario where annotator costs are negligible for low-resource languages, potentially overstate the method’s practical applicability. The authors should explicitly address this limitation in the revision, clarifying whether the approach remains viable under realistic cost constraints for truly low-resource settings. This transparency is essential to avoid overclaiming the method’s utility.  

The related work section would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages. Specifically, the authors should engage with recent ACL contributions such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), which directly address similar challenges. Acknowledging these works would strengthen the paper’s positioning and ensure scholarly completeness.  

Regarding data availability, I note the paper does not explicitly state whether datasets or code will be made publicly accessible. As a standard requirement for reproducibility in NLP research, this should be clearly indicated in the revised manuscript. I evaluate this point in good faith, presuming the authors will comply with community norms for data sharing upon acceptance.  

In summary, this is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in low-resource NLP. The corrected methodology, combined with the authors’ responsiveness to prior feedback, makes this a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance pending the minor revisions outlined above.
