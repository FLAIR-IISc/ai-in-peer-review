This paper presents a novel modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models designed for low-resource language learning. The authors address the challenge of leveraging both gold annotations and noisy, projected annotations by introducing a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, effectively creating a distribution over noisy labels alongside the traditional distribution over gold labels. This is a strong and well-motivated contribution. The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity and reports promising results.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages. The experiments utilize a small amount of gold data supplemented by a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations, showcasing a practical application in scenarios where labeled data is scarce. The modularity of the method is a significant strength, making it potentially valuable to researchers tackling a range of NLP problems in low-resource settings.

While the experimental setup convincingly demonstrates the method's utility, a more comprehensive empirical analysis would strengthen the findings. The current focus on extremely limited gold data (e.g., 1000 tokens) represents a somewhat niche use case. It would be highly informative to analyze the performance of the proposed method – alongside strong baselines – as the size of the gold annotation set varies. Specifically, plotting tagging accuracy against the number of gold annotations would address key questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? At what quantity of gold data does it become beneficial? And does this threshold vary depending on the target language? This analysis would better illustrate the practical trade-offs of using the proposed technique.

Furthermore, the paper currently restricts its investigation to noisy labels derived from cross-lingual projections with matching tag sets. While effective, this limits the broader applicability of the method. Noisy labels could also originate from alternative sources, such as crowdsourcing, or be presented in different tag sets than the gold annotations. Demonstrating the method’s robustness to these variations would significantly expand its potential impact, though exploring these scenarios is beyond the scope of a single paper.

The training objective’s equal weighting of gold and noisy labels is also noteworthy. Given the assumption of a limited gold corpus, it would be valuable to investigate the impact of tuning the relative contribution of these two terms. A sensitivity analysis exploring different weighting schemes could reveal optimal configurations for various scenarios and languages.

Finally, a clarification is needed regarding the vector representation of projected data described in line 357. The paper presents projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and vector representations (ỹ), but does not detail the composition of ỹ (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags). It would be helpful to explain how ỹ is constructed and whether the approach remains effective if ỹ is derived from token-level projected POS tags instead of aggregated word type predictions. Additionally, the handling of words without one-to-one alignments and the subsequent construction of ỹ for these cases requires further explanation.

There is a minor typographical error on line 267 where one of the closing brackets should be an opening bracket.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution with a clearly articulated approach and promising results. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and broaden its impact within the low-resource NLP community.
