- Proposed method: multiply the softmax output by a learned noise layer to model projection noise and train from both gold and projected annotations.  
- Overall assessment: strong, simple, elegant, modular method with good results on simulated and real low-resource languages.  
- Primary contribution: a novel noise layer that models the transformation between projected tag distributions and true tags.  
- Secondary contribution: thorough experimental validation across multiple simulated and real low-resource languages.  

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results, consistently outperforming existing approaches on tested languages.  
- Principled noise modeling that explicitly corrects projection inaccuracies rather than treating projected data as clean.  
- Comprehensive evaluation against strong baselines and diverse languages, increasing confidence in robustness.  
- Modularity and potential generalizability to other sequence tagging tasks and noise sources.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited exploration of annotation size—evaluation uses a fixed 1000-token gold set without learning curves for larger amounts.  
- Experiments assume tagset consistency between projected and gold annotations, limiting assessment of more complex cross-lingual scenarios.  
- Fixed equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the objective; no investigation of optimal weighting strategies.  
- Ambiguity in projected label representation (tilde y) and unclear handling of words with no direct alignment.  

Questions to authors:
- How does performance change as gold annotation size increases—can you provide learning curves?  
- How would the model be adapted for differing source and target tagsets?  
- Did you try different weightings for gold vs. noisy labels, and what were the results?  
- Clarify the representation of projected labels (is tilde y a distribution?) and treatment of unaligned tokens.
