The paper describes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models that enables learning model parameters from both gold and projected annotations in a low-resource language setting. The traditional softmax output layer, which defines a distribution over possible labels, is further multiplied by a fully connected layer modeling the noise generation process, resulting in a second output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels. 

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant. The paper reports good results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, leveraging a small set of gold annotations alongside a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The modularity of the method suggests that researchers working on diverse NLP problems in low-resource scenarios are likely to find it useful.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup is somewhat unusual. While scenarios exist where building a POS tagger with as few as 1000 token annotations is necessary (e.g., evaluations in certain DARPA-sponsored research projects), such situations are relatively rare. A more comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method would have involved plotting tagging accuracy—for both the proposed method and baselines—as a function of the size of the gold annotations. This plot would address important questions, such as: Does the method degrade performance on a target language when sufficient gold annotations are available? At approximately what amount of gold annotation data does this method become beneficial? And does this threshold depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could potentially be sourced from other means (e.g., crowd sourcing) and derived from tag sets differing from the gold annotations. While the potential impact of such extensions is exciting, the paper currently presents results exclusively with cross-lingual projections using a matching tag set. 

It is somewhat surprising that the proposed training objective assigns equal weights to gold and noisy labels. Given the assumption of a small gold-annotated corpus, reporting whether tuning the contribution of these two terms in the objective function yields improvements would have been informative.

In line 357, the paper describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but it does not explicitly define the structure of \tilde{y} (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the word type). A natural question arises regarding whether the approach remains effective if \tilde{y} is constructed using the projected POS tags at the token level, rather than aggregating predictions for the same word type. Furthermore, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, the method for constructing \tilde{y} for words without any alignments is unclear.

Finally, line 267 contains a typographical error; one of the two closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
