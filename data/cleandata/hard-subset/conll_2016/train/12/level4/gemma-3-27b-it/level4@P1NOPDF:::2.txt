The paper describes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models that enables learning model parameters from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource languages. The traditional softmax output layer, which defines a distribution over possible labels, is multiplied by a fully connected layer modeling the noise generation process, resulting in a second output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant. The paper reports strong results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, leveraging a small set of gold annotations alongside a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The modularity of the method suggests it will be readily adopted by researchers working on various NLP problems in low-resource scenarios.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup is somewhat unusual. While I can envision scenarios where building a POS tagger with as few as 1000 token annotations is necessary – for example, in evaluations within certain DARPA-sponsored research projects – this is relatively uncommon. A more comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method would involve plotting tagging accuracy, for both the proposed method and baselines, as the size of the gold annotations is varied. Such a plot would address important questions: Does employing this method negatively impact performance when ample gold annotations are available? Approximately how many gold annotations are needed for this method to become beneficial? And does this threshold depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could potentially be sourced from other methods (e.g., crowdsourcing) or utilize different tag sets than the gold annotations. While this broader potential impact is exciting, the paper currently only demonstrates results with cross-lingual projections using the same tag set. 

It is somewhat surprising that the proposed training objective assigns equal weights to gold versus noisy labels. Given that the setup assumes a limited gold-annotated corpus, it would have been informative to investigate whether tuning the contribution of each term in the objective function yields improvements.

On line 357, the paper describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations ̃y, but does not explicitly define the structure of these vector representations (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the word type). A natural question arises here: does the approach still function effectively if ̃y is constructed using projected POS tags at the token level, rather than aggregating predictions for the same word type? Furthermore, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it’s unclear how ̃y should be constructed for words that are never aligned.

Finally, on line 267, one of the closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
