- Introduces a noise layer in a BiLSTM to explicitly model and correct cross-lingual projection noise for sequence tagging in low-resource languages.  
- Learns to weight the reliability of projected tags via a learnable transformation between projected and gold tags.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).  
- Provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse languages and data conditions; learned noise matrices offer error insight.  
- Strength: effective, learnable noise modeling that discounts unreliable projections beyond filtering or heuristics.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance and consistent gains across multiple languages, indicating generalizability.  
- Strength: modular, practical design built on standard neural architectures, adaptable to other sequence labeling tasks.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of the learned noise matrices; lacks quantitative measures (e.g., entropy) and systematic confusion analysis.  
- Weakness: training objective uses equal weighting for gold and projected losses without exploring alternative weighting schemes.  
- Weakness: narrow scope focusing on one-to-one alignment projection noise; does not evaluate other noisy label sources or many-to-many alignments.  
- Question: how is the projected vector \tilde{y} constructed for NULL-aligned words — uniform distribution or another approach?  
- Question: do low-weighted/ignored tag combinations correlate with specific linguistic phenomena (syntactic divergence, morphology)?  
- Question: have you tried or considered adapting the noise layer to other noise types (crowd-sourced labels, annotator reliability)?
