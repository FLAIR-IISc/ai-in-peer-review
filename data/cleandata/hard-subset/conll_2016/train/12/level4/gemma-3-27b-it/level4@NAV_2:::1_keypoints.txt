- Contribution: Proposes joint learning on gold and projected data with an explicit noise layer to correct cross-lingual projection errors.
- Contribution: Demonstrates effectiveness across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving SOTA or competitive results.
- Contribution: Analyzes the learned noise transformation matrix to reveal common projection error patterns and how the model compensates.
- Strength: Effective noise modeling — explicit noise layer yields clear improvements in POS tagging over baselines that ignore or only implicitly handle projection noise.
- Strength: Strong empirical results — consistent improvements across diverse simulated and real datasets show robustness and generalizability.
- Strength: Insightful interpretability — visualizations of the noise matrix identify frequently confused tag pairs and model corrections.
- Strength: Practical relevance — realistic low-resource setup (only 1000 gold tokens plus parallel data) addresses real-world annotation constraints.
- Weakness: Evaluation artificiality — simulated low-resource settings may not fully capture practical challenges or true annotation vs. parallel-data costs.
- Weakness: Related work coverage limited — needs more discussion of recent projection work (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agic et al. 2015) to better contextualize contributions.
- Weakness: Dependence on alignment quality — model performance tied to word alignments; more analysis of alignment methods and mitigation strategies is needed.
- Question: Can the noise-layer approach generalize to other sequence tasks (e.g., NER, chunking)?
- Question: How sensitive are results to word-alignment quality and have different alignment tools/techniques been evaluated?
- Question: Will the code and data used in experiments be released to support reproducibility?
- Additional comment: Clarify whether reported results are from single runs or averaged over multiple runs and provide statistical significance testing.
