This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual transfer learning for dependency parsing, and I recommend it for acceptance to CONLL 2016. I previously reviewed an earlier version of this work as a short paper submission to ACL 2016, at which time I identified a flaw in the experimental setup. This flaw has now been addressed, and given that I indicated willingness to accept the paper at another ACL event contingent on this correction, I see no remaining obstacles to acceptance.

One reviewer raised a pertinent point regarding the artificiality of the experimental setup when considered in the context of truly low-resource languages. Specifically, the cost model employed—focusing on the expense of *finding* annotators versus the cost of *paying* them—may not accurately reflect real-world constraints for many low-resource settings. While the presented methodology is sound, the paper would benefit from acknowledging this limitation in the discussion. A more nuanced presentation, avoiding potential overselling of the method’s applicability in all low-resource scenarios, would strengthen the work.

The paper would also be enhanced by a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly research on annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages. Relevant studies such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agic et al. (2015, ACL) offer important context and provide a valuable point of comparison. Incorporating these references into the related work section would improve the completeness of the paper and demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the existing literature.

In summary, this is a well-executed and insightful study. The correction of the previously identified issue, combined with the overall quality of the research, leads me to a positive assessment and a vote to accept. 

Finally, please explicitly state whether the datasets used in this research will be made publicly available. While I evaluate the work assuming this will be the case – a best practice for reproducible research – clarity on this point is essential.
