This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, addressing the challenges of noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The core contribution lies in framing this problem as joint learning over both gold-standard and projected data, incorporating an explicit noise layer to correct projection errors. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of this approach’s effectiveness across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results. Finally, the insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix, revealing patterns of projection errors and successful noise mitigation, adds to the understanding of cross-lingual learning.

Strengths:
1. **Effective Noise Modeling:** The introduction of a noise layer to explicitly model and correct errors from cross-lingual projection is a strong point. The experimental results clearly demonstrate its effectiveness in improving POS tagging accuracy compared to baselines that either ignore or implicitly address projection noise. This is particularly evident in the performance gains on both the simulated and real low-resource languages.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The system consistently equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art on eight simulated low-resource settings and achieves competitive results on the real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. The consistent improvements across diverse datasets provide strong evidence for the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Noise Transformation:** The visualization and discussion of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of errors commonly made during cross-lingual projection. Identifying which tag pairs are frequently confused, and how the model learns to compensate, strengthens the understanding and interpretability of the proposed method.
4. **Practical Relevance to Low-Resource NLP:** The method addresses a critical problem in low-resource NLP – leveraging potentially noisy, yet readily available, data from cross-lingual projection. The experimental setup, utilizing only 1000 tokens of gold-standard data alongside parallel data, mirrors realistic scenarios where obtaining large amounts of annotated data is prohibitively expensive.

Weaknesses:
1. **Artificiality of the Evaluation Setting:** While the simulated low-resource setting provides a controlled environment for comparison, it might not fully reflect the practical challenges of working with truly low-resource languages, particularly regarding the relative costs of annotation versus obtaining parallel data. Acknowledging this limitation would strengthen the discussion.
2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of recent advances in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages, such as the work by Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), which could contextualize the contribution more effectively.
3. **Dependency on Word Alignment Quality:** The performance of the system is inherently tied to the quality of the word alignments used for projection. While the paper mentions alignment errors as a source of noise, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of different alignment techniques or error mitigation strategies would be beneficial.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on the potential applicability of the proposed noise layer approach to other sequence tagging tasks beyond POS tagging, such as Named Entity Recognition or Chunking?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or techniques to assess their impact on the results?
3. Please clarify the availability of the data and code used in the experiments. Will they be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

Additional Comments:
Please clarify if the reported results are based on a single run or averaged over multiple runs with different random initializations. Providing statistical significance tests would further strengthen the empirical analysis.
