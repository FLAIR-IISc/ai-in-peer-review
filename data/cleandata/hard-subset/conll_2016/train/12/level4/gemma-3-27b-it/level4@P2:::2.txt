This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, specifically addressing the challenges of noisy data arising from cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to incorporate an explicit “noise layer” into a neural network model, allowing it to learn to correct errors inherent in projected tags by jointly training on both gold-standard and projected data. The proposed model uses a bidirectional LSTM to generate both gold tags and uncertain projected tags, with the noise layer modeling the transformation between the two. The authors demonstrate strong performance on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective method for leveraging noisy, distantly supervised data – specifically, cross-lingual projections – in low-resource NLP. The explicit modeling of projection noise via the introduced noise layer is a well-motivated and clearly presented technique. A second key contribution lies in the demonstration of the method's efficacy across a diverse range of languages and settings, including both simulated and genuinely low-resource scenarios. Finally, the approach offers a flexible framework that could be readily adapted to incorporate other sources of noisy labels beyond cross-lingual projection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method consistently achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on both simulated and real low-resource language POS tagging tasks. The results in Tables 1 and 2 clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the noise layer in mitigating the issues associated with projected data. This is a significant achievement, particularly given the inherent difficulties of low-resource scenarios.
2. **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The noise layer is a conceptually simple yet powerful addition to the standard neural network architecture. Framing the problem as learning a transformation between noisy and clean labels is a well-motivated strategy, and the mathematical formulation is clear and concise. This makes the method relatively easy to understand and potentially adapt to other tasks.
3. **Practical Relevance:** The scenario addressed – leveraging limited gold annotations alongside abundant, but noisy, projected data – is highly relevant to many real-world NLP applications, particularly for under-resourced languages. The modularity of the method suggests it could be readily applied to other sequence tagging tasks and other noise sources.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation across eight simulated languages and two real languages provides a comprehensive assessment of the method’s generalizability. The comparison against strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art approaches, strengthens the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Layer:** While the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the noise layer, the analysis of the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4) is somewhat limited. A more detailed investigation into the patterns captured by the noise layer – for example, identifying specific types of projection errors that the model learns to correct – would provide valuable insights into its inner workings and potential limitations.
2. **Fixed Weighting of Loss Terms:** The training objective assigns equal weights to the gold and noisy data loss terms. The impact of varying these weights is not explored. It seems plausible that adjusting the weighting could further improve performance, especially when the amount of gold data is very small. Exploring this parameter sensitivity would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
3. **Projection Specificity:** The current experiments focus exclusively on cross-lingual projection with aligned tagsets. While this is a common and important scenario, the potential of the method for handling other types of noisy data (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations with differing tag sets) is not fully investigated. Expanding the evaluation to include these scenarios would broaden the applicability of the work.
4. **Evaluation Setting:** The evaluation primarily focuses on scenarios with a very limited amount of gold data (1000 tokens). While this setting is relevant, it would be valuable to assess the method’s performance as the amount of gold data increases, to understand its behavior in more typical scenarios where more resources are available. A learning curve plotting accuracy against gold data size would be informative.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (A), outlining specific error patterns that the model learns to correct?
2. Have you experimented with different weighting schemes for the gold and noisy data loss terms in the training objective? If so, what were the results?
3. How does the performance of the model change as the amount of gold annotated data increases? Would you be willing to provide a learning curve showing accuracy versus gold data size?
4.  In line 357, can you clarify what the vector representation \tilde{y} looks like? Is it a distribution over projected tags, and how is it constructed for words with no one-to-one alignment?
