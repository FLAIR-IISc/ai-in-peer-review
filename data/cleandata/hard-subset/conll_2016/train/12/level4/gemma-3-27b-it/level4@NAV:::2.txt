This paper introduces a novel modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, designed to facilitate learning in low-resource languages. The approach augments the standard softmax layer with a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, effectively learning a distribution over noisy labels alongside the distribution over gold labels.

Overall, this is a strong and well-conceived contribution. The proposed method is both conceptually simple and elegant in its implementation. The results presented demonstrate strong performance on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages, utilizing a combination of limited gold annotations and a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The modularity of the approach suggests broad applicability to other NLP tasks in low-resource settings.

However, the experimental evaluation focuses on a somewhat niche scenario. While building a POS tagger with extremely limited annotations (e.g., 1000 tokens) may be relevant in specific contexts, it is not a common requirement. A more comprehensive empirical analysis would involve plotting tagging accuracy as a function of gold annotation size for both the proposed method and baseline approaches. Such a plot would address key questions: What is the impact of this method when ample gold annotations are available? At what level of gold annotation scarcity does this method become advantageous? And does this benefit vary across different target languages? 

Furthermore, while the potential for leveraging noisy labels from sources beyond cross-lingual projection (e.g., crowdsourcing) and with differing tag sets is promising, the current work is limited to results using cross-lingual projections with identical tag sets. Additionally, the equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the training objective is somewhat surprising, given the expected scarcity of gold data. Reporting the results of tuning the relative contributions of these two terms would provide valuable insight.

Finally, the description of the projected data on line 357, representing word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, lacks clarity regarding the structure of \tilde{y} (e.g., whether it’s a distribution over projected POS tags). It would be useful to investigate the performance with \tilde{y} constructed from token-level projected POS tags rather than aggregated word type predictions, and to clarify how \tilde{y} is constructed for words lacking one-to-one alignments.

A minor typographical correction is needed on line 267, where one of the closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
