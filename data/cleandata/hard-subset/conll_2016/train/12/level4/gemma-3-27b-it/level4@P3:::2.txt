This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, leveraging both gold standard and cross-lingually projected data. The core contribution lies in explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection via a dedicated “noise layer” within a bidirectional LSTM network. This allows the model to learn to correct projection errors during training, effectively weighting the reliability of projected tags. The authors demonstrate strong performance on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach across diverse languages and data conditions. Finally, the learned noise transformation matrices offer valuable insights into the types of errors common in cross-lingual projection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise is a significant strength. By introducing a learnable transformation between projected and gold tags, the model effectively learns to discount unreliable projections, leading to improved tagging accuracy. This is a well-motivated and thoughtfully implemented approach, moving beyond simply filtering or heuristically correcting projected data. The results clearly demonstrate its effectiveness.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages is a strong indicator of the method's efficacy and generalizability. The consistent performance gains across multiple languages suggest the approach is not overly sensitive to specific linguistic characteristics.
3. **Practical Relevance & Modularity:** The proposed method addresses a common challenge in low-resource NLP – the scarcity of labeled data. The modular design, built around a standard neural network architecture, makes it readily adaptable to other sequence labeling tasks and potentially other noise sources beyond cross-lingual projection. This enhances its practical value for researchers working in resource-constrained settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Noise Layer:** While the paper presents the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4), a more in-depth analysis of these matrices would be beneficial.  For instance, quantifying the degree of noise (e.g., entropy of the row distributions) or identifying systematic patterns of confusion between specific tag pairs could provide further insights into the challenges of cross-lingual projection and the model’s learned corrections.
2. **Fixed Weighting of Loss Terms:** The training objective assigns equal weight to the gold and projected data losses. While understandable as a starting point, exploring different weighting schemes (e.g., using a hyperparameter to control the relative contribution of each term) could potentially lead to further performance improvements, particularly when the amount of gold data is very limited. Reporting results with varying weights would strengthen the analysis.
3. **Narrow Scope of Projection Noise:** The paper focuses solely on noise arising from cross-lingual projection using one-to-one alignments. Expanding the investigation to consider other forms of noisy data (e.g., crowd-sourced annotations, automatically generated labels) or alternative alignment strategies (many-to-many) would broaden the applicability and impact of the proposed method. The limitation is acknowledged by the authors, but further exploration would be valuable. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  In line 357, you mention the vector representation  \tilde{y} for projected data. Could you clarify how this representation is constructed when a word is not aligned to any English word (NULL alignment)? Is a uniform distribution used, or is another approach employed?
2.  The noise transformation matrix *A* seems to learn to "ignore" certain tag combinations (low values).  Do you observe any correlation between these ignored combinations and specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., syntactic divergence, differing morphological structures)?
3.  You mention the potential for applying this technique to other noise sources. Have you experimented with or considered adapting the noise layer to handle other types of noisy labels (e.g., crowd-sourced data with varying levels of annotator reliability)?
