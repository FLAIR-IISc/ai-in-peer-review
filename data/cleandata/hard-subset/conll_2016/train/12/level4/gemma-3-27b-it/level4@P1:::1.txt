This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, addressing the challenges of unreliable annotations resulting from cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer, effectively framing this as joint learning over both gold-standard and projected data. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: first, the explicit modeling of noise inherent in projected data via a dedicated noise layer within a deep learning framework; second, the effective integration of this noise model into a BiLSTM architecture for joint training on both annotated and projected data; and third, the strong empirical results demonstrating performance equal to or exceeding state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a strong contribution. Previous approaches largely treated projected data as fixed, potentially unreliable labels. This work’s noise layer allows the model to adaptively adjust to the characteristics of the projected data, effectively learning *when* and *how much* to trust it. This is demonstrated clearly with the visualization of the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4), revealing how the model mitigates noise and leverages the projected information.

2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on eight simulated low-resource settings *and* two real languages is a significant accomplishment. The consistent improvement over baseline methods (including the strong baseline from Duong et al., 2014a) and the reported accuracies (86.3% for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda) provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. 

3. **Well-Designed Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully chose a realistic evaluation setting, utilizing a limited amount of gold-standard data alongside more abundant parallel data – a common scenario in low-resource NLP. The use of the CoNLL data for simulation and the datasets from Garrette and Baldridge (2013) for the real languages provides a fair comparison with previous work. The ablation studies (comparing BiLSTM models with and without the noise layer) clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed approach.

4. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the model, the training process, and the experimental setup. The diagrams (Figure 2) are particularly helpful in illustrating the architecture of the proposed model.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificiality of the Setup:** While the evaluation setting is well-defined, the assumption of readily available parallel data for all low-resource languages might be overly optimistic. The paper acknowledges this briefly but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges associated with obtaining and processing parallel data for truly resource-scarce languages. The cost comparison of acquiring parallel data vs. paying for annotation could be expanded upon.

2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work on cross-lingual projection and low-resource NLP, it overlooks some recent approaches to annotation projection for *extremely* low-resource languages, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015). Incorporating a discussion of these methods would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art and better position the contribution of this work. 

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings, such as the learning rate or the dimensionality of the hidden layers. While the authors provide some details about the setup, a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters would strengthen the robustness of the findings.

4. **Data Availability:** The paper does not explicitly state whether the code and data used in the experiments will be made publicly available. Ensuring reproducibility and facilitating further research requires open access to these resources.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the challenges of obtaining parallel data for truly low-resource languages and discuss the potential trade-offs between acquiring parallel data and paying for annotation?
2. How does the performance of your model vary with different levels of noise in the projected data (e.g., with different word alignment qualities)?
3. Would you be willing to release the code and data used in your experiments to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrasing "Although cross-lingual POS projection is popular it has several problems" could be slightly more formal (e.g., "While cross-lingual POS projection is a popular technique, it suffers from several challenges...").
*   Consider clarifying the meaning of "mimicking" when discussing the transformation matrix (A). "Modeling" or "representing" might be more precise.
*   The sentence "Provided there are consistent patterns of noise..." is somewhat informal. Consider revising it to "If consistent patterns of noise exist...".
