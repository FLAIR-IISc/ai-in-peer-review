The paper describes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models which enables learning the model parameters from both gold and projected annotations in a low-resource language. The traditional softmax output layer which defines a distribution over possible labels is further multiplied by a fully connected layer which models the noise generation process, resulting in another output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels. 

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is apt, simple and elegant. The paper reports good results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, making use of a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The method is modular enough that researchers working on different NLP problems in low-resource scenarios are likely to use it.

The primary contribution of this work is a novel approach to leveraging noisy cross-lingual projected data in low-resource sequence tagging. Specifically, the authors introduce a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between the projected tag distribution and the true underlying distribution. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of this technique’s effectiveness across a comprehensive set of experiments, including both simulated and real low-resource languages. Finally, the thorough experimental setup and comparison to state-of-the-art methods provides a solid validation of the proposed approach.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms existing approaches on the tested languages, demonstrating a practical benefit for low-resource POS tagging. The results on both simulated and real languages strengthen the validity of the findings.
2. **Principled Approach to Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise via the introduced layer is a well-motivated and elegant solution. It moves beyond simply using projected data as additional training examples and actively attempts to correct for its inherent inaccuracies.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation, comparing against several strong baselines and exploring the impact of the method across a diverse set of languages. This comprehensive evaluation increases confidence in the robustness of the proposed technique.
4. **Modularity and Potential Generalizability:** The approach is modular and can be potentially applied to other sequence tagging tasks and noise sources beyond cross-lingual projection.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Exploration of Annotation Size:** The evaluation focuses on a fixed small annotation size (1000 tokens). A more in-depth analysis of performance as a function of annotation size (e.g., a learning curve) would provide a clearer understanding of the method’s benefits and limitations.  Specifically, how does performance change when larger amounts of gold data are available?
2. **Focus on Tagset Consistency:** The experiments are limited to scenarios where the projected and gold annotations use the same tagset. While this simplifies the evaluation, it limits the assessment of the method’s ability to handle more complex cross-lingual scenarios with differing tagset structures.
3. **Fixed Objective Function Weighting:** The training objective uses equal weighting for gold and noisy labels. An investigation into optimal weighting strategies could potentially further improve performance. Tuning this hyperparameter might lead to more robust results.
4. **Ambiguity in Projected Label Representation:** The description of how the projected labels are represented (specifically the nature of  \tilde{y} in line 357) could be clarified. Details regarding how projected labels are handled for words with no direct alignment are needed.

Questions to Authors:

1. How does the performance of the proposed method vary as the size of the gold annotated dataset increases? Could you provide a learning curve showing tagging accuracy as a function of annotation size?
2. How would the model need to be adapted to handle cross-lingual projections where the source and target languages have different tagsets?
3. Did you experiment with different weighting strategies for the gold and noisy label terms in the training objective? If so, what were the results?
4. Could you clarify the representation of the projected labels ( \tilde{y} ) – is it a distribution over tags, and how are labels assigned to tokens with no direct word alignment?
