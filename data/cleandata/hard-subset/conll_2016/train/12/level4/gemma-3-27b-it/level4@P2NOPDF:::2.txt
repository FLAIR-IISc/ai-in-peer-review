This paper introduces a novel modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models designed to effectively leverage both gold annotations and cross-lingually projected annotations when training models for low-resource languages. The authors address the challenge of limited labeled data by augmenting the standard softmax output layer with a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, effectively creating a distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong and well-motivated contribution. The proposed method is conceptually simple, elegant, and highly relevant to the challenges of low-resource NLP. The paper demonstrates promising results on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, successfully utilizing a small gold annotation set combined with a larger set of cross-lingual projections. A significant strength is the modularity of the approach, suggesting broad applicability to various NLP tasks in low-resource settings.

The experimental setup, while clearly defined, is somewhat specialized. While scenarios exist where building a POS tagger with minimal gold data (e.g., 1000 tokens) is necessary, they are not commonplace. To further validate the method's effectiveness, I recommend investigating the impact of varying the size of the gold annotation set. A learning curve plotting tagging accuracy against the number of gold annotations – for both the proposed method and relevant baselines – would provide valuable insights. Specifically, such a plot could address key questions: Does the method negatively impact performance when ample gold data is available? At what level of gold annotation scarcity does this approach become beneficial? And does this threshold vary across different target languages?

The paper currently focuses exclusively on cross-lingual projections as a source of noisy labels. However, the method’s potential extends to other sources of noisy data, such as crowd-sourcing, and potentially different tag sets than those used for gold annotations. While acknowledging this broader impact is exciting, the current experiments limit themselves to cross-lingual projections with matching tag sets. 

The decision to assign equal weights to gold and noisy labels in the training objective is somewhat surprising, given the inherent scarcity of the gold data. Exploring the impact of tuning the relative contribution of these two terms would be a valuable extension. Reporting results with varying weights could reveal whether prioritizing the limited gold data yields improved performance.

Regarding the technical details, line 357 describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and vector representations (ỹ), but lacks clarity on the structure of ỹ. Specifically, it is unclear whether ỹ represents a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the specific word type. Investigating the performance of the approach with ỹ constructed from token-level projected POS tags (as opposed to aggregating predictions for the same word type) would be insightful. Furthermore, the paper notes the use of only one-to-one word alignments. It would be helpful to discuss how ỹ is constructed for words that lack alignment. 

Finally, a minor typographical correction is needed on line 267, where one of the closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
