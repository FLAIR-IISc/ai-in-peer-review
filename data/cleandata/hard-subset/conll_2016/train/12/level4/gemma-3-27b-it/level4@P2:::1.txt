This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection with a novel approach to handling the inherent noise in projected data. The core contribution lies in modeling the projection noise explicitly through a “noise layer” within a bidirectional LSTM network, jointly trained on both gold-standard and projected data. This allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected annotations, effectively mitigating the inaccuracies stemming from word alignment errors and cross-lingual syntactic divergence. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of strong performance – equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art results – on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). Finally, the work provides a valuable empirical evaluation showing the benefit of explicitly modelling projection noise, supported by analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The most significant strength is the introduction of the noise layer.  Previous methods typically pre-process or constrain projected data; this work, in contrast, directly *models* the noise within the learning framework. This is a conceptually elegant and potentially powerful approach, as it allows the model to adapt to and compensate for the specific characteristics of the projection noise. The analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 4 in the paper) provides compelling evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful relationships between projected and gold-standard tags.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents consistently strong results across a diverse set of experiments, including both simulated and real low-resource languages. The fact that the method equals or exceeds state-of-the-art performance on all tested settings is a significant achievement. The performance gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly encouraging, demonstrating the practical applicability of the approach.
3. **Well-Designed Experiments & Evaluation:** The experimental setup, particularly the use of simulated low-resource settings via data subsetting, allows for a fair comparison with previous work. The use of the universal tagset facilitates cross-linguistic comparison.  The inclusion of both simulated and real low-resource languages strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificiality of the Setup:** While the experimental setup is sound, the paper acknowledges, and should more explicitly address, the potential disconnect between the simulated low-resource scenario and the realities of working with truly low-resource languages. The cost of acquiring parallel data (even if unannotated) is often significantly lower than the cost of human annotation, which influences the attractiveness of the proposed method in practice. Clarifying this trade-off would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.
2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive. The authors should acknowledge relevant work on annotation projection specifically tailored for *extremely* low-resource scenarios, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015).  Including these references would demonstrate a broader awareness of the existing landscape of research.
3. **Data Availability:** The paper does not explicitly state whether the data and code used in the experiments will be made publicly available.  Making these resources accessible would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the proposed model compared to simpler baselines (e.g., the maximum entropy classifier)?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection?  Have you investigated the impact of using different alignment tools or parameters?
3.  The noise transformation matrix A provides insights into the projection errors. Could you discuss any surprising or unexpected patterns observed in these matrices for different languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please clearly indicate the availability of the data and code.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on the availability of parallel data.
