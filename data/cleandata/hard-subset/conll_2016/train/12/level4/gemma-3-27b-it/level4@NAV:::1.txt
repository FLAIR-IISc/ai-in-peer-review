I previously reviewed an earlier version of this work as a short paper submission to ACL 2016. A noted experimental issue has now been addressed. Having indicated at that time that I would consider acceptance at another ACL venue contingent on this correction, I now see no reason to reconsider that assessment. 

One reviewer raised a valid point regarding the potential artificiality of the experimental setup when considered in the context of truly low-resource languages, specifically concerning the relative costs of annotation acquisition versus annotator compensation. The manuscript would benefit from acknowledging this nuance to avoid overstating the method’s applicability.

For a more complete discussion of related work, the authors should consider citing recent work on annotation projection for extremely low-resource scenarios, such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agic et al. (2015, ACL).

Overall, I believe this is a valuable contribution and recommend acceptance. Please clarify whether the data used in this work will be made publicly available; my evaluation is based on the assumption of public availability as is standard practice for research in this field.
