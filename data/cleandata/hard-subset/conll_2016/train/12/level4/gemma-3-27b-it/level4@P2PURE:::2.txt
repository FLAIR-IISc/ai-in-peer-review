The paper presents a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models designed to facilitate learning with both gold annotations and cross-lingually projected annotations in low-resource languages. The standard softmax output layer, which defines a probability distribution over possible labels, is extended by multiplication with a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, yielding a second output layer representing a distribution over noisy labels. 

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The proposed method is well-suited to the task, and its simplicity and elegance are noteworthy. The paper demonstrates promising results on part-of-speech (POS) tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, leveraging a small set of gold annotations in conjunction with a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The modularity of the method suggests it will be readily applicable to a range of NLP problems in low-resource settings.

The experimental setup is somewhat unusual. While scenarios exist – such as certain DARPA-funded research – where building a POS tagger with only 1000 token annotations is necessary, this is not a typical use case. A more comprehensive empirical validation would involve plotting tagging accuracy for the proposed method and baselines as a function of the size of the gold annotation set. Such a plot would illuminate critical questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold annotations are available? Approximately how many gold annotations are required for the method to become beneficial? And does this threshold vary by target language? 

While the paper focuses on cross-lingual projections, the potential to incorporate noisy labels from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and different tag sets is appealing. Although this broader impact is exciting, the current results are limited to cross-lingual projections with matching tag sets. 

The decision to assign equal weights to gold and noisy labels in the training objective is somewhat surprising, given the assumption of a limited gold corpus size. It would have been insightful to report whether tuning the relative contribution of these two terms improves performance.

On line 357, the paper describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but does not detail the structure of \tilde{y} (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the word type). A natural question arises regarding the method’s performance if \tilde{y} is constructed using token-level projected POS tags instead of aggregating predictions for the same word type. Furthermore, given that only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, the method of constructing \tilde{y} for unaligned words remains unclear.

Finally, on line 267, one of the closing brackets should be replaced with an opening bracket.
