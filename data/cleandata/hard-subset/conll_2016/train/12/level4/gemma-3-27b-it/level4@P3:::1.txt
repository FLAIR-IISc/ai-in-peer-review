## Review: Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection

This paper addresses the challenging problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, leveraging cross-lingual projection and a novel neural network architecture to mitigate the noise inherent in such approaches. The core idea – learning to explicitly model and correct errors arising from cross-lingual projection via a “noise layer” – is a significant contribution. While cross-lingual projection is a well-established technique, the explicit modelling of projection noise within a deep learning framework is a valuable addition to the field. A second, noteworthy contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real-world languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods. Finally, the provision of strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the chosen benchmarks, provides compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Noise Layer:** The introduction of the noise layer to explicitly model and learn the transformation between projected and ground truth tags is a strong point. This departs from previous work that often relies on heuristics or constraints, and allows the model to dynamically adjust its trust in projected data based on the specific context and language. The visualizations of the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) offer valuable insights into how the model learns to compensate for projection errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The consistent improvement over existing methods across a diverse set of languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported accuracies for Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%) are particularly impressive, exceeding state-of-the-art results.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, utilizing both simulated low-resource settings and two genuine low-resource languages. This demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed approach and its practical applicability. The ablation studies (comparing BiLSTM variants) effectively highlight the contribution of the noise layer.
4. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of directly applying cross-lingual projection due to inherent noise and proposes a principled solution that addresses these challenges. The motivation for learning a noise transformation is well-justified and intuitively appealing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Annotation Costs:** The paper frames the scenario as leveraging a small amount of annotated data alongside abundant parallel data. However, it doesn’t sufficiently acknowledge the cost (in terms of time or monetary resources) of acquiring *either* type of data, particularly in a real-world low-resource setting. While the authors mention the low cost of obtaining 1000 annotated tokens, they don’t address the effort required to acquire and clean the parallel data, which might be a limiting factor for some languages. This creates a slightly artificial setup that could be misleading.
2. **Related Work Could Be Expanded:** While the paper cites relevant work on cross-lingual projection, it could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related approaches specifically designed for *extremely* low-resource scenarios and annotation projection. Mentioning works like Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agic et al. (2015, ACL) would strengthen the positioning of the work within the broader research landscape.
3. **Complexity of the Model:** While the noise layer is a valuable innovation, the overall model (BiLSTM with additional layers) introduces a degree of complexity. The paper doesn’t provide a thorough analysis of the impact of each component or a clear justification for the specific architectural choices (e.g., the dimensionality of the hidden layers).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the effort required to acquire and clean the parallel data used in your experiments, particularly for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda? This would help contextualize the practicality of the approach in real-world low-resource scenarios.
2.  How does the performance of the proposed method vary with the amount of annotated data?  Is there a point at which adding more annotated data yields diminishing returns, and if so, how does this relate to the size of the parallel corpus?
3.  The learned transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provide valuable insights. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of these matrices, perhaps identifying specific patterns or tag confusions that are particularly prevalent in certain languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please explicitly state whether the datasets and code used in this work will be made publicly available. This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   Consider briefly discussing the potential limitations of the approach, such as its dependence on high-quality word alignments and its sensitivity to tagset discrepancies between the source and target languages.
