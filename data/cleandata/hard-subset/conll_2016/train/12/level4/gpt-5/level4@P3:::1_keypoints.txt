- Proposes a learnable noise layer that models confusion between true tags and noisy projected tags for POS tagging in low-resource settings.
- Presents a joint training framework that pretrains on gold labels then fine-tunes jointly with projected annotations.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results across eight simulated languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

- Explicit noise modeling via matrix A yields interpretable mapping from clean to projected tags and captures systematic projection errors.
- Consistently outperforms baselines and prior work on simulated and real low-resource datasets, with notable gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.
- Practical training strategy (pretrain then joint train) helps avoid poor local optima and integrates cleanly with BiLSTM taggers.
- Informative analyses of learned confusion matrices clarify where projection is reliable versus noisy.
- Addresses a timely and relevant problem: making cross-lingual projection more useful for truly low-resource languages.

- Realism concern: availability and size of parallel corpora (e.g., Malagasy ~100k) may overstate applicability to very low-parallel scenarios.
- Missing comparisons to projection work for extremely low-resource settings (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agić et al. 2015).
- Evaluation lacks variance reporting, sensitivity analyses (varying gold tokens, parallel data), and ablation studies on the noise layer.
- Method depends on one-to-one alignments and English tagger quality; sensitivity to alignment strategy and source tagger not quantified.
- Reproducibility unclear: paper should state whether code, models, and processed projection resources will be released.

- Will you release code, trained models, and processed projection annotations (including Malagasy and Kinyarwanda resources)?
- How does performance vary with number of gold tokens (e.g., 100, 500, 1k) and with size/quality of parallel corpora?
- Ablation: what are results when A is diagonal, fixed to identity, or regularized toward identity, and when parameters are/aren’t shared across branches?
- Did you try including many-to-one or one-to-many alignments with marginalization or confidence weighting, and how does that affect noise vs. coverage?
- How is A initialized and regularized, and what are observations on convergence/stability across languages?
- Do you expect the same noise-modeling approach to transfer to other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., NER) with different projection noise?

- Please add discussion of Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015) to better situate the work in extreme low-resource projection literature.
- Clarify the universal tag mapping procedure and handling of punctuation/X/PRT across languages.

- Recommendation: Accept — simple, well-motivated, and effective approach that improves projected supervision for low-resource POS tagging, pending stronger contextualization and reproducibility details.
