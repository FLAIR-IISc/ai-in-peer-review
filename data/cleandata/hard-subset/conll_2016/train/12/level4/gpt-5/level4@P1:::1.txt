Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a sequence tagging model for low-resource POS tagging that explicitly models noise in cross-lingual projection via a learned noise layer on top of a BiLSTM. The model is trained jointly on a small gold-annotated corpus and a larger set of projected tags.
- The approach targets the common problem that projected labels are noisy due to alignment errors and cross-lingual divergences, learning when and how much to trust the projected data.
- The authors report state-of-the-art or better results in eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Strengths
- Prior experimental flaw corrected: I reviewed an earlier ACL 2016 short paper draft that had a flaw in the experimental setup. That issue has now been addressed. I previously indicated I would be willing to accept the work if this flaw were corrected; with that correction in place, I see no remaining obstacles on this front.
- Clear and focused contribution: The central idea—explicitly modeling projection noise with a dedicated layer and training jointly on gold and projected data—is well-motivated and addresses a real limitation of prior projection-based methods.
- Positive empirical results: The paper reports that the approach equals or exceeds state-of-the-art performance across multiple simulated and real low-resource scenarios, which supports the practical value of the method.

Weaknesses
- Artificiality of the setup for truly low-resource contexts: As noted by another reviewer and with which I agree, the setup can be somewhat artificial if we consider the real costs and logistics in low-resource scenarios, particularly the trade-off between locating annotators and compensating them. This should be made explicit to avoid overselling the method’s applicability in the most resource-constrained settings.
- Related work coverage: There are relevant lines of work on annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages that should be discussed for completeness, such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agić et al. (2015, ACL). A brief reflection on how the proposed approach compares or complements these efforts would strengthen the paper.
- Data availability unclear: It is not indicated whether the data (and any code/models) will be made available. I am evaluating these aspects in good faith, presuming public availability, but this should be stated clearly.

Questions to the authors
- Will the datasets, alignments, and code/models be released to facilitate reproducibility?
- Can you expand the discussion of the practical constraints in truly low-resource settings (e.g., costs of finding vs. paying annotators), and clarify the scope of scenarios where your method is most applicable?
- Will you incorporate discussion of related work in extremely low-resource projection (e.g., Johannsen et al., 2016; Agić et al., 2015) to situate your contribution more fully?

Recommendation
- I consider this a solid contribution. With the previously noted experimental flaw corrected and the above clarifications added to avoid overselling and to improve related work coverage, I recommend acceptance.
