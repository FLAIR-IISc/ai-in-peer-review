Summary
This paper proposes a sequence tagging model that learns from both clean (gold) labels and noisy cross-lingually projected labels. The core idea is to augment a standard BiLSTM tagger with an explicit “noise layer” that maps the clean tag distribution produced by the model to a distribution over noisy projected tags. The model is trained jointly on a small gold-annotated corpus and a much larger set of projected annotations. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) show that the approach matches or surpasses prior work.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An explicit, learnable noise-transformation layer on top of a BiLSTM tagger that models the mapping from clean tag distributions to noisy projected labels, enabling effective use of distant supervision.
- Secondary: A joint training scheme that combines scarce gold annotations with plentiful projected labels in a simple, modular framework that can be trained end-to-end.
- Tertiary: Strong empirical results across 10 language settings, suggesting the approach is robust and practical for low-resource POS tagging.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The method cleanly separates the underlying tagger from the noise process via a small, interpretable transformation matrix. This framing is apt for cross-lingual projection, where systematic noise is expected.
- Strong empirical performance: The model consistently meets or exceeds prior state-of-the-art baselines in both simulated and true low-resource scenarios, demonstrating clear utility over training on gold-only, projected-only, or naïvely combined data.
- Practical applicability and modularity: The approach is easy to integrate into standard sequence taggers (here, a BiLSTM) and is likely to be adopted for other low-resource tagging tasks that have noisy supervision (e.g., other sequence labels, other sources of distant supervision).
- Sensible training strategy: Pretraining on gold labels followed by joint training mitigates harmful effects from noisy projections and is a pragmatic recipe likely to generalize.

Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of data regimes: The evaluation focuses on the extreme low-resource case (~1000 gold tokens). In practice, this regime is uncommon. Learning curves that vary the amount of gold data would clarify when the method is beneficial, whether it ever hurts when ample gold is available, and how benefits vary by language.
- Equal weighting of gold and noisy terms: The objective gives the gold-supervised and projection-supervised losses equal weight. It is unclear whether tuning this trade-off improves performance; this seems especially relevant with very small gold sets.
- Scope of noisy supervision: While the technique is presented as generally applicable to noisy labels, experiments only consider cross-lingual projection with a matched tagset. There is no empirical evidence for handling other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or for mismatched tagsets, despite the claim that the layer can be rectangular.
- Clarity on projected-label representation: The paper (around line 357) refers to projected data as pairs of tokens x_t and vectors ỹ but does not concretely specify how ỹ is constructed. The current description suggests type-level aggregation or sentence-level distributions; token-level construction (using the specific alignment for each token) is not evaluated. It is also unclear how ỹ is defined for unaligned target tokens when only one-to-one alignments are kept.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Data regime: How does performance vary as the amount of gold supervision increases (e.g., 1k, 5k, 10k, full)? Does the noise layer continue to help, and does it ever hurt relative to gold-only training?
- Loss weighting: Did you try weighting the gold- and projection-based losses differently? If so, what weights worked best, and how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?
- Projected-label construction: Precisely how is ỹ defined?
  - Is it token-level (based on the aligned source token) or a distribution aggregated over the sentence or type?
  - How do you construct ỹ for unaligned target tokens? Do you use sentence-level tag distributions, a default prior, or drop them?
- Generality to tagset mismatch and other noisy sources: Have you tried a rectangular noise matrix to map between different tag inventories, or noisy labels from non-projection sources (e.g., crowdsourced annotations)? Any pitfalls in practice?
- Training details: How many epochs are used for gold pretraining versus joint training? Did you find that initializing the noise matrix in a particular way (e.g., near-identity) matters?

Additional comments
