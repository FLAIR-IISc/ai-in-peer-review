The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural networks that enables learning from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource languages. The standard softmax layer, which produces a distribution over labels, is post-multiplied by a fully connected layer that models the noise-generation process, yielding a second output distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The method is appropriate, simple, and elegant. The paper reports solid results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, training with a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The approach is modular enough that researchers working on other low-resource NLP tasks are likely to adopt it.

From a practical perspective, the experimental setup is unusual. While I can imagine scenarios where one needs to build a POS tagger with as few as 1,000 token annotations (e.g., evaluations in some DARPA-sponsored projects), such cases are relatively rare. A stronger empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy for the proposed method (and baselines) as the amount of gold annotation varies. Such a plot would help answer:
- Does using this method hurt performance in a target language when plenty of gold annotations are available?
- Approximately how much gold annotation is required before the method is no longer beneficial?
- Does this threshold depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could also come from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and might use tag sets different from the gold annotations. Although this broader potential is compelling, the paper only presents results with cross-lingual projections that use the same tag set.

It is surprising that the training objective weights gold and noisy labels equally. Given that a small gold-annotated corpus is available, it would be informative to report whether tuning the relative contributions of these two terms in the objective function improves performance.

In line 357, the paper describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but does not specify what that vector looks like (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for a given word type). A natural question is whether the approach still works if \tilde{y} is constructed from token-level projected POS tags, rather than aggregating predictions across all tokens of the same word type. Also, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how to construct \tilde{y} for words that are never aligned.
