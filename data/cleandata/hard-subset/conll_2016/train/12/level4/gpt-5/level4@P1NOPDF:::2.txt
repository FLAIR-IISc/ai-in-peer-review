The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models that enables learning from both gold and projected annotations in a low-resource language. The standard softmax output layer, which defines a distribution over clean labels, is followed by a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, yielding a distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The method is well-motivated, simple, and elegant. The paper reports solid POS tagging results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, training with a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The approach is modular, and researchers working on other NLP tasks in low-resource settings are likely to adopt it.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup is unusual. While there are scenarios where one might need to build a POS tagger with as few as 1,000 token-level annotations (e.g., certain DARPA evaluations), this is relatively rare. A more informative empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy for the proposed method (and baselines) as a function of the amount of gold data. Such an analysis would help answer questions like: Does performance suffer on a target language if we use this method when ample gold annotations are available? Approximately how much gold data is needed before the method ceases to provide a benefit? Does the answer depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could plausibly come from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and may use tag sets that differ from the gold annotations. While this broader potential is exciting, the paper evaluates only cross-lingual projections with the same tag set.

It is also surprising that the training objective gives equal weight to the gold and noisy labels. Given that the setup assumes access to a small gold-annotated corpus, it would be informative to report whether tuning the relative contribution of the two terms in the objective improves performance.

Regarding clarity: in line 357, the paper describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but does not explicitly specify the form of \tilde{y} (e.g., whether it is a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the word type). A natural question is whether the approach still works if \tilde{y} is constructed using token-level projected POS tags directly, rather than aggregating over all occurrences of the same word type. In addition, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is constructed for tokens that are never aligned.
