The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network taggers that enables learning from both gold and projected (noisy) annotations in low-resource settings. The standard softmax over clean labels is followed by a learned fully connected “noise” layer that models the corruption process, yielding a distribution over observed noisy labels. This lets the model train jointly on clean and projected data.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The method is appropriate, simple, and elegant. Experiments on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages show solid gains using a small amount of gold data together with a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The approach is modular and likely to be adopted for other NLP tasks in low-resource scenarios.

That said, the experimental setup is somewhat unusual. Training with as few as 1,000 annotated tokens is uncommon in practice. A more informative empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy as a function of the size of the gold-annotated set for the proposed model and baselines. Such a curve would help answer:
- Does using the noise layer ever hurt when ample gold annotations are available?
- Approximately how much gold data is needed before the method ceases to provide a benefit?
- How does this threshold vary across target languages?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels may also come from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and may use tag sets that differ from the gold inventory. While the broader applicability is promising, the experiments only evaluate cross-lingual projections that share the same tag set, leaving the other scenarios untested.

It is also surprising that the training objective appears to weight gold and noisy labels equally. Given the presence of some gold supervision, it would be helpful to report whether tuning the relative contributions of the gold and noisy terms (or learning this weight) improves performance.

Clarifications on data representation would strengthen the paper. In line 357, the projected data are described as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but the form of \tilde{y} is not specified (e.g., is it a distribution over projected POS tags for the word type?). A natural question is whether the approach also works if \tilde{y} is constructed at the token level rather than aggregated across occurrences of the same type. Moreover, since only one-to-one alignments are retained, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is defined for tokens that are never aligned.

Minor editorial issue: in line 267, replace one of the two closing brackets with an opening bracket.

In sum, the paper is well-motivated and promising. Addressing the evaluation design, the balance between gold and noisy supervision, and the representation details would further strengthen the contribution.
