This paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network taggers that enables training from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource settings. The standard softmax over labels is coupled with a fully connected “noise” layer that models the corruption process, yielding an additional distribution over noisy labels. The approach is conceptually clean, easy to implement, and empirically effective.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant idea: modeling label noise via a simple linear layer on top of the softmax is apt and modular.
- Strong empirical results: the method improves POS tagging on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, using a small set of gold annotations and a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations.
- Broad applicability: the modular design should be useful for a range of low-resource NLP tasks where weak supervision or noisy labels are available.

Concerns and suggestions
- Experimental validation with respect to gold data size: The setup assumes extremely limited gold supervision (∼1,000 tokens), which is a somewhat unusual regime. A more informative evaluation would vary the amount of gold-labeled data and plot accuracy for the proposed method and baselines. This would address key questions:
  - Does using the noise layer ever hurt performance when ample gold annotations are available?
  - Approximately how much gold annotation is needed before the method’s advantage diminishes?
  - Does the crossover point vary by language?
  Such curves would substantially strengthen the practical guidance the paper offers.

- Weighting gold vs. noisy supervision: The objective appears to treat gold and noisy labels with equal weight. Given the availability of a trusted gold set, it would be valuable to report whether tuning a mixing weight (e.g., a hyperparameter that scales the contribution of the noisy-label term) improves results, and whether the optimal weight depends on (i) the size/quality of gold data, (ii) the amount/quality of projections, or (iii) the target language.

- Scope beyond cross-lingual projections: The paper motivates broader use cases (e.g., crowdsourcing or other weak signals), but only presents results with cross-lingual projections that share the same tag set as gold. Two extensions would clarify generality and impact:
  - Heterogeneous/noisy sources: Provide experiments (or at least a discussion) on integrating other noisy labels, such as crowd annotations, distant supervision, or heuristic taggers.
  - Label-set mismatch: Many practical settings involve different tag inventories. Can the noise layer naturally learn a mapping between label sets, or is an explicit mapping matrix needed? A small-scale experiment or analysis here would be compelling.

- Clarifying the projected supervision (line 357): The paper states that projected data are pairs of tokens xt and their vector representations ỹ, but does not specify the form of ỹ. Please clarify:
  - Is ỹ a probability distribution over POS tags aggregated at the type level or token level?
  - Would the approach still work with token-level projected distributions (e.g., per-aligned-token posteriors) rather than type-aggregated ones? This seems plausible and may preserve context-specific signals.
  - How is ỹ constructed for tokens that are never aligned, given the one-to-one alignment constraint? Do you back off to type aggregates, use neighborhood/context heuristics, or omit such tokens? Reporting coverage statistics and the chosen strategy would help reproducibility.

- Training details for the noise layer: A brief discussion of initialization and regularization would be helpful. For instance:
  - Do you initialize the noise matrix near identity (to bias toward clean labels) or randomly?
  - Do you constrain or regularize it (e.g., with entropy or L2 penalties) to prevent degenerate solutions?
  - How stable are results across random seeds, and is the noise layer identifiable enough to learn consistently?

- Additional baselines and analysis (if available):
  - Compare to training only on gold annotations and to simply mixing gold and projected labels without a learned noise model.
  - Include a variant with confidence-weighted projections (e.g., attention to alignment/posterior quality) to separate gains due to the learned noise model from gains due to leveraging more data.
  - Report per-language results with brief error analysis to illuminate where the method helps most (e.g., rare tags, morphologically rich languages).

Overall assessment
This is a strong submission: the method is simple, well-motivated, and produces solid gains in realistic low-resource scenarios. With additional analysis on the roles of (i) gold data size, (ii) weighting of gold vs. noisy supervision, and (iii) the form and coverage of projected labels, the paper would offer clearer practical guidance and stronger evidence of robustness and generality. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.

Minor comments
- Line 267: Replace one of the two closing brackets with an opening bracket.
- Line 357: Please define ỹ precisely (dimensions, whether it is a distribution, token- vs. type-level construction).
- Consider adding a brief complexity note: the additional fully connected layer should add negligible overhead; confirming this would reassure practitioners.
- A figure illustrating the clean softmax and the subsequent noise layer may improve readability.
