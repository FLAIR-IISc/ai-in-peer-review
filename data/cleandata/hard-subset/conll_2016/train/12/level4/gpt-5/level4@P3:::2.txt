Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a simple yet effective extension to sequence tagging models that enables joint learning from a small amount of gold-standard annotations and a large set of noisy, cross-lingually projected POS tags. The core idea is to augment a standard BiLSTM tagger with an explicit noise (confusion) layer that maps clean tag predictions to the distribution over projected (noisy) tags. Training alternates between clean supervision (gold tags) and noisy supervision (projected tags), allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected signal.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) A principled and modular noise-layer formulation for distant supervision in sequence tagging that explicitly models the mapping between clean and noisy labels, enabling effective joint training.
2) A thorough empirical evaluation on eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent gains over strong baselines and prior work.
3) An interpretable analysis of learned noise transformations across languages (via the A matrices), illustrating how the model captures systematic projection errors.

Strengths
- Effective and elegant modeling of projection noise: The explicit confusion layer offers a clear inductive bias for distant supervision. It is conceptually simple, easy to integrate into neural taggers, and yields interpretable parameters (the A matrix) that reveal systematic errors in projection (e.g., tag confusions, cases to ignore).
- Strong empirical results with realistic constraints: With only ~1K gold tokens, the method matches or outperforms prior state of the art across eight simulated and two truly low-resource languages. This supports the claim that the approach extracts substantial value from noisy projections while avoiding overfitting to scarce gold data.
- Practical training strategy: The two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint training with projected data) is sensible and appears necessary for stability. The paper clearly articulates this procedure.
- Generality and reusability: The noise-layer abstraction is modular and likely transferable to other NLP tasks and distant supervision settings (e.g., NER, relation extraction, crowd labels). It does not rely on language-specific heuristics beyond standard alignment and projection pipelines.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of A across languages help validate that the model learns meaningful corrections (e.g., diagonal dominance with plausible off-diagonal adjustments) and diagnose cases where projection quality varies with resource availability.

Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of supervision regimes: The empirical setup fixes the gold data size at ~1K tokens. While relevant for some low-resource scenarios, this is unusual in practice. Learning curves varying the gold annotation size would clarify when the method provides net benefit, how it scales with more supervision, and whether it degrades performance when abundant gold data is available.
- Equal weighting of gold and noisy objectives: The joint loss gives equal weight to gold and projected terms. Given the noise in projection, it is natural to tune a trade-off parameter (or schedule it over training). An ablation varying this weight would inform robustness and practical guidance.
- Scope limited to same tagset projection: Although the method can in principle handle mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), the paper only evaluates same-tagset projection. Demonstrating mismatched tagsets or alternative noisy sources (e.g., crowd labels) would strengthen the claim of generality.
- Incomplete ablations on components: It is unclear how much each design choice contributes (e.g., effect of pretraining vs joint-only training, sensitivity to the alignment filter, use of 1-to-1 alignments only, initialization and regularization of A). Such ablations would bolster the causal story behind the gains.
- Construction of projected supervision: The description of how ỹ is formed leaves ambiguity (token-level vs type-level aggregation; handling of unaligned tokens beyond sentence-level distributions). Since this construction can materially affect noise characteristics, more detail and comparison would be useful.

Questions to Authors
- Supervision regime: Can you report learning curves showing accuracy as a function of gold tokens (e.g., 0.5k, 1k, 2k, 5k, 10k)? Does performance converge to the gold-only baseline as annotations grow? Is there a regime where adding projected data hurts?
- Loss weighting: Did you tune a coefficient to weight gold vs projected losses? If so, what values worked; if not, can you report results with a tuned weight or an annealing schedule?
- Projected labels ỹ: Please clarify the exact construction. Are projected labels token-level or aggregated over types? How are unaligned tokens treated—uniform distribution, sentence-level distribution, or another heuristic? Can you compare these choices?
- Training details for A: How is A initialized (identity, zeros, random)? Any regularization (e.g., L2, temperature scaling, priors) to prevent degenerate solutions? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Ablations: What is the impact of (a) removing the pretraining stage, (b) using all alignments (1-to-many/many-to-1), (c) not filtering sentences by alignment scores?
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the quality of English tagging and alignment (e.g., different taggers/aligners)? Any evidence that the method is more/less helpful when projection noise is higher?
