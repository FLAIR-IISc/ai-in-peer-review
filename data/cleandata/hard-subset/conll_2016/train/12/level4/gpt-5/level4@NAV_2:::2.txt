The paper proposes a modification to the prediction head of a recurrent neural tagger that enables learning from both gold-standard and cross-lingually projected annotations in low-resource settings. Concretely, the standard softmax that yields a distribution over clean labels is followed by a learned linear transformation that models the noise process, producing a second output distribution over noisy (projected) labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The approach is appropriate, simple, and elegant, and the results are solid: the method delivers strong POS tagging performance across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, trained with a small gold-annotated set plus abundant projected labels. The design is modular enough that researchers tackling other low-resource NLP problems are likely to adopt it.

From a practical perspective, the experimental protocol is somewhat atypical. While there are scenarios where only ~1k tokens of gold POS annotations are available (e.g., certain program evaluations), this is relatively uncommon. A more informative empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy for the proposed method and baselines as the size of gold data varies. Such an analysis would clarify: whether using this approach in the presence of ample gold data impairs performance; the approximate gold annotation threshold below which the method yields gains; and how these trends vary by target language.

Beyond cross-lingual projection, noisy supervision can arise from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and may use tag sets that differ from the gold labels. Although this broader applicability is compelling, the experiments are limited to cross-lingual projections with the same tag inventory.

It is also unexpected that the training objective weights gold and noisy supervision equally. Given that a small gold set is assumed, it would be useful to report whether tuning the relative contribution of the two losses improves results.

In line 357, the projected data are described as pairs (x_t, ỹ) but the exact form of ỹ is not specified (e.g., whether it is a distribution over projected POS tags for a word type). A natural follow-up is whether the approach still works if ỹ is constructed at the token level (rather than aggregating across all instances of the same type). Additionally, since only one-to-one alignments are retained, it is unclear how ỹ is defined for tokens that are never aligned.
