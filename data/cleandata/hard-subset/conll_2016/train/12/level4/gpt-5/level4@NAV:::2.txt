The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models that allows learning parameters from both gold and projected annotations in a low-resource language. The standard softmax layer that defines a distribution over clean labels is post-multiplied by a fully connected layer that models the noise-generation channel, producing a second output layer that represents the distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The method is well-chosen, simple, and elegant. The paper reports solid POS tagging results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, training with a small amount of gold annotations and a large pool of cross-lingually projected annotations. The approach is modular enough that researchers tackling other NLP tasks in low-resource settings are likely to adopt it.

From a practical angle, the experimental setup is atypical. While I can imagine scenarios where one must build a POS tagger with as few as 1,000 token annotations (e.g., certain DARPA-sponsored evaluations), such cases are uncommon. A more informative empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy for the proposed method and the baselines as a function of the amount of gold annotation. This would help answer: Does applying this method hurt performance when ample gold supervision is available? Approximately how much gold data marks the threshold below which the method provides gains? Does this threshold vary by target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could also be sourced elsewhere (e.g., crowdsourcing) and may use tag sets that differ from the gold standard. Although this broader applicability is promising, the paper only evaluates cross-lingual projections using the same tag set.

It is unexpected that the training objective weights gold and noisy labels equally. Given access to a small gold-annotated corpus, it would be helpful to report whether tuning the relative contributions of these two loss terms is beneficial.

In line 357, the projected data are described as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but the form of that vector is not specified (e.g., whether it is a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for the word type). A natural question is whether the approach still works if \tilde{y} is constructed from token-level projected POS tags rather than aggregating across occurrences of the same word type. Also, since only one-to-one word alignments are retained, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is defined for tokens that are never aligned.
