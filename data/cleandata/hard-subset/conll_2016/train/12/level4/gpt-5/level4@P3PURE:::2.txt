This paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models that enables training from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource languages. Specifically, the standard softmax layer that defines a distribution over true labels is composed with a fully connected layer modeling a noise-generation process, yielding an additional output distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The approach is apt, simple, and elegant. The experiments report solid POS tagging results for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, using a small set of gold annotations together with a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The method is modular and likely to be adopted by researchers tackling other NLP problems in low-resource settings.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup is unusual. While I can imagine scenarios where one needs to train a POS tagger with as few as 1,000 token-level gold annotations (e.g., certain DARPA evaluations), this is uncommon. A more informative empirical validation would plot tagging accuracy for the proposed method and baselines as a function of the amount of gold annotations. Such a plot would help answer key questions:
- Does deploying this method harm performance when ample gold annotations are available?
- Approximately how much gold data marks the regime where this method is beneficial?
- Does this threshold depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could arise from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and may use tag sets that differ from the gold annotations. While this broader potential is compelling, the paper evaluates only cross-lingual projections with the same tag set.

It is also surprising that the training objective assigns equal weight to gold and noisy labels. Given that the setup assumes availability of a small gold-annotated corpus, it would be valuable to report whether tuning the relative contributions of the two objective terms improves performance.

Regarding clarity, line 357 describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y}, but it does not specify the form of \tilde{y} (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for this word type). It would be natural to ask whether the approach also works if \tilde{y} is constructed from token-level projected tags (rather than aggregating predictions across instances of a word type). Moreover, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is constructed for words that are never aligned.
