I previously reviewed an earlier version of this work when it was submitted as an ACL 2016 short paper. At that time, I identified a flaw in the experimental setup. In the current version, that flaw appears to be corrected, and my main concern has been addressed. Given this correction, I see no remaining obstacle to acceptance.

The paper presents a clear and useful contribution, with a method that is well-motivated and evaluated. The experimental design and analyses now read as sound, and the reported results support the main claims in the scenario studied. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

That said, the experimental setup still feels somewhat artificial when viewed through the lens of truly low-resource languages. In such settings, the dominant cost is often not payment per annotation but the time and effort required to find, recruit, and possibly train qualified annotators. This practical constraint can change the economics and feasibility of the proposed approach and may limit how well the experimental framing maps to real-world deployments. I recommend that the authors make this caveat explicit in the write-up—ideally as part of a limitations or scope-of-applicability discussion—so as not to oversell the method. A brief, qualitative cost model or sensitivity analysis would be especially helpful.

Related work could be broadened to better situate the contribution within the literature on techniques for extremely low-resource languages. In particular, please discuss annotation projection approaches such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agić et al. (2015, ACL), and clarify how the proposed method complements or differs from these lines of work. Even a concise paragraph would improve completeness and positioning.

Please also clarify data and resource availability. It should be explicitly stated whether code, data, and any annotation guidelines will be released. I am evaluating these aspects in good faith, presuming public release upon publication.

Minor suggestions for clarity and reproducibility:
- Clearly define the task and selection criteria for data and/or annotators.
- Report any inter-annotator agreement, annotation time, and (if relevant) cost statistics to ground the claims.
- Provide essential implementation details (e.g., preprocessing, hyperparameters) to facilitate replication.
- Include a brief error analysis to illuminate typical failure modes and guide future work.

Overall assessment: This is a solid and timely contribution. With the corrected experimental setup and the suggested additions—especially the realism caveat and expanded related work—I recommend acceptance.
