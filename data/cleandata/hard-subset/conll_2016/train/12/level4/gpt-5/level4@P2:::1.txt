Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper presents a BiLSTM POS tagger that jointly learns from a small gold-annotated corpus and noisy cross-lingual projected tags by introducing an explicit noise layer that models the mapping between clean and projected tags. With about 1,000 gold tokens plus parallel data, the method matches or surpasses prior work on eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Primary contribution: integrating an explicit projection-noise transformation into a sequence tagger and training it jointly on gold and projected data to learn when to trust the projections.
- Secondary contribution: broad empirical validation showing consistent improvements over strong baselines and prior state of the art under comparable setups.

Strengths
- The experimental flaw I flagged when reviewing the earlier ACL 2016 short-paper draft has been corrected. I previously indicated I would support acceptance if this were fixed; with the correction in place, I see no remaining obstacle on that front.
- The approach is principled and easy to motivate: modeling projection noise explicitly is a clear way to leverage distant supervision without letting it overwhelm the signal from scarce gold labels.
- Results are strong and consistent across both simulated and real low-resource scenarios, improving upon comparable baselines and prior methods under the same evaluation protocol.

Weaknesses
- The experimental setup remains somewhat artificial for truly low-resource contexts. In practice, the costs and logistics of locating annotators versus compensating them can dominate, and the paper’s setup abstracts away some of these issues. The write-up should make these limitations explicit so the method is not oversold for real-world deployment.
- The related work section would benefit from discussing recent annotation projection work targeting extremely low-resource languages, e.g., Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agić et al. (ACL 2015), to better situate the contribution.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Will the data, alignments/projections, and code be released? My evaluation assumes the relevant resources will be publicly available; please clarify the release plan.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a clear, well-motivated contribution with corrected experimental setup, solid empirical results, and practical relevance. Please add a brief discussion of the practical low-resource annotation constraints and include the missing related work to strengthen the camera-ready version.
