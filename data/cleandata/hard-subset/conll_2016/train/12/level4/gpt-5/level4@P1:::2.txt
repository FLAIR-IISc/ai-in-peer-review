Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a simple, effective modification to a BiLSTM sequence tagger that enables joint learning from a small gold-annotated corpus and a large set of cross-lingually projected (noisy) annotations. Specifically, the model adds a fully connected “noise” layer on top of the standard softmax output, transforming the clean label distribution into a distribution over noisy labels, thereby explicitly modeling the noise generation process in projection.
- The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), using about 1,000 gold-annotated tokens plus parallel data for projection. Results equal or surpass prior work. The method is modular and likely applicable beyond POS tagging.

Strengths
- Clear, apt, and elegant idea: Explicitly modeling projection noise with a small number of additional parameters is a principled way to reconcile clean and noisy supervision. The architectural change is minimal, end-to-end trainable, and integrates naturally with standard BiLSTMs.
- Strong empirical results: The method consistently improves over sensible BiLSTM baselines and the prior state of the art (e.g., Duong et al., 2014) across eight simulated settings and two real low-resource languages. The gains are especially notable given the very limited gold data.
- Interpretable analysis: The learned noise transformation matrices provide insight into cross-lingual confusions (e.g., cases where the model learns to trust or ignore certain projected tags), supporting the claim that the layer captures systematic noise patterns from alignment errors and syntactic divergence.
- Practical portability: The design is sufficiently general that it should be useful for other low-resource sequence tagging tasks where distant supervision is available, and potentially for other sources of noisy labels.

Weaknesses
- Practicality of the setup: Training with only ~1,000 gold tokens, while relevant in some settings (e.g., certain DARPA evaluations), is relatively rare. A more informative validation would include learning curves varying the amount of gold supervision. This would clarify: (i) whether the method helps or hurts when ample gold data is available; (ii) the approximate gold-data threshold below which the method is beneficial; and (iii) whether this threshold is language dependent.
- Scope of evidence: Although the paper argues that the approach applies to other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and potentially to mismatched tagsets, the experiments only cover cross-lingual projection with the same tagset. Demonstrating at least one of these additional settings would substantially strengthen the generality claims.
- Objective weighting: The training objective gives equal weight to gold and noisy labels. Given that a small amount of gold data is available, it would be useful to report whether tuning the relative weight of these two losses (or using a curriculum/schedule) affects performance. This seems particularly important when the projected data is abundant and noisier.
- Details on projected label representation: The paper (line 357) denotes the projected data as pairs (x_t, ỹ), but does not clearly specify the form of ỹ. It would be natural to ask whether ỹ is constructed at the token level (e.g., a one-hot or a token-specific distribution) versus the word type level, and how ỹ is constructed for tokens that are never aligned (only one-to-one alignments are retained). These details matter for reproducibility and for understanding how the noise layer is trained.

Questions to authors
- Please provide learning curves for varying amounts of gold supervision (e.g., 500, 1k, 5k, 10k tokens). Does the method still help when substantial gold data is available? Approximately where is the break-even point, and does it vary by language?
- How exactly is ỹ defined? Is it token-specific (e.g., a one-hot or a token-level distribution), or aggregated at the word type level or over the sentence? How do you construct ỹ for tokens with no alignment (given the one-to-one alignment restriction)?
- Did you experiment with weighting the gold and projected losses differently, or with a training schedule (e.g., gold-heavy early, then more projected)? If so, what was the impact?
- How sensitive is the method to the quantity/quality of projected data and to the one-to-one alignment restriction? Did relaxing or tightening alignment filtering change results?
- The model and equations suggest support for mismatched tagsets via a rectangular A. Did you try this setting? If not, are there implementation constraints preventing it?
- What regularization, if any, is applied to the noise matrix A to avoid degenerate solutions? How sensitive are results to initialization and the two-stage training (pretrain on gold, then joint)?

Additional comments
