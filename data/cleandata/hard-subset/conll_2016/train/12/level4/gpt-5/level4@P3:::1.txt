Summary and contributions:
This paper tackles POS tagging in low-resource settings by combining a small gold-annotated corpus with noisy cross-lingual projections from parallel text. The core idea is to explicitly model projection noise via a learnable “noise layer” on top of a BiLSTM tagger, trained jointly on gold and projected data. The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource settings and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving strong results.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) An explicit noise-modeling layer that learns a confusion transformation between clean tags and noisy projected tags, enabling effective use of distant supervision.
2) A joint training framework (with sensible pretraining and joint finetuning) that leverages both scarce gold labels and abundant projected annotations.
3) Empirical evidence of state-of-the-art or better performance across multiple languages, with insightful analyses of learned noise matrices.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled modeling of projection noise: Rather than relying on heuristics or implicit feature sharing, the model parameterizes the mapping from true tags to projected tags (matrix A) and learns it jointly. This makes the distant supervision signal more trustworthy and interpretable. The visualizations of A support the claim that the model captures systematic divergences and alignment artifacts (e.g., determiner–noun issues, verb projection uncertainty).

- Strong and consistent empirical gains: On eight simulated languages, the proposed model matches or surpasses prior state-of-the-art methods, and on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda it yields notable improvements (86.3% and 82.5% accuracy respectively), outperforming Duong et al. (2014a) and Garrette and Baldridge (2013). The comparisons to BiLSTM baselines trained on gold only, projection only, or their naive union convincingly demonstrate the value of the noise layer.

- Sound training strategy and practical applicability: Pretraining on gold followed by joint training with projected data is a practical solution to avoid poor local optima when projection noise is high. The approach is simple to implement, integrates naturally with modern sequence models, and is extensible to mismatched tagsets.

- Informative analysis: The paper goes beyond reporting numbers by analyzing learned confusion patterns across languages, highlighting where projection is trustworthy, where it fails, and how the model adapts. The contrast between Malagasy and Kinyarwanda (parallel data size vs. noise and gains) is particularly useful.

- Relevance and timeliness: Cross-lingual projection remains one of the most realistic sources of supervision for true low-resource languages. An explicit, learnable noise model represents a meaningful step forward.

Weaknesses:
- Realism of the data scenario: While the simulated settings are standard, the real-world applicability hinges on the availability and quality of parallel data and alignments. The Malagasy corpus (~100k sentences) is relatively large compared to what may exist for many low-resource languages; Kinyarwanda (~11k) is more modest. The paper should better contextualize the costs and feasibility of acquiring parallel data vs. collecting 1k gold tokens, and avoid overselling the approach for very low-parallel scenarios.

- Limited comparison to recent projection work in extremely low-resource settings: The related work omits relevant lines such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agić et al. (2015, ACL), which focus on projection under more extreme resource constraints. Positioning against these would strengthen the paper and clarify when the proposed approach is expected to be most effective.

- Evaluation depth and robustness: It would help to report variance over multiple runs (random seeds), details on early stopping/regularization, and sensitivity to the amount of gold supervision (e.g., 100, 500, 1,000 tokens) and to the amount/quality of parallel data. Ablations on the noise layer (e.g., diagonal-only A, fixed vs. learned A, training without gold pretraining) would solidify causal claims.

- Alignment and tagger dependencies: The method relies on one-to-one alignments and an English POS tagger. It would be useful to quantify sensitivity to different alignment strategies (e.g., including many-to-one with appropriate modeling) and to the quality of the source tagger. Modeling alignment uncertainty directly might further reduce noise.

- Reproducibility and data/code availability: The paper should clearly state whether code, trained models, projection resources, and processed datasets will be released. This is important given the multiple data sources (Europarl, web-sourced Malagasy, ARL MURI Kinyarwanda) and preprocessing steps (alignment filtering, tagset mapping).

Questions to authors:
- Data and code release: Will you release code, trained models, and the processed projection annotations (or scripts to reproduce them), including the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda resources? If not, what parts can be shared?

- Sensitivity analyses: How does performance vary with (i) the number of gold tokens (e.g., 100–1000), and (ii) the size/quality of parallel data? Any results on extremely small parallel corpora?

- Ablations on the noise layer: What happens if A is constrained to be diagonal or initialized to identity and not learned? Did you try sharing vs. not sharing parameters across the gold and projected branches beyond A?

- Alignment choices: Did you evaluate including many-to-one/one-to-many alignments with marginalization or confidence weighting, rather than discarding them? How does that trade off increased coverage vs. noise under your model?

- Initialization and optimization: How is A initialized? Do you regularize A toward the identity (or use priors) to stabilize learning? Any observations on convergence and stability across languages?

- Scope of applicability: Do you expect the same approach to help for other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., NER) where projection noise may be different in nature?

Additional comments:
- Please discuss Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agić et al. (2015, ACL) in related work to better situate your contribution within projection approaches for very low-resource settings.
- Clarify the universal tag mapping procedure and whether punctuation and X/PRT confusions are handled consistently across languages.

Overall recommendation: Accept. This is a clear, well-motivated contribution with a simple and effective idea that improves the usability of projected supervision for low-resource POS tagging. Strengthening the discussion of related work and the realism of the data scenario, and clarifying reproducibility plans, would further enhance the paper.
