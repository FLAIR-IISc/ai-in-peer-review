This paper introduces a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models designed to facilitate learning in low-resource languages by leveraging both gold and projected annotations. The method augments the standard softmax output layer with a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, creating a new output distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The proposed approach is well-motivated, straightforward, and elegant. The authors demonstrate promising results on POS tagging across eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages, utilizing a combination of limited gold annotations and a larger set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The modularity of the method suggests broad applicability to other NLP tasks in low-resource settings.

The experimental setup, however, is somewhat specialized. While scenarios requiring POS taggers trained with minimal gold data (e.g., certain DARPA-funded research) exist, they are not commonplace. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation would involve plotting tagging accuracy as a function of the size of the gold annotation set, for both the proposed method and baseline approaches. Such a plot would address key questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? At what level of gold annotation scarcity does it become advantageous? And does this threshold vary across target languages?

Furthermore, while the paper focuses on cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could originate from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or utilize different tag sets than the gold annotations. Although the potential impact of these scenarios is significant, the current work only presents results with cross-lingual projections and matching tag sets.

The equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the training objective is also noteworthy. Given the assumption of a small gold corpus, it would be valuable to investigate whether tuning the relative contribution of these two terms improves performance.

Regarding implementation details, line 357 describes the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and vector representations \tilde{y}, but lacks a clear explanation of the structure of \tilde{y} (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags). It would be useful to determine if the approach remains effective when \tilde{y} is constructed using token-level projected POS tags, rather than aggregating predictions for each word type. Additionally, the handling of words lacking one-to-one alignments and the construction of \tilde{y} for these cases remain unclear.

Finally, a minor correction is needed on line 267, where one of the closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
