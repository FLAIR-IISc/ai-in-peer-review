### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource settings by proposing a method to effectively utilize a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a much larger corpus of noisy, distantly-supervised data from cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to augment a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger with an explicit "noise layer." This layer, a linear transformation applied to the model's output probabilities, is trained to model the systematic errors and biases present in the projected annotations. The model is trained jointly on both the clean gold data (using a standard cross-entropy loss) and the noisy projected data (using a loss computed after the noise transformation), allowing the network to learn to de-noise the projected signal during backpropagation.

### **Contributions**

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A simple and elegant method for jointly training a sequence model on both gold-standard and noisy, projected annotations. The proposed "noise layer" is a modular component that explicitly models the transformation between the model's predictions and the noisy labels.
2.  Strong empirical results demonstrating that the proposed method equals or surpasses the state-of-the-art for POS tagging on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Effective and Elegant Method:** The proposed noise layer is a compelling solution to the problem of learning from noisy labels. It is intuitive, simple to implement, and adds very few parameters to the model. By explicitly modeling the noise process, it provides a principled way to leverage large but unreliable data sources.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including a state-of-the-art model from Duong et al. (2014a). The method achieves new state-of-the-art results on two real low-resource languages, providing compelling evidence of its practical utility.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The proposed architecture is not specific to POS tagging or cross-lingual projection. The noise layer is a modular component that could be readily applied to other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., NER) and other sources of noisy supervision (e.g., crowdsourcing, heuristic-based labeling). This generality significantly increases the potential impact of the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Experimental Scope (Varying Gold Data Size):** The experimental setup is exclusively focused on a single, extremely low-resource scenario (1000 gold tokens). While this demonstrates the method's effectiveness in that setting, it leaves important practical questions unanswered. A more thorough evaluation would include a learning curve analysis, showing performance as the amount of gold data increases. This would help clarify the trade-offs: At what point does the benefit of the noisy data diminish? Could this method potentially harm performance when a moderate amount of gold data is available? Understanding this dynamic is crucial for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.
2.  **Limited Validation of Generality:** The paper suggests the method is generalizable to other sources of noisy labels. However, the experiments are confined to a single type of noise (cross-lingual projection) and a single setting (where the projected and gold tag sets are the same). While the authors mention that the transformation matrix `A` could be rectangular for mismatched tag sets, this is not empirically validated. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on another type of distant supervision (e.g., crowd-sourced labels) would have substantially strengthened the claims of generality.
3.  **Unexplored Hyperparameter in the Loss Function:** The joint training objective gives equal weight to the loss from the small gold corpus and the large projected corpus. This seems counter-intuitive, as the gold data is much more reliable. It is common practice to introduce a hyperparameter to balance the contributions of different loss terms. An ablation study exploring the effect of weighting the gold-standard loss more heavily would have been informative and could potentially lead to further performance gains.

### **Questions to Authors**

The paper states that the projected data consists of pairs (x_t, ỹ_t), where ỹ_t denotes the projected POS tag. Section 3.1 clarifies that for one-to-one alignments, this is the single projected tag, and for multi-aligned or unaligned (NULL) tokens, ỹ_t is a distribution over tags from the source sentence. Could you please clarify if ỹ_t is treated as a one-hot vector in the first case and a probability distribution in the second? And is this representation constructed at the token level for each instance in the parallel corpus, or is it aggregated at the word type level across the entire corpus?
