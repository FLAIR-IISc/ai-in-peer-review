Paper introduces a simple extension to the RNN softmax output layer with an additional fully connected noise model to jointly train on gold and noisy projected annotations.  
Method is elegant, modular, and shows compelling POS tagging results on eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages.  
Likely to be adopted by other NLP tasks in low-resource settings due to its simplicity and modularity.  
Primary experimental critique: using only 1,000 gold-annotated tokens is an uncommon scenario and limits practical conclusions.  
Recommend empirical analysis varying gold-data size to show when the method helps, whether it degrades with abundant gold data, and whether thresholds depend on language.  
Broader applicability is under-discussed: experiments focus on cross-lingual projection with identical tag sets, but the method could apply to crowdsourcing or differing tag-set scenarios.  
Surprising choice to weight gold and noisy data equally in the objective; authors should report results when tuning the relative weight of the two terms.  
Ambiguity in representation of projected data \tilde{y} (line 357): unclear if it’s a distribution over projected POS tags or a type-level aggregated vector.  
Questions raised: would token-level projected labels work as well as type-aggregated ones, and how are \tilde{y} constructed for words that are never aligned?  
Minor editorial: replace a closing bracket with an opening one on line 267.
