This paper introduces a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural networks that enables joint training on both gold-standard and noisy, projected annotations in low-resource settings. The method extends the standard softmax layer, which produces a distribution over true labels, with an additional fully connected layer designed to model the noise generation process. The output of this noise model is a second distribution over the observed noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant. The paper reports compelling results on POS tagging for eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages, leveraging a small set of gold annotations alongside a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The modularity of the approach makes it highly likely to be adopted by researchers for other NLP tasks in low-resource scenarios.

From a practical standpoint, my primary critique concerns the experimental setup. While building a POS tagger with only 1,000 gold-annotated tokens is a relevant use case in some contexts (e.g., certain DARPA program evaluations), it is not a common scenario. A more thorough empirical validation would involve plotting the tagging accuracy of the proposed method and the baselines while varying the size of the gold annotation set. Such an analysis would help answer critical questions: Does the method degrade performance when ample gold data is available? What is the approximate amount of gold data below which this method becomes beneficial? Does this threshold depend on the target language?

The paper could also strengthen its contribution by discussing the method's broader applicability. The experiments are confined to noisy labels from cross-lingual projections where the gold and noisy tag sets are identical. However, the framework could potentially handle noisy labels from other sources, such as crowdsourcing, or even scenarios where the tag sets differ.

It is surprising that the proposed training objective assigns equal weight to both gold and noisy data. Given that the small set of gold annotations is presumably more reliable, it would be informative to report on whether tuning the relative contribution of the two terms in the objective function is beneficial.

Further clarification is needed regarding the representation of the projected data, \tilde{y}. Line 357 describes this as a vector representation for a word token (x_t) but does not explicitly state its form (e.g., a distribution over projected POS tags for that word type). This ambiguity raises two questions. First, would the approach still be effective if \tilde{y} were constructed from projected POS tags at the token level, rather than by aggregating all predictions for a given word type? Second, since only one-to-one word alignments are retained, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is constructed for words that are never aligned.

On line 267, a closing bracket should be replaced with an opening one.
