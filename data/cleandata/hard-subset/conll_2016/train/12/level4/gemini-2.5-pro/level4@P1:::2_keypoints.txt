- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger augmented with a linear "noise layer" to model systematic errors in distantly-supervised projected labels and jointly trains on clean and noisy data.  
- Core contribution: a simple, modular noise-layer method for jointly learning from gold-standard and noisy projected annotations.  
- Empirical contribution: matches or surpasses prior state-of-the-art on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).  
- Strength: noise layer is intuitive, easy to implement, adds few parameters, and provides a principled way to denoise large unreliable datasets.  
- Strength: consistent and significant empirical improvements over strong baselines, including new state-of-the-art on real low-resource languages.  
- Strength: method is general and could apply to other sequence labeling tasks and other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourcing).  
- Weakness: experiments only use a single extremely low-resource setting (1000 gold tokens); no learning curve analysis to show performance as gold data increases.  
- Weakness: generality is not validated beyond cross-lingual projection or mismatched tag sets; no experiments on other noise types (e.g., crowdsourced labels).  
- Weakness: joint loss weights gold and projected data equally without exploring a weighting hyperparameter or ablation study.  
- Question: clarify whether projected label ỹ_t is a one-hot for one-to-one alignments and a distribution for multi/unaligned tokens, and whether this is token-level or aggregated at word-type level.
