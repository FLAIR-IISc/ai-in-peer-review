### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger, noisier corpus created via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural sequence tagging model based on a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM). The model's key innovation is an explicit "noise layer," a linear transformation that learns the systematic confusion patterns between the clean, gold tags and the noisy, projected tags. The model is trained jointly on both the small clean dataset and the large noisy dataset, allowing the error signal from the noisy data to be "de-noised" before updating the shared BiLSTM parameters. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for modeling noise in distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of an explicit noise layer (parameterized by matrix A) to model the systematic errors inherent in cross-lingual annotation projection. This is a principled approach that allows a model to learn *when* and *how* to trust the noisy labels, rather than treating them as equally valid training instances or relying on complex pre-processing heuristics. This technique is simple, requires very few parameters, and is broadly applicable to other tasks using distant supervision.

2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical validation for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation across ten different languages. The consistent performance gains over strong baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art work by Duong et al. (2014a), convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed model. The analysis of the learned noise matrices further strengthens the results by providing an interpretable view of the cross-lingual error patterns the model has captured.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the projection noise via a learnable transformation matrix is both simple and powerful. It directly addresses the central challenge of using projected annotations: they are useful but unreliable. While prior work has used projected tags as features or in multi-task setups, this paper’s approach of learning a dedicated noise-modeling component within the network is a more direct and, as shown by the results, more effective solution. The joint training objective is well-formulated, allowing the model to benefit from the large projected corpus without being misled by its noise.

2.  **Convincing and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through extensive experiments. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on both simulated low-resource settings (Table 1) and two genuine low-resource languages (Table 2). The improvement over Duong et al. (2014a) is consistent across nearly all languages, and the gains on Malagasy (+1.0% accuracy) and Kinyarwanda (+4.2% accuracy) are substantial. The inclusion of baseline experiments (e.g., `BiLSTM Ann+Proj`) clearly shows that naively mixing clean and noisy data is suboptimal, reinforcing the value of the proposed noise layer.

3.  **Insightful Analysis and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly, aided by the helpful illustration in Figure 2. A particular strength is the qualitative analysis of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4. These visualizations provide valuable insight into the model's behavior, showing that it learns plausible and language-specific error patterns (e.g., the strong diagonal for Malagasy vs. the more diffuse noise in Kinyarwanda, which correlates with the quality of the available parallel data). This analysis increases confidence that the model is not just a black box but is learning meaningful linguistic phenomena.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion of the Practicality of the Low-Resource Scenario:** The paper's setup assumes the availability of a large parallel corpus but only 1,000 gold-annotated tokens. While this is a valid experimental paradigm, for some truly low-resource languages, sourcing a large, clean parallel corpus can be as challenging or costly as annotating a few thousand more tokens. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this trade-off. It could better characterize the specific types of low-resource scenarios where this approach is most beneficial (e.g., languages with substantial religious or governmental translations but lacking any NLP community or linguistic resources). Acknowledging this could prevent overselling the method's universal applicability.

2.  **Omission of Relevant Recent Work:** The related work section could be updated to include more recent and highly relevant research on annotation projection for low-resource languages. For instance, work on cross-lingual model projection and universal dependencies, such as Agić et al. (2015, ACL) and Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL), explores similar challenges. Situating this paper's contributions in the context of these more recent approaches would provide a more complete picture of the current state of the art and highlight the unique aspects of the proposed method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the practical cost-benefit trade-off of this approach? Specifically, how does the effort of finding, cleaning, and processing a large parallel corpus (e.g., 200k sentences for the simulated languages) compare to the effort of annotating a moderately larger gold dataset (e.g., 5,000-10,000 tokens), which might be sufficient for a standard supervised BiLSTM tagger to achieve competitive performance?

2.  To facilitate reproducibility and future research, do you plan to make your code and the specific data splits used for the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda experiments publicly available?
