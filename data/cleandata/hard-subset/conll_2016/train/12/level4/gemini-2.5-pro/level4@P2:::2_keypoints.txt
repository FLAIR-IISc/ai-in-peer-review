- Proposes combining small gold data with large noisy distant supervision via a linear "noise layer" on a BiLSTM tagger.
- Trained jointly on clean and noisy sets and evaluated on POS tagging for 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Contribution: simple neural architecture that explicitly models the noise process so the tagger can trust or discount distant supervision.
- Contribution: Strong empirical results—state-of-the-art on 10 languages using only 1000 gold-annotated tokens.
- Strength: Methodological elegance and simplicity—single fully connected noise layer easy to implement and integrate.
- Strength: Consistent performance gains over strong baselines (standard BiLSTM and Duong et al. 2014a) across languages.
- Strength: Potentially general—applicable to other sequence tagging tasks and other noisy label sources (e.g., crowdsourcing).
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to exactly 1000 gold tokens; lacks learning-curve analysis to show behavior with more gold data.
- Weakness: Equal weighting of clean and noisy losses is unjustified; no ablation or tuning of a loss-weighting hyperparameter.
- Weakness: Claimed generality is untested—only cross-lingual projection and identical tagsets are demonstrated experimentally.
- Question: How does model performance change as the amount of gold-annotated data increases; is there a point where joint training is harmful?
- Question: Have you experimented with weighting the two loss terms and how sensitive is the model to that weight?
- Question: Please clarify how projected label distribution ỹ is constructed for unaligned or many-to-one alignments, with a concrete example.
