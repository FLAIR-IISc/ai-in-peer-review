This paper presents a valuable contribution, and I recommend its acceptance.

I had the opportunity to review a previous version of this work submitted as a short paper to ACL 2016. My primary concern at that time was a flaw in the experimental setup. I am pleased to see that the authors have thoroughly addressed this issue in the current submission. As this correction resolves my main previous reservation, I now support the paper's publication.

While the core methodology is sound, there is an opportunity to improve the framing of the paper's contribution. The experimental setup, while now correct, could be considered somewhat idealized in the context of genuinely low-resource languages. Specifically, the model does not fully account for the practical and often significant overhead costs associated with discovering, recruiting, and training annotators for a language with very few existing resources. To present a more nuanced picture and manage reader expectations, I strongly suggest the authors add a discussion of this limitation. Acknowledging this point would help to accurately position the work and avoid overstating the method's immediate applicability in the most challenging low-resource scenarios.

Furthermore, the discussion of related work could be strengthened by connecting to recent research on annotation projection, particularly for extremely low-resource settings. The work of Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agic et al. (ACL 2015) is highly relevant, and situating this paper in relation to these approaches would provide a more complete context for the contribution.

In summary, the authors have successfully addressed the critical issue from the previous submission, resulting in a solid and interesting paper. For the benefit of the community and to ensure reproducibility, the final version should clearly state whether the data, models, and code developed for the experiments will be made publicly available. My positive evaluation is based on the assumption that the research artifacts will be accessible.
