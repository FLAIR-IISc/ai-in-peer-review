- Problem & approach: POS tagging for low-resource languages using a small gold set plus a large noisy projected corpus with a BiLSTM that includes a learnable linear "noise layer"; evaluated on 8 simulated and 2 real low-resource languages with consistent gains.

- Contribution 1: Introduces an explicit noise layer (matrix A) to model systematic errors in projected labels — simple, low-parameter, broadly applicable.

- Contribution 2: Strong empirical validation across ten languages, outperforming prior state-of-the-art and offering interpretable learned noise matrices.

- Strength: Elegant, well-motivated architecture that learns when/how to trust noisy labels via joint training.

- Strength: Convincing and comprehensive experiments showing consistent improvements and clear baselines demonstrating the value of the noise layer.

- Strength: Clear presentation and insightful analysis (figures and qualitative examination of noise matrices) revealing language-specific error patterns.

- Weakness: Limited discussion of practicality — assumes large parallel corpora are available and lacks analysis of trade-offs with annotating more gold data.

- Weakness: Omits citation/discussion of relevant recent work (e.g., Agić et al. 2015; Johannsen et al. 2016) in related work.

- Question: Please clarify the cost-benefit trade-off between collecting large parallel corpora versus annotating a larger gold dataset (e.g., 5k–10k tokens).

- Question: Will the authors release code and the specific Malagasy and Kinyarwanda data splits for reproducibility?
