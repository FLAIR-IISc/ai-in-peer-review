### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a much larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural network model, based on a Bi-directional LSTM, which is augmented with a "noise layer." This layer is a trainable transformation matrix that explicitly models the systematic errors and biases introduced during the projection process. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data, with the noise layer learning to map the model's internal "clean" predictions to the observed noisy labels from the projected data. This allows the core model to learn a more accurate tagging function while still benefiting from the large volume of distantly supervised data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling supervision noise:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a single, trainable matrix that explicitly models the transformation from clean labels to noisy ones. This is an elegant and intuitive way to incorporate noisy data into a deep learning framework, allowing the model to learn *from* the noise rather than being misled by it.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance in low-resource POS tagging:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration of the proposed method's effectiveness. It consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods and strong baselines on a wide range of languages in a challenging low-resource setting (using only 1000 gold-annotated tokens).

### Strengths
1.  **Methodological elegance and simplicity:** The proposed noise layer is a simple yet powerful idea. Instead of relying on complex heuristics or pre-processing steps to clean the projected data, the model learns to account for the noise directly as part of the end-to-end training process. This approach is intuitive and adds minimal complexity to the base model.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical results:** The authors validate their method on a diverse set of ten languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios. The model achieves consistent improvements over several strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art system of Duong et al. (2014a) and a standard BiLSTM trained on various combinations of the data. This provides convincing evidence that the noise layer is a more effective way to utilize projected data than simply treating it as gold-standard training data.
3.  **Generality and potential for wider impact:** Although applied to POS tagging with cross-lingual projection, the method itself is generic. The noise layer is a modular component that could be added to any neural classifier that outputs a probability distribution over labels. This makes the technique highly promising for other NLP tasks and different sources of noisy supervision, such as crowd-sourced annotations or data labeled by heuristic rules.
4.  **Insightful analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix (A) in Figures 3 and 4 provides valuable insight into the model's behavior. These figures clearly show the types of systematic errors the model learns to correct (e.g., confusion between specific POS tags) and how the degree of noise differs across languages, correlating with the quality of the parallel corpora.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited evaluation setting:** The experiments are conducted under a single, fixed low-resource setting (1000 gold-annotated tokens). While this is a relevant scenario, it fails to characterize the model's behavior as more annotated data becomes available. A learning curve analysis, plotting accuracy versus the amount of gold data, would significantly strengthen the paper by answering key questions: At what point does the benefit of the noisy data diminish? Does this method ever harm performance when a substantial amount of gold data is available?
2.  **Unjustified equal weighting of data sources:** The joint objective function gives equal importance to the loss from the small, high-quality gold dataset and the loss from the large, low-quality projected dataset. This is a strong and potentially suboptimal assumption. Intuitively, the model should trust the gold data more. The paper would be more convincing if it explored the effect of a weighting hyperparameter to balance the two loss terms.
3.  **Untapped potential for different tagsets:** The paper assumes the projected tags come from the same tagset as the gold annotations (the Universal POS tagset). However, the authors briefly mention in a footnote that the transformation matrix `A` could be rectangular to handle mismatched tagsets. This is a significant advantage of the proposed model over methods that simply mix data sources, but it is not explored or evaluated, leaving its practical utility unsubstantiated.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Have you experimented with introducing a weighting coefficient to balance the two terms in your joint loss function? For instance, giving higher weight to the loss on the gold-annotated data seems like a natural strategy to prioritize fitting the clean supervision signal.
2.  How does your model's performance, relative to the baselines, change as the amount of gold-standard annotated data increases (e.g., from 1k to 5k to 10k tokens)? A learning curve analysis would provide a clearer picture of the scenarios where this method is most beneficial.
3.  In Section 3.1, you state that for tokens aligned to multiple words or none at all, you assign a distribution over tags based on sentence-level frequencies. However, in Section 4.1.2, you mention that you retain only one-to-one alignments, following prior work. Could you please clarify this apparent contradiction and explain precisely how unaligned and multi-aligned tokens are handled during the creation of the projected dataset?
