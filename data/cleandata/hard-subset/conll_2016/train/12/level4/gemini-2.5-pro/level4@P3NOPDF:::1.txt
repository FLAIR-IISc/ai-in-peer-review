**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While I am in favor of accepting this paper, I believe it could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Framing the Experimental Setup:** While the experimental setup is now technically sound, its assumptions may be somewhat artificial when considering truly low-resource languages. The current model seems to focus primarily on the cost of paying annotators, but in many real-world low-resource scenarios, the primary bottleneck is the cost and logistical difficulty of *finding* qualified speakers, not just compensating them for their time. I strongly recommend that the authors explicitly discuss this limitation in the paper. Acknowledging this would provide a more nuanced perspective, prevent the method from being oversold, and more accurately position its practical applicability.

2.  **Related Work:** The discussion of related work would benefit from including recent, highly relevant research on annotation projection and cross-lingual methods for extremely low-resource languages. For completeness and to better contextualize the contribution, the authors should consider citing and discussing work such as:
    *   Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL)
    *   Agic et al. (2015, ACL)
    Positioning the current work in relation to these approaches would provide readers with a more comprehensive understanding of the state of the art.

3.  **Data and Code Availability:** For reproducibility and to maximize the impact of this research, it is crucial that the authors clarify whether the data, resources, and/or code used in the experiments will be made publicly available. I have reviewed the paper in good faith, assuming that the resources will be released in line with best practices in the field. A clear statement to this effect should be added to the paper.

In summary, this paper makes a nice contribution to the field. The authors have diligently corrected a major flaw from a previous version, and the work is now methodologically sound.
