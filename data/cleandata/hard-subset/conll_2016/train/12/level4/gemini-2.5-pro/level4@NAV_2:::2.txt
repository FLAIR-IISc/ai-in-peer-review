### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for part-of-speech (POS) tagging in low-resource settings by jointly learning from a small gold-standard annotated corpus and a larger corpus with noisy labels derived from cross-lingual projection. The proposed model, a bidirectional LSTM, is augmented with an explicit "noise layer." This additional fully connected layer learns a transformation from the model's predicted distribution over clean tags to a distribution over the noisy projected tags. This allows the model to leverage the abundant but unreliable projected data by explicitly modeling the noise generation process. The key contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and simple method for incorporating noisy, distantly supervised labels into a neural sequence tagger. The proposed noise layer explicitly models the mapping between clean and noisy tag distributions, allowing the model to be trained end-to-end on both gold and projected annotations within a joint objective function.
2.  A strong empirical validation of the proposed approach. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating its practical effectiveness over strong baselines.

### Strengths

1.  The proposed method is elegant, simple, and well-motivated. Adding a single linear transformation layer to model the noise is an intuitive way to handle imperfect supervision, making the approach easy to understand and implement. The modularity of this "noise layer" suggests it could be readily adapted to other NLP tasks facing similar challenges (e.g., Named Entity Recognition) and other sources of noisy labels (e.g., crowdsourcing), broadening its potential impact.
2.  The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements. The paper shows that its model outperforms several strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on various combinations of the data and the previous state-of-the-art method on this task. The gains across ten different languages provide convincing evidence that explicitly modeling the projection noise is more effective than simply combining the clean and noisy datasets.
3.  The analysis, particularly the visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix, offers valuable insights. It confirms that the model learns meaningful and language-specific error patterns in the projection process (e.g., the strong diagonal indicating trust, and specific off-diagonal weights indicating systematic confusions), providing a clear interpretation of what the noise layer captures.

### Weaknesses

1.  The practical utility of the method is evaluated under a single, extreme low-resource condition (1000 annotated tokens). While this scenario is relevant, a more comprehensive empirical validation would involve showing performance curves as the amount of gold-standard data increases. This would help answer important practical questions, such as how much gold data is needed before the benefit of using projected data diminishes, or if this method could even be detrimental when a substantial amount of annotated data is available.
2.  The paper claims the technique is general, but the experiments are limited to a single type of distant supervision (cross-lingual projection) where the gold and noisy tagsets are identical. The work would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated its effectiveness on other noisy data sources (e.g., crowdsourced annotations) or in a setting with mismatched tagsets between the gold and noisy data, which is mentioned as a possibility in a footnote but not explored experimentally.
3.  The joint training objective gives equal weight to the loss terms from the small, reliable gold corpus and the large, noisy projected corpus. This choice is not justified, and it seems counter-intuitive not to down-weight the less reliable data source. An ablation study exploring a hyperparameter to balance these two terms would have been informative to see if performance could be further improved.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The training objective gives equal importance to the gold-annotated data and the noisy projected data. Did you experiment with a weighting hyperparameter to control the relative influence of the two cross-entropy terms in the loss function? Given the high reliability of the gold data, it seems plausible that assigning it a higher weight could be beneficial.
2.  Could you provide more detail on the construction of the projected POS tag vector, ỹt? Specifically, how is this vector created for tokens in the low-resource language that are unaligned or aligned to multiple English words with differing POS tags? Is this vector constructed at the token level for each occurrence, or is it aggregated at the word-type level?
3.  The experiments focus on a 1000-token annotated set. How does the performance gain from your method change as more gold-annotated data becomes available? Is there a point where the noise layer offers no additional benefit over a standard BiLSTM trained only on the larger gold set?
