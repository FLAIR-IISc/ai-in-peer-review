### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach for Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging in low-resource settings by leveraging a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core of the proposed method is a bidirectional LSTM model augmented with an explicit "noise layer." This layer learns a transformation matrix that models the systematic errors and biases between the model's internal predictions and the noisy projected labels. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data sources. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuine low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel neural architecture for sequence tagging that explicitly models the noise from a distant supervision source (cross-lingual projection) via a learnable transformation layer. This provides a principled way to jointly learn from small, clean corpora and large, noisy corpora.
2.  Strong empirical validation of the proposed model, which consistently equals or exceeds the performance of prior state-of-the-art methods on a comprehensive set of ten languages, including two real-world low-resource languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Effective Noise Modeling:** The primary strength of this work is the explicit modeling of noise from distant supervision. Instead of using heuristics to filter the projected data or treating it as equally valid supervision, the proposed noise layer learns a confusion matrix between the true tags and the projected tags. This is an intuitive and powerful method for down-weighting unreliable projections and exploiting systematic patterns in the projection errors, allowing the model to better leverage the large but imperfectly labeled parallel data. The visualizations of the learned noise matrices in Figures 3 and 4 provide valuable insight into this process.
2.  **Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. The proposed model demonstrates significant improvements over several well-chosen baselines, including a standard BiLSTM tagger and the previous state-of-the-art system. The evaluation across both simulated and real-world low-resource settings provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness and robustness. Achieving new state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda is a notable achievement.
3.  **Generalizability of the Method:** Although the paper focuses on POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, the core technique of incorporating a noise layer is broadly applicable. This approach could readily be adapted to other NLP tasks and other forms of noisy or distant supervision, such as labels generated from crowd-sourcing or heuristic-based methods. This makes the contribution highly relevant to the wider NLP community working on learning from imperfect data.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Understated Practicality Assumptions:** The paper's framing could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the practical trade-offs in real-world low-resource scenarios. The method's success hinges on the availability of a reasonably large parallel corpus, which may not always be easier to obtain than annotating a few thousand more tokens of gold data. A discussion of the relative costs (in time or money) of acquiring parallel data versus manual annotation would help to properly contextualize the method's utility and avoid overselling it as a universally applicable solution.
2.  **Omission of Some Relevant Work:** The related work section could be strengthened by including and discussing more recent research on annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages. For instance, works such as Agic et al. (2015, ACL) and Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) address similar challenges and would provide valuable context for positioning this paper's contributions within the current landscape of cross-lingual learning.
3.  **Unclear Data and Code Availability:** For a study focused on low-resource languages, where data is the primary bottleneck, reproducibility is paramount. The paper does not explicitly state whether the parallel corpora, word alignments, and experimental code will be released. Making these resources publicly available would significantly increase the impact of this work.
