### Summary

This paper introduces a novel neural network model for part-of-speech (POS) tagging in low-resource languages. The proposed method leverages a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language. The core contribution is a BiLSTM architecture that includes an explicit "noise layer," which learns a transformation matrix to model and correct for the systematic errors and biases inherent in the projected annotations. The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments, showing state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and well-motivated neural architecture for sequence tagging that explicitly models the noise from a distant supervision source (cross-lingual projection) via a dedicated transformation layer.
2.  Strong empirical results that establish a new state-of-the-art for POS tagging on two real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating the practical utility of learning to trust distant supervision selectively.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Effective Method:** The primary strength is the paper's core methodological contribution. The idea of adding an explicit noise layer to model the confusion matrix between the model's predictions and the noisy projected labels is both intuitive and powerful. It provides a principled way to leverage large but unreliable data sources without letting the noise overwhelm the signal from the small, clean dataset. This approach is more explicit and potentially more interpretable than methods that combine features from both sources without modeling their relationship.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness. By testing on eight simulated low-resource languages (from Europarl), the authors demonstrate the generalizability of their approach and enable direct comparison with prior work. Furthermore, the experiments on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) confirm that the method is not just an artifact of a simulated setting but delivers tangible gains in a practical scenario.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves impressive results, consistently outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art across all languages tested. The ablation-like comparisons to BiLSTM models trained on only annotated data, only projected data, or their simple union, clearly illustrate the value added by the noise layer. The gains over the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a) are consistent and significant, especially on the real low-resource languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion of Practical Applicability:** The paper's setup assumes the availability of a small annotated corpus and a much larger parallel corpus. While this is a reasonable scenario, the authors could strengthen the paper by briefly discussing the potential limitations. In some extremely low-resource settings, a large, clean parallel corpus may be as difficult to obtain as a small annotated one. Acknowledging this trade-off would provide a more nuanced perspective and prevent overselling the method's universal applicability.

2.  **Missing Relevant Citations:** The related work section could be more complete. It overlooks recent and highly relevant work on annotation projection and cross-lingual methods for extremely low-resource languages. For example, work by Johannsen et al. (ACL 2016) and Agić et al. (ACL 2015) explores similar challenges. Situating this paper in the context of these contemporary approaches would provide a clearer picture of its contribution to the field.
