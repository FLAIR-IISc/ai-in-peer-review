This paper presents a novel modification to the output layer of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) designed to leverage both gold-standard and noisy, projected annotations for training in low-resource settings. The core idea is to augment the standard softmax output layer, which models the distribution over true labels, with an additional fully connected layer. This new layer models a confusion matrix, effectively learning the noise generation process and producing a final output distribution over the observed noisy labels. This elegant approach allows the model to be trained jointly on a small amount of clean, gold-standard data and a large volume of automatically projected, noisy data.

Overall, this is a strong and well-written submission with a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed method is simple, elegant, and well-motivated for the task of learning from noisy annotations in low-resource scenarios.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements on Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages.
*   The approach is modular and could be readily applied to other sequence labeling tasks or NLP problems where both limited gold data and abundant noisy data are available, highlighting its potential for broader impact.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, its claims and practical utility could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Analysis of Performance vs. Gold Data Size:** While the experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness with a very small (1000-token) gold dataset, the practicality of this specific setting could be further explored. It would significantly strengthen the paper's empirical validation to include an analysis of the model's performance as the amount of gold-standard data varies. A plot showing the accuracy of the proposed model and the baselines against the size of the gold training set would be highly informative. Such an analysis could help answer critical questions:
    *   At what point, if any, does the proposed method offer no benefit or even harm performance as more gold data becomes available?
    *   What is the approximate threshold of gold data below which this approach is most advantageous?
    *   Does this threshold vary across different target languages?

2.  **Weighting of Gold vs. Noisy Data:** The training objective currently assigns equal weight to the loss from gold-annotated data and noisy data. Given that the gold data is, by definition, more reliable, it seems plausible that up-weighting its contribution to the total loss could improve performance. It would be beneficial for the authors to report on experiments that explore tuning the relative weights of the two terms in the objective function, or at least to provide a justification for the current equal-weighting scheme.

3.  **Generalization to Other Noise Sources:** The paper focuses exclusively on cross-lingual projection as the source of noisy labels. While this is a well-motivated scenario, the method's potential impact is even broader. Discussing or experimenting with other sources of noise—such as labels from crowd-sourcing, distant supervision, or scenarios where noisy labels come from a different tag set than the gold annotations—would further strengthen the paper's contribution and demonstrate the generality of the proposed noise-modeling layer.

4.  **Clarity on Projected Data Representation:** The description of the projected data representation (`\tilde{y}`) could be clarified. The paper states (line 357) that this is a vector representation for a word token `x_t`, but does not specify its exact form (e.g., is it a one-hot vector of the most likely projected tag, or a full distribution over tags aggregated for the word type?). Furthermore, the text suggests that these representations are aggregated at the word *type* level. This raises two questions:
    *   Would the approach still be effective if `\tilde{y}` were constructed at the token level, using the specific projected tag for that instance rather than an aggregated type-level representation?
    *   How are representations for words that are never aligned in the parallel corpus handled?
    Clarifying these details would improve the paper's reproducibility.
