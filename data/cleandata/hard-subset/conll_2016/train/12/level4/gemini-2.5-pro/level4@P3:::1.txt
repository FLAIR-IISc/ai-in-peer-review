### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The authors propose a method that leverages a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a larger, noisier dataset created via cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). The core of their approach is a novel neural network architecture, based on a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM), which includes an explicit "noise layer." This layer, parameterized by a transformation matrix, is designed to learn the systematic errors and biases inherent in the projection process. The model is trained jointly on both the clean gold data and the noisy projected data. The authors evaluate their model on eight simulated low-resource settings using European languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. In all experiments, their method equals or outperforms existing state-of-the-art approaches.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for Denoising Distant Supervision:** The primary contribution is the introduction of an explicit "noise layer" within a BiLSTM sequence tagger. This layer effectively models the confusion matrix between the true POS tags and the noisy tags generated by cross-lingual projection. This provides a principled way to jointly learn from both a small, clean dataset and a large, noisy one, by learning to "trust" the projected data selectively.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation on a Wide Range of Languages:** The paper provides a thorough empirical demonstration of the proposed model's effectiveness. The method is shown to consistently outperform strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art, across eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages. The breadth of this evaluation provides compelling evidence for the model's robustness and general applicability.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea of explicitly modeling the noise from distant supervision is both elegant and effective. Rather than treating projected labels as flawed ground truth or using them as simple features, the model learns a transformation that accounts for systematic projection errors. This is a more sophisticated and powerful way to utilize noisy, distantly-supervised data.

2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup and Strong Results:** The evaluation is extensive and convincing. By testing on eight simulated low-resource languages, the authors demonstrate the general effectiveness of their approach. Crucially, they also validate it on two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing its practical utility. The results consistently surpass several baselines, including a standard BiLSTM and the previous state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a), establishing a new benchmark for this task.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting accuracy scores. The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix (A) in Figures 3 and 4 provides valuable insight into the model's behavior. This analysis reveals the specific types of noise and tag confusion the model learns to correct for each language, strengthening the paper's claims and improving the reader's understanding of the method.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Practical Resource Trade-offs:** The paper's setup assumes the availability of a large parallel corpus is more feasible than annotating more than 1000 gold-standard tokens. While often true, this assumption is not universally applicable. The cost and effort of creating, cleaning, and aligning a parallel corpus can be substantial, and in some contexts, it might be more efficient to pay annotators for a few thousand more tokens. The paper would be strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of these resource trade-offs to avoid overstating the method's applicability and to better define the specific low-resource scenarios where it is most impactful.

2.  **Omission of Relevant and Recent Related Work:** The related work section could be more complete. There are several highly relevant recent papers on annotation projection and POS tagging for low-resource languages that are not discussed. For instance, work by Agić et al. (2015, ACL) on projection for "extremely" low-resource languages and Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) on joint many-task models for multilingual POS tagging would be valuable additions. Situating the current work within this broader, recent context would provide a clearer picture of its contribution to the field.
