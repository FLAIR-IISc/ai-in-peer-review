This paper presents an adaptation to the output layer of recurrent neural networks that enables learning from both gold-standard and projected annotations, particularly in low-resource settings. The authors augment the conventional softmax layer, which calculates a distribution over true labels, with a fully connected layer designed to model the noise-generation process. The product of these two layers yields a new output layer that represents the distribution over the observed noisy labels.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper. The proposed methodology is well-suited for the problem, straightforward, and conceptually neat. The paper demonstrates strong performance on part-of-speech tagging across a comprehensive set of eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages, leveraging a small amount of gold-annotated data alongside a much larger corpus of cross-lingually projected annotations. The approach is notably modular, suggesting it could be readily adopted by researchers addressing other NLP tasks in similar low-resource contexts.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental design is somewhat unconventional. While scenarios requiring a POS tagger to be built from only 1000 annotated tokens exist (for instance, in certain DARPA-funded evaluations), such situations are not common. A more compelling empirical validation would involve plotting the performance of the proposed method and its baselines as a function of the available gold annotation size. Such an analysis would clarify several key points: Is there a performance degradation when applying this method in settings with ample gold data? What is the approximate threshold of gold data below which this method offers a clear advantage? Does this threshold vary depending on the target language?

While the paper focuses on cross-lingual projections, noisy labels can also originate from other sources, such as crowd-sourcing, and may even use a different tag set from the gold data. Although the potential for this broader applicability is significant, the current experiments are confined to noisy labels from cross-lingual projections that share the same tag set as the gold standard.

The decision to assign equal weight in the training objective to both the gold and noisy data is unexpected. Given that the experimental setup assumes a small gold-standard corpus is available, an ablation study on the benefits of tuning the relative contributions of these two terms in the objective function would have been highly informative.

A point of ambiguity arises in line 357, where the projected data is defined as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and vector representations (\tilde{y}). The paper does not specify the form of this vector representation (e.g., is it a probability distribution over projected POS tags for that word type?). This raises the question of whether the method would remain effective if \tilde{y} were constructed from token-level projections rather than by aggregating predictions for the same word type. Furthermore, as the methodology relies on one-to-one word alignments, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is generated for words that lack an alignment.

On line 267, one of the two closing brackets should be an opening bracket.
