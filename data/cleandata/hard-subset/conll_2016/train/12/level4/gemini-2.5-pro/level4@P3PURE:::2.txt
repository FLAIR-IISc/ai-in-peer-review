The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) models, enabling the learning of model parameters from both gold-standard and projected annotations in a low-resource setting. This is achieved by augmenting the standard softmax output layer, which defines a distribution over gold labels, with an additional fully connected layer. This new layer models the noise generation process, transforming the gold label distribution into a distribution over the noisy, projected labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is well-motivated, simple, and elegant. The authors report strong results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages by training on a small set of gold-standard annotations combined with a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The modularity of the method suggests it could be readily adopted by researchers working on other low-resource NLP tasks.

Despite these strengths, there are a few areas for improvement:

1.  **Experimental Setup:** From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup is somewhat unusual. While scenarios requiring a POS tagger to be built from as few as 1000 annotated tokens do exist (e.g., in certain DARPA-sponsored evaluations), they are not commonplace. The empirical validation could have been strengthened by plotting the tagging accuracy of the proposed method and the baselines as a function of the gold annotation size. Such a plot would provide valuable insights into several key questions:
    *   Does this method hurt performance on a target language when a large number of gold annotations are available?
    *   What is the approximate amount of gold annotations below which this method becomes beneficial?
    *   Does the answer to this question depend on the target language?

2.  **Scope of Application:** The paper focuses exclusively on noisy labels derived from cross-lingual projection. However, the concept is applicable to noisy labels from other sources, such as crowdsourcing, which may even use a different tag set than the gold annotations. While this broader applicability is exciting, the paper's evaluation is limited to projected labels that share the same tag set as the gold data, leaving these other promising avenues unexplored.

3.  **Training Objective:** It is surprising that the training objective gives equal weight to the loss on gold and noisy labels. Given that a small gold-annotated corpus is available, it would have been informative to see an analysis of tuning the relative weights of the two terms in the objective function to determine if a different balance would be more beneficial.

4.  **Clarity on Projected Data Representation:** The description of the projected data (line 357) as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations (\tilde{y}) lacks clarity. The paper does not explicitly define the structure of \tilde{y} (e.g., whether it is a distribution over projected POS tags for a given word *type*). This ambiguity raises two questions. First, would the approach still be effective if \tilde{y} were constructed using projected POS tags at the token level, rather than by aggregating predictions for the same word type? Second, since the method only uses one-to-one word alignments, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is constructed for words that are never aligned.
