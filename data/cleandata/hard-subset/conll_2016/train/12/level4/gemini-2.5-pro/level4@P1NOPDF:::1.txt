I previously reviewed an earlier version of this paper, submitted as a short paper to ACL 2016. At that time, I noted a flaw in the experimental setup. I am pleased to see that the authors have fully addressed this issue in the current submission. As this was my primary condition for acceptance, I now see no obstacle to its publication and recommend that it be accepted.

I would, however, like to raise two minor points for the authors to consider for the final version.

First, I concur with another reviewer's point that the experimental setup may be somewhat artificial in the context of truly low-resource languages. The paper's model does not fully account for the practical realities of annotation, where the logistical costs of identifying and training annotators can be significantly higher than the cost of the annotation work itself. To avoid overstating the method's immediate applicability and to manage reader expectations, I recommend the authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion.

Second, the related work section could be strengthened by discussing relevant research in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages, such as Johannsen et al. (2016, ACL) and Agic et al. (2015, ACL). Including these papers would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

In summary, this is a nice contribution to the field, and I vote for acceptance.

Finally, the paper should indicate whether the data and resources used will be made publicly available. My evaluation assumes, in good faith, that they will be, in keeping with best practices for reproducible research.
