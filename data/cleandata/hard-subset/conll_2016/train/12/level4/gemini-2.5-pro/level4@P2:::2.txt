### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for low-resource sequence tagging that effectively combines a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a large amount of noisy data from distant supervision. The authors introduce a novel modification to a standard BiLSTM-based tagger: an explicit "noise layer." This layer, a single linear transformation, is trained to model the systematic errors and biases present in the noisy annotations (in this case, cross-lingually projected POS tags). The model is trained jointly on both the clean and noisy datasets, allowing the main tagger to learn from the gold data while the noise layer learns to "de-noise" the signal from the projected data. The approach is evaluated on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A simple and effective neural architecture for jointly learning from clean and noisy labeled data. The core novelty is the explicit modeling of the noise process via a dedicated "noise layer," which allows the model to learn to trust or discount the distant supervision signal appropriately.
2.  Strong empirical results on low-resource POS tagging, demonstrating that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming previous methods on ten languages in a setting with only 1000 gold-annotated tokens.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed method is appealingly simple. The noise layer is a single, fully connected layer added after the standard output layer of a neural tagger. This makes the approach easy to understand, implement, and integrate into existing sequence tagging models. This simplicity is a significant advantage for encouraging broader adoption.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on ten languages, consistently demonstrating superior performance over several strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM tagger and the previous state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a). The gains are consistent across both simulated and real low-resource scenarios, providing strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
3.  **General Applicability:** While demonstrated on POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, the proposed framework is general. It can be applied to other sequence tagging tasks and other sources of noisy labels, such as those from crowdsourcing or other distant supervision techniques. This modularity makes the work potentially impactful for a wide range of low-resource NLP problems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation is Limited to a Single Data Setting:** The experiments are conducted exclusively in a setting with exactly 1000 gold-annotated tokens. While this demonstrates the method's utility in an extreme low-resource scenario, it leaves open important practical questions. A more comprehensive evaluation would include a learning curve analysis, plotting performance for all models as the amount of gold-standard data varies. This would reveal the data threshold below which the proposed method is beneficial and, crucially, whether the noise layer might be detrimental when a larger amount of clean data is available.
2.  **Unjustified Equal Weighting of Loss Terms:** The training objective combines the cross-entropy loss from the gold data and the noisy data with equal weighting. Given the significant differences in quality and quantity between the two data sources (a few thousand clean tokens vs. hundreds of thousands of noisy tokens), this choice is not well-motivated. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included an ablation study that explored tuning the relative contribution of the two loss terms, for instance with a weighting hyperparameter. It is plausible that down-weighting the noisy loss could improve performance further.
3.  **Untested Generality:** The paper claims the technique is general, but the experiments are limited to a single type of noise (cross-lingual projection) and a single task (POS tagging), where the gold and projected tagsets are identical. While the authors mention in a footnote that the method supports mismatching tagsets, this is not demonstrated experimentally. Validating the approach on other noise sources (e.g., crowdsourced labels) or tasks would have made the claims of generality much more convincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your experiments are fixed at 1000 gold tokens. Could you provide insight into how the model's performance (relative to the baselines) changes as the amount of gold-annotated data increases? Is there a point at which the noise layer and joint training cease to be beneficial, or even become harmful?
2.  In the joint objective function, you give equal importance to the loss from the small gold dataset and the large noisy dataset. Have you experimented with a weighting hyperparameter to balance these two terms? If so, how sensitive is the model to this parameter?
3.  Section 3.1 describes the process for generating projected labels. Could you clarify how the projected label distribution `ỹ` is constructed for tokens that are unaligned or part of many-to-one alignments? While the text mentions using a tag frequency distribution for NULL alignments, a more concrete example of the resulting vector would be helpful for reproducibility.
