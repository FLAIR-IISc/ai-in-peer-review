- Summary: Proposes POS tagging for low-resource languages using a BiLSTM trained on small gold data plus large noisy cross-lingual projections with a learnable noise layer modeling projection errors; evaluated on eight simulated and two real languages with state-of-the-art results.

- Contribution 1: Novel neural sequence-tagging architecture that explicitly models noise from distant supervision via a learnable transformation/confusion matrix, enabling joint learning from clean and noisy data.

- Contribution 2: Strong empirical validation showing consistent improvements and new state-of-the-art performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda across comprehensive experiments.

- Strength 1: Elegant and effective noise modeling that learns a confusion matrix to down-weight unreliable projections and captures systematic projection errors, with informative visualizations.

- Strength 2: Convincing empirical results with thorough comparisons to baselines and prior SOTA across simulated and real low-resource scenarios.

- Strength 3: Method is generalizable and likely adaptable to other NLP tasks and types of noisy or distant supervision (e.g., crowdsourcing, heuristics).

- Weakness 1: Practicality assumptions are understated—approach relies on sizable parallel corpora, and the paper lacks discussion of cost trade-offs versus collecting more gold annotations.

- Weakness 2: Related work is incomplete; important prior works on annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages (e.g., Agic et al. 2015; Johannsen et al. 2016) are not discussed.

- Weakness 3: Unclear whether parallel corpora, alignments, and code will be released, raising reproducibility concerns.

- Question to authors: Will the parallel data, alignments, and experimental code be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility?
