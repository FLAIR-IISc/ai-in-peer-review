- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit, learned "noise layer" to model systematic errors in cross-lingual projection.
- Trains jointly on a small gold-standard corpus and a larger noisy projected corpus to selectively trust projected labels.
- Evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European-language settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Method equals or outperforms prior state-of-the-art, establishing a new benchmark across tested languages.
- Main contribution: introducing a principled noise-layer architecture that models the confusion matrix between true and projected tags.
- Strong empirical validation showing consistent improvements over baselines including standard BiLSTM and Duong et al. (2014a).
- Elegant, well-motivated approach that treats projected labels probabilistically rather than as flawed ground truth or mere features.
- Comprehensive experiments and real-language validation demonstrating practical utility and robustness.
- Insightful analysis via visualization of the learned noise matrix, revealing language-specific projection errors the model corrects.
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical resource trade-offs—creating and aligning parallel corpora can be costly compared to additional annotation.
- Weakness: related work omits several recent, relevant papers on projection and multilingual POS tagging that should be discussed.
- Question for authors: will code, processed data (including projected annotations), and model configurations be released for reproducibility?
