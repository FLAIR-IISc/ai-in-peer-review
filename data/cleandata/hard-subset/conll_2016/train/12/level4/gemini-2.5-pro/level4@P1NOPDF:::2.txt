This paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural networks to enable joint learning from both gold and projected (noisy) annotations, a common scenario for low-resource languages. The standard softmax layer, which produces a distribution over true labels, is augmented with a fully connected layer designed to model the noise generation process. The final output of this composite layer is a distribution over the observed noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission. The proposed method is well-motivated, simple, and elegant. The authors report impressive results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, training on a small set of gold annotations alongside a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations. The method's modularity is a key strength, suggesting it could be readily adopted by researchers working on other low-resource NLP tasks.

From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup could be broadened to better demonstrate the method's utility. While scenarios with only 1,000 gold-annotated tokens exist (e.g., in some DARPA-sponsored evaluations), they are not typical. The empirical validation would be significantly strengthened by an analysis that varies the amount of available gold data. A plot of tagging accuracy for the proposed method and baselines against the size of the gold training set would be highly informative. This would help clarify critical questions, such as: (1) Does the method harm performance when gold annotations are abundant? (2) At what approximate amount of gold data does the method's benefit diminish? (3) Does this threshold vary across different target languages?

The paper focuses on noisy labels generated via cross-lingual projection, but the approach could potentially be applied to noise from other sources, such as crowdsourcing, or even to scenarios where the noisy and gold annotations use different tag sets. While this possibility highlights the broad potential impact of the work, the experiments are confined to a single source of noise and a shared tag set. Exploring these other scenarios would make the paper's contributions even more compelling.

It is somewhat surprising that the training objective gives equal weight to the loss on gold data and noisy data. Given that a small, trusted gold corpus is available, an ablation study on the effect of weighting these two terms in the objective function would be valuable. It seems plausible that giving higher weight to the gold data could further improve performance.

The description of the projected data representation, \tilde{y}, could be clarified. Line 357 describes it as a vector representation for a word token (x_t), but its exact form is not specified (e.g., is it a distribution over projected POS tags aggregated at the word type level?). This ambiguity leads to further questions. For instance, would the approach be effective if \tilde{y} were constructed from token-level projections rather than type-level aggregates? Furthermore, since the projections rely on one-to-one word alignments, it is unclear how \tilde{y} is constructed for words that are never aligned in the parallel corpus.
