This paper proposes a novel modification to the output layer of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) models, enabling them to learn from both gold-standard and noisy projected annotations in low-resource scenarios. The standard softmax layer, which produces a distribution over true labels, is augmented by a fully connected layer designed to model the noise generation process. The product of these two components yields a final output layer that represents the distribution over the observed noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong and well-motivated submission. The proposed method is simple, elegant, and well-suited to the problem. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on POS tagging for eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages, successfully leveraging a small amount of gold data alongside a larger corpus of cross-lingually projected annotations. A key strength of the approach is its modularity, which should make it easily adaptable for other NLP tasks facing similar low-resource, noisy-label challenges.

My main suggestions for improvement relate to the experimental validation and clarification of certain details.

**Experimental Setup**
From a practical standpoint, the experimental setup focuses on a very specific scenario. While the chosen setting of only 1,000 gold-annotated tokens is relevant for extremely low-resource languages, it represents a somewhat narrow use case. A more comprehensive empirical validation would involve plotting the performance of the proposed method and the baselines as a function of the gold-annotated data size. Such an analysis would provide crucial insights into several key questions:
1.  Does the proposed method harm performance when a large amount of gold data is available?
2.  What is the approximate threshold of gold data below which this method provides a significant benefit?
3.  How does this threshold vary across different target languages?

**Generalizability of the Noise Model**
The paper focuses exclusively on noisy labels generated via cross-lingual projection, where the tag set is consistent with the gold data. However, the proposed framework seems general enough to handle noise from other sources, such as crowdsourcing, or even scenarios where the noisy and gold annotations use different tag sets. While exploring these avenues is likely beyond the scope of this single paper, acknowledging this broader applicability would strengthen the paper's contribution and potential impact.

**Objective Function Weighting**
It is somewhat surprising that the training objective gives equal weight to the loss on gold data and noisy data. Given that the gold annotations are, by definition, more reliable, it seems intuitive that they should contribute more to the overall objective, especially when they are scarce. An ablation study or a discussion on tuning the relative weights of these two loss components would be a valuable addition, clarifying whether a simple 1:1 weighting is indeed optimal.

**Clarity on Projected Data Representation**
The description of the projected data representation, \tilde{y} (line 357), could be more explicit. The paper states that it is a vector representation but does not specify its form (e.g., is it a one-hot vector of the most likely tag, or a full distribution over all possible tags?). Furthermore, it is unclear whether \tilde{y} is constructed at the token level or aggregated at the word-type level. Clarification is also needed on how \tilde{y} is handled for words that are never aligned in the parallel corpus, as the text mentions that only one-to-one alignments are used.
