- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM-based sequence tagging model that explicitly models noise from cross-lingual projection via an output noising layer and jointly trains on gold and projected tags.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a neural model that explicitly models the noise generation process in cross-lingual projected annotations.

- Contribution 2: Trains the model jointly on gold and projected annotations end-to-end with backpropagation.

- Contribution 3: Shows effectiveness on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource and two true low-resource languages using few gold and many projected annotations.

- Strength 1: Method is simple, elegant, and easily extensible to other low-resource NLP problems.

- Strength 2: Reports strong POS tagging results across multiple simulated and real low-resource languages.

- Strength 3: Modular design makes the approach likely useful to other researchers and tasks.

- Weakness 1: Unusual experimental setup; should report tagging accuracy as gold annotation size varies.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation limited to cross-lingual projections with the same tag set; lacks exploration of other noisy label sources or differing tag sets.

- Weakness 3: Training objective weights gold and noisy labels equally; paper doesn't explore tuning their relative contributions.

- Question 1: How does performance change when varying the size of gold annotations?

- Question 2: Can the method be used with noisy labels from other sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or with different tag sets than the gold annotations?

- Question 3: Is it beneficial to tune the contribution of the gold vs. noisy terms in the objective, and how does that affect performance?

- Question 4: How does the approach perform if projected-data vectors use token-level projected POS tags instead of aggregating by word type?

- Question 5: How are projected-data vectors constructed for words that are never aligned?
