The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The authors evaluate their system on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, using only 1000 tokens tagged with gold standard tags, along with more plentiful parallel data.

**Summary of Contributions**

1. The authors propose a new neural network model for sequence tagging in a low-resource language, which uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) and a noise layer to model the transformation between clean and noisy projected tags.
2. The model is trained jointly on gold and distant projected annotations, and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation.
3. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant, and can be easily integrated into existing NLP pipelines.
2. The authors report good results on POS tagging for both simulated and real low-resource languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. The method is modular enough that researchers working on different NLP problems in low-resource scenarios are likely to use it.

**Weaknesses**

1. The experimental setup is unusual, and it is unclear how the proposed method would perform with varying amounts of gold annotations.
2. The paper only shows results with cross-lingual projections with the same tag set, and it is unclear how the approach would work with other sources of noisy labels or different tag sets.
3. The proposed training objective gives equal weights to gold vs. noisy labels, and it is unclear whether tuning the contribution of the two terms in the objective function would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the proposed method perform with varying amounts of gold annotations? Would it hurt the performance on a target language if we use this method while having plenty of gold annotations?
2. Can the approach still work if we construct the vector representation of noisy labels using projected POS tags at the token level rather than aggregating all predictions for the same word type?
3. How do you construct the vector representation of noisy labels for words which are never aligned?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the noise layer and its properties.
2. The authors should consider adding more baselines or comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods.
3. The paper could be improved with more visualizations or illustrations of the proposed method and its components.
