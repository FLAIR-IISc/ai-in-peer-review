The paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, enabling the learning of model parameters from both gold and projected annotations in low-resource languages. This modification involves multiplying the traditional softmax output layer, which defines a distribution over possible labels, by a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, resulting in another output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this is a strong submission, presenting a simple and elegant method. The paper reports good results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, utilizing a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The proposed method is modular and can be easily applied to various NLP problems in low-resource scenarios.

However, the experimental setup is unusual from a practical standpoint. While there may be certain circumstances that require building a POS tagger with limited annotations (e.g., some DARPA-sponsored research projects), such scenarios are rare. A more comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method would involve plotting the tagging accuracy of the proposed method (and baselines) while varying the size of gold annotations. This plot would provide insights into questions such as: Does the method's performance degrade on the target language when there are ample gold annotations? What is the approximate amount of gold annotations below which this method is beneficial? Does this threshold depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels can be obtained from other sources (e.g., crowd sourcing) and in different tag sets than gold annotations. Although the potential impact of this is exciting, the paper only demonstrates results with cross-lingual projections using the same tag set.

It is surprising that the proposed training objective assigns equal weights to gold and noisy labels. Given the assumption of a small gold-annotated corpus, it would be informative to investigate whether tuning the contribution of the two terms in the objective function is beneficial.

In the description of projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y} (line 357), the paper does not explicitly specify the format of the vector representation (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for this word type). A natural question arises: Does the approach still work if \tilde{y} is constructed using projected POS tags at the token level (rather than aggregating all predictions for the same word type)? Additionally, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how to construct \tilde{y} for words that are never aligned.

Finally, a minor correction is needed: in line 267, one of the two closing brackets should be replaced with an opening bracket.
