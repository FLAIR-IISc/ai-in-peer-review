The paper presents a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The method is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The authors evaluate their system using only 1000 tokens tagged with gold standard tags, along with more plentiful parallel data, and achieve state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource settings, as well as two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

Main Contributions:

1. The authors propose a new neural network model for sequence tagging in a low-resource language, suitable for training with both a tiny gold standard annotated corpus and distant supervision using cross-lingual tag projection.
2. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) and incorporates a noise transformation layer to encode the mapping between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both simulated and real low-resource languages, outperforming existing methods.

Strengths:

1. The proposed method is simple and elegant, making it a strong submission.
2. The paper reports good results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages.
3. The method is modular enough that researchers working on different NLP problems in low-resource scenarios are likely to use it.

Weaknesses:

1. The experimental setup is unusual, and it would be beneficial to plot the tagging accuracy of the proposed method (and baselines) while varying the size of gold annotations.
2. The paper only shows results with cross-lingual projections with the same tag set, and it would be interesting to explore other sources of noisy labels and different tag sets.
3. The proposed training objective gives equal weights to gold vs. noisy labels, and it would be informative to report whether it is beneficial to tune the contribution of the two terms in the objective function.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the vector representation of the projected data, and whether the approach still works if we construct it using the projected POS tags at the token level?
2. How do you construct the projected data for words that are never aligned?
3. Have you explored tuning the contribution of the two terms in the objective function, and if so, what were the results?
