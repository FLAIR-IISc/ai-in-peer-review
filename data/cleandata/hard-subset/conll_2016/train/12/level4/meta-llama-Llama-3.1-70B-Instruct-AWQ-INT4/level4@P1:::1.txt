I reviewed this paper earlier, when it was an ACL 2016 short paper draft. At that point, it had a flaw in the experiment setup, which is now corrected. Since back then I suggested I'd be willing to accept the draft for another ACL event provided that the flaw is corrected, I now see no obstacles in doing so.

**Summary of the Paper**
The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct the errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) and is trained jointly on gold and distant projected annotations.

**Main Contributions**
I identify the following main contributions of this work, in order of strength:

1. The authors propose a deep learning model that explicitly models the transformation between tagsets as part of a more expressive deep learning neural network.
2. The authors integrate the model into a deep neural network and jointly train the model on both annotated and projected data to make the model learn from better supervisions.
3. The authors evaluate the approach on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating that their approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods on simulated languages, and achieves state-of-the-art results on the two real-world languages.

**Strengths**
My strongest arguments supporting the acceptance of this submission are:

1. The authors propose a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct the errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The authors evaluate the approach on a wide range of languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource languages. This demonstrates the robustness and effectiveness of the approach.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including an examination of the noise transformation matrix A. This provides valuable insights into the performance of the model.

**Weaknesses**
My strongest arguments against the acceptance of this submission are:

1. The experiment setup may be somewhat artificial, particularly in the context of real low-resource languages. The authors assume that a small amount of annotated data is available, but in reality, this may not always be the case.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of the related work in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages. This is an important area of research that is relevant to the proposed approach.
3. The authors do not indicate whether the data used in the experiments is publicly available. This is an important consideration for reproducibility and future research.

**Questions to Authors**
I have the following questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the experiment setup and how it relates to real-world low-resource languages?
2. Can you discuss the related work in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages and how it relates to the proposed approach?
3. Is the data used in the experiments publicly available? If so, can you provide a link or details on how to access it?

**Additional Comments**
I believe that this is a nice contribution to the field of natural language processing, and I vote to accept the paper. However, I hope that the authors will address the weaknesses mentioned above and provide more details on the experiment setup and related work.
