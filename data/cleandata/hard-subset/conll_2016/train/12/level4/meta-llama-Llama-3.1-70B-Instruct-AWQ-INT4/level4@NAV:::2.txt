This paper proposes a modification to the output layer of recurrent neural network models, allowing for the learning of model parameters from both gold and projected annotations in a low-resource language setting. The traditional softmax output layer, defining a distribution over possible labels, is further combined with a fully connected layer that models the noise generation process, resulting in an additional output layer representing the distribution over noisy labels.

Overall, this submission is strong. The proposed approach is suitable, simple, and elegant. The paper presents promising results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, utilizing a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training. The method's modularity makes it likely to be adopted by researchers working on various NLP problems in low-resource scenarios.

However, the experimental setup is somewhat unusual from a practical perspective. Although there are scenarios where building a POS tagger with minimal annotations is necessary (e.g., certain DARPA-sponsored research projects), such cases are rare. A more comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method could be achieved by plotting the tagging accuracy of the proposed method (and baselines) while varying the size of gold annotations. This plot would help address questions such as: Does the method's performance on a target language suffer when using it with an abundance of gold annotations? What is the approximate amount of gold annotations below which this method is beneficial? Does the answer depend on the target language?

Beyond cross-lingual projections, noisy labels could be obtained from other sources (e.g., crowd sourcing) and in different tag sets than gold annotations. While the additional potential impact is exciting, the paper only demonstrates results with cross-lingual projections using the same tag set.

It is surprising that the proposed training objective assigns equal weights to gold and noisy labels. Given the setup's assumption of a small gold-annotated corpus, it would be informative to report whether tuning the contribution of the two terms in the objective function is beneficial.

In the description of the projected data as pairs of word tokens (x_t) and their vector representations \tilde{y} (line 357), the paper does not explicitly specify the nature of the vector representation (e.g., a distribution over cross-lingually projected POS tags for this word type). A natural question to ask is whether the approach remains effective if \tilde{y} is constructed using projected POS tags at the token level (rather than aggregating all predictions for the same word type). Additionally, since only one-to-one word alignments are preserved, it is unclear how to construct \tilde{y} for words that are never aligned.

Lastly, in line 267, one of the two closing brackets should be replaced with an opening bracket.
