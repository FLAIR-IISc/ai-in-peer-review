**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The authors evaluate their system on eight simulated low-resource settings, as well as two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art on all settings.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a deep learning model that explicitly models the transformation between tagsets as part of a more expressive neural network.
2. The authors integrate the model into a joint training framework that combines both annotated and projected data.
3. The authors evaluate their approach on a range of low-resource languages and demonstrate its effectiveness in improving POS tagging accuracy.

**Strengths**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to handling noise in cross-lingual projection, which is a significant challenge in low-resource language processing.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of low-resource languages, including simulated and real-world languages.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors' experiment setup may be somewhat artificial, as it assumes that a small amount of annotated data is available for the low-resource language.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the costs and benefits of their approach, particularly in terms of the trade-off between annotation costs and performance gains.
3. The authors could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, including recent approaches to annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can the authors provide more details on the costs and benefits of their approach, particularly in terms of the trade-off between annotation costs and performance gains?
2. How do the authors plan to address the challenge of obtaining annotated data for low-resource languages, particularly in cases where such data is scarce or expensive to obtain?
3. Can the authors provide more analysis on the learned noise transformation matrices, including insights into the types of errors that are most common in cross-lingual projection?
