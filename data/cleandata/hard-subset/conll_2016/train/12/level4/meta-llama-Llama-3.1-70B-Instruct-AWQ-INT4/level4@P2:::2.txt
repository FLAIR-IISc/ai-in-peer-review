**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) network, which produces two types of output: gold tags generated directly from the hidden state of the neural network, and uncertain projected tags generated after applying a further linear transformation. This transformation, referred to as the output noising layer, encodes the mapping between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, and learns when and how much to trust the projected data.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a new neural network model that explicitly models the noise generation process in cross-lingual projected annotations.
2. The model is trained jointly on gold and projected annotations, and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages, making use of a small set of gold annotations and a large set of cross-lingually projected annotations for training.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method is apt, simple, and elegant, and can be easily extended to other NLP problems in low-resource scenarios.
2. The paper reports good results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two truly low-resource languages.
3. The method is modular enough that researchers working on different NLP problems in low-resource scenarios are likely to use it.

**Weaknesses**

1. The experimental setup is unusual, and it would be beneficial to plot the tagging accuracy of the proposed method (and baselines) while varying the size of gold annotations.
2. The paper only shows results with cross-lingual projections with the same tag set, and it would be interesting to explore the use of noisy labels from other sources (e.g., crowd sourcing) and in different tag sets than gold annotations.
3. The proposed training objective gives equal weights to gold vs. noisy labels, and it would be informative to report whether it is beneficial to tune the contribution of the two terms in the objective function.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the performance of the proposed method change when varying the size of gold annotations?
2. Can the proposed method be used with noisy labels from other sources (e.g., crowd sourcing) and in different tag sets than gold annotations?
3. Is it beneficial to tune the contribution of the two terms in the objective function, and if so, how does this affect the performance of the proposed method?
4. How does the approach work if we construct the vector representation of the projected data using the projected POS tags at the token level (rather than aggregating all predictions for the same word type)?
5. How do we construct the vector representation of the projected data for words that are never aligned?
