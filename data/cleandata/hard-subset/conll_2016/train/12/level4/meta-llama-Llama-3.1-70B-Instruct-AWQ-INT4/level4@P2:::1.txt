This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The main contributions of this work are: (1) the introduction of a noise transformation to encode the mapping between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, (2) the integration of this model into a deep neural network, and (3) the joint training of the model on both annotated and projected data.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel approach to addressing the problem of noise in cross-lingual projection. The use of a noise layer to model the transformation between tagsets is a significant contribution.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, with the method outperforming state-of-the-art algorithms on both simulated and real low-resource languages.
3. The paper provides a clear and concise presentation of the proposed model, including a detailed explanation of the noise transformation and the joint training objective.

Weaknesses:

1. The experiment setup, while improved from the previous draft, still relies on simulated low-resource languages. This may not accurately reflect the challenges of working with real low-resource languages, where the costs of finding and paying annotators may be significant.
2. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly in the area of annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the costs and challenges of working with real low-resource languages, and how your method addresses these challenges?
2. How does your approach compare to other methods for annotation projection, such as those proposed by Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015)?
3. Will the data used in the experiments be made publicly available?
