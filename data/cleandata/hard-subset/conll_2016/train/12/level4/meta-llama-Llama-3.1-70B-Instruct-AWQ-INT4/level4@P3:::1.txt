This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The authors frame this as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors introduce a deep learning model that explicitly models the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations, allowing for better exploitation of this rich data resource in learning NLP models in low-resource languages.
2. The proposed model uses a bidirectional LSTM tagger with a noise layer to handle noisy projected labels, and is trained jointly on gold and projected annotations.
3. The authors evaluate their approach on simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. The authors propose a novel and effective approach to handling noisy cross-lingual projected annotations, which is a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving state-of-the-art results on both simulated and real low-resource languages.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the noise layer, including visualization of the noise transformation matrices, which helps to understand the behavior of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The experimental setup, while corrected from the previous draft, may still be considered somewhat artificial, as it focuses on real low-resource languages but does not fully account for the costs of finding vs. paying annotators.
2. The related work discussion could be more comprehensive, including relevant lines of work in annotation projection for extremely low-resource languages.
3. The authors do not indicate whether the data used in the experiments is publicly available, which is an important consideration for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on the costs and challenges of obtaining gold-standard annotations for low-resource languages, and how your approach addresses these challenges?
2. How do you plan to extend your approach to other NLP tasks and low-resource languages, and what are the potential limitations of your method?
3. Will you make the data used in the experiments publicly available, and if so, how will it be shared?
