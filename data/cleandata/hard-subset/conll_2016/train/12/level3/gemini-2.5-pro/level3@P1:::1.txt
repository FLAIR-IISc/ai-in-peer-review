### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages. The authors leverage a common scenario where a small amount of gold-standard annotated data (e.g., 1000 tokens) is available alongside a much larger, but noisy, dataset created by projecting POS tags from a high-resource language (English) via word alignments. The core proposal is a Bi-directional LSTM model that explicitly models the noise from the projection process. This is achieved by adding a "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation matrix—that learns the confusion probabilities between the true tag distribution and the noisy projected tag distribution. The model is trained jointly on both the small clean corpus and the large noisy corpus. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, achieving state-of-the-art results in both settings.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Architecture for Learning from Noisy Labels:** The primary contribution is the introduction of an explicit, trainable "noise layer" within a neural sequence model. This layer provides an elegant and principled way to jointly model clean and noisy supervision sources. By learning a transformation matrix that captures the systematic errors of the distant supervision (cross-lingual projection), the model can effectively "de-noise" the training signal from the projected data before updating the shared underlying BiLSTM parameters. This is a more sophisticated approach than simply mixing the data or using heuristics.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results for Low-Resource POS Tagging:** The paper presents a convincing and thorough evaluation, demonstrating that the proposed model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM and the previous state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a), across ten different languages. The gains are particularly notable on the two real low-resource languages, establishing a new state of the art for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda under this setup.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The core idea of isolating and explicitly modeling the noise from the projection process is very compelling. The noise layer is a simple yet powerful mechanism that is well-integrated into the end-to-end training framework. The visualizations of the learned noise matrix A in Figures 3 and 4 are particularly insightful, providing qualitative evidence that the model is indeed learning meaningful and language-specific noise patterns.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. By evaluating on both simulated settings (allowing for direct comparison with prior work) and real-world low-resource languages, the authors make a strong case for the model's effectiveness and generalizability. The choice of baselines is appropriate and covers the necessary ablations to demonstrate the value of both the BiLSTM architecture and, crucially, the proposed noise layer.

3.  **Well-Written and Clear Paper:** The paper is very clear, well-structured, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the model is described precisely (Figure 2 is a helpful illustration), and the results are presented in a straightforward manner. I also commend the authors for successfully addressing an experimental setup flaw I noted in a previous version of this review; this diligence strengthens my confidence in the final results and the overall quality of the work. Having previously offered conditional acceptance, I now see no major obstacles to publication.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Discussion of Relevant Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging and discussing some highly relevant recent work on annotation projection and learning from noisy data. Specifically, work by Johannsen et al. (2016) on joint morphological and syntactic analysis and Agić et al. (2015) on joint POS tagging and parsing in low-resource settings also deals with cross-lingual projection and modeling label uncertainty. Situating the current work with respect to these other recent approaches would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

2.  **Practicality of the Low-Resource Assumption:** The paper operates under the common assumption that large parallel corpora are more readily available for low-resource languages than even a small amount of annotated data. As another reviewer has noted, the cost-benefit of this scenario can be debatable in practice. The effort required to find, clean, and process a large parallel corpus and then run word alignment might, in some cases, exceed the cost of hiring a non-expert for a few hours to annotate the 1000+ tokens needed for a supervised baseline (as shown by Garrette and Baldridge, 2013). While this does not diminish the technical contribution of the paper, acknowledging this practical caveat would make the motivation more nuanced and robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Data and Code Availability:** The reproducibility of this work, especially on the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda experiments, depends on the specific parallel corpora and the resulting projected data. Will the processed data and the model implementation be made publicly available? My positive evaluation was made with the assumption that the work is reproducible, and a public release would be a significant contribution to the community working on low-resource languages.
