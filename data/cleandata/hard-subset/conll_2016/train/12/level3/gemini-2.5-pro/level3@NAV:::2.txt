### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel method for low-resource POS tagging that effectively combines a small amount of gold-annotated data with a larger corpus of noisy labels generated via cross-lingual projection. The core idea is to augment a standard BiLSTM tagger with an additional fully-connected "noise" layer, which explicitly models the transformation between the model's internal predictions and the noisy projected labels. This allows the model to be trained jointly on both clean and noisy data.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple and elegant method for modeling supervision noise:** The primary contribution is the proposed noise layer itself. This architectural modification is intuitive, modular, and allows the model to learn a transformation matrix that captures the systematic errors and biases present in the distantly supervised data.
2.  **Strong empirical results for low-resource POS tagging:** The paper provides a convincing empirical evaluation, demonstrating that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark of eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, using as few as 1000 gold-annotated tokens.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Modularity:** The proposed noise layer is a compelling and straightforward solution to a common problem. Instead of relying on complex heuristics to clean projected data, it learns to model the noise directly. Its modularity is a significant advantage, suggesting it could be easily integrated into other neural architectures and applied to different tasks beyond POS tagging.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The method consistently outperforms strong baselines and prior state-of-the-art results across all ten languages tested. The gains on the real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, are particularly noteworthy and validate the practical utility of the approach.
3.  **Realistic Low-Resource Focus:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. The experimental setup, which relies on only ~1000 tokens of gold data, realistically simulates the conditions under which NLP tools are often developed for low-resource languages, making the results highly relevant.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Analysis on the Impact of Gold Data Size:** The paper's most significant weakness is the absence of an analysis showing how performance varies with the amount of available gold data. The fixed setting of 1000 tokens is interesting but provides no insight into when the method is most beneficial or if it might even be harmful with more (or less) gold data. A plot of accuracy vs. gold data size would be critical for understanding the method's practical trade-offs.
2.  **Unjustified Design Choices and Missing Ablations:** The objective function gives equal weight to the loss from gold and noisy labels. This is a strong, non-obvious assumption that warrants investigation. The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study that explores tuning the relative contribution of these two terms.
3.  **Limited Scope of Noise and Task Evaluation:** While the authors claim general applicability, the evaluation is confined to noise from cross-lingual projection with a shared universal tagset. Its effectiveness on other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourced labels, other distant supervision heuristics) or in scenarios with differing source/target tagsets remains unexplored.
4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The precise representation of projected labels (`\tilde{y}`), especially for words that are unaligned or multiply aligned, is not sufficiently detailed, making reproducibility difficult.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide an analysis of how performance is affected by varying the amount of gold-annotated data? At what point does the benefit from the noisy projected data diminish or disappear?
2.  Have you experimented with a weighting hyperparameter to balance the gold and projected data loss terms in the objective function? It seems unlikely that an equal weighting is optimal across all settings.
3.  Could you please clarify how a projected label distribution `\tilde{y}` is constructed for tokens that are not one-to-one aligned in the parallel data?
