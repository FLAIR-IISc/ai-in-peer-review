### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel method for part-of-speech (POS) tagging in low-resource settings by effectively combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a larger corpus of noisy data generated via cross-lingual projection. The core of the proposed model is a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) tagger augmented with an explicit "noise layer." This layer, a learnable linear transformation matrix, is designed to model the systematic errors and biases inherent in the projection process. The model is trained jointly on both the clean gold data and the noisy projected data. The gold data trains the base BiLSTM directly, while the projected data trains the BiLSTM through the noise layer, effectively teaching the model to "de-noise" the projected labels' error signals during backpropagation. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art or superior performance compared to existing methods.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling supervision noise:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," a single fully-connected layer that transforms the model's output probabilities before comparing them to noisy labels. This is an elegant and lightweight mechanism for explicitly modeling the confusion between true tags and noisy projected tags. By learning this transformation jointly with the base tagger, the model learns to trust or distrust the projected data in a principled manner, extracting a cleaner training signal from a noisy source.

2.  **State-of-the-art results in low-resource POS tagging:** The paper presents a strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation. On eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages, the proposed BiLSTM with a noise layer consistently outperforms strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on various data combinations and the previous state-of-the-art method of Duong et al. (2014a). This demonstrates the practical effectiveness and robustness of the proposed technique.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Proposed Model:** The idea of adding an explicit, learnable noise transformation layer is both novel and compellingly simple. Instead of relying on complex heuristics or filtering steps to clean up projected data, this method integrates the noise-handling directly into the model's architecture and learning objective. This approach requires very few additional parameters (a K×K matrix, where K is the number of tags) and is easily interpretable through visualization of the learned transformation matrix, as shown in Figures 3 and 4.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across a wide range of languages. The consistent improvements over all baselines and prior work in both simulated and real low-resource settings provide strong evidence for the method's efficacy. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, using a realistic setup with a small amount of annotated data, highlights the practical value of this work for the low-resource NLP community.

3.  **Generality and Potential for Broader Impact:** Although demonstrated on POS tagging, the proposed noise layer is a modular component that is not specific to the task or the BiLSTM architecture. This framework for combining a small clean dataset with a large noisy one could be readily applied to other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., Named Entity Recognition) or even other NLP tasks where distant supervision is common (e.g., relation extraction). The approach could also be adapted to handle other sources of noise, such as labels from crowdsourcing or other automatic annotation tools.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Analysis on the Impact of Gold Data Size:** The paper's experiments are fixed on a setting with approximately 1000 gold-annotated tokens. While this is a realistic low-resource scenario, it leaves an important question unanswered: how does the model's performance and the benefit of the noise layer change as the amount of available gold data varies? A learning curve plotting accuracy against the number of gold training tokens (e.g., 100, 500, 1000, 5000) would provide critical insight into the regimes where this method is most beneficial. One would expect the utility of the projected data to diminish as the gold dataset grows, and quantifying this trade-off is essential for understanding the method's applicability.

2.  **Equal Weighting in the Joint Objective Function:** The loss function gives equal importance to the gold data and the projected data terms (after normalization by dataset size). This is a strong assumption that the signal from both sources is equally valuable. It is more common to introduce a hyperparameter to control the relative contribution of the auxiliary (noisy) loss term. An ablation study or analysis exploring the effect of weighting the projected data loss would strengthen the paper by showing whether this choice is optimal or if performance could be further improved by tuning this balance.

3.  **Limited Exploration of Generality:** The authors suggest the method is general, but the experiments are confined to a single type of noise (cross-lingual projection) where the source and target tagsets are identical (Universal POS tags). The paper briefly mentions that the noise matrix `A` could be rectangular to handle mismatched tagsets, but this is a significant use case that is not empirically validated. Evaluating the model on a different noise source (e.g., crowd-sourced labels) or in a scenario with differing tagsets would substantially bolster the claims of generality.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide an analysis of how the model's performance changes as the amount of annotated gold data is varied (e.g., from 100 to 5000 tokens)? Specifically, at what point (if any) does adding the noisy projected data via the noise layer cease to be beneficial or even become harmful?

2.  Have you experimented with a weighting hyperparameter to balance the influence of the gold and projected data terms in the loss function? Your current objective implicitly weights them equally, and it would be interesting to see if tuning this weight provides further improvements.

3.  Could you clarify the exact representation of the projected labels `ỹ`? The paper states that for unaligned or multiply-aligned tokens, a distribution over tags is used. Is `ỹ` a soft label vector in this case? If so, how is the cross-entropy term in the objective function computed with a soft target distribution?
