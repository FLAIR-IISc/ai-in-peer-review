### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, a setting where only a very small amount of gold-standard annotated data is available. The authors leverage a larger, but noisy, source of supervision obtained by projecting POS tags from a resource-rich language (English) across word alignments in a parallel corpus. The central idea is a novel deep learning model that learns to explicitly model the noise and biases inherent in this projection process. The proposed architecture uses a standard BiLSTM for sequence tagging, but adds a "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation—that maps the model's internal predictions to the noisy projected labels. The model is trained jointly with a dual objective: to predict the correct tags on the small clean dataset and to predict the noisy tags (after the transformation) on the larger projected dataset. This allows the model to learn a robust tagger while simultaneously learning the systematic error patterns of the distant supervision source.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel and elegant method for jointly learning from clean and noisy supervision.** The introduction of an explicit, learnable noise layer is a principled approach to handling systematic errors in distant supervision. This is a generalizable idea that could be applied to other sequence tasks and other sources of noisy labels beyond cross-lingual projection.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages. The proposed model consistently equals or outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods, setting a new benchmark for this low-resource POS tagging scenario.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Principled Approach:** The core contribution—the noise layer—is a simple, well-motivated, and effective innovation. Instead of using heuristics to filter projected data or treating it as equally valid as gold data, this method explicitly models the noise-generating process. The learned transformation matrix `A` provides an interpretable model of the confusion between tags during projection, as clearly demonstrated in the analysis (Figures 3 and 4). This is a more elegant and powerful way to leverage noisy data than simply using projected tags as features.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are comprehensive and the results are consistently strong across all 10 languages. The proposed model (BiLSTM+noise layer) significantly outperforms strong baselines, including a BiLSTM trained on the union of data and the previous state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a). The gains on the two real low-resource languages, Malagasy (+1.0%) and Kinyarwanda (+4.2%), are particularly impressive and demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.
3.  **Generalizability:** While applied to POS tagging, the framework of jointly training on gold data and distantly-supervised data via a noise-modeling layer is highly general. It could readily be adapted to other NLP tasks like Named Entity Recognition or parsing, and to other forms of distant supervision where systematic noise is expected, such as crowdsourced annotations or rule-based labeling. This significantly broadens the potential impact of the work.
4.  **Good Analysis:** The paper provides visualizations of the learned noise matrices, which offer valuable insight into what the model has learned about the projection process for different languages. For instance, the analysis highlights the stronger diagonal for Malagasy vs. Kinyarwanda, correlating the quality of the parallel data with the learned noise patterns and final performance. This analysis adds depth and credibility to the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be addressed to improve it further.

1.  **Missing Discussion of Recent Related Work:** The paper would benefit from positioning its contribution with respect to more recent work on cross-lingual learning and annotation projection. For example, work by Agic et al. (2015) on pre-training with synthetic data and Johannsen et al. (2016) on joint multi-task models for cross-lingual tasks touch upon similar themes of leveraging cross-lingual signals. A brief discussion of how this work differs from or complements these approaches would strengthen the related work section.
2.  **Practicality of the Low-Resource Scenario:** The experimental setup assumes the availability of a large parallel corpus, which itself can be a significant resource to acquire and process (e.g., running GIZA++). In some real-world scenarios, the effort required to obtain this parallel data might be comparable to the cost of annotating a few thousand more gold-standard tokens. A brief comment acknowledging this practical trade-off would help to better frame the applicability of the method.
3.  **Data and Code Availability:** The paper does not mention whether the code and processed data (alignments, projected tags) will be released. Given the importance of reproducibility in computational linguistics, making these resources available would be a significant service to the community and would allow other researchers to build directly upon this strong contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please discuss how your explicit noise modeling approach compares to other recent techniques for cross-lingual transfer, such as the pre-training strategy of Agic et al. (2015) or the multi-task learning frameworks used by Johannsen et al. (2016)?
2.  Could you comment on the practical trade-offs in a real-world deployment? Specifically, how does the human and computational effort of acquiring and processing a large parallel corpus compare to simply annotating a larger gold-standard training set (e.g., 5,000 tokens instead of 1,000)?
3.  Do you plan to make your code, word alignments, and projected datasets publicly available? I believe this would be highly valuable for the community for ensuring reproducibility and facilitating future research in this area.
