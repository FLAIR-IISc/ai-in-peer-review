### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a neural network approach for Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging in low-resource languages. The method leverages a small amount of gold-standard annotated data alongside a much larger, noisy dataset created by projecting POS tags from a high-resource language (English) via word alignments. The core of the proposed model is a bidirectional LSTM that is trained jointly on both data sources. To handle the noisy projected labels, the authors introduce a novel "noise layer"—a trainable linear transformation—that explicitly models the systematic errors and biases between the model's internal predictions and the noisy projected tags. The model is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages from Europarl and two genuine low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in both settings.

**Contributions of this work**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel and elegant method for integrating distant supervision from cross-lingual projection into a neural sequence tagger. The explicit "noise layer" is a simple yet powerful mechanism for learning to correct for systematic projection errors, allowing the model to effectively leverage large amounts of noisy data without being overwhelmed by it.
2.  A secondary, but significant, contribution is the strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The paper shows consistent improvements over several strong baselines, including the prior state-of-the-art, across ten different languages. The results on the real low-resource languages are particularly compelling and validate the practical utility of the approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The idea of explicitly modeling the transformation between clean and noisy labels with a dedicated, trainable layer is well-motivated and cleanly implemented. It provides a principled way to combine supervision from gold and projected data within a single end-to-end trainable model. This is a more direct approach than prior work that relies on feature engineering or heuristics to manage projection noise.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming the strong baseline from Duong et al. (2014a) on average across the eight simulated languages and on both real low-resource languages. The performance gains are consistent and demonstrate that the noise layer is effective at extracting a useful signal from the projected data.
3.  **Responsiveness to Prior Feedback:** I previously reviewed this paper and offered a conditional acceptance based on a flaw in the experimental setup. The authors have now fully addressed this issue, and the current evaluation appears sound and rigorous. This strengthens my confidence in the reported results. With this correction, I see no further obstacles to acceptance.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The visualization of the learned noise transformation matrix `A` in Figures 3 and 4 provides valuable insight into the model's behavior. It clearly shows how the model learns language-specific error patterns, such as the strong diagonal for relatively clean projections (e.g., Malagasy) versus the more diffuse patterns for noisier ones (e.g., Kinyarwanda), which aligns with the quality of the available parallel data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Idealized Low-Resource Setting:** The paper's framing assumes that parallel data is "plentiful" while gold annotation is scarce. As another reviewer has pointed out, for truly low-resource languages, finding and processing high-quality parallel corpora can be a significant cost and challenge in itself, potentially comparable to annotating a small corpus. The paper would be more balanced if it acknowledged this caveat about the practicalities of data acquisition in its problem setup. This is a minor point but would add important context for readers.
2.  **Missing Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section is reasonable but could be strengthened by discussing other recent approaches to improving annotation projection. Specifically, work by Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015) explores related ideas in cross-lingual transfer and would provide valuable context for the contributions of this paper. I encourage the authors to incorporate a brief discussion of these and other relevant recent works in the final version.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The paper's reproducibility and its value to the low-resource NLP community would be greatly enhanced if the code and data were made publicly available. This includes the processed parallel corpora, word alignments, and final data splits used for the experiments, especially for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda. My positive evaluation was made with the assumption that these resources would be released. Could you please confirm in the final version whether the data and code will be made available?
