1. Proposes a simple learnable linear "noise layer" on top of a BiLSTM to model confusion between true POS tags and noisy cross-lingual projections.  
2. Jointly trains on a small gold-annotated set and a large noisy projected set to learn to "de-noise" projected labels.  
3. Evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, reporting state-of-the-art results.  
4. Main contribution: a novel, interpretable, and architecturally simple noise-layer that parameterizes the noise process.  
5. Main contribution: strong, comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent gains over strong baselines and prior SOTA.  
6. Strength: methodological elegance and ease of implementation, with informative visualizations of learned noise matrices.  
7. Strength: convincing experimental breadth and consistent improvements across multiple languages and baselines.  
8. Strength: method-agnostic design with potential applicability to other sequence labeling tasks and noisy supervision sources.  
9. Weakness: no analysis of sensitivity to varying amounts of gold data (e.g., how benefits change with more than ~1k tokens).  
10. Weakness: equal weighting of gold and noisy losses is unjustified and unexamined; no ablation on reweighting (λ).  
11. Weakness: limited evaluation scope—no experiments on mismatched tagsets or other noisy sources like crowdsourcing.  
12. Question: clarify the exact representation of projected labels (one-hot vs. dense distributions) and how cross-entropy handles mixed targets.  
13. Question: did you try a balancing hyperparameter λ between gold and projected loss terms, and what were the results?  
14. Question: what is the computational cost of joint training (time per epoch) compared to the BiLSTM Ann+Proj baseline?
