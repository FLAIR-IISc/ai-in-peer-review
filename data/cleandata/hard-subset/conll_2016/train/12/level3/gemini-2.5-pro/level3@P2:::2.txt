### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages by proposing a method to combine a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with a larger, noisier corpus created via cross-lingual projection. The core of the proposed method is a Bi-directional LSTM model augmented with a "noise layer." This layer is a learnable linear transformation applied to the model's output probabilities before comparing them to the noisy projected labels. This allows the model to be trained jointly on both the clean and noisy data, explicitly learning the systematic errors (or "noise") introduced by the projection process. The model is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating superior performance over existing methods.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective method for modeling supervision noise:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a "noise layer," which is an elegant, learnable mechanism for explicitly modeling the confusion between true labels and noisy labels from a distant supervision source. This allows for end-to-end joint training on clean and noisy data, where the error signal from the noisy data is effectively "de-noised" before being used to update the underlying model. This approach is conceptually simple and appears highly effective.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on low-resource POS tagging:** The paper presents a strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation, showing that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on a wide range of languages. The consistent improvements over strong baselines, including prior work using the same data setup, validate the effectiveness of the proposed noise-modeling technique.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Proposed Model:** The method of adding a learnable linear transformation (the matrix A) to the output layer is a simple yet powerful way to handle the noise inherent in distant supervision. Rather than treating projected labels as infallible or employing complex pre-processing heuristics, the model explicitly learns the confusion matrix between the true underlying tags and the noisy projected ones. This allows the model to leverage the large quantity of noisy data much more effectively. The visualizations of the learned matrix A in Figures 3 and 4 are particularly insightful, providing a clear interpretation of the noise patterns learned by the model for different languages.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their approach on a wide range of languages: eight simulated low-resource settings using the Europarl corpus and two genuinely low-resource languages. The model ("BiLSTM+noise layer") consistently equals or surpasses strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art from Duong et al. (2014a). The inclusion of several baselines, particularly the naive `BiLSTM Ann+Proj`, effectively demonstrates that simply mixing the gold and projected data is suboptimal and highlights the importance of the proposed noise layer.
3.  **High Potential for Generalization:** Although demonstrated on POS tagging using cross-lingual projection, the noise layer is model-agnostic and could be integrated into any probabilistic classifier. The technique seems directly applicable to a wide range of low-resource NLP tasks that rely on other forms of noisy supervision, such as crowdsourcing (modeling annotator disagreement) or other distant supervision methods. This modularity makes the contribution potentially impactful for a broad set of problems beyond the specific application in this paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Impact of Gold Data Size:** The experiments are conducted using a fixed, very small amount of annotated data (1000 tokens). While this successfully simulates an extreme low-resource scenario, it represents only a single data point. A crucial analysis is missing: how does the model's performance—and the marginal benefit of the noise layer—change as the amount of gold data increases? It is plausible that this method is most beneficial when gold data is extremely scarce and that its advantage diminishes as more clean data becomes available. A plot showing accuracy vs. the number of gold annotated tokens (e.g., 1k, 5k, 10k) would significantly strengthen the paper by defining the conditions under which this method is most effective.
2.  **Unexplored Hyperparameters in the Loss Function:** The joint loss function gives equal weight to the loss from the projected data and the annotated data (by averaging over the tokens in each respective dataset). This is a strong, non-obvious design choice. It seems more intuitive to introduce a hyperparameter to control the relative influence of the two terms (e.g., `L = -λ * L_proj - (1-λ) * L_gold`). Given the vast difference in quality and quantity between the two data sources, tuning this weight could be critical for optimal performance. The paper does not explore this or provide a justification for the implicit equal weighting.
3.  **Scope of Noise Modeling is Narrowly Evaluated:** The paper presents the method as a general way to handle noisy annotations but only evaluates it on one specific type of noise: cross-lingual projection where the source and target tagsets are identical (the universal tagset). The authors mention in a footnote that the matrix A could be made rectangular to handle mismatched tagsets, but this important capability is not demonstrated. Furthermore, the applicability to other noise sources like crowdsourcing, which might have different error patterns, is claimed but not tested. Evaluating on at least one other noise source or demonstrating the mismatched tagset case would make the claims of generality far more convincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In your joint loss function, you effectively give equal importance to the gold and projected data terms by averaging over each corpus separately. Have you experimented with a weighting hyperparameter to control the relative contribution of the noisy data loss? It seems possible that down-weighting the noisy term could lead to further improvements.
2.  Your experiments are fixed at 1000 gold-annotated tokens. Could you provide an analysis showing how your model's performance compares to the baselines as the amount of gold data increases? This would help clarify the regimes in which your method provides the most benefit.
3.  Could you clarify how the projected label `ỹ_t` is represented for training? Section 3.1 states that for tokens with multiple or no alignments, you assign a distribution. Is `ỹ_t` a one-hot vector for single-aligned tokens and a probability distribution otherwise? If so, how exactly is the cross-entropy loss `⟨ỹ_t, log softmax(A*o_t)⟩` calculated when `ỹ_t` is a distribution?

### Additional Comments
-   There appears to be a typo in the citation for Duong et al. (2014a) and (2014b); they seem to refer to the same paper.
-   The two-stage training process (pre-training on gold data first) seems critical. It would be useful to include an ablation study showing the performance if the model is trained jointly from scratch, to quantify the importance of this pre-training step.
