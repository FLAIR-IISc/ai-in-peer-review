- Task/setup: Tackles low-resource POS tagging using a small gold dataset plus a larger noisy set from cross-lingual projection.
- Method: Proposes a BiLSTM tagger with a learnable linear "noise layer" that maps clean predictions to noisy projected labels, trained jointly on both data sources.
- Contribution: Introduces a principled, generalizable noise-layer to model systematic errors in distant supervision.
- Contribution: Demonstrates strong empirical gains, outperforming prior state-of-the-art on 10 languages (including real low-resource languages).
- Strength: The noise-layer is simple, interpretable (learned confusion matrix) and more principled than heuristic filtering.
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments show consistent improvements across simulated and real languages.
- Strength: Framework is generalizable to other sequence tasks and noisy supervision contexts (e.g., NER, crowdsourcing).
- Strength: Good analysis and visualizations of learned noise matrices that explain performance differences across languages.
- Weakness: Lacks discussion of more recent related work on cross-lingual transfer (e.g., Agic et al. 2015, Johannsen et al. 2016).
- Weakness: Assumes availability of large parallel corpora and alignments, which may be costly or impractical compared to more annotation.
- Weakness: No statement about releasing code, alignments, or projected datasets for reproducibility.
- Question: How does the noise-modeling approach compare empirically to pre-training with synthetic data (Agic et al.) or multi-task cross-lingual models (Johannsen et al.)?
- Question: What are the practical trade-offs (human/computational cost) of obtaining parallel data versus annotating more gold data?
- Question: Do you plan to release code, word alignments, and projected datasets to support reproducibility?
