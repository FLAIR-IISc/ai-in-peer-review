This paper presents a neural network architecture for part-of-speech (POS) tagging in low-resource settings, leveraging a small gold-standard corpus and a larger, noisy corpus created via cross-lingual projection. The core of the method is a BiLSTM augmented with a "noise layer"—a linear transformation that explicitly models the systematic errors between the projected labels and the true labels. By jointly training on both clean and noisy data, the model learns to effectively de-noise the signal from the distant supervision. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on eight simulated and two genuine low-resource languages.

I see the primary contributions of this work as:
1.  The introduction of the noise layer, a simple yet elegant mechanism for explicitly modeling and compensating for noise in distant supervision within a neural framework.
2.  A strong empirical validation showing that this method significantly outperforms prior work on a wide range of languages, demonstrating its practical utility for low-resource POS tagging.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The noise layer is a clever contribution. Instead of simply mixing data or using heuristics, it provides a principled way to learn the error patterns of the projection process. This approach is generalizable to other tasks that rely on distant supervision.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are comprehensive and the results are convincing. The method consistently equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art across all ten languages, including two real-world, low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).
3.  **Improved Manuscript:** I previously reviewed this paper and offered a conditional acceptance based on a flaw in the experimental setup. The authors have fully corrected this issue in the revised draft, significantly strengthening the validity of their conclusions. I now see no obstacles to acceptance.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper should acknowledge and discuss relevant prior work on annotation projection, specifically Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015). Situating the proposed method relative to these works would provide a more complete picture of the landscape.
2.  **Practical Applicability Caveat:** The assumption that large parallel corpora are more easily obtained than a small amount of direct annotation may not hold for all truly low-resource languages, where the cost of finding and aligning such data can be substantial. The paper would be strengthened by briefly acknowledging this caveat.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  My positive evaluation assumed that the code and data used, particularly for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, will be made publicly available to ensure reproducibility. Could the authors please clarify their data and code release plans in the final version?
