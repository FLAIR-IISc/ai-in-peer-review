### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of part-of-speech (POS) tagging for low-resource languages, where only a small amount of gold-annotated data is available alongside a larger corpus of parallel text. The authors propose a method to leverage noisy POS tags generated via cross-lingual projection from a resource-rich language (English). The core idea is a simple but elegant modification to a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger. They introduce a "noise layer"—a learnable linear transformation matrix—that is applied to the network's output probabilities before computing the loss on the noisy, projected data. This allows the model to be jointly trained on both the small, clean dataset and the large, noisy dataset, effectively learning a mapping from the model's predicted "clean" tag distribution to the observed noisy distribution. This approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results in both settings.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel and simple method for modeling noise in distant supervision.** The primary contribution is the introduction of a learnable "noise layer" (a single fully-connected layer without a non-linearity) that explicitly models the confusion matrix between true labels and noisy labels from a distant source. This is an architecturally simple and intuitive way to allow a model to jointly learn from both gold and noisy data, effectively learning to "de-noise" the training signal from the projected labels.

2.  **Strong empirical results for low-resource POS tagging.** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across ten languages, demonstrating that the proposed method consistently and significantly outperforms strong baselines, including a standard BiLSTM trained on gold data, projected data, or a naive union of the two, as well as prior state-of-the-art work on the same low-resource task setup.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed noise layer is a compellingly simple and well-motivated idea. By adding a single learnable matrix `A`, the model directly parameterizes the noise process, which is more explicit and interpretable than simply treating the projected labels as true labels or using them as features. This simplicity makes the method easy to understand, implement, and likely to be adopted. The visualizations of the learned `A` matrices in Figures 3 and 4 are particularly effective at providing insight into the learned noise patterns.

2.  **Convincing and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and the results are strong. The method is shown to be effective not only on eight simulated low-resource settings but also on two real-world low-resource languages, where it achieves new state-of-the-art performance. The consistent gains over multiple strong baselines (MaxEnt, BiLSTM variants, and the previous SOTA from Duong et al., 2014a) provide compelling evidence for the efficacy of the noise layer.

3.  **High Potential for Broader Impact:** Although demonstrated on POS tagging, the proposed method is model-agnostic and not specific to cross-lingual projection. This modular approach could easily be applied to other sequence labeling tasks (e.g., NER) and other forms of noisy supervision, such as labels from crowdsourcing or heuristic-based systems. This generality significantly increases the potential impact of the work beyond its immediate application.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Analysis on Sensitivity to Gold Data Size:** The paper's core motivation is leveraging distant supervision when gold data is scarce (e.g., ~1000 tokens). However, all experiments are conducted with a fixed amount of gold data. A crucial missing analysis is how the model's performance (and the advantage of the noise layer) changes as the amount of gold annotation varies. It is unclear if the method is still beneficial with 5,000 gold tokens, or if it might become harmful compared to a standard supervised model. A plot of accuracy vs. gold data size for the proposed model and key baselines would greatly strengthen the paper by defining the conditions under which this method is most valuable.

2.  **Questionable Equal Weighting in the Loss Function:** The joint objective function implicitly gives equal importance to the loss from a gold-annotated token and a noisily projected token by simply averaging the loss over each respective dataset. This seems counter-intuitive, as the gold data is by definition more reliable. The paper does not justify this choice or explore the effect of introducing a hyperparameter to tune the relative weight of the two loss terms. Such an ablation study is standard for multi-task or joint-learning objectives and its absence is a notable omission.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluated Noise Scenarios:** The evaluation is confined to cross-lingual projection where the source and target languages use the same universal POS tagset. The authors note in a footnote that their method could support mismatching tagsets by using a rectangular transformation matrix `A`, but this important and practical scenario is not experimentally validated. Furthermore, while the conclusion suggests the method is applicable to other noisy sources like crowdsourcing, this claim is not empirically supported, which somewhat limits the proven generality of the approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact vector representation of the projected labels `\tilde{y}_t`? Section 3.1 states that for unaligned or multiply-aligned words, you assign "a distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." Is `\tilde{y}_t` therefore a one-hot vector for direct one-to-one alignments but a dense probability distribution otherwise? How is the cross-entropy loss term in the objective function formulated to handle this mixture of target types?

2.  Have you experimented with introducing a hyperparameter `λ` to balance the contribution of the gold data loss and the projected data loss in your objective function? It seems plausible that down-weighting the noisy loss term could lead to better performance.

3.  Could you comment on the computational cost of this method? While adding one linear layer seems minor, the joint training regimen doubles the amount of data used per epoch. How does the training time compare to the `BiLSTM Ann+Proj` baseline?
