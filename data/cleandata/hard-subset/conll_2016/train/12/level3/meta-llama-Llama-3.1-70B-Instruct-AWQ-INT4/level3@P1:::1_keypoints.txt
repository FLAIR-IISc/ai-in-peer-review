- Proposes a sequence-tagging method that uses an explicit noise layer to learn to correct errors from cross-lingual projection.
- Frames learning as joint training on a gold-annotated corpus and a corpus with projected (noisy) tags.
- Implements the model with a BiLSTM plus a noise transformation layer and trains end-to-end with backpropagation.
- Contribution: introduces the explicit noise-layer approach for correcting projection errors in sequence tagging.
- Contribution: integrates the noise layer into a deep neural network and jointly trains on annotated and projected data.
- Contribution: evaluates on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing equal or better performance than prior methods.
- Strength: novel and significant idea of modeling projection noise explicitly for NLP sequence tagging.
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation across multiple simulated and real low-resource scenarios.
- Strength: detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix A, offering insight into projection error patterns.
- Strength: comparison to state-of-the-art methods showing superior performance in many cases.
- Weakness: experimental setup may be somewhat artificial for real low-resource languages because annotator cost/availability is not considered.
- Weakness: lacks detailed reporting of computational resources required to train the model, important for low-resource settings.
- Weakness: missing detailed comparison to related annotation-projection work (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agic et al. 2015).
- Weakness: unclear whether the experimental data will be released, raising reproducibility concerns.
- Question: please provide detailed analysis of computational resources and implications for low-resource applicability.
- Question: please compare more thoroughly with other annotation-projection studies (Johannsen et al. 2016; Agic et al. 2015).
- Question: will the experimental data be made available, and how can it be accessed?
- Question: please provide more details on preprocessing steps (tokenization, normalization) used for training.
