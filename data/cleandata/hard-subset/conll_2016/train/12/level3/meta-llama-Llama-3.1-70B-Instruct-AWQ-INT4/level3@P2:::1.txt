**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages, using cross-lingual projection of linguistic annotation. The authors propose a deep neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The model is trained jointly on gold standard and projected annotations, and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages**: The authors propose a new neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer.
2. **Joint training on gold standard and projected annotations**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can effectively utilize both gold standard and projected annotations to improve POS tagging accuracy.
3. **State-of-the-art results on simulated and real low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective utilization of projected annotations**: The authors demonstrate that their approach can effectively utilize projected annotations to improve POS tagging accuracy, even in the presence of noise and biases.
2. **State-of-the-art results on simulated and real low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their approach on a range of languages and demonstrate that their approach equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art results.
3. **Novel approach to modeling noise in cross-lingual projection**: The authors propose a new approach to modeling noise in cross-lingual projection, which is a key contribution of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Artificial experiment setup**: One reviewer noted that the experiment setup is somewhat artificial for real low-resource languages, as it assumes that gold standard annotations are available for a small subset of the data.
2. **Limited discussion of related work**: The authors could have provided a more comprehensive discussion of related work on annotation projection, including recent papers by Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015).
3. **Unclear data availability**: The authors should have stated whether the data used in the paper will be made available to the public.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can the authors provide more details on how they plan to make the data used in the paper available to the public?
2. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of the artificial experiment setup, which assumes that gold standard annotations are available for a small subset of the data?
3. Can the authors provide more discussion on the related work on annotation projection, including recent papers by Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015)?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and evaluate it on a range of languages. However, the authors could have provided more discussion on the limitations of their approach and the potential for future work. Overall, the paper is a nice contribution to the field of natural language processing and is likely to be of interest to researchers working on low-resource languages.
