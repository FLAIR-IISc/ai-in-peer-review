**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The method is applied to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection. The authors introduce a deep neural network model that jointly learns from both gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected data. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) recurrent network with a noise transformation layer to encode the mapping between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags. The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Noise modeling**: The authors propose a novel noise modeling approach that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. This is a significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.
2. **Deep neural network model**: The authors introduce a deep neural network model that jointly learns from both gold-standard annotated data and noisy projected data. This model is modular and can be applied to other low-resource NLP tasks.
3. **Strong empirical results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. **Simple and elegant modification**: The authors propose a simple and elegant modification to the RNN output layer that models noise by multiplying the softmax by a learned “noise” FC layer. This approach is easy to implement and can be applied to other NLP tasks.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Modular model**: The authors' model is modular and can be applied to other low-resource NLP tasks. This makes the approach more generalizable and useful for other researchers.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. **Unrealistic experimental setup**: The authors train their model with as little as ~1000 token annotations, which may be unrealistic for many settings. It would be useful to plot accuracy while varying gold annotation size to show when the method helps or harms.
2. **Lack of analysis**: The authors do not provide an analysis of performance vs. amount of gold data. This would be useful to understand the trade-offs between using more gold data and relying on noisy projected data.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate cross-lingual projection with the same tagset. It would be useful to explore the potential applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and differing tagsets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the representation of projected labels \tilde{y} (token-level vs. aggregated type-level distribution) and how to handle words that are never aligned?
2. How do you decide the relative contribution of the two terms in the objective function? Have you tried tuning the relative contribution of the two terms?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the performance vs. amount of gold data? How does the method perform when using more gold data?
4. Have you explored the potential applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and differing tagsets?
