**Paper Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) recurrent neural network, which produces two types of output: gold tags generated directly from the hidden state of the neural network, and uncertain projected tags generated after applying a further linear transformation. This transformation, referred to as output noising, encodes the mapping between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, and learning when and how much to trust the projected data.

**Contributions**

Based on the paper, I identify the following contributions:

1. **Novel approach to modeling noise in cross-lingual projection**: The paper proposes a new model that explicitly models the transformation between tagsets as part of a more expressive deep learning neural network.
2. **Effective use of noisy projected data**: The approach learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer, which improves the POS tagging accuracy.
3. **Strong empirical results on low-resource languages**: The method achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages.

**Strengths**

1. **Simple and elegant modification to the RNN output layer**: The proposed modification is easy to implement and can be applied to other low-resource NLP tasks.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The method achieves state-of-the-art results on a range of low-resource languages, demonstrating its effectiveness.
3. **Modular approach**: The method is modular and can be easily adapted to other NLP tasks that involve noisy annotations.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Unrealistic experimental setup**: The paper trains the model with as little as ~1000 token annotations, which may not be representative of real-world scenarios.
2. **Lack of analysis on performance vs. amount of gold data**: The paper does not provide an analysis of how the method performs with varying amounts of gold data.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates cross-lingual projection with the same tagset and does not explore the applicability of the method to other noisy sources or differing tagsets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide an analysis of how the method performs with varying amounts of gold data?
2. How does the method handle words that are never aligned in the parallel data?
3. Can you clarify the representation of projected labels \tilde{y} (token-level vs. aggregated type-level distribution)?
4. Have you explored the applicability of the method to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and differing tagsets?
5. Can you test tuning the relative contribution of the two terms in the objective function?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is easy to follow. However, there are some minor editorial issues, such as a bracket typo on line 267 and unclear language on line 357 regarding the format of \tilde{y}.
