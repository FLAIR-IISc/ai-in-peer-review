**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The model uses a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) network with a noise transformation layer to encode the mapping between projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags. The model is trained jointly on gold and distant projected annotations and can be trained end-to-end with backpropagation.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer.
2. The authors integrate the noise layer into a deep neural network and jointly train the model on both annotated and projected data to make the model learn from better supervisions.
3. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and demonstrate that their approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods.

**Strengths**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the noise transformation matrix A, which provides insights into the types of errors that occur in cross-lingual projection and how the model learns to correct them.
4. The authors provide a comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms, demonstrating that their approach outperforms existing methods in many cases.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors note that the experiment setup is somewhat artificial for real low-resource languages, as the cost of finding vs. paying annotators is not taken into account. This caveat should be acknowledged in the writeup.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train the model, which may be a concern for low-resource languages.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other related work on annotation projection, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015).
4. The authors do not state whether the data will be made available, which may be a concern for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train the model, and how this may impact the applicability of the approach to low-resource languages?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other related work on annotation projection, such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015)?
3. Will the data used in the experiments be made available, and if so, how can it be accessed?
4. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing steps used to prepare the data for training, such as tokenization and normalization?
