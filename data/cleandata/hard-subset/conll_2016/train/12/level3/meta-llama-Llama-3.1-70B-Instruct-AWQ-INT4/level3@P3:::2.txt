This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages, utilizing both gold standard annotations and noisy cross-lingual projected annotations. The authors introduce a new neural network model that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer, which is trained jointly on gold and projected annotations.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) the proposal of a deep learning model based on a long short-term memory recurrent structure to model the noise affecting cross-lingual projected annotations; (2) the integration of the model into a deep neural network and joint training on both annotated and projected data; and (3) the evaluation on eight simulated and two real-world low-resource languages, demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

The authors' approach is modular and likely useful across other low-resource NLP tasks. The experimental setup, although unusual, provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. The strong empirical results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The experimental setup, which involves training with as little as ~1000 token annotations, may be unrealistic for many settings. The paper lacks an analysis of performance vs. the amount of gold data, which would provide valuable insights into when the method helps or harms. Additionally, the paper only evaluates cross-lingual projection with the same tagset, and the potential applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and differing tagsets is not explored.

The authors' decision to use equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the objective is surprising, and it would be interesting to test tuning the relative contribution of the two terms. Furthermore, the representation of projected labels and how to handle words that are never aligned are not clearly explained. Finally, there are minor editorial issues, such as unclear text in line 357 and a bracket typo in line 267.

Strengths:

1. The proposed approach is modular and can be easily integrated into other deep learning models, making it a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.
2. The experimental setup, although unusual, provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, demonstrating its effectiveness in various settings.
3. The strong empirical results on POS tagging for eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Weaknesses:

1. The experimental setup may be unrealistic for many settings, and it would be beneficial to evaluate the method with varying amounts of gold data.
2. The paper lacks an analysis of performance vs. the amount of gold data, which would provide valuable insights into when the method helps or harms.
3. The potential applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and differing tagsets is not explored.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do you plan to address the issue of varying amounts of gold data in future work?
2. Can you provide more insights into the representation of projected labels and how to handle words that are never aligned?
3. Have you considered evaluating the proposed approach on other NLP tasks, such as named entity recognition or dependency parsing?
