**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sequence tagging that learns to correct the errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The approach is framed as joint learning over two corpora, one tagged with gold standard and the other with projected tags. The authors evaluate their method on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and show that it equals or exceeds the state-of-the-art on all settings.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Noise Layer**: The authors propose a novel noise layer that models the transformation between the projected high-resource tags and low-resource tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.
2. **Joint Learning**: The authors propose a joint learning approach that trains the model on both annotated and projected data, allowing the model to learn from better supervisions.
3. **Evaluation on Low-Resource Languages**: The authors evaluate their method on two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, and show that it outperforms other algorithms.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Noisy Projected Data**: The authors' approach effectively uses the noisy projected data to improve the POS tagging accuracy, outperforming other algorithms that use the projected data directly.
2. **Robustness to Noise**: The authors' approach is robust to noise in the projected data, allowing it to perform well even in settings where the projected data is of low quality.
3. **Generalizability**: The authors' approach is generalizable to other low-resource languages, as demonstrated by its performance on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Assumes Availability of Parallel Data**: The authors' approach assumes the availability of parallel data, which may not always be available for low-resource languages.
2. **Requires Annotated Data**: The authors' approach requires annotated data, which may be difficult to obtain for low-resource languages.
3. **May Not Perform Well on Highly Noisy Data**: The authors' approach may not perform well on highly noisy data, as demonstrated by its performance on Kinyarwanda.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of availability of parallel data for low-resource languages?
2. Can you provide more details on how the noise layer is learned and how it is used to improve the POS tagging accuracy?
3. How do you plan to extend your approach to other NLP tasks, such as named entity recognition and dependency parsing?
