- Proposes a novel sequence-tagging model that uses a BiLSTM plus an explicit noise layer to correct errors from cross-lingual tag projection.  
- Trains the model with both a tiny gold annotated corpus and distant supervision from projected tags.  
- Evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Main contribution: introducing an explicit noise-layer approach to learn corrections for projected-tag errors.  
- Contribution: modular, generalizable method that can integrate with other deep learning models.  
- Contribution: strong empirical results across multiple simulated and real low-resource languages.  
- Contribution: demonstrated robustness to noise in projected data.  
- Strength: novel solution addressing scarce annotated data in low-resource settings.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance across a range of languages.  
- Strength: method is modular and easy to combine with other architectures.  
- Strength: method effectively learns to correct noisy projected labels.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of how performance varies with the amount of gold data.  
- Weakness: experimental setup (training with as few as 1,000 tokens) may be unrealistic for many real-world scenarios.  
- Weakness: does not explore other noisy annotation sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or mismatched tagsets.  
- Weakness: uses equal weighting for gold and noisy labels in the objective without justification or exploration of alternatives.  
- Question: How does performance change as the amount of gold data varies?  
- Question: Can you clarify the experimental setup and its realism for practical scenarios?  
- Question: Have you tried other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or different tagsets, and what were the results?  
- Question: Why use equal weighting for gold and noisy labels, and is that optimal?
