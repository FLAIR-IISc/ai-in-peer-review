## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of POS tagging in low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modeling the noise inherent in this process. The core idea – a simple yet effective modification to the RNN output layer using a learned “noise” FC layer – is well-motivated and demonstrably improves performance. The authors convincingly show that their method equals or exceeds state-of-the-art results on both simulated and real low-resource languages, a significant achievement. The modularity of the approach is also a strong point, suggesting potential applicability to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of explicitly modeling projection noise within a neural network framework. While the noise layer itself is conceptually straightforward, its integration into the BiLSTM architecture and the resulting performance gains are noteworthy. A second contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both simulated and genuinely low-resource settings. This broad evaluation strengthens the claims of the method’s robustness and generalizability. Finally, the paper provides a useful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices, offering insights into the types of errors commonly introduced by cross-lingual projection.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The experimental setup, focusing on training with as little as 1000 annotated tokens, feels somewhat unrealistic. While valuable for demonstrating the method’s potential in extremely low-resource scenarios, it’s unclear how performance scales with increasing amounts of gold data. A plot showing accuracy as a function of annotation size would be highly informative. Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to cross-lingual projection with the same tagset. Exploring the applicability to differing tagsets or other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) would broaden the impact of the work. The equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the objective function also seems arbitrary; a sensitivity analysis varying this weighting could reveal optimal configurations. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a plot illustrating the performance of your method (and baselines) as the amount of gold annotation data increases?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the weighting of the gold and noisy label loss terms in the objective function?
3.  Do you anticipate that the proposed noise layer would be effective when applied to noisy labels generated from sources other than cross-lingual projection (e.g., crowdsourcing)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Line 357: The format of  “ỹ” is unclear. Please clarify whether it represents a token-level distribution or an aggregated type-level distribution.
*   Line 267: There is a bracket typo.
*   Clarify how words that are never aligned during projection are handled. Are they simply assigned a default tag, or is there a more sophisticated mechanism?
