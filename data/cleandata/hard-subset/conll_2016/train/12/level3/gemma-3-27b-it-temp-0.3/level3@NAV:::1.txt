## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a BiLSTM model with a “noise layer” that learns to correct errors introduced during projection from a high-resource language (English) to a low-resource language. The core idea of learning a transformation between projected and gold-standard tags is compelling and demonstrably effective, as shown through experiments on both simulated and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and successful implementation of this noise layer, allowing for more effective utilization of noisy projected data alongside limited gold-standard annotations. A secondary, but significant, contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple languages, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed method. Finally, the correction of the experimental setup flaw identified in previous drafts strengthens the validity of the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The gains on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda are particularly noteworthy, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
2. **Novel Noise Modeling:** Explicitly modeling the noise in projected data as a learnable transformation is a clever and effective strategy. This allows the model to adapt to the specific types of errors introduced during projection, rather than treating all projected data as equally reliable.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thorough, including both simulated low-resource settings and evaluations on two actual low-resource languages. This provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed method.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The inclusion of visualizations of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the model’s behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificial Setup:** As noted by another reviewer, the experimental setup, while common in this area, is somewhat artificial. The cost of obtaining parallel data is often lower than the cost of obtaining annotated data, potentially skewing the perceived value of the proposed method. This should be acknowledged more explicitly in the discussion.
2. **Missing Related Work:** The paper would benefit from citing and discussing relevant work on annotation projection, specifically Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015). These papers explore techniques for improving the quality of projected annotations and provide valuable context for the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of learning the noise transformation matrix *A*, particularly as the tagsets of the source and target languages differ in size?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or techniques?

**Additional Comments:**

Please state whether the datasets used in the experiments will be made publicly available. My evaluation was predicated on the assumption of public availability.
