

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

**Summary:** The paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by using cross-lingual projection from a resource-rich language (English) via parallel corpora. The authors propose a novel neural network architecture that explicitly models noise in projected tags through a "noise layer" added to the RNN output. This layer learns a transformation matrix that corrects errors in projected tags during joint training on both gold-standard and projected annotations. The approach is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with only 1,000 gold tokens) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), showing state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1. A simple yet effective neural network modification that explicitly models noise in projected tags through a learned transformation matrix, enabling better utilization of cross-lingual projection data.
2. Strong empirical validation across both simulated and real-world low-resource POS tagging scenarios, demonstrating consistent improvements over previous methods.
3. A modular approach with potential applicability to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel noise modeling approach:** The noise layer is elegantly simple yet effective, adding minimal complexity while significantly improving performance. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms previous methods by learning when and how much to trust projected data, rather than treating it as uniform noise. The visualization of the learned transformation matrices (Figures 3 and 4) provides intuitive insight into the model's behavior.

2. **Strong empirical results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on both simulated low-resource settings (8 languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), with improvements of 1-3% over prior work. This demonstrates the practical value of the approach in realistic low-resource scenarios.

3. **Modularity and generalizability:** The approach is designed as a modular extension to existing neural sequence taggers, making it easy to apply to other low-resource NLP tasks. The authors correctly note this potential applicability to other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourcing), which represents a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Unrealistic experimental setup:** The evaluation uses only ~1,000 gold tokens for training, which is likely impractical for many low-resource languages. While the paper acknowledges this as a "simulation" of low-resource settings, the authors fail to discuss how this compares to the actual annotation effort required in practice. For many languages, obtaining 1,000 high-quality annotated tokens would be extremely challenging.

2. **Lack of analysis on gold data quantity:** The paper does not provide results showing how performance varies with the amount of gold data. A plot of accuracy versus gold annotation size would be crucial to understand the practical utility of the approach and when it provides the most benefit.

3. **Inadequate exploration of key design choices:** The authors use equal weighting for gold and projected data in the loss function without testing alternative weightings. This is surprising given the different quality of these two data sources, and it's unclear whether tuning this parameter would improve results. Additionally, the paper doesn't explore the potential of the method for other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or differing tagsets.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. The paper mentions that words never aligned to English are handled through a "distribution over tags according to the tag frequency distribution over the whole English sentence." Could you clarify how this is implemented in the model (token-level vs. aggregated type-level), and how the model handles words that are never aligned in the training data?

2. The paper states "the projected data consists of pairs (x_t, ỹ)" but doesn't clearly explain whether ỹ represents a token-level tag or an aggregated type-level distribution. This is crucial for understanding how the model handles unaligned words.

3. The authors use equal weighting for gold and projected data in the loss function. Have you explored tuning the relative contribution of these two terms, and how would this affect performance, especially when the amount of gold data varies?

**Minor Editorial Issues:** 
- Line 357 is unclear about the format of ỹ.
- Line 267 contains a bracket typo.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a simple yet effective approach to low-resource POS tagging. The method is elegant, the results are strong, and the potential for broader application is clear. With some additional analysis of key design choices and a more realistic discussion of the annotation effort required, this would be an even stronger contribution to the field.
