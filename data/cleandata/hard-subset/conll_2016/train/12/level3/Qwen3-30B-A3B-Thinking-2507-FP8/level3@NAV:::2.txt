

This paper proposes a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by modeling noise in cross-lingual projected annotations through a simple modification to the RNN output layer. The main contribution is the introduction of a noise transformation layer that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection, enabling joint training on both gold-standard and projected annotations. This is demonstrated to be effective across eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), with consistent improvements over strong baselines.

Strengths: The noise modeling approach is elegant and simple, requiring minimal modification to standard sequence tagging architectures. The empirical results are robust across multiple languages, with the method achieving state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource settings. The modularity of the approach suggests potential applicability to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.

Weaknesses: The experimental setup with only 1,000 gold tokens is highly unusual and likely unrealistic for many practical low-resource scenarios. The paper lacks analysis of how performance varies with different amounts of gold data, which would help determine when the method provides benefits versus potential harm. The equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the objective function appears arbitrary without justification or ablation study, and the paper does not explore tuning this hyperparameter. Additionally, the paper only evaluates cross-lingual projection with identical tagsets, without investigating applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) or differing tagsets. The representation of projected labels (token-level vs. type-level) and handling of never-aligned words requires clarification.

The paper contains minor editorial issues, including unclear notation at line 357 and a bracket typo at line 267. While the method shows promise, these weaknesses significantly impact the paper's contribution and practical relevance. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.
