

This paper presents an innovative approach to low-resource POS tagging by learning to correct errors in cross-lingual projected annotations through an explicit noise layer. The authors frame sequence tagging as joint learning over two corpora: one tagged with gold standard annotations and another with projected tags from a resource-rich language. The key contribution is a neural model that incorporates a noise transformation matrix to learn when and how much to trust projected tags, rather than treating them as reliable supervision.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) an explicit modeling of the noise process affecting cross-lingual projected annotations, (2) a joint learning framework that integrates gold and projected data through a noise layer in a deep neural network, and (3) comprehensive evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated low-resource settings and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).

Strengths:
1. The paper's core innovation of explicitly modeling projection noise through a learnable transformation matrix is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The noise layer provides a principled way to handle unreliable projected annotations rather than relying on heuristics or implicit modeling as in prior work. The experimental results clearly demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms naive use of projected data (BiLSTM Projected) and matches or exceeds the state-of-the-art (Duong et al., 2014a) across all eight simulated languages.

2. The evaluation on real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) is particularly compelling, with the model achieving 86.3% accuracy for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, substantially exceeding prior work. The authors' analysis of the learned noise matrices (Figure 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into how the model adapts to different languages' specific noise patterns.

3. The paper's practical utility is well-established through the demonstration that only 1,000 gold-standard tokens are needed for training, with the rest of the data coming from parallel resources. This aligns with real-world constraints for low-resource language processing where gold data is scarce but parallel data is often more readily available.

Weaknesses:
1. The experimental setup, while well-justified for benchmarking, remains somewhat artificial for real low-resource language scenarios. As noted by another reviewer, the paper does not sufficiently acknowledge the practical difference between "finding" parallel data (which may be free but of variable quality) versus "paying" for gold annotations (which is costly but high quality). The authors should clarify this limitation in the writeup.

2. The paper omits important related work on annotation projection, specifically Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), which also address similar challenges in cross-lingual projection. These should be cited and discussed in the related work section to situate the contribution more precisely within the literature.

3. The paper does not explicitly state whether the datasets and code will be made publicly available, which is increasingly expected in NLP research. The reviewer assumed public availability when evaluating the work, but this should be clearly stated in the paper.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the practical implications of your noise matrix learning for real-world deployment where parallel data quality may vary significantly?
2. How would your approach perform if the projected tags were generated from a different source language rather than English, and what would be the implications for model performance?
3. Will the datasets and code be made publicly available, and if so, when?
