- Paper proposes a neural POS tagger that models noise in cross-lingual projected tags via a learned "noise layer" (transformation matrix) on RNN outputs.  
- Jointly trains on small gold-standard data and noisy projected annotations to correct projection errors.  
- Evaluated on eight simulated low-resource European languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), reporting state-of-the-art results.  
- Contribution: simple, effective noise modeling that learns when and how much to trust projected data.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation showing 1–3% improvements over prior work in both simulated and real settings.  
- Contribution: modular approach potentially applicable to other low-resource NLP tasks and noisy supervision sources.  
- Strength: noise layer is elegant, low-complexity, and provides interpretable transformation matrices (visualized in Figures 3 and 4).  
- Strength: consistent, strong empirical gains across multiple languages and realistic scenarios.  
- Strength: design is modular and generalizable to other tagging tasks or noisy labels (e.g., crowdsourcing).  
- Weakness: experimental setup using only ~1,000 gold tokens may be unrealistic for many truly low-resource languages, and annotation effort is not discussed.  
- Weakness: no analysis of how performance scales with varying amounts of gold data (missing accuracy vs. gold-size curve).  
- Weakness: design choices underexplored — e.g., equal weighting of gold vs. projected loss not justified or tuned, and limited exploration of other noisy supervision types or tagset differences.  
- Question: clarify how unaligned words are handled — is the tag distribution applied at token-level or aggregated type-level, and how are never-aligned tokens treated?  
- Question: specify whether projected label ỹ denotes a token-level tag or an aggregated type-level distribution.  
- Question: did you experiment with tuning the relative weighting of gold and projected data in the loss, and how does that interact with gold-data size?  
- Minor issues: line 357 unclear about format of ỹ; line 267 contains a bracket typo.  
- Overall: well-executed, elegant method with strong results; would benefit from additional analysis of gold-data dependence and design choices, and more discussion of annotation practicality.
