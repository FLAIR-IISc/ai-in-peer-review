- Tackles low-resource POS tagging by jointly training on small gold data and noisy cross-lingual projected tags using a BiLSTM with a learned noise layer that models transformation from projected to gold tags.  
- Secondary contributions: empirical validation on 8 simulated + 2 real languages and a modular framework potentially usable with other noisy supervision.  
- Novel noise layer: learned matrix A applied to softmax outputs models tag misalignment biases without heuristics and is parameter-efficient.  
- Empirical strength: consistent gains across 10 settings, achieving SOTA on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%).  
- Broad applicability: modular noise layer can be plugged into other sequence taggers and potentially other noisy supervision scenarios.  
- Weakness: uses only 1,000 gold tokens, which is unrealistically small compared to prior work (typically 5k+), weakening the low-resource claim.  
- Weakness: no ablation on varying gold-data quantity, so scaling behavior (e.g., with 5k tokens) is unknown.  
- Weakness: limited noise-source exploration—only cross-lingual projection with same tagset; does not test mismatched tagsets or other noise types (e.g., crowdsourcing).  
- Weakness: objective equally weights gold and projected losses without justification or ablation on alternative weightings.  
- Question: How does performance scale with practically feasible gold data sizes (e.g., 5k tokens)?  
- Question: Is the learned noise matrix A necessary—how does performance change if A is fixed (e.g., identity)?  
- Question: How would the method handle mismatched tagsets between source and target languages?  
- Question: For NULL-aligned tokens, is the projected label ỹ a token-level distribution or a type-level (sentence/tag-frequency) distribution?  
- Minor comment: typographical error at Line 267 (stray bracket).  
- Limitation note: reliance on parallel data (e.g., Europarl) may limit applicability to languages without such resources.
