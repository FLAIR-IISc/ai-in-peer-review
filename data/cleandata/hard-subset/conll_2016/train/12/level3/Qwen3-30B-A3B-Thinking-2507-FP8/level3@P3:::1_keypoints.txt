- Presents a BiLSTM POS tagger that jointly learns from small gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected tags using an explicit learnable noise layer.  
- Evaluates method on eight simulated low-resource European-language settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent improvements.  
- Primary contribution: explicit noise transformation layer that corrects projection errors and enables better use of noisy cross-lingual data.  
- Secondary contribution: comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating strong accuracy gains (avg. 91.7% across eight languages; 86.3% Malagasy; 82.5% Kinyarwanda).  
- Tertiary contribution: analysis of learned noise matrices revealing meaningful, language-specific projection error patterns.  
- Strength: conceptually novel approach to modeling projection noise rather than using heuristics or implicit correlations.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation on both simulated and real low-resource settings.  
- Strength: clear visualization and analysis of noise patterns supporting model interpretability.  
- Weakness: evaluation setup is somewhat artificial—assumes parallel data availability and that obtaining 1,000 gold tokens is inexpensive.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion and citation of related annotation-projection work (e.g., Johannsen et al., 2016; Agic et al., 2015).  
- Weakness: paper lacks an explicit data and code availability statement to ensure reproducibility.  
- Question: how is the K×K noise matrix A handled across different tagsets (e.g., universal tags) in multilingual settings?  
- Question: please clarify exact comparisons to prior work (Garrette & Baldridge 2013) and whether identical data splits were used.  
- Question: confirm whether the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda annotated data used are the same as in Garrette & Baldridge (2013) or newly collected.  
- Overall: strong contribution to low-resource POS tagging and recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing dataset realism, related work citations, and data availability.
