

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by jointly training on small gold-standard data and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The primary contribution is a *noise layer* that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags (from parallel English data) and gold tags, integrated into a BiLSTM framework. This enables the model to learn *when* to trust projected annotations. Secondary contributions are (1) comprehensive empirical validation across 8 simulated low-resource languages and 2 real-world languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), and (2) a modular framework applicable to other noisy supervision sources. The authors’ claim of "learning when to trust" projected data is the strongest contribution, as it directly tackles the core challenge of noisy distant supervision.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel noise layer mechanism**: The noise layer (a learned matrix *A* applied to softmax outputs) effectively models tag misalignment biases without heuristics. It consistently outperforms baselines (e.g., +2.4% over Duong et al. on Malagasy) by adapting to language-specific noise patterns (e.g., Swedish’s *PRT/X* confusion, Figure 3). This is a clean, parameter-efficient solution to a well-known problem.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: Results on 10 diverse settings (8 simulated languages + 2 real low-resource languages) demonstrate consistent gains over state-of-the-art. The method achieves SOTA on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%), exceeding prior work by 1.2–6.2% (Table 2). The noise matrices (Figure 4) visually confirm the model learns language-specific error patterns.  
3. **Broad applicability**: The modular design (noise layer as a plug-in for any sequence tagger) suggests utility beyond POS tagging (e.g., for crowdsourced or distant supervision). The authors’ claim of generalizability is well-supported by the framework’s simplicity.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unrealistic gold data size**: Training on *only 1,000 tokens* of gold data is highly questionable for real-world low-resource scenarios. Most prior work (e.g., Garrette & Baldridge, 2013) uses 5k+ tokens for Malagasy/Kinyarwanda. This undermines the paper’s low-resource claim—1,000 tokens is *less* than typical human annotation time (e.g., 2 hours for 1,000 tokens, per the paper).  
2. **No ablation on gold data quantity**: The paper lacks analysis of how performance scales with *more* gold data (e.g., 5k tokens). Without this, it’s unclear whether the noise layer *helps* when more gold data is available (e.g., does it degrade performance with 5k tokens?). This is critical for assessing practical utility.  
3. **Limited exploration of noise sources**: The method is tested *only* on cross-lingual projection (same tagset). It doesn’t address: (a) differing tagsets (e.g., English vs. Malagasy POS schemes), or (b) other noise sources (e.g., crowdsourcing). The claim of "generalizable to noisy annotations" is unsupported.  
4. **Unjustified loss weighting**: The objective equally weights gold and projected losses (*L(θ,A)*), yet no ablation studies test tuning this (e.g., weighting projected loss higher/lower). This could significantly impact performance (e.g., overfitting to noisy data).

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The gold data size (1,000 tokens) is exceptionally small for real low-resource applications. How does the model’s performance scale with *practically feasible* gold data sizes (e.g., 5k tokens)? Provide results for 5k tokens to validate the method’s utility beyond the 1,000-token benchmark.  
2. The noise layer assumes *A* is learned jointly. What if *A* is fixed (e.g., identity matrix)? Does the model degrade significantly? This tests the necessity of the noise layer.  
3. How would the method handle *mismatched tagsets* (e.g., English universal tags vs. Malagasy-specific tags)? The paper claims generalizability but doesn’t address this.  
4. Clarify the projected label representation: For NULL-aligned tokens, is *ỹ* a *token-level* distribution (per word) or *type-level* (per tag frequency in the sentence)? Line 357 is ambiguous.
