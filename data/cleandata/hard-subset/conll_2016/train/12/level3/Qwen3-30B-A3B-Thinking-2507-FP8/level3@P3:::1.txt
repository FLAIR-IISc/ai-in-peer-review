

# Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly leverages a small amount of gold-standard data with noisy cross-lingual projected data. The primary contribution is a BiLSTM model with an explicit noise layer that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection. The authors frame this as a joint learning problem over two corpora (gold-standard and projected tags), where the noise layer explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and true tags. A secondary contribution is a comprehensive evaluation across eight simulated low-resource settings (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines. The third contribution is a detailed analysis of the noise transformation matrix, revealing language-specific patterns of projection errors.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to handling noisy projected data**: The explicit noise layer represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work that either used heuristics to clean projected tags or implicitly captured tagset correlations. By modeling the noise explicitly as a learnable transformation, the authors enable more effective utilization of noisy cross-lingual data without requiring additional hand-crafted rules. The experimental results (91.7% average accuracy across eight languages) clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach compared to baselines.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real low-resource settings**: The paper provides a strong empirical foundation by testing the approach on both simulated low-resource scenarios (using European languages with limited gold data) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The results on real languages (86.3% accuracy for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda) are particularly compelling, showing consistent improvements over prior work.

3. **Clear analysis of noise patterns**: The authors provide valuable insights into the nature of projection errors through their noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 and 4). These visualizations reveal language-specific patterns of error (e.g., NUM vs. DET confusion in Italian, PRT vs. X in Swedish), demonstrating that the model learns meaningful patterns rather than simply memorizing data. This analysis provides important theoretical justification for the approach.

## Weaknesses
1. **Artificiality of the low-resource setup**: As noted by another reviewer, the evaluation setup is somewhat artificial for real low-resource languages. The paper assumes that parallel data is readily available (which may not be the case for many low-resource languages) and that obtaining 1,000 tokens of gold data is inexpensive (which may not account for the actual costs of finding and paying annotators). The authors should acknowledge this limitation in the writeup.

2. **Insufficient discussion of related work on annotation projection**: The paper should cite and discuss relevant prior work on annotation projection, specifically Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), which also address similar challenges. While the authors cite Duong et al. (2014a) as the closest work, they should explicitly position their contribution relative to these other approaches.

3. **Lack of data availability statement**: The paper should explicitly state whether the datasets and code will be made publicly available. As a reviewer, I assumed public availability when evaluating the paper, but this should be clearly stated in the paper to facilitate reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper mentions that the noise layer is "parameterized by a K×K matrix A." For languages with different tagsets (e.g., when using universal tags), how is the matrix A handled? The paper mentions this as a possibility but doesn't provide details.

2. The authors claim "our method outperforms the state of the art on both languages (Duong et al., 2014a; Garrette and Baldridge, 2013)." However, Garrette and Baldridge (2013) reported 81.2% accuracy for Malagasy, while the authors report 86.3% with their method. Please clarify the exact comparison (e.g., whether the same data split was used).

3. The paper states that for the real-world experiments, they use "the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda data from Garrette and Baldridge (2013)." Could the authors clarify whether they used the same annotated data as Garrette and Baldridge, or if they collected new data?

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a strong contribution to low-resource POS tagging through a novel neural network architecture with an explicit noise layer for handling cross-lingual projection errors. The experiment setup flaw from the earlier draft has been corrected, and the authors have addressed previous concerns. The paper is now ready for acceptance with minor revisions to address the artificiality of the low-resource setup, add relevant citations to Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), and include a clear statement about data availability. The reviewer previously offered conditional acceptance and now sees no obstacles to acceptance. This is a nice contribution to the field of low-resource NLP.
