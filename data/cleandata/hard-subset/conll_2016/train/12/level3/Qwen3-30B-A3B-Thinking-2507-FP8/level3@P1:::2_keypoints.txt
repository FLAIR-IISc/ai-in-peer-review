- Introduces a noise layer that models errors in cross-lingual projected tags via a learnable transformation matrix for sequence tagging in low-resource languages.
- Proposes a simple, effective neural architecture that explicitly transforms projected tags to better match gold tags.
- Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation showing state-of-the-art results on simulated and real low-resource languages with as few as 1,000 gold tokens.
- Presents a modular framework that can be readily applied to other low-resource NLP sequence-labeling tasks.
- Elegant, computationally efficient solution that clearly models when and how much to trust projected data through learned transformation matrices.
- Learned noise matrices capture language-specific noise patterns, supporting the method’s effectiveness.
- Empirical results are strong and statistically significant, with consistent improvements across eight simulated and two real languages (notably Malagasy and Kinyarwanda).
- Modularity and simplicity make the approach attractive for adaptation to other noisy supervision scenarios.
- Experimental setup relies on an unrealistically small fixed gold-data size (1,000 tokens), limiting real-world applicability.
- Lacks analysis of performance across varying amounts of gold data to establish the method’s data efficiency and scaling behavior.
- Provides no empirical evidence for applicability to other noisy annotation sources (e.g., crowdsourcing), despite claiming generality.
- Uses equal weighting for gold and projected data in the loss without justification or exploration of alternative weightings.
- Asks how the method performs with larger, more realistic gold-data sizes (e.g., 10,000 tokens).
- Requests the learned noise matrices for Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, clarification on handling missing projected tags/unaligned words, and whether projected labels are token-level tags or aggregated distributions.
