

This paper presents a novel neural network approach for POS tagging in low-resource languages that jointly learns from a small amount of gold-standard annotations and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key contribution is a noise layer that explicitly models the transformation between projected tags and gold tags, allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. The authors evaluate their approach on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

Strengths:
1) The proposed noise layer framework provides a principled way to incorporate noisy distant supervision, addressing a key limitation in previous work that treated projected tags as reliable.
2) The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements over baselines across multiple languages and settings.
3) The model architecture is elegant and well-motivated, with the noise transformation matrix providing interpretable insights into the nature of projection errors.

Weaknesses:
1) The paper should acknowledge the caveat noted by another reviewer that the experimental setup is somewhat artificial for real low-resource languages, particularly regarding the cost of finding versus paying annotators.
2) The related work section lacks citations to relevant annotation projection literature (Johannsen et al. 2016; Agic et al. 2015), which should be added to properly position the contribution.
3) The paper does not state whether the data will be made publicly available, which is important for reproducibility.

The authors have corrected the experiment setup flaw noted in the previous review, and the paper now appears ready for acceptance. The contribution is significant for low-resource NLP, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the acknowledged weaknesses. The authors should explicitly acknowledge the artificiality of their experimental setup in the discussion, cite the missing related work, and state whether data will be made available.
