

This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging in low-resource languages by learning to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The main contributions of this work are: (1) a simple yet effective neural network architecture that explicitly models the noise in cross-lingual projected tags through a learnable transformation matrix; (2) comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages with minimal gold annotation (as few as 1000 tokens); and (3) a modular framework that could be readily applied to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.

The strongest strength of this work is its elegant solution to a significant problem in low-resource NLP. The authors propose a straightforward modification to the standard RNN output layer that incorporates a noise transformation matrix to model the mapping between projected tags and gold tags. This approach is simple yet powerful, addressing a fundamental limitation of cross-lingual projection where directly using projected tags often degrades performance. The noise layer effectively learns when and how much to trust the projected data, as evidenced by the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3) that capture language-specific noise patterns. The solution is computationally efficient, requiring minimal additional parameters, while significantly improving performance over naive approaches.

Second, the empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The authors demonstrate consistent improvement across eight simulated low-resource languages (using European languages with only 1000 gold tokens) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), with absolute improvements of up to 5.4% over the previous state-of-the-art. The results show that their approach uniformly equals or exceeds existing methods, with particularly strong gains on Malagasy (86.3% vs. 85.3% for Duong et al.) and Kinyarwanda (82.5% vs. 78.3%). The evaluation is thorough, using standard datasets and metrics, and the results are statistically significant.

Third, the modularity of the approach suggests broad applicability beyond POS tagging. The noise layer could be easily incorporated into other sequence labeling tasks and potentially adapted to other sources of noisy supervision, such as crowdsourcing or distant supervision. The authors correctly note this potential generalizability in their conclusion, and the framework's simplicity makes it an attractive option for other low-resource NLP applications.

The primary weakness is the experimental setup, which uses an unrealistically small amount of gold data (1000 tokens) for training. While this is common in simulation studies, it does not reflect realistic scenarios where even 1000 tokens might be difficult to obtain, and the authors do not provide evidence that their method would scale to more typical low-resource settings with slightly more data. A more convincing case would require showing performance improvements across a range of gold data sizes.

Second, the paper lacks analysis of performance as a function of gold data size. The authors should have included experiments varying the amount of gold data (e.g., 100, 500, 1000, 2000 tokens) to demonstrate when their method provides the most benefit and to establish the data efficiency of their approach. Without this, it's unclear whether the method would be beneficial in settings with slightly more gold data.

Third, the paper does not explore the method's applicability beyond cross-lingual projection. The authors claim their approach could be useful for other noisy annotation sources, but they provide no evidence of this. The authors should have conducted experiments with alternative noisy data sources (e.g., crowdsourced annotations) to support this claim.

Fourth, the paper uses equal weighting for gold and projected data in the loss function without justification or exploration of alternative weightings. The authors should have tested different ratios of gold to projected data contributions to the loss, as the relative importance of these components might vary across languages and data sizes.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would your method perform with more realistic amounts of gold data (e.g., 10,000 tokens) compared to the 1,000-token setting?
2. Could you provide the learned noise matrices for the real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) to help understand language-specific noise patterns?
3. How does your approach handle cases where projected tags are missing (e.g., words with no alignment), and what is the impact of different strategies for handling unaligned words?
4. Could you clarify the representation of projected labels (line 357) - are they token-level tags or aggregated tag distributions?
