

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel neural network approach for low-resource POS tagging that learns to correct errors from cross-lingual projection using an explicit noise layer. The main contributions are: (1) a joint learning framework that models the transformation between projected tags and gold tags through an explicit noise layer, (2) comprehensive evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both simulated low-resource settings (8 languages) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), and (3) the demonstration that this approach can effectively leverage noisy projected data without requiring additional heuristics for cleaning.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a principled approach to handling noise in cross-lingual projected data through an explicit noise layer, which is a significant conceptual advance over previous work that either ignored noise or used ad-hoc cleaning heuristics. The noise layer mechanism (parameterized by matrix A) allows the model to learn when and how much to trust projected tags, as evidenced by the clear diagonal patterns and off-diagonal noise patterns visualized in Figures 3 and 4.

2. The experimental results are compelling and consistent across multiple settings. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages (exceeding Duong et al.'s 2014 work by 0.2-3.7% accuracy) and demonstrates significant improvements on real low-resource languages (86.3% for Malagasy and 82.5% for Kinyarwanda, outperforming previous work by 1.0% and 4.6% respectively). The results are robust across different languages with varying levels of noise.

3. The approach is simple yet effective, requiring only a small modification to the standard BiLSTM architecture (adding the noise layer) and minimal additional parameters (the K×K noise matrix A). This makes the method computationally efficient while providing substantial performance gains.

4. The paper provides valuable insights into the nature of noise in cross-lingual projection through the visualization of the noise transformation matrices. These visualizations reveal language-specific patterns of noise (e.g., PRT and X confusion in Swedish, NUM vs DET in Italian), demonstrating the model's ability to capture meaningful patterns in the noise.

**Weaknesses**

1. The evaluation setup for real low-resource languages is somewhat artificial in the sense that it assumes parallel data is readily available and cheap to obtain, without accounting for the real-world costs of finding such data versus paying annotators. The authors should acknowledge this limitation in the paper to provide a more accurate assessment of the method's practical utility.

2. The related work section omits important recent contributions on annotation projection, specifically Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015). These works should be cited and discussed to better situate the current contribution within the broader literature on cross-lingual annotation projection.

3. The paper does not explicitly state whether the datasets and code will be made publicly available. As this is crucial for reproducibility and community adoption, the authors should clearly state their data availability policy.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you clarify the practical considerations regarding the availability of parallel data for real low-resource languages? Specifically, how does the cost of obtaining parallel data compare to the cost of annotating 1,000 tokens for these languages?

2. Will the datasets and code be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

3. Could you provide more details on how the noise transformation matrix A is initialized during training? The paper mentions pretraining on annotated data, but the exact initialization procedure for A is not specified.
