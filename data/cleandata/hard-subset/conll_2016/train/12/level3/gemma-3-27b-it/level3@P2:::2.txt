This paper introduces a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a bidirectional LSTM network with a learned “noise layer” applied to the output, enabling joint training on both gold-standard and projected data. The primary contribution of this work lies in this innovative noise modeling technique, which allows the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. A second significant contribution is the strong empirical validation of the approach, demonstrating competitive or superior performance on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). Finally, the simplicity and modularity of the proposed method make it a potentially valuable technique for other low-resource NLP tasks leveraging noisy supervision.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Noise Modeling:** The introduction of the noise layer is a clever and effective way to address the challenges of using noisy projected data. By learning a transformation between projected and gold tags, the model can adapt to systematic errors in the projection process. This is a significant improvement over methods that simply filter or heuristically correct projected labels. The visualization of the learned noise matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides insightful qualitative analysis of the model's behavior, showcasing learned confusions and ignored projections.

2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse set of languages and experimental settings. Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on both simulated and real low-resource scenarios provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported accuracy on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%) is particularly impressive.

3. **Modularity and Generalizability:** The proposed method is relatively straightforward to implement and can be integrated into existing neural network architectures. The concept of learning to weight noisy supervision is also broadly applicable and could be beneficial in other low-resource NLP tasks where distant supervision or crowd-sourced data are used.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Gold Data Size:** The experimental setup consistently uses a small amount of gold-standard data (approximately 1000 tokens). While this is representative of a low-resource scenario, the paper lacks a systematic analysis of how performance changes as the size of the gold dataset increases. A plot of accuracy versus gold annotation size would be invaluable to understand the trade-offs between gold data and projected data, and to determine when the proposed method provides the most benefit.

2. **Tagset Restriction and Generalizability of Projection:** The experiments are limited to cross-lingual projection with identical tagsets. This restriction potentially limits the broader applicability of the method. Exploring the performance with differing tagsets would enhance the practical impact of the research and assess its suitability for more complex scenarios. Furthermore, investigating the applicability to other noisy supervision sources such as crowdsourcing would broaden the scope of the work.

3. **Equal Weighting of Loss Terms:** The paper employs equal weighting for the cross-entropy loss terms associated with gold and projected data. The rationale for this equal weighting is not fully explained and could be suboptimal. A sensitivity analysis varying the weighting of these terms could reveal optimal configurations and potentially further improve performance. The impact of different weighting schemes should be investigated.

4. **Clarification of Projected Label Representation:** The paper does not fully clarify the representation of projected labels (ỹ). Is ỹ a token-level assignment of a single tag, or is it an aggregated type-level distribution over tags, particularly for words with multiple or no alignments? A more precise description of this representation is necessary for a complete understanding of the model.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a plot of POS tagging accuracy as a function of the amount of gold-standard training data used?
2. Have you experimented with different weighting schemes for the gold and projected data loss terms? If so, what were the results?
3. How is the projected label ỹ represented for tokens with multiple alignments or no alignments? Is it a distribution over tags, and if so, how is that distribution computed?
4. Do you foresee challenges adapting this approach to scenarios where the source and target languages use different tagsets?
