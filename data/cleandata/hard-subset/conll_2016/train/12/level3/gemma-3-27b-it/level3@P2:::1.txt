This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, leveraging cross-lingual projection and explicitly modelling the noise inherent in this process. The authors propose a bidirectional LSTM model with a “noise layer” to learn to correct errors from projected tags, trained jointly on gold-standard and projected data. The core contribution lies in this explicit noise modelling, allowing the system to effectively learn *when* and *how much* to trust the projected data. A secondary, but significant, contribution is the demonstration of strong performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving state-of-the-art results. The experimental validation, encompassing eight simulated low-resource settings alongside the two real languages, provides robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Noise Modelling:** The explicit modelling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a strong and innovative aspect of this work. Previous approaches primarily focused on heuristics for cleaning projected data *before* learning. This method integrates noise handling directly into the learning process, offering a more adaptable and potentially more accurate solution. The visualisations of the learned noise matrices (Figures 3 & 4) provide compelling insight into how the model learns to mitigate projection errors.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the proposed method across eight simulated languages and two real low-resource languages is a significant strength. Achieving state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, particularly given the limited data available, demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach. The performance gains are well-documented and statistically convincing.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation setup, including both simulated and real low-resource scenarios, is thorough and provides a comprehensive assessment of the method's generalization ability. The use of established benchmarks (CoNLL data, Garrette & Baldridge datasets) allows for direct comparison with previous work.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the core ideas and experimental results easy to follow. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figure 1, Figure 2) enhances understanding of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificial Evaluation Setup:** While the simulation experiments offer a controlled evaluation environment, the simulated low-resource setting – artificially reducing the gold annotation size from existing rich-resource data – may not fully reflect the realities of working with truly low-resource languages. As another reviewer pointed out, the cost of acquiring parallel data often differs substantially from the cost of direct annotation, a nuance not fully addressed. The authors should acknowledge this limitation in the final version.
2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section could be strengthened by including more recent work on annotation projection and noise reduction techniques. Specifically, the authors should cite and discuss Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015) which present relevant approaches to leveraging noisy projected data. Failing to engage with this recent work weakens the positioning of the contribution.
3. **Data Availability:** The paper does not explicitly state whether the code and data used in the experiments will be made publicly available. While I assumed availability during my evaluation, it is crucial to state this clearly, as open access to resources is essential for reproducibility and further research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the choice of the specific noise transformation matrix (A) and explore alternative parameterizations? 
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection? Would using more sophisticated alignment techniques (beyond Giza++) lead to further improvements?
3. The model seems to perform better when the parallel corpus is larger (Malagasy vs. Kinyarwanda). Could you provide more detailed analysis on the interaction between parallel data size and model performance?

**Additional Comments:**

Please clarify whether the models are publicly available (code and data). This information is critical for reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses outlined above, this is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The novel noise modelling technique, combined with the strong empirical results, makes this paper a valuable addition to the CONLL 2016 proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
