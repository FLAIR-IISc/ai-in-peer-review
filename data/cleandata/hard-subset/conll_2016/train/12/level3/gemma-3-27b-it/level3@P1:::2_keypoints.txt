- Proposal: introduce a neural POS tagger with a learnable “noise layer” to correct errors from cross-lingual projected tags and jointly train on gold and distant supervision.  
- Empirical result: matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).  
- Main contribution: learnable noise transformation that compensates for projection errors within a deep-learning framework rather than via pre/post heuristics.  
- Secondary contribution: strong empirical validation using very limited gold data (as little as 1,000 tokens), showing robustness and generalizability.  
- Model properties: simple, modular addition to a BiLSTM that adds minimal complexity and can scale to larger parallel corpora or complex tagsets.  
- Analysis: detailed inspection of learned noise matrices reveals interpretable tag-confusion patterns and insight into error correction.  
- Strength: elegant, intuitive solution to noisy cross-lingual projections that leverages projected data effectively.  
- Strength: extensive experiments with consistent improvements and clear motivation compared to prior work.  
- Weakness: reliance on extremely small gold datasets raises questions about generalizability to settings with more (but still limited) annotated data.  
- Weakness: paper evaluates only identical tagset projection; applicability to differing tagsets, crowdsourced noise, or typologically distant language pairs is unexplored.  
- Weakness: equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the loss seems arbitrary and merits hyperparameter tuning or justification.  
- Clarification needed: representation of projected label \tilde{y} (token-level vs aggregated distribution) and handling of unaligned words with no projected tag.  
- Practical question: how sensitive is performance to the gold/noisy loss weighting and what is the computational overhead of the noise layer versus a standard BiLSTM?  
- Minor fixes: clarify description of \tilde{y} (line 357) and correct a bracket typo (line 267).
