This paper introduces a novel approach to sequence tagging for low-resource languages, specifically addressing the challenges of noisy data arising from cross-lingual projection. The authors propose a neural network model incorporating an explicit “noise layer” to learn and correct errors inherent in projected annotations, enabling joint learning from both gold-standard and distantly supervised data. The primary contribution lies in this modeling of projection noise within a deep learning framework, allowing the system to learn when and how much to trust the projected data. A secondary, but significant, contribution is the demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach across a comprehensive set of experiments, including eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), consistently equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art results.  Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix, provides valuable insights into the behavior of the model and the nature of projection errors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Noise Modeling:** The explicit modeling of projection noise as a learnable transformation is a significant advance. Previous work often relies on heuristics or constraints to *filter* noisy projections, whereas this work *adapts* to the noise, potentially leveraging more of the available data. The noise layer effectively learns to de-noise the projected labels, leading to improved performance. This is particularly compelling as it addresses a core challenge in cross-lingual transfer learning.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the proposed model across a diverse set of languages and experimental settings is a strong indicator of its effectiveness. Achieving state-of-the-art results on both simulated and real low-resource languages demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.  The reported accuracy gains on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%) are notable, especially given the limited amount of gold-standard data used.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison to several strong baselines, an ablation study to assess the contribution of the noise layer, and an analysis of the learned noise transformation matrix (Figure 3 & 4). This detailed analysis helps to validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and provides insights into its inner workings. The visualization of the 'A' matrix is particularly insightful, revealing patterns of confusion between tags and informing understanding of the types of errors the model learns to correct.
4. **Well-Defined Problem and Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource POS tagging and the challenges associated with relying on noisy cross-lingual projections. The motivation for the proposed approach is well-justified, and the technical details are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificiality of Simulated Low-Resource Setting:** While the use of simulated low-resource settings allows for controlled comparison with existing methods, the setup – using only the first 1000 tokens of the CoNLL data – might not fully reflect the realities of low-resource language scenarios. The costs associated with obtaining projected data (requiring parallel corpora and alignment tools) may be comparable to, or even exceed, the costs of directly annotating data, particularly in contexts where paying annotators is feasible. This limitation should be acknowledged.
2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work on annotation projection. Specifically, the authors should cite and discuss recent relevant studies such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015), which explore different techniques for leveraging noisy projected annotations. This would further contextualize the contributions of this work within the broader research landscape.
3. **Data Availability:** The paper does not explicitly state whether the trained models or the processed data will be made publicly available.  Given the value of the resources created (especially for low-resource languages), making the data and models accessible to the research community would significantly enhance the impact of this work. It was assumed in the evaluation, but the paper should explicitly mention it.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training the model with the noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model without it?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the word alignments used for projection? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or techniques to mitigate the impact of alignment errors?
3. The 'A' matrix visualized in Figures 3 & 4 seems to capture important patterns. Could you provide a more detailed qualitative analysis of these patterns, perhaps highlighting specific tag confusions that the model learns to correct?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to understand.
*   The experimental setup is well-defined and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner.
*   The authors should consider discussing the limitations of their approach, such as its dependence on the availability of parallel data and the potential for bias introduced by the projection process.



This is a well-executed and impactful piece of work. The authors have addressed a significant challenge in low-resource NLP and proposed a novel and effective solution. I recommend acceptance.
