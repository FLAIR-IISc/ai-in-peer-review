- Primary contribution: proposes a bidirectional LSTM with a learned “noise layer” to model cross-lingual projection noise and jointly train on gold and projected data.  
- Empirical contribution: strong results on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, outperforming or matching prior work (Malagasy 86.3%, Kinyarwanda 82.5%).  
- Practical contribution: method is simple, modular, and potentially applicable to other low-resource tasks using noisy supervision.  

Strengths:
- Novel noise modeling: the noise layer learns transformations between projected and gold tags, adapting to systematic projection errors.  
- Qualitative analysis: learned noise matrices (Figures 3–4) provide insight into confusions and ignored projections.  
- Strong empirical validation: consistent performance across diverse languages and settings.  
- Modularity: straightforward to integrate into existing neural architectures and generalize to other noisy supervision scenarios.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited gold-data analysis: no systematic study of performance as gold-standard dataset size varies; needs accuracy vs. gold-size plot.  
- Tagset and projection limitations: experiments assume identical tagsets, limiting assessment of applicability to differing tagsets or other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing).  
- Equal loss weighting: uses equal weights for gold and projected losses without justification; sensitivity analysis of weighting is missing.  
- Unclear projected-label representation: unclear whether ỹ is a token-level single tag or a type-level/distributional label for tokens with multiple/no alignments.  

Questions to authors:
- Please provide POS accuracy as a function of gold-standard training data size.  
- Have you tried different weightings for gold vs. projected loss terms, and what were the results?  
- How is the projected label ỹ represented for tokens with multiple or no alignments (single tag vs. distribution; computation method)?  
- What challenges do you foresee when adapting the approach to source/target languages with different tagsets?
