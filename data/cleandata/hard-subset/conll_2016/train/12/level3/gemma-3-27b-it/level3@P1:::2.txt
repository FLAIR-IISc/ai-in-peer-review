## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper proposes a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging by explicitly modeling the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The authors introduce a neural network model with a “noise layer” that learns to correct errors from projected tags, effectively integrating both gold-standard and distant supervision data in a joint training framework. The model achieves strong empirical results, equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art performance on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real low-resource languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the insightful incorporation of a learnable noise transformation to bridge the gap between projected and gold-standard annotations. This approach moves beyond simple heuristics or constraints applied *before* or *during* learning, instead learning to compensate for projection errors directly *within* a powerful deep learning framework. The resulting model demonstrates the efficacy of this approach, demonstrating the value of effectively weighting the contribution of noisy, but readily available, projected data alongside limited gold annotations. A second contribution is the strong empirical validation of the proposed method. The consistent outperformance across a diverse set of languages—both simulated and genuinely low-resource—provides compelling evidence for the method’s robustness and generalizability.  The experimental setup, using extremely limited gold data (as little as 1000 tokens), showcases the potential of this method for truly resource-scarce scenarios. Finally, the method itself is relatively simple and modular, suggesting potential applicability to other low-resource NLP tasks beyond POS tagging.

Several strengths support a positive assessment of this submission. First, the proposed model offers an elegant and intuitive solution to a common problem in cross-lingual transfer learning: handling noisy projections. The noise layer is a parsimonious addition to a standard BiLSTM architecture, adding minimal complexity while allowing the model to adaptively learn the mapping between projected and ground-truth tags. Second, the extensive experimental evaluation provides strong support for the proposed approach. The results are consistently competitive, and in many cases surpass existing state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise layer in leveraging projected data. Third, the authors clearly articulate the motivation for their work, acknowledging the limitations of existing approaches and highlighting the need for a more nuanced approach to handling noisy labels.  The framework lends itself naturally to scaling with larger parallel corpora and more complex tagsets. Finally, the detailed analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the types of errors the model is correcting and reveals interesting patterns of tag confusion.

Despite these strengths, several weaknesses require consideration. The reliance on a very limited amount of gold data (1000 tokens) raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. While impressive, the results may be overly optimistic in settings where larger, albeit still small, annotated corpora are available. A more thorough analysis of performance as a function of gold data size would strengthen the conclusions. It would be valuable to see a learning curve plotting accuracy against increasing amounts of gold data to understand when the proposed method provides the most benefit and when it might even hinder performance. Second, the paper focuses exclusively on cross-lingual projection using identical tagsets. Exploring the method’s applicability to scenarios with differing tagsets, or to other sources of noisy labels (e.g., crowdsourcing), would broaden its potential impact.  A natural extension would be to investigate the performance on projections between languages with significantly different typological properties. Third, the equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the loss function seems somewhat arbitrary. An investigation of optimal weighting strategies, perhaps through hyperparameter tuning, could further improve performance. The rationale for this equal weighting should also be elaborated. 

Finally, some clarification is needed regarding the representation of projected labels. Is the projected label \tilde{y} represented at the token level, or is an aggregated distribution over tag types used?  How does the method handle words that are never aligned in the parallel corpus, resulting in no projected tag? This should be clarified in the paper.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a learning curve showing the relationship between the size of the gold annotated dataset and the POS tagging accuracy?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the weighting between the gold and noisy label loss terms?  Have you explored different weighting strategies?
3.  Could you elaborate on the representation of the projected labels \tilde{y}? Specifically, how are words without alignments handled?
4.  How computationally expensive is the training process with the added noise layer compared to a standard BiLSTM model?
