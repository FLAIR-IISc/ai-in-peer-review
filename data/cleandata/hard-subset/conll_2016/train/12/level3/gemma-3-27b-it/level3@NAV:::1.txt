## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging leveraging cross-lingual projection and a neural network architecture designed to explicitly model and correct for the noise inherent in projected annotations. The core idea – using a ‘noise layer’ to learn the transformation between projected and gold-standard tags – is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The experimental results across eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda) demonstrate consistently strong performance, equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art results. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the effective integration of noise modeling within a deep learning framework for cross-lingual projection. The authors don't simply *attempt* to clean projected data, but rather *learn* how to best utilize it given its inherent imperfections, resulting in significant gains, particularly in the real-language evaluation. A secondary, but important contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation. Thoroughly testing the approach across a range of languages and against strong baselines builds confidence in the robustness and generalizability of the method. Finally, the insightful analysis of the learned noise transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4) provides valuable qualitative understanding of the model's behavior and the nature of projection errors.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The correction of the experimental setup flaw from the previous draft is crucial, and the consistently strong performance across both simulated and real datasets is compelling. The method is well-motivated by the practical challenges of low-resource NLP and provides a clear pathway for utilizing readily available parallel corpora.  The inclusion of both simulation and real-world experiments significantly strengthens the evaluation.

However, the paper would benefit from acknowledging a caveat noted by another reviewer: the setup is somewhat artificial. The assumption of relatively easy access to aligned parallel data for projection may not hold true for *all* low-resource languages, as the cost of finding suitable parallel data can sometimes exceed the cost of annotation. Additionally, the related work section should be expanded to include relevant research on *annotation projection* specifically. Papers like Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015) explore related techniques for transferring annotations and offer valuable context.

Finally, the authors should explicitly state whether the data and code used in the experiments will be made publicly available. I operated under the assumption of public availability when evaluating this work, and making the resources accessible would greatly enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training the model, particularly the noise transformation matrix A, as the tagset size increases?
2.  The analysis of the noise transformation matrix is insightful. Could you provide more examples of specific linguistic phenomena that are captured by the learned transformation (beyond those mentioned in the paper)?
