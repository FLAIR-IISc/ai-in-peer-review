## Review of "Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection"

This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging, addressing the noise inherent in cross-lingual projection. The core idea – augmenting an RNN with a learned “noise layer” to model projection errors and jointly train on gold and projected data – is well-motivated and reasonably executed. The empirical results, demonstrating competitive performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages, are a significant achievement. The method's modularity and potential applicability to other low-resource tasks are also strong points.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the effective integration of a differentiable noise model directly into the sequence tagging architecture. Previous work often relies on heuristics to clean projected data; this approach *learns* to handle the noise, offering a more principled and potentially adaptable solution. A secondary, but still important, contribution is the thorough empirical validation across a diverse set of languages, including real-world scenarios. Finally, the experimental setup, despite its limitations (discussed below), is commendably focused on a truly low-resource setting (training with ~1000 tokens).

However, several weaknesses warrant attention. The most significant is the lack of analysis of performance as a function of gold data size. A plot showing accuracy versus the number of gold annotations would be crucial to understand when the proposed method provides the most benefit and whether it could even *harm* performance with sufficient gold data. Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to cross-lingual projection with identical tagsets. Exploring the applicability to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) and different tagsets would broaden the impact. The equal weighting of gold and noisy labels in the objective function also seems somewhat arbitrary; an ablation study tuning this weighting could reveal important insights. 

Finally, the paper requires clarification on a couple of points. The representation of projected labels (token-level vs. aggregated type-level distribution) should be explicitly stated. Also, the handling of words that are never aligned during projection needs to be described in more detail. Minor editorial issues exist, as noted in the provided feedback (lines 357 and 267).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a plot of POS tagging accuracy as a function of the size of the gold annotation dataset?
2.  How does the proposed method perform when applied to noisy data sources *other* than cross-lingual projection (e.g., crowdsourced annotations)?
3.  Have you experimented with weighting the gold and noisy label contributions differently in the objective function? If so, what were the results?
4.  Please clarify how the projected labels (ỹ) are represented and how words with no alignment are handled.
