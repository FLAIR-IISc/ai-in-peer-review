- Introduces a bidirectional LSTM with a learnable “noise layer” to correct errors in cross-lingual tag projection, trained jointly on gold and projected data.  
- Core contribution: explicit modelling of projection noise as a learnable transformation that decides when/how much to trust projected labels.  
- Secondary contribution: strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.  
- Evaluation includes eight simulated low-resource settings plus two real languages, showing consistent improvements and robust generalization.  
- Visualizations of learned noise matrices provide interpretable insight into how the model mitigates projection errors.  
- Uses established benchmarks (CoNLL, Garrette & Baldridge) for comparative evaluation.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized, with helpful illustrative examples and figures.  
- Concern: simulated low-resource experiments (downsampling rich-resource data) may not fully reflect real low-resource conditions or costs of acquiring parallel data.  
- Related work discussion is limited; should cite and engage with Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015) and other recent annotation-projection/noise-reduction methods.  
- Unclear whether code and experimental data will be released—important for reproducibility.  
- Question: rationale for the specific noise transformation matrix (A) and whether alternative parameterizations were considered.  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to word alignment quality and would better aligners than Giza++ improve results?  
- Question: request deeper analysis of how parallel corpus size interacts with model performance (e.g., Malagasy vs. Kinyarwanda).  
- Overall: well-executed, significant contribution to low-resource NLP; recommended for acceptance.
