This paper introduces a novel method for improving cross-lingual projection of POS tags in low-resource settings. The authors address the inherent noise in projected annotations by incorporating a “noise layer” into a BiLSTM sequence tagger, allowing the model to learn which projected tags to trust and which to discount. The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), achieving strong results that often surpass existing state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the elegant and conceptually simple “noise layer” which effectively models and mitigates the errors introduced by cross-lingual projection. This allows for joint training on both gold and projected data, leveraging the benefits of both resources without being overly penalized by the inaccuracies of the latter. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages, demonstrating the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, in particular, are noteworthy. Finally, the work provides a well-motivated and clearly described framework for integrating noisy, distantly supervised data into a neural sequence tagging model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Elegance of the Noise Layer:** The core idea of learning a noise transformation matrix to adjust tag probabilities is both novel and surprisingly effective. This approach provides a principled way to handle the inherent uncertainty in projected annotations, moving beyond simple filtering or heuristic adjustments. The simplicity of the implementation is also a significant advantage.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements across a broad range of languages, including both simulated and real-world low-resource scenarios. Equaling or exceeding state-of-the-art results on all tested languages is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. The improvements are particularly substantial in the real-world language experiments.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup, while having some limitations (discussed in weaknesses), is relatively comprehensive. Evaluating on eight simulated languages and two real languages provides a good sense of the method’s generalizability. The comparison with several strong baselines, including the work of Duong et al. (2014a), further strengthens the findings.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The diagrams (e.g., Figure 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The problem of low-resource NLP is a significant challenge, and this work offers a practical and effective solution for leveraging cross-lingual projection in such settings. The method’s ability to operate with only a small amount of gold data (e.g., 1000 tokens) is particularly appealing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Unrealistic Training Data Size:** The experimental setup, based on training with only ~1000 token annotations, feels somewhat artificial. While the authors motivate this based on the cost of annotation, it is unclear how representative this scenario is of many real-world low-resource NLP applications. More annotations are often available (even if more costly) and the performance of the method with varying amounts of gold data is unknown.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study on Gold Data Size:** Crucially, the paper lacks an analysis of performance *vs.* the amount of gold data. A plot showing accuracy as a function of gold annotation size would be extremely valuable. This would reveal whether the method consistently helps, or if it is only beneficial when the amount of gold data is very limited. It would also clarify whether adding more gold data eventually outweighs the benefits of the noise layer.
3. **Limited Exploration of Tagset Mismatch:** The paper only evaluates cross-lingual projection with the same tagset. The potential applicability of the method to scenarios involving differing tagsets (e.g., projecting from a rich-resource language with a detailed tagset to a low-resource language with a coarser tagset) is not explored. This is a significant limitation, as tagset mismatches are common in real-world applications.
4. **Fixed Weighting of Gold and Noisy Labels:** The authors assign equal weighting to the gold and noisy labels in the objective function. The rationale for this choice is not fully explained, and it is possible that a different weighting scheme would lead to improved performance. A sensitivity analysis varying the relative contribution of the two terms would be informative.
5. **Ambiguity in Projected Label Representation:** The representation of the projected labels (ỹ) is not fully clear. Is it a token-level prediction or an aggregated type-level distribution? Additionally, the paper does not explicitly address how words that are never aligned during projection are handled. Clarification on these points is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of the proposed method change as the amount of gold annotation data increases? Please provide a plot showing accuracy as a function of gold annotation size.
2. Could you elaborate on how the projected labels (ỹ) are represented? Specifically, is it a token-level prediction or an aggregated type-level distribution? How are unaligned words handled?
3. Did you experiment with different weighting schemes for the gold and noisy labels in the objective function? If so, what were the results? If not, why did you choose to use equal weighting?
4. How computationally expensive is the training process, particularly with the added noise layer? Is it significantly slower than training a standard BiLSTM tagger?
