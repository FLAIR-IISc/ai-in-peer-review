## Review for “Learning when to trust distant supervision: An application to low-resource POS tagging using cross-lingual projection”

This paper presents a novel approach to low-resource POS tagging that addresses the inherent noise in cross-lingual projection by learning a noise transformation layer within a BiLSTM network. The core idea—explicitly modelling the discrepancies between projected and gold-standard tags—is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both simulated and real low-resource languages.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper tackles the challenge of POS tagging in low-resource languages by combining a small amount of gold-standard annotated data with more abundant, but noisy, data generated through cross-lingual projection from a high-resource language (English). Instead of attempting to pre-clean the projected data or implicitly capture correlations between tagsets, the authors propose a neural network model that *learns* the noise inherent in the projection process. This is achieved through a “noise layer” that transforms the projected tags, allowing the model to effectively discount unreliable projections and focus on the more trustworthy annotations. The model is jointly trained on both gold and projected data, and the authors demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages, Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Noise-Aware Model:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a neural network architecture specifically designed to handle noisy cross-lingual projections. The "noise layer" is a clever mechanism for learning the transformation between projected and gold-standard tags, effectively allowing the model to learn when and how much to trust the projected data.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates significant and consistent performance gains on a diverse set of experiments, including both simulated low-resource scenarios and real-world low-resource languages. Achieving state-of-the-art results on both types of data strengthens the validity of the proposed approach.
3. **Practical Methodology & Data:** The work presents a practical methodology for utilizing cross-lingual projection, particularly in scenarios where only limited gold-standard data is available. The reported results on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, languages with limited NLP resources, are particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a core challenge in low-resource NLP: leveraging noisy distant supervision signals. Cross-lingual projection is a powerful technique, but its effectiveness is limited by the inherent noise. This paper directly addresses this issue with a principled and effective approach.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The proposed noise layer is a well-motivated and technically sound solution. It’s conceptually simple but surprisingly effective, and its integration with the BiLSTM architecture is seamless. The mathematical formulation is clear and the explanation is concise.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, including experiments on both simulated and real low-resource languages. The comparison with strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art method from Duong et al. (2014a), provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Reproducibility:** The paper clearly describes the experimental setup, including data sources, preprocessing steps, and model parameters. This facilitates reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.
5. **Corrected Experimental Flaw:** The authors acknowledge and have corrected a flaw in the experimental setup from a previous draft, demonstrating a commitment to rigorous research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Artificial Setup Caveat:** The reviewer correctly pointed out that the experimental setup, while common, is somewhat artificial. The cost of obtaining parallel data versus the cost of annotating data may not accurately reflect the real-world constraints faced by researchers working with truly low-resource languages. The paper would benefit from acknowledging this caveat more explicitly in the discussion.
2. **Missing Related Work:** The paper initially lacked citations to relevant work on annotation projection. While the authors are addressing this (as indicated in the prompt), it is crucial to discuss how the proposed approach differs from and builds upon existing techniques such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015). A deeper engagement with this literature would strengthen the paper's positioning within the field.
3. **Limited Analysis of Noise Layer:** While the paper presents the learned transformation matrices (Figure 3 & 4), a more detailed analysis of these matrices could provide valuable insights into the types of noise the model is learning to correct. What specific tag confusions are most prevalent? Are there language-specific patterns in the noise transformation? This deeper analysis would enhance the paper's contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the experimental setup and discuss how the results might generalize to scenarios where obtaining parallel data is significantly more expensive than annotating data?
2.  Please discuss how your approach compares to, and builds upon, existing work on annotation projection, specifically citing Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agic et al. (2015).
3.  Will the trained models and/or the annotated data used in this study be made publicly available? This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area. (The reviewer assumed public availability during evaluation).

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper's writing is clear and concise.
*   The use of the universal tagset is a good choice for facilitating cross-lingual comparisons.
*   The detailed explanation of the experimental setup is appreciated.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource NLP. The proposed noise-aware model is innovative and effective, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
