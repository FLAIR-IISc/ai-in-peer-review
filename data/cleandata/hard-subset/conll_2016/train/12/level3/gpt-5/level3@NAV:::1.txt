Summary
The paper proposes a POS tagging approach for low-resource languages that jointly learns from a tiny gold dataset and noisy cross-lingual projections by inserting an explicit noise layer atop a BiLSTM tagger. The model is pretrained on gold data, then trained jointly to predict gold tags and to model a confusion matrix that maps clean tags to projected ones. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines and prior work.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
- Primary: An explicit, learnable noise transformation for cross-lingual projection that allows effective joint training on gold and projected tags, yielding state-of-the-art or competitive results across 10 settings.
- Secondary: An empirical study clarifying when projected data helps, including ablations (gold-only, projected-only, naive union) and insightful analyses of learned confusion matrices.
- Tertiary: A simple two-stage training strategy (gold pretraining + joint training) that stabilizes learning with noisy supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea with strong empirical validation: The noise layer consistently improves over BiLSTM baselines and Duong et al. (2014), including on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%).
- Faithful analysis and interpretability: Visualization of the learned noise matrices gives concrete insight into systematic projection biases (e.g., determiner/number confusions), supporting the modeling choice.
- Practical low-resource setup: Strong performance with only ~1k gold tokens mirrors realistic constraints; results on two truly low-resource languages strengthen external validity.
- Experimental rigor: Comparisons against multiple baselines and prior SOTA; the earlier experimental setup flaw raised in review has been corrected in this revision.

Weaknesses
- External validity caveat: As another reviewer noted, the simulation protocol abstracts away the real costs of locating vs. paying annotators; this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.
- Related work coverage: The writeup should cite and contrast with annotation projection and label transfer work such as Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015), clarifying novelty over prior projection/denoising strategies.
- Data/code availability unclear: The paper should explicitly state whether trained models, projection resources, and learned noise matrices will be released; my evaluation assumed public availability.
- Scope of mismatch settings: The text mentions support for mismatched tagsets (rectangular A), but experiments do not demonstrate this; a brief empirical or synthetic test would strengthen the claim.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Will you release code, trained models, projection scripts, and the learned A matrices for all languages?
- Can you comment on robustness to smaller or noisier parallel corpora and to different alignment choices (e.g., including many-to-one alignments)?
- Briefly position your approach vs. Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015): what is newly enabled by the explicit noise layer?

Recommendation: Accept. I previously gave a conditional accept; with the setup correction, I see no remaining obstacles to acceptance.
