Summary
The paper proposes a simple, explicit “noise layer” atop an RNN tagger to model systematic errors in cross-lingual POS projection. The model is trained jointly on a tiny gold corpus and a larger projected corpus: gold labels supervise the base softmax, while projected labels supervise a linearly transformed distribution parameterized by a learned K×K “confusion” matrix. Results show consistent gains over strong baselines on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A modular, interpretable noise-modeling layer that calibrates projected labels during joint training, yielding strong and robust improvements.
2) Empirical validation across 10 languages with competitive or SOTA performance in low-resource regimes.
3) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining, then joint training) to stably exploit noisy supervision.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across many languages: The approach matches or exceeds prior work in 8 simulated and 2 truly low-resource settings, demonstrating reliability and generality.
- Simplicity and modularity: The method is a small, easily pluggable K×K layer that could be reused in other noisy-supervision scenarios (e.g., distant supervision, crowdsourcing), reducing engineering burden.
- Interpretability: Visualizations of the learned confusion matrices provide insight into projection noise patterns and when the model trusts or ignores projected tags.
- Sound joint objective with end-to-end training: The two-term loss cleanly separates supervision from gold vs. noisy data and uses pretraining to avoid bad local optima.

Weaknesses
- Missing data-efficiency analysis: No curves showing performance vs. amount of gold data; it is unclear when the method helps or hurts as gold supervision grows.
- Fixed equal weighting of gold and noisy losses: Not tuning the relative contribution risks under- or over-emphasizing noisy labels; an ablation is needed.
- Limited scope of noise sources: Only cross-lingual projection with the same universal tagset is evaluated; differing tagsets and alternative noisy sources are not explored despite being claimed as applicable.
- Ambiguity in projected label representation: It is unclear whether ỹ is token-level categorical vs. a distribution (sentence-level aggregation), and how unaligned tokens are handled; different choices may materially affect learning.
- Experimental realism: The “~1000 tokens” framing conflicts with using >5k gold tokens in real-language experiments; clarify the setting and applicability.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Please report accuracy vs. gold data size (e.g., 200–2000 tokens) and identify regimes where the noise layer is most beneficial.
- Did you tune a mixing coefficient between gold and projected losses? If tried, how sensitive are results?
- Precisely define ỹ: token-level one-hot vs. soft distribution; how are NULL/multi-alignments encoded?
- Can you show results with mismatched tagsets (rectangular A) and/or another noisy source (e.g., crowd labels)?
- What is the sensitivity to parallel data size/quality and to initialization of A?
