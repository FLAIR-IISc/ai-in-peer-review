Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes a simple yet effective extension to a BiLSTM POS tagger that explicitly models noise in cross-lingual projection. A learnable confusion (noise) layer transforms the model’s clean tag distribution into the projected/noisy label space, enabling joint training on a small gold corpus and large projected data.
- The method is validated on eight simulated low-resource languages and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), equaling or surpassing prior work.
- In my view, the main contributions (in order):
  1) Explicit noise modeling for distant supervision in sequence tagging via a lightweight learnable confusion matrix integrated into the output layer, coupled with a two-stage training recipe (pretrain on gold, then joint training).
  2) Strong empirical results under low-resource conditions, with consistent gains over competitive baselines and prior art.
  3) Interpretability and modularity of the noise layer, suggesting applicability to other noisy supervision regimes.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, minimal modification with strong effect. Modeling projection noise as a learned confusion matrix on top of the BiLSTM output is elegant, adds very few parameters, and is easily interpretable (Figures 3–4 show meaningful patterns, e.g., DET/NOUN/NUM relations and PRT/X confusion). The training setup—pretrain on clean data then jointly optimize with projected data—is practical and well-justified.
- Solid empirical gains across diverse settings. On the 8 simulated languages, the model achieves the best average accuracy (91.7%) vs. Duong et al. (91.3%) and beats BiLSTM Annotated/Projected/Ann+Proj baselines by clear margins. On the real low-resource languages, it sets new best results (Malagasy 86.3% vs. 85.3; Kinyarwanda 82.5% vs. 78.3 for Duong et al.), and also outperforms Garrette & Baldridge on Malagasy while being competitive on Kinyarwanda.
- Thorough and relevant comparisons. The paper includes appropriate baselines: supervised MaxEnt, BiLSTM variants (gold only, projected only, and union), and the prior SOTA. This makes the incremental benefits of the noise layer clear, particularly versus naïvely mixing gold and projected data (BiLSTM Ann+Proj).
- Practical low-resource focus. The simulated setting with only ~1000 gold tokens (following prior work) and the real experiments with limited parallel data align well with the paper’s target use case. The constraints on alignments and sentence selection (IBM Model 3 scores) reflect standard practice and are well-documented.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of supervision regimes. The paper fixes the gold vs. projected loss with equal weighting. Given the heterogeneity of projection quality across languages (e.g., Figures 3–4), a tunable mixing coefficient (or curriculum) is likely important. An ablation sweeping this weight would strengthen the claim that the method adapts to different noise levels.
- Missing data-efficiency curves. A key question for low-resource scenarios is how performance scales with the amount of gold-labeled data. Plotting accuracy vs. gold token count would clarify when the noise layer is most beneficial and whether there are regimes where it hurts or saturates.
- Ambiguity around the projected label representation. The text suggests token-level projected labels are either a single tag (1:1 alignment) or a distribution over tags based on sentence-level tag frequencies for many-to-one/NULL cases. This deserves more explicit specification and justification, as this choice can substantially affect the supervision signal on unaligned or multiply aligned tokens.
- Claims on tagset mismatch and generality are not empirically supported. While the model accommodates mismatched tagsets in principle (rectangular A), there are no experiments beyond the shared universal tagset. Similarly, the broader claim of utility for other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing) is plausible but untested.
- Realism and consistency of gold data setups. The simulated experiments use ~1000 gold tokens, but the real low-resource experiments use ~5k gold tokens per language. The paper should better reconcile these conditions and discuss what users can expect when only ~1k tokens are available in practice.

Questions to authors
- Loss weighting: Did you try a tunable weight between the gold and projected cross-entropy terms? If so, how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter across languages? If not, can you report a small ablation (e.g., λ ∈ {0.2, 0.5, 0.8})?
- Projected label distribution: For tokens aligned to multiple English words or unaligned (NULL), please clarify precisely how ỹ is formed. Is it the normalized frequency distribution of universal tags in the English sentence? Are stopwords or punctuation treated differently? Is any confidence/quality signal from alignments used to weight tokens?
- Use of the noise layer at test time: Am I correct that the noise layer is used only during training on projected data and is not applied at inference (i.e., predictions come from the clean softmax o)? If not, please clarify.
- Data regime: For the real low-resource experiments with ~5k gold tokens, what happens when you restrict training to 1k tokens (to match the simulated setting)? How do performance and the learned A matrices change?
- Initialization and stability: How is A initialized (e.g., identity plus small noise)? Did you observe instability when training from scratch without the two-stage procedure?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the format of ỹ in Section 3.1/3.2 (line ~357), including whether it is always token-level and how distributions are computed.
- Consider reporting statistical significance for key improvements over Duong et al.
