Summary
The paper proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger that learns to exploit noisy cross-lingual projections by inserting an explicit “noise layer” (a learned confusion transformation) between the model’s clean tag distribution and the projected supervision. The model is trained jointly on a very small gold corpus (∼1k tokens) and large quantities of projected labels from parallel text. A two-stage training procedure (pretrain on gold, then jointly train with projections) stabilizes learning. Results exceed or match prior state of the art on 8 simulated low-resource languages and two true low-resource languages (Malagasy and Kinyarwanda). The analysis visualizes learned noise matrices and offers plausible interpretations (e.g., determiner/noun mappings, verb noise).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, principled mechanism to learn when to trust projected supervision: an explicit label-noise transformation (confusion matrix) on top of a BiLSTM tagger, trained jointly with gold and projected labels.
2) A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) that reliably leverages large amounts of noisy projections without harming performance.
3) Strong empirical validation across 10 languages with useful diagnostics (noise matrices), yielding new SOTA in two truly low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Clear, effective modeling of projection noise. The learned K×K transformation cleanly captures systematic biases (e.g., tagset divergences, alignment errors) and demonstrably improves over naïve use of projections. The visualizations support the claims by showing diagonally dominant but informative off-diagonal patterns.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains. The approach equals or outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) on 8 simulated languages and achieves 86.3% (Malagasy) and 82.5% (Kinyarwanda), surpassing prior work. Ablations show that direct projection or naive union can be harmful, underscoring the value of the noise layer.
- Practical low-resource setting and minimal supervision. The 1k-token gold setup is realistic in spirit and aligns with prior literature; the model capitalizes on widely available parallel text.
- Solid analysis and interpretability. The per-language noise matrices enable error diagnosis (e.g., higher noise for Kinyarwanda due to smaller/poorer parallel data), adding credibility beyond headline numbers.
- Corrected experimental setup. An earlier setup flaw has been addressed in the current version; I now see no obstacles to acceptance.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior noisy-label modeling. The noise layer is essentially a learned label confusion matrix, a known idea in learning with noisy labels. Its application to cross-lingual POS projection is sensible but not conceptually deep.
- Some aspects of the evaluation setting are artificial. Simulated low-resource settings using Europarl (clean alignments, abundant parallel data) may overstate gains versus typical low-resource realities. As another reviewer noted, in practice the dominant cost may be finding, not paying, annotators; the paper should explicitly acknowledge this caveat when motivating the 1k-token assumption.
- Comparisons to closely related projection work are missing. Recent large-scale annotation projection methods (e.g., Johannsen et al., 2016; Agić et al., 2015) explore multi-source projection, voting, and sentence selection strategies. The paper should position its approach w.r.t. these, and ideally include baselines or discussion of complementarity.
- Limited exploration of design choices for A. It is unclear how sensitive training is to the initialization/regularization of A, whether diagonal dominance is encouraged, and how the method behaves with mismatched tagsets in practice (the text mentions support but provides no empirical evidence).
- Dependency on alignment/tagger quality and projection heuristics. The approach relies on one-to-one alignments and sentence filtering; it would help to quantify performance sensitivity to alignment noise, tagger domain mismatch, or using all alignments.

Questions to Authors
- Will code, trained models, alignments, and processed projected labels be released? Please also clarify licensing/availability for the Malagasy and Kinyarwanda resources. My evaluation assumed public availability.
- How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized? Did you try identity initialization, L2 regularization toward identity, or temperature scaling to stabilize training? Any observed degenerate solutions?
- Did you attempt multi-source projection (several source languages) or voting/aggregation over multiple projections? How would A extend to that setting?
- Can you report sensitivity to the amount/quality of parallel data (learning curves) and to using many-to-one/one-to-many alignments?
- Have you tried adding a CRF layer on top of BiLSTM, and if so, does the noise layer still help?

Additional comments
- Please add and discuss related work on annotation projection, including Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015), and clarify how your method differs/complements them (e.g., explicit noise modeling vs multi-source voting/selection).
- Please acknowledge explicitly that the 1k-token setup is a modeling convenience; for truly low-resource scenarios, effort often goes into identifying/organizing annotators rather than per-token cost alone.
- Recommendation: Accept. This is a clean, well-motivated idea with strong empirical results and useful analysis; with minor additions to related work and a brief caveat on the setup, it will make a solid contribution.
