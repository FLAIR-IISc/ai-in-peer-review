Summary
This paper proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger augmented with an explicit “noise” layer that models the mapping from clean tags to noisy projections. The model is trained jointly on a small gold-annotated corpus and a large parallel corpus with projected tags. The noise layer is a learned linear transform applied to the model’s clean output distribution before a second softmax, encouraging the model to match the statistics of projected tags while backpropagating a de-noised signal to the base tagger. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two genuinely low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines, including Duong et al. (2014a). The learned confusion matrices provide interpretable insight into projection noise patterns.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, modular output-layer modification that explicitly models label noise from projection, enabling effective joint training on gold and projected annotations.
2) Strong empirical validation across 10 languages, including two real low-resource cases, with consistent improvements over prior state of the art and neural baselines under low-resource supervision.
3) Qualitative analysis via learned noise matrices that sheds light on cross-lingual projection errors.

Strengths
- Elegant and practical method for noisy supervision: The introduced noise layer (softmax(A o)) is a compact, interpretable way to capture consistent projection confusions without complicating the base tagger. It cleanly separates “what the tagger believes” from “what projection tends to look like,” and the two-stage training mitigates early noise domination.
- Strong empirical results across diverse settings: On eight simulated languages, the method improves average accuracy over Duong et al. (91.7 vs. 91.3) and beats all BiLSTM baselines, including joint training without a noise layer (e.g., Danish 92.3 vs. 85.4/90.2 for Annotated/Ann+Proj). On Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, it achieves 86.3 and 82.5, outperforming Duong et al. (85.3, 78.3) and the supervised BiLSTM (81.5, 76.9). The gains are especially notable in the truly low-resource scenarios.
- Modularity and potential broader utility: The approach is model-agnostic with minimal overhead and should port to other sequence labeling tasks and noisy supervision regimes (e.g., distant supervision, crowdsourcing) with little engineering.
- Insightful analysis of noise: The learned matrices (Figures 3–4) clearly reveal language-specific confusions (e.g., NOUN→ADJ/NUM in Malagasy; PRT↔X in Swedish) and diagonal dominance where projection is reliable. This both validates the approach and is valuable diagnostic information.
- Sensible experimental design choices: Pretraining on gold labels, then joint training, is a reasonable and effective recipe. The comparisons include both neural and non-neural baselines under the same low-resource constraints.

Weaknesses
- Missing analysis of performance vs. amount of gold data: The paper focuses on ~1k gold tokens in simulations and ~5k in real languages, but does not show how accuracy varies as a function of gold supervision. A plot of accuracy across several gold sizes (e.g., 500/1k/2k/5k/10k) is important to establish when the noise layer helps or harms relative to purely supervised training.
- Fixed equal weighting of gold and noisy losses: The objective sums two cross-entropy terms with equal weight. Given the vastly different reliability of the two supervision sources, this design choice should be validated. A simple sweep over mixing weights or a schedule (e.g., curriculum or temperature on the noise term) could reveal better trade-offs.
- Scope limited to cross-lingual projection with the same tagset: Although a rectangular A allows for tagset mismatch in principle, the paper only evaluates same-tagset projection. Applicability to other noisy label sources (crowdsourcing, dictionaries) and to tagset mismatches (e.g., mapping UD to language-specific tags) is not empirically explored.
- Projection labeling details and handling of unaligned/multi-aligned tokens: The representation of ỹ is not consistently clear. The text describes one-to-one alignments receiving a token-level tag and many-to-one/unaligned tokens receiving a sentence-level tag distribution. This unusual choice warrants justification and ablation (e.g., type-level distributions, uniform over plausible tags, or dropping unaligned tokens) and clearer documentation.
- External validity of the low-resource setup: Training with only ~1k gold tokens for simulated settings is very stringent; many practical low-resource scenarios can obtain 5k–20k tokens. It would be useful to show that the benefits persist at higher annotation budgets and to discuss annotation cost trade-offs relative to gains from parallel data and the noise layer.

Questions to Authors (to address in rebuttal)
- Loss weighting: Did you experiment with different weights between gold and projected losses, or with curriculum/mixing schedules? If so, what was the sensitivity across languages?
- Gold-data scaling: How does the method perform as gold annotation increases? Please include plots of accuracy vs. gold size for at least a few languages, and indicate the crossover point where purely supervised BiLSTM catches up.
- Representation of projected labels ỹ: Please clarify precisely whether ỹ is a one-hot tag (for 1–1 alignments) and a sentence-level tag distribution otherwise. Did you try token-level distributions derived from local alignments or type-level priors? How do you represent words never aligned across the entire parallel corpus?
- Constraints/regularization on A: Are entries unconstrained? Did you try logit-space modeling (A applied to logits), diagonal priors, or encouraging A to be near identity to prevent pathologies? Any language-specific sensitivity?
- Sensitivity to projection heuristics: How do results change if you include many-to-one/one-to-many alignments, different alignment models, or less aggressive sentence filtering? Is the method robust to noisier projections?
- Generalization beyond projection: Have you tried the noise layer with other noisy supervision sources (e.g., crowdsourced POS, distant supervision for NER) or with mismatched tagsets (rectangular A)? Even small-scale experiments would strengthen the general claim of modularity.
