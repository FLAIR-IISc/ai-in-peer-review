- Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit learned “noise layer” (confusion matrix) between clean tag distribution and noisy cross-lingual projections, trained jointly with ~1k gold tokens and large projected data using a two-stage training recipe.

- Contribution: introduces a simple, principled label-noise transformation on top of a BiLSTM to learn when to trust projected supervision.

- Contribution: provides a practical training protocol (pretrain on gold, then joint training) that leverages noisy projections without degrading performance.

- Contribution: shows strong empirical results across 10 languages (including Malagasy and Kinyarwanda), with diagnostics (noise matrices) and new SOTA on two true low-resource languages.

- Strength: clearly models projection noise; learned K×K matrices capture systematic biases and off-diagonal errors, supported by visualizations.

- Strength: consistent empirical gains, outperforming or matching prior work on simulated and real low-resource languages; ablations show naive projection can be harmful.

- Strength: realistic minimal-supervision setup (1k tokens) and practical use of widely available parallel text.

- Strength: solid interpretability and analysis; per-language noise matrices aid error diagnosis and explain performance differences.

- Strength: corrected prior experimental issues; reviewer sees no obstacles to acceptance now.

- Weakness: methodological novelty is incremental—noise matrix idea is known in noisy-label literature and not deeply novel.

- Weakness: some evaluation settings are artificial (Europarl simulations, clean alignments, abundant parallel data) and may overstate gains; 1k-token assumption needs caveating.

- Weakness: missing comparisons to related projection work (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agić et al. 2015) and lacking baselines for multi-source/voting strategies.

- Weakness: limited exploration of design choices for the noise matrix A (initialization, regularization, encouraging diagonal dominance, handling mismatched tagsets).

- Weakness: depends on alignment/tagger quality and projection heuristics; lacks quantification of sensitivity to alignment noise and many-to-one/one-to-many alignments.

- Question: will code, trained models, alignments, and processed projected labels be released, and what is the licensing/availability of Malagasy and Kinyarwanda resources?

- Question: how is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized (identity init, L2 toward identity, temperature scaling), and were there degenerate solutions?

- Question: was multi-source projection or voting/aggregation attempted, and how would A extend to multi-source settings?

- Question: can you report sensitivity to amount/quality of parallel data (learning curves) and to using many-to-one/one-to-many alignments?

- Question: was a CRF layer on top of BiLSTM tried, and does the noise layer still help with CRF?

- Additional: add and discuss related work on annotation projection (Johannsen et al., Agić et al.) and clarify differences/complementarity.

- Additional: explicitly acknowledge that the 1k-token setup is a modeling convenience and that real low-resource efforts often involve locating/organizing annotators.

- Recommendation: Accept — clean, well-motivated method with strong empirical results and useful analysis; minor related-work additions and a caveat on the setup requested.
