- Summary: Proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with a learned noise layer modeling clean→noisy tag mappings, trained jointly on small gold and large projected-tag corpora, improving accuracy and offering interpretable confusion matrices.
- Contribution 1: Introduces a simple, modular output-layer noise model enabling joint training on gold and projected annotations.
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates consistent empirical gains across eight simulated and two real low-resource languages, outperforming prior work and neural baselines.
- Contribution 3: Provides qualitative analysis via learned confusion matrices that reveal interpretable projection error patterns.
- Strength: Elegant, practical noise-layer design that separates model beliefs from projection statistics and mitigates early noise domination.
- Strength: Strong and consistent empirical improvements, especially in truly low-resource settings (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Strength: Method is model-agnostic and likely transferable to other sequence-labeling/noisy-supervision scenarios with low overhead.
- Strength: Learned noise matrices give clear, language-specific diagnostics (e.g., NOUN→ADJ/NUM in Malagasy).
- Strength: Reasonable experimental protocol (pretrain on gold then joint train) and fair baseline comparisons.
- Weakness: No analysis of performance as a function of gold-data size; missing accuracy vs. gold-size curves to show when the noise layer helps or hurts.
- Weakness: Equal weighting of gold and projected losses is unvalidated; no sweeps or schedules for mixing weights are reported.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to same-tagset projection; rectangular A and other noisy-label sources (crowdsourcing, tagset mismatch) not empirically tested.
- Weakness: Unclear representation/handling of projected labels ỹ for unaligned/multi-aligned tokens; atypical sentence-level tag distribution choice needs justification/ablation.
- Weakness: External validity concerns—extremely small gold budgets (∼1k) may not reflect many practical low-resource scenarios; no analysis at larger budgets.
- Question: Did you try different weights or curriculum schedules between gold and projected losses, and how sensitive are results across languages?
- Question: How does accuracy scale with increasing gold annotations; please provide plots and the crossover point where supervised BiLSTM matches joint training?
- Question: Precisely how is ỹ represented (one-hot for 1–1 alignments, sentence-level distribution otherwise); were token/type-level distributions or drop strategies tried for unaligned words?
- Question: Are entries of A constrained or regularized (e.g., applied to logits, identity priors); any experiments to avoid pathological A behavior?
- Question: How sensitive are results to projection heuristics (many-to-one/one-to-many alignments, alignment models, sentence filtering)?
- Question: Any experiments applying the noise layer to other noisy supervision sources or mismatched tagsets (rectangular A)?
- Additional comment: Clarify line 357 about ỹ format (token vs. distribution) and fix line 267 bracket typo.
- Additional comment: Report standard deviations or multiple-run results given small gold data variance.
- Additional comment: Report the ratio of projected to gold tokens per epoch and the minibatch mixing strategy.
