- Paper tackles low-resource POS tagging by adding a learned output-noise transformation on top of a BiLSTM to model cross-lingual projection noise and jointly train on small gold and larger projected corpora.
- Training is two-stage (pretrain on gold, then joint training); evaluated on eight simulated and two real low-resource languages with consistent gains over prior work.
- Main contribution: a simple, modular K×K output-noise layer that models confusion between clean and projected tags.
- Main contribution: strong empirical improvements across 10 languages, including Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, outperforming prior projection baselines.
- Main contribution: qualitative analysis of learned noise matrices that reveals systematic projection errors and language-specific confusions.
- Strength: principled, end-to-end learnable modeling of projection noise that integrates with backpropagation rather than relying on heuristics.
- Strength: consistent empirical gains and broad evaluation, showing robustness across simulated and real low-resource settings.
- Strength: practical experimental setup reflecting realistic annotation budgets (1,000 gold tokens) and modest-quality parallel data.
- Strength: interpretability of the noise matrix A provides diagnostic value for projection errors.
- Strength: method is architecturally minimal and likely transferable to other sequence-tagging/noisy-supervision scenarios.
- Weakness: no ablation varying gold-data size, so unclear how benefits scale with more or less gold supervision.
- Weakness: loss equally weights gold and projected objectives without tuning a balancing coefficient (λ), potentially affecting performance in low-quality projection regimes.
- Weakness: limited empirical validation of broader applicability claims (rectangular A for tagset mismatches, other noisy sources like crowdsourcing).
- Weakness: baselines omit common strong enhancements (CRF decoding, character features, pretrained embeddings), leaving open whether gains persist against stronger neural baselines.
- Weakness: clarity gaps on projected label representation (token vs. sentence-level, NULL/multi-alignments), initialization/regularization of A, and inconsistency around reported gold-token counts.
- Question: did you try weighting the projected loss with a λ and how sensitive are results to λ, especially for Kinyarwanda?
- Question: how are projected labels ỹ represented (one-hot token-level vs. sentence-level distributions), and how are NULL/multi-alignments and smoothing handled?
- Question: how is A initialized and regularized (identity bias, normalization, L1/L2, priors) to avoid degenerate solutions?
- Question: can you provide learning curves (accuracy vs. gold-token counts e.g., 100/300/1k/5k) and Malagasy/Kinyarwanda results at 1k to match simulated settings?
- Question: were stronger baselines (BiLSTM-CRF, char-aware, pretrained embeddings) evaluated, and are the noise-layer gains orthogonal to those enhancements?
- Question: how do results vary with parallel-data quantity/quality and alignment filtering (beyond 1–1), and any domain-mismatch analysis?
- Question: is there an empirical demo of rectangular A for tagset mismatches and its impact?
- Question: at inference time, do you use the clean output only (o) or include the noise layer—please clarify.
- Additional comment: quantify properties of A (diagonal mass, entropy, off-diagonal mass) and correlate with accuracy to strengthen analysis.
- Additional comment: address minor editorial issues, provide code/hyperparameters/random seeds, and fix typos for reproducibility.
