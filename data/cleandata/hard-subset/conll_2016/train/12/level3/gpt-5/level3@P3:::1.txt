Summary
The paper proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger that jointly learns from a tiny gold-annotated corpus (≈1k tokens) and noisy cross-lingual projected tags. The key idea is an explicit noise layer (a learned confusion matrix A) that transforms the model’s clean tag distribution into the distribution observed in projected data, enabling end-to-end training that “learns when to trust” projections. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two truly low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An explicit, simple, and effective noise model for cross-lingual tag projection integrated into a neural sequence tagger, with joint training on gold and projected data.
- Secondary: Strong empirical results across 10 languages, including real low-resource cases, with interpretable analysis of learned noise matrices.
- Tertiary: A practical training recipe (gold pretraining + joint training) that robustly leverages very small gold datasets together with projected supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling of projection noise with strong empirical benefits. The learned A matrix directly captures systematic projection errors and attenuates unreliable signals. This yields consistent improvements over both a BiLSTM trained on gold-only or naively mixing gold+projected, and over Duong et al. (2014a). On the eight simulated languages, your model averages 91.7 vs. 91.3; on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda, 86.3 and 82.5 vs. 85.3 and 78.3, respectively—substantive gains, especially in the noisier Kinyarwanda setting.
- Interpretable analysis. Visualizations of A reveal sensible patterns (e.g., strong diagonals, plausible confusions such as NOUN→ADJ/NUM in Malagasy), lending insight into cross-lingual divergences and alignment-induced errors, and demonstrating the method’s transparency.
- Realistic low-resource supervision. The main setup uses ≈1k gold tokens plus parallel data, aligning with prior work and common practical constraints. Results on Malagasy/Kinyarwanda (public datasets, standard alignment/tagging tools) indicate the approach’s viability beyond simulated settings.
- Experimental clarity and corrected setup. The flaw that affected an earlier draft’s experimental setup has been corrected; the revised setup is sound, comparisons are fair, and the findings are credible. This addresses my previous conditional acceptance: I now see no obstacles to acceptance.
- Simple and broadly applicable. The approach is lightweight (a single confusion layer), easy to implement, and likely portable to other sequence tagging tasks or distant supervision regimes.

Weaknesses
- External validity for truly low-resource scenarios. The setup still relies on sizable parallel data (Europarl-scale for simulations, nontrivial for Malagasy; less for Kinyarwanda). As another reviewer noted, in many truly low-resource situations, the bottleneck is finding annotators rather than paying them, and parallel data may be scarce or domain-mismatched. Please acknowledge this caveat and clarify applicability when parallel resources are limited.
- Missing discussion of closely related projection work. The related work should discuss and cite recent annotation projection literature, e.g., Agić et al. (2015) and Johannsen et al. (2016), which explore multi-source or improved projection pipelines. Position your explicit noise modeling relative to these advances (e.g., complementary vs. alternative, potential for combination).
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis. It would strengthen the paper to report: (i) sensitivity to the amount of gold data (e.g., 500/1000/2000 tokens), (ii) sensitivity to the amount/quality of parallel data and alignment heuristics (one-to-one vs. including many-to-one), (iii) the impact of the two-stage training and initialization/regularization of A, and (iv) statistical significance of improvements.
- Data/code availability unclear. It is not stated whether code, projected datasets/alignments, and learned A matrices will be released. Reproducibility hinges on these details. I assumed public availability in evaluating the paper; please state concretely.

Questions to Authors (for response)
- Will you release code, projection pipelines (alignments, tagger configurations), and the learned A matrices for all languages? If not all, which parts?
- How sensitive are results to the size of gold supervision? Do gains persist with fewer than 1k gold tokens and scale with more gold?
- Can the noise layer leverage multi-source projection (aggregating projections from multiple source languages)? Any preliminary results or expected behavior?
- How is A initialized and regularized? Did you observe degenerate solutions without gold pretraining? Any constraints (e.g., diagonal bias) used or tried?
- Did you perform significance testing for the main results, particularly on the two real low-resource languages?

Additional comments
- Please add discussion of the artificiality caveat raised above and scope conditions for applicability (parallel data requirements, domain effects, costs of finding vs. paying annotators).
- Please cite and discuss: Agić et al., 2015; Johannsen et al., 2016 (annotation projection, multi-source projection, and denoising strategies) and clarify how your approach complements or improves upon them.
- Minor clarity: briefly justify the choice to discard non-1–1 alignments and the sentence-level tag distribution heuristic for unaligned tokens; consider reporting an ablation including many-to-one alignments.
- The note on rectangular A for mismatched tagsets is interesting; if feasible, one short experiment or a clarifying remark on feasibility would add value.

Overall recommendation
Accept. I previously recommended conditional acceptance pending correction of an experimental setup flaw; that issue is now addressed. This is a neat, well-motivated contribution with solid results and clear analysis. With the requested related-work additions, caveat acknowledgment, and a statement on data/code release, the paper is in good shape for publication.
