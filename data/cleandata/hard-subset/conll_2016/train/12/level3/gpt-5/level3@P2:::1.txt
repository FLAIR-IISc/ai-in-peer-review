Summary
This paper addresses low-resource POS tagging by learning when to trust noisy projected supervision from a high-resource language. The core idea is a BiLSTM tagger augmented with an explicit “noise layer” (a learned confusion transformation) that maps clean tag predictions to the distribution observed in projected tags. The model is trained jointly on a small gold-annotated corpus (≈1k tokens) and a large set of projected labels from parallel text. Experiments on eight simulated low-resource settings and two true low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) show consistent gains over strong baselines and prior work, with useful analyses of the learned noise matrices.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Explicit modeling of projection noise via a learnable confusion transformation on top of a neural sequence tagger, enabling the model to exploit projected labels while accounting for systematic biases and alignment errors.
- A simple joint training procedure that combines tiny gold supervision and large projected supervision, yielding state-of-the-art accuracy across many languages in a realistic low-resource regime.
- Empirical and qualitative analysis (learned noise matrices) that sheds light on cross-lingual projection errors and how the model learns to trust—or ignore—specific projected tags.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical results: The proposed model matches or surpasses Duong et al. (2014) and other baselines on 8 languages, and sets new SOTA on Malagasy (86.3%) and Kinyarwanda (82.5%). The diagnostic baselines (Annotated, Projected, Ann+Proj) convincingly show why naive use of projections is suboptimal and motivate the noise layer.
- Methodological clarity and simplicity: The noise layer is a small, interpretable K×K transformation that integrates cleanly with a standard BiLSTM tagger and joint training. It is architecture-agnostic and likely extensible to other sequence labeling tasks or mismatched tagsets.
- Interpretability and analysis: Visualizations of the learned noise matrices provide insight into cross-lingual divergences and alignment artifacts (e.g., Swedish PRT/X confusion), illustrating what the model learns to trust/ignore.
- Practical low-supervision regime: Achieving strong results with only 1k gold tokens is compelling for real low-resource scenarios, and the two real-language evaluations support external validity beyond simulations.
- Responsiveness: The earlier experimental setup issue identified in a prior draft has been corrected; the current setup is sound.

Weaknesses
- External validity caveat: The simulation setting (Europarl-scale parallel data, one-to-one alignments, high-quality English tagging) can be more favorable than many real low-resource contexts. As another reviewer noted, the practical costs (finding vs. paying annotators) and availability/quality of parallel data/alignments are not fully discussed. Please acknowledge these limits explicitly.
- Missing related work discussion: Recent advances in annotation projection and multi-source projection (e.g., Johannsen et al., 2016; Agić et al., 2015) are not cited or contrasted. It would help to position the proposed noise modeling relative to these (e.g., could multi-source projection be combined with a noise layer?).
- Limited ablations: Key design choices (initialization and regularization of the A matrix; pretraining schedule; effect of sentence selection heuristics; handling of NULL/multi-alignments; CRF head vs. independent softmax) are not systematically ablated. Some of these could affect both performance and interpretability.
- Sensitivity analyses: Results are reported at a single gold budget (≈1k tokens) and particular parallel data sizes/quality. Curves vs. amount/quality of projected data and gold supervision would clarify when the method confers the most benefit.
- Reproducibility: It is not stated whether code, alignments, projected labels, and trained models will be released. Given the data pipeline complexity (alignment, filtering, projection), availability is important.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Will you release code, data processing scripts (alignment, filtering, projection), projected labels, and trained models? If not, what can be shared?
- How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized (e.g., diagonal bias, weight decay)? Did you observe instability without pretraining, and how sensitive are results to the pretrain/fine-tune schedule?
- For tokens with NULL or many-to-one/one-to-many alignments, you assign a sentence-level tag distribution. Can you provide an ablation comparing this to alternatives (e.g., ignore, marginalize over aligned tokens, uniform prior)?
- How sensitive is performance to alignment quality and to parallel data size (e.g., 10k vs. 100k vs. 200k sentences)? Any degradation curves?
- How does your approach compare/relate to Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015)? Could your noise layer improve multi-source projection?

Additional comments
- Thank you for correcting the earlier setup flaw; I now see no obstacles to acceptance.
- Please add a paragraph acknowledging the artificial aspects of the simulation setting and discussing applicability conditions (availability/quality of parallel data, alignment errors, domain mismatch).
- Consider briefly elaborating on the rectangular A case (mismatched tagsets) and, if possible, include a small experiment or clarification on whether you tried it.
- Reporting mean/std over multiple random seeds would increase robustness of claims.
- The paper should explicitly state data/code availability; my evaluation assumes public release.

Overall assessment
A clear, well-executed, and practically useful contribution. The explicit noise modeling is simple, interpretable, and effective, and the empirical gains are convincing. I recommend acceptance.
