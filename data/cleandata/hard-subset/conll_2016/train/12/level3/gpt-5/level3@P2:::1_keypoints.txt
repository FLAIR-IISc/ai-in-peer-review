- Addresses low-resource POS tagging by learning a noise layer that maps clean tag predictions to noisy projected supervision from a high-resource language.
- Uses a BiLSTM tagger trained jointly on ≈1k gold tokens and large amounts of projected labels from parallel text.
- Evaluated on eight simulated low-resource settings and two real languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda) with consistent gains and analyses of learned noise matrices.

- Contribution: Explicitly models projection noise via a learnable K×K confusion transformation on top of a neural sequence tagger.
- Contribution: Simple joint training combining tiny gold supervision with large projected supervision, yielding strong accuracy in low-resource regimes.
- Contribution: Empirical and qualitative analyses (learned noise matrices) that illuminate cross-lingual projection errors and model trust.

- Strength: Strong, consistent empirical results, outperforming baselines and setting new SOTA on Malagasy and Kinyarwanda.
- Strength: Methodological clarity and simplicity; noise layer is interpretable, small, and architecture-agnostic.
- Strength: Interpretability—visualized noise matrices reveal cross-lingual divergences and alignment artifacts.
- Strength: Practical low-supervision regime—effective with only ~1k gold tokens and validated on real languages.
- Strength: Authors addressed and corrected an earlier experimental setup issue.

- Weakness: External validity—simulation (Europarl-scale, one-to-one alignments, high-quality English tagging) may be more favorable than many real low-resource scenarios; practical costs and data availability not fully discussed.
- Weakness: Missing related work discussion, e.g., multi-source projection (Johannsen et al., 2016; Agić et al., 2015) and how the noise layer relates or could combine with them.
- Weakness: Limited ablations on key design choices (A matrix initialization/regularization, pretraining schedule, sentence selection, NULL/multi-alignments, CRF vs. softmax).
- Weakness: Sensitivity analyses absent—results reported at a single gold budget and fixed parallel data size/quality without curves vs. data amounts.
- Weakness: Reproducibility unclear—no statement about releasing code, alignments, projected labels, or trained models.

- Question: Will you release code, alignment/projection scripts, projected labels, and trained models?
- Question: How is the noise matrix A initialized and regularized, and how sensitive are results to pretrain/fine-tune schedule?
- Question: For NULL or many-to-one/one-to-many alignments, why use sentence-level tag distributions and how does that compare to alternatives (ignore, marginalize, uniform)?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to alignment quality and parallel data size (e.g., 10k vs. 100k vs. 200k sentences)?
- Question: How does this approach compare/relate to Johannsen et al. (2016) and Agić et al. (2015), and could the noise layer improve multi-source projection?

- Additional: Add explicit discussion acknowledging artificial aspects of the simulation setting and applicability conditions (parallel data availability/quality, domain mismatch).
- Additional: Consider experiments or clarification on rectangular A (mismatched tagsets).
- Additional: Report mean/std over multiple random seeds to increase robustness of claims.
- Additional: Explicitly state data/code availability in the paper.
- Additional: Reviewer now sees no obstacles to acceptance after corrections.

- Overall assessment: Clear, well-executed, interpretable, and practically useful contribution; recommend acceptance.
