Summary and contributions
This paper tackles POS tagging for low-resource languages by leveraging cross-lingual projection while explicitly modeling projection noise. The core idea is to attach a learned “noise” transformation on top of a BiLSTM tagger’s output distribution, enabling joint training on a small gold-annotated corpus and a larger projected corpus. Training proceeds in two stages (pretrain on gold, then continue jointly). The method is evaluated on eight simulated low-resource settings (Europarl) and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda), showing consistent gains over prior work.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) A simple and effective output-noise layer that models confusion between clean and projected tags, enabling principled use of noisy supervision. This is an elegant, modular change to standard neural taggers.
2) Strong empirical results across 10 languages, including two genuinely low-resource languages, with clear improvements over strong prior cross-lingual projection baselines.
3) Useful qualitative analysis of learned noise matrices that sheds light on systematic projection errors and cross-lingual divergences.

Strengths
- Clear, principled treatment of projection noise: The learned K×K transformation maps clean tag probabilities to projected-tag distributions, allowing the model to “explain away” misalignments and divergences. This is more systematic than heuristics or joint feature engineering, and integrates cleanly with backpropagation.
- Strong empirical gains and breadth: On average across eight languages, the BiLSTM+noise layer outperforms Duong et al. (2014a) (e.g., average 91.7 vs. 91.3). On real low-resource languages, it improves to 86.3 for Malagasy and 82.5 for Kinyarwanda, beating both Duong et al. and Garrette & Baldridge on Kinyarwanda. The consistent pattern across diverse settings bolsters the claim of robustness.
- Practical low-resource setup: The simulated setting restricts gold supervision to 1,000 tokens, aligning with realistic annotation budgets in many scenarios. The real-language experiments use modest-quality parallel data and still achieve gains, demonstrating viability outside Europarl.
- Interpretability of the noise layer: Visualizations of A expose language-specific confusion patterns (e.g., noun/adjective/number interactions in Malagasy; PRT/X in Swedish). This offers diagnostic value and supports model plausibility.
- Simple to adopt and extend: The approach is architecturally minimal (one additional linear layer), likely transferable to other sequence tagging tasks and noise-prone supervision schemes.

Weaknesses
- Missing ablation on gold data size: The benefits of the noise layer likely depend on the amount of gold supervision, yet there is no learning curve varying the number of gold tokens. This limits guidance for practitioners on when the method helps or harms (especially beyond 1k tokens).
- Equal weighting of gold and projected objectives: The loss averages the two terms without tuning a balancing coefficient. A λ hyperparameter could materially affect outcomes, especially in low-quality projection regimes (e.g., Kinyarwanda). Sensitivity to λ should be explored.
- Limited exploration of broader applicability: While the text mentions support for tagset mismatches (rectangular A) and potential extension to other noisy sources (e.g., crowdsourcing), there is no empirical validation of these scenarios. This weakens claims of generality.
- Baseline strength and completeness: The BiLSTM baselines lack common enhancements such as a CRF decoding layer, character-level features, or pretrained embeddings, which can significantly boost POS tagging. It is unclear whether the reported gains would persist against stronger neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char CNN/LSTM).
- Clarity and detail gaps: The representation of projected labels ỹ is underspecified (token-level vs. sentence-level distributions, treatment of NULL/multi-alignments). Initialization/regularization of A is not detailed, even though training stability hinges on sensible initial conditions. There is also some inconsistency between the “1,000 tokens” framing and the larger gold sets used for real languages.

Questions to authors
- Loss weighting: Did you try a λ to weight the projected loss term relative to the gold term? How sensitive are results to λ across languages, particularly Kinyarwanda?
- Projected labels representation: Is ỹ token-level one-hot when aligned 1–1, and a sentence-level frequency distribution otherwise? How are NULL-aligned tokens handled? Is there smoothing?
- Initialization and regularization of A: How is A initialized (identity, near-identity, random)? Any constraints or priors (e.g., diagonal bias, L2/L1, softmax row normalization) to prevent degenerate solutions?
- Learning curves: Can you report accuracy vs. number of gold tokens (e.g., 100, 300, 1k, 5k) to show where the method helps most? For Malagasy/Kinyarwanda, what are the results at 1k gold tokens to match the simulated setting?
- Strength of baselines: Did you evaluate a BiLSTM-CRF and/or char-aware baselines on Annotated and Ann+Proj? If not, can you comment on whether the noise layer’s gains are orthogonal to these enhancements?
- Parallel data quality/quantity: How do results vary with the number of projected sentences and with stricter/looser alignment filtering (beyond 1–1)? Any analysis of domain mismatch?
- Tagset mismatch: Do you have a working demo of rectangular A (e.g., Penn Treebank tags projected to Universal Tags) and its empirical impact?
- Inference-time path: At test time, do you use the clean output (o) only, ignoring the noise layer? Please clarify and justify.

Additional comments
- The analysis of A is compelling; consider quantifying it (e.g., diagonal mass, entropy, off-diagonal mass) and correlating with accuracy to strengthen the story.
- Minor editorial issues: line 357 unclear on the exact format of ỹ; line 267 has a bracket typo. Please fix.
- Reproducibility: You mention using clab/cnn; consider releasing code and scripts, and specifying hyperparameters and random seeds.
