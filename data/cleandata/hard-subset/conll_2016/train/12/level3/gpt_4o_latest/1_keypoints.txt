- Paper proposes a BiLSTM POS tagger with an explicit noise layer to model errors in cross-lingual projected annotations.
- Model is trained jointly on a small gold-standard corpus and larger noisy projected data to learn when to trust projections.
- Main contributions: novel noise layer for sequence tagging, integration into a deep joint-training framework, and empirical validation across languages.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on eight simulated low-resource languages and two real low-resource languages (Malagasy, Kinyarwanda).
- Practical relevance: effective for low-resource settings and requires only minimal gold-standard data (e.g., ~1000 tokens).
- Strength: novel and well-motivated technical contribution addressing projection noise.
- Strength: strong, robust empirical results demonstrating generalizability across datasets and languages.
- Strength: clear presentation and useful analysis (e.g., noise transformation matrices).
- Weakness: artificial assumptions about costs of finding vs. paying annotators need clarification.
- Weakness: related work omits some relevant prior studies (e.g., Johannsen et al. 2016; Agić et al. 2015).
- Weakness: unclear whether experimental data and resources are publicly available; reproducibility should be clarified.
- Questions for authors: clarify cost assumptions and their impact on generalizability, and state whether the datasets/parallels will be released.
- Recommendation: paper is a valuable contribution and recommended for acceptance, pending clarifications on costs, related work, and data availability.
