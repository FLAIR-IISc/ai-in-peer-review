Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes simple, dictionary-based approaches for learning massively multilingual word embeddings without requiring sentence-aligned parallel corpora, and introduces improved intrinsic evaluation metrics tailored to the multilingual setting. The authors also provide an evaluation portal with data and code.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, addressing theoretical flaws of QVEC (basis dependence, lack of normalization) and extending it to multilingual settings. The paper shows substantially stronger correlations with downstream tasks than widely used intrinsic metrics.
2) Practical, scalable methods for multilingual embeddings from monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries: multiCCA (a hub-based CCA projection) and multiCluster (shared cluster embeddings), with trained embeddings for 59 languages.
3) A systematic empirical study comparing intrinsic metrics to downstream performance (document classification, dependency parsing), and a public evaluation portal that can standardize comparisons.

Strengths
- Strong evidence that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA better reflect downstream utility. The correlation table shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates very well with multilingual document classification (r≈0.90) and multiQVEC correlates best with parsing (r≈0.44), while word similarity and word translation correlate poorly (near zero or negative). This is an important and concrete contribution to evaluation methodology.
- Practical, parallel-free estimation that scales to 59 languages. multiCCA’s hub-and-spoke CCA mapping is simple, easy to reproduce, and performs consistently well across metrics, especially at scale where multiCluster degrades due to conflation of senses. The focus on realistic resources (monolingual data + dictionaries) increases applicability to low-parallel settings.
- Thorough, multi-metric evaluation with clear baselines. The paper compares four families of methods (multiCCA, multiCluster, a skipgram-based parallel method, and translation-invariance), across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, on both 12-language and 59-language settings. The findings are nuanced: dictionary-based methods are competitive or best on most metrics and extrinsic tasks; translation-invariance can win on some intrinsic metrics; multiSkip underperforms without monolingual data.
- Thoughtful discussion of coverage confounds in intrinsic evaluation. Reporting coverage and testing on vocabulary intersections (in supplementary) is good practice; the portal can help standardize such reporting.
- Community impact via the evaluation portal and released resources. This materially lowers the barrier to entry and should improve comparability across future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and resources for multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA. The multilingual linguistic matrix relies on supersense annotations available for only three languages (en, da, it), constraining the correlation study to a narrow set. Although the paper acknowledges this and suggests POS tags as an alternative, the generality of conclusions across language types is not fully established. Correlations are estimated from 17 embedding variants over three languages; confidence intervals or significance tests are not reported.
- Dependence on noisy or proprietary dictionaries for many languages. For 47 languages, Google Translate-derived dictionaries are used; for 12, dictionaries are extracted from parallel data via fast_align with a fixed threshold. The impact of dictionary noise/coverage on embedding quality and cross-lingual alignment is not quantified. No sensitivity analysis is presented for the threshold τ, cluster size limits, or dictionary size.
- Hub-based design choices in multiCCA are underexplored. Mapping all languages into the English space is practical but may bias representations toward English distributions and harm typologically distant or low-resource languages. Alternatives (e.g., joint/iterative CCA, multiple pivots, orthogonal Procrustes constraints) are not compared.
- Extrinsic evaluations are simplified and somewhat narrow. The parser is deliberately deprived of POS/morphology and embeddings are frozen; while this isolates embedding utility, it may understate practical downstream gains and shift the task difficulty. The correlation experiments use only three languages; broader extrinsic tasks (e.g., NER, POS, NLI) and more languages would strengthen the claims.
- multiCluster exacerbates polysemy by collapsing translationally equivalent words across many languages into a single type-level vector. While the paper observes the degradation at 59 languages, it does not attempt mitigations (e.g., sense induction, context-conditioned embeddings, or constraints limiting cross-lingual conflation).

Questions to Authors
- Could you report confidence intervals and statistical significance for the correlations in Table 1 and for key extrinsic differences in Tables 2–3?
- How sensitive are results to dictionary quality and size (e.g., varying τ for fast_align-derived dictionaries, varying the number of translation pairs from GT, or adding noise)? Can you quantify the effect on multiCCA vs multiCluster?
- For multiCCA, you state the learned projections are non-singular and invert Ten→m,en; do you observe numerical instability? Would enforcing orthogonality (e.g., via Procrustes) or regularization improve robustness?
- Have you tried alternative pivots or multi-pivot strategies (e.g., averaging projections to several high-resource hubs) to reduce English-centric bias?
- Can multiQVEC-CCA be instantiated with POS/morphological tag dictionaries (e.g., UD) to extend beyond en/da/it? Any preliminary results?
- What are the training and evaluation runtimes/memory requirements for the 59-language models? Any guidance for practitioners on resource needs?

Additional Comments
- Qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors across languages; translation retrieval examples) would help diagnose strengths/failures, especially for typologically diverse languages.
- Please clarify precisely how coverage is computed per metric and ensure that vocabulary-intersection results for intrinsic metrics are prominently reported.
- The paper would benefit from an ablation on multiCluster’s maximum cluster size and from experimenting with soft-sharing or partial constraints to mitigate sense conflation.
