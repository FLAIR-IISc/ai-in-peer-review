Summary and contributions
The paper tackles massively multilingual word embeddings without requiring parallel corpora. It presents two estimation methods, multiCCA (a multilingual extension of CCA-based projection into English space) and multiCluster (cluster-and-share across languages using bilingual dictionaries), alongside a new intrinsic evaluation, multiQVEC-CCA, designed to correlate with downstream tasks. A public evaluation portal is introduced to facilitate standardized, replicable comparisons.

As seen here, the primary contributions are:
1) A principled intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that addresses basis-variance and normalization issues in QVEC and shows substantially stronger correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) Scalable, dictionary-based estimation techniques (multiCCA and multiCluster) for multilingual embeddings across 59 languages, without requiring sentence-aligned parallel data.
3) A comprehensive evaluation suite and portal with datasets, code, and trained models intended to standardize and accelerate research.

Strengths
- Clear evidence that multiQVEC-CCA improves intrinsic–extrinsic correlation. The correlation study over 17 embedding settings shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates 0.896 with multilingual document classification and outperforms word similarity (0.386) and word translation (0.066). This provides concrete guidance for intrinsic evaluation practice.
- Scalable multilingual estimation with minimal supervision. Training embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries is valuable for low-resource scenarios. The results indicate multiCCA scales more robustly than multiCluster as the number of languages grows.
- Thorough and balanced empirical setup. The work evaluates four families of methods (dictionary-based and parallel-based), reports both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, and discusses coverage effects, including reporting coverage percentages and a vocabulary-intersection analysis to mitigate bias.
- Practical impact via tooling and data. The evaluation portal, datasets, code, and trained models (with development/test splits) are a significant contribution for reproducibility and future comparisons.
- Sensible baselines and analysis. The paper includes translation-invariance factorization and a multilingual skip-gram variant. The discussion identifies when dictionary-based methods win (including both extrinsic tasks in the 12-language evaluation) and where translation-invariance excels on some intrinsic metrics, offering a nuanced view.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation data quality and potential leakage. For many languages, bilingual dictionaries for training and evaluation are derived from Google Translate. Using the same source for supervision and testing risks optimistic estimates and domain coupling. Wiktionary is used for some pairs, but a systematic separation of supervision sources from evaluation sources is not ensured across languages.
- Limited language coverage for the proposed intrinsic metrics. multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA rely on supersense annotations available for only a few languages (en, da, it). This restricts the practical applicability of the proposed intrinsic metrics and may bias correlation conclusions to a narrow typological set.
- Baseline comparability for multiSkip. The multiSkip implementation explicitly omits monolingual corpora, whereas related prior work (Coulmance et al., Guo et al.) leverages both monolingual and parallel data. This design choice likely handicaps multiSkip and may understate the strength of parallel-based approaches under comparable resource settings.
- Methodological details and ablations are limited. Several key choices are fixed without sensitivity analysis: the dictionary threshold τ = 0.1, pivot selection (English), CCA regularization, and monolingual corpus size/quality per language. The claim that multiCCA “consistently” outperforms multiCluster at 59 languages would be stronger with per-language breakdowns and ablations over dictionary noise/size and pivot language.
- Mixed extrinsic gains and lack of significance testing. In the 12-language setting, multiCluster attains the highest parsing score (61.0 UAS), while document classification has a tie (92.1). The absence of statistical significance testing and more granular analysis (e.g., per-language results) limits the strength of conclusions about method superiority.

Questions to authors
- How robust is multiCCA to the choice of pivot language? Would using a high-resource non-English pivot (or multi-pivot alignment) change the results?
- What regularization (e.g., whitening or shrinkage) is used in CCA, and how sensitive are results to these choices and dimensionality?
- How sensitive are both methods to dictionary noise and size? Please report performance as a function of dictionary coverage and quality, including τ sensitivity.
- To what extent does using Google Translate for both training dictionaries and evaluation data inflate results? Can a clean separation be ensured (e.g., training on one source and evaluating on another) and reported?
- Can the parallel-data methods be re-run with monolingual corpora included (as in Coulmance et al., Guo et al.) to ensure resource parity?
- Are the reported improvements in Table 3 statistically significant for extrinsic tasks? If so, please add significance tests; if not, please discuss variability.
- Coverage handling: beyond reporting percentages, could intrinsic scores be recomputed on shared vocabularies and contrasted against full-coverage scores to quantify the effect size of coverage differences?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider per-language extrinsic results and typological analysis to assess which languages benefit more from multiCCA vs multiCluster.
- The cluster-sharing approach may exacerbate polysemy; a brief exploration of sense-disambiguated or token-level methods would clarify the trade-offs.
- Where feasible, include alternative intrinsic evaluations that do not require language-specific annotations (e.g., cross-lingual lexical induction with independent gold dictionaries) to complement multiQVEC-CCA.
- Please ensure the evaluation portal and any URLs are compatible with double-blind review (the manuscript currently includes a concrete host/IP).
- Clarify training details: monolingual corpus sources/sizes per language, tokenization/normalization, and whether CCA mappings are orthogonal or unconstrained.

Overall assessment
The paper advances both methodology and evaluation for multilingual embeddings at scale, with a particularly valuable contribution in intrinsic–extrinsic correlation analysis and tooling. Addressing evaluation-source leakage, baseline parity, and providing additional robustness analyses would substantially strengthen the claims and impact. Pending these clarifications, the work is a strong candidate for acceptance.
