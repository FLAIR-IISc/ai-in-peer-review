Summary: The paper proposes scalable methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings without parallel corpora and introduces improved intrinsic evaluation for such embeddings. Two dictionary-based estimators are presented: multiCCA (projecting monolingual spaces via CCA into an English hub space) and multiCluster (clustering translationally equivalent types and training on cluster sequences). The authors further propose multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual variants of QVEC, showing stronger correlations with downstream tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) than commonly used intrinsic metrics. A public evaluation portal and resources accompany the work.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) An improved intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a systematic correlation study demonstrating it correlates substantially better with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation.
2) A practical, dictionary-only multilingual embedding pipeline (multiCCA) that scales to 59 languages and is competitive with parallel-data methods on several metrics.
3) An evaluation portal with standardized datasets, trained models, and tooling to facilitate reproducible comparison.

Strengths:
- Strong evidence that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA better predict downstream performance (e.g., 0.896 correlation with document classification), addressing a known gap in intrinsic evaluation.
- Breadth and practicality: training embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and dictionaries is valuable for low-resource settings.
- Thorough experimental comparison across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, with clear baselines (skip-gram-based, translation-invariance, multiSkip).
- Reproducibility and community impact via released resources and a web portal.
- Sensible discussion of coverage effects and an attempt to control for them.

Weaknesses:
- Methodological novelty is moderate; multiCCA is a straightforward extension of prior bilingual CCA, and multiCluster exacerbates polysemy, degrading performance as languages increase.
- Intrinsic evaluation relies on supersense annotations for only en/da/it, limiting generality; correlations with parsing are weaker (multiQVEC-CCA: 0.273).
- Heavy reliance on Google Translate both for training/evaluation dictionaries in many languages risks noise and circularity; robustness to dictionary quality is not analyzed.
- Coverage confounds persist; results sometimes compare on differing vocabularies despite acknowledgments.
- The correlation study uses 17 models—useful but limited for statistically robust conclusions; more downstream tasks (e.g., NER, MT lexicon induction) would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors:
- How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary size/noise? Can you report performance vs. dictionary quality/coverage?
- Does using a non-English hub (or multi-hub/joint CCA) change results for typologically distant languages?
- Can you extend multiQVEC(-CCA) beyond supersenses (e.g., UD POS/morphology) to broaden language coverage?
- For evaluation, can you avoid GT-derived test pairs (e.g., Wiktionary-only) to reduce bias?
- Could subword modeling mitigate multiCluster’s sense conflation and improve low-resource languages?
