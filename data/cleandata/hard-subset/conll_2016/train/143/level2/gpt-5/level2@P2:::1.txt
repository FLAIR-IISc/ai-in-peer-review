Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes two simple, dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual word embeddings that do not require parallel corpora: (i) multiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into a shared English space using per-language CCA; and (ii) multiCluster, which clusters translation-equivalent word types across languages and trains skip-gram on cluster IDs.
- It introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics that align embeddings with linguistic-feature matrices (supersenses), arguing that QVEC-CCA remedies basis-rotation sensitivity and dimensionality issues in QVEC, and showing higher correlation with two downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- It releases a web portal, code, and pretrained embeddings for up to 59 languages to facilitate standardized training and evaluation.

Contributions as I see them (in order of significance)
1) A principled intrinsic evaluation metric (QVEC-CCA and its multilingual variant) that substantially improves correlation with downstream tasks in the reported setting.
2) A practical recipe (multiCCA) to build a single shared embedding space for many languages from bilingual dictionaries and monolingual corpora.
3) Resources: a public evaluation portal and multilingual embeddings.

Strengths
- Clear, useful intrinsic evaluation contribution with empirical support. The paper carefully identifies two theoretical shortcomings of QVEC and proposes QVEC-CCA, which is basis-invariant and comparable across dimensions. On 17 embedding variants over three languages, multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with document classification and parsing than cross-lingual word similarity and word translation (e.g., 0.896 with document classification), addressing a long-standing gap in intrinsic evaluation.
- Pragmatic multilingual scaling without parallel data. MultiCCA’s hub-based projection is simple and scalable; using only bilingual dictionaries and monolingual data is attractive for low-resource settings. Training for 59 languages is a meaningful step toward massively multilingual embeddings.
- Thorough and diverse evaluation. The paper evaluates across several intrinsic metrics (including coverage reporting and intersection-of-vocab control) and two extrinsic tasks, and discusses coverage confounds explicitly—an often-overlooked issue in embedding evaluation.
- Honest analysis of method behavior. The discussion that multiCluster exacerbates type-level ambiguity and degrades with many languages is insightful and consistent with results (multiCCA > multiCluster in 59-language setup).
- Community impact via portal and data. Providing standardized datasets and an evaluation portal (with dev/test splits) helps reproducibility and comparability across future work.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and strength of baselines. The multiSkip baseline is acknowledged to use only parallel data and no monolingual corpora, unlike comparable methods in the literature; the translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller window due to computational limits. These choices likely disadvantage both baselines and complicate the conclusion that the proposed methods are superior. Suggestion: Include a version of multiSkip that also leverages monolingual data (as in Coulmance et al.; Guo et al.), and scale translation-invariance to comparable hyperparameters or report sensitivity analyses.
- Potential bias from using Google Translate for both training and evaluation in the 59-language setting. Dictionaries for training (47 languages) and test dictionaries for word-translation evaluation are both derived from Google Translate. This risks leakage and overestimation on that metric, and GT quality varies across languages. Suggestion: Use independent evaluation lexicons (e.g., Wiktionary, PanLex) or at least quantify overlap and re-evaluate on disjoint test pairs.
- Limited scope of correlation study. Correlation with downstream tasks is computed over 17 models and only three languages (en/da/it), with extrinsic tasks that also target those languages. This may inflate correlations for multiQVEC(-CCA), whose linguistic matrices are built from supersenses in the same languages. Suggestion: Expand correlations to more languages, and to downstream tasks not overlapping with the linguistic resources (or use universal POS as an alternative matrix), and report confidence intervals/significance tests for correlation differences.
- Hub-based projection and non-English–non-English quality. In multiCCA, all languages are projected to English; distances between two non-English languages are mediated by the hub and may be suboptimal. While cross-lingual similarity includes some non-English pairs, there is limited diagnostic analysis of performance degradation across pair types. Suggestion: Evaluate similarity/translation on non-English pairs more broadly and compare hub-based multiCCA to a joint alignment that optimizes all languages simultaneously (e.g., multi-source CCA/Procrustes).
- Method novelty is incremental. MultiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s bilingual CCA to multiple languages via a hub, and multiCluster is a natural application of skip-gram to dictionary-induced clusters. The main novelty resides in evaluation rather than modeling. This is acceptable for a resource/evaluation paper, but the paper should more clearly position modeling novelty and limits.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide results for a multiSkip variant that also uses monolingual corpora (as in related work), and for translation-invariance with a larger window or with negative sampling to address computational cost?
- How much overlap exists between the Google-Translate-derived training dictionaries and the evaluation dictionaries in the 59-language experiments? Can you report results on a disjoint evaluation set?
- In multiCCA, why use T_en→m,en inverse composed with T_m→m,en, instead of mapping both languages to a shared canonical space directly? Are the CCA projections constrained to be orthogonal? Please clarify the derivation and numerical stability (conditioning) of the inverses.
- How does performance vary with dictionary size/quality and with embedding dimensionality (d)? Any sensitivity or ablation studies?
- Could you provide analyses for non-English–non-English pairs and for languages distant from English (e.g., agglutinative or morphologically rich), where the hub assumption may be weaker?

Additional comments
- The portal and data are valuable; please ensure that the GT-derived dictionaries and evaluation lists are redistributable, or provide scripts to recreate them.
- Consider adding significance tests for downstream comparisons, and reporting runtime/memory for training each method to guide practical adoption.
- There are minor reference formatting issues (e.g., placeholders and question marks); please clean up for the camera-ready.
