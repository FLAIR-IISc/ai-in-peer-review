This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The core contributions are two embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), a refined evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development of both new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) and a publicly available web portal is a significant contribution. The authors demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics. Providing tools and benchmarks to the community will undoubtedly facilitate future research in this area. The thorough experimental setup, including both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, strengthens the claims about the evaluation metric's effectiveness.
2. **Addressing a Gap in Multilingual Embedding Estimation:** The paper directly addresses the challenge of estimating embeddings for a large number of languages (>2) using only dictionary data. While the methods aren’t groundbreaking in their individual components (building on CCA and clustering approaches), adapting them to the multilingual setting and providing a systematic evaluation is valuable. The focus on dictionary-based methods is important, as parallel corpora are limited resources.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, especially those comparing the different estimation methods across various evaluation tasks, are convincing. The detailed analysis in Section 5.2 provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each method.  The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of each evaluation metric and the impact of vocabulary coverage.  The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations greatly strengthens the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While the adaptation of multiCluster and multiCCA to a multilingual setting is a valid contribution, the core ideas themselves are not entirely novel. The methods build directly upon existing techniques (clustering, CCA) and the improvements are largely in adapting them to handle a larger number of languages. A clearer articulation of *how* these adaptations are unique and non-trivial would be beneficial.
2. **Dictionary Creation Methodology:** The method for creating dictionaries from Google Translate for languages without existing resources isn't thoroughly justified. The fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) seems arbitrary, and the potential biases introduced by machine translation quality aren't adequately discussed. This impacts the reliability of the embeddings trained on these dictionaries.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper presents comprehensive results, a deeper dive into *why* certain methods perform better or worse in specific scenarios would be insightful.  For instance, what types of languages benefit most from multiCCA versus multiCluster? Are there particular linguistic properties that influence performance?  Analyzing failure cases could reveal valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the Google Translate dictionary creation: What steps were taken to mitigate the potential biases introduced by the machine translation system? Did you perform any manual validation of the generated dictionaries?
2.  The paper mentions that QVEC and QVEC-CCA rely on supersense tag annotations. How sensitive are these methods to the quality and consistency of the annotations across different languages?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of each of the proposed methods, particularly as the number of languages increases? This would be helpful for researchers considering applying these techniques to even larger multilingual datasets.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The inclusion of code and data via the web portal is a strong positive aspect.
*   Consider briefly discussing potential future work, such as exploring methods to leverage monolingual data more effectively or investigating the use of other dictionary resources.
