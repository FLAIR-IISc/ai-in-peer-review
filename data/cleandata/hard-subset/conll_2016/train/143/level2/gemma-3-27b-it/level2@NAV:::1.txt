## Review of "Estimating and Evaluating Multilingual Embeddings"

This paper presents methods for estimating and evaluating word embeddings across 59 languages, leveraging dictionaries and monolingual data. The authors propose two estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) along with a publicly available web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation of multilingual embedding estimation methods and the introduction of multiQVEC-CCA, which demonstrates improved correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing intrinsic metrics. The development and release of a web portal to facilitate research in this area is a valuable secondary contribution, providing a resource for the community. A third contribution is the thorough experimental analysis across a large number of languages, demonstrating the performance of the proposed methods at scale.

**Strengths:**

* **Large-Scale Evaluation:** The experiments cover a significantly larger number of languages (59) than most prior work, providing a robust assessment of the proposed methods.
* **Improved Evaluation Metric:** The multiQVEC-CCA metric shows promising results, correlating better with downstream tasks than previously used intrinsic evaluation methods. This is a significant step towards more reliable evaluation of multilingual embeddings.
* **Publicly Available Resources:** The web portal and open-source code facilitate reproducibility and further research.  This commitment to accessibility is commendable.
* **Rigorous Experimentation:** The authors perform a detailed correlation analysis and compare their methods against strong baselines. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Gains over Baselines:** While multiQVEC-CCA demonstrates better correlation with downstream tasks, the absolute performance gains over baseline methods (particularly the translation-invariance method) aren't always substantial.  The practical impact of these gains could be further investigated.
* **Dictionary Creation:** The process of creating dictionaries for the 47 languages without parallel corpora relies on Google Translate, which introduces potential noise and bias. This could impact the quality of the resulting embeddings.
* **Coverage Issues:** The results highlight varying coverage across different methods. While the authors attempt to address this in their analysis, the inherent bias introduced by evaluating on different subsets of the vocabulary remains a concern.
* **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper focuses on overall performance metrics but lacks detailed error analysis. Investigating specific cases where the methods succeed or fail could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by each estimation method? Are there specific language pairs or semantic domains where certain methods perform particularly poorly?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of threshold (τ) in the dictionary creation process, particularly for the languages where dictionaries are extracted from parallel corpora?
3.  Given the potential bias introduced by using Google Translate for dictionary creation, have you considered strategies to mitigate this bias, such as using back-translation or human validation?



This is a solid contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. Addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon minor revisions to address these points.
