This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The primary contributions are two embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. 

The strengths of this work lie in its comprehensive approach to multilingual embedding evaluation and its provision of a valuable resource for the research community. The development of both multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA is a strong contribution. The paper convincingly demonstrates that these new evaluation metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than previously used intrinsic metrics, addressing a significant need in the field. Furthermore, the creation and release of a web portal with benchmark datasets is a significant practical contribution, facilitating further research and comparability of methods. The thorough experimentation, encompassing both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, with clear reporting of results and ablation studies, adds credibility to the findings. The analysis of correlation between intrinsic metrics and downstream task performance provides valuable insights for researchers. The method addresses a practical problem – the lack of strong, reliable evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings – and offers a solution that appears to be genuinely effective.

However, there are several weaknesses that should be addressed. The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the practical considerations of applying the methods to a wider range of languages. While 59 languages are mentioned, the specific data sources and pre-processing steps for each language are not clearly outlined. This limits the reproducibility of the results and hinders the potential for broader adoption of the methods. The choice of using Google Translate for dictionary creation for many languages raises concerns about the quality and potential biases introduced into the embeddings. A more rigorous analysis of the impact of dictionary quality on embedding performance would be beneficial. The description of the multiCluster method could be more detailed; the impact of the cluster size parameter (1000/10000) on performance isn’t fully explored, and its sensitivity to different datasets isn't discussed. The performance gains achieved by multiCCA and multiCluster aren't overwhelmingly substantial, and further analysis is needed to pinpoint the specific scenarios where these methods outperform existing approaches. The comparison with existing methods isn't consistently fair; for example, multiSkip is limited to parallel data, potentially disadvantaging it. The paper focuses heavily on English as a central language. Exploring the performance of the methods with other central languages could reveal valuable insights. While the paper acknowledges limitations regarding supersense tag consistency across languages, this issue could be mitigated by utilizing more robust and multilingual resources.

Several questions should be addressed by the authors. What is the computational cost associated with each of the embedding estimation methods, particularly as the number of languages increases? How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the embedding dimension and the learning rate? The paper mentions coverage percentages in tables, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of coverage on the evaluation results would be helpful. What steps were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the bilingual dictionaries obtained from different sources? What is the robustness of the proposed methods to noise or errors in the bilingual dictionaries?



Additional Comments:
- The supplementary material should include detailed information about the data sources and pre-processing steps for all 59 languages.
- It would be helpful to provide a more detailed analysis of the error cases for the downstream tasks.
- A discussion of potential applications of the multilingual embeddings beyond the tasks presented in the paper would broaden the impact of the work.
