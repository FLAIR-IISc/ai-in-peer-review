

Summary: This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces two improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and provides a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which demonstrates superior correlation with downstream tasks compared to previous evaluation metrics; (2) the creation of a practical web portal that will facilitate standardized evaluation in this field; and (3) the proposed estimation methods for multilingual embeddings.

Strengths:
1. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is compelling and demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA achieves a strong correlation (0.896) with document classification performance, significantly outperforming previous intrinsic metrics (0.386 for word similarity, 0.066 for word translation). This represents a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a better way to evaluate multilingual embeddings.
2. The web portal is a valuable practical contribution that will substantially help the community by enabling standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The portal's design with development/test modes promotes sound research practices, and the open-source release of all methods will facilitate reproducibility.
3. The paper demonstrates the feasibility of training embeddings for 59 languages using only dictionaries, which represents a significant scale-up from previous work. The authors provide a thorough evaluation across multiple languages and metrics, showing that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's claim that their methods "do not require parallel data" is misleading. They require bilingual dictionaries, which are often scarce and require significant effort to create (e.g., using Google Translate for 47 languages). The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the practical applicability of their approach, especially for low-resource languages where dictionaries are least available.
2. The correlation between evaluation metrics and parsing performance remains low (0.273 for multiQVEC-CCA), which weakens the claim that their evaluation metrics are universally superior. The paper doesn't explain why this correlation is so low, making it difficult to assess the metrics' utility for syntactic tasks.
3. The paper lacks adequate comparison to recent unsupervised multilingual embedding approaches (e.g., Conneau et al. 2017, which doesn't require dictionaries). This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their contribution, as their method is more limited than recent unsupervised approaches.

Questions to authors:
1. How do you address the practical limitation of requiring bilingual dictionaries for all languages, particularly for low-resource languages where dictionaries are scarce? Would your approach scale to languages with no available dictionaries?
2. Why does the correlation between multiQVEC-CCA and parsing performance remain so low (0.273), and how do you explain this discrepancy compared to the strong correlation with document classification?
3. How would your methods compare to recent unsupervised multilingual embedding approaches that don't require any parallel data or dictionaries, such as those by Conneau et al. (2017) or Artetxe et al. (2018)?
