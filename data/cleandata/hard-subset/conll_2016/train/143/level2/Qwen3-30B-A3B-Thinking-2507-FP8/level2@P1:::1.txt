

Summary: This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only dictionaries and monolingual data. The main contributions are (1) two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), (2) a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlates with downstream tasks than previous metrics, and (3) a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution with multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses a critical gap in multilingual embedding evaluation. The correlation analysis in Table 1 is compelling evidence that their proposed metric correlates much more strongly with downstream tasks (0.896 for document classification and 0.273 for parsing) than previous metrics (max 0.635 for multiQVEC and 0.386 for word similarity). This is an important practical contribution that will help the community evaluate future methods more effectively.

2. The multiCCA method is well-motivated and demonstrates consistent performance across multiple evaluation metrics, particularly for the large-scale 59-language setting where it outperforms multiCluster in all evaluations. The extension of CCA to multilingual settings is a natural and elegant solution to the problem of aligning multiple language embeddings.

3. The web portal is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate future research in this area. The portal provides standardized evaluation tools, download access to training data, and the ability for researchers to upload their own embeddings for evaluation. This addresses a real need in the community for reproducible and comparable evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim that the methods "do not require parallel data" is somewhat misleading. While the estimation methods only require dictionaries, the paper extracts dictionaries from parallel corpora for the 12-language set (Europarl) and uses Google Translate for the 59-language set. This means the methods still rely on some form of parallel data or translation services, which contradicts the paper's main claim.

2. The paper does not adequately explain why multiCluster underperforms in the 59-language setting, despite acknowledging that it "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings. The paper states this as a given without providing sufficient evidence or analysis to support this claim. A more thorough exploration of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

3. The evaluation metrics' dependence on supersense annotations (limited to English, Italian, and Danish) is a significant limitation that isn't properly addressed. The paper acknowledges that "supersense annotations exist for other languages but are inconsistent across languages," yet the metric cannot be applied to languages without these annotations. The authors should discuss how this limitation affects the broader applicability of their evaluation method.

4. The paper claims that multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than the translation-invariance method, yet this confounds performance comparisons. The authors acknowledge this issue but don't provide a clear solution beyond reporting coverage percentages in brackets. The paper should explain how to properly compare methods with different coverage.

5. The evaluation of multiCluster is limited to a single baseline (the 12-language setting) and doesn't explore why it performs better in the smaller language set than in the larger one. A more thorough analysis of this difference would help readers understand the method's limitations.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you justify the specific cluster size thresholds used in multiCluster (1,000 for 12 languages, 10,000 for 59 languages)? Did you perform any ablation studies to determine optimal cluster sizes?

2. The paper states that "most differences in coverage between multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively small," but the coverage percentages in Table 3 (e.g., 70.9% vs 69.3% for dependency parsing) suggest this may not always be the case. How do you handle evaluation when coverage differs significantly between methods?

3. The authors claim that multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming" of multiQVEC, but the paper doesn't clearly explain what this shortcoming is. Could you elaborate on the theoretical issues with QVEC that necessitated the development of multiQVEC-CCA?

Additional Comments:
The paper provides a solid contribution to the field, particularly with the evaluation metric and web portal. The empirical results are well-presented, and the paper makes a clear case for the importance of better evaluation methods in multilingual NLP. The authors should consider expanding the discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the dependence on supersense annotations for their evaluation metric.
