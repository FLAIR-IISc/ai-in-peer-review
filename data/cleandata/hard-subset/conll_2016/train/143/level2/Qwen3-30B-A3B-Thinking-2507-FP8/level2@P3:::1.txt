

Summary of the paper: The paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across more than fifty languages using dictionaries and monolingual data rather than requiring parallel data. The main contributions are two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), an improved evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlates with downstream tasks, and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

Primary contributions:
1. A novel method (multiCCA) for constructing multilingual embeddings that extends bilingual approaches to more than two languages while using only monolingual data and dictionaries.
2. An improved evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous metrics and shows better correlation with downstream tasks.
3. A web portal enabling researchers to evaluate multilingual embeddings using standardized metrics.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to evaluation methodology with multiQVEC-CCA, which directly addresses a clear gap in the literature. The demonstration that this metric correlates better with downstream tasks (0.896 for document classification, 0.273 for parsing) compared to previous metrics (max 0.635 for multiQVEC) is well-supported with empirical evidence across multiple languages.
2. The motivation for the work is strong, highlighting the importance of multilingual embeddings for transfer learning and machine translation, and the paper clearly articulates how it advances beyond previous bilingual-focused work.
3. The web portal is a valuable resource that will substantially facilitate future research in this area, providing standardized evaluation data and an accessible interface for researchers.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately explain why multiCluster performs significantly worse than multiCCA when scaling to 59 languages, despite noting that multiCluster "exacerbates semantic ambiguity." A deeper analysis of this performance gap would strengthen the paper.
2. The evaluation of the estimation methods is limited to specific language sets (59 languages for dictionary-based methods, 12 languages for parallel data-based methods), without sufficient justification for these particular language selections or discussion of how representative these sets are of the broader multilingual NLP landscape.
3. The paper acknowledges that multiQVEC-CCA relies on supersense annotations which may not be consistently available across languages, but does not propose a clear solution for this limitation or discuss how it might impact the metric's generalizability to languages without such annotations.

Questions to Authors:
1. How might the evaluation metrics be adapted to work with languages that lack consistent supersense annotations?
2. What specific linguistic phenomena cause multiCluster to perform worse with 59 languages compared to 12 languages, and how might this be mitigated?
3. What is the computational cost of training multiCCA embeddings for 59 languages compared to the standard 12-language setup, and how might this scale to even larger language sets?

Additional Comments:
The paper's claim that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics for 59 languages is supported by the results, but the paper could provide more insight into why this is the case. The mention that multiCluster "exacerbates semantic ambiguity" is a good start, but a more detailed analysis would be valuable. The paper also does not discuss the potential for using the web portal to evaluate embeddings for languages not included in the current 59-language set, which would be an important consideration for future adoption.
