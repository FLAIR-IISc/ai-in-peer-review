

This paper proposes methods for multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only dictionaries and monolingual data, introducing two estimation approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA). The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the development of a reliable evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlates with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, (2) the creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, and (3) the demonstration that dictionary-based methods can effectively scale to 59 languages.

Strengths: The paper's strongest contribution is the evaluation method, which demonstrates significant improvements in correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification and 0.273 with parsing). This is compelling evidence for the metric's utility. The web portal is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate reproducibility and standardization in the field. The experiments across 59 languages demonstrate the scalability of their approach, and the thorough correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics provides strong evidence for their evaluation framework.

Weaknesses: The novelty of the estimation methods is limited as they build directly on prior work (e.g., multiCCA extends Faruqui and Dyer 2014). The paper doesn't adequately address the coverage differences between methods during evaluation, which confounds comparisons (e.g., multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than translation-invariance). Additionally, the paper doesn't compare against more recent multilingual embedding approaches like multilingual BERT variants, which have become standard in the field. The paper also fails to fully address the limitation of inconsistent supersense annotations across languages for their evaluation metric.

The authors should clarify how their methods compare to recent transformer-based multilingual approaches in the revision. They should also provide a more thorough discussion of how coverage differences affect evaluation results and propose a standardized approach to handle this issue in future work. The paper's practical contributions are strong, but the theoretical novelty of the estimation methods requires stronger justification.
