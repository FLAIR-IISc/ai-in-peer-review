- New evaluation framework: proposes multiQVEC-CCA to fix QVEC’s lack of rotational invariance and unnormalized scores, showing stronger correlation with downstream tasks.
- Two scalable embedding methods: multiCluster and multiCCA use monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries (no parallel data) and authors release 59-language embeddings.
- Empirical comparison and release: extensive experiments against parallel-data baselines and an open-source web portal, code, and pretrained models for reproducibility.
- Strength — evaluation contribution: multiQVEC-CCA is well-motivated and Table 1 shows it is a more reliable proxy for downstream performance.
- Strength — practical methods: dictionary-based multiCluster and multiCCA scale to many languages and outperform parallel-data methods on extrinsic tasks in the 12-language setting.
- Strength — comprehensive experiments: evaluation covers four methods, nine metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic), and two settings (12- and 59-language) for breadth.
- Strength — community resource: public evaluation portal and released code/models promote transparency and future research.
- Weakness — limited novelty: estimation methods are incremental extensions of prior ideas rather than major algorithmic innovations.
- Weakness — inconsistent results: different methods excel on different tasks (intrinsic vs extrinsic) without deep analysis of why.
- Weakness — dictionary quality risk: many 59-language dictionaries came from Google Translate with unknown noise; no sensitivity analysis on dictionary errors.
- Weakness — vocabulary coverage confound: large differences in coverage (e.g., 23% vs 71%) bias intrinsic comparisons and intersection analysis is relegated to the supplement.
- Question — pivot choice: how does using English as the single pivot in multiCCA affect performance, especially for typologically distant languages?
- Question — model biases: can authors hypothesize architectural reasons (e.g., matrix factorization vs global projection) for the mixed performance patterns?
- Question — computational cost: what are training time and memory requirements, particularly for the 59-language experiment?
