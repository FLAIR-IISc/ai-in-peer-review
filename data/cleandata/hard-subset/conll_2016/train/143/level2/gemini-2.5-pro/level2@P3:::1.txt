### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating shared vector-space embeddings for a large number of languages. The primary contributions of this work are threefold. First, it introduces a new intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which improves upon prior work by being invariant to the embeddings' basis and producing a normalized score. Crucially, this metric is shown to have a significantly higher correlation with performance on downstream tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than existing intrinsic metrics. Second, the paper proposes two novel dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual embeddings, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, which do not require parallel corpora and are scaled to 59 languages. These methods are demonstrated to be competitive with, and in some extrinsic evaluations superior to, baseline methods that rely on parallel data. Finally, the work contributes a public web portal for standardized evaluation, along with open-source code and data, to facilitate future reproducible research in this domain.

### Strengths
1.  **Rigorous Evaluation of Evaluation Metrics:** The paper's most significant strength is the systematic analysis of how well intrinsic evaluation metrics correlate with extrinsic, task-based performance. By training 17 different embedding models and evaluating them across a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, the work provides strong empirical evidence (Table 1) that the proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric is a more reliable proxy for downstream utility than commonly used metrics like word similarity and word translation. This is a valuable contribution that helps address a critical need for more meaningful intrinsic evaluation in the field.

2.  **Scalable and Resource-Light Estimation Methods:** The proposed `multiCCA` and `multiCluster` methods are notable for their scalability and minimal data requirements. By relying only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, these approaches are applicable to a far wider set of languages than methods that depend on large, curated parallel corpora. The successful training of embeddings for 59 languages demonstrates the practical utility of this approach for "massively multilingual" NLP, especially for lower-resource languages where parallel data is scarce.

3.  **Comprehensive and Thoughtful Experiments:** The experimental design is thorough, comparing the proposed estimation methods against strong baselines across nine different evaluation metrics. The paper demonstrates a nuanced understanding of potential confounding factors. For example, the extrinsic parsing task is carefully designed to isolate the contribution of the pre-trained embeddings by removing other lexical and morphological features. The analysis also thoughtfully discusses the issue of vocabulary coverage and its potential to bias intrinsic evaluation scores, reporting coverage percentages and performing follow-up analysis on the vocabulary intersection.

4.  **Contribution to Community and Reproducibility:** The development of a public evaluation portal, accompanied by the release of datasets, trained embeddings, and source code, represents a commendable service to the research community. This infrastructure lowers the barrier to entry for new research and promotes standardized, comparable, and reproducible evaluation, which is essential for making steady progress in the field.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Applicability of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The primary evaluation contribution, `multiQVEC-CCA`, relies on the availability of manually created linguistic resources (specifically, supersense tag annotations) for its construction. As presented, this resource is available for only three languages (English, Danish, Italian), which limits the metric's direct applicability for evaluating embeddings across the full set of 59 languages presented in the paper. While the paper suggests that resources like universal POS tag dictionaries could be a substitute for syntactic tasks, this is not empirically validated. A more detailed discussion or preliminary experiment on extending the `multiQVEC-CCA` framework to more languages using more widely available resources would strengthen this contribution.

2.  **Unclear Justification for `multiCCA` Formulation:** The description of the `multiCCA` method in Section 2.2 could benefit from greater detail. The final embedding for a word `v` in a non-pivot language `m` is defined as `E_CCA(m, v) = T⁻¹_en→m,en * T_m→m,en * E_m(v)`. The intuition for using this particular combination of projection matrices, especially the inverse of the projection from English to `m`, is not explicitly justified. A clearer explanation for this design choice would improve the reader's understanding of the method.

3.  **Inconsistent Performance of the `multiCluster` Method:** The `multiCluster` method shows promising results in the 12-language setting but performs poorly in the 59-language setting. The paper posits that this is due to increased semantic ambiguity from collapsing more words into single clusters. This suggests a potential lack of robustness in the method as the number of languages increases. A more in-depth analysis of this limitation—perhaps by examining cluster purity, size distribution, or the trade-off between cluster size and performance—would provide a more complete understanding of the method's applicability.

4.  **Slightly Uneven Comparison to Baselines:** The `multiSkip` baseline is noted to be at a disadvantage because the implementation used only learns from parallel corpora, whereas the other methods leverage large monolingual corpora. This makes the comparison less direct. A stronger baseline would have been a model that, like those described in the cited works (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016), integrates both parallel and monolingual data. This would allow for a more direct comparison of dictionary-based versus parallel-data-based training signals.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the `multiCCA` formulation, could the paper provide additional intuition for the choice to project from a non-English space `m` into the shared English space using the product of two matrices, `T⁻¹_en→m,en * T_m→m,en`? How does this formulation compare to simpler alternatives, such as using a single projection matrix learned directly from language `m` to English?

2.  The `multiQVEC-CCA` metric is compelling but is currently constrained by the limited availability of multilingual supersense annotations. What are the prospects for extending this evaluation framework to a much wider set of languages? Have any experiments been run using more broadly available linguistic annotations, such as those derived from a universal POS tagset, and if so, how do the resulting correlations with downstream tasks compare to those reported in Table 1?
