**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive study on the estimation and evaluation of word embeddings in a shared vector space for a large number of languages. The work makes three main contributions. The primary contribution is the introduction of a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA. This metric addresses theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and is shown to have a substantially higher correlation with performance on downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification and parsing) than other common intrinsic metrics. The second contribution consists of two novel estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which can create multilingual embeddings for dozens of languages using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, removing the need for scarce parallel data. The third contribution is a practical one: the development of a public web portal for the standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, which provides data and tools to facilitate reproducible research in this area.

**Strengths**

1.  **A Significantly Improved Evaluation Metric:** The paper's most significant contribution is the development and validation of multiQVEC-CCA. The authors provide a clear and compelling critique of the prior QVEC metric, identifying its lack of invariance to basis rotation and its unnormalized scoring as major theoretical weaknesses. Their proposed solution, using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), elegantly resolves both issues. The empirical evidence presented in Table 1 is striking, showing that multiQVEC-CCA's correlation with document classification accuracy (0.896) is vastly superior to that of standard metrics like cross-lingual word similarity (0.386). This is an important step forward, as it provides the community with a more reliable and computationally efficient proxy for downstream task performance when developing new embedding models.

2.  **Effective and Scalable Estimation without Parallel Data:** The proposed multiCCA and multiCluster methods demonstrate that high-quality multilingual embeddings can be learned without parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for expanding to low-resource languages. The multiCCA method, in particular, is shown to scale to 59 languages while achieving strong performance. In the 12-language controlled comparison (Table 3), the dictionary-based methods are highly competitive, outperforming the parallel-data-based translation-invariance method on five of nine metrics, including both extrinsic tasks. This is a powerful result, highlighting the viability of dictionary-based methods for creating truly "massively multilingual" embedding spaces.

3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimentation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and carefully conducted. The authors test their methods on a wide variety of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, across different language sets (up to 59), and compare against relevant baselines. Their handling of the vocabulary coverage problem in intrinsic evaluation is particularly commendable. By acknowledging that comparing models with different vocabularies can be misleading and performing a follow-up analysis on the intersection of vocabularies, the authors demonstrate a high degree of scientific rigor and provide a more trustworthy interpretation of their results.

4.  **Valuable Resource for the Community:** The creation of an open-source evaluation portal is a laudable contribution that will benefit the entire research community. By centralizing datasets, evaluation scripts, and providing a platform for direct comparison, this portal lowers the barrier to entry for new research and promotes standardized, reproducible science in this domain.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty of the multiCCA Method:** While effective and well-implemented, the multiCCA method is described by the authors themselves as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of a prior bilingual method (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). The core technical innovation is the application of this existing bilingual CCA projection technique in a hub-and-spoke model with English as the pivot. While demonstrating the scalability and effectiveness of this approach is a valuable engineering contribution, the underlying algorithmic novelty is limited. The paper would be more precise if it framed this contribution more in terms of its successful application at an unprecedented scale rather than as a fundamentally new algorithm.

2.  **Dependence on Pivot Language and Dictionaries:** Both proposed methods are highly dependent on external resources: bilingual dictionaries for both, and a pivot language (English) for multiCCA. The quality of the final embeddings for any given language is therefore contingent on the quality of the English embeddings and the corresponding English-X dictionary. The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the model to these choices (e.g., What if a different pivot language were used? How much does performance degrade if a lower-quality dictionary is used?). This is a key limitation, as high-quality resources involving English are not universally available, especially for low-resource language pairs.

3.  **Resource Scarcity for the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The paper’s strongest contribution, the multiQVEC-CCA metric, ironically suffers from a resource scarcity problem itself. Its effectiveness relies on manually created linguistic resources (supersense-tagged corpora), which, as the authors acknowledge, are available for only a handful of languages. This dependency limits the metric's utility for evaluating the very "massively multilingual" embeddings (e.g., the 59-language set) that the paper champions. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic evaluation, the primary, validated use case remains limited to a few well-resourced languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method relies on English as a pivot to create the shared space. How sensitive are the results to this choice? Have you considered experiments with a different pivot language, or perhaps a non-pivot-based approach that averages projections across multiple language pairs to create a more decentralized shared space?

2.  The multiCluster method's performance degrades significantly when scaling from 12 to 59 languages, which you attribute to increased semantic ambiguity within the large cross-lingual clusters. Could this be mitigated by a more nuanced clustering approach, for instance, by limiting cluster size or by creating separate clusters for different senses of a polysemous word if dictionary information allows?
