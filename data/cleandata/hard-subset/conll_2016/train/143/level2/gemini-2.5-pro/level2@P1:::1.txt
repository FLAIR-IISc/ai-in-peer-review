**Summary of the paper and its main contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating shared-space word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors make three primary contributions.

1.  The most significant contribution is a comprehensive framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors propose a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical limitations (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized scores) of a prior metric, QVEC. Crucially, they demonstrate empirically that their proposed metrics correlate much more strongly with performance on downstream tasks (document classification and parsing) than commonly used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation.
2.  The paper introduces two novel methods for estimating multilingual embeddings, multiCluster and multiCCA. These methods are practical and scalable as they only require monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, not large parallel corpora. The authors demonstrate the utility of these methods by training and releasing embeddings for 59 languages.
3.  The authors provide an extensive empirical comparison of their proposed methods against two baselines that use parallel data. To facilitate future research and ensure reproducibility, they have also developed and released an open-source web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, alongside their code and pre-trained models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Contribution to Evaluation Methodology:** The work on evaluation is a standout feature. The paper clearly identifies the weaknesses of existing intrinsic evaluation metrics and proposes a well-motivated alternative, multiQVEC-CCA. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that multiQVEC-CCA is a more reliable proxy for downstream performance than previous metrics. This is an important finding that can help guide future research in this area toward more meaningful objectives.
2.  **Practical and Scalable Embedding Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods are valuable due to their scalability. By avoiding the need for parallel corpora, they can be applied to a much wider range of languages, as demonstrated by the creation of 59-language embeddings. The experiments show that these dictionary-based methods are not only scalable but also highly competitive, outperforming parallel-data-based methods on both extrinsic tasks in the 12-language experiment (Table 3).
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The paper is supported by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. The authors compare four distinct methods on a diverse suite of nine evaluation metrics, covering both intrinsic and extrinsic qualities. The inclusion of two separate experimental settings (a 12-language setting for comparing with parallel-data methods and a 59-language setting to showcase scalability) provides a broad view of the methods' capabilities.
4.  **Significant Community Resource:** The development of a public web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is an excellent contribution to the research community. This, along with the release of code and pre-trained embeddings, promotes transparency, reproducibility, and will undoubtedly facilitate future work in multilingual representation learning.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While the application and scaling are novel, the core ideas behind the proposed estimation methods are extensions of prior work. MultiCluster builds on the common approach of using translation pairs to form cross-lingual links, and multiCCA is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The paper's strength lies more in its comprehensive evaluation and scaling than in groundbreaking algorithmic innovation.
2.  **Inconsistent Results and Need for Deeper Analysis:** The results in the 12-language comparison (Table 3) are inconsistent, with different methods performing best on different tasks. For instance, the translation-invariance method excels at several intrinsic tasks, while the proposed methods perform best on extrinsic tasks. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of *why* these differences occur. A deeper analysis of the architectural biases of each model and how they relate to the demands of specific tasks would provide more insight than the current presentation of results.
3.  **Potential Impact of Dictionary Quality:** For the 59-language experiments, the dictionaries for 47 of the languages were generated using Google Translate. The quality of these dictionaries is unknown and likely variable, which could significantly affect the performance of the dictionary-based methods. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis or discussion regarding how noise or errors in the dictionary might impact the final embeddings. This is an important practical consideration for anyone wishing to apply these methods.
4.  **Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage:** The authors correctly identify that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias intrinsic evaluation scores, and they provide coverage numbers in the tables. However, some of these differences are very large (e.g., in Table 3, translation-invariance has 23% coverage on monolingual word similarity vs. 71% for multiCCA), making direct comparison of the scores problematic. While an analysis on the vocabulary intersection is mentioned for the supplementary material, this issue is significant enough that it warrants more prominent discussion in the main paper, as it directly impacts the interpretation of the central results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method uses English as a single pivot language to project all other languages into. Could you comment on the potential effects of this choice? Have you considered how the choice of pivot might affect performance, particularly for languages typologically distant from English?
2.  Regarding the mixed results in Table 3, could you offer any hypotheses about the architectural biases of the different models? For example, is it possible that the matrix factorization approach of translation-invariance is better suited for preserving the fine-grained local similarities needed for word translation, while the global projection of multiCCA is more effective for the topic-level distinctions required in document classification?
3.  Could you provide some information on the computational cost (e.g., training time, memory usage) for training the embeddings with your methods, particularly for the large-scale 59-language experiment? This would be very useful for researchers considering adopting your methods.
