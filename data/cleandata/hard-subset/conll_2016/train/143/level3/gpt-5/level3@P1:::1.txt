Summary and contributions
- The paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and introduces two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel corpora. It evaluates them against two parallel-data baselines (multiSkip and translation invariance).
- It proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA as intrinsic evaluation metrics that better correlate with downstream performance than prior intrinsic metrics, and extends them to the multilingual setting.
- It delivers an evaluation portal with datasets, scripts, and pretrained models to support reproducibility.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A systematic evaluation showing that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (e.g., cross-lingual similarity, word translation), with strong correlations for document classification (e.g., 0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA).
2) Practical, scalable dictionary-based methods (especially multiCCA) for building shared-space embeddings across 59 languages without parallel data, and an extensive empirical comparison against parallel-data approaches.
3) An evaluation portal and released resources that enable replicable, community-wide benchmarking.

Strengths
- Strong empirical scope and rigor: The paper spans 59 languages (dictionary-based) and 12 languages (parallel-based), and evaluates with both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing). The correlation analysis across 17 embeddings is particularly valuable for the community’s understanding of intrinsic metrics’ utility.
- Useful intrinsic metrics with clear theoretical advantages: multiQVEC-CCA fixes QVEC’s basis-variance and dimensionality issues via CCA and normalizes scores, which is important when comparing models of different dimensions. The demonstrated higher correlation with downstream performance (e.g., 0.896 with document classification) is compelling evidence of practical utility.
- Simple, effective estimation method: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA that uses English as a hub and achieves consistently strong performance with better coverage. Its scalability and reliance on dictionary resources make it attractive when parallel corpora are scarce.
- Reproducibility and community impact: The portal, datasets, and pretrained models are an asset. Reporting coverage alongside scores is a good practice and highlights an often-overlooked confound.
- Clear writing and presentation: The methods and evaluation protocol are described well, making the contributions accessible.

Weaknesses
- Positioning and novelty relative to prior work: The paper’s novelty around estimation methods is incremental. multiCCA is a natural multilingual extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and the translation-invariance baseline (Gardner et al., 2015) is prior work; the paper should more explicitly delineate what is new (beyond scale) and what is replicated or adapted. The reader is left unsure whether any methodological contributions beyond multiCCA/multiCluster and the metric are novel.
- Coverage confounds are under-analyzed: While coverage percentages are reported, several comparisons (e.g., Table 3) likely reflect coverage differences as much as quality differences. Extrinsic tasks are not actually immune: if embeddings are fixed, OOVs receive UNK vectors, which meaningfully affects accuracy. A systematic analysis of OOV rates per method and their impact on each metric (including controlled intersection-vocabulary comparisons and per-frequency breakdowns) is needed.
- Inconsistent and puzzling result patterns: In Table 3, translation-invariance dominates word translation; multiCluster—despite conflating many-word senses into clusters—tops some extrinsic tasks; multiCCA has better coverage but not always the best scores. These patterns deserve deeper analysis: effects of dictionary noise, clustering-induced smoothing, hub language choice in multiCCA, and monolingual vs parallel corpus size should be disentangled via ablations.
- Multilingual supersense signal and fairness: The multilingual extension of QVEC/QVEC-CCA depends on supersense resources that are inconsistent across languages. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify how much cross-language feature overlap exists, nor how sensitive the correlations are to that overlap. There is a risk of English-centric bias if English dominates the feature space.
- Dictionary quality and potential evaluation bias: For many languages, the training and evaluation dictionaries come from Google Translate or word alignments. This can introduce noise and domain bias, and risks circularity if GT is used both to construct supervision and to evaluate. The paper should validate against independent, curated lexicons and assess robustness to dictionary noise.

Questions to authors
- Dependency parsing setup: What is the absolute performance drop from freezing embeddings compared to a standard parser with trainable embeddings and POS/morphology features? Also, what is the gain of using your pretrained embeddings over random embeddings when embeddings are fixed? Please report both, per language if possible.
- Correlation table: Is Table 1 computed across all 17 embeddings described in §5.1, with each embedding producing one pair of (intrinsic, extrinsic) scores? Please confirm the sample size per correlation and provide confidence intervals or significance tests.
- multiSkip vs. multiCCA pipeline: Are there specific advantages to training multiSkip directly over training bilingual embeddings and then using multiCCA to map them into a shared space (e.g., efficiency, coverage, quality on extrinsic tasks)? An empirical comparison would help.
- Dictionary sources: For 59 languages, did you evaluate with any curated bilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary gold subsets, PanLex, or language academy lexicons), distinct from those used for training? If not, could you provide a small-scale validation to rule out biases from GT-derived dictionaries?
- Coverage analysis: Please provide OOV rates per method for each task, and re-run key comparisons on (a) intersection vocabularies and (b) frequency-matched subsets. Does the ranking change?
- Ablations and sensitivity: For multiCluster, how do cluster size thresholds and dictionary precision/recall (τ) affect extrinsic performance? For multiCCA, how sensitive are results to the choice of English as the hub and to dictionary noise (e.g., randomized or pruned dictionaries)?

Overall recommendation
A solid, practical paper with valuable evaluation insights and resources. Clarifying novelty, adding a careful coverage/OOV analysis, and addressing the dictionary and supersense concerns would significantly strengthen the work. Pending these clarifications, I lean toward acceptance for its empirical scope, useful metrics, and community contributions.
