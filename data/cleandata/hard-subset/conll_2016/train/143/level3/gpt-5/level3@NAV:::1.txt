Summary and contributions
- Primary: A multilingual intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that aligns embeddings to supersense features via CCA, yielding rotation- and dimensionality-invariant scores and showing stronger correlation with downstream tasks than standard intrinsic metrics.
- Secondary: Two practical, dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) that scale to 59 languages without parallel corpora; the multilingual extension of CCA anchored in English is simple and effective.
- Tertiary: A public evaluation portal and comprehensive experimental suite across 12–59 languages.

Strengths
- Evaluation innovation and evidence: The CCA-based reformulation addresses known QVEC shortcomings (basis sensitivity, dimensionality bias), and Table 1 shows notably stronger correlations with document classification (0.896) and reasonable correlations with parsing versus traditional metrics.
- Scale and accessibility: Training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages, plus releasing code/portal, meaningfully lowers barriers for future work and enables reproducibility.
- Practical methods: The dictionary-only approaches are attractive where parallel data is scarce. MultiCCA’s “English-as-anchor” design is straightforward, easy to apply, and attains strong coverage.
- Thorough experimental scope: Multiple intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, coverage reporting, and multilingual settings provide a broad empirical picture.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to Gardner et al. (2015): The “translation invariance” component appears largely inherited; the manuscript is unclear on what is new beyond using it as a baseline. If a multilingual generalization or new training/regularization is proposed, it must be explicitly delineated.
- Supersense coverage across languages: Using en/da/it supersenses risks a very small intersection of features; the paper references this (footnote 9) but does not quantify the per-language overlap or its impact. Without this, multiQVEC(-CCA) scores may be biased by coverage artifacts.
- Coverage confounds: Tables 2–3 show sizable coverage differences (e.g., parsing and classification for multiCluster vs multiCCA). The current analysis acknowledges this and provides an intersection-only comparison in the supplement; a central, coverage-controlled analysis and stratification by frequency are needed to interpret the results fairly.
- Inconsistent results need explanation: Table 3 patterns are puzzling—translation invariance dominates word translation, intrinsic metrics often do not favor multilingual techniques, yet multiCluster (which collapses inter-language distinctions) wins several extrinsic tasks. The paper should analyze why (e.g., cluster-induced denoising aiding transfer, hubness/anisotropy, alignment noise, or objective-task mismatch).

Questions to authors
- What is the parsing performance loss when embeddings are fixed vs. fine-tuned, and what gains arise from merely replacing random with pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
- Is Table 1 computed over the 17 embeddings described in §5.1, and if so, how are correlations aggregated (per-embedding pairs vs. per-setting averages)?
- Does multiSkip offer advantages over training bilingual embeddings and then using multiCCA to map into a shared space? Please compare directly.
- Dictionaries: beyond alignments and Google Translate, did you evaluate with curated multilingual lexicons to assess robustness to dictionary noise?
