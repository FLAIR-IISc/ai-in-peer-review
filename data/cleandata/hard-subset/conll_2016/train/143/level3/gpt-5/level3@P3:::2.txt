Summary and key contributions
- Primary: QVEC-CCA and its multilingual variant. The paper identifies two theoretical issues with QVEC (basis dependence, dimensionality bias) and proposes QVEC-CCA, which shows markedly stronger correlation with downstream tasks in the reported setting. Extending the supersense-based evaluation to multiple languages is also practical.
- Secondary: Public evaluation portal, datasets, and code. The portal and releases materially improve reproducibility and comparability for multilingual embeddings research.
- Tertiary: Two simple dictionary-based estimation methods (MultiCCA and MultiCluster) that scale to 59 languages without parallel corpora, occasionally outperforming strong baselines.

Strengths
- Evaluation rigor and resources: The work evaluates across many intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, over 12- and 59-language settings, and provides a web portal with benchmarks and code. This is a substantial community contribution that will standardize evaluation and lower entry barriers.
- Improved intrinsic metric with evidence: QVEC-CCA is a well-motivated correction to QVEC’s weaknesses and achieves substantially higher correlations with downstream document classification than prior intrinsic metrics; multiQVEC also correlates well, supporting the utility of linguistically grounded evaluations.
- Practical multilingual estimation methods: MultiCCA (CCA to an English hub) and MultiCluster (dictionary-induced cluster IDs + skip-gram) are simple and effective in several settings, require only monolingual data plus dictionaries, and scale to 59 languages—an attractive engineering trade-off.
- Clarity and documentation: The paper is clearly written, careful about coverage caveats, and generally meticulous in explaining setups, metrics, and training details.

Weaknesses
- Limited analytical depth: The paper favors breadth over depth. Key design choices (e.g., English-as-hub in CCA; dictionary extraction and noise control; weighting of monolingual corpora; post-normalization) are not analyzed, and ablations/sensitivity studies are largely missing.
- Unexplained MultiCluster discrepancies: MultiCluster performs poorly in the 59-language setting but is competitive (even best) in several 12-language metrics. The paper does not probe why. Plausible factors include: greater sense conflation from large connected components as language count grows; higher dictionary noise in the 59-language setup (Google Translate) vs. Europarl-derived dictionaries; max cluster size; rare-word handling; and imbalanced corpus sizes across languages fueling cluster frequency skew.
- Insufficient CCA methodological discussion: The English hub, the use of Ten→m,en inverse, and CCA hyperparameters (regularization, dimensionality chosen, whitening, vocabulary selection, dictionary sparsity) are not studied. Alternative formulations (e.g., multi-view/joint CCA, choosing different hubs, or averaging multiple hubs) are not compared, leaving unclear whether the observed gains are robust.
- Evaluation and reporting gaps: Table 1 reports correlations only for multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA; monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlations with extrinsic tasks are omitted, weakening the claim that QVEC-CCA is broadly superior. Coverage confounds are acknowledged, but coverage-controlled results are relegated to supplementary; key comparisons (e.g., Table 3) would benefit from putting both “full” and “intersection” scores side by side in the main text and reporting statistical significance where applicable.
- Related work and citation inconsistencies: Lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. for MultiSkip, but §2.3 only mentions Luong et al.; the correspondence among these methods and the specific differences of the authors’ “multiSkip” should be clarified. Two equations on p.3 exceed the column margin. The phrase “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” (l.304) is misleading—please specify exactly which datasets and how they are aggregated. The related-work section could better situate dictionary-based clustering approaches and multilingual/translational context methods; several relevant works are not discussed.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- MultiCluster analysis: Can you break down performance by (a) cluster size distribution, (b) dictionary source (Europarl vs. Google Translate), (c) τ threshold used to select translation pairs, and (d) cap on max cluster size? How do results change if you restrict to high-confidence dictionary edges, or prune cross-language edges that induce large polysemous components?
- Corpus weighting and frequency skew: In MultiCluster, do you weight sentences/languages equally when concatenating monolingual corpora? Does reweighting to equalize per-language contribution reduce cluster frequency dominance and improve 59-language results?
- CCA choices: What are the CCA regularization and target dimensionality? Are the projections whitened? How sensitive are results to the choice of English as hub versus, e.g., German or a jointly learned space? What happens if you use Ten→m,en directly (without inversion) or adopt a symmetric mapping to a shared latent space (multi-view CCA)?
- Coverage control and significance: For Table 3, can you present main-text results both on full coverage and on the intersection vocabulary, with standard errors and significance tests (e.g., Fisher z for correlations, bootstrap or paired tests for tasks)?
- Correlation table: Please add monolingual QVEC and monolingual QVEC-CCA correlations with both extrinsic tasks (alongside multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) to support the general QVEC-CCA improvement claim.

Additional comments (minor/formatting/citations)
- Wording: Replace “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” with a precise description (list datasets, number of pairs per set, aggregation protocol, and whether scores are averaged or pooled).
- Formatting: Two equations on p.3 exceed the column margin; please reflow to fit the format.
- Citations and method naming: Clarify how your “multiSkip” relates to Coulmance et al. (TransGram) and Guo et al. (bilingual/multilingual skip-gram variants), beyond noting lack of monolingual data. Ensure consistent citations between lines 121/147 and §2.3.
- Fairness details: Align context window choices across methods where possible (translation-invariance uses window=3 vs. 5 elsewhere). Report random seeds and variance over multiple runs for key scores.
- Related work: Expand discussion of multilingual dictionary-based mappings and clustering approaches, as well as methods leveraging translational context beyond word alignments, to better contextualize MultiCluster and MultiCCA within the taxonomy.

Overall recommendation
Accept if space allows, primarily for the practical impact: the improved intrinsic metric (QVEC-CCA) with demonstrated correlation to downstream tasks, and the evaluation portal and resources. The estimation methods are simple and sometimes competitive, but the paper offers limited new research insights without deeper analysis. Strengthening the methodological discussion and adding the requested analyses would significantly increase the paper’s scientific value.
