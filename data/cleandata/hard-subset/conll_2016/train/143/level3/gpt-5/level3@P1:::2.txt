Summary
The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for learning massively multilingual word embeddings without requiring parallel corpora: (1) MultiCluster, which ties translationally equivalent word forms into shared clusters and learns embeddings over cluster sequences; and (2) MultiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into a shared space via CCA using bilingual dictionaries, with English as the pivot. The paper also introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics based on supersense annotations, and presents an evaluation web portal. Experiments cover up to 59 languages and multiple intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The key empirical findings are that the proposed intrinsic metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than widely used alternatives, and that MultiCCA is consistently strong and generally more robust than MultiCluster when scaling to many languages.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Improved intrinsic evaluation for multilingual word embeddings (multiQVEC-CCA in particular), showing substantially higher correlation with extrinsic tasks than word similarity and word translation. This directly addresses the evaluation gap and has community-facing value.
2) Public evaluation portal and resources that standardize benchmarking across intrinsic/extrinsic metrics and languages, improving reproducibility and comparability.
3) Practical dictionary-based estimation methods (MultiCCA and MultiCluster) that avoid parallel corpora and scale to 59 languages, with MultiCCA achieving strong coverage and competitive performance.

Strengths
- Strong evidence that multiQVEC-CCA better reflects downstream utility: The correlation analysis (Table 1) shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates 0.896 with document classification and outperforms word similarity (0.386) and word translation (0.066). MultiQVEC also outperforms prior intrinsic metrics for both downstream tasks. This is a meaningful step toward closing the intrinsic–extrinsic gap.
- Breadth of evaluation and scale: Training across 59 languages and evaluating on a suite of intrinsic (mono- and cross-lingual similarity, word translation, multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA) and extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification, dependency parsing) provides a comprehensive view and increases the generality of the conclusions.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The web portal, open-source release, and standardized datasets (including dev/test splits for metrics) will likely have immediate community impact by reducing friction in comparing methods and encouraging sound experimental practices.
- Clear exposition and careful documentation: The paper is well written, provides concrete design choices (e.g., dictionary extraction threshold τ, minimal frequency cutoff, coverage reporting), and offers nuanced caveats (e.g., coverage confounds in intrinsic evaluation), which enhances trustworthiness.

Weaknesses
- Breadth over depth: Analysis of why methods perform as they do is limited. For example, the conflicting MultiCluster results across 59 vs 12 languages are only briefly attributed to semantic ambiguity from clustering; a deeper investigation is warranted (e.g., cluster size distributions, polysemy rates, dictionary noise, and hubness effects).
- Limited sensitivity analyses: There is little exploration of how design choices influence outcomes. For MultiCluster: cluster-size caps (1K/10K), dictionary threshold τ, minimum frequency cutoff, and window size could materially affect ambiguity and performance. For MultiCCA: pivot-language choice, stability/invertibility of mappings (T−1), dimensionality, and using bilingual vs multi-dictionary CCA are not probed.
- Evaluation confounds: Some comparisons may favor specific methods due to data choices. MultiSkip is trained only on parallel text, whereas competing methods exploit richer monolingual corpora; this weakens causal interpretation when it ranks last. For 59 languages, dictionaries from Google Translate are used both for training and (partly) evaluation, potentially inflating translation-based metrics. Coverage differences, while reported, still complicate intrinsic comparisons; more intersection-based or coverage-controlled analyses would help.
- Incomplete support for the QVEC-CCA claim: Table 1 reports correlations only for multilingual metrics. Including analogous correlations for monolingual QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA would strengthen the argument that CCA addresses QVEC’s theoretical flaws in practice.
- Dependency on English resources: MultiCCA anchors all languages in English space, and multiQVEC(-CCA) relies on supersense annotations in English (plus Danish and Italian). The impact of pivot choice and uneven resource availability is not characterized, limiting generality.

Questions to Authors
- MultiCluster discrepancies: Can you analyze the 59-language failures in terms of (a) cluster size/degree distributions, (b) fraction of clusters mixing multiple senses, and (c) dictionary precision/recall at τ=0.1? Do performance and ambiguity correlate? Would smaller max cluster sizes mitigate degradation?
- MultiCCA design: Why constrain to an English pivot rather than jointly solving a multi-view CCA? How sensitive are results to pivot choice, mapping conditioning (e.g., orthogonality), and numerical stability of T−1?
- Parameter sensitivity: How do window size, dimensionality, minimal frequency threshold, and normalization affect each method? Are the main conclusions robust across seeds?
- Coverage controls: Could you provide intersection-vocabulary results for all intrinsic metrics and extrinsic results with controlled sub-vocabularies to better isolate method quality from coverage effects?
- Portal details: Will the portal accept private uploads with reproducible identifiers? Are evaluation datasets versioned, and are scripts to compute all metrics publicly available to facilitate local benchmarking?

Additional Comments
- Clarify MultiSkip correspondences: Lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., but §2.3 describes Luong et al. Please clearly map your implementation to these prior variants and highlight differences (e.g., use of monolingual data).
- Add related work context: Expand discussion connecting your methods to cross-lingual word classes (Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012), bilingual mapping with orthogonal transforms (Mikolov et al., 2013b), and CCA-based lexicon induction (Haghighi et al., 2008), situating MultiCCA/MultiCluster within these lines.
- Support for QVEC-CCA: Include monolingual correlation results (analogous to Table 1) to empirically validate the basis-invariance and normalization advantages.
- Minor wording: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” (l.304) is ambiguous; specify which datasets and how they are combined.
- Formatting: Two equations on p.3 exceed the column margin; please fix.
- Notation check: Ensure consistent use of indices and matrix shapes in CCA/multiCCA definitions, and briefly comment on the conditioning of Ten→m,en and its inversion.

Overall evaluation
Accept with minor revisions. The paper’s main value lies in better multilingual evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA) and the portal/resources, plus scalable practical methods. Deeper analysis and sensitivity studies would improve scientific insight, but the contribution to the community is strong and timely.
