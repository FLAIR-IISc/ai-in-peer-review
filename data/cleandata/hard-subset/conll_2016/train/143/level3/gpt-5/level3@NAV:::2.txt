Summary and Contributions
- Primary: QVEC-CCA, a CCA-based intrinsic evaluation that addresses QVEC’s basis dependence and scale issues, and its multilingual supersense extension. This yields stronger correlations with downstream tasks and is a substantive, broadly useful contribution.
- Secondary: A public evaluation portal with datasets, scripts, and upload capability that standardizes multilingual embedding benchmarking and facilitates reproducibility.
- Tertiary: Two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods, MultiCCA (pivoted CCA into English space) and MultiCluster (cluster-and-share), trained up to 59 languages; these sometimes outperform baselines but are comparatively incremental.

Strengths
- Practical impact and community service: The web portal, data packaging, and code release materially lower the barrier to entry and should catalyze progress and comparability in multilingual embeddings.
- Improved intrinsic evaluation: QVEC-CCA is well motivated (basis-invariance, normalization), multilingualized, and empirically correlates better with extrinsic tasks than popular alternatives; this is likely to see adoption.
- Breadth of evaluation: The paper spans 59- and 12-language settings, multiple intrinsic metrics, and two extrinsic tasks (doc classification, parsing), offering a comprehensive empirical picture.
- Clear exposition: The paper is well written and meticulously documented; methodological descriptions are accessible and reproducibility appears high.

Weaknesses
- Breadth over depth: Design choices and failure modes are under-analyzed. The paper would benefit from focused ablations and error analyses rather than expanding more tasks/languages.
- Unexplained inconsistency: MultiCluster’s strong showing in 12 languages versus its degradation in 59 is noted but not dissected (e.g., cluster transitive closure effects, polysemy conflation, cluster size caps, dictionary noise).
- Limited parameter sensitivity: Little is reported on τ for dictionary extraction, maximum cluster size, CCA dimensionality/regularization, pivot choice (English), or coverage filtering decisions; downstream impacts remain unclear.
- Evidence gap in correlations: To substantiate the QVEC-CCA improvement claim, include monolingual QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA correlations with downstream tasks (e.g., alongside Table 1).
- Related work and citations: Inconsistency between text and citations for MultiSkip (Coulmance/Guo vs. Luong-only in §2.3); broader positioning w.r.t. multilingual clustering and translational context methods could be expanded.

Questions to Authors
- What drives MultiCluster’s divergence between 12 and 59 languages? Can you quantify the roles of cluster chaining, polysemy, and dictionary noise (e.g., ablate max cluster size, restrict transitivity depth)?
- How sensitive are results to τ, cluster size caps, and CCA regularization/dimensionality? Please provide sensitivity plots.
- Why fix English as pivot in MultiCCA? Would alternative pivots or multi-pivot/joint CCA change results?
- Can you report monolingual QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA correlations with each extrinsic task (scatter plots)?

Additional Comments
- Wording: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” (l.304) is misleading—specify exactly which datasets and the aggregation protocol.
- Formatting: Two equations on p.3 exceed the column margin—please fix.
- Citations: Clarify correspondences for MultiSkip (lines 121/147 vs. §2.3). Consider expanding related work on multilingual embeddings, clustering, and translational context to better situate MultiCluster and MultiCCA.
- Overall: High practical value (QVEC-CCA, portal), but limited new methodological insight without deeper analysis.
