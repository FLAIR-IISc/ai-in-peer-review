Summary and contributions
This paper proposes methods and resources for massively multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. It introduces two dictionary-based estimation techniques—multiCluster and multiCCA—that jointly embed 50+ languages without requiring sentence-aligned parallel data. It also adapts QVEC to the multilingual setting (multiQVEC) and proposes QVEC-CCA/multiQVEC-CCA, an evaluation measure addressing basis-variance and normalization issues in QVEC. Finally, it releases a web portal and datasets that standardize intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Evaluation contribution: multiQVEC-CCA (and multiQVEC) as intrinsic metrics with stronger correlation to downstream tasks than word similarity/translation, plus an evaluation portal and standardized datasets that facilitate reproducible comparison.
2) Practical large-scale embedding resources: dictionary-based training of multilingual embeddings across 59 languages, along with careful, multi-metric evaluation.
3) Two estimation methods, particularly the simple yet effective multilingual CCA projection (multiCCA). While method novelty is modest, the practical formulation and breadth of experiments are valuable.

Strengths
- Strong practical impact via evaluation resources and portal. The web portal, open data, and standardized dev/test splits will likely become widely used. The paper’s emphasis on replicability (coverage reporting, fixed hyperparameters, release plans) is exemplary and addresses a recurring pain point in this area.
- Improved intrinsic metrics with demonstrated correlation to extrinsic tasks. The reported correlations (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA: 0.896 with document classification; multiQVEC: 0.635 with doc classification and 0.444 with parsing) convincingly show advantages over cross-lingual word similarity and word translation tasks, which correlate poorly with downstream performance.
- Breadth of empirical evaluation across tasks and languages. The paper evaluates both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, and compares dictionary-based and parallel-based estimators across 12- and 59-language settings, yielding a comprehensive empirical picture.
- Simple and scalable estimation methods. MultiCCA’s pivot-to-English CCA is easy to implement, leverages monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, and achieves competitive results with strong coverage across languages. MultiCluster offers a clear clustering formulation and demonstrates competitive performance in the 12-language scenario.
- Clear and careful presentation. The paper is generally well written, with explicit descriptions of setups, hyperparameters, and thoughtful commentary on coverage biases in intrinsic evaluations.

Weaknesses
- Breadth over depth; limited analysis of key design choices. The paper surveys many settings but often stops short of deeper analysis. The conflicting multiCluster results—strong in 12 languages (e.g., top on parsing; tied on doc classification) yet clearly weaker than multiCCA in 59 languages—deserve a more thorough investigation (e.g., sense conflation vs. coverage vs. dictionary noise vs. maximum cluster size).
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analyses. Important decisions are fixed early without reported sensitivity studies: dictionary threshold τ=0.1, maximum cluster sizes (1,000 vs. 10,000), window sizes, epochs, unit-length normalization, CCA pivot to English, dictionary size/quality (Europarl vs. Google Translate). Understanding robustness to these choices would strengthen conclusions and help practitioners.
- Evaluation comparability and resource regimes. Comparisons between dictionary-based (often using Google Translate for 47 languages) and parallel-based methods can be confounded by resource quality and coverage differences. While the paper notes coverage effects, a fairer comparison could include matched-resource settings (e.g., use the same dictionaries for all methods when possible, or controlled-noise variants) and vocabulary-intersection analyses beyond what’s relegated to supplementary material.
- Missing correlation evidence for monolingual QVEC/QVEC-CCA. The paper claims QVEC-CCA addresses theoretical flaws and better correlates with downstream tasks; however, the correlation table omits monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA. Including their correlations with the extrinsic tasks (even if on fewer languages) would directly support the improvement claim.
- Minor clarity and consistency issues. 
  - Citation mapping: Lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. in relation to multiSkip, yet §2.3 describes only Luong et al. Please clarify the exact correspondences and differences (resource usage, monolingual data, training objectives).
  - Wording: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” is vague; list datasets and sampling protocol.
  - Formatting: two equations on p.3 exceed the column margin.

Questions to authors (for response)
- MultiCluster vs. multiCCA at scale: What factors most drive multiCluster’s degradation at 59 languages? Please provide ablations on (i) dictionary noise/precision (vary τ and/or dictionary source), (ii) maximum cluster size, (iii) handling of polysemy (e.g., pruning hub nodes, limiting degree), and (iv) coverage vs. performance trade-offs.
- Parameter sensitivity: How sensitive are results to (a) CCA pivot language (e.g., de, es) vs. en, (b) monolingual embeddings (dimensions, algorithms), (c) number/quality/size of dictionary pairs, and (d) skip-gram window/epochs? Any evidence that orthogonality constraints or Procrustes alignments change results?
- Fairer cross-regime comparisons: Can you include a setting where all methods use the same bilingual signal (e.g., only dictionaries extracted from the same parallel data), to isolate method effects from resource differences?
- Correlation completeness: Please add monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlations with both extrinsic tasks to Table 1 (or an expanded table) to substantiate the QVEC-CCA improvement claim.
- MultiSkip relation to prior work: Precisely delineate how your multiSkip differs from Coulmance et al. (Trans-gram), Guo et al., and Luong et al. (objectives, use of monolingual/parallel data, alignment requirements).

Additional comments
- Related work: It would help to broaden discussion on multilingual embeddings and clustering/translation contexts, contrasting your methods with Klementiev et al. (2012), Gouws et al. (BilBOWA), Luong et al. (2015a,b), Coulmance et al. (2015), Guo et al. (2015, 2016), Al-Rfou et al. (2013), Täckström et al. (2012), Och (1999), and Haghighi et al. (2008), emphasizing differences in supervision, scalability, and evaluation.
- The reliance on supersense annotations for en/da/it limits multilingual generality of multiQVEC/CCA; consider discussing plans for broader linguistic resources (e.g., universal POS/supersense harmonization).
- Despite limited novelty, the improved intrinsic metric and the evaluation portal are likely to be impactful, and the paper is a solid contribution to community infrastructure.
