Summary
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings. It proposes two dictionary-based estimation methods—multiCluster (clustering translationally equivalent surface forms and training one vector per cluster) and multiCCA (projecting each non-English monolingual space into an English anchor space via CCA)—and compares them to a multilingual extension of bilingual skip-gram (multiSkip) and to the translation-invariance matrix factorization of Gardner et al. The paper further introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a rotation/scale-invariant intrinsic evaluation that aligns embeddings with multilingual linguistic features via CCA, and shows that it correlates better with downstream tasks than standard similarity/translation tests. Extensive experiments cover up to 59 languages (dictionary-based methods) and 12 languages when parallel data are required, plus a web portal and resources.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) An intrinsic evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA and its multilingual extension) that better correlates with downstream document classification and parsing than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) A valuable, reproducible evaluation suite and resource release (evaluation portal, benchmarks, and large multilingual embedding sets).
3) Two simple, scalable dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) with competitive performance across many languages.

Strengths
- Evaluation rigor and breadth: The paper evaluates four families of embeddings on nine metrics, across 12–59 languages, and explicitly studies intrinsic–extrinsic correlations. Table 1 convincingly shows that multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates better with downstream tasks than word similarity or word translation.
- Practicality and scaling: multiCCA is easy to implement, relies only on monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, and scales to 59 languages. The web portal and released resources substantially lower barriers for subsequent work.
- Clear exposition: The estimation objectives (Sections 2.1–2.4) and the evaluation methods (Section 3) are well motivated and described.
- Competitive results: For 59 languages, multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster. For 12 languages, dictionary-based methods often top the two extrinsic tasks, while translation-invariance excels on word translation.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a straightforward multilingual extension of Faruqui and Dyer’s bilingual CCA by anchoring to English, and multiCluster is closely related to prior cross-lingual clustering/tying. The paper’s primary novelty lies more in evaluation and scale than in modeling. Please be explicit about what, if anything, is novel relative to Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance beyond using it as a baseline (e.g., a multilingual extension or new training setup).
- Multilingual supersense evaluation risks sparsity: Using supersense tag matrices for en/da/it could yield a very small cross-lingual intersection of annotated types/features. Footnote 9 acknowledges inconsistencies. The paper should quantify (i) the number of shared word types per language and per metric instance, (ii) the number of supersense features actually present per language, and (iii) how much the multiQVEC(-CCA) scores change when restricting to the intersection versus union. Alternatives like universal POS features are mentioned but not instantiated.
- Coverage confounds: Coverage differs notably across methods (e.g., monolingual similarity: invariance has 23% coverage but the best score; doc classification: multiCCA covers 62.8% vs multiCluster 48.1% with identical accuracy). While extrinsic metrics compute on all tokens, coverage still affects how often words become OOV and mapped to UNK, which in turn affects performance. Please analyze coverage’s impact by reporting OOV token/type rates per language/task/method, results on the intersection-of-coverage vocabulary, and controlled ablations where UNK behavior is held constant.
- Inconsistent patterns in Table 3 need explanation: Intrinsic metrics often do not favor multilingual techniques; translation-invariance dominates word-translation; and multiCluster—despite collapsing translationally equivalent words and discarding within-cluster distinctions—performs best on parsing and ties on document classification. Likely contributors include (a) strong cross-lingual tying that helps low-resource transfer in extrinsic tasks when embeddings are fixed, (b) English-anchored multiCCA penalizing non-English–non-English relations, and (c) invariance directly optimizing cross-lingual alignment signals, inflating word-translation. The paper should test these hypotheses: e.g., evaluate en↔X vs X↔Y translation separately; measure the effect of tying by varying cluster sizes; include a version of multiCCA that symmetrically aligns into a learned shared space rather than anchoring to English; and run ablations on dictionary noise.
- Calibration of the parsing setting: Fixing embeddings and removing POS/morphology highlights embeddings, but it also departs from standard practice. The paper should quantify (i) the absolute performance loss from fixing embeddings versus fine-tuning, and (ii) the gains over random initializations when plugged into a standard trainable stack-LSTM parser. Without these, it is hard to judge practical utility.

Questions to Authors
1) Dependency parsing calibration: What is the absolute LAS/UAS loss from freezing embeddings versus allowing fine-tuning? And what are the gains from replacing random embeddings with each pretrained set in a standard trainable stack-LSTM parser?
2) Table 1 computation: Are the reported correlations computed over the 17 embedding configurations described in Section 5.1 (i.e., 17 points per intrinsic–extrinsic pair), averaged across languages? Please clarify precisely how the 102 values are formed.
3) multiSkip vs bilingual+multiCCA: Are there principled advantages to training a single multiSkip model over training bilingual skip-gram models per pair and then projecting them into a shared space with multiCCA? Have you compared these two pipelines under matched data?
4) Dictionaries: Since Europarl alignments and Google Translate may not reflect true lexicons, did you evaluate against (or train with) curated bilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary/OMEGAGLOT/CC-CEDICT) and measure the effect on both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics?
5) Supersense intersection: What are the sizes of the multilingual supersense matrices per language (types×features), their intersections, and the proportion of features actually active per language? How sensitive are multiQVEC(-CCA) scores to these intersections?
6) Coverage/OOV analysis: For Table 3, please report per-language OOV token rates in parsing and document classification for each method, and re-run both extrinsic tasks with a fixed UNK handling and with intersection-of-coverage vocabularies to isolate coverage effects.
7) English anchoring: For multiCCA, have you tried a joint CCA into a learned shared space (or Procrustes alignment to a barycenter) rather than anchoring to English? Does this improve X↔Y translation for non-English pairs and multiQVEC(-CCA)?
