Summary and Contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. It introduces two dictionary-based estimation methods that do not require parallel corpora (multiCluster, multiCCA), proposes multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) intended to correlate with downstream tasks, and provides a public evaluation portal. Experiments span up to 59 languages, include both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, and analyze correlations between them.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Evaluation: multiQVEC-CCA (and its multilingual variant) addresses QVEC’s rotation and dimensionality issues via CCA and shows substantially stronger correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics; the correlation analysis across 17 embedding settings is valuable.
2) Practical resources: a public evaluation portal with standardized datasets and protocols, plus open-source implementations, to facilitate reproducible multilingual embedding research.
3) Estimation methods: two dictionary-based approaches (multiCCA with projection into an English pivot space, and multiCluster using dictionary-induced clusters), trained at scale (59 languages) and compared to parallel-data baselines.

Strengths
- Strong, carefully designed evaluation and correlation analysis: The study directly tests the link between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. Table 1 shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates very well with document classification (0.896) and multiQVEC correlates best with parsing (0.444), while standard word similarity/translation correlate poorly. This is a useful community result.
- Scalable, low-resource-friendly estimation: Both multiCCA and multiCluster use only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, enabling coverage over 59 languages without parallel text. This is an important practical contribution for multilingual NLP.
- Breadth and transparency of experiments: Results are reported across multiple metrics (monolingual/cross-lingual similarity, word translation, QVEC-based metrics, parsing, document classification), with attention to coverage effects and a comparison against parallel-based baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance).
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, the methods are easy to follow, and the portal/code/data plan (even with double-blind redaction) supports replication and future comparison.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty for multiCCA and unclear positioning vs. prior work: multiCCA is essentially a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA to a multilingual setting via a pivot (English). The paper acknowledges this as a “simple extension (in hindsight).” The novelty over related projection-based methods (e.g., other bilingual-to-multilingual mappings via CCA/Procrustes) should be spelled out, and the comparison to Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance (and other projection methods) could be more explicit about what is substantively new here.
- Coverage confounds some reported comparisons: The paper notes coverage issues and reports coverage percentages, but several key takeaways (e.g., Table 3 intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks) are still difficult to interpret without a consistent vocabulary or stronger control analyses. The supplementary intersection analysis is mentioned; however, the main text needs a more thorough, quantitative discussion of how much conclusions change when controlling for coverage.
- Inconsistencies across metrics are underexplained: In Table 3, translation-invariance dramatically outperforms on word translation, while multiCluster (which forces cross-lingual type sharing and can increase polysemy) competes or wins on extrinsic tasks; multiCCA often has the best coverage but not the best scores across all metrics. These patterns are interesting but currently puzzling. The paper would benefit from analyses attributing differences to data domain, model capacity, induced ambiguity, or optimization choices, and from error analyses on extrinsic tasks.
- Reliance on MT-derived dictionaries for both training and evaluation: For the 59-language setup, dictionaries are built via Google Translate, and evaluation for word translation also uses Google Translate for many languages. This raises concerns about bias and circularity (training and evaluation distributions potentially aligned by the same MT system). Results may not reflect real lexicons; corroboration with independent human- or lexicon-derived dictionaries is needed.
- Multilingual supersense resource limitations: multiQVEC(-CCA) relies on supersense annotations in en/da/it, with known inconsistencies across languages (acknowledged in a footnote). The paper should elaborate on how tag inconsistencies and the possibly small cross-lingual intersection of features affect metric stability, and provide sensitivity analyses or alternative tagsets (e.g., universal POS for syntactic information).

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
1) Dependency parsing setup: What is the performance gap between (a) a parser with trainable (randomly initialized) embeddings, (b) the same parser with pretrained embeddings that are fine-tuned, and (c) the reported setting where embeddings are fixed? Please quantify the absolute/relative gains from replacing random embeddings with each pretrained embedding type.
2) Correlation computation in Table 1: Please confirm that each correlation is computed over the 17 embeddings described in §5.1, with one scalar score per embedding per metric, and report the exact N and p-values or confidence intervals.
3) multiSkip vs. projection pipelines: Beyond the reported results, are there cases where training multiSkip directly over parallel data offers advantages over learning bilingual embeddings followed by multiCCA into a shared space? For example, in domains with rich parallel coverage but limited dictionaries, or regarding convergence/stability?
4) Dictionary quality and realism: Did you evaluate with any manually curated multilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary subsets vetted for quality, PanLex, or bilingual lexicons from NLP shared tasks)? If so, how do results compare to Google Translate–derived dictionaries? If not, can you comment on potential bias and plans to include independent lexicon-based evaluations?
5) Coverage effects: For the main tables, can you add controlled comparisons on the intersection of vocabularies and quantify how rankings change, especially for document classification and parsing? A plot of score vs. coverage could also help.
6) multiCluster ambiguity: Can you analyze how often clusters merge non-synonymous translations (polysemy) and how this affects intrinsic vs. extrinsic performance? Ablations varying cluster size thresholds or dictionary precision/recall (τ) would be informative.

Additional Comments
- Please clarify the novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance: is there any multilingual extension or implementation change beyond applying their method as a baseline?
- Consider discussing alternative projection objectives (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) and whether they differ materially from CCA in the multilingual setting.
- The portal is a valuable resource; a brief note on long-term hosting and the plan to include independent, non-MT-derived evaluation lexicons would strengthen its impact.
