

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating word embeddings across more than 50 languages in a single shared embedding space. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise dictionaries, and develop a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that improves upon previous intrinsic evaluation metrics. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that demonstrates superior correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing metrics.
2. A practical web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will facilitate future research in this area.
3. Two new methods for estimating multilingual embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that enable training on a large number of languages.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical evidence for improved evaluation metric**: The paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA correlates significantly better (0.896) with downstream document classification performance than previous intrinsic metrics (e.g., 0.386 for word similarity). This is a substantial contribution to the field, as reliable evaluation is critical for progress in multilingual NLP. The authors' correlation analysis across multiple languages and tasks provides compelling evidence for the metric's value.

2. **High practical value of the evaluation portal**: The web portal they developed will significantly lower the barrier to entry for researchers working on multilingual embeddings. The portal supports nine evaluation metrics, provides standard datasets, and enables easy comparison of methods. This practical contribution will have immediate impact on the community and is well-documented in the paper.

3. **Demonstrated scalability to 59 languages**: The paper successfully trains multilingual embeddings across 59 languages, which represents a significant expansion of previous work. The authors show that their methods (particularly multiCCA) can achieve good performance at this scale, addressing an important gap in the literature.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of depth in result analysis**: The paper presents interesting results but fails to adequately analyze them. For instance, multiCluster performs well on 12 languages but significantly worse on 59 languages, yet the paper provides no thorough explanation for this discrepancy. A deeper analysis of why the method scales poorly would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Insufficient discussion of design choices**: The paper lacks detailed exploration of how design decisions (e.g., cluster size in multiCluster, threshold τ for dictionary extraction) affect performance. The authors claim multiCCA outperforms multiCluster but don't provide evidence about how sensitive their results are to parameter choices.

3. **Inadequate support for the QVEC-CCA improvement claim**: The paper asserts that multiQVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC but doesn't provide sufficient evidence. Specifically, the authors should have included monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results (as suggested in Table 1) to better support their claim that the new metric is an improvement.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please provide a more detailed analysis of why multiCluster's performance drops significantly when applied to 59 languages versus 12 languages, and what modifications could improve its scalability.

2. Please elaborate on the parameter sensitivity of your methods, particularly for multiCluster (e.g., cluster size, threshold τ), and how these choices impact performance across different language sets.

3. The paper claims multiQVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC, but lacks evidence for this in the monolingual setting. Please include monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results to better support this claim.

## Additional Comments
- The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (line 304) is misleading as it implies the datasets were combined rather than aggregated.
- Two equations on page 3 exceed the column margin, which should be fixed in the final version.
- There is a citation inconsistency: Lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. for MultiSkip, but section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. (2015b). Please clarify this correspondence.
- The paper would benefit from additional related work on multilingual embeddings, particularly recent advances in clustering and translational context. Several relevant papers (e.g., Täckström et al. 2012, Haghighi et al. 2008) should be cited.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with its improved evaluation metric and web portal, but would be significantly strengthened by deeper analysis of the results and better support for its methodological claims. The practical value of the work is high, though the research insights are somewhat limited.
