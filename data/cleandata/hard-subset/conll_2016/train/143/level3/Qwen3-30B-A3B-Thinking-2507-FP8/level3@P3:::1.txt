

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings" (CONLL 2016)

## Summary
This paper proposes four methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages: multiCluster (dictionary-based clustering), multiCCA (projecting languages into English anchor via CCA), multiSkip (extending bilingual skipgram to multiple languages), and Translation Invariance (low-rank PMI decomposition with bilingual alignment terms). It introduces a new evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA) that demonstrates better correlation with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics. The authors also develop a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

## Main Contributions
1. **Evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA)** - The most significant contribution, as it addresses a critical theoretical flaw in previous evaluation metrics and demonstrates superior correlation with downstream tasks.
2. **Dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA)** - Useful practical methods that don't require parallel data, though novelty is limited.
3. **Web portal for multilingual embedding evaluation** - A valuable resource that will facilitate future research in the field.

## Strengths
1. **Strong evaluation framework**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that their proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric correlates better with downstream tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification, 0.273 with parsing) than previous metrics (word similarity: 0.386, word translation: 0.066). This addresses a critical gap in the field, as prior work lacked a metric that properly correlates with real-world performance.

2. **Clear presentation and practical contribution**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of methods and thorough evaluation. The web portal for evaluation is a major practical contribution that will significantly benefit the community by enabling standardized, replicable evaluations.

3. **Comprehensive analysis**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of how different metrics correlate with downstream tasks (Table 1), demonstrating that standard intrinsic metrics (word similarity, translation) perform poorly compared to their proposed metrics. This is a significant step forward in the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unclear novelty**: The paper fails to explicitly explain the novelty of their multilingual extension of translation invariance (Gardner et al. 2015). While they propose a multilingual version, the key contribution appears to be the extension to multiple languages rather than the underlying method. This needs to be clarified in the paper.

2. **Supersense annotation issues**: The use of supersense annotations across multiple languages risks a tiny intersection of features (footnote 9), yet the paper doesn't adequately address this limitation. The authors acknowledge this problem but provide no concrete solution beyond the footnote, making the evaluation results potentially misleading.

3. **Inconsistent results in Table 3**: The paper reports inconsistent results where multiCluster (which discards inter-cluster semantics) performs best on extrinsic tasks despite having the lowest scores on intrinsic metrics. This is puzzling and requires explanation, especially since multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster on intrinsic metrics.

4. **Coverage impact unaddressed**: The paper notes coverage differences between methods (e.g., multiCCA vs. multiCluster in Table 2), but fails to analyze how coverage affects the scores. This is particularly problematic for the dependency parsing results where multiCluster has higher coverage but similar scores to multiCCA.

## Questions to Authors
1. What is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gains occur from simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?

2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

3. Are there advantages to multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project between spaces?

4. Dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real lexicons—did the authors evaluate with real multilingual dictionaries?
