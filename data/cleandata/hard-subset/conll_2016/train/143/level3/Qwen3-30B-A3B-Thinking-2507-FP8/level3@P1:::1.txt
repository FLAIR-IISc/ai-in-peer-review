

This paper presents work on multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using dictionary-based methods. The primary contributions are: (1) a new evaluation metric (multiQVECCCA) that shows stronger correlation with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, (2) a web portal for consistent evaluation of multilingual embeddings, and (3) two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for creating embeddings without parallel data. The authors demonstrate that their evaluation method correlates better with document classification (0.896) and dependency parsing (0.273) than existing metrics, and provide a valuable resource for future research.

Strengths: The paper's most significant contribution is the development of multiQVECCCA, which addresses critical theoretical shortcomings in existing evaluation metrics. The authors provide strong empirical evidence showing that their metric correlates better with downstream tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification) than previous methods (max 0.635 for multiQVEC). This represents a substantial advance in how we evaluate multilingual embeddings. Second, the web portal they created is a genuinely valuable resource for the community, providing standardized evaluation metrics and development/test data across multiple languages. This will facilitate more consistent and reproducible research in the field. Third, the paper demonstrates the feasibility of creating multilingual embeddings across 59 languages using only dictionaries and monolingual data, which is a significant practical contribution to the field.

Weaknesses: The paper's novelty claims regarding the estimation methods are unclear. The authors present multiCCA as a novel extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), but fail to clearly distinguish it from Gardner et al.'s translation invariance method (2015), which already proposed a multilingual extension approach. The paper should explicitly clarify the theoretical and practical differences between multiCCA and translation invariance. Second, the use of supersense annotations across multiple languages (English, Italian, Danish) risks a small intersection of features, which could limit the evaluation's effectiveness. The authors mention this limitation in footnote 9 but don't provide a thorough analysis of how it affects their results. Third, the coverage differences between methods (e.g., multiCluster 70.9% vs. multiCCA 69.3% in dependency parsing) likely impact the evaluation scores, yet the authors don't adequately address how these coverage differences affect the results. The paper should analyze how performance changes when restricting evaluation to the intersection of covered words. Fourth, the results in Table 3 are inconsistent and puzzling: multiCluster (which discards inter-cluster semantics) performs best on extrinsic tasks, while translation invariance wins on word translation by a large margin. The authors don't adequately explain this disconnect between intrinsic and extrinsic performance.

Questions to Authors: 
1. In the dependency parsing experiments, what is the performance difference when fixing the embeddings versus allowing them to be trained? What gains occur from simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1, or does it represent a different experimental setup?
3. Are there advantages to multiSkip over first learning bilingual embeddings and then using multiCCA to project between languages, given that multiSkip only uses parallel data?
4. The paper uses Google Translate for dictionary extraction in many cases. Did the authors evaluate their methods using real multilingual dictionaries (e.g., from multilingual thesauri) rather than automatically extracted ones?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its evaluation metric and web portal, but would benefit from clearer novelty claims for its estimation methods, better analysis of coverage effects, and more thorough explanation of the inconsistent results between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The web portal is particularly noteworthy as a community resource that will facilitate future research in multilingual embeddings.
