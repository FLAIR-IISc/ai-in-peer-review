

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the problem of creating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries, without requiring parallel corpora. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and introduce a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that shows better correlation with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics. The paper also provides a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:
1. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, a novel evaluation metric that correlates significantly better with downstream tasks (document classification: 0.896, dependency parsing: 0.273) than existing metrics (e.g., cross-lingual word similarity: 0.386, word translation: 0.066).
2. The creation of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will substantially facilitate future research in this area by providing standardized evaluation tools and datasets.
3. The dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), which enable multilingual embedding learning without parallel data, though their novelty relative to prior work requires clarification.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying and addressing a critical gap in multilingual embedding evaluation. The correlation analysis in Table 1 demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA correlates substantially better with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, which is a major advancement in the field. This is the most important contribution of the paper.

2. The web portal is a valuable resource for the community that will enable standardized, replicable evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The authors provide comprehensive infrastructure including downloadable data, evaluation tasks, and an interface for uploading embeddings, which will substantially lower the barrier to entry for future research.

3. The paper is well-written and clearly describes the methods and experiments. The technical details of multiCCA (projecting all languages into an English anchor space) and multiCluster (using clusters of translationally equivalent words) are well-explained, making the approach accessible to readers.

## Weaknesses
1. The novelty of the proposed estimation methods is unclear relative to prior work. The paper claims to extend Gardner et al. (2015) with a multilingual extension, but fails to clearly articulate what is novel about their approach compared to the translation-invariance method described in Gardner et al. (which was already designed for multilingual embeddings). The paper would benefit from a more precise comparison with prior work to establish the novelty of their methods.

2. The evaluation approach using supersense annotations across multiple languages (English, Italian, Danish) likely suffers from a very small intersection of linguistic features, as noted in footnote 9. The paper does not adequately address how this limited feature set affects the evaluation results, particularly since the authors note that "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages."

3. The evaluation results in Table 3 are inconsistent and difficult to interpret. For example, translation-invariance achieves the highest score on word translation (63.9 vs. 43.7 for multiCluster), but multiCluster achieves the highest scores on extrinsic tasks (document classification: 92.1 vs. 91.1 for translation-invariance). The paper does not provide a compelling explanation for this discrepancy, which undermines confidence in the evaluation framework.

4. Coverage differences between methods significantly impact the evaluation scores (e.g., multiCluster has 70.9% coverage vs. 69.3% for multiCCA in dependency parsing), but the paper does not adequately address how these coverage differences affect the comparison. The supplementary material mentions repeating evaluation on intersecting vocabularies, but the main paper does not discuss this analysis or its implications.

## Questions to Authors
1. In Table 3, multiCluster achieves the highest scores on extrinsic tasks despite having a method that discards semantic context (by forcing all translationally equivalent words to share a single vector). Could the authors elaborate on why this approach works better for downstream tasks than methods that preserve context-specific meaning?

2. The paper states that multiQVEC-CCA achieves the highest correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 for document classification), but Table 3 shows multiCCA has the highest document classification score (92.1). How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction?

3. The paper uses Google Translate to build dictionaries for 47 languages. Did the authors evaluate the impact of using machine-translated dictionaries versus manually curated dictionaries on the quality of the embeddings?

4. The paper claims to train embeddings for 59 languages, but the evaluation in Table 2 only shows results for 3 languages (English, Italian, Danish). How do the authors justify the generalization to 59 languages based on evaluation limited to three languages?
