

This paper makes three key contributions to the field of multilingual word embeddings. First, it proposes multiQVEC-CCA, an improved intrinsic evaluation metric that addresses theoretical shortcomings of QVEC by using canonical correlation analysis instead of dimension-wise correlation. Second, it introduces two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel data. Third, it provides a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will facilitate future research in this area.

The strongest strength of this work is the multiQVEC-CCA metric, which demonstrates significantly better correlation with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics. The authors show Pearson correlations of 0.896 and 0.273 for multiQVEC-CCA with these tasks, compared to 0.635 and 0.444 for multiQVEC and much lower values for cross-lingual word similarity and word translation. This is a substantial improvement over prior work and addresses a critical gap in the evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

Second, the paper's web portal is a highly valuable contribution that will substantially lower the barrier to entry for future research in multilingual embeddings. It provides a standardized way to evaluate embeddings across multiple languages and tasks, with both development and test modes to encourage sound experimentation practices. This is particularly important given the lack of standard evaluation frameworks in the field.

Third, the multiCCA method demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple evaluation metrics. It consistently outperforms multiCluster and other baselines, particularly on the 59-language setup, and achieves the best results in five of nine evaluations. The authors' insight to use English as a reference space for projecting other languages' embeddings is both practical and effective.

The paper's main weaknesses are its lack of depth in analysis and insufficient support for its key claims. The most significant weakness is the insufficient explanation for why multiCluster performs well on 12 languages but poorly on 59 languages, despite the authors noting this discrepancy. The paper states that "the added semantic ambiguity severely hurts the performance of multiCluster with 59 languages," but provides no detailed analysis of this phenomenon or discussion of how to mitigate it.

Second, the paper lacks thorough investigation of the effects of estimation parameters and methodological choices. For example, the paper does not discuss how the cluster size parameter affects multiCluster's performance, or how the threshold τ for dictionary extraction impacts the results. This omission makes it difficult to understand the robustness of their methods.

Third, the claim that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC is not fully supported. The paper presents multiQVEC-CCA's correlation with downstream tasks but does not include monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results (e.g., in Table 1) to directly demonstrate the improvement. Without this comparative evidence, the claim that multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming" remains somewhat unsubstantiated.

The paper also has several minor issues that should be addressed: the phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (l.304) is misleading as the authors actually use specific datasets; there are two equations on p.3 that exceed the column margin; and the citation of Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. for MultiSkip (lines 121/147) is inconsistent with section 2.3, which only mentions Luong et al.

The paper is well written and meticulously documented, and the practical value of the web portal and improved evaluation metric cannot be overstated. However, the authors' claim that developing massively multilingual embeddings will "play an important role in the future of multilingual NLP" is not sufficiently supported by the current results, which show only modest improvements over previous methods in most tasks.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why multiCluster performs so differently on 59 vs. 12 languages, and what design changes might improve its performance at scale?
2. How sensitive are your methods to the cluster size parameter in multiCluster, and how did you determine the optimal values for the 12- and 59-language setups?
3. Could you include monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results in Table 1 to better support your claim that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC?
4. The paper mentions that "supersense annotations exist for other languages" but are inconsistent. How would you recommend standardizing these annotations for broader adoption of your evaluation metric?
