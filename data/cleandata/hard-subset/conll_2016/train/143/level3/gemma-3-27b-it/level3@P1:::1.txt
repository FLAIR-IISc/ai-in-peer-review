This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating word embeddings across 59 languages in a shared space. The authors propose two novel dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, along with improvements to the evaluation metric QVEC, extending it to multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. Finally, they provide a web portal and open-source releases to facilitate further research. The primary contributions, as I see them, are the comprehensive empirical evaluation of multiple multilingual embedding techniques and the development of the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric. The web portal is a valuable resource but less central to the core research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of several multilingual embedding methods, including baselines and the authors’ proposed approaches, across multiple evaluation metrics and downstream tasks. This large-scale evaluation is a significant contribution, offering valuable insights into the performance of different techniques and providing a benchmark for future research. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations strengthens the validity of the findings.
2. **Novel Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses theoretical shortcomings of the original QVEC by introducing CCA, resulting in a rotation- and linear-invariant score. The authors demonstrate that this new metric correlates better with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic methods, suggesting it's a more reliable indicator of embedding quality. This improvement in evaluation methodology is a noteworthy contribution.
3. **Resource Provision (Web Portal & Code):** The authors’ creation of a web portal and public release of code and trained embeddings are commendable. These resources significantly lower the barrier to entry for researchers in this field, enabling reproducibility and further investigation of multilingual embeddings. This promotes open science and accelerates progress in the area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are clearly described, their fundamental novelty relative to existing work, particularly Gardner et al. (2015)'s translation-invariance approach, is not sufficiently emphasized. The paper needs to explicitly highlight what aspects of these methods are truly new and contribute beyond a simple multilingual extension of existing techniques. A clearer differentiation is crucial.
2. **Potential Bias from Supersense Annotation:**  The use of supersense tags for multilingual evaluation, while theoretically sound, introduces a potential bias. The paper acknowledges that the annotations are not perfectly consistent across languages. The impact of this inconsistency, and the small intersection of features it creates, on the evaluation results is not adequately addressed beyond the footnote. A more detailed analysis of this issue, and perhaps exploration of alternative evaluation data, is needed.
3. **Coverage Effects & Inconsistent Results:** The results presented in Table 2 are potentially skewed by variations in vocabulary coverage between the different methods. The paper notes this, but a more in-depth analysis of how coverage impacts the reported scores, particularly in tasks like dependency parsing, is crucial. The inconsistent results in Table 3 – where intrinsic metrics don’t favor multilingual methods and multiCluster outperforms methods utilizing semantic information – are puzzling and require further explanation. The reasons for these discrepancies need to be investigated and discussed.
4. **Unclear Advantage of MultiSkip:** The paper does not clearly demonstrate a significant advantage of the multiSkip method over a more straightforward approach of first learning bilingual embeddings and then using multiCCA to project between languages. A justification for the added complexity of multiSkip is needed, or its limitations should be acknowledged.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dependency Parsing Performance:** What is the performance loss incurred when freezing the embeddings in the dependency parser, and what performance gain is achieved simply by replacing randomly initialized embeddings with the pretrained multilingual embeddings? This would provide context for the impact of the embedding quality on the downstream task.
2. **Table 1 Correlation Analysis:** Is Table 1 presenting correlations averaged across all 17 embeddings evaluated in Section 5.1? Clarification of the statistical basis for these correlation coefficients is necessary.
3. **MultiSkip vs. Bilingual + CCA:** Are there specific scenarios or language pairs where multiSkip demonstrably outperforms the combined approach of learning bilingual embeddings followed by projection using multiCCA?
4. **Real-World Dictionary Evaluation:** Were the methods evaluated using high-quality, curated multilingual dictionaries, or solely relying on dictionaries extracted from parallel corpora and/or machine translation? Using real-world lexicons would provide a more robust assessment of the method's performance.

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and the ideas are clearly presented. The experiments are ambitious and the datasets used are appropriate. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly clarifying the novelty of the proposed methods, analyzing the impact of coverage and annotation quality, and providing a clearer interpretation of the inconsistent results, will significantly strengthen the paper.
