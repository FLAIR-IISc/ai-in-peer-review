This paper introduces new methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and a novel evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, designed to address limitations of existing methods. A key contribution of this work is the development of a publicly available web portal to facilitate research in this area, providing access to data, methods, and evaluation tools.

The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, is the development and release of the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric and the accompanying web portal. While the proposed estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are reasonable extensions of existing techniques, their performance gains are not consistently demonstrated, and the improvements offered are incremental. The multiQVEC-CCA metric, however, provides a theoretically sound and practically useful alternative to existing intrinsic evaluation approaches, and the web portal significantly lowers the barrier to entry for researchers in multilingual embedding evaluation. The meticulous documentation and open-source release of all methods further enhance the value of this work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA represents a clear improvement over existing intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings. The authors convincingly argue that QVEC suffers from limitations related to basis invariance and normalization, and multiQVEC-CCA effectively addresses these shortcomings using Canonical Correlation Analysis.
2. **Practical Utility of the Web Portal:** The development and public release of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a significant contribution. This resource streamlines the evaluation process, provides standardized benchmarks, and facilitates reproducibility, accelerating research in the field. The provision of data and code alongside the portal is particularly commendable.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct experiments on a diverse set of languages and tasks, providing a relatively thorough evaluation of the proposed methods. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, along with analyses of correlation between metrics, adds to the robustness of the findings.
4. **Thorough Documentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and documented. The details regarding implementation, data sources, and experimental settings are clearly described, enabling other researchers to reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvements in Embedding Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are valid approaches, the performance gains achieved compared to baseline methods are not substantial or consistent across all evaluations. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of *why* these methods succeed in some cases but not others.
2. **Inconsistent Results & Lack of Deep Analysis:** The discrepancy in performance of multiCluster between the 59-language and 12-language setups is noteworthy, yet the paper offers limited explanation for this difference. A deeper analysis of the impact of data sparsity and dictionary quality on the performance of multiCluster is warranted.
3. **Limited Investigation of Parameter Sensitivity:** The authors fix several key parameters (e.g., the threshold τ in dictionary creation, cluster size in multiCluster) without a thorough sensitivity analysis. Investigating the impact of these parameters on performance would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Missing Baselines in Correlation Analysis:** Table 1 presents correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, but does not include the correlations for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA. Including these values would provide a more complete picture and better support the claim that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement.
5. **Imprecise Language in Section 3.4:** The phrasing “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” (line 304) is slightly misleading; the datasets are not combined, but rather evaluated separately.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the reasons for the performance difference observed for multiCluster between the 59-language and 12-language settings? Specifically, how does dictionary quality and data sparsity affect the performance of this method?
2. What is the sensitivity of multiCluster and multiCCA to key parameters (e.g., cluster size, the threshold τ for dictionary creation)? Were any parameter tuning experiments performed?
3. Could you include the correlation coefficients for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA in Table 1 to provide a more complete comparison and strengthen the claim that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Equation (1) and (2) on p.3 exceed the column margin.
*   There is a slight inconsistency in the citations for the MultiSkip method (lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., while section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al.). Please clarify.
*   Consider including more related work on multilingual embeddings, clustering methods, and the use of translational context for embedding learning (e.g., Al-Rfou et al., 2013; Haghighi et al., 2008; Täckström et al., 2012).

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. While the proposed embedding methods themselves offer incremental improvements, the novel evaluation metric and publicly available web portal significantly enhance the accessibility and reproducibility of research in this area. The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and presents a rigorous experimental evaluation. Despite the identified weaknesses, the strengths of this work outweigh its limitations, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
