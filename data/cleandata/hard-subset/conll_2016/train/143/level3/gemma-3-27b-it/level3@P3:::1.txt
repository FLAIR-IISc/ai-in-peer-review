This paper introduces four methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance) and a novel evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA). The authors also provide a web portal and open-source releases to facilitate further research.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Multilingual Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The most significant contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric that addresses shortcomings of existing methods (QVEC) by achieving invariance to basis rotations and normalization. The demonstrated correlation with downstream tasks is a strong indicator of its utility.
2. **Extension of Existing Methods to Multilingual Setting:** The paper successfully extends existing bilingual embedding techniques (CCA, skip-gram, translation invariance) to a multilingual context, providing implementations and demonstrating their feasibility across a large number of languages (59).
3. **Resource Provision (Portal and Code):** The authors provide a valuable resource to the community – a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings alongside the code, facilitating reproducible research and benchmark comparisons.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is generally very well-written and easy to follow. The methods are clearly described, and the experimental setup is adequately detailed.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a broad range of experiments, evaluating the proposed methods on several intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, across multiple languages. This provides a strong basis for assessing their performance.
3. **Strong Correlation of multiQVEC-CCA:** The results clearly show that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than existing intrinsic evaluation methods, suggesting its potential as a reliable evaluation tool.
4. **Resource Accessibility:** The public availability of the code and evaluation portal will significantly benefit the research community and accelerate progress in this area.
5. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The paper provides a thorough comparison of the proposed methods with relevant baselines, allowing for a nuanced assessment of their strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty Regarding Translation Invariance:** The paper does not clearly articulate the novelty of its translation invariance implementation compared to Gardner et al. (2015). Simply extending a method to multiple languages is insufficient justification for a contribution, and a detailed explanation of any modifications or improvements is needed. What specific aspects of the multilingual extension are novel?
2. **Supersense Annotation Intersection:** Using supersense annotations across multiple languages introduces the risk of a minimal intersection of features. While footnote 9 acknowledges this, it doesn’t adequately explain how this issue impacts the reliability of the multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA scores. Is the limited overlap a major concern, and are the results potentially biased toward features common across languages?
3. **Coverage Effects:** The impact of vocabulary coverage on the evaluation results (Table 2 and 3) is not sufficiently analyzed. The differing coverage of multiCluster versus multiCCA, despite similar scores, raises concerns about fair comparison. A detailed analysis of how coverage affects the metrics is crucial. Specifically, repeating evaluations on the intersection of vocabularies would provide a more robust comparison.
4. **Inconsistent Results and Puzzling Observations:** Table 3 presents inconsistent results. The fact that multiCluster, which explicitly discards inter-cluster semantics, performs best on extrinsic tasks is counterintuitive and requires further explanation. Furthermore, the dominance of translation invariance on word translation tasks, contrasted with the relatively weaker performance on other metrics, needs to be discussed. The lack of consistent preference for multilingual techniques on intrinsic metrics is also concerning.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dependency Parser Performance:** What is the performance drop when fixing the embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gain in performance is achieved by simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings? This would provide a more direct assessment of the value of the pretraining.
2. **Table 1 Averaging:** Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1? If so, please clarify how the averaging was performed.
3. **multiSkip vs. Bilingual + multiCCA:** Are there advantages to using multiSkip over first learning bilingual embeddings and then using multiCCA to project between spaces? What are the trade-offs in terms of performance and computational cost?
4. **Dictionary Quality:** The authors use dictionaries extracted via parallel alignments or Google Translate. Did they evaluate the performance of the methods with real, manually curated multilingual dictionaries to assess the impact of dictionary quality on the results?
