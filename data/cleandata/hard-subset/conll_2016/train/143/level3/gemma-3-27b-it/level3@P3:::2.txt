This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods, MultiCluster and MultiCCA, for estimating multilingual word embeddings, alongside a new evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, and a web portal for evaluating embeddings. The primary contributions, as I see them, are the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric and the accompanying web portal, which provide a valuable resource for the multilingual NLP community. The proposed embedding methods themselves are less novel and suffer from a lack of in-depth analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Evaluation Resource (Primary):** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the associated web portal are significant contributions. The authors identify shortcomings in existing evaluation methods (QVEC’s basis dependence and unnormalized scores) and propose a sound improvement (CCA).  The portal facilitates reproducible research and provides a platform for easy comparison of different multilingual embedding methods. The availability of data and benchmarks further enhances its utility.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper demonstrates a broad experimental evaluation across 59 languages and multiple tasks (document classification, dependency parsing). This wide-ranging assessment is commendable and provides a good overview of the methods’ performance in diverse settings.
3. **Well-Written and Documented:** The paper is clearly written and meticulously documented. The descriptions of the methods are easy to follow, and the experimental setup is explained in detail. This clarity facilitates understanding and reproducibility.
4. **Identified Limitations of Existing Evaluation Metrics:** The authors correctly identify the need for better evaluation metrics that correlate well with downstream tasks and provide a principled approach to addressing the flaws of QVEC.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Breadth Over Depth (Primary):** The paper prioritizes covering a large number of languages and methods over a deep analysis of any single aspect. Results are presented descriptively without sufficient statistical significance testing or detailed error analysis. The lack of thorough investigation hinders understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed methods.
2. **Inconsistent and Underexplored Results:** The conflicting results between the 59- and 12-language setups for MultiCluster are not adequately addressed. Why does MultiCluster perform better with fewer languages? A deeper investigation into the impact of dictionary quality and cluster size across different language groups is needed.
3. **Lack of Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The effect of key estimation parameters (e.g., the threshold τ for dictionary creation in MultiCluster, dimensionality of embeddings) and methodological decisions (e.g., the choice of monolingual embedding model) are not systematically investigated. This limits the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Insufficient Justification for Improvement:** While multiQVEC-CCA is claimed to be an improvement over QVEC, the paper lacks monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results (e.g., included as a comparison in Table 1) to directly support this claim. Demonstrating improvement on monolingual data would strengthen the argument before moving to multilingual evaluation.
5. **Misleading Phrasing:** The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (l.304) is misleading. It doesn’t convey whether these are aggregated into a single dataset or used separately.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the conflicting MultiCluster results between the 59- and 12-language setups? What factors contribute to the performance difference?
2.  What is the sensitivity of MultiCluster and MultiCCA to the dictionary creation threshold (τ) and other key estimation parameters?  Did you perform any parameter tuning?
3.  Could you include monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlation results (e.g., in a modified Table 1) to directly demonstrate the improvement offered by QVEC-CCA?
4.  In section 2.3, the correspondence between the citations for MultiSkip (lines 121/147) and the description in the text is unclear.  Please clarify which work each citation refers to, and explain any differences.
5.  Could you comment on the computational resources needed to run your experiments, and the time required for training the different embedding methods, especially for the 59-language setup?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The formatting issue with equations exceeding the column margin on page 3 should be addressed.
*  The related work section could be expanded to include more recent and relevant papers on multilingual embeddings (e.g., Artetxe et al., 2019; Conneau et al., 2018), clustering (e.g., Ng, Jordan, & Weiss, 2002), and translational context (e.g., Lample & Conneau, 2019).
