This paper introduces new methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings, focusing on scenarios with limited parallel data. The core contributions lie in the proposal of two dictionary-based embedding estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, a novel evaluation metric – multiQVEC-CCA – building upon existing work with QVEC, and the development of a publicly available web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

The authors successfully demonstrate the feasibility of estimating embeddings for 59 languages using only bilingual dictionaries and monolingual data, a significant undertaking. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, addressing limitations in the original QVEC metric by employing Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) for a more robust evaluation, is a notable improvement.  Furthermore, the web portal represents a valuable resource for the research community, providing a platform for standardized evaluation and dissemination of multilingual embedding techniques. The meticulous documentation and availability of the code and data further enhance the practical value of this work.

However, while the paper is broad in scope and technically sound, it suffers from a lack of depth in analysis. The strengths of this submission lie in its comprehensive experimentation and the practical tools it provides, rather than groundbreaking theoretical advancements.

Several weaknesses deserve attention.  Firstly, the results presented in Table 3, comparing the four estimation methods, exhibit inconsistencies across different evaluation metrics. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of *why* these inconsistencies exist is needed. The performance of multiCluster, particularly the contrast between the 59-language and 12-language setups, warrants further investigation. What factors contribute to the degradation in performance when scaling to a larger number of languages? A deeper analysis of the effect of cluster size, dictionary quality, and the influence of low-resource languages would be valuable.

Secondly, the paper lacks a thorough examination of the impact of hyperparameter choices and methodological decisions within multiCluster and multiCCA. For instance, the fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary construction is not justified beyond "manual inspection."  A sensitivity analysis exploring the effects of varying this threshold, or different dictionary construction methods, would strengthen the findings. Similarly, the choice of dimensionality (512) for the embeddings is presented without justification.  

Thirdly, the claim that multiQVEC-CCA outperforms QVEC is not fully substantiated.  Table 1 presents correlations with downstream tasks, but it would be more compelling to see the corresponding correlations for the monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA metrics themselves. Including these baseline correlations would provide a more direct comparison and support the assertion of improvement.

Additionally, minor issues detract from the overall polish of the paper. The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (line 304) is misleading; these datasets are individual, not combined. Two equations on page 3 exceed the column margin and should be reformatted. A citation inconsistency exists in lines 121/147 regarding MultiSkip; the connection between Luong et al. and Coulmance et al./Guo et al. needs clarification. Finally, the related work section could benefit from expanded coverage of existing literature on multilingual embeddings, clustering approaches, and the utilization of translational context (e.g., Al-Rfou et al., 2013; Hermann & Blunsom, 2014; Täckström et al., 2012).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed analysis of the performance differences between multiCluster in the 59-language and 12-language setups? What specific factors contribute to the degradation in performance as the number of languages increases?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing a fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary construction? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis to evaluate the impact of different threshold values on the overall performance?
3.  Please provide the correlation results for the monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA metrics, comparable to those presented for the downstream tasks in Table 1.
4.  Could you clarify the relationship between the attribution of MultiSkip to Luong et al. in section 2.3 and its prior citation to Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. in lines 121 and 147?



In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual NLP through its practical tools and comprehensive evaluation. However, the lack of in-depth analysis and justification for certain design choices limits its scientific impact. While the paper doesn’t represent a major theoretical breakthrough, the publicly available resources and improved evaluation metric make it a worthwhile addition to the literature.
