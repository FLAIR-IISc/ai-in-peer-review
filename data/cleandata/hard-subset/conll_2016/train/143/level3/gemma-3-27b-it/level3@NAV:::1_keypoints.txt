- Paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, Translation Invariance) and a refined evaluation metric, multiQVECCCA, with an accompanying web portal and open-source code.  
- Primary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation framework (multiQVECCCA) and public resources that enable systematic comparison across languages and tasks.  
- Secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics.  
- Methods themselves largely build on existing work; novelty—especially for the Translation Invariance extension—is not clearly established.  
- Use of supersense annotations across languages is risky due to potential semantic divergence and limited feature overlap; the paper notes this but lacks deeper impact analysis.  
- Vocabulary coverage differences between methods (Tables 2 & 3) likely bias reported scores and require coverage-adjusted comparisons.  
- Inconsistent results (Table 3): multiCluster’s strong extrinsic performance despite discarding inter-cluster semantics is unexpected and needs explanation.  
- Authors should explicitly differentiate their Translation Invariance extension from Gardner et al. (2015) and demonstrate improvements.  
- A more detailed analysis is needed on how cross-lingual supersense divergence affects evaluation outcomes.  
- Recommend performing coverage-adjusted analyses to ensure fair method comparisons.  
- Strength: multiQVECCCA metric and web portal provide valuable community resources for evaluation and reproducibility.  
- Strength: systematic comparison across many languages and tasks adds empirical breadth.  
- Strength: public release of code and data supports reproducibility and further research.  
- Weakness: lack of novelty justification for proposed methods, particularly Translation Invariance.  
- Question: For dependency parser experiments, what is the performance drop when embeddings are fixed vs. trained, and what gains come from replacing random embeddings with pretrained ones?  
- Question: Is Table 1 averaged over the 17 embeddings in Section 5.1 or are results reported per embedding?  
- Question: What advantages does multiSkip have over learning bilingual embeddings first (e.g., multiCCA) and then projecting between spaces?  
- Question: Did the authors evaluate using manually curated, high-quality multilingual dictionaries instead of parallel alignments or Google Translate, which may not reflect real-world lexicons?
