This paper introduces new methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and extend the QVEC evaluation method to a multilingual setting, creating multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. They also provide a web portal to facilitate evaluation and release their code and data.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the systematic evaluation of multilingual embeddings and the development of improved evaluation metrics. While the estimation methods themselves build upon existing techniques, the comprehensive evaluation framework – encompassing intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, and the novel multiQVEC-CCA metric – provides a valuable resource for the community. A second contribution is the creation and public release of the evaluation portal, which lowers the barrier to entry for researchers in this field. Finally, the large-scale experiments training embeddings for 59 languages, even if the methods don't uniformly outperform baselines, provides a significant benchmark dataset and insights into multilingual embedding challenges.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The most significant strength of this work is the robust evaluation strategy. The combination of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, alongside the careful consideration of evaluation metric limitations, demonstrates a thorough and thoughtful approach. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, addressing shortcomings of the original QVEC, is particularly noteworthy. The correlation analysis presented in Table 1 provides valuable evidence supporting the superiority of the proposed evaluation metrics over prior work.
2. **Large-Scale Experiments & Resource Creation:** Training embeddings for 59 languages is a substantial undertaking. The authors’ willingness to share the trained embeddings, code, and evaluation portal significantly benefits the research community. This contributes to reproducibility and facilitates future advancements. The careful documentation of data sources and experimental setup further enhances this contribution.
3. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. The technical details of the methods are explained adequately, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The inclusion of related work provides helpful context and situates the contribution within the existing literature.
4. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Methods:** The authors explicitly acknowledge the limitations of existing intrinsic evaluation metrics, particularly regarding coverage and the impact of vocabulary differences. The discussion in Section 3 and the attempt to mitigate these issues demonstrate a critical awareness of the challenges in evaluating multilingual embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Estimation Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are well-explained, their novelty is incremental. The extension of existing methods, particularly CCA, does not represent a significant breakthrough. The connection to, and distinction from, Gardner et al.'s translation invariance is insufficiently discussed. A more detailed explanation of what fundamentally differentiates these methods is required.
2. **Potential Bias in Supersense Tag Evaluation:** The use of supersense tags across multiple languages raises concerns about feature intersection and potentially skewed results. The authors acknowledge this in footnote 9, but the mitigation strategies are not sufficiently elaborated. The impact of limited feature overlap on the evaluation results needs further investigation.
3. **Inconsistent and Puzzling Experimental Results:** The results presented in Table 3 are difficult to interpret. The fact that intrinsic metrics often *do not* favor the multilingual techniques, and that translation invariance dominates word translation, is counterintuitive. The superior performance of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks, despite its loss of inter-cluster semantic information, is also puzzling. A deeper analysis of these discrepancies is crucial. The discussion of coverage effects is insufficient.
4. **Coverage and its Impact on Evaluation:** The impact of coverage on evaluation scores is a critical issue, especially given the differing coverage rates observed in the experiments. The authors mention analyzing results with vocabulary intersection, but the results of this analysis are not presented, and the discussion lacks depth. A more thorough investigation of coverage effects is required to draw meaningful conclusions from the experimental results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the performance degradation when fixing the embeddings in the dependency parser, and what performance gains are observed simply by replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings within the LSTM parser architecture? This would help establish the value added by the embeddings themselves.
2. Is Table 1 based on an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1? Clarification on the statistical significance of the correlations would be helpful.
3.  Are there demonstrable advantages of using multiSkip over first learning bilingual embeddings and then utilizing multiCCA to project between those spaces? What specific scenarios would favor multiSkip?
4.  The dictionary extraction process employed (parallel alignment or Google Translate) may introduce noise. Did the authors conduct evaluations using genuinely curated, high-quality multilingual dictionaries to assess the robustness of their methods?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of each estimation method.
*   A more extensive error analysis of the extrinsic tasks could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the different embedding techniques.
*   Consider providing visual representations of the learned embedding spaces to aid understanding.
