## Review of “Estimating and Evaluating Multilingual Embeddings”

This paper presents four methods for creating multilingual word embeddings – multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance – and introduces a refined evaluation approach, multiQVECCCA, built upon QVEC. The authors also provide a web portal and open-source code to facilitate further research. The paper is generally well-written and clearly describes the proposed methods and evaluation procedures. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the comprehensive evaluation framework (multiQVECCCA and the associated web portal) and the systematic comparison of several multilingual embedding techniques. While the proposed embedding methods themselves build upon existing work, the thorough evaluation across multiple languages and tasks, alongside the publicly available resources, offers a valuable contribution to the field. A secondary contribution is the empirical investigation of the correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the novelty of the proposed methods, particularly concerning Translation Invariance, is not sufficiently established. The authors need to explicitly delineate how their multilingual extension differs from, and improves upon, the original work by Gardner et al. (2015). Secondly, using supersense annotations across multiple languages is risky due to potential semantic divergence and limited feature overlap. While footnote 9 acknowledges this, the paper lacks a more detailed analysis of the impact of this issue on the evaluation results. Thirdly, the differing vocabulary coverage between methods (as indicated in Table 2 & 3) likely influences the reported scores, and a coverage-adjusted analysis is crucial for fair comparison. Finally, the inconsistent results in Table 3 are perplexing. The superior performance of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks, despite discarding inter-cluster semantics, contradicts the expectation that methods capturing more cross-lingual information should excel. This discrepancy demands further explanation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The multiQVECCCA metric and the web portal provide a valuable resource for the community.
*   **Systematic Comparison:** The paper compares multiple approaches across numerous languages and tasks.
*   **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of code and data promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty Justification:** The paper doesn't clearly articulate the advancements over existing work, especially regarding Translation Invariance.
*   **Supersense Annotation Issues:** Limited feature intersection across languages using supersense tags raises concerns about evaluation validity.
*   **Coverage Bias:** Vocabulary coverage differences likely impact reported scores and require further analysis.
*   **Inconsistent Results:** The unexpected performance of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks needs thorough explanation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the dependency parser experiments, what is the performance drop when fixing the embeddings versus allowing them to be trained, and what gains are achieved by replacing random embeddings with the pretrained embeddings?
2.  Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1, or are the results reported for each embedding individually?
3.  What are the advantages of multiSkip over first learning bilingual embeddings (e.g., via multiCCA) and then projecting between spaces?
4.  The dictionary extraction process using parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect accurate real-world lexicons. Did the authors evaluate performance with manually curated, high-quality multilingual dictionaries?
