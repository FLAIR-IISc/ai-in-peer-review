- Paper proposes dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation (multiCluster, multiCCA), a multilingual evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluation.
- Main contribution 1: two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) using monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries.
- Main contribution 2: multiQVEC-CCA, an extension of QVEC for multilingual evaluation.
- Main contribution 3: a web portal offering intrinsic and extrinsic metrics for evaluating multilingual embeddings.
- Strength: novel estimation methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) shown to be effective.
- Strength: improved multilingual evaluation via multiQVEC-CCA compared to prior methods.
- Strength: web portal facilitates evaluation and future research.
- Weakness: unclear novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach.
- Weakness: use of supersense annotations across languages may cause only tiny feature intersections and lacks explanation of mitigation.
- Weakness: no analysis of how coverage affects scores reported in Table 2.
- Weakness: inconsistent results in Table 3 not explained, with intrinsic metrics often not favoring multilingual techniques.
- Question: what is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gains arise from replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
- Question: is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
- Question: are there advantages of multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project between spaces?
- Question: did the authors evaluate using real multilingual dictionaries rather than dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate?
