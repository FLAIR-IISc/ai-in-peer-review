This paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods and evaluates them using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The proposed methods include multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance. The authors also introduce a new evaluation method, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses some of the limitations of the existing QVEC method.

The paper is well-written and clearly describes the proposed methods and evaluation metrics. However, there are some major issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, the novelty of the proposed methods, particularly multiCluster and multiCCA, is not clearly explained. The authors should provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, such as Gardner et al.'s translation invariance method, to highlight the contributions of their work.

Secondly, the use of supersense annotations across multiple languages may lead to a small intersection of features, which could affect the performance of the evaluation metrics. The authors should provide more details on how they addressed this issue, beyond the footnote 9.

Thirdly, the coverage of the evaluation metrics may affect the scores in Table 2. For example, the dependency parsing task shows different coverage for multiCluster and multiCCA, despite similar scores. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of coverage on the evaluation metrics.

Lastly, the results in Table 3 are inconsistent, with multiCluster performing best on extrinsic tasks, despite discarding inter-cluster semantics. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation for these results and discuss the implications for future research.

In terms of contributions, the paper makes several significant contributions. Firstly, the proposed methods, particularly multiCCA, show promising results on multilingual embedding tasks. Secondly, the introduction of the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric provides a more robust way of evaluating multilingual embeddings. Finally, the development of the web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings provides a valuable resource for the research community.

However, the paper also has some limitations. Firstly, the evaluation metrics are not comprehensive, and the authors should consider including more metrics to provide a more complete picture of the performance of the proposed methods. Secondly, the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of the proposed methods on downstream tasks, such as machine translation and cross-lingual information retrieval.

In conclusion, this paper makes significant contributions to the field of multilingual embeddings, but there are some major issues that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more details on the novelty of the proposed methods, address the issue of supersense annotations, and provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of coverage on the evaluation metrics.

Questions to Authors:

* What is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gains occur from simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
* Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
* Are there advantages to multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project between spaces?
* Did the authors evaluate with real multilingual dictionaries, rather than relying on dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate?
