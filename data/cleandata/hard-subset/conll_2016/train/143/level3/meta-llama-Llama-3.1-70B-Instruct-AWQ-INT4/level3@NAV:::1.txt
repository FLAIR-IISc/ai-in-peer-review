**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance. The authors also introduce a new evaluation method, multiQVECCCA, which extends QVEC to multilingual evaluation. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods using various intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, including word similarity, word translation, and downstream tasks such as document classification and dependency parsing. The results show that the proposed methods outperform existing methods in some settings, and the new evaluation metric, multiQVECCCA, correlates better with downstream tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel multilingual embedding methods**: The paper proposes two new dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which can estimate multilingual embeddings without requiring parallel data.
2. **Improved evaluation metric**: The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, multiQVECCCA, which extends QVEC to multilingual evaluation and addresses some of its limitations.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods using various intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, including word similarity, word translation, and downstream tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written and clearly described**: The paper is well-written and clearly describes the proposed methods and evaluation metrics.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods using various intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
3. **Improved evaluation metric**: The paper introduces a new evaluation metric, multiQVECCCA, which extends QVEC to multilingual evaluation and addresses some of its limitations.
4. **Novel multilingual embedding methods**: The paper proposes two new dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which can estimate multilingual embeddings without requiring parallel data.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Unclear novelty relative to Gardner et al.'s translation invariance**: The paper does not clearly explain the novelty of the proposed methods relative to Gardner et al.'s translation invariance method.
2. **Using supersense annotations across multiple languages risks a tiny intersection of features**: The paper does not fully address the issue of using supersense annotations across multiple languages, which may result in a tiny intersection of features.
3. **Coverage likely affects scores in Table 2**: The paper does not fully address the issue of coverage affecting scores in Table 2, which may impact the comparison of methods.
4. **Inconsistent results in Table 3**: The paper presents inconsistent results in Table 3, which may be puzzling and require further explanation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. What is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gains occur from simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
3. Are there advantages to multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project between spaces?
4. Dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real lexicons — did the authors evaluate with real multilingual dictionaries?
