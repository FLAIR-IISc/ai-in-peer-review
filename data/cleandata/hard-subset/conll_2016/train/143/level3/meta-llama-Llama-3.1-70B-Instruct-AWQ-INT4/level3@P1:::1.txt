**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes new methods for estimating and evaluating word embeddings in multiple languages in a shared embedding space. The authors introduce two dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which use monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries to estimate multilingual embeddings. They also propose a new evaluation method, multiQVEC-CCA, which extends the QVEC method to multilingual evaluations. The paper also describes a web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Dictionary-based estimation methods**: The authors propose two new methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating multilingual embeddings using monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries.
2. **MultiQVEC-CCA evaluation method**: The authors propose a new evaluation method, multiQVEC-CCA, which extends the QVEC method to multilingual evaluations.
3. **Web portal for evaluation**: The authors describe a web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel estimation methods**: The authors propose two new dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which are shown to be effective in estimating multilingual embeddings.
2. **Improved evaluation method**: The authors propose a new evaluation method, multiQVEC-CCA, which is shown to be more effective than previous methods in evaluating multilingual embeddings.
3. **Web portal for evaluation**: The authors describe a web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings, which can facilitate future research in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Unclear novelty relative to Gardner et al.'s translation invariance**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation of what is new in their approach compared to Gardner et al.'s translation invariance method.
2. **Using supersense annotations across multiple languages**: The authors use supersense annotations across multiple languages, which may risk a tiny intersection of features. They do not provide a clear explanation of how this is addressed.
3. **Coverage impact**: The authors do not provide a clear analysis of how coverage affects scores in Table 2.
4. **Inconsistent results in Table 3**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation for the inconsistent results in Table 3, where intrinsic metrics often do not favor multilingual techniques.

**Questions to Authors**

1. What is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gains occur from simply replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
3. Are there advantages to multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project between spaces?
4. Dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real lexicons — did the authors evaluate with real multilingual dictionaries?
