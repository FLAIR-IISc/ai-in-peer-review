### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces new methods for creating and evaluating word embeddings that share a common vector space across a large number of languages (up to 59). The authors propose two methods that do not require parallel corpora, relying instead on monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries: multiCluster, which groups translation equivalents into clusters before training, and multiCCA, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis to project monolingual embeddings into a shared English space. The paper also introduces an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the prior QVEC metric and is shown to correlate better with downstream task performance. Finally, the authors present a web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **An improved intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and an accompanying public evaluation portal.** The demonstration that multiQVEC-CCA has a stronger correlation with downstream task performance than previous metrics is a significant finding. The web portal is a valuable service to the research community that will lower the barrier to entry and encourage standardized, reproducible evaluation.
2.  **Two dictionary-based methods for creating massively multilingual embeddings.** The paper demonstrates that simple methods like multiCluster and multiCCA, which do not rely on scarce parallel data, can be used to generate embeddings for dozens of languages and can be competitive with, or even outperform, methods that require parallel corpora.
3.  **A large-scale empirical study of multilingual embeddings.** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across nine different evaluation tasks (both intrinsic and extrinsic) and two language sets (12 and 59 languages), providing a broad benchmark for future work.

### Strengths

1.  **High practical value and contribution to the community.** The strongest aspect of this paper is its direct contribution of resources to the NLP community. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric, backed by evidence of stronger correlation with extrinsic tasks (Table 1 shows a correlation of 0.896 with document classification), is a clear improvement for intrinsic evaluation. The development of an open-source web portal for standardized evaluation is an excellent initiative that will facilitate progress and reproducibility in this area.
2.  **Thorough empirical evaluation.** The authors evaluate their methods on an impressively broad suite of tasks, including document classification, dependency parsing, word similarity, and word translation. The scale of the experiments, particularly the creation of embeddings for 59 languages, is commendable. The meta-evaluation in Section 5.1, which assesses the quality of the intrinsic metrics themselves, is a rigorous and insightful piece of analysis that strengthens the paper's claims.
3.  **Well-motivated and accessible methods.** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods are simple, intuitive, and address a key resource bottleneck by not requiring parallel corpora. This makes them applicable to a much wider range of languages than many existing techniques. The paper shows that these dictionary-based methods can achieve strong performance, outperforming baselines on several tasks, including both extrinsic evaluations in the 12-language setting (Table 3).
4.  **Clarity and organization.** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methods, experimental setup, and results are all meticulously documented, which makes the work easy to understand and build upon.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of depth in analysis and discussion.** The paper's main weakness is its preference for breadth over depth. It presents a large number of results across many tasks and methods but provides very little analysis or insight into *why* the results are the way they are. For example, the relative performance of the four methods varies significantly across tasks in Table 3, but the authors offer no discussion of what properties of the embeddings might lead to these differences. This leaves the reader with a set of benchmarks but limited new scientific understanding.
2.  **Inadequate explanation of key results for multiCluster.** The performance of the multiCluster method drops dramatically when moving from 12 languages to 59 languages (compare its strong results in Table 3 to its poor results in Table 2). The paper's only explanation is a brief note about "added semantic ambiguity," which is vague and unsupported by any analysis. This is a crucial result that warrants a much deeper investigation, as it has significant implications for the scalability of the method.
3.  **Unexplored sensitivity to parameters and design choices.** The proposed methods have several important parameters and design choices that are not explored. For multiCluster, the effect of the maximum cluster size is not analyzed. For multiCCA, English is chosen as the pivot language without justification. How would the results change if a different (e.g., morphologically richer) language were used as the pivot? This lack of sensitivity analysis makes it difficult to understand the robustness of the methods and the generality of the results.
4.  **Incomplete empirical support for QVEC-CCA's improvement.** The paper claims QVEC-CCA improves on QVEC by being basis-invariant and normalized. While theoretically sound, the empirical evidence in Table 1 only compares the multilingual versions (multiQVEC vs. multiQVEC-CCA). To make the claim of general improvement more convincing, a direct comparison of the correlation scores for the original monolingual QVEC vs. the new monolingual QVEC-CCA should also be presented.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance of multiCluster is strong in the 12-language setup but very poor in the 59-language setup. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this performance drop? For instance, is the degradation graceful as more languages are added, or is there a tipping point? Does collapsing words from different language families into single clusters contribute disproportionately to the ambiguity problem?
2.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your methods to key design choices? Specifically, how does the maximum cluster size affect multiCluster's performance, and what was the rationale for choosing English as the pivot language for multiCCA? Have you experimented with other pivot languages?
3.  Table 1 provides strong evidence for multiQVEC-CCA. To fully substantiate the claim that QVEC-CCA is a superior formulation to QVEC in general, could you also provide the correlation results for the monolingual QVEC and monolingual QVEC-CCA metrics in that table?

### Additional Comments

*   The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (line 304) is slightly misleading, as it seems to refer to a single aggregated dataset (RG65 across six language pairs). Please clarify.
*   The objective function for Translation-invariance and the equation for QVEC on page 3 exceed the column margins, which should be fixed.
*   The relationship between multiSkip and the cited works is confusing. Lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016), but Section 2.3 only describes the model of Luong et al. (2015b). Please clarify the correspondences.
*   The related work section could be strengthened by including more discussion on multilingual clustering (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012) and methods that leverage translational context from parallel data without explicit alignment (e.g., Gouws et al., 2014; Coulmance et al., 2015).
