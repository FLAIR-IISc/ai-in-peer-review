### Review

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for creating massively multilingual word embeddings in a shared space: MultiCluster, a clustering-based approach, and MultiCCA, which extends canonical correlation analysis. The authors also propose an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, QVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the original QVEC and is shown to correlate better with downstream task performance. The methods are evaluated across a wide range of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1.  **An improved evaluation metric (QVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal.** The new metric and its correlation analysis are valuable tools for the community, and the web portal significantly enhances reproducibility and lowers the barrier for future research.
2.  **Two novel dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods.** The proposed MultiCluster and MultiCCA methods provide alternatives to parallel-data-dependent approaches, demonstrating competitive performance in several, though not all, evaluation settings.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Practical Value and Contribution to the Community:** The paper's strongest contribution is its focus on evaluation. The development of QVEC-CCA, which shows stronger correlation with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics (Table 1), is a solid step forward. The creation of a public web portal for standardized evaluation is an excellent resource that will facilitate reproducible research in multilingual embeddings.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Scope:** The authors conduct an extensive evaluation, testing their methods on up to 59 languages across nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This breadth provides a wide-ranging, if not always deep, view of the methods' performance characteristics.
3.  **Clarity and Documentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the proposed methods, baselines, and evaluation setup. The meticulous documentation and commitment to releasing data and code are commendable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Depth of Analysis:** The paper’s main weakness is its preference for breadth over depth. It presents a large number of results but offers little discussion or insight into *why* certain methods perform better in specific contexts. This is most evident in the conflicting results for MultiCluster, which is competitive in the 12-language setup but performs poorly in the 59-language one. The paper notes the issue of semantic ambiguity but does not analyze it further, leaving the reader with observations rather than understanding.
2.  **Lack of Ablation and Parameter Investigation:** The paper does not explore the impact of key methodological decisions. For instance, the effect of the maximum cluster size in MultiCluster or the choice of a pivot language (English) in MultiCCA is not investigated. This omission makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the methods and understand their underlying properties.
3.  **Missing Evidence and Minor Issues:** The claim that QVEC-CCA improves on QVEC is not fully substantiated without showing the baseline monolingual QVEC correlation results in Table 1. Additionally, several minor but distracting issues are present, including inconsistent citations for MultiSkip (Section 2.3 vs. Lines 121/147), formatting errors (equations on p.3), and potentially misleading phrasing (l.304).

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a deeper analysis of the conflicting performance of MultiCluster between the 12- and 59-language settings? Is this performance drop solely due to the semantic ambiguity issue you mention, and did you experiment with mitigating it (e.g., by varying the cluster size thresholds)?
2.  To better understand the properties of your proposed methods, could you comment on the sensitivity of MultiCluster and MultiCCA to their key parameters and design choices, such as the cluster size limit and the selection of a pivot language?
