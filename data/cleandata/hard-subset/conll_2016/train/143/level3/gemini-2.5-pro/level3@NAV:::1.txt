**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents and evaluates four methods for creating multilingual word embeddings, including two dictionary-based approaches (`multiCluster` and `multiCCA`) and two that leverage parallel data. A primary contribution is the introduction of a new intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which is an extension of QVEC designed to be invariant to linear transformations and is shown to correlate better with downstream tasks than previous metrics. The authors use these methods to train embeddings in up to 59 languages and introduce a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation for future research.

**Contributions**
1.  The development and validation of `multiQVEC-CCA`, a new rotation- and linear-invariant intrinsic evaluation metric, and the empirical analysis showing its improved correlation with extrinsic task performance.
2.  A comprehensive empirical comparison of dictionary-based and parallel corpus-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings across a wide range of tasks, languages, and metrics.
3.  The introduction of two practical, dictionary-based embedding methods (`multiCluster`, `multiCCA`) that do not require parallel corpora.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper is very well-written, and the proposed methods and evaluation framework are described with excellent clarity.
2.  The rigorous focus on evaluation methodology is a major strength. Proposing an improved intrinsic metric and validating it against downstream tasks addresses a critical need in the community.
3.  The experiments are extensive, providing a valuable comparative analysis of four distinct embedding techniques on both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.
4.  The release of an open evaluation portal is a commendable contribution that will lower the barrier for future research and improve reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The novelty of the "Translation Invariance" method is not clearly distinguished from Gardner et al. (2015). The paper should explicitly state its specific contribution, such as the extension to a fully multilingual setting.
2.  The results presented in Table 3 are inconsistent and counter-intuitive. It is particularly puzzling that `multiCluster`, a method that discards fine-grained semantics by collapsing words into clusters, outperforms other models on extrinsic tasks. This surprising result requires a much deeper analysis and explanation.
3.  The experimental analysis fails to properly disentangle the impact of vocabulary coverage from model performance, which is a significant confounding factor when comparing scores in Table 3.
4.  The `multiQVEC-CCA` metric's reliance on supersense annotations raises scalability concerns. The paper does not adequately address how it handles the potentially tiny intersection of linguistic features across many languages.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  For the dependency parsing experiments, what is the performance gain when using your pretrained embeddings compared to a randomly initialized embedding layer? What is the performance drop from keeping the embeddings fixed versus fine-tuning them?
2.  Are the correlation scores reported in Table 1 an average over all 17 embedding models mentioned in Section 5.1?
3.  Are there theoretical or empirical advantages to the end-to-end `multiSkip` approach over a two-stage pipeline of learning separate bilingual embeddings and then projecting them into a common space using `multi-CCA`?
4.  Since your dictionaries were either extracted or machine-translated, have you evaluated your methods with professionally curated multilingual lexicons to assess the impact of dictionary quality on performance?
