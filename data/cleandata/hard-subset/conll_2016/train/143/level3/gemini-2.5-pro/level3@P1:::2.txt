This paper presents a comprehensive study on creating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across a large number of languages (up to 59). The authors propose two new methods for estimating embeddings that rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries: multiCluster, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters before training embeddings, and multiCCA, which learns projections from monolingual embedding spaces into a shared space (pivoting on English). Furthermore, the paper introduces an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the prior QVEC method (namely, lack of rotational invariance and an unnormalized score) by employing Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). The authors conduct extensive experiments, comparing their proposed methods against two baselines on a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. A key contribution is the release of an open-source web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, along with all associated code and data.

The main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:
1.  **A new, principled intrinsic evaluation metric and a thorough correlation analysis.** The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses significant theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC method. The experimental validation in Table 1, showing that multiQVEC-CCA correlates more strongly with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics like word translation and cross-lingual similarity, is a valuable finding for the community.
2.  **The creation and release of a public evaluation portal and associated resources.** The web portal, along with the standardized datasets and trained embeddings for 59 languages, represents a significant practical contribution that will facilitate reproducible and comparable research in multilingual embeddings.
3.  **Two novel dictionary-based methods for creating massively multilingual embeddings.** The multiCluster and multiCCA methods offer simple and effective ways to create shared embeddings without requiring parallel corpora. While the underlying ideas (clustering, CCA) are not entirely new, their application and evaluation at this scale are noteworthy.

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable Community Resources and Commitment to Reproducibility.** The most significant strength of this paper is its contribution of tangible resources to the research community. The development of a web portal for standardized evaluation is an excellent initiative that lowers the barrier for entry and helps unify evaluation practices. Releasing the code, data, and 59-language embeddings further enhances the paper's impact and facilitates future work.
2.  **A Methodologically Sound Improvement in Evaluation.** The paper clearly identifies two major weaknesses in the QVEC metric (non-invariance to basis rotation, unnormalized score) and proposes a sensible solution with QVEC-CCA. The empirical evidence presented in Table 1, which demonstrates that the proposed metric has a much higher correlation (0.896) with document classification than prior methods, provides strong support for its adoption. This is a solid and well-argued contribution.
3.  **Impressive Experimental Breadth and Scale.** The authors evaluate four different methods on nine distinct tasks across two large language sets (12 and 59 languages). This broad experimental setup provides a comprehensive, if not always conclusive, picture of the performance landscape. The successful training of embeddings for 59 languages demonstrates the scalability of the proposed dictionary-based approaches.
4.  **Clarity and Organization.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methods are described clearly, the experimental setup is detailed, and the results are presented in an organized manner. This makes the work accessible and its findings easy to interpret.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of In-depth Analysis and Discussion.** The paper's main weakness is that it favors breadth over depth. While a massive number of experiments are presented, the discussion of the results is surprisingly shallow. The paper reports what happened, but rarely delves into *why* it happened. This leaves the reader with many unanswered questions about the properties of the methods and the implications of the results.
2.  **Conflicting and Unexplained Results for MultiCluster.** The performance of the multiCluster method is contradictory across the two experimental setups. In the 59-language experiment (Table 2, not shown in provided text but inferred from discussion), multiCCA is reported to consistently outperform multiCluster. However, in the 12-language experiment (Table 3), multiCluster is highly competitive, even outperforming all other methods on three tasks, including the dependency parsing extrinsic task. The paper attributes the poor 59-language performance to "added semantic ambiguity" but does not investigate this claim. Is the difference due to the quality of the dictionaries (extracted from Europarl vs. generated via Google Translate)? Or is it an inherent scaling problem of the clustering approach? A deeper analysis is crucial for understanding the method's limitations.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Methodological Choices and Parameters.** Several key design choices are presented without justification or sensitivity analysis. For instance, in multiCCA, English is chosen as the pivot language for the shared space. How would the results change if a different language were chosen? For multiCluster, hyperparameters like the maximum cluster size are mentioned but their impact on performance is not explored. This lack of ablation studies makes it difficult to fully appreciate the design of the proposed methods.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the stark performance difference of the multiCluster method between the 12-language and 59-language settings? Specifically, can you disentangle the effect of dictionary quality (Europarl-extracted vs. Google Translate-generated) from the potential issue of semantic drift in larger, more diverse clusters?
2.  The paper claims that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement over multiQVEC. Table 1 supports this in a multilingual context. To fully substantiate the claim that QVEC-CCA fixes fundamental flaws in QVEC, could you also provide the correlation results for the monolingual versions (QVEC and QVEC-CCA) with the downstream tasks? This would demonstrate whether the improvement holds in the original monolingual setting as well.
3.  Regarding multiCCA, what is the rationale for choosing English as the pivot language, and have you experimented with other pivot languages? How sensitive are the final embeddings to this choice, especially for language families not closely related to English?

### Additional Comments

*   The phrasing in Section 3.1, "we report results on ... a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets," is slightly ambiguous. It is clearer to state that the scores on several datasets were aggregated, as you do later for the RG-65 datasets in Section 5.2.
*   In Section 2.3, the text describes a method from Luong et al. (2015b). However, the introduction of multiSkip in Section 2 cites Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016). Please clarify the relationship between your multiSkip implementation and these three papers.
*   The formatting of the two equations on page 3 (the skipgram objective functions) appears to exceed the column width.
*   The related work section could be strengthened by including other relevant work on multilingual embeddings that use translational context from parallel corpora (e.g., Chandar et al., 2014; Soyer et al., 2015) or multilingual clustering (e.g., Ammar et al., 2016).
