### Summary

This paper presents a comprehensive study on massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two novel dictionary-based methods for creating a shared embedding space for many languages: MultiCluster, which groups translational equivalents into clusters before training embeddings, and MultiCCA, which extends bilingual CCA-based projection to a multilingual "hub-and-spoke" model with English as the hub.

Beyond estimation, the paper makes significant contributions to evaluation. It introduces QVEC-CCA, a new intrinsic evaluation metric that improves upon the existing QVEC method by using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to provide a normalized, basis-invariant score. The authors demonstrate that their new metric correlates more strongly with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic measures. To facilitate future research, the authors have developed and released a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings across a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The proposed methods are evaluated in 12- and 59-language settings, showing competitive but inconsistent performance against baselines.

### Contributions

1.  **A new intrinsic evaluation metric (QVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation platform.** This is the paper's strongest contribution. QVEC-CCA addresses key theoretical flaws in its predecessor (QVEC) and is shown to have a much stronger correlation with downstream task performance than other common intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation. The accompanying web portal provides a valuable, standardized resource that lowers the barrier to entry for researchers and promotes reproducible science in this area.
2.  **Two novel, scalable, dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings.** The paper introduces MultiCluster and MultiCCA, which can scale to dozens of languages without requiring parallel corpora. While the methods themselves are conceptually straightforward extensions of existing ideas (graph clustering and bilingual CCA), their application and evaluation in a massively multilingual setting are valuable.

### Strengths

1.  **High practical value for the research community.** The development of the QVEC-CCA metric and the public evaluation portal are significant practical contributions. The finding that QVEC-CCA correlates strongly with document classification performance (r=0.896) is particularly compelling and suggests it can serve as a reliable and efficient proxy for extrinsic evaluation. This infrastructure will greatly benefit future work on multilingual representations.
2.  **Thorough and wide-ranging experimental setup.** The authors evaluate their methods on an impressive array of tasks, including four intrinsic metrics (word similarity, translation, QVEC, QVEC-CCA) and two extrinsic tasks (document classification, dependency parsing). Furthermore, they conduct experiments in both a 12-language setting (to compare with parallel-data baselines) and a large-scale 59-language setting, demonstrating the scalability of their proposed methods.
3.  **Well-written and meticulously documented paper.** The paper is clear, well-structured, and easy to follow. The authors' commitment to releasing their code, trained embeddings, and evaluation data via the portal is commendable and sets a high standard for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The paper favors breadth over depth, limiting research insights.** While the scope of the work is a strength, it comes at the cost of deeper analysis. The paper introduces two new methods, a new evaluation metric, and a web portal, but provides limited discussion of the underlying reasons for the observed results. The performance of the proposed methods is mixed, sometimes outperforming baselines and sometimes not, but the paper does not delve into why this might be the case, leaving the reader with few generalizable takeaways about what makes a multilingual embedding method successful.
2.  **Lack of analysis for key experimental results and design choices.**
    *   The conflicting results for MultiCluster are not adequately explained. The method is competitive in the 12-language setting (ranking first in three tasks) but is "severely hurt" in the 59-language setting. The paper briefly attributes this to "added semantic ambiguity," but a more thorough analysis is needed. For example, does performance degrade gracefully as more languages are added? Is there a qualitative difference in the clusters being formed?
    *   The paper does not investigate the impact of important methodological decisions and hyperparameters. For MultiCluster, how does the maximum cluster size affect performance? For MultiCCA, how does the choice of the pivot language (English) influence the final embeddings for other languages? An ablation study or sensitivity analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
3.  **The central claim of QVEC-CCA's superiority is not fully supported by the provided evidence.** The authors claim QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC, partly because it is basis-invariant and normalized. While Table 1 shows strong multilingual correlations for QVEC-CCA, it doesn't provide a direct comparison against monolingual QVEC's correlation with the same downstream tasks. Including monolingual QVEC and monolingual QVEC-CCA correlation results would provide the necessary evidence to substantiate this claim.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why MultiCluster's performance degrades so significantly when moving from 12 to 59 languages? For instance, does the average cluster size or semantic coherence change drastically?
2.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your methods to key design choices? Specifically, what is the effect of the maximum cluster size in MultiCluster, and how does the choice of pivot language or the quality of the dictionaries affect the MultiCCA projections?
3.  To better support the claim that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC, could you add the monolingual correlation scores for both QVEC and QVEC-CCA to Table 1? This would allow for a direct comparison of their effectiveness on a single-language basis before extending to the multilingual setting.

### Additional Comments

*   **Related Work:** The related work section could be strengthened by including more work on multilingual embeddings beyond the bilingual case, as well as connections to cross-lingual clustering (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012) and learning from translational context (e.g., Gouws et al., 2014).
*   **Citation Inconsistency:** Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016) in reference to "multiSkip," but the detailed description in Section 2.3 only cites Luong et al. (2015b). Please clarify the relationship between your implementation and these other works.
*   **Minor Wording:** The phrasing on line 304, "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets," is vague. Please specify which datasets were combined.
*   **Formatting:** The two equations at the top of page 3 run past the column margin.
