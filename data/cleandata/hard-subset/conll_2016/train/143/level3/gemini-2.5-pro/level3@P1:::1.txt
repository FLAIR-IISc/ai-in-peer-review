This paper presents a comprehensive study on the estimation and evaluation of multilingual word embeddings, with a focus on methods that do not require parallel corpora. It introduces two dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and applies them to create embeddings for 59 languages. For evaluation, the paper proposes an improved intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which corrects theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and is shown to correlate better with downstream task performance. The authors also contribute a public web portal with standardized datasets and evaluation scripts to facilitate future research in this area. The work is empirically thorough, comparing the proposed methods against baselines that use parallel data (multiSkip, translation-invariance) on a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Comprehensive Evaluation Framework and Community Resource:** The paper's strongest contribution is the development of a principled framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings. This includes the new multiQVEC-CCA metric, which addresses key limitations of prior work (i.e., it is invariant to linear transformations and produces a normalized score), and an empirical demonstration of its superior correlation with downstream tasks. The accompanying web portal for standardized evaluation is a significant practical contribution that will foster reproducibility and accelerate progress in the field.
2.  **Large-Scale Empirical Analysis of Multilingual Embedding Methods:** The paper presents one of the first large-scale, systematic comparisons of different families of multilingual embedding techniques (dictionary-based vs. parallel-corpus-based) across dozens of languages. The scale of this study (up to 59 languages) and the breadth of the evaluation (nine different metrics) provide a valuable empirical snapshot of the current landscape.

**Strengths**

1.  **Ambitious Scope and Resource Provision:** The effort to build and evaluate embeddings for 59 languages is highly ambitious and results in a valuable resource for the multilingual NLP community. The release of an open-source evaluation portal is an exemplary contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for other researchers and promotes standardized, comparable evaluation.
2.  **Principled Development of a New Evaluation Metric:** The authors clearly identify the theoretical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric—namely its sensitivity to the embedding basis and its unnormalized scoring—and propose multiQVEC-CCA as an elegant solution based on Canonical Correlation Analysis. The validation experiment showing its stronger correlation with parsing and document classification performance is convincing.
3.  **Thorough and Broad Evaluation:** The experimental design is comprehensive, comparing four different estimation methods on a wide variety of intrinsic (e.g., word similarity, word translation) and extrinsic (parsing, document classification) tasks. This provides a multi-faceted view of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed methods are described clearly, and the motivation for the work is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Methodological Novelty:** The novelty of the proposed estimation methods is not well-differentiated from prior art. MultiCCA is explicitly described as a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The description of the translation-invariance baseline does not clarify how the implementation differs from or extends the original work by Gardner et al. (2015), which may have been bilingual-only. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly situated its methodological contributions beyond the scaling of existing ideas.
2.  **Inconsistent and Puzzling Experimental Results:** The results presented in Table 3 lack a clear, coherent narrative. It is surprising that multiCluster, a simple method that conflates all words in a translation cluster into a single vector, achieves the best performance on both extrinsic tasks. Conversely, the translation-invariance method excels at the word translation task by a large margin but is less competitive elsewhere. This inconsistency across tasks makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about which method is superior and suggests that the interaction between embedding properties and task requirements is not yet fully understood.
3.  **Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage on Evaluation:** The evaluation is significantly confounded by large differences in vocabulary coverage between the models, as indicated in the bracketed numbers in Table 3. For intrinsic tasks, this means that models are being compared on different sets of test instances, which can introduce serious bias. While the authors acknowledge this and mention an analysis on the vocabulary intersection in the supplementary material, this issue is critical enough to warrant a more central and thorough treatment in the main paper.
4.  **Practical Limitations of the MultiQVEC-CCA Metric:** While multiQVEC-CCA is a strong conceptual contribution, its practical application relies on the availability and consistency of linguistic resources like supersense tags across languages. As hinted at in footnote 9, annotations may be inconsistent, and the paper does not explain how it addresses the challenge of aligning potentially disparate or sparse feature sets across the three languages used in the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The dependency parsing experiment freezes the pre-trained embeddings to isolate their quality. To better understand the impact of this setup, could you report two additional numbers: (1) the performance of the parser when its embeddings are initialized with your pre-trained vectors but are allowed to be fine-tuned, and (2) the baseline performance when the parser's embeddings are randomly initialized?
2.  Could you please clarify how the correlation coefficients in Table 1 were computed? The text mentions the evaluation of 17 different embeddings, and it would be helpful to understand precisely how these data points were aggregated to produce the final Pearson's correlation coefficients.
3.  Your implementation of multiSkip performs poorly, which you attribute to its inability to use monolingual data. Given that the much simpler multiCCA approach performs well, what are the remaining theoretical or practical advantages of a joint training approach like multiSkip over a two-step "train-then-project" pipeline like multiCCA?
4.  The dictionaries used in your experiments were generated automatically (via alignments or machine translation). How sensitive are the multiCluster and multiCCA methods to dictionary quality? Have you performed any experiments using manually curated, higher-precision bilingual dictionaries to see how performance is affected?
