### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings in a shared vector space across a large number of languages (59 in this work). The authors make contributions in two main areas: estimation methods and evaluation metrics. They propose two new methods that rely on bilingual dictionaries and monolingual corpora: `multiCluster`, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters and learns embeddings for these clusters, and `multiCCA`, which learns linear projections from individual monolingual embedding spaces into a shared English-anchored space. The paper also introduces an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to measure the alignment between an embedding space and a linguistic feature space. This new metric is shown to be theoretically superior to its predecessor (QVEC) and to correlate better with downstream task performance than other common intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors present a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

The main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A new intrinsic evaluation metric (`multiQVEC-CCA`) and its validation:** The paper introduces a theoretically-grounded metric for evaluating embeddings that is invariant to linear transformations and produces a normalized score. Crucially, the authors demonstrate empirically that this metric has a much stronger correlation with performance on downstream tasks (document classification and parsing) than widely-used metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy.
2.  **Resource-light methods for massively multilingual embeddings:** The paper proposes two methods (`multiCluster` and `multiCCA`) capable of learning embeddings for dozens of languages without requiring parallel corpora, which are a scarce resource. This enables the creation of the 59-language embeddings released with the paper.
3.  **A valuable public resource for the research community:** The development of a web portal for standardized evaluation, along with the release of pre-trained embeddings, monolingual data, and dictionaries, represents a significant practical contribution that will facilitate reproducible research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a critical gap in evaluation methodology:** The paper compellingly argues that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings correlate poorly with their utility in downstream applications. The proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric is a well-motivated and significant step toward bridging this gap. The thorough correlation analysis in Section 5.1 provides strong evidence for the superiority of this new metric.
2.  **Scalability and practicality of proposed methods:** The focus on dictionary-based methods is a major strength. `multiCCA` in particular is an elegant and simple approach that scales effectively to a large number of languages (59 in this work), using resources (monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries) that are far more widely available than the parallel corpora required by many competing methods.
3.  **Comprehensive experimental design:** The authors conduct an extensive evaluation, comparing four different estimation methods across a diverse suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This broad comparison provides a rich, albeit complex, picture of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches to multilingual representation learning.
4.  **Significant contribution to community resources:** The creation of an open evaluation portal is an outstanding contribution. By providing a common platform, standardized datasets, and baseline results, the authors are fostering a more rigorous and comparable evaluation culture in this subfield. Releasing the 59-language embeddings is also a highly valuable asset for the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent and counter-intuitive experimental results:** The primary weakness of the paper is the set of results presented in Table 3, which are difficult to interpret and lack a clear narrative. The ranking of the four estimation methods is inconsistent across the nine evaluation metrics. Most puzzlingly, the simplistic `multiCluster` method, which conflates the semantics of many words into a single vector, achieves the best performance on both downstream tasks. This result is highly counter-intuitive and is not adequately explained. The lack of a coherent explanation for these disparate results undermines the paper's ability to offer clear guidance on which embedding methods are preferable for specific applications.
2.  **Potential for biased comparison due to vocabulary coverage differences:** The experiments do not adequately control for the confounding variable of vocabulary coverage. Different models cover different percentages of the test sets, yet the scores are reported on these differing subsets. This is particularly problematic for intrinsic evaluations, where a model covering only easy, high-frequency words could gain an unfair advantage. While the authors mention an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies in the supplementary material, this is a critical methodological point that should be addressed in the main paper to ensure the central claims are well-supported.
3.  **Practical limitations of the proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric:** While `multiQVEC-CCA` is a strong theoretical contribution, its practical utility is constrained by its reliance on multilingual supersense annotations, which are only available for a handful of languages and may suffer from inconsistencies. The paper's correlation analysis is based on just three languages (EN, IT, DA), which raises questions about how well these findings generalize. The paper should discuss this limitation more directly and explore the viability of using more widely available resources (like universal POS tags) as an alternative linguistic basis for the metric.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a deeper analysis or hypothesis for the inconsistent performance patterns in Table 3? In particular, why do you believe the relatively simple `multiCluster` method outperforms more complex models on extrinsic tasks, while the `translation-invariance` model dominates the word translation task so decisively but performs poorly elsewhere?
2.  To what extent do the embeddings contribute to the performance of the dependency parser? It would be helpful to know the baseline performance using randomly initialized word embeddings and the performance when the pre-trained embeddings are allowed to be fine-tuned during training. This would better contextualize the unlabeled attachment scores reported.
3.  Your dictionary-based methods rely on dictionaries extracted automatically from parallel corpora or generated via machine translation. How robust are `multiCCA` and `multiCluster` to noise and quality variations in the input dictionaries? Have you performed any analysis with smaller but higher-quality, manually curated lexicons?
4.  Regarding the generalization of `multiQVEC-CCA`, you suggest in a footnote that universal POS tags could be used instead of supersense tags. Have you explored whether this alternative linguistic resource maintains a strong correlation with downstream *semantic* tasks like document classification?
