### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings that share a single vector space. The authors propose and compare four methods for estimating these embeddings: two novel dictionary-based methods, multiCluster (which groups translational equivalents into clusters and learns embeddings for clusters) and multiCCA (which uses CCA to project monolingual embeddings into a shared English space), and two baseline methods, multiSkip (an extension of the bilingual skip-gram objective) and Translation Invariance (based on low-rank PMI matrix factorization). A key contribution is a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which improves upon QVEC by using CCA to create a rotation- and linear-invariant score. The authors demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics. The paper presents a broad empirical study across up to 59 languages and introduces a web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation.

### Contributions

1.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of different strategies for building large-scale multilingual embeddings.** The paper's primary strength is its broad evaluation of four distinct methods (dictionary-based, parallel corpus-based, matrix factorization) on a massive scale (up to 59 languages) across a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This provides a valuable, if complex, snapshot of the current landscape.
2.  **A principled new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA.** The authors identify significant theoretical flaws in the prior QVEC metric (sensitivity to basis rotation, lack of normalization) and propose a CCA-based solution that is both theoretically sounder and empirically shown to have a stronger correlation with downstream task performance, particularly document classification.
3.  **A valuable public resource for the community.** The development of an open-source evaluation portal, along with the release of trained embeddings and code, is a significant practical contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for reproducible research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Ambitious Scope and Scale:** The work moves beyond the typical bilingual or small-scale multilingual setting to tackle embeddings for 59 languages simultaneously. This "massively multilingual" focus is highly relevant and addresses a key challenge for developing NLP technologies for a wider range of the world's languages.
2.  **Methodological Improvements in Evaluation:** The proposal of multiQVEC-CCA is well-motivated and addresses clear weaknesses in prior work. By ensuring invariance to linear transformations, it provides a more robust way to compare different embedding models whose vector space orientations are arbitrary. The high correlation reported with document classification (0.896) is particularly impressive and suggests it is a meaningful metric.
3.  **Clarity and Commitment to Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and the different methodologies are described clearly. The authors' commitment to open-sourcing their methods, data, and evaluation framework via the web portal is commendable and a major strength that will benefit the research community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Results and Lack of Explanatory Analysis:** The experimental results in Table 3 are puzzling and undermine the paper's conclusions. There is no single "best" method; instead, performance varies drastically and counter-intuitively across tasks. For example, multiCluster—a conceptually simple method that discards all semantic distinctions between words in a translation cluster—surprisingly achieves the best performance on complex extrinsic tasks (parsing and document classification). Meanwhile, the translation-invariance method dominates the word translation task by a large margin but is middling elsewhere. The paper presents these results without offering a deep analysis or plausible hypothesis for why these inconsistencies arise, making it difficult for the reader to draw clear takeaways.
2.  **Unclear Novelty of the "Translation Invariance" Method:** The paper positions translation invariance as a baseline from Gardner et al. (2015), but it is unclear what, if any, novel contributions were made in this paper's implementation. Gardner et al.'s work was presented in a bilingual context. If the authors extended it to a truly multilingual setting (beyond just pairwise application), this is a non-trivial contribution that needs to be explicitly described and claimed. As written, it's difficult to assess the work's novelty in this area.
3.  **Confounding Factors in the Evaluation:** The comparison between methods is clouded by several unaddressed variables.
    *   **Vocabulary Coverage:** The authors note that coverage differences can bias results but do not adequately control for this. In Table 3, methods are directly compared despite significant coverage disparities (e.g., 92.1% accuracy for document classification with 48.1% coverage for multiCluster vs. 31.3% for translation-invariance). This makes it impossible to know if performance differences are due to the method's quality or the subset of vocabulary it covers. A more rigorous analysis on a controlled vocabulary intersection is needed.
    *   **Linguistic Feature Intersection:** The multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA metrics rely on supersense annotations across English, Danish, and Italian. As noted in footnote 9, these tagsets are often inconsistent. A major concern is that the intersection of shared, comparable linguistic features could be very small, making the resulting multilingual evaluation score noisy or unreliable. This weakness is acknowledged but not addressed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  For the dependency parsing experiments, could you clarify the baseline performance? Specifically, what is the performance loss when fixing embeddings versus fine-tuning them, and what is the gain from using these pretrained embeddings over randomly initialized word vectors in the stack-LSTM parser? This context is crucial for interpreting the absolute scores.
2.  Could you explicitly state the novel contributions in your implementation of the translation-invariance method relative to Gardner et al. (2015)? Was the method extended to a joint multilingual setting beyond pairwise training, and if so, what was the modification to the objective function?
3.  In the multilingual multiQVEC-CCA evaluation, how was the linguistic feature matrix constructed from inconsistent supersense annotations across the three languages? Was it based on the intersection of available tags? If so, how large is this intersection, and how might its small size affect the metric's stability?
4.  The dictionaries for 47 of the 59 languages were created using Google Translate. Have you performed any analysis on the effect of dictionary quality? For example, by comparing results on a subset of languages using a high-quality, manually curated dictionary versus the machine-translated one?
5.  What are the theoretical advantages of the end-to-end multiSkip model over a simpler two-stage pipeline: learning high-quality bilingual embeddings for each language pair (e.g., fr-en, de-en) and then using multi-CCA to project all non-English languages into the shared English space?
