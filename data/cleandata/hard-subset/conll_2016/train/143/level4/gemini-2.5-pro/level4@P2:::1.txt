### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings across a large number of languages in a single, shared vector space. The authors introduce two novel, dictionary-based methods for estimating these embeddings: **multiCluster**, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters and learns embeddings for the clusters, and **multiCCA**, which extends a bilingual CCA-based approach by projecting multiple languages into a shared space (anchored by English). These are compared against two baselines that leverage parallel corpora: **multiSkip** and **translation-invariance**.

A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to evaluation. The authors identify theoretical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric (sensitivity to basis rotation, lack of normalization) and propose an improved version, **multiQVEC-CCA**, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis to provide a normalized, rotation-invariant score. They empirically validate this new metric by showing it correlates more strongly with downstream task performance (document classification and parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors present a web portal and open-source code to facilitate reproducible research and standardized evaluation in this area.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** This includes the proposal of multiQVEC-CCA, a theoretically grounded improvement over prior correlation-based metrics, and a rigorous correlation analysis (Table 1) that demonstrates its superiority for predicting downstream performance. The development of a public web portal for standardized evaluation is a substantial service to the research community.
2.  **The proposal and evaluation of scalable, dictionary-based estimation methods.** The multiCCA method, in particular, is an intuitive and effective approach that scales to 59 languages without requiring scarce parallel corpora. This makes it highly practical for creating truly "massively multilingual" resources.
3.  **An extensive empirical comparison of four different multilingual embedding methods** across nine intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks. While the results do not point to a single best method, the breadth of this investigation provides valuable insights into the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong focus on rigorous and replicable evaluation.** The paper's most significant strength is its contribution to the methodology of evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors correctly identify the flaws in existing metrics and propose a well-motivated alternative (multiQVEC-CCA). The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides strong evidence that their proposed metric is a better proxy for performance on downstream tasks than widely used metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy. The accompanying web portal is an excellent contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for future research and encourages standardized, comparable results.

2.  **Scalability and practicality of the proposed methods.** The dictionary-based methods, particularly multiCCA, do not rely on parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for extending multilingual systems beyond a few dozen well-resourced languages. The successful training of embeddings for 59 languages demonstrates the real-world applicability of this approach and is a key step towards massively multilingual NLP.

3.  **Comprehensive experimental design.** The authors evaluate four distinct methods on a wide array of tasks, including two extrinsic evaluations (document classification and dependency parsing). This breadth allows for a nuanced comparison and reveals that different methods excel on different types of tasks (e.g., dictionary-based methods for extrinsic tasks vs. translation-invariance for some intrinsic tasks). This thoroughness provides a valuable benchmark for the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent performance of proposed estimation methods.** The primary weakness is that the experimental results in Table 3 do not present a clear case for the superiority of the newly proposed estimation methods. The translation-invariance baseline achieves the best scores on four of the nine intrinsic metrics, including monolingual and multilingual QVEC-CCA. Furthermore, it is surprising that multiCluster—a method that conflates the semantics of all words in a translation cluster into a single vector—outperforms other methods on both extrinsic tasks. This counter-intuitive result is not sufficiently analyzed or explained, weakening the claims about the effectiveness of the proposed models.

2.  **The confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on results.** The authors correctly note that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias the results of intrinsic evaluations. This issue is particularly salient in Table 3, where the coverage of the translation-invariance method is significantly lower than the others on several tasks (e.g., monolingual word similarity: 23.0% vs. 71.0% for multiCCA). While an analysis on the vocabulary intersection is mentioned for the supplementary material, its absence from the main paper makes it difficult to fairly assess the performance comparisons. For a paper focused on evaluation, this feels like a critical detail that should have been addressed more directly in the main results.

3.  **Practical limitations of the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric.** While multiQVEC-CCA is theoretically sound, its practical application is constrained by the need for manually created, consistently annotated linguistic resources (like supersense tags) across multiple languages. As the authors acknowledge in a footnote, such resources are rare, which limits the metric's utility to a small set of languages. This is at odds with the paper's broader goal of advancing "massively multilingual" embeddings, as the primary evaluation tool cannot be applied in such a setting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the translation-invariance baseline: Is your implementation a direct application of the bilingual method from Gardner et al. (2015), or did you introduce a novel extension to handle the multilingual setting? A clarification on this point would be helpful.

2.  In the dependency parsing experiment, you froze the pre-trained embeddings to isolate their contribution. Could you quantify the performance drop this causes compared to a setup where the parser is allowed to fine-tune the embeddings? This would help contextualize the absolute scores and better gauge how much useful syntactic information is captured by the static embeddings alone.

3.  The dictionaries used were generated from high-quality parallel corpora or Google Translate. How do you expect the performance of dictionary-based methods like multiCCA to degrade when using noisier, real-world lexical resources (e.g., scraped from Wiktionary) which may have lower coverage and precision?
