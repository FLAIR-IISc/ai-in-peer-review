This paper introduces two dictionary-based techniques for learning multilingual word representations: MultiCluster, which is grounded in clustering, and MultiCCA, which leverages canonical correlation analysis. In addition, the authors put forward an enhanced supersense similarity measure that builds upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and integrating multilingual evaluation. A comprehensive evaluation across a diverse range of tasks, conducted via a custom-developed web portal, demonstrates that the proposed methods can outperform existing baselines in specific contexts.

The manuscript is well-written and represents a substantial body of work. The proposed methods for representation learning and evaluation are highly relevant, and the meticulous documentation is commendable. My main reservation, however, is that the paper seems to favor breadth over analytical depth. A more thorough discussion of the results would be beneficial, particularly concerning the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster in the 59- and 12-language settings, and the influence of different estimation parameters and design choices for MultiCluster/CCA. Therefore, while the paper's practical contributions (the enhanced QVEC measure, the web portal) are valuable to the research community, it offers limited new insights into the core research questions it aims to address.

Here are some more specific suggestions:

To substantiate the claim in lines 326-328 that the proposed QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC, it would be beneficial to include the correlation results for the monolingual variants of both measures in Table 1.

Minor issues:

*   Line 304: The phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" could imply the datasets are of different types. For clarity, it might be better to specify that they are of the same kind, differing only in language.
*   Page 3: Two equations appear to extend beyond the column margin.
*   The reference to the MultiSkip baseline is slightly confusing. Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., whereas Section 2.3 only refers to Luong et al. Please clarify the relationship between these works in the context of the MultiSkip baseline.

While the literature review is adequate, it could be made more comprehensive by including the following relevant works:

**Multilingual embeddings and clustering:**
*   Chandar A P, S., Lauly, S., Larochelle, H., Khapra, M. M., Ravindran, B., Raykar, V. C., and Saha, A. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. In NIPS.
*   Hill, F., Cho, K., Jean, S., Devin, C., and Bengio, Y. (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv preprint arXiv:1412.6448.
*   Lu, A., Wang, W., Bansal, M., Gimpel, K., & Livescu, K. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. In NAACL.
*   Faruqui, M., & Dyer, C. (2013). An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. In ACL.

**Multilingual training for improved source-language embeddings:**
*   Suster, S., Titov, I., and van Noord, G. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. In NAACL-HLT.
*   Guo, J., Che, W., Wang, H., and Liu, T. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. In COLING.

**Broader use of translational context:**
*   Diab, M., & Resnik, P. (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. In ACL.
