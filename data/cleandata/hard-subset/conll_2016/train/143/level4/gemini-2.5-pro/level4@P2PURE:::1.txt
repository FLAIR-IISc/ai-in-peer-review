This paper presents four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation.

The proposed embedding methods are:
1)  **multiCluster:** This method uses a dictionary to group words into multilingual clusters. An embedding is then learned for each cluster, and this cluster embedding is assigned to all words within that cluster.
2)  **multiCCA:** An extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), this approach scales their bilingual method to the multilingual setting. It designates English as an "anchor" space and uses bilingual dictionaries to learn projections from other monolingual embedding spaces into this common English space.
3)  **multiSkip:** This method extends the bilingual model of Luong et al. (2015b), which uses aligned source and target contexts. The extension involves modifying the objective function to incorporate signals from all available parallel corpora simultaneously.
4)  **Translation Invariance:** This approach is based on a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, incorporating a bilingual alignment frequency component into the objective function. The paper is unclear if this method extends beyond the bilingual case.

The proposed evaluation metric is a modified version of QVEC. It employs Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to maximize the correlation between the word embeddings and linguistic feature vectors. The resulting score, derived from the basis vectors of the aligned dimensions, is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. This metric is also extended for multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed methods are described clearly. However, I have a few major concerns:

1.  The contribution of the translation invariance approach relative to Gardner et al. is not clear. If the novelty lies in extending the method to the multilingual setting, this should be explicitly stated and explained.
2.  The reliance on super-sense annotations for multilingual evaluation is concerning. The feature set resulting from the intersection of annotations across multiple languages could be very sparse. The paper should address this potential data sparsity issue more directly than the brief mention in footnote 9.
3.  The analysis of the results in Table 2 is incomplete without a discussion of the impact of coverage. For instance, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA achieve similar scores despite having significantly different coverage. The authors should clarify how coverage might be influencing these results.
4.  The results presented in Table 3 lack a clear, consistent narrative. For most intrinsic metrics, the proposed multilingual methods are not the top performers. It is particularly surprising that the translation invariance approach is by far the best on the word translation task, given that this was a primary motivation for the other models. Furthermore, it is counter-intuitive that the multiCluster approach, which discards inter-cluster semantic information, performs best on the extrinsic tasks. The paper would be stronger if it provided some analysis or intuition for these seemingly contradictory results.

Additional Questions and Clarifications:

1.  For the dependency parsing task, what is the performance drop when the pre-trained embeddings are kept fixed during training? Conversely, what was the performance gain of using these embeddings for initialization compared to a baseline with randomly initialized embeddings?
2.  Do the results in Table 1 represent an average over all 17 languages mentioned in Section 5.1?
3.  What are the conceptual or empirical advantages of the multiSkip approach over a simpler pipeline, such as learning separate bilingual embeddings for each language pair and then mapping them to a common space using the multiCCA projection method?
4.  The dictionaries used were extracted automatically from parallel corpora or machine translation. This process may not reflect the challenges of using existing, manually-curated lexicons (e.g., coverage, noise). Have the authors considered evaluating their methods using such resources?
