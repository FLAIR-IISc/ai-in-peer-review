- Paper studies creating and evaluating multilingual embeddings without parallel corpora, proposing two dictionary-based methods and an improved intrinsic metric, evaluated across up to 59 languages with released code and a web portal.  
- Contribution: multiQVEC-CCA, a theoretically principled intrinsic metric using CCA that corrects QVEC's flaws and correlates better with downstream tasks.  
- Contribution: multiCluster, a dictionary-based method grouping translationally equivalent words before learning shared embeddings.  
- Contribution: multiCCA, a dictionary-based method projecting monolingual embeddings into a common space via CCA.  
- Contribution: Public web portal, open-source code, and trained models to standardize evaluation and improve reproducibility.  
- Strength: Strong empirical validation of multiQVEC-CCA using correlations between multiple intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks across 17 embedding models.  
- Strength: Large-scale, broad evaluation covering 59 languages and nine intrinsic/extrinsic tasks, providing a solid benchmark.  
- Strength: Clear, well-organized writing and thorough experimental documentation.  
- Weakness: Results lack deep analysis—e.g., no detailed investigation of why multiCluster degrades from 12 to 59 languages.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of methodological choices and hyperparameters (pivot language choice for multiCCA, thresholds and cluster sizes for multiCluster).  
- Weakness: multiSkip baseline comparison may be unfair because the authors’ implementation omits monolingual data used by prior work, disadvantaging that baseline.  
- Question: Please report monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA correlations with extrinsic tasks to validate improvements independent of the multilingual extension.  
- Question: Clarify how your multiSkip implementation relates to Luong et al. (2015b), Coulmance et al. (2015), and Guo et al. (2016)—is it based on one or a combination?  
- Question: Explain the source of increased semantic ambiguity causing multiCluster’s performance drop on 59 languages—dictionary quality, sense conflation from more languages, or larger cluster sizes?
