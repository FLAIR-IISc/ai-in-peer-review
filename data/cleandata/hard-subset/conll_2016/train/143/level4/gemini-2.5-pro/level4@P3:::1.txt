### Summary

This paper introduces methods for creating and evaluating word embeddings in a shared vector space across a large number of languages (up to 59). The main contributions are:

1.  **New Dictionary-Based Embedding Methods:** The authors propose two novel methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, for training multilingual embeddings. A key advantage of these methods is that they only require monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, obviating the need for large parallel corpora which are unavailable for most language pairs.
2.  **An Improved Intrinsic Evaluation Metric:** The paper introduces `multiQVEC-CCA`, an extension and improvement upon the existing QVEC metric. The authors demonstrate through correlation analysis that their proposed metric aligns better with performance on downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification and parsing) than previously used intrinsic metrics like word translation.
3.  **A Public Evaluation Platform and Resources:** The authors have developed and released a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, alongside the embeddings trained for 59 languages. This is a valuable contribution to the community that facilitates reproducible research.

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable Contribution to Evaluation Methodology:** A significant strength of this work is the critical analysis of existing evaluation metrics and the development of a new one, `multiQVEC-CCA`. The experiments in Section 5.1 convincingly show that commonly used intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation) have poor correlation with performance on downstream tasks. By contrast, the proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` shows a substantially higher correlation (Pearson's r of 0.896 with document classification). This finding is important for the field, as it provides a more reliable and efficient way to evaluate multilingual embeddings without running expensive extrinsic tasks.

2.  **Impressive Scale and Commitment to Open Science:** The effort to train and release embeddings for 59 languages is commendable. More importantly, the creation of a public web portal for evaluation is a major service to the research community. By providing standardized datasets, evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparison, the authors lower the barrier to entry and promote more rigorous, comparable, and reproducible research in this area. This contribution has the potential for long-term impact on the field.

3.  **Simple and Effective Methods for Low-Resource Scenarios:** The proposed `multiCCA` method is an elegant and simple extension of prior work that performs robustly. Its reliance on only bilingual dictionaries and monolingual data makes it applicable to a far wider range of languages than methods requiring parallel corpora. The results for 59 languages in Table 2 show that `multiCCA` consistently outperforms the more complex `multiCluster` approach, demonstrating its effectiveness and scalability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Experimental Results and Unclear Takeaways:** The results of the 12-language comparison in Table 3 are difficult to interpret and do not present a clear winner. The proposed methods, `multiCCA` and `multiCluster`, are frequently outperformed by the `translation-invariance` baseline on several intrinsic metrics (e.g., monolingual word similarity, word translation, `multiQVEC-CCA`). Paradoxically, the `multiCluster` method—which makes the strong, simplifying assumption of conflating all words in a translation cluster into a single vector—achieves the best performance on both extrinsic tasks. This is counter-intuitive and leaves the reader wondering about the trade-offs between the methods and why this loss of semantic nuance is beneficial for complex tasks like parsing. The lack of a consistent story weakens the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed estimation methods.

2.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` Metric:** While `multiQVEC-CCA` shows strong correlation with downstream tasks, its practical utility is limited by its reliance on parallel, manually-annotated linguistic resources (specifically, supersense tags). The paper demonstrates the metric using annotations for only three languages (English, Danish, Italian). As the authors acknowledge in Footnote 9, creating consistent annotations of this type across many languages is a significant challenge. This bottleneck raises questions about the scalability and applicability of `multiQVEC-CCA` as a truly "massively multilingual" evaluation tool, which is the paper's primary focus.

3.  **The Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage on Evaluation:** Across the experiments, there are significant differences in vocabulary coverage between the different embedding methods (e.g., in Table 3, `translation-invariance` covers only 23% on word similarity vs. 71% for `multiCCA`). Evaluating on different subsets of the test data makes a direct comparison of scores unreliable, as some methods may be evaluated on an "easier" set of more frequent words. The authors acknowledge this and mention that an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies is provided in the supplementary material. However, this is a critical detail for a fair comparison and should be presented and discussed in the main paper to strengthen the validity of the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the dependency parsing experiment: The parser was forced to rely solely on the pre-trained embeddings by fixing them during training. Could you quantify the performance of these embeddings relative to two other conditions: (a) a standard randomly initialized embedding layer, and (b) allowing the pre-trained embeddings to be fine-tuned during parser training? This would provide a clearer picture of their practical value.

2.  Could you provide more intuition as to why the `multiCluster` method, which discards intra-cluster semantic distinctions, performs so well on the extrinsic tasks? Does this suggest that for these tasks, a coarse-grained, translationally-equivalent representation is more robust than a fine-grained semantic one?

3.  The novelty of the translation-invariance baseline in the multilingual setting is not explicitly stated. Did you extend the original bilingual method from Gardner et al. (2015) to handle more than two languages simultaneously, or was it applied in a pairwise fashion?

4.  The dictionaries used in your experiments were generated automatically. How do you believe the performance of `multiCCA` and `multiCluster` would be affected by using real-world, manually curated dictionaries, which may be noisier or have different coverage properties?
