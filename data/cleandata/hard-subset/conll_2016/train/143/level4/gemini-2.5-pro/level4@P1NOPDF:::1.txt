This paper presents four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their effectiveness.

The proposed embedding methods are:
1.  **multiCluster:** This method uses a dictionary to map words into multilingual clusters. A single embedding is then learned for each cluster, which serves as the representation for all words within that cluster.
2.  **multiCCA:** An extension of the bilingual approach from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). This method treats English as an 'anchor' space and uses bilingual dictionaries (mapping from other languages to English) to learn projections from other monolingual embedding spaces into this shared anchor space.
3.  **multiSkip:** This model extends the bilingual method of Luong et al. (2015b), which uses both source and target context via word alignments. The objective function is modified to incorporate components from all available parallel corpora, making it multilingual.
4.  **Translation Invariance:** This approach uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, with an objective function that incorporates bilingual alignment frequencies. It is unclear if this method extends beyond the bilingual case.

The proposed evaluation metric, a modification of QVEC, uses Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and linguistic features (e.g., handcrafted lexicons). It learns basis vectors for the aligned dimensions, yielding a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. This metric is also extended for multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed methods are clearly described. However, I have a few major concerns:

1.  The contribution of the translation invariance approach is unclear relative to the prior work of Gardner et al. If the novelty lies in extending this method to the multilingual setting, this should be explicitly stated and explained.
2.  The reliance on super-sense annotations for multilingual evaluation is problematic. The intersection of such features across many languages is likely to be very small, potentially leading to sparse and unreliable evaluation. The authors should elaborate on how they address this issue beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.
3.  The results in Table 2 are difficult to interpret without understanding the impact of vocabulary coverage. For instance, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA achieve similar scores despite having significantly different coverage. The authors should clarify how coverage affects the reported performance metrics.
4.  The results presented in Table 3 are inconsistent. For most intrinsic metrics, the novel multilingual methods proposed in this paper do not achieve the best performance. Specifically, given that a primary goal was to perform well on word translation, it is concerning that the baseline translation invariance method performs best by a large margin. Furthermore, it is surprising that multiCluster, a method that discards fine-grained semantic information, achieves the best results on the extrinsic tasks. This suggests a disconnect between the intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, which warrants further discussion.

Minor Questions and Points for Clarification:

1.  For the dependency parsing task, what is the performance drop when the pre-trained embeddings are kept fixed versus fine-tuned? Additionally, what was the performance gain of using these embeddings compared to randomly initialized embeddings in the same parser architecture?
2.  Are the results in Table 1 averaged across all 17 languages described in Section 5.1?
3.  What are the conceptual or empirical advantages of the joint-training multiSkip approach compared to a pipeline approach of first training bilingual embeddings and then projecting them into a common space using multiCCA?
4.  The dictionaries used in this work were extracted automatically from parallel corpora or Google Translate. This process may not reflect the challenges of using real, potentially noisy or incomplete, lexicographical resources. Have the authors experimented with publicly available, manually curated multilingual dictionaries?
