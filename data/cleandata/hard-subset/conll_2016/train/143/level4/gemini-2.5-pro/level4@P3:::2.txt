### Summary of the paper
This paper presents a comprehensive study on massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors make contributions in three main areas: 1) They propose two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating word embeddings in a shared space for many languages using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. 2) They introduce an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the previous QVEC metric and demonstrate its superior correlation with downstream task performance. 3) They have developed and released a public web portal to standardize the evaluation of multilingual embeddings, providing data and a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks. The proposed methods are used to train embeddings for 59 languages, and are compared against baseline methods on a smaller set of 12 languages.

### Contributions
1.  **An improved intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a rigorous correlation analysis.** The primary contribution of this work is the development of a new evaluation metric that is invariant to linear transformations and produces a normalized score, addressing key weaknesses of the prior QVEC method. Crucially, the authors demonstrate through a correlation analysis (Table 1) that their proposed metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) are better predictors of downstream performance on parsing and document classification tasks than existing intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy. This provides a valuable tool and a stronger methodological foundation for future research in this area.

2.  **A public evaluation platform for multilingual embeddings.** The authors have created a web portal that serves as a one-stop-shop for evaluating multilingual word embeddings. By providing standardized datasets, evaluation scripts for nine different metrics, and a user-friendly interface for uploading and testing new embeddings, this platform represents a significant contribution to the community. It will facilitate reproducible research and lower the barrier to entry for developing and comparing new embedding estimation techniques.

3.  **Two dictionary-based methods for large-scale multilingual embedding.** The paper introduces multiCluster and multiCCA, two methods that can scale to a large number of languages (59 in this work) without requiring parallel corpora. The experiments show that these methods, particularly multiCCA, are effective and often outperform methods that rely on scarcer parallel data, especially on downstream extrinsic tasks.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong focus on evaluation methodology.** The paper's most significant strength is its thorough and critical approach to evaluation. Rather than just presenting results on existing metrics, the authors "evaluate the evaluation metrics" themselves. The analysis in Section 5.1, which shows that widely used intrinsic metrics like word similarity correlate poorly with extrinsic task performance, is an important finding. The subsequent introduction and validation of multiQVEC-CCA provide the community with a more reliable and theoretically-grounded intrinsic evaluation tool.

2.  **High practical value and contribution to the research community.** The release of the evaluation portal, along with the trained embeddings for 59 languages and open-source code, is a substantial practical contribution. This infrastructure promotes transparency, standardization, and reproducibility, which are essential for making steady progress in a field with many competing models and evaluation paradigms.

3.  **Impressive scale and comprehensive experiments.** The authors conduct an extensive set of experiments, training embeddings for 59 languages and evaluating them across nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This large-scale study provides a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the scalability of the proposed dictionary-based methods. The comparison between dictionary-based and parallel-data-based methods (Table 3) is particularly insightful.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Analysis of results lacks depth.** While the paper presents a breadth of experimental results, the discussion often stops short of providing deep insights. For instance, the performance of multiCluster is competitive on the 12-language setup but degrades significantly on the 59-language setup. The paper attributes this to "added semantic ambiguity" but does not explore this hypothesis further. A more in-depth analysis of the errors, the effect of key hyperparameters (e.g., cluster size), or the properties of the learned representations would have strengthened the paper's scientific contribution and provided readers with a better understanding of the methods' behavior.

2.  **Experimental comparisons are confounded by vocabulary coverage.** In several key comparisons (notably Table 3), there are large discrepancies in vocabulary coverage between the different methods. For example, the translation-invariance method often has dramatically lower coverage than multiCCA. The authors acknowledge this issue and state that an evaluation on the intersection of vocabularies is provided in the supplementary material. However, this makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions from the main results, as the scores may not be directly comparable. This is a significant methodological concern that weakens the claims about the relative performance of the different estimation methods.

3.  **Limited novelty in the embedding estimation methods.** The proposed estimation methods, while effective, are presented as relatively straightforward extensions of prior work. MultiCCA is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is an intuitive combination of existing clustering and embedding techniques. While novelty is not the only measure of a paper's value, the contribution would be stronger if the paper more clearly articulated the non-trivial challenges or insights involved in extending these methods to the massively multilingual setting.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The performance of multiCluster drops significantly when moving from 12 languages to 59, which you attribute to semantic ambiguity. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this phenomenon? For example, does this affect certain types of words (e.g., polysemous words) more than others? Have you explored whether this could be mitigated by adjusting clustering parameters or by incorporating other constraints?

2.  In Table 1, you show strong correlation results for your proposed multilingual metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. To fully support the claim that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC in general (as stated in Section 3.3), could you also provide the correlation scores for the monolingual versions of these metrics on the same downstream tasks?

3.  In Section 2, the multiSkip baseline is introduced with references to Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016). However, the detailed description in Section 2.3 only discusses the method of Luong et al. (2015b). Could you please clarify the precise relationship between your multiSkip implementation and these three works?

### Additional Comments
-   Minor: The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" in Section 3.1 could be clarified. It appears these are datasets of the same kind, just for different language pairs, not datasets of a different nature.
-   The paper is generally well-written and well-documented.
-   The list of related work is good, but could be expanded to include other relevant work on learning bilingual/multilingual embeddings via autoencoders (e.g., Chandar et al., 2014; Suster et al., 2016) or correlation-based methods (e.g., Lu et al., 2015).
