This paper presents two novel dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for generating word embeddings in a shared space across many languages. It also introduces an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which adapts and corrects issues in the existing QVEC metric. The work provides a comprehensive experimental comparison of its proposed methods against two baselines that use parallel corpora. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A new intrinsic evaluation metric for word embeddings, multiQVEC-CCA, and a rigorous analysis showing it correlates more strongly with performance on downstream NLP tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than previously used intrinsic metrics like word translation and cross-lingual word similarity.
2.  The development and release of a public evaluation portal, along with open-source code and pre-trained models. This is a significant resource that facilitates standardized and reproducible research on multilingual embeddings.
3.  Two methods for learning multilingual embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, demonstrating scalability to 59 languages.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper’s most significant strength is its thorough and principled approach to evaluation. By performing a correlation analysis between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic task performance (Table 1), the authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of existing metrics and provide strong evidence for their proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric. This meta-evaluation is a valuable contribution that encourages more rigorous assessment of word embeddings in future work.
2.  The creation of a public web portal for standardized evaluation is a commendable effort that will greatly benefit the research community. Providing open access to the data, models, and evaluation scripts lowers the barrier to entry for new research, fosters reproducibility, and helps establish common benchmarks for the field.
3.  The proposed embedding methods demonstrate impressive scalability. Training embeddings for 59 languages is a substantial undertaking that moves beyond the typical bilingual or few-language focus of prior work. The ability of the dictionary-based methods, particularly multiCCA, to achieve this scale without requiring scarce parallel corpora is a key practical advantage.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The experimental results do not tell a consistent story or establish a clear superiority for the proposed embedding methods. In the 12-language comparison (Table 3), the baseline translation-invariance method performs best on several intrinsic tasks, including word translation, which is a key objective for multilingual embeddings. More surprisingly, the multiCluster method—which conflates the semantics of all words within a translation cluster into a single vector—achieves the best performance on both extrinsic tasks. This counter-intuitive result suggests that the evaluation tasks may not be sensitive to the fine-grained semantic distinctions that more sophisticated models aim to capture, a point that requires more discussion and analysis.
2.  The applicability of the proposed evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, is severely limited in the "massively multilingual" setting that the paper advocates for. The metric relies on the existence of supersense tag annotations, a manually curated resource that is only available for a very small number of languages. This makes it impossible to use the paper's best-correlated metric to evaluate the 59-language embeddings, which is a central focus of the work. This limitation undermines the goal of providing a comprehensive evaluation framework for a large number of languages.
3.  The comparison between embedding methods is confounded by large differences in vocabulary coverage, which makes a direct comparison of scores difficult. For example, in Table 3, multiCCA has significantly higher coverage than the translation-invariance method on several tasks. While the authors note that an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies is provided in supplementary material, this is a critical factor for a fair comparison and its results should be integrated more prominently into the main paper to properly contextualize the performance claims.
4.  The novelty of the baseline methods as presented is unclear. For the translation-invariance method from Gardner et al. (2015), it is not specified whether the authors extended the original bilingual method to the multilingual setting, and if so, how. Clarifying the contribution in adapting these baselines would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The dependency parsing evaluation was conducted with fixed, pre-trained word embeddings. What was the rationale for this choice? Could you report the performance difference if the embeddings were fine-tuned during parser training? This would help clarify whether these embeddings are more useful as fixed feature extractors or as initializations.
2.  The strong performance of the multiCluster method on extrinsic tasks is surprising. Can you offer an intuition or further analysis as to why discarding intra-cluster semantic information (i.e., treating all words in a translation set as identical) is so effective for complex tasks like dependency parsing?
3.  Your dictionary-based methods rely on dictionaries automatically extracted from parallel text or created using machine translation. How do you expect performance to be affected by using real, manually curated lexicons, which may have different coverage properties and types of noise? Have you experimented with any such resources?
