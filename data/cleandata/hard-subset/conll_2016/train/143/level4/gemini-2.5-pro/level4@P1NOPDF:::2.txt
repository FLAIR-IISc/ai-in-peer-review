This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, which is based on clustering, and MultiCCA, which is based on canonical correlation analysis (CCA). Additionally, the authors propose a new supersense similarity measure, QVEC-CCA, that improves upon the existing QVEC method. This improvement is achieved by replacing QVEC's correlation component with CCA and extending the evaluation to a multilingual setting. The proposed methods are evaluated on a wide range of tasks via a web portal also developed by the authors. The results show that in some cases, the new methods outperform the two baselines.

The paper is very well written and represents a substantial amount of work. The proposed methods for representation learning and evaluation are timely, and the authors are to be commended for their meticulous documentation.

My main concern, however, is that the paper prioritizes breadth at the expense of depth. I would have preferred to see a more thorough discussion of the results. For example, the paper could have explored the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, or analyzed the effect of different estimation parameters and design choices within the MultiCluster and MultiCCA frameworks. Consequently, while the paper's practical contributions are valuable to the research community (e.g., the improved QVEC measure and the web portal), its contributions in terms of new research insights are less clear. The paper presents new methods but does not fully explore the research questions they could help answer.

Below are some more specific comments.

To substantiate the claim that the proposed QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC (lines 326-328), it would be beneficial to include the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and monolingual QVEC-CCA as well.
