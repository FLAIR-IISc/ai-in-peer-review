### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive study on creating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings in a shared vector space, focusing on methods that do not require parallel corpora. The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based estimation methods: `multiCluster`, which groups translationally equivalent words before learning embeddings, and `multiCCA`, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis to project monolingual embeddings into a common space. Furthermore, the paper introduces an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which addresses theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC metric and demonstrates a significantly stronger correlation with performance on downstream tasks like document classification and dependency parsing. The authors conduct an extensive evaluation of their proposed methods and several baselines across as many as 59 languages. Finally, they contribute a public web portal and open-source software to facilitate standardized evaluation and future research in this area.

### Contributions
1.  **A new and empirically superior intrinsic evaluation metric (`multiQVEC-CCA`).** The paper's strongest contribution is the proposal of `multiQVEC-CCA`. It clearly identifies theoretical weaknesses in the prior QVEC metric (lack of invariance to linear transformations, unnormalized scores) and provides a principled solution using CCA. More importantly, the authors empirically validate this new metric, showing in Table 1 that it correlates substantially better with downstream task performance than previously used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and word translation.
2.  **Scalable, dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings.** The paper introduces two novel methods (`multiCluster` and `multiCCA`) for inducing embeddings for a large number of languages (up to 59) using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. These methods provide a practical alternative to approaches that rely on often-scarce parallel text and are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing methods, particularly on extrinsic tasks.
3.  **A valuable public resource for the research community.** The authors have developed and released a web portal for the standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. This, along with the release of their code and trained models, represents a significant practical contribution that will lower the barrier to entry and encourage reproducible research in this domain.

### Strengths
1.  **Thorough Validation of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The experimental design to validate `multiQVEC-CCA` is a major strength. By training 17 different embedding models and measuring the correlation between various intrinsic metrics and two distinct extrinsic tasks (Table 1), the authors provide convincing evidence that their proposed metric is a more reliable proxy for downstream utility than existing alternatives. This finding is highly valuable for future work on word embeddings.
2.  **Impressive Scale and Breadth of Evaluation:** The paper presents a large-scale empirical study that is notable for its breadth. Evaluating multiple methods across 59 languages on a suite of nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks is a substantial undertaking. This extensive experimentation provides a solid benchmark for the presented methods and a valuable point of comparison for future work.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their methods, and detailing their experimental setup. The meticulous approach to research and documentation is commendable.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of In-Depth Analysis of Results:** While the paper presents a wealth of experimental results, the discussion often remains at a surface level, missing an opportunity for deeper insight. For instance, the authors note that `multiCluster`'s performance degrades significantly when moving from 12 to 59 languages due to "added semantic ambiguity," but they do not investigate this phenomenon further. A more thorough analysis of why certain methods excel on specific tasks or how factors like dictionary quality and language family affect performance would significantly increase the paper's scientific contribution.
2.  **Limited Exploration of Methodological Choices:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the key hyperparameters and design decisions within the proposed methods. For `multiCCA`, the choice of English as the central "pivot" language is crucial; how would results differ if another language was chosen? For `multiCluster`, the impact of the dictionary extraction threshold (τ) or the maximum cluster size is not explored. An ablation study or sensitivity analysis would provide a better understanding of the methods' robustness and behavior.
3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison for the `multiSkip` Baseline:** The `multiSkip` baseline is presented as an alternative that uses parallel corpora. However, the authors note that their implementation, unlike some of the works it is based on (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015), does not use monolingual data. This puts it at a significant disadvantage, as it can only learn from the relatively small parallel corpora, which may explain its poor performance in several evaluations. A more direct and equitable comparison would involve either implementing a version that also leverages monolingual data or being more explicit about the limitations of this specific comparison.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Table 1 provides compelling evidence for the superiority of your multilingual evaluation metrics. Could you also report the correlations for the *monolingual* versions of QVEC and QVEC-CCA with the extrinsic tasks? This would directly validate the claim that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC on a fundamental level, independent of the multilingual extension.
2.  Could you please clarify the relationship between your `multiSkip` implementation and the works by Luong et al. (2015b), Coulmance et al. (2015), and Guo et al. (2016)? Section 2.3 primarily details the model from Luong et al., but the initial references also include the other two. Is your implementation a combination, or is it primarily based on one of them?
3.  Regarding the performance drop of `multiCluster` on the 59-language set, could you elaborate on the source of the increased semantic ambiguity? Is it caused by the lower quality of the automatically generated dictionaries (via Google Translate) compared to those extracted from parallel corpora, the larger number of languages leading to more diverse word senses being conflated, or the increased maximum cluster size?
