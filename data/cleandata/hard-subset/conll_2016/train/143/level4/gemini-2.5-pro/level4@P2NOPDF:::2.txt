This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, which is based on clustering, and MultiCCA, which is based on canonical correlation analysis. The authors also propose an improved supersense similarity measure, QVEC-CCA, which enhances the original QVEC by substituting its correlation component with CCA and extending it for multilingual evaluation. The methods are evaluated on a range of tasks using a web portal also developed by the authors, demonstrating competitive performance against two baselines on several tasks.

The paper is well-written, timely, and represents a substantial amount of work. The authors are also to be commended for their meticulous documentation and the development of the accompanying web portal.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its focus on breadth at the expense of depth. While several methods and evaluations are presented, the discussion of the results is often superficial. A more thorough analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, the authors should discuss the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster when using 59 versus 12 languages, and provide more insight into the effects of different parameter choices and design decisions for both MultiCluster and MultiCCA. Consequently, while the practical contributions (the improved QVEC-CCA measure and the web portal) are valuable to the research community, the paper is less successful in addressing and answering specific research questions, which limits the scientific insight one can gain from it.

Below are some more specific comments:

*   To fully substantiate the claim that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC (lines 326-328), the authors should include the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA in Table 1 for a direct comparison.

*   Lines 121 and 147 reference Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. for the MultiSkip baseline, but Section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. Please clarify the relationship between these works and the implemented baseline.

*   The paper provides a reasonable overview of related work, but its contribution would be better contextualized by including several highly relevant publications. The following are suggested:

    **Multilingual Embeddings and Clustering:**
    *   Chandar A P, S., Lauly, S., Larochelle, H., Khapra, M. M., Ravindran, B., Raykar, V. C., & Saha, A. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. In NIPS.
    *   Hill, F., Cho, K., Jean, S., Devin, C., & Bengio, Y. (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv preprint arXiv:1412.6448.
    *   Lu, A., Wang, W., Bansal, M., Gimpel, K., & Livescu, K. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. In NAACL.
    *   Faruqui, M., & Dyer, C. (2013). An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. In ACL.

    **Multilingual Training for Improved Source-Language Embeddings:**
    *   Suster, S., Titov, I., & van Noord, G. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. In NAACL-HLT.
    *   Guo, J., Che, W., Wang, H., & Liu, T. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. In COLING.

    **Translational Context:**
    *   Diab, M., & Resnik, P. (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. In ACL.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 304: The phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" could be misleading. It suggests the datasets are heterogeneous, whereas they appear to be of the same type, just for different languages. Please consider rephrasing for clarity.
*   Page 3: Two equations exceed the column margin.
