This paper presents four methods for learning multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy.

The proposed embedding methods are:
1.  **multiCluster**: Uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters. Embeddings are then learned for these clusters, which in turn serve as the embeddings for the words within them.
2.  **multiCCA**: Extends the bilingual approach of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to the multilingual setting by using English as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (mapping from other languages to English) are used to learn projections from monolingual embedding spaces into this shared anchor space.
3.  **multiSkip**: Adapts the bilingual model of Luong et al. (2015b), which uses both source and target context, by extending the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora simultaneously.
4.  **Translation Invariance**: Employs a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, with an objective function that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. The paper suggests this may only be applicable for bilingual embeddings.

For evaluation, the paper proposes a modified QVEC metric that uses Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and linguistic features. This process yields basis vectors for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. This evaluation method is also extended to the multilingual setting.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the work is described clearly. However, there are several major issues that should be addressed:

1.  The novelty of the translation invariance embedding approach with respect to Gardner et al. is not clear. If the contribution is the extension to multilingual embeddings, a brief explanation of this novelty would strengthen the paper.
2.  The use of super-sense annotations for cross-lingual evaluation is a potential concern. The number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become very small. How do the authors propose to address this problem beyond the brief mention in footnote 9?
3.  The relationship between corpus coverage and performance in Table 2 needs clarification. For example, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA have significantly different coverage but achieve similar scores. This makes it difficult to fairly compare the methods.
4.  The results in Table 3 do not present a clear, consistent picture. For most intrinsic metrics, the proposed multilingual embedding techniques do not appear to perform the best. Given that a primary goal was to create embeddings that perform well on word translation, it is surprising that the bilingual invariance approach performs best by a large margin. Similarly, it is surprising that the multi-cluster approach—which discards fine-grained semantic information—performs best on the extrinsic metrics.

Other questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the dependency parsing task: What is the performance impact of keeping the word embeddings fixed during training? Furthermore, what was the performance gain of using these pre-trained embeddings compared to a baseline with randomly initialized embeddings in the LSTM parser?
2.  Are the results in Table 1 an average over all 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
3.  What are the conceptual or empirical advantages of the joint multi-Skip approach compared to a pipeline approach of first learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project them into a common space?
4.  The dictionaries used in this work are extracted from parallel corpora or Google Translate. This controlled setting may not reflect the challenges posed by real-world lexicons. Did the authors explore using any existing, potentially noisier, multilingual dictionaries?
