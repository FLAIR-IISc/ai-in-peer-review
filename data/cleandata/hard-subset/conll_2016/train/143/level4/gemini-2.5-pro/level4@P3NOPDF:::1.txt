This paper presents a comprehensive study of four methods for inducing multilingual word embeddings and proposes a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The four embedding methods explored are:

1.  **multiCluster:** This method uses a dictionary to map words into multilingual clusters. Embeddings are then learned for these clusters, and all words within a given cluster share the same embedding.
2.  **multiCCA:** This approach extends the bilingual CCA-based method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to the multilingual setting. It uses English as a central "anchor" space and learns projections from other monolingual embedding spaces into this anchor space using bilingual dictionaries.
3.  **multiSkip:** This method adapts the bilingual skip-gram model of Luong et al. (2015b), which uses both source and target contexts. The objective function is extended to simultaneously incorporate signals from all available parallel corpora.
4.  **Translation Invariance:** This approach is based on a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, with an objective function that incorporates bilingual alignment frequencies.

The paper also introduces a multilingual extension of the QVEC evaluation metric, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to measure the correlation between word embeddings and external linguistic feature sets. This produces a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations.

Overall, the paper is well-written, clearly structured, and addresses the important problem of creating and evaluating high-quality multilingual word embeddings. The breadth of the comparative study is a significant strength. However, there are several major points that should be addressed to strengthen the paper's contributions and clarify its conclusions.

**Major Issues**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty (Translation Invariance):** The paper should more clearly articulate its novel contribution with respect to the translation invariance approach of Gardner et al. (2015). If the primary contribution is the extension to the multilingual setting, this should be explicitly stated and briefly elaborated upon, as the current description suggests it may only be suitable for bilingual cases.

2.  **Scalability of Super-Sense Evaluation:** The use of super-sense annotations for evaluation raises a practical concern. As more languages are included in the evaluation, the intersection of available linguistic features (i.e., super-senses that exist across all languages) is likely to shrink significantly. This could lead to an evaluation based on a very small and potentially unrepresentative feature set. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this limitation and potential mitigation strategies beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.

3.  **Analysis of Coverage vs. Performance:** The relationship between embedding coverage and task performance in Table 2 is not fully explored. For instance, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA achieve similar F1 scores (81.0 vs. 80.9) despite having substantially different coverage (98% vs. 87%). An analysis of how coverage impacts the reported metrics would provide valuable context and help readers better interpret the trade-offs between different methods.

4.  **Inconsistent and Counter-intuitive Results:** The experimental results, particularly in Table 3, lack a clear and consistent narrative.
    *   The proposed multilingual methods (multiCCA, multiSkip, multiCluster) are often outperformed by other approaches on the intrinsic evaluation tasks.
    *   Given that a primary motivation is to create embeddings effective for word translation, it is surprising that the bilingual "invariance" approach outperforms all other methods on this task by a very large margin.
    *   Furthermore, the strong performance of the multiCluster method on extrinsic tasks is counter-intuitive. This model discards all fine-grained semantic information between individual words within a cluster, yet it proves to be the most effective for downstream tasks.
    The paper would be significantly enhanced by a discussion of these unexpected outcomes. An analysis of why a seemingly simpler model excels extrinsically, and why dedicated multilingual models lag in intrinsic multilingual tasks, is crucial for understanding the implications of this work.

**Minor Comments and Questions**

1.  **Dependency Parsing Ablation:** For the dependency parsing experiment, what is the performance loss when the word embeddings are kept fixed during training? Additionally, providing a baseline score for the LSTM parser with randomly initialized embeddings would help to better contextualize the gains achieved by using any of the pre-trained embeddings.

2.  **Table 1 Results:** Does Table 1 report scores averaged across all 17 languages described in Section 5.1, or for a specific subset? Please clarify.

3.  **multiSkip vs. Pipeline Approach:** What are the conceptual or empirical advantages of the integrated multiSkip approach over a simpler pipeline? For example, one could learn separate bilingual embeddings for each language pair and then project them into a common space using the multiCCA method. A comparison or discussion would be informative.

4.  **Dictionary Realism:** The dictionaries used in this work are extracted from parallel corpora or machine translation systems. This clean, high-coverage setup may not reflect the challenges of using noisier, pre-existing lexical resources. Have the authors considered evaluating their methods using standard (and potentially less complete) multilingual dictionaries to test their robustness?

In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable and large-scale comparison of multilingual embedding techniques. While the experimental setup is thorough, the paper could be improved by providing deeper analysis and discussion of the results, which currently present a somewhat puzzling picture of the relative merits of each approach. Addressing the issues outlined above would greatly increase the clarity and impact of this work.
