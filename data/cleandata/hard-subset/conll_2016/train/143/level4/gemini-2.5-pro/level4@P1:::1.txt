### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces methods for creating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages within a single, shared vector space. The main contributions are:

1.  **Two new estimation methods:** The paper proposes `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, two methods for training multilingual embeddings that require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, not parallel text. `multiCluster` groups translationally equivalent words into clusters and learns a single vector per cluster. `multiCCA` extends a bilingual CCA-based approach by selecting English as a central "anchor" space and learning projections from other languages into it. These are compared against two baselines: `multiSkip` (a parallel-corpus-based method) and translation invariance.

2.  **An improved evaluation metric:** The authors propose `multiQVEC-CCA`, a new intrinsic evaluation metric that addresses theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC metric. By using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), `multiQVEC-CCA` is invariant to linear transformations of the embedding space and produces a normalized score. The authors empirically demonstrate that it correlates better with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics.

3.  **An evaluation portal and resources:** The paper introduces a web portal for the community to evaluate multilingual embeddings on a suite of standardized intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The data and trained models are also made available, facilitating reproducible research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging task of creating "massively multilingual" embeddings, demonstrating methods that scale to 59 languages. The proposed `multiCluster` and `multiCCA` methods are highly practical, as they rely on dictionaries and monolingual data, which are available for far more languages than the large parallel corpora required by many alternative methods.

2.  **A Principled and Empirically Validated Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of `multiQVEC-CCA` is a significant contribution. It is well-motivated, addressing clear theoretical shortcomings of the prior QVEC metric (i.e., lack of rotational invariance and unnormalized scores). The authors validate its utility by showing in Table 1 that it achieves a much stronger correlation with downstream task performance compared to other commonly used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and word translation.

3.  **Valuable Contribution to the Research Community:** The development and release of an open-source evaluation portal is a commendable effort that significantly benefits the field. By providing a standardized platform for benchmarking, along with access to data and models, the authors lower the barrier to entry for new research, promote reproducibility, and enable fairer comparisons between different embedding techniques.

4.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical study comparing four distinct estimation methods across nine different evaluation metrics. The experiments are conducted on both a smaller set of 12 high-resource languages and a larger set of 59 languages, providing a broad perspective on the performance of each method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent and Puzzling Experimental Results:** The experimental results presented in Table 3 do not offer a clear or consistent narrative. The proposed methods are often outperformed by the translation-invariance baseline on intrinsic evaluation tasks. It is particularly concerning that the baseline method performs best by a large margin on the word translation task, as improving performance on this task is a primary motivation for developing multilingual embeddings.

2.  **Counter-intuitive Extrinsic Task Performance:** The extrinsic results are difficult to interpret. The `multiCluster` method, which is arguably the simplest model—conflating all words in a translational cluster into a single vector and thus losing semantic nuance—emerges as the top performer. This counter-intuitive result suggests that the extrinsic evaluation setup (e.g., freezing embeddings and removing all other linguistic features from the parser) may not be sensitive enough to the fine-grained semantic quality that more sophisticated embeddings are designed to capture.

3.  **Unclear Novelty of the Strongest Baseline:** The translation-invariance method from Gardner et al. (2015) is used as a baseline. However, the paper is unclear about whether this is a novel extension of the original bilingual method to the multilingual setting or simply a direct application. Given its strong performance, if the authors have developed a novel extension, this should be clarified and presented as a contribution in its own right.

4.  **Scalability Concerns for the Proposed Metric:** While `multiQVEC-CCA` is a well-designed metric, its reliance on supersense tag annotations raises concerns about its scalability and applicability. As noted in Footnote 9, these resources are not available for most languages, and creating consistent annotations is a major challenge. As more languages are included in an evaluation, the intersection of available linguistic features could become prohibitively small, limiting the metric's usefulness in a truly "massively multilingual" setting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the dependency parsing experiments, could you quantify the impact of freezing the word embeddings during training? Specifically, what is the performance loss compared to fine-tuning them? Also, how much of a gain do these pre-trained embeddings provide over a standard baseline, such as randomly initialized embeddings, in the stack-LSTM parser?

2.  In Table 3, results for some methods are based on substantially different vocabulary coverage (e.g., document classification scores for `multiCluster` [48.1%] vs. `multiCCA` [62.8%] are identical). How sensitive are the rankings to these differences? Does re-evaluating on the intersection of covered vocabularies change the conclusions?

3.  The dictionaries used were automatically extracted from parallel corpora or generated via Google Translate. Have you explored using existing, manually curated multilingual lexicons (e.g., from Wiktionary or PanLex)? This could test how the methods perform with the different types of coverage and noise present in real-world lexical resources.

4.  Could you please clarify how the correlation coefficients in Table 1 were calculated? Are they computed across the individual scores of the 17 different embedding models for each pair of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks?

5.  The `multiCCA` approach maps all languages to a single anchor (English) space. Could this create a bottleneck or bias, especially for languages typologically distant from English? Have you considered an alternative, such as learning multiple bilingual spaces and then learning transformations between them?
