This paper presents and evaluates four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings, alongside a modified QVEC metric for intrinsic evaluation.

The proposed embedding methods are:
1.  **multiCluster:** This method uses a dictionary to group words from different languages into multilingual clusters. A single embedding is then learned for each cluster, which serves as the representation for all constituent words.
2.  **multiCCA:** This approach extends the bilingual CCA method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to a multilingual setting. It establishes English as a common "anchor" space and learns projection matrices to map monolingual embeddings from other languages into this shared space using bilingual dictionaries.
3.  **multiSkip:** This method adapts the bilingual skip-gram model of Luong et al. (2015b), which utilizes source and target contexts. The objective function is extended to simultaneously incorporate signals from all available parallel corpora in a joint training process.
4.  **Translation Invariance:** Based on Gardner et al. (2015), this approach uses a low-rank decomposition of the PMI matrix, with an objective function that incorporates bilingual alignment frequencies. The paper suggests an extension to the multilingual case, although the details are sparse.

For evaluation, the paper proposes a multilingual extension of the QVEC metric. This intrinsic metric employs Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to measure the correlation between the learned word embeddings and external, manually crafted linguistic features (e.g., supersense tags). The resulting score is invariant to rotation and linear transformations, making it a robust measure of captured linguistic properties.

The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed methods are described with clarity. However, there are several major issues that weaken its contribution.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Unclear Contribution of the Translation Invariance Method:** The primary novelty of the translation invariance approach presented here is not clear. The paper should explicitly detail how the original bilingual method from Gardner et al. is extended to the multilingual setting. Without this explanation, it is difficult to assess the contribution.

2.  **Scalability of the Evaluation Metric:** The reliance on cross-lingual supersense annotations for evaluation is a potential weakness. As the number of languages increases, the intersection of available linguistic features is likely to shrink dramatically. This could limit the applicability and reliability of the evaluation, especially for low-resource languages. The paper should address this scalability concern more directly beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.

3.  **Inconsistent and Unconvincing Experimental Results:** A significant concern is that the experimental results do not present a compelling or consistent argument for the proposed multilingual methods.
    *   In the intrinsic evaluation (Table 3), the bilingual translation invariance method consistently and substantially outperforms the more complex multilingual approaches. This outcome seems to undermine one of the paper's primary motivations, which is to develop superior multilingual embeddings.
    *   In the extrinsic evaluations (dependency parsing), the simple multiCluster method often performs best. This is surprising, as this method discards much of the fine-grained semantic information both within and across languages by collapsing words into discrete clusters.
    *   The lack of a clear winner among the novel multilingual techniques raises questions about their practical benefits over simpler or existing bilingual methods. The results fail to tell a coherent story about when and why one method should be preferred over another.

4.  **Impact of Coverage on Results:** The results in Table 2 are difficult to interpret without understanding the role of embedding coverage. For example, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA achieve similar scores despite having significantly different coverage figures. The authors should clarify how coverage affects performance and whether scores are normalized or are otherwise comparable across methods with varying vocabulary sizes.

**Points for Clarification:**

1.  **Dependency Parsing Experiment:** For the dependency parsing experiments, what was the performance impact of keeping the pre-trained embeddings fixed versus allowing them to be fine-tuned during training? Furthermore, a baseline result showing the gain from using these embeddings over randomly initialized embeddings in the LSTM parser would provide valuable context.

2.  **Table 1 Results:** Please clarify whether the results reported in Table 1 represent an average performance across all 17 language pairs described in Section 5.1.

3.  **multiSkip vs. Pipelined Approach:** What are the theoretical or practical advantages of the joint-training multiSkip approach compared to a simpler pipeline, such as learning separate bilingual embeddings for each language pair and subsequently mapping them into a common space using multiCCA?

4.  **Dictionary Realism:** The dictionaries used in this work were extracted automatically from parallel corpora or machine translation systems. How might the performance of these methods be affected when using real-world lexical resources, which are often noisier and less comprehensive? A discussion or experiment addressing this point would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about applicability.
