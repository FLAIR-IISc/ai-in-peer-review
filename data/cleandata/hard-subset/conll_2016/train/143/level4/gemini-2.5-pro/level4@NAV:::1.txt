This work presents four approaches for generating multilingual word embeddings, alongside a modified QVEC metric designed to assess their quality. The proposed embedding techniques are as follows:
(1) multiCluster: Employs a dictionary to group words into multilingual clusters, with the resulting cluster embeddings serving as the representations for the constituent words.
(2) multiCCA: Adapts the bilingual embedding method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to the multilingual setting by designating English as an anchor space. It then uses bilingual dictionaries to project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this shared space.
(3) multiSkip: Generalizes the work of Luong et al. (2015b), which uses aligned source and target contexts, by modifying the objective function to incorporate signals from all available parallel corpora.
(4) Translation invariance: Relies on a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, guided by an objective function that incorporates bilingual alignment frequencies. The authors note this may be limited to bilingual applications.
The proposed evaluation metric utilizes Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to find the maximal correlation between the learned word embeddings and external linguistic resources. By deriving basis vectors for the aligned dimensions, it produces a quality score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. This evaluation framework is also extended to the multilingual context.

Overall, the paper is well-structured and lucidly presents its contributions. However, several major points require clarification:
(1) Regarding the translation invariance embedding method, what is the specific novel contribution beyond the work of Gardner et al.? If the novelty lies in extending it to a multilingual setting, this should be explicitly stated and briefly explained.
(2) The reliance on super-sense annotations for evaluation across multiple languages seems problematic. The intersection of available features could become vanishingly small as more languages are added. How do the authors plan to mitigate this issue beyond the brief mention in footnote 9?
(3) In Table 2, to what extent does vocabulary coverage influence the reported scores? For instance, in the dependency parsing task, multiCluster and multiCCA achieve similar scores despite having substantially different coverage.
(4) The results presented in Table 3 do not present a clear or consistent narrative. Most of the proposed multilingual techniques are outperformed on intrinsic evaluation metrics. It is particularly concerning that the invariance approach is superior by a large margin on the word translation task, a central motivation for this work. Furthermore, it is surprising that the multi-cluster method, which ignores fine-grained semantic information between clusters, performs best on the extrinsic tasks.

Additional questions for the authors:
(1) For the dependency parsing experiment, what was the performance degradation when the word embeddings were kept fixed during training? Conversely, what was the performance improvement gained by initializing the LSTM parser with these embeddings compared to a random initialization?
(2) Do the values reported in Table 1 represent an average across the 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1?
(3) Are there specific advantages to using the multi-Skip approach over a two-stage pipeline where bilingual embeddings are first learned independently and then mapped to a common space using multi-CCA?
(4) The methods used for dictionary extraction (from aligned parallel text or Google Translate) may not capture the challenges posed by real-world lexical resources. Was the use of existing, potentially noisier, multilingual dictionaries considered or explored?
