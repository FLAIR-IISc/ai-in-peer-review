This paper introduces and evaluates methods for creating and assessing word embeddings across a large number of languages within a unified vector space.

The primary contributions of this work are as follows:
1.  The development of a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of prior work (QVEC) and is shown to correlate more strongly with performance on downstream NLP tasks than other intrinsic evaluation measures.
2.  The creation and release of a public web portal for standardized evaluation, providing a valuable resource to the research community for benchmarking multilingual embeddings.
3.  The introduction of two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel corpora, and an extensive empirical comparison against baseline methods on up to 59 languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Methodological Contribution to Evaluation.** The paper's strongest contribution is the improvement upon existing evaluation metrics. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses key theoretical limitations of QVEC (namely, basis invariance and normalization). The empirical analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that multiQVEC-CCA is a more reliable proxy for downstream performance than commonly used metrics like word translation and similarity, which is a highly valuable finding for the community.

2.  **Valuable Community Resources.** The authors have developed and released a public evaluation portal. This is a significant practical contribution that will lower the barrier for entry, encourage standardized evaluation, and facilitate more reproducible research in this area. This, combined with the release of the trained embeddings and methods, is commendable.

3.  **Comprehensive and Large-Scale Experiments.** The paper presents a substantial empirical study, evaluating four distinct embedding methods across nine different evaluation tasks and for two sets of languages (12 and 59). This breadth of evaluation provides a solid benchmark and offers a wide-angle view of the performance of different approaches to multilingual representation learning.

4.  **Novel and Accessible Embedding Methods.** The paper proposes two novel methods for creating multilingual embeddings. A key advantage of these methods is their reliance on bilingual dictionaries rather than parallel corpora, which are a much scarcer resource. The finding that these dictionary-based methods are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, methods that use parallel data is an important result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Superficial Analysis of Results.** The paper's main weakness is that its analysis lacks depth. While a great deal of experimental results are presented, the discussion rarely moves beyond a surface-level description of which method performed best. For example, the paper notes that multiCluster performs poorly on the 59-language set due to "semantic ambiguity" but does not provide a deeper investigation into this phenomenon or contrast it with the relative robustness of multiCCA. A more thorough analysis of the results would significantly elevate the paper's scientific contribution.

2.  **Lack of Ablation and Parameter Sensitivity Analysis.** The paper does not explore the impact of key design decisions and hyperparameters on the proposed methods. For example, for multiCluster, the effect of the maximum cluster size is not investigated. For multiCCA, the authors use English as the central "pivot" language to project other languages into, but they do not justify this choice or analyze how it might affect the resulting embedding space. This makes it difficult to fully understand the properties and limitations of the proposed methods.

3.  **Insufficient Connection to Related Work.** While the paper cites relevant prior art, the literature review could be more comprehensive. Several highly relevant lines of work are not discussed, including methods based on autoencoders (Chandar et al., 2014), deep correlation models (Lu et al., 2015), or the use of bilingual resources for learning sense-specific embeddings (Guo et al., 2014; Suster et al., 2016). Situating the proposed methods within this broader context would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The central claim regarding the new evaluation metric is that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC. To provide direct evidence for this, could you expand Table 1 to also include the correlations for the monolingual versions of these metrics (i.e., QVEC and QVEC-CCA) against the downstream tasks?

2.  In the introduction and baseline descriptions, you refer to the multiSkip baseline as a variant of Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016). However, the detailed description in Section 2.3 focuses exclusively on the model by Luong et al. (2015b). Could you please clarify the precise relationship between these models and specify which one was implemented for the experiments?

3.  In the multiCCA method, you use English as the pivot language space. What was the reasoning behind this choice beyond data availability? Have you considered the potential impact of this choice, such as inducing an anglocentric bias in the shared space or performing worse for language pairs that are typologically distant from English?
