This paper presents two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, which groups translationally equivalent words, and MultiCCA, which extends bilingual CCA-based projection to a multilingual setting. The authors also propose an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the existing QVEC metric and is shown to correlate better with downstream task performance. Finally, the paper introduces a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The experimental section evaluates the proposed methods against baselines on a wide range of tasks across as many as 59 languages, demonstrating their effectiveness in several settings.

### **Contributions**

1.  **An Improved Evaluation Metric and Analysis:** The paper introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a principled improvement over the existing QVEC metric for evaluating word embeddings. Crucially, the authors demonstrate through a correlation analysis (Table 1) that their proposed metric, along with multiQVEC, is a better predictor of downstream performance on parsing and document classification tasks than existing intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation. This is a valuable contribution to establishing more reliable and meaningful intrinsic evaluation methods for word embeddings.
2.  **A Public Resource for Standardized Evaluation:** The authors have developed and released a web portal that provides standardized datasets and evaluation scripts for a suite of nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This is a significant practical contribution that will facilitate reproducible research and lower the barrier to entry for a fair comparison of new multilingual embedding methods.
3.  **Two Dictionary-Based Multilingual Embedding Methods:** The paper proposes MultiCluster and MultiCCA, two methods for inducing embeddings for a large number of languages (59 in this work) using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, without the need for parallel corpora. The experiments show that these methods are competitive and, in some cases, outperform baselines that rely on parallel data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Practical Value and Community Impact:** The development of the multiQVEC-CCA metric and the public evaluation portal are substantial contributions that will benefit the entire research community. The portal, in particular, addresses a critical need for standardized and reproducible evaluation in this rapidly growing area.
2.  **Impressive Scope and Thoroughness of Experiments:** The authors have conducted a massive amount of work, training and evaluating embeddings across 59 languages on nine different evaluation tasks. The scale of this empirical investigation is commendable and provides a valuable new benchmark for future work.
3.  **Clarity and Meticulous Documentation:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The methods and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail, which, combined with the released code and data, makes the work highly accessible and reproducible.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Breadth at the Expense of Analytical Depth:** While the experimental results are extensive, the discussion lacks a deep analysis of the findings. The paper presents a large collection of results but stops short of explaining them in detail or deriving broader insights. For example, the paper notes the conflicting performance of MultiCluster (competitive for 12 languages but poor for 59) without offering a thorough investigation into why this occurs. Similarly, there is little discussion of the impact of key hyperparameters and design decisions (e.g., cluster size in MultiCluster, choice of projection space in MultiCCA) on the final embeddings.
2.  **Limited New Scientific Insight:** As a consequence of the point above, the paper excels as an engineering and resource contribution but offers limited new scientific takeaways. It successfully demonstrates *that* certain methods perform well on certain tasks, but it does not provide a deeper understanding of *why*. The central research questions feel underexplored, leaving the reader with valuable tools and benchmarks but not necessarily new knowledge about the principles of learning multilingual representations.
3.  **Incomplete Argument for the New Evaluation Metric:** The claim that multiQVEC-CCA is an improvement over multiQVEC would be strengthened by including monolingual correlation results in Table 1. Showing the performance of the original QVEC and the proposed QVEC-CCA on the monolingual downstream tasks would provide a more direct and convincing comparison to validate the improvements claimed in Section 3.3.

### **Additional Comments**

*   **Clarity on MultiSkip Baseline:** The paper first introduces MultiSkip as a variant of Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016), but the detailed description in Section 2.3 only references Luong et al. (2015b). Please clarify the exact relationship between your baseline and these different works.
*   **Minor Wording:** On line 304, the phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" could be misinterpreted. It would be clearer to state that the evaluation aggregates results from datasets of the same type across several language pairs.
*   **Formatting:** The two equations in Section 2.3 appear to exceed the column margin.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The literature review is reasonable, but could be strengthened by including other relevant work on multilingual embeddings and the use of bilingual signals. For instance:
    *   **Autoencoder and Correlation Approaches:**
        *   Chandar et al. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations.
        *   Lu et al. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings.
    *   **Bilingual signals for sense-specific or improved monolingual embeddings:**
        *   Guo et al. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources.
        *   Suster et al. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders.
    *   **Early work on translational context:**
        *   Diab & Resnik (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora.
