This paper presents four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods include: 

(1) multiCluster: This approach utilizes a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, from which cluster embeddings are obtained to serve as embeddings for the words within each cluster.

(2) multiCCA: Building upon the work of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), this method extends bilingual word embeddings to the multilingual case by using English embeddings as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) are employed to obtain projections from other monolingual embeddings for words in other languages to the anchor space.

(3) multiSkip: This approach extends the method presented by Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding using source and target context (via alignment) to the multilingual case by modifying the objective function to incorporate components for all available parallel corpora.

(4) Translation invariance: This method utilizes a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. However, it may only be applicable to bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method employs CCA to maximize the correlation between the word embeddings and possibly hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this to multilingual evaluations.

In general, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the work. However, there are a few major issues that require attention:

(1) The authors should clarify the new contribution of their translation invariance embedding approach with respect to the work of Gardner et al. If the novelty lies in the extension to multilingual embeddings, a brief explanation would be beneficial.

(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages poses a problem, as the number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become significantly small. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how they propose to address this issue beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.

(3) The impact of coverage on the score in Table 2 is unclear. For instance, the coverage numbers for dependency parsing differ significantly between multi-cluster and multiCCA, yet their scores are comparable. The authors should provide more insight into how coverage affects the results.

(4) The results in Table 3 do not present a consistent story. Notably, the multilingual embedding techniques do not perform best for most intrinsic metrics. Given that one of the primary goals of this paper was to create embeddings that perform well under the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the invariance approach performs best by a significant margin. Furthermore, it is surprising that the multi-cluster approach, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, performs best with respect to the extrinsic metrics.

Additional questions for the authors:

(1) What is the loss in performance when fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the gain achieved by simply using these embeddings as alternatives to the random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

(3) Are there any advantages to using the multi-Skip approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across distinct spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction approach (from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate) may not accurately reflect the challenges of using real lexicons. Did the authors explore the use of any real multilingual dictionaries?
