- Paper introduces four multilingual embedding methods (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, Translation Invariance) and a modified QVEC evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA) using CCA to relate embeddings to linguistic data.

- Contributions: two novel dictionary-based methods (multiCluster, multiCCA), adaptation of QVEC to multilingual settings (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

- Strength: clear writing and presentation of the work.

- Strength: multiCluster and multiCCA are novel and empirically promising.

- Strength: multiQVEC-CCA improves on QVEC and yields a more robust evaluation metric.

- Weakness: novelty of the Translation Invariance method relative to Gardner et al. is not clearly explained.

- Weakness: using supersense annotations across languages may produce very few shared features, limiting evaluation.

- Weakness: results (Table 3) are inconsistent and multilingual techniques do not consistently outperform baselines.

- Weakness: embedding coverage affects scores in Table 2, but the influence is not clearly analyzed.

- Question: How much performance is lost by fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing, and what gain occurs by replacing random embeddings in the LSTM parser?

- Question: Is Table 1 averaged over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

- Question: What are the advantages of multi-Skip versus learning bilingual embeddings and then using multi-CCA to project them into a shared space?

- Question: Did you try real multilingual dictionaries instead of extracting dictionaries from parallel corpora or using Google Translate?
