This paper proposes four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are: 

(1) multiCluster: This approach uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, and then obtains cluster embeddings that serve as embeddings for the words in each cluster. 
(2) multiCCA: Building upon the work of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) on bilingual word embeddings, this method extends their approach to multilingual words by using English embeddings as the anchor space. It utilizes bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) to obtain projections from other monolingual embeddings to the anchor space. 
(3) multiSkip: This method extends the approach of Luong et al. (2015b) on embedding words using source and target context (via alignment) to the multilingual case by modifying the objective function to include components for all available parallel corpora. 
(4) Translation invariance: This approach uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. However, it may only be applicable to bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method employs CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and potentially hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this to multilingual evaluations.

In general, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the work. However, there are a few major issues:

(1) The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the novelty of the translation invariance embedding approach, particularly with respect to the work of Gardner et al. A few lines explaining the contribution would be helpful.

(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages is problematic, as the number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become very small. The authors should address this issue more thoroughly, beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.

(3) The impact of coverage on the score in Table 2 is unclear. For example, the dependency parsing results show that multi-cluster and multiCCA have significantly different coverage numbers, yet their scores are close. The authors should provide more insight into how coverage affects the results.

(4) The results in Table 3 are inconsistent. Specifically, the multilingual embedding techniques do not perform best on most intrinsic metrics. Given that one of the primary goals of this paper was to create embeddings that perform well on the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the invariance approach performs best by a significant margin. Furthermore, it is surprising that the multi-cluster approach, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, performs best on the extrinsic metrics.

The authors should also address the following questions:

(1) What is the loss in performance when fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the gain from using these embeddings as alternatives to random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

(3) What are the advantages of using the multi-Skip approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across distinct spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction approach (from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate) may not accurately reflect the challenges of using real lexicons. Did the authors explore the use of any real multilingual dictionaries?
