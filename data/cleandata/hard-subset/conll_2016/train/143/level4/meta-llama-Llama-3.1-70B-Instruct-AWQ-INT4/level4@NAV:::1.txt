This paper presents four approaches for generating multilingual word embeddings, along with a modified QVEC metric to assess their effectiveness. The methods are as follows:
 
(1) multiCluster: This approach utilizes a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, then derives cluster embeddings to serve as representations for the words within each cluster.

(2) multiCCA: Building upon the work of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) for bilingual word embeddings, this method extends the approach to multilingual words by employing English embeddings as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) are used to obtain projections from other monolingual embeddings to the anchor space.

(3) multiSkip: This method expands upon the approach presented by Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding using source and target context (via alignment), extending it to the multilingual case by incorporating components for all available parallel corpora into the objective function.

(4) Translation invariance: This approach uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components, which may only be effective for bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method utilizes CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and possibly handcrafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. This method is also extended to multilingual evaluations.

In general, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the work. However, several major concerns need to be addressed:
 
(1) The authors should clarify the novelty of their translation invariance embedding approach in relation to Gardner et al.'s work. If the contribution lies in the extension to multilingual embeddings, a brief explanation would be beneficial.

(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages poses a problem, as the number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become very small. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how they plan to address this issue beyond the information provided in footnote 9.

(3) The impact of coverage on the score in table 2 is unclear. For instance, the coverage numbers for dependency parsing are significantly different for multi-cluster and multiCCA, yet their scores are close. The authors should provide more insight into this discrepancy.

(4) The results in table 3 do not present a consistent narrative. Notably, the multilingual embedding techniques do not perform the best for most intrinsic metrics. Given the primary goal of this paper was to create embeddings that perform well under the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the invariance approach performs the best by a significant margin. Furthermore, it is surprising that the multi-cluster approach, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, performs the best with respect to the extrinsic metrics.

The authors should also address the following questions:

(1) What is the loss in performance when fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the gain from simply using these embeddings as alternatives to the random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in section 5.1?

(3) What are the advantages of using the multi-Skip approach over learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across the distinct spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction approach (from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate) may not accurately reflect the challenges of using real lexicons. Did the authors explore the use of any real multilingual dictionaries?
