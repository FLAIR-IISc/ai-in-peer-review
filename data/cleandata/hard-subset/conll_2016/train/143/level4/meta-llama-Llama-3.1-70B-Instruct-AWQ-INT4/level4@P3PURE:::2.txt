This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, one motivated by clustering (MultiCluster) and another by canonical correlation analysis (MultiCCA). Additionally, a supersense similarity measure is proposed that improves upon QVEC by substituting its correlation component with CCA and by taking into account multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is performed on a wide range of tasks using the web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform two other baselines in some cases.

I think the paper is very well written and represents a substantial amount of work. The presented representation-learning and evaluation methods are certainly timely, and I applaud the authors for their meticulous documentation. However, my general impression is that the paper may be covering too much breadth at the expense of depth. I would prefer to see a more thorough discussion of results, such as the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, and the effect of estimation parameters and decisions in MultiCluster and CCA. While I believe the paper has high practical value to me and the research community (improved QVEC measure, web portal), I did not gain much insight from reading it in terms of research questions addressed and answered.

Below are some more specific remarks.

It would be beneficial to include the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA as well. After all, it is stated in lines 326-328 that the proposed QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC.

Minor points:

* Line 304: "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" could be rephrased, as it implies that the datasets are of different nature, whereas they are actually of the same kind, just in different languages.
* Page 3: Two equations exceed the column margin.
* Lines 121 and 147 only mention Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when referring to the MultiSkip baseline, but Section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. It would be helpful to clarify the correspondence between these works.
* While the paper does a reasonable job of citing related works, there are additional relevant papers that could be included:
  * Multilingual embeddings and clustering:
    + Chandar et al. (2014): An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. In NIPS.
    + Hill et al. (2014): Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv preprint arXiv:1412.6448.
    + Lu et al. (2015): Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. In NAACL.
    + Faruqui and Dyer (2013): An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. In ACL.
  * Multilingual training of embeddings for the sake of better source-language embeddings:
    + Suster et al. (2016): Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. In NAACL-HLT.
    + Guo et al. (2014): Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. In COLING.
  * More broadly, translational context has been explored, e.g., in:
    + Diab and Resnik (2002): An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. In ACL.
