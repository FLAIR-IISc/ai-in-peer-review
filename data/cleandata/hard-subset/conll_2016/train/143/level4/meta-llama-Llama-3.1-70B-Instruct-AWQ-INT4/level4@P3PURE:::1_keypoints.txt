- Paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (dictionary-based multilingual clusters), multiCCA (projects languages into English anchor space via bilingual dictionaries), multiSkip (multilingual extension of skip-gram with alignment-based contexts), and a translation-invariance method (low-rank PMI decomposition with bilingual alignment components, possibly bilingual-only).

- Authors introduce a modified QVEC evaluation using CCA to align embeddings with linguistic features and extend this invariance-based evaluation to multilingual settings.

- Paper is generally well-written and clearly described.

- Major issue: novelty of the translation-invariance method vs. Gardner et al. is unclear—need clarification if the contribution is just the multilingual extension.

- Major issue: using super-sense annotations across multiple languages may lead to very small intersection feature sets; this concern needs deeper treatment beyond a footnote.

- Major issue: impact of coverage on Table 2 scores is unclear (e.g., multi-cluster vs. multiCCA have similar scores but very different coverage in dependency parsing).

- Major issue: Table 3 results are inconsistent—multilingual methods often do not top intrinsic metrics, the invariance method dominates word-translation despite the paper’s goal, and multiCluster unexpectedly excels on extrinsic metrics despite discarding inter-cluster semantics.

- Question: What is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in dependency parsing, and what gain do these embeddings provide versus random initialization in the LSTM parser?

- Question: Is Table 1 reporting an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

- Question: Are there advantages of multiSkip over learning bilingual embeddings separately and then using multi-CCA to project into a shared space?

- Question: Dictionary extraction from alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real lexicon challenges—did the authors test with real multilingual dictionaries?
