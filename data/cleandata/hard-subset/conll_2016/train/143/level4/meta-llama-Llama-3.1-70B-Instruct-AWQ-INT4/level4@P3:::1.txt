This paper describes four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating the efficacy of these embeddings. The embedding methods are: 

1. multiCluster: Uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters. Cluster embeddings are then obtained which serve as embeddings for the words that reside in each cluster.
2. multiCCA: Extends the approach presented by Faruqui and Dyer (2014) for embedding bilingual words, to multilingual words by using English embeddings as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) are then used to obtain projections from other monolingual embeddings for words in other languages to the anchor space.
3. multiSkip: Extends the approach presented by Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding using source and target context (via alignment), to the multilingual case by extending the objective function to include components for all available parallel corpora.
4. Translation invariance: Uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. May only work for bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method uses CCA to maximize the correlation between the word embeddings and possibly hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions which produce a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this to multilingual evaluations.

**Contributions:**

1. The authors propose two novel dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual embeddings that only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries.
2. The authors adapt QVEC to evaluating multilingual embeddings (multiQVEC) and develop a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that addresses a theoretical shortcoming of multiQVEC.
3. The authors create an easy-to-use web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-written and clearly describes the work.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their methods using various intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
3. The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable resource for the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not clearly explain the novelty of the translation invariance embedding approach with respect to Gardner et al. (2015).
2. The use of supersense annotations across multiple languages may result in a small number of features in the intersection of multiple languages, which could affect the performance of the evaluation method.
3. The results in Table 3 do not tell a consistent story, and the multilingual embedding techniques do not always perform the best.
4. The paper does not discuss the potential loss in performance by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task.

**Questions for the authors:**

1. Can you explain the novelty of the translation invariance embedding approach with respect to Gardner et al. (2015)?
2. How do you propose to address the issue of supersense annotations across multiple languages?
3. Can you provide more insights into the results in Table 3 and explain why the multilingual embedding techniques do not always perform the best?
4. Did you explore the use of real multilingual dictionaries instead of extracting dictionaries from parallel corpora or using Google Translate?
5. What is the loss in performance by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task?
6. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in section 5.1?
7. Are there any advantages of using the multi-Skip approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across the distinct spaces?
