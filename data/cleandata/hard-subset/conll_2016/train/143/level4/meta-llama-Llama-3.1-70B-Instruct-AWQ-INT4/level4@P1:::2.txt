This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, one motivated in clustering (MultiCluster) and another in canonical correlation analysis (MultiCCA). In addition, a supersense similarity measure is proposed that improves on QVEC by substituting its correlation component with CCA, and by taking into account multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is performed on a wide range of tasks using the web portal developed by the authors; it is shown that in some cases the proposed representation methods outperform two other baselines.

The paper is well-written and represents a substantial amount of work. The presented representation-learning and evaluation methods are certainly timely. The meticulous documentation is also commendable. However, the paper's breadth may come at the expense of some depth. A more thorough discussion of results would be beneficial, particularly regarding the conflicting outcome for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, as well as the effect of estimation parameters and decisions in MultiCluster/CCA.

While the paper has high practical value, particularly with the improved QVEC measure and web portal, it could be improved by addressing more research questions and providing more in-depth analysis. Some specific points to consider include:

* Including the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA in Table 1, as the proposed QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC.
* Clarifying the nature of the cross-lingual word similarity datasets used, as the current description may be misleading.
* Ensuring that equations are properly formatted and do not exceed column margins.
* Providing a clearer correspondence between the works mentioned in the MultiSkip baseline, specifically Coulmance et al., Guo et al., and Luong et al.
* Considering additional relevant works on multilingual embeddings and clustering, such as those by Chandar et al., Hill et al., Lu et al., Faruqui et al., and Suster et al.

Summary of the paper:

The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, MultiCluster and MultiCCA. It also introduces a supersense similarity measure that improves on QVEC. The evaluation is performed on a wide range of tasks using a web portal developed by the authors.

Main contributions claimed by the authors:

* Two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, MultiCluster and MultiCCA.
* A supersense similarity measure that improves on QVEC.
* A web portal for evaluating multilingual word embeddings.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes two new methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The supersense similarity measure is an improvement over QVEC and can be used for multilingual evaluation.
* The web portal developed by the authors is a valuable resource for the research community.

Weaknesses:

* The paper's breadth may come at the expense of some depth, and a more thorough discussion of results would be beneficial.
* The conflicting outcome for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups is not fully explained.
* The effect of estimation parameters and decisions in MultiCluster/CCA is not fully explored.

Questions to authors:

* Can you provide more insight into the conflicting outcome for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups?
* How do the estimation parameters and decisions in MultiCluster/CCA affect the results?
* Can you clarify the correspondence between the works mentioned in the MultiSkip baseline, specifically Coulmance et al., Guo et al., and Luong et al.?
