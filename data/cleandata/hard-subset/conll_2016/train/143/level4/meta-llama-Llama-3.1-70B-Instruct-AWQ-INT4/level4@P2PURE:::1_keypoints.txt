- Proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (dictionary → multilingual clusters; cluster embeddings as word embeddings).
- multiCCA: extends Faruqui & Dyer by using English as an anchor and projecting other monolingual embeddings via bilingual dictionaries.
- multiSkip: extends Luong et al. by modifying the objective to include source/target context components from all available parallel corpora.
- Translation invariance: low-rank PMI decomposition with bilingual alignment-frequency terms; may be applicable only to bilingual embeddings.
- Evaluation: modified QVEC using CCA to align embeddings with linguistic features, yielding rotation- and linear-invariant multilingual scores.
- Paper is well-written and clearly described.
- Major issue: novelty of the translation invariance approach is unclear relative to Gardner et al.; needs clearer explanation of the contribution.
- Major issue: using super-sense annotations across multiple languages is problematic because the intersection of features can become very small; needs more thorough treatment than a footnote.
- Major issue: unclear how coverage affects scores in Table 2—e.g., multiCluster and multiCCA have different coverage but similar scores; needs analysis.
- Major issue: inconsistent results in Table 3—translation invariance dominates intrinsic metrics despite goals, and multiCluster unexpectedly performs best on extrinsic tasks; requires explanation.
- Question: what is the loss in performance when fixing embeddings in dependency parsing, and what gain do these embeddings provide over random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?
- Question: is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
- Question: what are the advantages of multiSkip versus learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multi-CCA to project spaces?
- Question: did the authors evaluate using real multilingual dictionaries (vs. dictionaries extracted from alignments or Google Translate), and if not, why?
