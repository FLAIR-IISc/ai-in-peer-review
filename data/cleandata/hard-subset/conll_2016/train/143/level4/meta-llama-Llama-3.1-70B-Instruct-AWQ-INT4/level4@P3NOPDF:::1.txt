This paper presents four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are: 

(1) multiCluster: This approach uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters. Cluster embeddings are then obtained, serving as embeddings for the words that reside in each cluster. 

(2) multiCCA: Extending the approach presented by Faruqui and Dyer (2014) for embedding bilingual words, this method uses English embeddings as the anchor space for multilingual words. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) are then used to obtain projections from other monolingual embeddings for words in other languages to the anchor space.

(3) multiSkip: This method extends the approach presented by Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding using source and target context (via alignment) to the multilingual case. It does this by extending the objective function to include components for all available parallel corpora.

(4) Translation invariance: This approach uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. However, it may only be suitable for bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method employs CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and possibly hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this to multilingual evaluations.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly describes the work. However, there are a few major issues that need to be addressed:

Firstly, it is unclear what the new contribution is with respect to the translation invariance embedding approach of Gardner et al. If the novelty lies in the extension to multilingual embeddings, a few explanatory lines would be helpful.

Secondly, the use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages poses a problem, as the number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become very small. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how they propose to address this issue, beyond the brief mention in footnote 9.

Thirdly, the impact of coverage on the score in Table 2 is unclear. For instance, for dependency parsing, multi-cluster and multi-CCA have significantly different coverage numbers yet achieve similar scores.

Lastly, the results in Table 3 do not present a consistent story. Notably, for most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual embedding techniques do not seem to perform the best. Given that one of the primary goals of this paper was to create embeddings that perform well under the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the method that performs best (by far) is the invariance approach. Furthermore, it is surprising that the multi-cluster approach, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, performs the best with respect to extrinsic metrics.

The authors should also consider addressing the following questions:

1. What is the loss in performance by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the gain by simply using these embeddings as alternatives to random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in section 5.1?

3. Are there any advantages of using the multi-Skip approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across distinct spaces?

4. The dictionary extraction approach (from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate) may not reflect the challenges of using real lexicons. Did the authors explore the use of any real multilingual dictionaries?

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting contribution to the field of multilingual word embeddings, it requires further clarification and addressing of the issues mentioned above to strengthen its impact.
