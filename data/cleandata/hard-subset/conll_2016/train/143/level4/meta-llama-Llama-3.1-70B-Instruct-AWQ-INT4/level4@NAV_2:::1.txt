This paper presents four methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are: (1) multiCluster, which uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters and obtains cluster embeddings that serve as embeddings for the words in each cluster; (2) multiCCA, which extends the approach of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) for embedding bilingual words to multilingual words by using English embeddings as the anchor space; (3) multiSkip, which extends the approach of Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding using source and target context to the multilingual case; and (4) Translation invariance, which uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components.

The evaluation method uses CCA to maximize the correlation between the word embeddings and possibly hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are obtained for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this to multilingual evaluations.

The main contributions of this work are the two dictionary-based estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and the evaluation method, multiQVEC-CCA. The authors also provide a web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a clear and well-structured description of the methods and evaluation metrics.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics.
3. The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable resource for the research community.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the novelty of the translation invariance embedding approach with respect to Gardner et al. (2015).
2. The use of supersense annotations across multiple languages may result in a small number of features in the intersection of multiple languages. The authors should address this limitation and propose a solution.
3. The coverage of the embeddings affects the score in Table 2, and the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of this effect.
4. The results in Table 3 do not tell a consistent story, and the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of these results.

Questions to Authors:

1. What is the loss in performance by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the gain by simply using these embeddings as alternatives to the random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?
2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
3. Are there any advantages of using the multi-Skip approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and performing multi-CCA to learn projections across the distinct spaces?
4. Did you explore the use of any real multilingual dictionaries instead of extracting dictionaries from parallel corpora or using Google Translate?
