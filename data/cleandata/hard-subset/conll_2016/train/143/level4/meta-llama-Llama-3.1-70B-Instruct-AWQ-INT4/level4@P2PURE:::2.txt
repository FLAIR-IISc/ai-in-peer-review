This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, namely MultiCluster, motivated by clustering, and MultiCCA, based on canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors introduce a supersense similarity measure that improves upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and considering multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is conducted on a wide range of tasks using a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform two other baselines in some cases.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have invested a substantial amount of work in the presented representation-learning and evaluation methods, which are certainly timely. The meticulous documentation is also praiseworthy.

However, my overall impression is that the paper may have sacrificed some depth for the sake of breadth. A more thorough discussion of the results would be beneficial, particularly regarding the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, as well as the effect of estimation parameters and decisions in MultiCluster/CCA. While I acknowledge the paper's high practical value to me and the research community, including the improved QVEC measure and the web portal, I did not gain significant insights from reading it in terms of research questions addressed and answered.

Some specific remarks are as follows:

It would be informative to include the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA as well, given that the proposed QVEC-CCA is stated to be an improvement over QVEC (lines 326-328).

Minor issues include:

* Line 304: The phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" might be rephrased, as it implies that the datasets are of different nature, whereas they are actually of the same kind, just in different languages.
* Page 3: Two equations exceed the column margin.
* Lines 121 and 147 only mention Coulmance et al and Guo et al when referring to the MultiSkip baseline, but section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. It would be helpful to clarify the correspondence between these works.

While the paper provides reasonable coverage of related works, there are additional relevant studies that could be included, such as:

* Multilingual embeddings and clustering:
  + Chandar et al. (2014) - An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations.
  + Hill et al. (2014) - Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation.
  + Lu et al. (2015) - Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings.
  + Faruqui and Dyer (2013) - An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering.
* Multilingual training of embeddings for better source-language embeddings:
  + Suster et al. (2016) - Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders.
  + Guo et al. (2014) - Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources.
* More broadly, translational context has been explored in:
  + Diab and Resnik (2002) - An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora.
