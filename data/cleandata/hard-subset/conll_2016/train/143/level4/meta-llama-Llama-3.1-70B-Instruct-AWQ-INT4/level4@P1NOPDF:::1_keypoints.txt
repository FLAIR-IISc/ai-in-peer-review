- Paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and a translation-invariance approach.
- multiCluster: maps words to multilingual clusters via a dictionary and uses cluster embeddings as word embeddings.
- multiCCA: projects non-English embeddings into an English anchor space using bilingual dictionaries (extension of Faruqui & Dyer).
- multiSkip: extends bilingual source/target context objectives to multilingual settings by summing over available parallel corpora.
- Translation-invariance: low-rank decomposition of PMI with bilingual alignment terms; may be limited to bilingual embeddings.
- Proposes a modified QVEC-like evaluation using CCA to align embeddings with linguistic annotations, extended to multilingual evaluation.
- Paper is generally well-written and clear.
- Major issue 1: Clarify novelty of the translation-invariance method relative to Gardner et al., especially whether multilingual extension is the contribution.
- Major issue 2: Super-sense annotations across languages may yield very small intersection feature sets; propose solutions beyond footnote 9.
- Major issue 3: Coverage differences (Table 2) affect scores but their impact is unclear (e.g., dependency parsing coverage vs. score discrepancies).
- Major issue 4: Results (Table 3) are inconsistent—multilingual methods often do not win intrinsic metrics, invariance method dominates word-translation, and multiCluster unexpectedly excels on extrinsic metrics.
- Question 1: What performance loss occurs when fixing embeddings in the dependency parser, and what gain from replacing random embeddings with these pretrained ones?
- Question 2: Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
- Question 3: Any advantage of multiSkip versus learning bilingual embeddings then applying multi-CCA to align spaces?
- Question 4: Did the authors test with real multilingual dictionaries (instead of extracted alignments or Google Translate), and how realistic are their dictionary extraction methods?
