This paper describes four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are: (1) multiCluster, which uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters and then learns cluster embeddings; (2) multiCCA, extending Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multilingual settings by using English embeddings as an anchor space and projecting other languages onto it via bilingual dictionaries; (3) multiSkip, extending Luong et al. (2015b) to the multilingual case by incorporating parallel corpora from multiple languages into the objective function; and (4) Translation invariance, utilizing a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies. The evaluation method employs CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data, with extensions for multilingual evaluations. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly describes the proposed methods. However, several points require clarification and further attention.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the accompanying web portal provide a valuable resource for the community. The paper demonstrates a clear need for improved evaluation metrics and offers a well-motivated and tested alternative to existing methods.
2. **Scaling dictionary-based methods to a large number of languages:**  While dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings exist, this work systematically extends and evaluates them to 59 languages, representing a substantial scaling effort. 
3. **Empirical analysis of intrinsic vs. extrinsic evaluation:** The thorough correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics provides insightful observations about the reliability of different evaluation methods in predicting downstream task performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation across multiple metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic) and datasets, demonstrating the robustness of their analysis. The inclusion of both monolingual and cross-lingual evaluation tasks is particularly valuable.
2. **Practical Resource:** The web portal is a significant contribution. It facilitates reproducible research and enables researchers to easily evaluate their own multilingual embeddings using a standardized set of metrics and datasets.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The paper clearly explains the mathematical formulations of each method, allowing for straightforward implementation and replication.
4. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The authors provide comprehensive details about the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings, data preprocessing, and evaluation procedures. This level of detail is crucial for reproducibility.
5. **Identifying Limitations of Existing Metrics:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the limitations of existing intrinsic evaluation metrics and motivates the development of multiQVEC-CCA.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Translation Invariance Extension:** The extension of the translation invariance approach to multiple languages is not sufficiently elaborated upon. A more detailed explanation of the modifications and the challenges faced during the extension is needed to justify its inclusion as a separate method.
2. **Supersense Tag Coverage Issues:** The reliance on supersense tags across languages raises concerns about data sparsity and potentially skewed results. While the authors acknowledge this in a footnote, a more substantive discussion of mitigation strategies is needed.  Simply stating that the intersection of features may become small is insufficient.
3. **Coverage Effects in Results:** The impact of vocabulary coverage on the evaluation results is not adequately addressed. The differing coverage between methods, particularly for dependency parsing, complicates the interpretation of the results in Table 2. A more nuanced analysis, potentially including results using a common vocabulary, would be beneficial.
4. **Inconsistent Results & Interpretation:** The results in Table 3 present a somewhat contradictory picture. The performance of the proposed methods on intrinsic metrics is not always superior to the baseline, and the unexpectedly strong performance of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks requires further investigation. The authors should delve deeper into *why* multiCluster performs well on extrinsic tasks despite discarding inter-cluster semantic information.
5. **Limited Discussion of Dictionary Quality:** The paper lacks a discussion on the quality and potential biases of the dictionaries used for training. The impact of using machine-translated dictionaries versus curated multilingual dictionaries is not explored.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dependency Parsing Ablation:** What is the performance degradation when fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What was the improvement gained by using these pretrained embeddings compared to random initialization?
2. **Table 1 Correlation Details:** Is Table 1 presenting the average correlation across all 17 embeddings tested in Section 5.1?  If so, please clarify this in the caption.
3. **MultiSkip vs. Multi-CCA Rationale:** Are there specific scenarios where multiSkip would be preferred over learning bilingual embeddings followed by multi-CCA? What are the computational tradeoffs?
4. **Real-World Lexicons:** Did you explore the use of any curated, real-world multilingual dictionaries (e.g., Open Multilingual Wordnet) in your experiments? If not, why? How might the results change with higher-quality dictionaries?
5. **MultiCluster performance explanation:** What is the hypothesized reason for the strong performance of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks, given its discarding of inter-cluster semantic information?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the vocabulary coverage for each method and task.
*   The reference list is extensive but could benefit from more recent publications on multilingual embeddings.
* The phrase "intra-language" on page 26 doesn't seem quite right. Perhaps "cross-lingual word translation" would be more appropriate.
