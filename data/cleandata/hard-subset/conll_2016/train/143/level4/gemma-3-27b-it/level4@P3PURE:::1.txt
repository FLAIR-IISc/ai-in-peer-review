This paper describes four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are: (1) *multiCluster*, which uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, deriving cluster embeddings that serve as representations for the words within each cluster; (2) *multiCCA*, an extension of the bilingual embedding approach by Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to the multilingual setting, leveraging English embeddings as an anchor space and using bilingual dictionaries (other language → English) to project monolingual embeddings into this space; (3) *multiSkip*, which extends the alignment-based embedding approach of Luong et al. (2015b) to multiple languages by incorporating parallel corpora components into the objective function; and (4) *Translation invariance*, which employs a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective function including bilingual alignment frequencies – though this may be limited to bilingual embeddings. The evaluation method utilizes CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and, potentially, hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are derived for the aligned dimensions, resulting in a score invariant to rotation and linear transformations, which is then extended to multilingual evaluations. 

In general, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the presented work. However, several major issues warrant attention:

(1) What is the novel contribution of the translation invariance embedding approach compared to the work of Gardner et al.? If the extension to multilingual embeddings constitutes the novelty, a brief explanation would be beneficial.

(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages presents a potential problem. The intersection of features across languages may become impractically small. Beyond the note in footnote 9, how do the authors propose to address this issue?

(3) To what extent does coverage affect the scores reported in Table 2? For instance, in the dependency parsing results, *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* exhibit significantly different coverage numbers yet achieve comparable scores.

(4) The results in Table 3 do not present a consistently clear picture. Notably, for most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual embedding techniques do not appear to outperform baseline methods. Given the paper’s stated goal of creating embeddings that perform well under the word translation metric (intra-language), it is somewhat disappointing that the translation invariance approach achieves the best performance by a significant margin. Furthermore, the superior performance of *multiCluster* with respect to the extrinsic metrics is surprising, given that it discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information. 

Further questions for the authors:

(1) What is the performance loss incurred by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What gain was observed by substituting these embeddings for the random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

(3) Are there any advantages to employing the *multiSkip* approach over learning bilingual embeddings and subsequently performing multi-CCA to learn projections between the distinct embedding spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction approach, based on alignments from parallel corpora or Google Translate, may not accurately reflect the challenges of utilizing real-world lexicons. Did the authors investigate the use of any existing multilingual dictionaries?
