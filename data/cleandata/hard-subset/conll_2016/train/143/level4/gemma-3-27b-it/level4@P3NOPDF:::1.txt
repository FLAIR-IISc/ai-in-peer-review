This paper presents four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings – multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance – alongside a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The embedding methods leverage dictionary information, parallel corpora, and word co-occurrence statistics to map words across languages into a shared vector space. The evaluation framework utilizes Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to assess alignment with linguistic properties, introducing a rotation and translation-invariant score suitable for multilingual comparisons. Overall, the paper is clearly written and describes the methodologies effectively. However, several key issues require clarification and further discussion.

First, the contribution of the Translation Invariance approach, particularly in comparison to prior work by Gardner et al., needs to be explicitly highlighted. While the extension to multilingual embeddings is a natural progression, a concise explanation of the novelty within the paper would strengthen its presentation.

Second, the application of super-sense annotations across multiple languages raises concerns about data sparsity. The intersection of super-sense tags across languages is likely to be limited, potentially impacting the reliability of the evaluation. While Footnote 9 acknowledges this issue, a more detailed discussion of strategies to mitigate this problem – beyond simply noting its potential effect – is necessary. The authors should address how they handled or accounted for this sparsity in their analysis.

Third, the results presented in Table 2 warrant further investigation regarding the influence of coverage. The observed proximity in scores between multiCluster and multiCCA for dependency parsing, despite significantly differing coverage rates, is perplexing. A discussion on the relationship between coverage and performance in this context would provide valuable insight. Do the methods compensate for lower coverage in other ways?

Fourth, the overall narrative conveyed by the results in Table 3 is inconsistent. Contrary to expectations, the multilingual embedding techniques do not consistently outperform baseline approaches across the intrinsic metrics. Given the paper’s stated goal of creating embeddings that excel in word translation (intra-language), the superior performance of the Translation Invariance method is somewhat disappointing. Furthermore, the strong performance of multiCluster on extrinsic metrics, despite its disregard for inter-cluster semantic information (across words and languages), requires explanation. Why does discarding this information lead to better extrinsic results?

Beyond these major points, several questions remain:

1. Regarding the dependency parsing task, what performance degradation resulted from fixing the pre-trained word embeddings? Conversely, what was the quantifiable benefit gained by substituting these embeddings for random initialization within the LSTM stack parser? Understanding the magnitude of these changes will better contextualize the effectiveness of the learned embeddings.

2. Table 1 appears to present aggregated results. Is this an average across all 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1? Clarifying the aggregation method is essential for accurate interpretation.

3. What advantages does the multiSkip approach offer over the more straightforward strategy of learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA for projection between embedding spaces? A comparative analysis of the benefits and drawbacks of each approach would be beneficial.

4. The dictionary extraction process, relying on alignments from parallel corpora or Google Translate, may not accurately reflect the complexities of utilizing genuine multilingual lexicons. Did the authors consider incorporating or evaluating their methods with established, manually curated multilingual dictionaries? Exploring this would enhance the practical relevance of their work.



In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual word embeddings. However, addressing the points raised above – particularly regarding novelty, data sparsity, coverage effects, and the interpretation of results – would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. A more in-depth discussion of these issues and a more nuanced analysis of the experimental results are recommended.
