This paper introduces methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and a novel evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, designed to assess their quality. The authors propose four embedding techniques: multiCluster, which leverages dictionary-based clustering; multiCCA, an extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) using English embeddings as an anchor; multiSkip, a multilingual adaptation of Luong et al. (2015b)’s parallel corpus method; and translation invariance, based on low-rank decomposition of word co-occurrence matrices. The evaluation framework centers on multiQVEC-CCA, a correlation-based method intended to address limitations of previous metrics and extend them to multiple languages.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) a comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual word embeddings, particularly the multiQVEC-CCA metric which demonstrates improved correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing intrinsic methods; (2) a publicly available web portal and benchmark datasets to facilitate research in this area, promoting reproducibility and comparability; and (3) a practical demonstration of dictionary-based methods for generating multilingual embeddings, covering a substantial number of languages (59).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Thorough Evaluation Framework:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA represents a significant step forward in evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors identify shortcomings in existing metrics and offer a theoretically sound and empirically validated alternative. The correlation analysis presented in Table 1 convincingly demonstrates the improved relationship between multiQVEC-CCA and downstream task performance. 
2. **Resource Provision & Reproducibility:**  The authors' commitment to reproducibility is commendable. The release of the web portal, benchmark datasets, and code for their methods provides a valuable resource to the research community, accelerating progress in multilingual NLP. This accessibility is crucial for fostering collaboration and building upon this work.
3. **Large-Scale Multilingual Coverage:**  The application of these methods to 59 languages is ambitious and notable.  While the performance variations are explored, the sheer scale of coverage distinguishes this work from many prior efforts limited to smaller language sets. The exploration of dictionary-based methods for resource-poor languages is particularly valuable.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experiments. The organization is logical, and the technical details are presented in a readily understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** The core embedding techniques – multiCluster, multiCCA, and multiSkip – largely adapt existing approaches to the multilingual setting. The incremental improvements, while valuable, do not represent groundbreaking methodological innovations. The translation-invariance approach also appears less thoroughly integrated into the multilingual framework.
2. **Inconsistent Results and Unclear Interpretations:** The experimental results, especially in Table 3, lack a clear and consistent narrative. The fact that the translation invariance method outperforms the proposed methods on intrinsic tasks, and multiCluster performs surprisingly well on extrinsic tasks despite its simplification, raises questions about the interplay between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation and the underlying mechanisms driving performance. A more in-depth discussion of these discrepancies is needed.
3. **Sensitivity to Dictionary Quality and Coverage:** The reliance on bilingual dictionaries, particularly those created via machine translation, raises concerns about data quality and potential biases.  The authors acknowledge this in footnote 9 but lack a thorough investigation of how dictionary quality impacts performance. The influence of coverage on evaluation scores, especially given the variations across languages, is also not adequately addressed.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies & Comparative Analyses:** The paper lacks detailed ablation studies examining the impact of individual design choices within the proposed methods. Furthermore, a more comprehensive comparison with strong baseline methods beyond those presented is warranted to firmly establish the relative advantages of the proposed approaches.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Regarding the translation invariance method, could you elaborate on the specific adaptations made for multilingual embeddings and quantify the contributions of this extension relative to the original bilingual method?
2. The paper mentions potential issues with supersense tagging across languages. What strategies could be employed to mitigate the impact of low intersection in features, beyond simply acknowledging the problem?  Could alternative linguistic resources be explored?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between embedding coverage and evaluation scores, particularly for the dependency parsing task? How much do variations in coverage contribute to the observed differences in performance?
4.  The extrinsic task results with multiCluster are unexpected given the simplification of semantic information. Can you provide hypotheses to explain this result, and explore whether similar effects are observed with different downstream tasks?
5.  What is the computational cost of each of the methods, and how does this scale with the number of languages? This would be helpful for researchers considering applying these methods to very large language sets.



**Additional Comments:**

* Table 1 seems to show correlation values between intrinsic and extrinsic tasks over 17 embeddings. Is this correct? Providing further details about those 17 embeddings (hyperparameter settings, exact data used, etc.) would be beneficial.
*  Exploring the use of existing high-quality multilingual dictionaries could provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed methods.
* Clarifying the loss in performance from fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task, and the gains from using them instead of random embeddings would be valuable.
