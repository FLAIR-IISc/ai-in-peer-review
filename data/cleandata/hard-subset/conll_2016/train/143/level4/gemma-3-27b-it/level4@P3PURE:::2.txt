This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, motivated by clustering techniques, and MultiCCA, based on canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors present a supersense similarity measure that improves upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is performed across a range of tasks using a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform two baselines in certain scenarios. 

The paper is well-written and represents a substantial body of work. The presented representation-learning and evaluation methods are timely, and the meticulous documentation is commendable. However, my overall impression is that the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. I would appreciate a more thorough discussion of the results – for example, regarding the conflicting performance of MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, and concerning the effect of estimation parameters and design choices within MultiCluster and MultiCCA. While I believe the paper holds high practical value for both myself and the research community (particularly through the improved QVEC measure and the accessible web portal), I found the insights gained from reading it to be limited in terms of addressing and answering novel research questions.

Below are some more concrete remarks.

Including the correlation results (Table 1) for both monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA would be beneficial, especially given the claim in lines 326-328 that QVEC-CCA represents an improvement over QVEC. 

Minor comment: Line 304 states "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets,” which suggests datasets of differing natures, while they appear to be datasets of the same kind, differing only by language.

On page 3, two equations exceed the column margin.

Lines 121 and 147 mention Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when referencing the MultiSkip baseline, but section 2.3 only cites Luong et al. Clarification of the correspondence between these works is needed.

While the paper provides reasonable coverage of related work, several relevant contributions could be included:

Multilingual embeddings and clustering:
Chandar A P, S., Lauly, S., Larochelle, H., Khapra, M. M., Ravindran, B., Raykar, V. C., and Saha, A. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. In NIPS.
Hill, F., Cho, K., Jean, S., Devin, C., and Bengio, Y. (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv preprint arXiv:1412.6448.
Lu, A., Wang, W., Bansal, M., Gimpel, K., & Livescu, K. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. In NAACL.
Faruqui, M., & Dyer, C. (2013). An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. In ACL.

Multilingual training of embeddings for the sake of better source-language embeddings:
Suster, S., Titov, I., and van Noord, G. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. In NAACL-HLT.
Guo, J., Che, W., Wang, H., and Liu, T. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. In COLING.

More broadly, the exploration of translational context, as demonstrated in Diab, M., & Resnik, P. (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. In ACL, might also be relevant.
