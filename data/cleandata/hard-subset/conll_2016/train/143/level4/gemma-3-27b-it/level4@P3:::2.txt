This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, MultiCluster and MultiCCA. Additionally, it introduces a supersense similarity measure, multiQVEC-CCA, designed to improve upon QVEC by employing canonical correlation analysis instead of correlation and extending the evaluation to multiple languages. The evaluation is performed across a variety of tasks, facilitated by a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods sometimes outperform baseline approaches.

The paper is well-written and represents a significant body of work. The proposed representation learning and evaluation methods are timely and practically valuable, particularly the improved QVEC measure and the accompanying web portal. However, the paper feels broad in scope at the expense of depth. A more thorough discussion of results – for example, regarding the differing performance of MultiCluster with 59 versus 12 languages, and the impact of estimation parameters on MultiCluster/CCA – would strengthen the contribution. While valuable to the community, the paper doesn't present a strong narrative of novel research questions addressed and answered.

The primary contribution of this work is the development and release of the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric and the associated web portal. While the proposed embedding methods (MultiCluster and MultiCCA) are a contribution, they lack substantial novelty compared to existing techniques. The evaluation infrastructure, facilitating reproducible research in multilingual embeddings, is the most significant aspect of this submission.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Improved Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The development of multiQVEC-CCA directly addresses limitations of existing evaluation metrics (QVEC), providing a more robust and theoretically sound method for assessing the quality of multilingual embeddings. The reported correlations with downstream tasks (Table 1) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of this metric, showing substantially higher correlation with extrinsic tasks than prior methods. This is a significant step towards better evaluation practices in the field.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Public Resource (Web Portal):** The authors have undertaken a comprehensive evaluation of their methods and baselines across several intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.  More importantly, the creation and public release of the evaluation portal is a substantial contribution. This resource will greatly benefit the research community by providing a standardized platform for evaluating multilingual embeddings, facilitating reproducibility and accelerating progress in the field. The availability of data and trained embeddings further enhances the utility of this resource.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined and relatively thorough, including comparisons to multiple baselines and analyses on varying language sets (12 and 59).  The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., vocabulary coverage) and attempt to mitigate them through analysis.  The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation provides a well-rounded assessment of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Embedding Methods:** While MultiCluster and MultiCCA are reasonable approaches, they build upon existing techniques (clustering and CCA) and do not present substantial algorithmic innovations. The results do not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage of these methods over established baselines across all tasks, especially considering the complexity added.  The conflicting results between the 59-language and 12-language setups for MultiCluster raise concerns about the robustness and scalability of the method.
2. **Insufficient Depth of Analysis:** The paper lacks a deep dive into the results. The authors acknowledge the need for further analysis of the performance discrepancies between MultiCluster with different language sets, but do not provide such analysis. A more detailed exploration of the impact of different parameter settings and design choices within MultiCluster and MultiCCA would strengthen the paper. The discussion of the results feels somewhat superficial.
3. **Presentation of Results & Completeness:** Table 1 should include the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA, as stated in line 326-328 the latter is an improvement over the former and should be presented for proper comparison. There are minor clarity issues (e.g., l.304 phrasing) and formatting inconsistencies (equations exceeding column margin). The explanation of the relationship between Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. with Luong et al. (lines 121 & 147, section 2.3) is unclear and needs clarification.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance differences between MultiCluster when trained on 59 languages versus 12 languages? What factors contribute to the observed discrepancies, and what steps could be taken to improve the scalability and robustness of MultiCluster?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific algorithmic choices within MultiCCA and MultiCluster that have the most significant impact on performance?  Would an ablation study on these choices be feasible?
3.  What considerations were given to the choice of the English supersense tag annotations for QVEC/QVEC-CCA, and what are the potential limitations of using annotations from a single language for evaluating multilingual embeddings?
