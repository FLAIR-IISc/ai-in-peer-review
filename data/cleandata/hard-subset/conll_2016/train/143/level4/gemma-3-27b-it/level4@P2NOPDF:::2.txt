This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for generating multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, inspired by clustering techniques, and MultiCCA, based on canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors propose an enhanced supersense similarity measure, building upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The methods are evaluated across a diverse set of tasks using a web portal created by the authors, demonstrating performance gains over several baselines in certain scenarios. 

The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates a significant research effort. The proposed representation learning and evaluation methodologies are highly relevant and timely. The meticulous documentation, particularly the publicly available web portal, is commendable. 

However, my overall impression is that the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. While the range of explored methods and evaluations is impressive, the discussion of the results feels somewhat superficial. I would appreciate a more in-depth analysis of the findings, particularly concerning the contrasting performance of MultiCluster in the 59-language and 12-language setups, and a more detailed investigation into the impact of parameter choices and implementation decisions within MultiCluster and MultiCCA. Despite its high practical value – notably the improved QVEC measure and the valuable web portal resource – I found myself learning relatively little new theoretical insight from this work regarding the research questions investigated and answered.

Here are some specific suggestions for improvement:

*   **QVEC-CCA Correlation Results:** Including the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA (as presented in Table 1) would strengthen the claim of improvement asserted in lines 326-328.
*   **Clarity of Baseline Attribution (l.304):** The phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (l.304) could be misinterpreted as suggesting datasets of different *types*. It would be more accurate to clarify that these datasets differ only in *language*, not in fundamental nature.
*   **MultiSkip Baseline Consistency (Lines 121, 147, Section 2.3):** The attribution of the MultiSkip baseline is inconsistent. Lines 121 and 147 cite both Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., while Section 2.3 only references Luong et al. Clarifying the correspondence between these works is necessary.
*   **Related Work Expansion:** While the paper provides a reasonable overview of related work, several relevant publications could be included to provide a more comprehensive context. Specifically, consider the following:

    *   **Multilingual Embeddings and Clustering:**
        *   Chandar et al. (2014) – An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations.
        *   Hill et al. (2014) – Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation.
        *   Lu et al. (2015) – Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings.
        *   Faruqui & Dyer (2013) – An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering.
    *   **Multilingual Training for Improved Source-Language Embeddings:**
        *   Suster et al. (2016) – Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders.
        *   Guo et al. (2014) – Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources.
    *   **Leveraging Translational Context:**
        *   Diab & Resnik (2002) – An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora.
