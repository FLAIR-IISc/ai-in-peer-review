This paper describes four methods for obtaining multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their effectiveness. The embedding methods are:

(1) **multiCluster:** This approach uses a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, then derives cluster embeddings that serve as embeddings for the words within each cluster.

(2) **multiCCA:** This extends the bilingual embedding approach of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to the multilingual case, using English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other language → English) are employed to project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this anchor space.

(3) **multiSkip:** This expands upon the approach presented by Luong et al. (2015b) for embedding words using source and target context (via alignment) to the multilingual setting. It achieves this by extending the objective function to incorporate components from all available parallel corpora.

(4) **Translation invariance:** This method utilizes a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective function that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. However, it may be limited to bilingual embeddings. 

The evaluation method leverages CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and potentially hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors are derived for the aligned dimensions, producing a score that is invariant to rotation and linear transformations. The proposed method also extends this evaluation to a multilingual setting.

In general, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the work. However, several major issues require attention:

(1) What is the novel contribution of this work with respect to the translation invariance embedding approach of Gardner et al.? If the key novelty is the extension to multilingual embeddings, a few lines explicitly stating this would be beneficial.

(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages presents a challenge. The number of features in the intersection across languages may become very small. Beyond footnote 9, how do the authors propose to address this data sparsity issue?

(3) How does coverage affect the scores presented in Table 2? For instance, in dependency parsing, *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* have significantly different coverage numbers, yet their scores are comparable.

(4) The results in Table 3 do not present a consistently clear picture. Specifically, for most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual embedding techniques do not appear to outperform baselines. Given that a primary goal of this paper was to create embeddings that perform well on the word translation metric (intra-language), it’s disappointing that the translation invariance approach performs best by a considerable margin. It is also surprising that *multiCluster*, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, performs best with respect to the extrinsic metrics. 

Further questions for the authors:

(1) What performance loss is incurred by fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What gain was observed by using these embeddings as alternatives to random embeddings within the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

(3) Are there advantages to using the *multiSkip* approach instead of learning bilingual embeddings and then applying *multiCCA* to learn projections across the resulting spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction approach—whether from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate—may not fully reflect the challenges of utilizing real-world lexicons. Did the authors explore the use of any existing, curated multilingual dictionaries?
