This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, motivated by clustering techniques, and MultiCCA, based on canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors propose a novel supersense similarity measure that refines QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The evaluation leverages a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform several baselines in certain scenarios. 

The paper is well-written, represents a significant body of work, and presents timely representation learning and evaluation methods. The authors’ meticulous documentation is also commendable. However, my overall impression is that the work prioritizes breadth over depth. I would have appreciated a more in-depth discussion of the results, particularly regarding the conflicting performance of MultiCluster on the 59- and 12-language setups, and the impact of estimation parameters and design choices in both MultiCluster and MultiCCA. Despite its high practical value – specifically the improved QVEC measure and the publicly available web portal – I found the paper less insightful in terms of addressing and answering fundamental research questions.

Here are some more specific observations:

Including the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA would be beneficial, given the claim (lines 326-328) that QVEC-CCA represents an improvement over QVEC. 

Minor point: line 304 states “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets,” which implies datasets of differing types, when in fact they represent the same type of data across different languages.

On page 3, two equations extend beyond the column margin. 

Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when referencing the MultiSkip baseline, yet Section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. Clarification regarding the relationship between these works is needed.

While the paper provides a reasonable overview of related work, several relevant publications could be included:

*   Multilingual embeddings and clustering: Chandar et al. (2014), Hill et al. (2014), Lu et al. (2015), and Faruqui & Dyer (2013).
*   Multilingual training of embeddings for improved source-language embeddings: Suster et al. (2016) and Guo et al. (2014).
*   More broadly, the exploration of translational context, such as in Diab & Resnik (2002), would also be relevant.
