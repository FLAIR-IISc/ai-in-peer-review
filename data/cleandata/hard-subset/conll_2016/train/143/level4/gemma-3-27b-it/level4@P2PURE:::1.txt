This paper presents four methods for learning multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their effectiveness. The embedding methods are: (1) *multiCluster*, which maps words to multilingual clusters using a dictionary and then represents words with the embeddings of their respective clusters; (2) *multiCCA*, an extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) that leverages English embeddings as an anchor space and employs bilingual dictionaries (other language to English) to project monolingual embeddings into this space; (3) *multiSkip*, which generalizes the alignment-based approach of Luong et al. (2015b) to multiple languages by extending the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora; and (4) *Translation invariance*, which utilizes a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective function incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies, although its applicability may be limited to bilingual embeddings. The evaluation metric employs CCA to maximize the correlation between word embeddings and linguistic data (potentially hand-crafted), deriving basis vectors for aligned dimensions to yield a score invariant to rotation and linear transformations, and extending this concept to multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and well-organized. However, several key issues require attention:

(1) The paper lacks a clear statement of its novel contributions regarding the translation invariance embedding approach originally proposed by Gardner et al. If the primary novelty lies in extending this method to a multilingual setting, a concise explanation would be beneficial.

(2) Utilizing super-sense annotations across multiple languages presents a challenge due to the potential for drastically reduced feature intersection. The authors briefly address this in footnote 9, but a more detailed discussion of how they propose to mitigate this problem is needed.

(3) The impact of coverage on the scores presented in Table 2 is unclear. For instance, in dependency parsing, *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* exhibit significantly different coverage levels despite achieving comparable scores.

(4) The results in Table 3 are somewhat inconsistent. Notably, the multilingual embedding techniques generally do not outperform other methods on most intrinsic metrics. Given the paper's stated goal of creating embeddings that excel in word translation (intra-language performance), it is concerning that the invariance approach performs best by a substantial margin. Furthermore, the strong performance of *multi-cluster*, which discards inter-cluster semantic information (relating words and languages), on extrinsic metrics is unexpected.

Further questions for the authors:

(1) What performance decrease results from fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? Conversely, what gain was observed by substituting these embeddings for random embeddings within the LSTM stack parser?

(2) Is Table 1 an average computed across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

(3) What advantages does the *multi-Skip* approach offer compared to learning bilingual embeddings and then applying *multi-CCA* to establish projections between the resulting spaces?

(4) The dictionary extraction method, employing either alignments from parallel corpora or Google Translate, may not accurately reflect the complexities of working with real-world lexicons. Did the authors investigate the use of any existing, manually-curated multilingual dictionaries?
