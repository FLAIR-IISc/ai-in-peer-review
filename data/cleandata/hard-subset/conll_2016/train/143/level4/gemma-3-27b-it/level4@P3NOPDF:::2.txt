This paper presents two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings – MultiCluster, motivated by clustering techniques, and MultiCCA, based on canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors propose an improved supersense similarity measure, QVEC-CCA, which builds upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is comprehensive, utilizing a web portal developed by the authors, and demonstrates that the proposed methods outperform several baselines in specific scenarios.

Overall, this is a well-written and substantial piece of work that addresses a timely topic in representation learning. The authors are to be commended for the meticulous documentation of their methods and the provision of a publicly accessible evaluation platform. However, while the paper demonstrates practical value through the improved QVEC measure and the web portal, my overall impression is that it prioritizes breadth over depth. I would have appreciated a more thorough discussion of the results, particularly regarding the seemingly contradictory performance of MultiCluster in the 59-language versus 12-language setups, and a deeper analysis of the impact of parameter choices and design decisions within MultiCluster and MultiCCA. 

Despite this, I believe the paper will be valuable to the research community. The advancements to QVEC and the availability of the evaluation portal are particularly noteworthy. However, I found myself learning less about underlying research questions and their answers than I had hoped for.

Below are some specific comments and suggestions for improvement:

*   **QVEC-CCA Comparison:** Including the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA would strengthen the justification for claiming an improvement with QVEC-CCA (as stated in lines 326-328).
*   **Dataset Description (l.304):** The phrase "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" implies heterogeneity in dataset type, when they are in fact comparable datasets in different languages. Consider rephrasing to clarify this point.
*   **MultiSkip Baseline Consistency:** Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when discussing the MultiSkip baseline, while Section 2.3 only references Luong et al. Please clarify the relationship between these works and the specific implementation of MultiSkip used.
*   **Related Work:** While the paper provides a reasonable overview of related work, several relevant publications could be included to provide a more complete context. Specifically, consider adding the following:
    *   Chandar et al. (2014) – An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations.
    *   Hill et al. (2014) – Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation.
    *   Lu et al. (2015) – Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings.
    *   Faruqui & Dyer (2013) – An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering.
    *   Suster et al. (2016) – Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders.
    *   Guo et al. (2014) – Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources.
    *   Diab & Resnik (2002) – An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora (to represent broader translational context approaches).
*   **Formatting:** Equations on page 3 exceed the column margin and should be adjusted for better readability.



In conclusion, this paper is a valuable contribution with practical benefits. Addressing the suggestions above, particularly a more in-depth analysis of the results and a more comprehensive review of related work, would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
