This paper introduces four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and a refined QVEC metric for their evaluation. The proposed embedding methods are: (1) *multiCluster*, which maps words to multilingual clusters and represents each word with its cluster’s embedding; (2) *multiCCA*, an extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) that leverages English embeddings as an anchor space and projects embeddings from other languages using bilingual dictionaries; (3) *multiSkip*, which expands on Luong et al. (2015b) by incorporating parallel corpora from multiple languages into the skip-gram objective function; and (4) *Translation invariance*, based on a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies (potentially limited to bilingual scenarios). The evaluation methodology employs Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data, deriving rotation- and linear transformation-invariant scores, and extending this approach to multilingual evaluations.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and effectively explains the proposed techniques. However, several key issues require clarification.

First, the novelty of the *Translation invariance* approach, particularly its extension to multilingual embeddings, needs to be more explicitly articulated. A brief discussion comparing it to the work of Gardner et al. would be valuable.

Second, the use of cross-lingual super-sense annotations raises concerns about feature sparsity due to potentially small intersections across languages. While footnote 9 acknowledges this issue, a more detailed explanation of how the authors address this challenge is necessary. 

Third, the influence of coverage on the reported results in Table 2 warrants investigation. The near-identical scores for *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* in the dependency parsing task, despite significant differences in their coverage, are particularly noteworthy and require explanation.

Fourth, the results presented in Table 3 are somewhat inconclusive. Surprisingly, the multilingual embedding techniques generally do not outperform baselines on intrinsic evaluation metrics. Given the paper’s objective to develop embeddings excelling in word translation (intra-language performance), the superior performance of the *Translation invariance* approach is unexpected. Similarly, the strong performance of *multiCluster* on extrinsic metrics, despite discarding inter-cluster semantic information, is counterintuitive and requires further discussion.

Further clarification is requested on the following points:

1. What performance degradation results from fixing word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? Conversely, what gain was achieved by simply replacing random embeddings with the proposed embeddings within the LSTM stack parser?

2. Is Table 1 a result averaged across all 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1? Please confirm.

3. What specific advantages does *multiSkip* offer compared to learning bilingual embeddings and then applying *multiCCA* to learn projections between embedding spaces?

4. The method of dictionary extraction – either from parallel corpora alignments or Google Translate – may not fully reflect the complexities of utilizing real-world lexicons. Did the authors explore the incorporation of existing, manually curated multilingual dictionaries into their evaluation?
