This paper introduces four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings—multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation invariance—and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their quality. The methods are: (1) multiCluster, which leverages a dictionary to map words into multilingual clusters and uses cluster embeddings to represent words within those clusters; (2) multiCCA, an extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) that utilizes English embeddings as an anchor space and projects other monolingual embeddings into this space via bilingual dictionaries; (3) multiSkip, which adapts the alignment-based approach of Luong et al. (2015b) to a multilingual setting by extending the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora; and (4) Translation invariance, based on low-rank decomposition of a word PMI matrix with bilingual alignment frequency components, potentially limited to bilingual embeddings. The evaluation utilizes CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data, employing basis vectors for rotation- and transformation-invariant scoring, and extending this to multilingual evaluations.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed work. However, several key concerns require addressing:

(1) The novelty of the Translation invariance approach, particularly concerning its extension to multilingual embeddings in relation to the work of Gardner et al., needs clearer articulation. A brief explanation would significantly strengthen the contribution.

(2) The use of cross-lingual super-sense annotations presents a challenge due to potential feature sparsity resulting from limited intersection across languages. The authors’ proposed solution (beyond what is stated in footnote 9) warrants further elaboration.

(3) The influence of coverage on the scores presented in Table 2 should be investigated. The comparable scores of multiCluster and multiCCA, despite substantial differences in coverage for the dependency parsing task, raise questions about the metric’s sensitivity to this factor.

(4) The results in Table 3 are somewhat inconclusive. The multilingual embedding techniques generally do not outperform baseline approaches on most intrinsic metrics. Given the paper’s objective to create embeddings excelling in word translation (intra-language performance), the superior performance of the invariance approach is unexpected. Similarly, the strong performance of multiCluster on extrinsic metrics, despite discarding inter-cluster semantic information, is surprising and requires explanation.

Further clarification is requested regarding:

(1) The performance trade-offs associated with fixing word embeddings during the dependency parsing task, and the specific benefits realized by using the proposed embeddings instead of random initialization within the LSTM stack parser.

(2) The nature of Table 1—specifically, whether it represents an average across the 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1.

(3) The advantages of using multiSkip compared to learning bilingual embeddings followed by multi-CCA for projection across embedding spaces.

(4) The potential limitations of using dictionaries extracted from parallel corpora or Google Translate, and whether the authors considered incorporating real-world multilingual dictionaries into their evaluation.
