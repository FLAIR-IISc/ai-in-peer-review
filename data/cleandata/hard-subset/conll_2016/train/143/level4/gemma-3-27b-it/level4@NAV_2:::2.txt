This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings, MultiCluster and MultiCCA. Additionally, a supersense similarity measure is proposed that improves upon QVEC by substituting its correlation component with CCA and by incorporating multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is conducted across a range of tasks using a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform two baselines in certain scenarios.

I find this paper to be well-written and represents a substantial body of work. The presented representation learning and evaluation methods are timely, and I commend the authors for their meticulous documentation. However, my overall impression is that the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. I would appreciate a more thorough discussion of the results—for instance, concerning the conflicting performance of MultiCluster across the 59- and 12-language setups, and regarding the impact of estimation parameters and choices within MultiCluster/CCA. While I believe the paper holds high practical value for myself and the research community (particularly the improved QVEC measure and the web portal), I did not gain significant new research insights from reading it in terms of addressed and answered research questions. 

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are (1) the development of a publicly available and comprehensive evaluation suite – including an improved metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal – for multilingual embeddings, which will be invaluable for future research; (2) a demonstration of the effectiveness of dictionary-based approaches, specifically multiCCA, for creating multilingual embeddings, particularly when leveraging a large number of languages; and (3) a thorough empirical evaluation of several embedding techniques across multiple languages and tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Suite:** The development and release of the evaluation portal and the improved multiQVEC-CCA metric are strong assets. The portal will significantly facilitate reproducible research in the field, and the metric appears to correlate better with downstream tasks than existing alternatives. This contribution addresses a clear need within the community for standardized and reliable evaluation procedures.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors performed extensive experiments across a large number of languages and tasks, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of their methods. The detailed results, even when showing nuanced differences between approaches, contribute valuable insights into the performance characteristics of each method. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations adds to the robustness of the findings.
3.  **Practical Dictionary-Based Approach:** MultiCCA consistently performs well, especially when applied to a large number of languages. This suggests that dictionary-based methods, when carefully implemented, can be a viable alternative to parallel corpora-dependent methods, opening avenues for creating multilingual embeddings in resource-scarce settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of In-Depth Analysis:** While the paper presents a wealth of results, the analysis often feels superficial. For example, the conflicting results of MultiCluster across different language set sizes (59 vs. 12) are mentioned but not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed examination of the impact of hyperparameters and design choices would strengthen the conclusions.
2.  **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of components is valuable, many individual techniques (e.g., CCA, skipgram models) are well-established. The paper does not deeply explore novel aspects of these techniques or propose significant algorithmic improvements. The contribution lies more in the comprehensive application and evaluation of existing methods within a multilingual context.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the different methods. Understanding *where* and *why* the methods fail could provide valuable insights for future research and method refinement. A qualitative assessment of the embeddings generated by different approaches would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA, as stated in line 326-328 the latter is an improvement over the former?
2.  Regarding the contrasting performance of MultiCluster in the 59-language vs. 12-language setup, what hypotheses do you have for this discrepancy? Have you investigated the impact of cluster size or dictionary quality on this difference?
3.  In the description of MultiSkip (Section 2.3), the correspondence between Luong et al. and Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. is unclear. Could you clarify which parts of MultiSkip are derived from each of these works?
4.  The paper states that “the vocabulary used in two sets of word embeddings is different” potentially biasing the evaluation. How was the vocabulary intersection selected/constructed and can you comment on the potential bias introduced by this selection?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Line 304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” might be better phrased as “a combination of cross-lingual word similarity datasets for different language pairs,” as they are of the same type.
*   Equations on page 3 exceed the column margin.
