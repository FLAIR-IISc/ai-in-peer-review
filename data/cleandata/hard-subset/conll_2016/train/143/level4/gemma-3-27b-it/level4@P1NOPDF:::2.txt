This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings: one based on clustering (MultiCluster) and another on canonical correlation analysis (MultiCCA). Additionally, the authors introduce a supersense similarity measure that improves upon QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The evaluation is performed across a range of tasks using a web portal developed by the authors, demonstrating that the proposed representation methods outperform two baselines in certain scenarios.

I found the paper to be very well written and represent a substantial body of work. The presented representation learning and evaluation methods are certainly timely, and I commend the authors for their meticulous documentation. 

However, my overall impression is that the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. I would have appreciated a more thorough discussion of the results—for example, regarding the inconsistent performance of MultiCluster between the 59-language and 12-language setups, and concerning the impact of estimation parameters and design choices in MultiCluster and MultiCCA. While I believe the paper holds high practical value for both myself and the research community—specifically through the improved QVEC measure and the accessible web portal—I didn’t feel I gained significant new insights from reading it in terms of addressing and answering key research questions. 

Here are some more specific comments:

Including the correlation results (Table 1) for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA would be beneficial. After all, the paper states (lines 326–328) that QVEC-CCA is an improvement over QVEC.

Minor comment: Line 304, “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” could be misinterpreted as implying datasets of different *types*. They are, in fact, datasets of the same kind, simply for different languages, correct?

On page 3, two equations extend beyond the column margin.

Lines 121 and 147 mention Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when referring to the MultiSkip baseline, but Section 2.3 only cites Luong et al. Clarification of the correspondence between these works would be helpful.

While the paper provides a reasonable overview of related work, several relevant publications could be included:

**Multilingual embeddings and clustering:**

Chandar A P, S., Lauly, S., Larochelle, H., Khapra, M. M., Ravindran, B., Raykar, V. C., and Saha, A. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. In *NIPS*.
Hill, F., Cho, K., Jean, S., Devin, C., and Bengio, Y. (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. *arXiv preprint arXiv:1412.6448*.
Lu, A., Wang, W., Bansal, M., Gimpel, K., & Livescu, K. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. In *NAACL*.
Faruqui, M., & Dyer, C. (2013). An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. In *ACL*.

**Multilingual training of embeddings for the sake of better source-language embeddings:**

Suster, S., Titov, I., and van Noord, G. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. In *NAACL-HLT*.
Guo, J., Che, W., Wang, H., and Liu, T. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. In *COLING*.

More broadly, the use of translational context has been explored in:

Diab, M., & Resnik, P. (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. In *ACL*.
