- Paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance.
- multiCluster groups words into multilingual clusters via a dictionary and assigns cluster-level embeddings to all words in a cluster.
- multiCCA projects monolingual embeddings into an English anchor space using bilingual dictionaries, extending Faruqui & Dyer (2014) to multilingual.
- multiSkip generalizes Luong et al. (2015b) by extending the bilingual source/target context alignment objective to all available parallel corpora.
- Translation Invariance uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with bilingual alignment frequency terms, but may be limited to bilingual settings.
- Evaluation uses a modified QVEC/CCA framework to align embeddings with linguistic annotations, producing rotation- and linear-invariant scores and extended to multilingual evaluation.
- Paper is well-written and methods are clearly explained.
- Concern: Novelty of the Translation Invariance method is unclear relative to Gardner et al.; any multilingual extension should be explicitly clarified.
- Concern: Super-sense annotations across languages may be very sparse when intersected; the paper only briefly addresses this (footnote 9).
- Concern: Coverage differences (Table 2) may affect reported scores — need clarification why methods with very different coverage yield comparable parser performance.
- Concern: Inconsistent results in Table 3 — multilingual methods often fail on intrinsic metrics, while Translation Invariance dominates translation tasks and multiCluster unexpectedly excels on extrinsic tasks.
- Question: What is the performance loss when fixing embeddings in dependency parsing, and the gain when replacing random embeddings with these in the LSTM parser?
- Question: Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
- Question: What are the advantages of multiSkip versus learning bilingual embeddings then applying multiCCA projections across languages?
- Question: Dictionary extraction via parallel alignments or Google Translate may not mirror real-world lexicons — did the authors test with real multilingual dictionaries?
