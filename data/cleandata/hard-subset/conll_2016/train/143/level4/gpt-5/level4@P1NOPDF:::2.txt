This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: a clustering-inspired approach (MultiCluster) and a canonical correlation analysis approach (MultiCCA). It also introduces a supersense-based evaluation measure that extends QVEC by replacing its correlation component with CCA (QVEC-CCA) and by supporting multilingual evaluation. The authors evaluate across a wide range of tasks via their web portal and show that, in some settings, their representations outperform two baselines.

The paper is clearly written and reflects substantial, timely work. I especially appreciate the careful documentation and the public evaluation portal. That said, the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. I would have liked a deeper analysis of the results—e.g., an explanation of the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59- and 12-language setups, and a more thorough discussion of how estimation choices and hyperparameters affect both MultiCluster and MultiCCA. While the improved QVEC-CCA measure and the web portal are of high practical value, I learned less than I hoped in terms of concrete research questions and definitive takeaways.

More specific remarks:

- It would be useful to add monolingual correlation results for QVEC and QVEC-CCA to Table 1. Since lines 326–328 claim QVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC, showing the monolingual comparison would help substantiate the claim alongside the multilingual results.

Minor comments:

- Line 304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” reads as if the datasets are of different types; they are the same type across different languages, correct? Consider rephrasing for clarity.
- Page 3: two equations exceed the column margin; please fix formatting.
- Lines 121 and 147 reference Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when discussing the MultiSkip baseline, whereas Section 2.3 only mentions Luong et al. Please clarify how these works correspond and which specific implementation or variant you evaluate as “MultiSkip.”

Multilingual embeddings and clustering:
- Chandar A P, S., Lauly, S., Larochelle, H., Khapra, M. M., Ravindran, B., Raykar, V. C., and Saha, A. (2014). An autoencoder approach to learning bilingual word representations. NIPS.
- Hill, F., Cho, K., Jean, S., Devin, C., and Bengio, Y. (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv:1412.6448.
- Lu, A., Wang, W., Bansal, M., Gimpel, K., and Livescu, K. (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. NAACL.
- Faruqui, M., and Dyer, C. (2013). An Information Theoretic Approach to Bilingual Word Clustering. ACL.

Multilingual training to improve source-language embeddings:
- Suster, S., Titov, I., and van Noord, G. (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. NAACL-HLT.
- Guo, J., Che, W., Wang, H., and Liu, T. (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. COLING.

More broadly on translational context:
- Diab, M., and Resnik, P. (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. ACL.

Overall, this is a strong and useful paper with solid engineering contributions and community resources. To strengthen the scientific narrative, I encourage:
- A deeper error and sensitivity analysis of MultiCluster and MultiCCA (e.g., impact of dictionary coverage/quality, choice of clustering criteria, CCA dimensionality, regularization, and preprocessing).
- A clearer account of why MultiCluster’s performance diverges between the 59- and 12-language setups (e.g., effects of language coverage, domain mismatch, or noise in lexical resources).
- More explicit articulation of research questions and hypotheses, with corresponding analyses that produce concrete, generalizable insights beyond the observed task-wise performance.
