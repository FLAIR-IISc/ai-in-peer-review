This paper presents four approaches for learning multilingual word embeddings and proposes a modified QVEC evaluation metric designed to be invariant to rotation and linear transformations, with a multilingual extension.

Summary of methods:
- multiCluster: Uses a bilingual dictionary to map translationally equivalent words into multilingual clusters and assigns the same embedding to all words in a cluster by training cluster-level embeddings.
- multiCCA: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual settings by selecting English as an anchor space and learning linear projections from other languages into that space using bilingual dictionaries.
- multiSkip: Generalizes Luong et al. (2015b) to the multilingual case by incorporating aligned source/target context across all available parallel corpora in the objective.
- Translation invariance: Factorizes the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency terms; appears primarily applicable to bilingual embeddings.

Evaluation: The paper introduces a CCA-based variant of QVEC that aligns embedding dimensions with linguistic features (including hand-crafted resources) and computes a correlation score invariant to rotation and other linear transforms. The approach is also extended to evaluate multilingual embeddings.

Strengths:
- Clear presentation of multiple multilingual embedding methods within a unified framework.
- Careful attempt to address evaluation pitfalls by proposing a rotation-invariant CCA-based metric and extending it to multilingual settings.
- Broad empirical evaluation across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

Major concerns and requests for clarification:
1) Novelty relative to translation invariance work (e.g., Gardner et al.): It is unclear what is new in your formulation beyond prior translation-invariance approaches. If the contribution is a multilingual extension, please make the novelty explicit: What is changed in the objective, constraints, or training regime to support more than two languages? Are there theoretical or empirical properties unique to your variant?

2) Reliance on supersense annotations across languages: Using the intersection of features across multiple languages risks a very small feature space, which can bias both training and evaluation. Beyond the brief mention in footnote 9, how do you mitigate feature sparsity? For example:
- Do you back off to language-specific features plus a shared core?
- Do you use cross-lingual projection of features or semi-supervised expansion?
- How sensitive are results to the size of the cross-lingual feature intersection?

3) Coverage effects in extrinsic evaluation (Table 2): Coverage differs notably across methods (e.g., multiCluster vs. multiCCA) while scores are close. Please quantify how coverage impacts performance. Suggestions:
- Report results on the intersection of covered tokens across methods.
- Provide performance as a function of coverage (e.g., bucketed by token frequency).
- Include a controlled ablation where training data is restricted to tokens covered by all methods.

4) Inconsistent story in Table 3: The intrinsic metrics often do not favor the multilingual techniques, and the translation-invariance approach dominates the word translation task despite the paper’s focus on multilingual embeddings. Additionally, multiCluster—despite collapsing inter-word and cross-language distinctions—performs best on several extrinsic tasks. Please discuss:
- Why does multiCluster work well extrinsically despite its coarse representation?
- Why do more sophisticated multilingual methods not consistently outperform monolingual/bilingual baselines on intrinsic metrics?
- Are there domain, frequency, or morphology effects that explain these patterns?
- Can you provide significance tests to assess whether differences are reliable?

Additional questions for the authors:
- Parsing with fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings: What is the performance loss from freezing embeddings in the dependency parsing experiments? Conversely, how much gain do you obtain when using your embeddings solely as initialization compared to random initialization in the LSTM stack parser? Please include both settings to clarify the practical utility of the embeddings.

- Table 1 aggregation: Is Table 1 an average over all 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1? If not, please specify the averaging protocol, weighting, and variance (e.g., per-language standard deviations).

- multiSkip vs. bilingual+multiCCA pipeline: What are the empirical and practical advantages of training a full multilingual skip-gram model relative to training bilingual embeddings and then projecting them into a shared space via multiCCA? Consider data requirements, computational cost, robustness to noisy alignments, and downstream performance.

- Dictionaries and realism: The dictionaries induced from parallel corpora (via alignments) or from Google Translate may not reflect real lexicon noise, coverage gaps, or sense granularity. Did you test with any curated multilingual dictionaries (e.g., PanLex, Wiktionary, or proprietary lexicons)? If not, can you discuss expected robustness to dictionary quality and coverage?

- Resource details: Please report, per language pair, dictionary sizes, coverage on evaluation sets, parallel corpus sizes, alignment quality metrics, and OOV rates. This will help interpret variability across tasks and languages.

- Multilingual QVEC variant: Provide more detail on the construction of the multilingual linguistic matrix, how features are shared/aligned across languages, and how you handle language-specific features. It would also be useful to report correlations per language and their variance.

- Statistical testing: For both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, include significance testing across runs or bootstrapped samples to support claims of superiority.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses an important problem and is clearly written, with a valuable attempt to improve evaluation via a rotation-invariant CCA-based metric. The comparative study of multiple multilingual embedding methods is timely. However, the empirical narrative is currently mixed, and several methodological details—especially regarding the translation-invariance contribution, coverage effects, and the construction of multilingual linguistic features—need clarification. Strengthening the analysis (coverage controls, significance tests, fine-tuning vs. freezing) and expanding the discussion of surprising findings (e.g., multiCluster’s strong extrinsic performance) would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
