- multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA are proposed intrinsic metrics that correlate much better with downstream tasks than word similarity or translation metrics.  
- multiCCA and multiCluster provide scalable multilingual embedding estimation without parallel corpora using bilingual dictionaries and monolingual corpora (tested up to 59 languages).  
- The authors release a public evaluation portal, code, and data to standardize multilingual embedding evaluation.  
- Strong empirical evidence: multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA show substantially higher correlations with document classification and parsing than existing intrinsic metrics.  
- Large-scale, broad experiments: embeddings trained/evaluated for 59 languages with detailed 12-language comparisons and controlled extrinsic tasks.  
- Practical dictionary-based methods suit low-resource settings; multiCCA is simple, effective, and achieves strong coverage.  
- Reproducibility and community value through a web portal, standardized datasets, and released software with separate dev/test modes.  
- Clear presentation and acknowledgment of evaluation caveats (coverage effects, clustering ambiguity).  
- Unclear novelty for the translation-invariance baseline; need clarification if any multilingual adaptation was introduced.  
- Reliance on multilingual supersense annotations limits generality due to sparse/inconsistent resources and may bias evaluation.  
- Coverage differences between methods confound comparisons; results may reflect coverage rather than embedding quality.  
- Mixed and counterintuitive metric outcomes (e.g., translation-invariance best on word translation, multiCluster best on extrinsic tasks) need reconciliation and analysis.  
- multiSkip baseline may be disadvantaged because it was trained only on parallel data, unlike related work that uses monolingual augmentation.  
- Question: Clarify whether the translation-invariance baseline includes any methodological novelty for multilingual use.  
- Question: How do you handle small intersections of supersense annotations across languages; consider normalizing tagsets, using universal tags, or projection strategies?  
- Question: Can you report coverage-normalized or intersection-based scores (e.g., for 59-language parsing) and quantify absolute/relative changes?  
- Question: What is the performance difference when parsers fine-tune embeddings, and do method rankings/correlations hold under fine-tuning?  
- Question: Are Table 1 correlations computed over the 17 embedding instances from §5.1, and can you provide confidence intervals or significance tests?  
- Question: Does training bilingual embeddings on parallel data then aligning with multiCCA ever outperform direct multiSkip—trade-offs in coverage, stability, and cost?  
- Question: Did you evaluate curated multilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary) and how robust are methods to noise in automatically constructed dictionaries?  
- Overall: Solid, timely paper with the intrinsic metrics as the most compelling contribution; large-scale experiments and released resources add significant value.  
- Recommended improvements: clarify novelty of translation-invariance baseline, analyze coverage effects systematically, and address limitations of supersense-based evaluation.
