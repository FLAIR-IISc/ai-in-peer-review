This paper presents four approaches to constructing multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for assessing their effectiveness. The embedding methods are:
(1) multiCluster: Relies on a dictionary to group words into multilingual clusters. Embeddings are learned for clusters and assigned to all words within each cluster.
(2) multiCCA: Generalizes Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual by treating English as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language -> English) are used to project monolingual embeddings from other languages into the anchor.
(3) multiSkip: Extends Luong et al. (2015b), which leverages source and target contexts via alignments, to the multilingual setting by augmenting the objective with terms spanning all available parallel corpora.
(4) Translation invariance: Performs a low-rank factorization of the word PMI matrix with an objective that incorporates bilingual alignment frequency terms. This may be limited to bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation employs CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and (potentially hand-engineered) linguistic resources. Basis vectors for aligned dimensions yield a score invariant to rotations and linear transformations. The method is also extended to multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the methods are well explained. A few major issues:
(1) What is novel relative to the translation-invariance approach of Gardner et al.? If the contribution is the multilingual extension, please clarify the innovation.
(2) Using supersense annotations across multiple languages is problematic: the feature intersection across languages can become very small. How do the authors plan to mitigate this (beyond footnote 9)?
(3) How sensitive are the scores in Table 2 to coverage? For instance, in dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage but similar scores.
(4) The results in Table 3 are not entirely consistent. For most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual methods are not the top performers. Given that one key goal was to obtain embeddings that perform well on the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the invariance approach is by far the strongest. It is also unexpected that the multiCluster approach, which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information, achieves the best performance on extrinsic metrics.

Other questions for the authors:
(1) What is the performance drop from freezing word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What gains arise from simply replacing random embeddings with these embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?
(2) Is Table 1 averaged over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
(3) Are there advantages to using multiSkip instead of training bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to learn projections across the separate spaces?
(4) Dictionaries induced from parallel data via alignments or from Google Translate may not capture the challenges of real lexicons. Did you experiment with any actual multilingual dictionaries?
