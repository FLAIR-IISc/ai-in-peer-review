Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An intrinsic evaluation measure for multilingual embeddings, multiQVEC-CCA, which replaces the alignment-and-sum in QVEC with CCA and extends it to the multilingual setting. The paper shows higher correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- Secondary: Two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual embeddings without parallel corpora: multiCluster (clustering translational equivalents and training a shared embedding) and multiCCA (projecting monolingual spaces into English via CCA). Embeddings are trained for up to 59 languages.
- Tertiary: A public evaluation portal and associated resources (code, data splits) that standardize and facilitate intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation.

Strengths
- Strong contribution to evaluation methodology. The correlation study (Table 1) indicates multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA correlate more consistently with downstream tasks than cross-lingual word similarity and word translation, directly addressing the persistent gap between intrinsic and extrinsic assessments.
- Practical scalability and resource efficiency. MultiCCA and multiCluster use monolingual data plus bilingual dictionaries, avoiding the need for parallel corpora and enabling coverage across 59 languages. This is valuable for low-resource scenarios.
- Breadth and transparency of evaluation. The work compares multiple estimation methods (dictionary-based, skip-gram–based, translation-invariance) on both intrinsic (word similarity, translation, QVEC variants) and extrinsic (document classification, parsing) metrics, and reports coverage alongside scores, helping readers interpret trade-offs.
- Useful infrastructure for the community. The evaluation portal and standardized benchmarks lower barriers to replicability and future comparison. Documentation is clear and thorough, increasing practical utility.

Weaknesses
- Limited analytical depth on key empirical findings. The paper reports notable discrepancies (e.g., multiCluster’s competitiveness in the 12-language setup but degradation at 59 languages) without sufficient diagnosis. A deeper analysis of semantic conflation from clustering, cluster-size effects, dictionary noise, and language-imbalance would strengthen the conclusions and provide guidance on when to use each method.
- Under-specified sensitivity and ablation studies. Critical estimation choices (e.g., dictionary threshold τ, maximum cluster size, window size, choice of English as the hub in multiCCA, monolingual corpus size/quality) are not systematically explored. Readers cannot easily infer robustness or best practices.
- Baseline clarity and positioning. The text references Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when introducing the multiSkip baseline, but Section 2.3 then centers on Luong et al., leaving the relationship among these variants unclear (e.g., which features of TransGram/BiSkip are included or omitted, and the implications of using only parallel data vs. monolingual+parallel).
- Evaluation confounds due to coverage and dictionary quality. Several intrinsic results rely on differing vocabularies per method; although coverage is reported and an intersection analysis is mentioned, the main text could more directly quantify how vocabulary overlap affects rank order. Additionally, for 59 languages, dictionaries derived via Google Translate likely introduce noise and bias that may disproportionately affect cluster-based estimation and translation tasks.
- Presentation and completeness issues. Given that QVEC-CCA is a central claim, correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA vs. downstream tasks would make the argument more complete. Minor clarity and formatting issues (see below) also detract from polish.

Questions to the authors
- Why does multiCluster degrade substantially when scaling from 12 to 59 languages? Can you quantify the impact of polysemy amplification, cluster-size caps, and dictionary noise? Would limiting cross-lingual cluster connectivity (e.g., tighter thresholds or language-specific subclustering) mitigate semantic conflation?
- How sensitive are multiCluster and multiCCA to τ, maximum cluster size, context window, negative sampling, and the size/quality of monolingual corpora? Please provide ablations or at least directional trends.
- For multiCCA, what is the effect of using English as the hub space? Did you try alternative hubs or joint CCA alignment across multiple pivot languages? How does dictionary asymmetry or hub bias influence non-English pairwise alignments?
- Could you report correlations for monolingual QVEC and monolingual QVEC-CCA with downstream tasks (document classification, parsing), alongside the multilingual results in Table 1?
- In the 59-language setup, how do you estimate the noise level of Google Translate dictionaries? Any filtering, confidence thresholds, or coverage diagnostics? How much does this choice affect word translation and multiCluster outcomes?
- For fairness under varying coverage, can you include main-text results on vocabulary intersections across methods (not only in the supplement), and quantify how rank changes?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Clarify phrasing at l.304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” suggests heterogeneity; consider stating that the datasets are of the same kind but in different language pairs.
- Formatting: Two equations on p.3 overflow the column margin.
- Baselines: Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., but Section 2.3 only details Luong et al. Please make the correspondence explicit (similarities/differences in training signals, use of monolingual data, and objective).
- Related work that seems relevant and could be cited:
  - Multilingual embeddings and clustering: Chandar et al. (2014, NIPS), Hill et al. (2014), Lu et al. (2015, NAACL), Faruqui & Dyer (2013, ACL).
  - Multilingual training aiming to improve source-language embeddings: Suster et al. (2016, NAACL), Guo et al. (2014, COLING).
  - Translational signal for sense disambiguation: Diab & Resnik (2002, ACL).
- Given that dictionary creation and coverage are key to the 59-language experiments, adding a short data-quality analysis (e.g., precision/recall estimates vs. a gold subset) would improve the interpretability of the results.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written, practically valuable paper with strong contributions to evaluation methodology and community resources, and solid though uneven empirical performance across methods and settings. The breadth is impressive, but the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of the most surprising empirical behaviors, clearer positioning of baselines, and fuller reporting of correlations for the proposed evaluation method.
