This paper presents four approaches for constructing multilingual word embeddings and a revised QVEC-style evaluation metric to assess them. The embedding methods are:
(1) multiCluster: Builds multilingual clusters via bilingual dictionaries, then learns a single embedding per cluster from monolingual corpora; the cluster vector is used as the representation for all words mapped to that cluster.
(2) multiCCA: Generalizes Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual by treating English as a shared target space and learning dictionary-driven linear projections from each language’s monolingual embeddings into the English space.
(3) multiSkip: Extends the Luong et al. (2015b) bilingual skip-gram objective to multiple languages by summing the aligned context objectives over all available parallel corpora.
(4) Translation invariance: Performs low-rank factorization of a PMI matrix with additional terms incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies; it may primarily support bilingual settings.

The evaluation metric leverages CCA to maximize correlation between embedding dimensions and hand-crafted linguistic feature matrices. It finds paired bases for the two spaces, yielding a score invariant to rotations and other linear reparameterizations, and is extended to the multilingual case.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the technical ideas are presented coherently. A few major concerns:
(1) Clarify the novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance approach. If the contribution is specifically a multilingual extension, please articulate what is new and why it is nontrivial.
(2) Relying on supersense annotations across multiple languages risks a very small intersection of shared features. Beyond footnote 9, how do you mitigate this sparsity and inconsistency in practice?
(3) How sensitive are the Table 2 outcomes to coverage differences? For instance, in dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage yet very similar scores.
(4) The Table 3 results are hard to reconcile into a single narrative. On most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual methods are not top-performing. Given that a core objective was strong performance on word translation, it is disappointing that the translation-invariance baseline is best by a large margin. It is also counterintuitive that multiCluster, which collapses cross-word and cross-language distinctions within clusters, performs best on the extrinsic tasks.

Questions to Authors:
(1) In the parsing setup, what is the performance drop due to freezing pretrained word embeddings? Conversely, what improvement do these embeddings provide over randomized embeddings in the stack-LSTM parser?
(2) Does Table 1 report correlations aggregated over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
(3) What, if any, advantages does multiSkip offer compared to learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to align multiple spaces?
(4) Since dictionaries derived from alignments or Google Translate might not reflect the challenges of curated lexicons, did you experiment with any real multilingual dictionaries?
