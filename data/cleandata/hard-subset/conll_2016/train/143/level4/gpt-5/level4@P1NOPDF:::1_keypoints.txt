- Paper introduces four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (dictionary-based multilingual clusters with shared embeddings).
- multiCCA: extends bilingual CCA to multilingual by projecting monolingual spaces into an English anchor via bilingual dictionaries.
- multiSkip: generalizes bilingual skip-gram with alignment contexts to multilingual by augmenting the objective for all parallel corpora.
- Translation invariance: low-rank decomposition of PMI with bilingual alignment components (appears to be primarily bilingual).
- Proposes a modified QVEC evaluation using CCA to align embeddings with linguistic feature vectors, extended to multilingual evaluation and invariant to rotations/linear transforms.
- Paper is clearly written and methods are well described.
- Major issue 1: Unclear novel contribution relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance—if novelty is multilingual extension, authors should state the technical innovation succinctly.
- Major issue 2: Reliance on supersense annotations across languages risks severe sparsity because feature intersections across languages can be very small; need a plan beyond footnote 9 to address coverage.
- Major issue 3: Coverage differences may bias reported scores (e.g., multiCluster vs multiCCA in dependency parsing); request quantitative sensitivity analysis to coverage.
- Major issue 4: Results (Table 3) are inconsistent—multilingual methods rarely top intrinsic metrics, the invariance approach dominates word-translation, and multiCluster surprisingly yields best extrinsic performance despite discarding inter-cluster semantics.
- Question 1: In dependency parsing, what is the performance loss when fixing embeddings and the gain from replacing random embeddings with these pretrained embeddings in the LSTM parser?
- Question 2: Is Table 1 computed as an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
- Question 3: Are there benefits to training multiSkip directly instead of training bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to align them?
- Question 4: Do the dictionary extraction methods (alignments or Google Translate) reflect real lexicon challenges—were any real multilingual dictionaries used for evaluation?
