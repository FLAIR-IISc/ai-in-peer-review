This paper presents four methods for learning multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their effectiveness. The embedding methods are:
(1) multiCluster: Uses a dictionary to map words into multilingual clusters. Cluster embeddings are then computed and used as the embeddings for all words in each cluster.
(2) multiCCA: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual by using English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other language → English) are used to learn projections from other monolingual embedding spaces into this anchor space.
(3) multiSkip: Extends Luong et al. (2015b), which leverages source and target contexts via alignments, to the multilingual setting by augmenting the objective to include components for all available parallel corpora.
(4) Translation invariance: Performs a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency components. This appears to apply only to bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation method uses CCA to maximize correlation between word embeddings and (possibly handcrafted) linguistic features. It derives basis vectors for the aligned dimensions, yielding a score invariant to rotations and linear transformations, and is extended to multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the methods are well described. Major issues:
(1) What is the novel contribution relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach? If the contribution is its extension to multilingual embeddings, please clarify what is technically new in a few lines.
(2) Relying on supersense annotations across multiple languages is problematic: the feature intersection across languages can become very small. Beyond footnote 9, how do the authors propose to address this sparsity/coverage issue?
(3) How much does coverage affect the scores in Table 2? For instance, in dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage but similar scores. Please quantify or analyze the sensitivity of the reported results to coverage differences.
(4) The results in Table 3 do not tell a consistent story. For most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual techniques are not the top performers. Given that one goal of the paper is to create embeddings that perform well on the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the invariance approach outperforms the others by a wide margin. It is also surprising that multiCluster—which discards inter-cluster semantic information across words and languages—achieves the best extrinsic results.

Other questions for the authors:
(1) In the dependency parsing experiments, what is the performance loss from fixing the word embeddings? Conversely, what is the gain from simply using these embeddings as substitutes for random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?
(2) Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
(3) Are there advantages to using multiSkip instead of training bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to learn projections across the distinct spaces?
(4) The dictionary extraction methods (from parallel corpora via alignments or via Google Translate) may not reflect the challenges of real lexicons. Did you evaluate with any real multilingual dictionaries?
