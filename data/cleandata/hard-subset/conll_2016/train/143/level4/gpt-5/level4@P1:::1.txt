Summary and contributions
This paper presents methods for learning multilingual word embeddings and a modified evaluation metric for assessing their quality.

- Estimation methods:
  1) multiCluster: uses bilingual dictionaries to cluster translationally equivalent word types across languages, then trains a single embedding per cluster from concatenated monolingual corpora.
  2) multiCCA: extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual by projecting monolingual embeddings into an English anchor space via CCA using bilingual dictionaries.
  3) multiSkip: extends Luong et al. (2015b) to the multilingual setting by summing bilingual skip-gram objectives over available parallel corpora.
  4) Translation-invariance: factorizes a PMI matrix with additional terms derived from bilingual alignment frequencies (Gardner et al., 2015); this may only support bilingual embeddings.

- Evaluation: the paper adapts QVEC to multilingual settings (multiQVEC) and proposes QVEC-CCA (and multiQVEC-CCA), which aligns embeddings to linguistic features using CCA to yield an intrinsic score invariant to rotations/linear transformations and normalized across dimensions.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Two dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster, multiCCA) to learn multilingual embeddings without requiring parallel corpora.
2) An improved intrinsic evaluation metric (QVEC-CCA) and its multilingual extension (multiQVEC-CCA), designed to correlate better with downstream tasks.
3) A comprehensive empirical comparison across multiple languages and both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, with attention to coverage.

Strengths
- Clear exposition: The paper is generally well written and the methods are described clearly, including the estimation objectives and the CCA-based evaluation.
- Sound evaluation design: The use of both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, and an explicit analysis of correlations between them, is valuable. The QVEC-CCA formulation addresses two important issues of QVEC (basis dependence and lack of normalization).
- Practical scope: The multilingual extensions and large-scale experiments (including many languages) are ambitious and relevant to the community. Reporting coverage alongside scores is a good practice.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to translation-invariance (Gardner et al., 2015): It is unclear what is new in your treatment of the translation-invariance approach. If your contribution is its extension to multilingual embeddings, please explain precisely what changes are required and why this is non-trivial.

2) Dependence on supersense annotations: Relying on supersense tag inventories across multiple languages is problematic. The intersection of features across languages can become very small, undermining stability and comparability. Beyond footnote 9, please discuss how you handle sparse and inconsistent tagsets and whether there is a plan to mitigate this (e.g., standardized tag mappings, alternative linguistic resources such as universal POS).

3) Coverage effects on reported results: Coverage differs substantially across methods, yet scores are close in some settings (e.g., dependency parsing, where multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage). Please quantify how coverage affects scores in Table 2, and, where feasible, include comparisons restricted to the shared vocabulary to isolate performance from coverage.

4) Inconsistent story across metrics (esp. Table 3): The results do not present a consistent picture. For most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual embedding techniques you emphasize do not perform best. Given that a key goal is strong performance on word translation, it is disappointing that the translation-invariance method substantially outperforms others on that metric. Conversely, it is surprising that multiCluster—despite discarding inter-cluster semantic variation within and across languages—performs best on extrinsic metrics. Please reconcile these findings and discuss when each method is preferable, and why.

Questions to authors
1) Dependency parsing setup: What is the performance loss from fixing pretrained embeddings (i.e., not fine-tuning) compared to allowing the parser to update them? Conversely, how much do these pretrained embeddings improve over randomly initialized embeddings in the same stack-LSTM parser?

2) Table 1 correlations: Is Table 1 computed over the 17 multilingual embeddings described in Section 5.1? Please clarify the exact set of models and metrics used to compute each correlation.

3) multiSkip vs. multiCCA: Are there scenarios where multiSkip has advantages over learning bilingual embeddings followed by multiCCA projections into a shared space (e.g., data requirements, computational cost, or specific downstream tasks)?

4) Dictionary construction: The extraction via alignments and Google Translate may not reflect the challenges of real lexical resources. Did you experiment with existing human-curated multilingual dictionaries, and if so, how did results compare?

5) Translation-invariance scope: You note the method “may only work for bilingual embeddings.” Did you attempt a true multilingual extension (e.g., multiple alignment matrices or a unified alignment operator), and what were the empirical outcomes?

Overall, this is a clear and ambitious paper with useful methodological and evaluative contributions. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying the novelty relative to translation-invariance, the implications of coverage, and the inconsistency across metrics—would strengthen the paper significantly.
