Summary and Contributions
This paper presents two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings without requiring parallel corpora: (1) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent words and trains embeddings over cluster IDs; and (2) multiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into a shared space via CCA, using English as a pivot. The paper also proposes new intrinsic evaluation metrics—multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA—that extend QVEC to the multilingual setting and replace QVEC’s dimension-wise correlation with CCA to address basis-rotation sensitivity and normalization issues. A web portal for standardized evaluation across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks is introduced. Large-scale experiments cover 59 languages, with focused comparisons on a 12-language subset where parallel data exist.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A practical and better-correlated intrinsic evaluation framework (multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal that standardizes multilingual embedding evaluation.
2) Scalable, dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) that avoid the need for parallel corpora and achieve competitive results across languages and tasks.
3) An empirical study comparing intrinsic metrics’ correlations with downstream performance, highlighting limitations of commonly used metrics.

Strengths
- Evaluation framework with demonstrated utility: The paper convincingly shows that conventional intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual similarity, word translation) correlate weakly with downstream tasks, whereas multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better. This is a valuable insight for the community and directly actionable for future work.
- Practical methodologies at scale: Training embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries is compelling. The multiCCA approach in particular is simple, resource-efficient, and achieves strong coverage and performance.
- Reproducibility and resources: The web portal, shared benchmarks, and clear experimental setup make it easy to reproduce and extend the work. This infrastructure contribution is significant.
- Broad empirical comparison: The paper evaluates four estimation methods across multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, and reports coverage alongside scores, which improves the interpretability of results where vocabularies differ.
- Clear writing and organization: The paper is well written, and technical descriptions are accessible and complete.

Weaknesses
- Breadth over depth in analysis: While the empirical sweep is broad, the discussion sometimes lacks depth. Notably, the divergent behavior of multiCluster (competitive in 12 languages but substantially weaker in 59) is only briefly attributed to ambiguity; more rigorous analysis (e.g., cluster size effects, dictionary noise, τ threshold, and coverage interactions) would strengthen the conclusions.
- Dictionary noise and evaluation leakage: For 47 languages, Google Translate dictionaries are used both for training (dictionary creation) and for evaluating word translation (in the 59-language setting). The paper does not quantify dictionary quality or its impact on training and test metrics, raising concerns about noise, bias, and comparability across languages.
- Missing correlations for monolingual metrics: Table 1 does not include correlations for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA, though QVEC-CCA is proposed as an improvement over QVEC. Including them would provide a more complete picture of intrinsic–extrinsic relationships.
- Baseline clarity: The “multiSkip” baseline is described with mixed references (Coulmance et al., Guo et al., and Luong et al.), and your implementation uses only parallel data. It would help to clarify precisely which variant is implemented and how it differs from these works, particularly since some prior methods also exploit monolingual corpora.
- Coverage confounds remain: Although coverage rates are reported, key intrinsic comparisons are still influenced by differing vocabularies. Intersection-of-vocabulary evaluations are relegated to supplementary material; bringing the essential comparisons into the main text would improve fairness and clarity.

Questions to Authors
- MultiCluster behavior: Can you provide ablations on (a) maximum cluster size, (b) dictionary threshold τ, (c) dictionary source (Europarl vs. Google Translate), and (d) monolingual corpus size by language to explain why multiCluster degrades at 59 languages but is competitive at 12? How much of the drop is due to increased semantic ambiguity vs. dictionary noise?
- Pivot choice in multiCCA: How sensitive is performance to the chosen pivot language? Did you try high-resource pivots other than English, or multi-pivot CCA alignments?
- Dictionary quality: Do you have any manual quality estimates (precision/recall, spot checks) for the Google Translate dictionaries? Were any filtering or consistency checks beyond excluding identical/multiword translations applied?
- Training details for downstream tasks: In document classification, were embeddings fixed or fine-tuned? Are all methods compared at identical dimensionality and preprocessing (e.g., normalization), and were hyperparameters tuned equivalently?
- Intrinsic–extrinsic correlations: Could you add monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA to Table 1? Also, multiQVEC-CCA correlates less with parsing than multiQVEC in Table 1—can you comment on why?
- Scaling multiQVEC(-CCA): Given supersense availability in only en/da/it, what are your plans to expand the annotation resource, or to substitute with widely available resources (e.g., universal POS) for broader multilingual applicability?

Additional Comments (Minor)
- “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets”: please clarify that they are multiple datasets of the same type across different language pairs.
- Two equations exceed the column margin; please fix formatting.
- Harmonize the description and citations for the multiSkip baseline: lines 121/147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., while §2.3 focuses on Luong et al. Please clarify the correspondence and the exact baseline implemented.
- Consider adding related work: Chandar et al. (2014); Hill et al. (2014); Lu et al. (2015); Faruqui and Dyer (2013) on clustering; Suster et al. (2016); Guo et al. (2014); and earlier work on translational context for sense disambiguation (Diab and Resnik, 2002).
- Ensure the portal and datasets remain accessible post-review; the RCV licensing constraint is noted but a clear pointer to how others can replicate that part would be helpful.

Overall Evaluation
A strong, timely, and well-executed paper with practical contributions to both methods and evaluation. I recommend acceptance after addressing the depth and clarity issues above, especially the analysis of multiCluster at scale, dictionary quality, and the completeness of correlation results.
