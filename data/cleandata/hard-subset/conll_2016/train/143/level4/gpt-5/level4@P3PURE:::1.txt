This paper presents four approaches for learning multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their quality. The embedding methods are:

- multiCluster: Uses a bilingual/multilingual dictionary to group translation-equivalent words into shared clusters. A single cluster embedding is learned and used for all words assigned to that cluster.

- multiCCA: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual settings by treating English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other language → English) are used to learn projections from monolingual embedding spaces into the shared English space.

- multiSkip: Extends Luong et al. (2015b) from bilingual to multilingual settings by incorporating source–target context (via alignments) across all available parallel corpora, augmenting the objective with cross-lingual components for each language pair.

- Translation invariance: Uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment-frequency components. As presented here, it may only apply to bilingual embeddings.

For evaluation, the paper proposes a CCA-based variant of QVEC that maximizes correlation between embeddings and (potentially hand-crafted) linguistic resources. The approach derives basis vectors for aligned dimensions and yields a score invariant to rotation and linear transformations, with an extension to multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the methods are described in a straightforward manner. That said, I have several major concerns and questions.

Major issues:
1) Novelty relative to Gardner et al. (translation invariance): What is the specific new contribution beyond Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance approach? If the novelty is the extension to multilingual embeddings, please make this explicit and add a few lines clarifying what is technically new.

2) Reliance on supersense annotations across multiple languages: When intersecting features across languages, the shared feature set can become very small. How do the authors plan to address this sparsity beyond what is briefly noted in footnote 9?

3) Coverage effects in Table 2: How sensitive are the reported scores to coverage differences? For instance, in dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage while yielding similar scores. A controlled analysis of coverage vs. performance would help interpret these results.

4) Inconsistency of Table 3: The intrinsic metrics in Table 3 do not present a consistent picture, and the multilingual methods often do not perform best. Given that a primary goal is to produce embeddings that perform well on the word-translation metric, it is disappointing that the translation-invariance approach performs best by a wide margin. It is also puzzling that multiCluster—despite discarding inter-cluster semantic distinctions across words and languages—performs best on extrinsic tasks.

Other questions for the authors:
1) Dependency parsing setup: What is the performance loss from fixing the word embeddings during training? Conversely, what is the gain from simply replacing random embeddings with the proposed embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?

2) Table 1 aggregation: Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?

3) multiSkip vs. multiCCA pipeline: Are there advantages to using multiSkip directly instead of first learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to project into a shared space?

4) Dictionaries: The dictionaries induced from parallel alignments or obtained from Google Translate may not reflect the challenges posed by real lexicons. Did you experiment with any curated multilingual dictionaries?

In summary, the work is well presented, but clearer positioning relative to prior translation-invariance work, a deeper analysis of coverage and feature sparsity, and a more coherent explanation of why the simplest methods outperform on extrinsic tasks are needed to solidify the paper’s contributions.
