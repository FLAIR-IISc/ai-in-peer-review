This paper presents four approaches for learning multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their effectiveness. The methods are:
1) multiCluster: Uses a dictionary to map words into multilingual clusters; a single embedding is then learned per cluster and used for all words in that cluster.
2) multiCCA: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual by treating English as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other language -> English) are used to learn projections from other languages’ monolingual embeddings into the anchor space.
3) multiSkip: Extends Luong et al. (2015b)—which leverages aligned source and target context—to the multilingual setting by adding objective components for all available parallel corpora.
4) Translation invariance: Uses a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment-frequency terms; this may only support bilingual embeddings.

The evaluation adapts QVEC by using CCA to maximize correlation between word embeddings and (potentially hand-crafted) linguistic features. Basis vectors for the aligned dimensions produce a score invariant to rotations and other linear transformations. The method is further extended to multilingual evaluation.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. Major issues:
1) What is the new contribution relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach? If the novelty is the multilingual extension, please add a brief explanation of what is new.
2) Using supersense annotations across multiple languages is problematic: the feature intersection across languages can be very small. Beyond footnote 9, how do the authors propose to address this?
3) How does coverage affect the results in Table 2? For dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have markedly different coverage but similar scores.
4) The results in Table 3 are not consistent. On most intrinsic metrics, the multilingual methods do not perform best. Given that one of the primary goals is to obtain embeddings that perform well on the word translation metric (intra-language), it is disappointing that the translation invariance method performs best by a wide margin. It is also puzzling that the multiCluster approach—which discards inter-cluster (word and language) semantic information—performs best on the extrinsic metrics.

Additional questions:
1) What is the performance loss from fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What is the gain from simply using these embeddings as substitutes for random initialization in the LSTM stack parser?
2) Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?
3) Are there advantages to using multiSkip instead of training bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to learn projections across the separate spaces?
4) Dictionaries extracted from parallel corpora via alignments or from Google Translate may not reflect the challenges of real lexicons. Did you explore using any real multilingual dictionaries?
