This paper presents four approaches to learning multilingual word embeddings and a modified QVEC-based evaluation for assessing their quality.

Methods:
- multiCluster: Uses a bilingual dictionary to group translationally equivalent words into multilingual clusters, then learns a single embedding per cluster, which is assigned to all words in that cluster.
- multiCCA: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) from bilingual to multilingual settings by treating English as the anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other language → English) are used to learn projections from each monolingual space into the English embedding space.
- multiSkip: Generalizes Luong et al. (2015b) by incorporating source–target alignment contexts across all available parallel corpora, extending the training objective to the multilingual case.
- Translation invariance: Factorizes the PMI matrix with an objective that includes bilingual alignment frequency terms. As described, this approach appears limited to bilingual embeddings.

Evaluation:
The paper proposes a modification of QVEC that uses CCA to maximize the correlation between embedding dimensions and linguistically motivated features, producing scores invariant to rotation and other linear transformations. The method is also extended to handle multilingual evaluations.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the approaches are well described. That said, I have several major concerns:

1) Novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance embeddings:
- What is the new contribution here? If the novelty is the multilingual extension, please clarify what is technically new beyond a straightforward generalization, and what challenges arise in the multilingual case that are not present in the bilingual setting.

2) Cross-lingual super-sense features:
- Relying on supersense annotations across multiple languages risks a very small intersection of shared features. Beyond footnote 9, how do the authors mitigate sparsity and ensure that the evaluation remains meaningful and comparable across languages?

3) Coverage effects in Table 2:
- How sensitive are the reported scores to coverage differences? For instance, in dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage but similar performance. Please quantify the impact of coverage (e.g., by normalizing scores or reporting results at matched coverage levels).

4) Inconsistencies in Table 3:
- The intrinsic metrics do not consistently favor the multilingual methods, and for the word translation task—a stated primary goal—the translation-invariance approach substantially outperforms the others. Moreover, it is surprising that multiCluster, which collapses inter-cluster semantic distinctions across words and languages, achieves the strongest extrinsic results. Please provide analysis that reconciles these observations (e.g., which properties of multiCluster favor downstream tasks, or why multilingual objectives may not yield gains on the chosen intrinsic metrics).

Additional questions for the authors:

1) Dependency parsing setup:
- What is the performance loss from fixing embeddings rather than fine-tuning them? Conversely, what is the gain from using these pretrained embeddings versus random initialization in the LSTM stack parser? Reporting both would help disentangle pretraining benefits from fine-tuning effects.

2) Table 1 aggregation:
- Is Table 1 computed as an average over the 17 embeddings described in §5.1? If not, please clarify the aggregation and selection protocol.

3) multiSkip vs. bilingual + multiCCA:
- Are there practical or performance advantages to training multiSkip directly over multiple parallel corpora, as opposed to learning bilingual embeddings and then aligning them via multiCCA into a shared space? A discussion of accuracy, data requirements, and computational cost would be helpful.

4) Dictionary realism:
- Dictionaries derived from alignments or Google Translate may not reflect the noise, coverage gaps, or sense granularity of real lexicons. Did you experiment with any curated multilingual dictionaries? If so, how did results change? If not, please comment on expected robustness under more realistic lexicon conditions.

In summary, the paper contributes a useful set of multilingual embedding methods and an evaluation framework that aims to be invariant to linear transformations and extendable to multiple languages. Clarifying the novelty of the translation-invariance variant, addressing coverage and feature sparsity in the evaluation, and explaining the apparent mismatch between intrinsic and extrinsic results would significantly strengthen the work.
