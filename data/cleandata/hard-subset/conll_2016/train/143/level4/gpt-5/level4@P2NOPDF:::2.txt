This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, inspired by cross-lingual clustering, and MultiCCA, which leverages canonical correlation analysis (CCA). It also proposes a supersense-based intrinsic evaluation that extends QVEC by replacing its correlation step with CCA and by incorporating multilingual evaluation. The authors evaluate across a wide set of tasks via their web portal and show that, in some settings, the proposed representations outperform two baselines.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and reflects substantial effort.
- The representation-learning approaches and the evaluation framework are timely.
- The documentation is meticulous, and the web portal plus the improved QVEC variant are valuable resources.

Main concerns:
While the breadth of experiments and tools is impressive, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of key results. In particular:
- Discuss the conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59-language and 12-language settings. What properties of the data (e.g., dictionary coverage, language family composition, token frequency distributions) drive this divergence?
- Analyze sensitivity to key estimation choices in both MultiCluster and MultiCCA (e.g., clustering criteria and granularity, normalization, dictionary size/quality, CCA dimensionality and regularization). Ablations isolating the contributions of the dictionary, monolingual data, and multilingual signals would clarify when each approach is expected to excel.
- More thorough discussion of these issues would strengthen the scientific takeaways, beyond the practical contributions.

Evaluation measure:
The paper states (l.326–328) that QVEC-CCA improves on QVEC. To substantiate this, Table 1 should also include monolingual correlation results for QVEC and QVEC-CCA, so readers can separate the benefits of CCA from those of multilingual evaluation.

Clarity and minor issues:
- l.304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” suggests heterogeneous task types; if these datasets are of the same kind across different languages, please rephrase to avoid implying otherwise.
- p.3: two equations exceed the column margin; please fix the formatting.
- Baseline attribution: Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. when referring to the MultiSkip baseline, but §2.3 mentions Luong et al. Please clarify how these works relate and which implementation/variant you use.

Related work suggestions:
- Multilingual embeddings and clustering:
  - Chandar et al. (2014), an autoencoder approach to bilingual word representations (NIPS).
  - Hill et al. (2014), embedding word similarity with neural machine translation (arXiv:1412.6448).
  - Lu et al. (2015), deep multilingual correlation for improved embeddings (NAACL).
  - Faruqui & Dyer (2013), an information-theoretic approach to bilingual word clustering (ACL).
- Multilingual training to improve source-language embeddings:
  - Šuster, Titov, and van Noord (2016), bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders (NAACL-HLT).
  - Guo et al. (2014), learning sense-specific embeddings via bilingual resources (COLING).
- Use of translational context:
  - Diab & Resnik (2002), unsupervised word sense tagging using parallel corpora (ACL).

Overall assessment:
The paper offers practical and well-engineered contributions—two multilingual embedding methods, an improved QVEC-style metric, and a useful evaluation portal. To maximize impact, I recommend expanding the analysis to explain the differing MultiCluster results across language settings, assessing parameter sensitivity for both approaches, and clarifying baseline lineage. Including monolingual QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA scores in Table 1 will also strengthen the evaluation claims. With these additions and the suggested citations, the work would present clearer insights alongside its strong practical value.
