Summary and contributions
This paper studies multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. It introduces two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel corpora, compares them to two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), proposes new intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and provides a public evaluation portal. In my view, the key contributions, in order of importance, are:
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, which show substantially stronger correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (word similarity/translation).
2) Scalable multilingual estimation without parallel data: multiCCA and multiCluster, trained across up to 59 languages using bilingual dictionaries plus monolingual corpora.
3) Resources and infrastructure: a web portal and released code/data to standardize evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

Strengths
- Strong evidence that the proposed intrinsic metrics are more predictive of downstream performance. Table 1 shows much higher correlations for multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA with both document classification (0.635/0.896) and parsing (0.444/0.273) than for word similarity (0.386/0.007) and word translation (0.066/−0.292). This directly addresses the common criticism that intrinsic scores are weakly informative for applications.
- Scale and breadth of experiments. The paper trains and evaluates embeddings for 59 languages and presents detailed comparisons on a 12-language subset using multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The inclusion of both document classification and dependency parsing, with explicit control to force models to rely on pretrained embeddings, provides a meaningful, application-oriented assessment.
- Practical, dictionary-based methods. Both multiCCA and multiCluster rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, a realistic setting for low-resource languages where large parallel corpora are scarce. multiCCA is particularly simple and effective: project monolingual spaces into a shared anchor space (English), achieving strong coverage and competitive performance.
- Reproducibility and community value. The evaluation portal, standardized datasets, and released software promote comparability and reuse. The design explicitly separates development and test modes, encouraging sound experimental practice.
- Clear presentation of methods and evaluation caveats. The paper explains each method concisely and acknowledges known pitfalls (e.g., coverage effects in intrinsic evaluation and ambiguity introduced by clustering).

Weaknesses
- Unclear methodological novelty for the translation-invariance baseline. The paper includes Gardner et al. (2015) as a baseline but does not articulate any new contribution or extension to the multilingual setting beyond applying the method. If there is novelty (e.g., a multilingual adaptation), it needs to be stated precisely.
- Dependence on multilingual supersense annotations limits generality. multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA require aligned linguistic property matrices. Multilingual supersense resources are sparse and inconsistent, which constrains language coverage and may bias evaluation toward a small subset. While this is acknowledged, the paper would benefit from a concrete plan to mitigate this (e.g., interoperable tagsets, projection strategies, or alternate resources like universal POS/morphology).
- Coverage differences confound comparisons. Several results (e.g., dependency parsing in the 59-language setting) compare methods with notably different coverage. Although the paper reports coverage and mentions intersection-based re-evaluation, the main tables still leave ambiguity about how much of the performance gap is due to coverage versus embedding quality. A systematic coverage-controlled analysis would strengthen claims.
- Mixed and sometimes counterintuitive results across metrics. In the 12-language experiments (Table 3), the translation-invariance method is best on the word translation metric, while multiCluster—despite its coarse granularity and increased ambiguity in large multilingual settings—performs best on both extrinsic tasks. The paper could do more to reconcile these findings with the narrative, for example by analyzing when semantic granularity helps or hurts transfer and why word translation correlates poorly with downstream performance.
- Baseline design may disadvantage multiSkip. The presented multiSkip implementation trains only on parallel data, whereas related work augments with monolingual corpora. This may make the comparison less informative about the strengths of this class of methods in realistic settings.

Questions to authors
1) Translation-invariance baseline: Is there any methodological novelty in your use of Gardner et al. (2015) for the multilingual case (e.g., changes to the objective, alignment matrices across multiple languages), or is it a direct application? Please clarify the contribution.
2) Supersense-based evaluation: How do you address the small intersection of annotated vocabularies across languages? Have you considered normalizing tag inventories across languages, using universal POS/morph tags, or learning cross-lingual mappings from richer English resources to other languages?
3) Coverage effects: For key results (e.g., 59-language dependency parsing), can you report scores on the intersection of vocabularies covered by all methods and quantify the absolute/relative change vs. the main table? A coverage-normalized metric or stratification by frequency bands would also be helpful.
4) Parsing with fixed vs. trainable embeddings: What is the performance delta when allowing the parser to fine-tune embeddings? Do the relative method rankings and the intrinsic–extrinsic correlations in Table 1 hold when embeddings are updated?
5) Correlation computation: Are the Table 1 correlations computed over the full set of 17 embedding instances described in §5.1? Please provide confidence intervals or significance tests for the reported coefficients.
6) multiSkip vs. multiCCA pipeline: Are there cases where training bilingual embeddings on parallel data and then aligning via multiCCA outperforms your direct multiSkip? What are the pros/cons in terms of coverage, stability, and computational cost?
7) Dictionaries: Beyond alignments and Google Translate, did you evaluate with curated multilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary dumps filtered by quality or other lexical resources)? If not, can you comment on expected robustness to noise in automatically constructed dictionaries?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely paper. The proposed intrinsic metrics are the most compelling contribution, supported by strong correlation evidence. The large-scale experiments and released resources add significant community value. Clarifying the novelty around the translation-invariance baseline, providing deeper analysis of coverage effects, and discussing the limitations of supersense-based evaluation would further strengthen the submission.
