Summary and contributions
This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: MultiCluster, which clusters translational equivalents and learns shared vectors, and MultiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into a shared space via CCA with English as the pivot. It further proposes a multilingual extension of QVEC and an improved variant, multiQVEC-CCA, which replaces QVEC’s dimension-wise correlation with CCA to address basis-invariance and normalization issues. The authors also release an evaluation web portal and resources, and report results across a broad suite of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, including experiments over 59 languages.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation: multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA as improved intrinsic metrics, with evidence they correlate better with downstream performance than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) Methods: two dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimators (MultiCluster and MultiCCA) that avoid reliance on parallel corpora and scale to many languages.
3) Resources: an evaluation portal, datasets, and trained embeddings for large-scale multilingual evaluation.

Strengths
- Strong practical impact via evaluation methodology and tooling. The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics are well motivated; Table 1 shows they correlate substantially better with downstream document classification and parsing than cross-lingual word similarity and word translation. The evaluation portal, datasets, and trained models will materially aid reproducibility and future work.
- Broad and careful empirical evaluation. The paper evaluates across nine metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic), multiple language settings (12 and 59 languages), and multiple baselines (including translation-invariance and multiSkip). Reporting coverage alongside scores is a welcome practice, and the discussion acknowledges coverage-induced biases in intrinsic evaluations.
- Clear exposition and thorough documentation. The paper is well written, with careful definitions, reasonable training details, and thoughtful discussion of goals for multilingual embeddings and evaluation (e.g., coverage, computational efficiency, and correlation with downstream tasks).
- Scalable, resource-efficient estimation. The dictionary-based approaches leverage monolingual corpora and bilingual lexicons without requiring parallel data, making them widely applicable. The 59-language experiments demonstrate scalability.

Weaknesses
- Breadth over depth in analysis. The paper covers many settings but leaves important analyses underexplored. In particular:
  • The conflicting outcomes for MultiCluster between the 59-language and 12-language setups merit deeper investigation (e.g., semantic conflation from larger clusters, cluster size thresholds, dictionary noise).
  • The sensitivity of MultiCluster and MultiCCA to estimation choices (e.g., clustering graph construction, threshold τ, maximum cluster size, monolingual training parameters, choice of pivot language for MultiCCA) is not analyzed.
- Incomplete correlation analysis. Table 1 omits correlations for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA, even though the paper argues QVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC. Including these would help substantiate the claims about QVEC-CCA’s superiority more comprehensively.
- Baseline description clarity. Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. in reference to the multiSkip baseline, while §2.3 then focuses on Luong et al. The correspondence among these methods (and the exact differences from the implementation used here) needs to be clarified, especially given that multiSkip here does not use monolingual data whereas related work often does.
- Related work breadth. While generally well cited, several relevant works on multilingual embeddings, correlation-based approaches, and cross-lingual clustering/word-sense modeling are not discussed (see references below). Including them would better situate the contributions.
- Minor presentation issues. Some equations exceed the column margins; a phrase about “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” (l. 304) might mislead readers into thinking the datasets differ in nature rather than language.

Questions to authors
- Could you analyze why MultiCluster performs competitively in the 12-language setting but degrades in the 59-language setting? For example, how do dictionary noise, cluster connectivity, and maximum cluster size affect semantic conflation and performance?
- How sensitive are MultiCluster and MultiCCA to key hyperparameters and design choices (e.g., τ in dictionary extraction, context window, number of epochs, choice of English as pivot for MultiCCA)? Any ablation or variance analysis would be helpful.
- Please clarify the relationship between your multiSkip implementation and the methods of Coulmance et al. (2015), Guo et al. (2016), and Luong et al. (2015b). What are the training differences (use of monolingual data, alignment strategy) and expected empirical consequences?
- Could you add the correlations for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA to Table 1 to more fully support the claim that QVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC?

Minor comments
- l. 304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” could be rephrased to indicate they are the same type of dataset in different language pairs.
- p. 3: two equations exceed the column margin; please adjust formatting.
- Lines 121 and 147 vs. §2.3: please reconcile the citation and naming of the multiSkip baseline vis-à-vis Luong et al., Coulmance et al., and Guo et al.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong, timely paper with clear practical contributions (improved intrinsic metrics and an evaluation portal) and solid methods. My main concern is the limited depth of analysis on key empirical findings and design choices. Addressing the questions above—particularly the MultiCluster behavior across language scales and the inclusion of monolingual QVEC/CCA correlations—would significantly strengthen the paper.
