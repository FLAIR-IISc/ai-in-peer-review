Summary and contributions
The paper studies multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. It presents four estimation approaches: (1) multiCluster, which collapses translationally equivalent words into clusters and learns a single embedding per cluster from monolingual corpora; (2) multiCCA, which learns monolingual embeddings and projects each non-English language into the English embedding space via CCA using bilingual dictionaries; (3) multiSkip, a skip-gram style model extended to multilingual settings using parallel corpora and word alignments; and (4) a translation-invariance matrix factorization approach that incorporates alignment statistics. The paper also proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics adapted from QVEC, and provides an evaluation portal with datasets, trained models, and code.

Contributions as I see them:
- A new intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, that is basis-invariant and shows stronger correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- Two dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimators, particularly multiCCA, that scale to 59 languages without requiring parallel text.
- A public evaluation portal and benchmark suite that standardize intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation across many languages.

Strengths
- The evaluation proposal is compelling: multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with downstream performance than word similarity or word translation (e.g., Table 1 shows substantially higher correlations with document classification), directly addressing a persistent gap between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
- The dictionary-based estimation methods are practical and scalable. Training embeddings for 59 languages without parallel corpora is valuable, and multiCCA attains consistently strong coverage and performance across metrics in the 59-language setting.
- The experimental design is broad, spanning multiple intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing), with attention to coverage effects and their potential to bias intrinsic evaluations.
- The web portal and released resources (code, data, trained embeddings) will facilitate reproducibility and future comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to translation-invariance embeddings (Gardner et al., 2015) is unclear. The paper reuses this approach as a baseline but does not explicitly detail what, if anything, is new in the multilingual setting (e.g., algorithmic changes, scalability, or theoretical insights). Given that this baseline performs best on the word translation metric in Table 3, clarifying conceptual and empirical novelty is important.
- The reliance on supersense annotations across languages to build the linguistic matrix for multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA raises concerns about coverage and cross-lingual consistency. The intersection of annotated vocabularies can be small and uneven across languages, potentially biasing the intrinsic scores. Beyond the remark in footnote 9, the paper should propose concrete mitigation strategies (e.g., using universal POS tag dictionaries, harmonized supersense guidelines, or cross-lingual projection) and quantify sensitivity to the size and quality of the linguistic resource.
- Coverage differences confound several comparisons. For example, in Table 2 (dependency parsing), multiCluster and multiCCA have notably different coverage yet similar scores. Since extrinsic evaluations necessarily use all tokens, the paper should either normalize for coverage (e.g., by intersecting vocabularies where feasible) or provide ablations showing how coverage alone affects downstream scores.
- The results in Table 3 tell a mixed story. On many intrinsic metrics, methods designed explicitly for multilinguality do not top the leaderboard; the translation-invariance baseline is best on word translation, a key cross-lingual criterion. Conversely, multiCluster—despite collapsing many-to-one across words and languages and thereby discarding within-cluster distinctions—performs best on both extrinsic tasks. This tension merits deeper analysis: when and why does cluster-level conflation help downstream tasks, and how does that square with weaker intrinsic performance?
- The multiSkip baseline is disadvantaged by not using monolingual contexts, unlike related work that augments parallel data with monolingual corpora. This weak baseline makes it harder to draw strong conclusions about parallel-data-driven approaches versus dictionary-based ones.

Questions to authors
- Translation-invariance baseline: What is the specific novelty (if any) in your use of Gardner et al.’s method for multilingual embeddings? If you extended it beyond the original bilingual formulation, please detail the modifications and their effects.
- Supersense resources: How sensitive are multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlations to the size and cross-lingual consistency of the supersense matrix? Have you tried universal POS tag dictionaries or other broadly available resources, and how do correlations change?
- Coverage: For dependency parsing in Table 2, can you quantify the effect of coverage on LAS/UAS (e.g., by masking inputs to equalize coverage or via controlled ablations)?
- Parsing setup: What is the performance delta when embeddings are frozen versus fine-tuned in the stack-LSTM parser? And what is the gain from initializing with your embeddings versus random initialization under your no-POS/morphology regime?
- Correlation computation: For Table 1, are the reported coefficients computed across the 17 systems described in §5.1 (i.e., one point per system per metric)? Please clarify the aggregation protocol and whether any outliers drive the results.
- multiSkip vs bilingual+CCA: Are there practical advantages to training one multilingual skip-gram model over training bilingual embeddings and aligning them via multiCCA (e.g., compute cost, coverage, ease of adding languages)?
- Dictionaries: Beyond alignments and Google Translate, did you evaluate with curated bilingual/multilingual lexicons? If so, how do dictionary noise and domain mismatch affect multiCCA/multiCluster performance and coverage?
