This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for inducing multilingual word embeddings: a clustering-based approach (MultiCluster) and a CCA-based approach (MultiCCA). It also introduces QVEC-CCA, a supersense-based intrinsic evaluation that replaces QVEC’s correlation component with CCA and extends evaluation to multilingual settings. The authors evaluate broadly across tasks and languages via a public web portal and show that the proposed representations outperform baselines in several cases. The paper is clearly written, timely, and accompanied by meticulous documentation.

Strengths
- Clear problem setting and practical focus; both MultiCluster and MultiCCA are simple, reproducible, and useful.
- QVEC-CCA is a sensible and well-motivated improvement over QVEC, addressing correlation pitfalls and supporting multilingual evaluation.
- The evaluation portal and thorough documentation are valuable contributions for the community.
- Broad empirical evaluation across many languages and tasks.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Breadth over depth: The paper covers many settings but often stops short of explaining why methods work (or fail) in particular conditions. Please provide deeper analysis, for example:
  - MultiCluster’s conflicting outcomes in the 59- vs 12-language setups: What drives the reversal? Language-family composition, dictionary coverage, homonymy/polysemy effects of clustering, or differences in training corpora?
  - Sensitivity to estimation choices: For both MultiCluster and MultiCCA, analyze the effect of key parameters (e.g., dictionary size/quality, clustering criteria, CCA dimensionality/regularization, frequency thresholds). Ablations and learning-curve plots would greatly help.
  - Stability across language families and scripts: Are gains uniform or concentrated in certain typological groups?
  - Statistical rigor: Report confidence intervals or significance tests for key comparisons and include effect sizes where appropriate.
- QVEC-CCA evaluation: Since QVEC-CCA is claimed to improve upon QVEC (l.326–328), please include monolingual correlation results for QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA alongside the multilingual results (Table 1 or an additional table) to substantiate the improvement across both settings.
- Baselines and prior work clarity: Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. for MultiSkip, while §2.3 references Luong et al. Please clarify the relationships among these works, what exactly is implemented as the MultiSkip baseline, and any deviations from the original setups.
- Interpretation of “wins” vs. “losses”: Where the proposed methods outperform only in some tasks/languages, provide hypotheses or diagnostics (e.g., alignment quality, domain mismatch, vocabulary coverage) to guide users on when to prefer which method.

Minor comments
- l.304: “a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets” suggests heterogeneity of task types. If they are the same task type across different languages, consider rephrasing to “a combination of word similarity datasets across multiple languages.”
- p.3: Two equations exceed the column margin; please fix formatting.
- Reproducibility: Consider adding more implementation details (hyperparameters, preprocessing decisions, dictionary filtering rules) in the appendix or portal to facilitate exact replication.

  - Hill, Cho, Jean, Devin, Bengio (2014). Embedding word similarity with neural machine translation. arXiv:1412.6448.
  - Lu, Wang, Bansal, Gimpel, Livescu (2015). Deep multilingual correlation for improved word embeddings. NAACL.
  - Faruqui, Dyer (2013). An information theoretic approach to bilingual word clustering. ACL.
- Multilingual training to improve source-language embeddings:
  - Suster, Titov, van Noord (2016). Bilingual learning of multi-sense embeddings with discrete autoencoders. NAACL-HLT.
  - Guo, Che, Wang, Liu (2014). Learning sense-specific word embeddings by exploiting bilingual resources. COLING.
- Translational context and sense information:
  - Diab, Resnik (2002). An unsupervised method for word sense tagging using parallel corpora. ACL.

Overall assessment
This paper delivers strong practical contributions: two simple, effective multilingual embedding methods; a principled extension of QVEC via CCA; and a well-engineered evaluation portal. The work is timely and well-presented. My main reservation is the limited depth of analysis in explaining performance patterns and the conditions under which each method excels. Addressing the points above—especially deeper diagnostics for MultiCluster vs. MultiCCA, sensitivity analyses, clearer baseline provenance, and monolingual QVEC vs. QVEC-CCA results—would substantially strengthen the paper’s scientific impact without sacrificing its practical utility.
