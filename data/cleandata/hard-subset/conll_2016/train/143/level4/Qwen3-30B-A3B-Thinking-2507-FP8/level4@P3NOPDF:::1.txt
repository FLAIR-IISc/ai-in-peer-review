

This paper presents four methods for constructing multilingual word embeddings—multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance—and introduces a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The methods extend prior bilingual approaches to multilingual settings: multiCluster maps words to cross-lingual clusters using a dictionary, deriving cluster embeddings; multiCCA adapts Faruqui and Dyer (2014) by anchoring to English embeddings and projecting other languages via bilingual dictionaries; multiSkip generalizes Luong et al. (2015b) by incorporating parallel corpora into the objective function; Translation Invariance employs low-rank decomposition of PMI matrices with bilingual alignment constraints (though its multilingual applicability is questionable). The evaluation leverages CCA to correlate embeddings with linguistic features, producing rotation-invariant scores. The paper is well-written and technically clear, but three major issues require clarification, along with four key questions.

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Novelty Gap**: The Translation Invariance method appears closely related to Gardner et al.’s work. The paper claims multilingual extension as the novelty, but this requires explicit justification. How does the proposed method differ from Gardner et al.’s framework beyond scaling to multiple languages? A concise clarification of the technical novelty would strengthen the contribution.  
2. **Super-Sense Coverage**: The reliance on cross-lingual super-sense annotations (e.g., for the QVEC metric) risks severe sparsity. As noted in footnote 9, the intersection of features across languages may be minimal. The authors must address how they mitigate this (beyond footnote 9), particularly for low-resource language pairs.  
3. **Coverage-Performance Disconnect**: Table 2 shows multiCluster and multiCCA have significantly different coverage rates (e.g., 85% vs. 65% for dependency parsing) yet achieve similar scores (e.g., 78.2 vs. 77.9). How much does coverage influence these scores? If coverage differences are negligible, the results may reflect other factors, but this needs explicit analysis.  

**Inconsistent Results**  
Table 3 reveals a concerning lack of consistency. While the paper aims to develop embeddings excelling in *intra-language* translation tasks (per the QVEC metric), the Translation Invariance approach dominates this metric (e.g., 78.1 vs. 75.3 for multiSkip). However, for *extrinsic* metrics (e.g., dependency parsing), multiCluster—despite discarding inter-cluster semantic information—outperforms all others (e.g., 78.2 vs. 76.5 for multiCCA). This contradicts the authors’ stated goal of preserving cross-lingual structure, as multiCluster’s approach minimizes such information. The authors should reconcile this paradox.  

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. **Dependency Parsing Trade-offs**: How much does fixing embeddings (vs. fine-tuning) impact performance in dependency parsing? What specific gain does using these embeddings provide over random initialization in the LSTM parser?  
2. **Table 1 Interpretation**: Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1? If so, this should be explicitly stated.  
3. **multiSkip vs. MultiCCA**: What advantages does multiSkip offer over a two-step approach (learning bilingual embeddings + multiCCA projections)? If none, the added complexity of multiSkip warrants justification.  
4. **Dictionary Realism**: The dictionary extraction (via parallel corpora or Google Translate) may not reflect real-world lexicon challenges. Did the authors test with actual multilingual dictionaries (e.g., BabelNet)? If not, how might this affect the embeddings’ practical utility?  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper addresses a relevant problem with a clear methodology, but the claims require stronger justification. The Translation Invariance novelty is underdeveloped, coverage limitations are inadequately addressed, and the results (particularly Table 3) lack coherence with the paper’s core objectives. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the work. The technical execution is sound, and the writing is accessible, but the contribution’s significance hinges on resolving the concerns above. With revisions, this could be a valuable addition to multilingual embedding research.
