

This paper presents four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding approaches are:  
(1) *multiCluster*: Maps words to multilingual clusters via a dictionary, using cluster embeddings as word representations.  
(2) *multiCCA*: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multilingual settings by anchoring embeddings in English space; bilingual dictionaries (*other_language* → English) project monolingual embeddings into this anchor space.  
(3) *multiSkip*: Adapts Luong et al. (2015b) to multilingual contexts by extending the objective function to incorporate parallel corpora from all languages.  
(4) *Translation invariance*: Leverages low-rank decomposition of word PMI matrices with bilingual alignment constraints; primarily designed for bilingual embeddings (the multilingual extension appears limited).  

The evaluation employs CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data (e.g., super-sense annotations), deriving rotation-invariant scores via aligned basis vectors. The method further generalizes this to multilingual settings.  

The paper is well-written and clearly describes the methodology. However, four major concerns require clarification:  
(1) **Novelty of translation invariance extension**: The paper claims to extend Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach to multilingual embeddings. A brief clarification of this novelty—specifically how the multilingual extension differs from prior bilingual work—is essential.  
(2) **Super-sense coverage across languages**: The reliance on cross-lingual super-sense annotations risks severe feature sparsity in language intersections. How do the authors address this beyond footnote 9? For instance, do they use feature smoothing or language-specific subsets?  
(3) **Coverage impact on Table 2 results**: For dependency parsing, *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* exhibit significantly different coverage rates yet comparable scores. To what extent does coverage drive these results? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the evaluation.  
(4) **Inconsistent results in Table 3**: The intrinsic metrics show no clear advantage for multilingual embeddings. Notably, the translation invariance method (despite its bilingual origins) outperforms others by a large margin on the word translation metric—contrary to the paper’s core objective. Additionally, *multiCluster* (which discards inter-cluster semantic information) achieves the best extrinsic results, which is counterintuitive.  

**Additional questions for the authors**:  
(1) What performance loss occurs when fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing? How much did using these embeddings (vs. random initialization) improve the LSTM stack parser?  
(2) Is Table 1 averaged over the 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1?  
(3) What advantages does *multiSkip* offer over learning bilingual embeddings followed by *multiCCA* projections?  
(4) The dictionary extraction (via parallel corpora or Google Translate) may not reflect real-world lexicon challenges. Did the authors test embeddings with actual multilingual dictionaries (e.g., BabelNet)?
