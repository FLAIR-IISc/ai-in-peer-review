- Introduces two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) that use only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries, no parallel corpora.  
- multiCCA extends bilingual embeddings to multilingual settings using English as an anchor space.  
- Proposes multiQVECCCA, an improved intrinsic evaluation metric addressing theoretical shortcomings of prior metrics.  
- Builds a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings across nine metrics with dev/test modes.  
- multiQVECCCA demonstrates substantially better correlation with downstream tasks (e.g., 0.896 for document classification, 0.273 for parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics.  
- The evaluation portal is practical and will facilitate future research by providing standardized benchmarks and interfaces.  
- Methods successfully produce embeddings for 59 languages from dictionaries and monolingual data, valuable for low-resource languages.  
- Novelty of extending the translation-invariance approach to multilingual settings is not clearly articulated or justified.  
- Using supersense annotations across languages may yield feature intersections that are too small to be meaningful; no concrete solution is offered.  
- Evaluation results are inconsistent: the translation-invariance method outperforms the proposed methods on 4 of 8 metrics, including word translation.  
- Coverage differences between methods (e.g., multiCluster 70.9% vs. multiCCA 69.3% for parsing) significantly affect results but are not adequately addressed.  
- Question: How do methods compare when evaluated only on words covered by all four methods to control for coverage confounds?  
- Question: Why does multiCluster, which forces translationally equivalent words to share embeddings, perform best on extrinsic metrics despite theoretically discarding semantic distinctions?  
- Question: Did the authors try established multilingual dictionaries (e.g., Wiktionary) instead of Google Translate for dictionary extraction to reflect real-world challenges?  
- Question: Clarify the apparent discrepancy in Table 1 where multiQVEC (0.635) vs. multiQVECCCA (0.896) is discussed for document classification correlation.  
- Overall: The paper provides valuable practical tools and an improved metric, but inconsistent results and insufficient justification of the multilingual extension weaken its primary claims.
