

This paper presents four approaches for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The embedding methods are:  
(1) multiCluster: Leverages a dictionary to assign words to multilingual clusters, with cluster embeddings serving as representations for all words within each cluster.  
(2) multiCCA: Adapts Faruqui and Dyer (2014)’s bilingual embedding method to multilingual settings by using English embeddings as an anchor space; bilingual dictionaries (other_language → English) project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this anchor space.  
(3) multiSkip: Extends Luong et al. (2015b)’s context-based bilingual embedding approach to multilingual scenarios by modifying the objective function to incorporate components from all available parallel corpora.  
(4) Translation invariance: Employs low-rank decomposition of word PMI matrices with an objective incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies; may be limited to bilingual embeddings.  

The evaluation method utilizes CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and potentially hand-crafted linguistic data, deriving basis vectors for aligned dimensions to produce rotation- and linear-transformation-invariant scores, extended to multilingual settings.  

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly describes the methodology. However, three major concerns require clarification:  
(1) The novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance embedding approach is unclear. If the extension to multilingual embeddings constitutes the primary contribution, a concise explanation of this novelty is needed.  
(2) The reliance on super-sense annotations across languages risks severe feature intersection sparsity. Beyond Footnote 9, how do the authors address this issue?  
(3) Coverage differences in Table 2 (e.g., multiCluster vs. multiCCA for dependency parsing) appear to conflict with their similar scores; how significantly does coverage impact the results?  
(4) Table 3 results lack consistency: multilingual embedding techniques rarely outperform baselines on intrinsic metrics, despite the paper’s goal of optimizing for the word translation metric (intra-language). Notably, the translation invariance method achieves the best performance (by a large margin), while multiCluster—discarding inter-cluster semantic information—leads extrinsic metrics. This is counterintuitive.  

Additional queries for the authors:  
(1) What performance loss occurs when fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing? What specific gain do these embeddings provide over random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?  
(2) Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1?  
(3) What advantages does multiSkip offer over learning bilingual embeddings followed by multiCCA projection across distinct spaces?  
(4) The dictionary extraction method (via parallel corpus alignments or Google Translate) may not reflect real-world lexicon challenges. Did the authors test real multilingual dictionaries?
