- multiCluster: maps words into multilingual clusters via a dictionary; cluster embeddings are used for all words in a cluster.  
- multiCCA: extends bilingual CCA to multilingual by using English as an anchor and projecting other languages via bilingual dictionaries.  
- multiSkip: extends bilingual source-target context alignment to multilingual by modifying the skip-gram objective to use all parallel corpora.  
- Translation invariance: low-rank PMI decomposition with bilingual alignment frequency terms; possibly limited to bilingual embeddings and novelty vs. prior work unclear.  
- Evaluation: uses CCA to align embeddings with hand-crafted linguistic features, yielding a rotation- and linear-transformation-invariant multilingual QVEC variant.  
- Paper quality: well-written and clearly described.  
- Major concern 1: novelty over Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach is unclear—needs explicit clarification if multilingual extension is the main contribution.  
- Major concern 2: using super-sense annotations across languages risks extremely small feature intersections—insufficiently addressed beyond a footnote.  
- Major concern 3: large coverage differences (e.g., multiCluster vs. multiCCA) but similar scores—need analysis of how coverage affects results.  
- Major concern 4: Table 3 is inconsistent with paper goals—multilingual methods rarely win intrinsic metrics, translation invariance dominates, and multiCluster performs best on extrinsic tasks despite losing inter-cluster semantics.  
- Question 1: what is the performance loss when fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing, and what gain did the LSTM parser get over random embeddings?  
- Question 2: is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?  
- Question 3: does multiSkip offer advantages over training bilingual embeddings separately and then projecting them into a shared space with multiCCA?  
- Question 4: dictionary extraction using parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real lexicon challenges—did the authors evaluate with real multilingual dictionaries?
