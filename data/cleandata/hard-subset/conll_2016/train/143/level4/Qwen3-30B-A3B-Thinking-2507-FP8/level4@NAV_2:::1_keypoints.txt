- Summary: Proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) for multilingual embeddings across 59 languages without parallel data, introduces multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, and a web portal for standardized evaluation.
- Contribution 1: multiQVEC-CCA corrects theoretical limitations of prior metrics and shows stronger correlation with downstream tasks.
- Contribution 2: A web portal that enables standardized, reproducible evaluation and lowers barriers for multilingual embedding research.
- Contribution 3: Dictionary-based embedding methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) demonstrating scalable 59-language embeddings, though secondary to the evaluation contribution.
- Strength 1: multiQVEC-CCA is invariant to linear transformations, normalized, and correlates much better with extrinsic tasks (e.g., 0.896 vs. 0.635 for multiQVEC and 0.386 for word similarity).
- Strength 2: The evaluation portal provides downloadable datasets, standardized tasks, and an interface for uploading embeddings, addressing reproducibility needs.
- Strength 3: Demonstrates large-scale (59-language) embeddings using only dictionaries and monolingual data, improving scalability over bilingual-focused work.
- Weakness 1: Coverage differences (e.g., multiCluster 70.9% vs. multiCCA 69.3%) confound performance comparisons and are insufficiently discussed.
- Weakness 2: Translation-invariance method outperforms dictionary methods on 4/9 metrics (including word translation 63.9 vs. multiCCA 35.7), contradicting the paper’s core claim.
- Weakness 3: Reliance on supersense annotations limits scalability because the intersection across languages is small; no viable alternative proposed.
- Weakness 4: Dictionary extraction uses Google Translate for 47 languages, which may not reflect real-world resource constraints; no evaluation with real multilingual lexicons (e.g., BabelNet).
- Weakness 5: multiCluster assigns identical vectors to translation equivalents, which seems theoretically at odds with preserving cross-lingual semantics despite strong extrinsic performance; this paradox is unexplained.
- Question 1: How do you reconcile translation-invariance’s superior word translation performance (63.9) with claims favoring dictionary-based methods (multiCCA 35.7)?
- Question 2: In Table 3, how was vocabulary mismatch handled given coverage differences (multiCCA 69.3% vs. translation-invariance 68.6%) and why aren’t these differences reflected in scores?
- Question 3: What is the impact of coverage differences (e.g., 70.9% vs. 69.3%) on dependency parsing results and the interpretation of multiCluster’s competitiveness?
- Question 4: Did you evaluate embeddings using real multilingual lexicons (e.g., BabelNet) to test practicality beyond Google Translate-based dictionaries?
