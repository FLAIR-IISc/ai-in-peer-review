

This paper presents four approaches for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding methods are:  
(1) *multiCluster*: Maps words to multilingual clusters using a dictionary; cluster embeddings serve as word embeddings for all words within each cluster.  
(2) *multiCCA*: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014) for bilingual embeddings to the multilingual setting by using English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language → English) project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this anchor space.  
(3) *multiSkip*: Extends Luong et al. (2015b) for bilingual embeddings using source-target context alignment to the multilingual case by modifying the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora.  
(4) *Translation invariance*: Employs low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective including bilingual alignment frequency components; may be limited to bilingual embeddings.  

The evaluation method uses CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and hand-crafted linguistic data. Basis vectors for aligned dimensions yield a rotation- and linear-transformation-invariant score, extended to multilingual evaluations.  

The paper is well-written and clearly describes the work. However, several major concerns require clarification:  
(1) The novelty with respect to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach is unclear. If the extension to multilingual embeddings is the key contribution, a brief clarification of this novelty is needed.  
(2) The use of super-sense annotations across multiple languages risks extremely small feature intersections. How do the authors address this issue beyond footnote 9?  
(3) Table 2 shows significant coverage differences between *multiCluster* and *multiCCA* (e.g., for dependency parsing), yet their scores are similar. How much does coverage impact the scores?  
(4) Table 3 results are inconsistent: multilingual methods rarely outperform others on intrinsic metrics, despite the paper’s primary goal of optimizing for word translation (intra-language). Notably, the *translation invariance* approach dominates (by a large margin), and *multiCluster*—which discards inter-cluster semantic information—performs best on extrinsic metrics. This is counterintuitive.  

Additional questions:  
(1) What performance loss occurs when fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing? What was the gain over random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?  
(2) Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?  
(3) Are there advantages of *multiSkip* over learning bilingual embeddings followed by *multiCCA* projections across distinct spaces?  
(4) The dictionary extraction (via parallel corpora alignments or Google Translate) may not reflect real lexicon challenges. Did the authors explore real multilingual dictionaries?
