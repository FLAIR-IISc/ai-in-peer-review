

This paper introduces two novel dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise bilingual dictionaries, without needing parallel corpora. The authors also propose an improved evaluation metric, multiQVECCCA, which demonstrates stronger correlation with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, and develop a web portal to facilitate future research in this area. The primary contributions are: (1) the multiCCA method, which extends bilingual embeddings to multilingual settings using English as an anchor space; (2) the multiQVECCCA evaluation metric, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous approaches; and (3) the web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

Strengths:
1. The multiQVECCCA metric represents a significant methodological advancement, demonstrating substantially better correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 for document classification, 0.273 for parsing) compared to previous intrinsic metrics (0.386 and 0.007 for word similarity, 0.066 and -0.292 for word translation). This provides researchers with a more reliable tool for evaluating multilingual embeddings.
2. The paper's evaluation portal is highly practical and will substantially facilitate future research, offering standardized benchmarks and an interface for evaluating embeddings across nine metrics with development/test modes to encourage sound experimentation practices.
3. The methods successfully train embeddings for 59 languages using only dictionaries and monolingual data, demonstrating the feasibility of large-scale multilingual embedding estimation without parallel corpora, which is particularly valuable for low-resource languages.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of extending the translation-invariance approach (Gardner et al., 2015) to multilingual settings isn't clearly articulated. The authors should explicitly explain how their multilingual extension differs from the bilingual approach and why it's a meaningful contribution beyond simple extension.
2. The use of supersense annotations across multiple languages creates a significant practical limitation, as the intersection of features across languages may become too small to be meaningful. The authors' footnote 9 acknowledges this but doesn't provide a concrete solution for future work.
3. The evaluation results show inconsistent performance across metrics, undermining the paper's core claim that their methods produce superior embeddings. Specifically, the translation-invariance method outperforms all proposed methods on four of the eight evaluation metrics, including the word translation metric that was one of the primary goals of the work.
4. The coverage differences between methods (e.g., multiCluster: 70.9% vs. multiCCA: 69.3% for dependency parsing) significantly impact results, yet the authors don't adequately address how these differences affect the interpretation of their findings.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the performance of the translation-invariance method compare to the proposed methods when evaluating only on words covered by all four methods? This would address the coverage confound in Table 3.
2. The multiCluster method theoretically discards semantic information by forcing translationally equivalent words to share the same embedding, yet it performs best on extrinsic metrics. How do the authors explain this counterintuitive result?
3. The dictionary extraction approach using Google Translate may not reflect real-world challenges. Did the authors explore using established multilingual dictionaries (e.g., from Wiktionary or existing lexicons) instead of machine-translated dictionaries?
4. The paper claims that multiQVECCCA "correlates better than previous ones with two downstream tasks," but Table 1 shows multiQVEC (not multiQVECCCA) has the strongest correlation with document classification (0.635 vs. 0.896). Please clarify this discrepancy.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual NLP by providing practical tools for embedding estimation and evaluation. While the core evaluation metric represents a significant advance, the inconsistent results across metrics and insufficient justification of the multilingual extension of translation-invariance approach weaken the paper's primary claims. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the contribution.
