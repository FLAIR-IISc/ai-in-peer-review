

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages in a shared vector space. The primary contributions are:  
1. A novel evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which significantly outperforms existing intrinsic metrics in correlation with downstream tasks (document classification and parsing).  
2. A web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will facilitate reproducible research in this area.  
3. Two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and dictionaries, avoiding the need for parallel corpora.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical validation of evaluation metrics**: The paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA achieves high correlation (0.896) with document classification performance, substantially outperforming existing metrics (e.g., cross-lingual word similarity at 0.386). This directly addresses a critical gap in multilingual NLP research, where intrinsic evaluation metrics have historically lacked strong alignment with downstream task performance.  
2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal**: The web portal enables researchers to easily evaluate embeddings across nine metrics, with development/test modes to encourage rigorous experimentation. This infrastructure will significantly lower barriers to entry for future work in multilingual embeddings, as evidenced by the paper's open-source release of all methods and data.  
3. **Clear demonstration of methodological innovation**: The paper successfully extends bilingual embedding techniques (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014) to the multilingual setting via multiCCA, while showing that multiCluster—despite its semantic ambiguity—remains competitive in certain scenarios. The analysis of why multiCluster underperforms with 59 languages (due to cluster-level semantic ambiguity) provides valuable theoretical insight.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of clarity on novelty over Gardner et al. (2015)**: The paper claims to extend translation-invariance to multilingual embeddings but fails to clearly articulate how this differs from Gardner's method. The translation-invariance approach (as described) appears fundamentally limited to two languages, yet the paper uses it for 59 languages without justification. This undermines a key claim of the work.  
2. **Inconsistent results across evaluation metrics**: The paper's primary goal—creating embeddings that excel at word translation (a core multilingual objective)—is not met by the proposed methods. The translation-invariance approach consistently outperforms multiCCA and multiCluster on word translation (63.9% vs. 35.7% and 43.7%), which contradicts the paper's emphasis on this metric. The claim that "the proposed methods work well in some settings" lacks specificity, as the results show no clear dominance of the authors' methods.  
3. **Coverage issues confounding results**: The paper acknowledges coverage differences (e.g., multiCCA covers 69.3% vs. multiCluster's 70.9% in dependency parsing), yet these differences are not systematically addressed in evaluation. For instance, multiCCA's slightly lower coverage does not explain its superior performance on monolingual word similarity (43.0% vs. 38.0%), suggesting coverage may not be the primary factor. The authors should clarify whether coverage differences drive the observed performance gaps.  
4. **Super-sense annotation limitations**: The paper notes that supersense annotations across languages are inconsistent (footnote 9), yet the evaluation relies on them. The authors do not propose a scalable solution for this problem, which limits the generalizability of their evaluation metric. This is particularly problematic given the paper's claim that multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming" of QVEC.  
5. **Limited exploration of real-world dictionaries**: The dictionary construction (via Google Translate or parallel corpus alignment) does not reflect the challenges of using actual multilingual lexicons. The authors do not test their methods with curated dictionaries (e.g., BabelNet), which would strengthen the practical relevance of their work.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does multiCCA's extension of translation-invariance (Gardner et al., 2015) differ from the original method, and why is it applicable to 59 languages when Gardner's approach is explicitly bilingual?  
2. Why does the translation-invariance method dominate on word translation (the paper's stated goal), while multiCCA outperforms it on downstream tasks? Does this suggest the word translation metric is misaligned with practical utility?  
3. How would the results change if evaluation were restricted to the vocabulary intersection across all methods (to eliminate coverage bias), as mentioned in Section 5.2?  
4. Could the authors provide examples where multiCluster's cluster-level embeddings improve downstream performance compared to word-level embeddings, given the paper's claim that "the added semantic ambiguity severely hurts performance"?
