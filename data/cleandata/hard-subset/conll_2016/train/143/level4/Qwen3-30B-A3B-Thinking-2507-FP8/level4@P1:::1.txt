

This paper proposes two novel dictionary-based methods for multilingual word embedding estimation (multiCluster and multiCCA), extends QVEC to multilingual evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA), and introduces a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The authors demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA correlates significantly better with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, and that their dictionary-based methods can produce embeddings for 59 languages without requiring parallel data.

Strengths:
1. The proposed evaluation framework significantly advances the field. The authors demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA correlates much more strongly with downstream performance (0.896 with document classification, 0.273 with parsing) than existing metrics (max correlation of 0.386 for word similarity), addressing a critical gap in the literature. This represents a substantial methodological contribution.

2. The web portal they developed provides a valuable resource for the community. It enables standardized evaluation across multiple metrics and languages, with clear development/test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. This will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP by reducing barriers to reproducible evaluation.

3. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to multilingual embedding estimation. The methods work well for 59 languages using only monolingual data and dictionaries, avoiding the need for expensive parallel corpora. Their approach is practical and directly addresses a key challenge in building multilingual systems.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the multilingual extension to translation-invariance is insufficiently justified. Gardner et al. (2015) explicitly state their method is designed for bilingual embeddings, and the authors fail to clearly articulate how their extension to multiple languages constitutes a meaningful contribution beyond a straightforward generalization. The paper should better clarify this distinction.

2. The evaluation framework has a significant limitation regarding supersense annotations. The authors acknowledge that the number of features in the intersection of multiple languages may become very small, and their proposed solution (using a consistent set of supersense tags across languages) is only mentioned in a footnote. This is a critical issue that requires more substantial discussion, as it limits the applicability of their evaluation method.

3. The results in Table 3 are inconsistent and somewhat contradictory. While the paper's primary goal was to create embeddings that perform well under the word translation metric, the translation-invariance method consistently outperforms the proposed methods on most intrinsic metrics, including word translation. The multiCluster method, which discards inter-cluster semantic information, surprisingly performs best on extrinsic metrics. The authors should address this unexpected finding more thoroughly.

4. The coverage differences across methods (e.g., 70.9% vs. 69.3% for multiCluster vs. multiCCA in dependency parsing) significantly impact the results, yet the paper does not adequately address how these coverage differences affect the comparison. The authors should provide results on the intersection vocabulary to ensure fair comparison.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does fixing the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task affect performance compared to training embeddings alongside the parser? What was the specific performance gain from using these embeddings versus random embeddings?

2. Is Table 1 an average over the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1, or does it represent a different set of embeddings? The paper states they trained 17 embeddings but Table 1 appears to represent results across multiple embeddings.

3. What are the advantages of using multiSkip instead of first learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA to project across spaces? The paper does not clearly articulate this distinction.

4. The dictionary extraction approach (using Google Translate or parallel corpora) may not reflect the challenges of using real multilingual lexicons. Did the authors explore using any real multilingual dictionaries, and if so, how did the results compare?
