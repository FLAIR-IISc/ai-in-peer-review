- Paper proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (dictionary-based clusters), multiCCA (English-anchored projections), multiSkip (multilingual context-based objective), and translation-invariance (low-rank PMI decomposition, possibly limited to bilinguals).  
- Evaluation uses a modified QVEC with CCA to align embeddings to linguistic annotations, producing rotation- and linear-transformation-invariant multilingual scores.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly describes the methodology.  
- Major concern 1: Novelty relative to Gardner et al.’s translation-invariance approach is unclear; need clarification if the multilingual extension is the main contribution.  
- Major concern 2: Reliance on super-sense annotations risks severe feature intersection sparsity; authors must address this beyond Footnote 9.  
- Major concern 3: Coverage differences in Table 2 (e.g., multiCluster vs. multiCCA) may confound similar scores; quantify how coverage affects results.  
- Major concern 4: Table 3 shows inconsistencies—multilingual methods rarely outperform baselines on intrinsic metrics; translation-invariance wins intrinsically while multiCluster leads extrinsically, which is counterintuitive and unexplained.  
- Additional query 1: What is the performance loss when fixing word embeddings in dependency parsing, and what specific gains do these embeddings provide over random embeddings in the LSTM parser?  
- Additional query 2: Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?  
- Additional query 3: What advantages does multiSkip offer over training bilingual embeddings and then projecting them into a shared space via multiCCA?  
- Additional query 4: Dictionary extraction using parallel alignments or Google Translate may not reflect real-world lexicons—did the authors test real multilingual dictionaries?
