

This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for multilingual word embedding estimation—MultiCluster (clustering-inspired) and MultiCCA (CCA-inspired)—and proposes an improved supersense similarity measure (QVEC-CCA) that replaces QVEC’s correlation component with CCA and incorporates multilingual evaluation. The authors evaluate these approaches across diverse tasks using a custom web portal, demonstrating competitive performance against baselines in specific settings.  

The paper is exceptionally well-written, meticulously documented, and addresses timely research challenges. The practical contributions—enhanced QVEC-CCA and the evaluation portal—hold significant value for the community. However, while the scope is broad and the implementation thorough, the work prioritizes breadth over depth in its analysis. Key results warrant deeper exploration, particularly:  
1. The conflicting performance of MultiCluster across 59- vs. 12-language setups (Section 4.2), where it outperforms baselines in one configuration but not the other.  
2. The sensitivity of MultiCluster/CCA to parameter choices and estimation decisions (e.g., dictionary quality, CCA dimensionality), which are currently underdiscussed.  
Without this analysis, the paper’s core contributions—especially the *why* behind the methods’ varying efficacy—remain partially obscured. As a result, the work offers strong utility but limited novel theoretical or methodological insights for the research community.  

**Specific Recommendations:**  
- **Table 1**: Include correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA to directly substantiate the claim (lines 326–328) that QVEC-CCA improves upon QVEC.  
- **Line 304**: Clarify "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" to avoid implying heterogeneity; these datasets are linguistically aligned (e.g., same task across languages), so rephrase as "multiple language pairs from a unified cross-lingual word similarity benchmark."  
- **Baseline Citations (Section 2.3)**: Resolve the discrepancy between the MultiSkip baseline citations (lines 121, 147: Coulmance et al., Guo et al.) and the subsequent reference to Luong et al. (2015). Explicitly state the relationship (e.g., "Luong et al. (2015) extend the MultiSkip approach introduced by Guo et al. (2014)").  
- **Related Work**: Expand to include these relevant contributions:  
  *For multilingual embeddings/clustering*: Chandar et al. (2014) on autoencoder-based bilingual embeddings; Hill et al. (2014) on neural machine translation for similarity; Faruqui & Dyer (2013) on information-theoretic bilingual clustering.  
  *For multilingual training enhancing source embeddings*: Suster et al. (2016) on sense-specific embeddings via discrete autoencoders; Guo et al. (2014) on sense-specific embeddings using bilingual resources.  
  *For translational context*: Diab & Resnik (2002) on unsupervised sense tagging via parallel corpora (to contextualize the supersense approach).  

**Minor Technical Notes**:  
- Page 3: Adjust equations exceeding column margins for readability.  
- Section 4.1: Briefly clarify why the 59-language setup (vs. 12-language) was chosen for MultiCluster’s conflicting results.  

**Overall Assessment**:  
This is a strong, practical contribution with clear implementation value. The methods address real-world needs (e.g., improved evaluation via QVEC-CCA, accessible benchmarking via the portal), and the writing is exemplary. However, the lack of deeper analysis into *why* certain methods succeed or fail limits its scientific impact. To elevate the paper, the authors should substantially expand the discussion of results—particularly the MultiCluster inconsistencies and parameter sensitivity—to transform it from a "good implementation" into a "meaningful advancement." With these revisions, the paper would be a compelling addition to CONLL proceedings. As it stands, it is a solid submission with room for significant refinement.
