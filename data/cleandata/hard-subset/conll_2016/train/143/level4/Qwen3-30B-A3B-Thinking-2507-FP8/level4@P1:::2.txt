

Summary:
This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, without needing parallel data. The authors also propose an improved evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous intrinsic metrics and demonstrates better correlation with downstream tasks. Finally, they provide a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, along with open-source implementations. The main contributions are: (1) practical methods for estimating multilingual embeddings without parallel data, (2) a more effective evaluation metric that better correlates with extrinsic tasks, and (3) a valuable community resource through their evaluation portal.

Strengths:
1. The proposed evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) represents a significant contribution to the field. By addressing theoretical shortcomings of QVEC (e.g., lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores), the authors provide a more robust metric that consistently correlates better with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics. This is a major step forward in evaluating multilingual embeddings.

2. The proposed methods are highly practical, requiring only widely available resources (monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries), which makes them accessible to a broad range of researchers without the need for expensive parallel data. This accessibility is a substantial advantage over existing approaches.

3. The web portal is an excellent practical resource that will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP. By providing standardized evaluation metrics, development/test data sets, and a platform for uploading embeddings, the authors create a valuable infrastructure that promotes reproducibility and progress in the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient depth in analyzing the performance discrepancy between the 59-language and 12-language settings for multiCluster. The authors note that multiCluster performs poorly with 59 languages but competes with baselines for 12 languages, yet provide no detailed explanation for this difference. A thorough analysis of how language coverage scale affects the performance of their methods would strengthen the paper considerably.

2. The paper does not adequately explore the sensitivity of the methods to parameter choices (e.g., cluster size in multiCluster, dictionary extraction threshold τ). This lack of analysis makes it difficult to understand how to best apply these methods in practice and whether the reported results are robust to reasonable parameter variations.

3. The results show inconsistent ranking across different evaluation metrics, with no method consistently outperforming others. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not sufficiently explain why this inconsistency occurs or which method is generally preferable. This undermines the claim that their methods "work well in some settings."

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on why multiCluster performs significantly worse with 59 languages compared to 12 languages, despite being competitive in the smaller setting? What specific aspects of the larger language set cause this degradation in performance?

2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of dictionary extraction threshold (τ = 0.1) and cluster size parameters? Would tuning these parameters improve performance, and if so, how?

3. The evaluation metric multiQVEC-CCA relies on supersense annotations from English, Italian, and Danish. How would the metric perform if extended to languages with less available linguistic resources, and what alternatives might be viable for such languages?
