

**Summary**  
This paper introduces two novel dictionary-based methods—multiCluster and multiCCA—for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages without requiring parallel data. The primary contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric that addresses theoretical shortcomings of QVEC and demonstrates stronger correlation with downstream tasks (text categorization and parsing) compared to existing intrinsic metrics. A secondary contribution is a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, which includes benchmarks and facilitates future research. The paper also provides open-source implementations of all methods and a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and tasks.

**Strengths**  
1. **Significant advancement in evaluation methodology**: The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric substantially improves upon QVEC by addressing its two key limitations (basis dependence and unnormalized scoring), resulting in stronger correlations with downstream tasks (Table 1). This is a critical contribution, as reliable evaluation has been a major bottleneck in multilingual embedding research.

2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal**: The authors' web portal provides a much-needed standardized framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings, with nine metrics across development and test modes. This will significantly lower barriers for future research and improve reproducibility, as the portal includes shared datasets and evaluation protocols.

3. **Comprehensive empirical validation**: The paper demonstrates the utility of the proposed methods across 59 languages, showing that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster in the large-scale setting. The correlation analysis (Table 1) convincingly establishes that the authors' evaluation metrics are more meaningful than existing alternatives.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient analysis of methodological limitations**: The paper notes that multiCluster performs poorly with 59 languages but competes with baselines in 12 languages, yet fails to provide a thorough analysis of why this discrepancy occurs. The authors should explore whether this is due to language diversity, dictionary quality, or inherent limitations of the clustering approach.

2. **Lack of parameter sensitivity analysis**: The paper uses fixed parameters (e.g., τ = 0.1 for dictionary construction, cluster size limits) without exploring their impact on performance. For instance, how do results change with different τ values or cluster size thresholds? This omission limits the method's practical applicability.

3. **Limited language diversity in evaluation**: The multiQVEC-CCA metric is evaluated only on English, Italian, and Danish, which are all Indo-European languages. The paper should demonstrate its effectiveness across more linguistically diverse language pairs to establish broader validity.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper shows that multiCluster outperforms multiCCA in the 12-language setting but underperforms in the 59-language setting. What specific factors (e.g., language diversity, dictionary quality, or semantic ambiguity) explain this discrepancy, and how might these be mitigated?

2. The authors use Google Translate to construct dictionaries for 47 languages. How does the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries impact the performance of the proposed methods, and what error margin can be expected from this approach?

3. The paper mentions that "multiCluster exacerbates semantic ambiguity by estimating one vector per cluster of translationally equivalent words." Can the authors elaborate on how this limitation manifests in the 59-language setting and whether there are potential modifications to the method that could address this issue?

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-written and demonstrates substantial technical effort, particularly in the development of the evaluation portal. The authors should clarify the relationship between MultiSkip and the works by Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016), as the paper currently refers to these works inconsistently. Additionally, the mention of "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" (l. 304) is misleading; these are variations of the same dataset across different language pairs, not fundamentally different datasets.
