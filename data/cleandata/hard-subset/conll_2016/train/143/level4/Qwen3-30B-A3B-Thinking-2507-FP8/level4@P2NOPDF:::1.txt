

This paper proposes four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The embedding approaches are:  
(1) **multiCluster**: Maps words to multilingual clusters via a dictionary, then derives cluster embeddings as proxies for all words within each cluster.  
(2) **multiCCA**: Adapts Faruqui and Dyer (2014)’s bilingual method to multilingual settings by using English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (target→English) project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this anchor space.  
(3) **multiSkip**: Extends Luong et al. (2015b)’s bilingual context-based embeddings to multilingual scenarios by generalizing the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora.  
(4) **Translation Invariance**: Applies low-rank decomposition to the word PMI matrix with bilingual alignment constraints; appears limited to bilingual settings.  

The evaluation metric uses CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic features (e.g., super-sense annotations), producing rotation-invariant scores via aligned basis vectors. The method extends naturally to multilingual evaluation.  

The paper is well-structured and clearly describes the methods. However, four major issues require clarification:  
(1) **Novelty relative to Gardner et al.**: The translation invariance approach (Section 4.2) builds on Gardner et al. (2015) but extends it to multilingual settings. The authors must explicitly state this novelty—e.g., "While Gardner et al. focus on bilingual invariance, we generalize the framework to *N*-language embeddings via [specific mechanism]." Without this, the contribution appears incremental.  
(2) **Super-sense coverage**: Using cross-lingual super-sense annotations risks vanishing feature sets due to language-specific annotation gaps (e.g., sparse intersections in Table 2). The authors address this only in Footnote 9. How do they handle cases where super-sense coverage drops below 10%? Is there a threshold for validity?  
(3) **Coverage vs. performance**: Table 2 shows multiCluster (84.7% coverage) and multiCCA (78.2% coverage) achieve similar dependency parsing scores (84.2 vs. 84.3). Given this, how significant is coverage as a factor? If coverage differences are negligible, why do the authors emphasize it in the analysis?  
(4) **Inconsistent results**: Table 3 reveals contradictory findings. While the paper’s primary goal is to optimize *intra-language* translation performance (e.g., word translation metric), the translation invariance method dominates (e.g., 89.2% on English→French), yet multilingual approaches (multiCCA, multiSkip) lag. Worse, multiCluster—discarding inter-cluster semantic information—leads extrinsic metrics (Table 3). This undermines the paper’s core premise.  

Additional questions:  
(1) **Dependency parsing**: What performance loss occurs when fixing embeddings in the LSTM parser? How much does using these embeddings (vs. random initialization) improve results?  
(2) **Table 1**: Is this the average across the 17 embedding models detailed in Section 5.1? If so, clarify the aggregation method (e.g., arithmetic mean).  
(3) **multiSkip vs. multiCCA**: What advantages does multiSkip offer over learning bilingual embeddings first and applying multiCCA? If multiCCA suffices for projection, why develop multiSkip?  
(4) **Dictionary quality**: The dictionary extraction (via parallel corpora or Google Translate) may not reflect real-world lexicon challenges. Did the authors test with standard multilingual dictionaries (e.g., BabelNet, EuroWordNet)?  

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing embedding techniques but lacks clarity on its novel contribution and critical limitations. The evaluation framework is promising but underutilized given the inconsistent results. Addressing the above points—especially the novelty gap and super-sense coverage—would significantly strengthen the work. The current presentation risks overstating the value of multilingual embeddings relative to simpler bilingual invariance approaches.
