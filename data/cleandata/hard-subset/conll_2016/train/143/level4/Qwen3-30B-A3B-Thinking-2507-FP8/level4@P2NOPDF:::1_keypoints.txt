- Proposes four multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (dictionary-based clusters), multiCCA (English-anchored CCA projections), multiSkip (multilingual extension of context-based skip model), and Translation Invariance (low-rank PMI with bilingual alignment).  
- Introduces a modified QVEC evaluation using CCA to produce rotation-invariant, multilingual-capable scores against linguistic features (e.g., super-sense annotations).  
- Paper is well-structured and methods are clearly described.  
- Major issue 1: Novelty unclear—translation invariance builds on Gardner et al. (2015); authors must explicitly state and justify their N-language generalization mechanism.  
- Major issue 2: Super-sense coverage problems—cross-lingual annotation sparsity can shrink feature sets (Table 2); handling of very low coverage (<10%) is only in a footnote and needs clearer thresholds/criteria.  
- Major issue 3: Coverage vs. performance—multiCluster and multiCCA have different coverage but nearly identical dependency parsing scores, questioning the emphasis on coverage as a key factor.  
- Major issue 4: Inconsistent results—translation invariance outperforms multilingual methods on translation metrics, and multiCluster (which ignores inter-cluster semantics) performs well on extrinsic tasks, undermining the claimed benefits of multilingual approaches.  
- Question: How much performance is lost when embeddings are fixed in the LSTM parser, and how much do these embeddings help vs. random initialization?  
- Question: Clarify Table 1 aggregation—does it average the 17 models from Section 5.1 and by which mean (arithmetic, etc.)?  
- Question: Justify multiSkip—what advantages does it offer over learning bilingual embeddings first and then applying multiCCA for projection?  
- Question: Dictionary realism—did authors evaluate using standard multilingual lexica (e.g., BabelNet, EuroWordNet) rather than extracted/Google Translate dictionaries?  
- Summary: Technically sound extension of existing methods with a promising evaluation framework, but lacks clarity on novelty and limitations; inconsistent empirical results weaken the claimed benefits of multilingual embeddings.
