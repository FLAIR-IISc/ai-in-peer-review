

This paper presents four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings and introduces a modified QVEC metric for evaluating their efficacy. The embedding approaches are:  
(1) **multiCluster**: Utilizes a dictionary to map words to multilingual clusters, with cluster embeddings serving as representations for all words within each cluster.  
(2) **multiCCA**: Extends Faruqui and Dyer (2014)’s bilingual embedding approach to the multilingual setting by using English embeddings as an anchor space. Bilingual dictionaries (other_language → English) project monolingual embeddings from other languages into this anchor space.  
(3) **multiSkip**: Adapts Luong et al. (2015b)’s bilingual context-based embedding method to multilingual scenarios by extending the objective function to incorporate all available parallel corpora.  
(4) **Translation invariance**: Employs a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix with an objective incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies. This method may be limited to bilingual settings.  

The evaluation framework uses CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data (e.g., super-sense annotations), deriving rotation-invariant basis vectors for aligned dimensions. The proposed metric extends this to multilingual evaluation.  

The paper is well-structured and clearly describes the methodology. However, three major issues require clarification:  
(1) The novelty with respect to Gardner et al.’s translation invariance approach is unclear. If the extension to multilingual embeddings is the key contribution, a concise explanation of this novelty is needed.  
(2) Super-sense annotations across languages risk extremely sparse feature intersections due to language-specific coverage. Beyond footnote 9, how do the authors address this sparsity?  
(3) Coverage rates significantly impact results (Table 2). For dependency parsing, multiCluster and multiCCA exhibit markedly different coverage but similar scores—how does coverage explain this discrepancy?  

The results (Table 3) lack consistency. Most intrinsic metrics show multilingual methods underperforming, which is disappointing given the primary goal of optimizing for word translation (intra-language). Notably, the translation invariance approach (not designed for this task) achieves the best results, while multiCluster—discarding inter-cluster semantic information—performs best on extrinsic metrics.  

Additional questions:  
(1) What performance degradation occurs from fixing embeddings in dependency parsing? What incremental gain do these embeddings provide over random embeddings in the LSTM stack parser?  
(2) Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings described in Section 5.1?  
(3) What advantages does multiSkip offer over learning bilingual embeddings followed by multiCCA projections?  
(4) The dictionary extraction methods (parallel corpora alignments or Google Translate) may not reflect real-world lexicon challenges. Did the authors test standard multilingual dictionaries?
