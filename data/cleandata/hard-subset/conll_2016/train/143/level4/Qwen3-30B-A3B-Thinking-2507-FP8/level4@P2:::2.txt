

**Summary**
This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages: multiCluster (clustering-based) and multiCCA (canonical correlation analysis-based). The authors propose an improved evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous methods and shows better correlation with downstream tasks. They also develop a web portal to facilitate evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, which significantly improves correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing metrics; (2) the web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will substantially facilitate future research; and (3) the multiCCA method, which demonstrates strong performance across multiple evaluation metrics.

**Strengths**
1. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric represents a substantial methodological improvement over previous evaluation approaches. As shown in Table 1, multiQVEC-CCA achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification performance, significantly outperforming other intrinsic metrics (e.g., cross-lingual word similarity: 0.386; word translation: 0.066). This strong correlation with downstream tasks validates the metric's utility for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

2. The web portal is an exceptionally valuable contribution to the community. It enables researchers to easily evaluate their multilingual embeddings across nine metrics with development and test modes, promoting replicable and standardized evaluation practices. The portal also provides downloadable data and the ability to register new evaluation datasets, creating a sustainable resource for future research.

3. The paper demonstrates a clear methodology for creating multilingual embeddings across a large number of languages (59) using only dictionaries and monolingual data. This approach is particularly valuable for resource-constrained languages where parallel data is scarce. The thorough experimental evaluation across multiple metrics and tasks provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses**
1. The paper lacks sufficient depth in analyzing why multiCluster performs well with 12 languages but significantly worse with 59 languages. The authors note that multiCluster "exacerbates" semantic ambiguity when trained on many languages, but fail to provide a thorough analysis of this phenomenon or explore how to mitigate it. This represents a missed opportunity to provide deeper theoretical insights.

2. The paper does not adequately explore the impact of key parameter choices on the performance of multiCluster and multiCCA. For instance, the threshold τ (0.1) for dictionary extraction and the cluster size limits (1,000 vs. 10,000) are not systematically evaluated. Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand how sensitive the methods are to these choices or how to optimize them for different language sets.

3. The discussion of related work is incomplete, particularly regarding multilingual embedding methods that use clustering approaches. Key papers such as Faruqui & Dyer (2013) on bilingual word clustering and Chandar et al. (2014) on autoencoder-based bilingual representations are not properly situated relative to the authors' multiCluster method. This weakens the paper's contribution claim by not clearly differentiating from prior work.

4. The paper prioritizes breadth over depth. While the evaluation covers a wide range of metrics and languages, the analysis lacks the depth needed to provide substantial theoretical insights. For instance, the conflicting results for multiCluster across 12 vs. 59 languages could have been a rich opportunity for deeper analysis, but the paper merely notes the discrepancy without exploring its implications.

**Questions to Authors**
1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why multiCluster's performance degrades significantly when scaling to 59 languages compared to 12 languages? What specific aspects of the clustering approach contribute to this performance drop?

2. How sensitive are the proposed methods to the choice of threshold τ for dictionary extraction? Would a more systematic parameter search improve performance, and how would you recommend setting this parameter for different language pairs?

3. The paper mentions that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA rely on supersense annotations, which are inconsistent across languages. What are your recommendations for future work to address this limitation, and how would you suggest standardizing supersense annotations for multilingual evaluation?
