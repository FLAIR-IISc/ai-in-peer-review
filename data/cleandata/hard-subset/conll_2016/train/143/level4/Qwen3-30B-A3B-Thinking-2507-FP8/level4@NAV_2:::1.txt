

Summary of the paper: The authors propose two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages without requiring parallel data, along with a novel evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that demonstrates superior correlation with downstream tasks compared to prior intrinsic metrics. They also introduce a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

Main contributions (as assessed by reviewer):
1. The multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, which addresses theoretical limitations of prior methods and achieves stronger correlation with extrinsic tasks (document classification and parsing) than all existing intrinsic metrics.
2. The web portal enabling standardized, reproducible evaluation of multilingual embeddings, significantly lowering barriers for future research.
3. The dictionary-based embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), though their performance is secondary to the evaluation contribution.

Strengths:
1. The multiQVEC-CCA metric fundamentally improves upon QVEC by being invariant to linear transformations and producing normalized scores, resolving critical flaws in prior evaluation approaches. Its superior correlation with extrinsic tasks (0.896 with document classification vs. 0.635 for multiQVEC and 0.386 for word similarity) demonstrates clear practical value for the field.
2. The evaluation portal provides a crucial infrastructure for reproducible research, offering downloadable datasets, standardized evaluation tasks, and an accessible interface for uploading embeddings. This directly addresses the community's need for consistent benchmarking in multilingual NLP.
3. The demonstration of 59-language embeddings using only dictionaries and monolingual data (without parallel data) represents a significant scalability advance over prior bilingual-focused work, though the primary contribution lies in the evaluation framework.

Weaknesses:
1. Coverage differences critically confound performance comparisons across methods. For example, in dependency parsing, multiCluster (61.0 [70.9]) and multiCCA (58.7 [69.3]) have nearly identical scores but substantially different coverage (70.9% vs. 69.3%), making it impossible to determine if the score difference is meaningful or merely an artifact of vocabulary size. The paper's limited discussion of this issue undermines the validity of their comparative results.
2. The core claim that their methods produce embeddings optimized for cross-lingual tasks is weakened by inconsistent results: the translation-invariance method outperforms all dictionary-based approaches on 4/9 metrics (including word translation, where it scores 63.9 [30.3] vs. multiCCA's 35.7 [53.2]), contradicting the paper's central motivation.
3. The reliance on supersense annotations across languages creates a severe scalability bottleneck. As noted in footnote 9, the intersection of supersense tags across languages is small, limiting multiQVEC-CCA's applicability. The paper fails to propose a viable solution beyond this footnote (e.g., using universal POS tags or cross-lingual annotation transfer).
4. The dictionary extraction method (using Google Translate for 47 languages) does not reflect real-world resource constraints. The authors do not evaluate embeddings trained on actual multilingual lexicons (e.g., BabelNet), raising questions about the practical relevance of their approach.
5. The multiCluster method's design (assigning identical vectors to translationally equivalent words) appears to contradict its stated goal of preserving cross-lingual semantic relationships, yet it performs best on extrinsic tasks. This paradox is not adequately addressed, undermining the method's theoretical coherence.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you reconcile the superior performance of the translation-invariance method on word translation (63.9 vs. 35.7 for multiCCA) with the paper's claim that the primary goal is improving cross-lingual embedding quality?
2. In Table 3, the coverage values for multiCCA (69.3%) and translation-invariance (68.6%) differ by 0.7% in dependency parsing. How did you handle vocabulary mismatch during evaluation, and why is this difference not reflected in the scores?
3. What is the impact of the coverage difference (e.g., 70.9% vs. 69.3%) on the dependency parsing results, and how does it affect the interpretation of the "competitive" performance of multiCluster?
4. Did you evaluate embeddings trained on real multilingual lexicons (e.g., BabelNet) to assess the practicality of your dictionary extraction approach, beyond Google Translate?
