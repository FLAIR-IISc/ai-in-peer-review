This paper introduces four methods for generating multilingual word embeddings – multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, and Translation Invariance – alongside a modified QVEC metric for their evaluation. The multiCluster method leverages dictionaries to map words to multilingual clusters, deriving embeddings from these clusters. multiCCA extends the bilingual embedding approach of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages, using English embeddings as an anchor and projecting other languages via bilingual dictionaries. multiSkip adapts the alignment-based approach of Luong et al. (2015b) to a multilingual setting by incorporating parallel corpora from all available languages into the objective function. Finally, Translation Invariance utilizes a low-rank decomposition of the word PMI matrix, incorporating bilingual alignment frequencies, though its applicability may be limited to bilingual embeddings. The QVEC evaluation metric employs CCA to maximize correlation between embeddings and linguistic data, deriving rotation- and linear transformation-invariant scores, and extending this to multilingual evaluations.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the described work. However, several key issues require attention:

(1) The novelty of the Translation Invariance approach, particularly its extension to multilingual embeddings, needs clearer articulation. A brief explanation of the contribution beyond this extension would be beneficial.
(2) The application of super-sense annotations across multiple languages raises concerns about feature sparsity due to potentially small intersections between languages. The authors’ proposed solution (beyond footnote 9) warrants further elaboration.
(3) The impact of coverage on the scores presented in Table 2 is unclear. The close scores of multiCluster and multiCCA, despite significant differences in coverage for the dependency parsing task, require explanation.
(4) The results in Table 3 present an inconsistent picture. The multilingual embedding techniques generally do not outperform other methods on intrinsic metrics. Given the paper’s goal of creating embeddings excelling in word translation (intra-language performance), the superior performance of the Translation Invariance approach is unexpected. Similarly, the strong performance of multiCluster on extrinsic metrics, despite discarding inter-cluster semantic information, is surprising.

Further clarification is requested on the following points:

(1) What performance degradation results from fixing word embeddings in the dependency parsing task? What improvement was observed by substituting these embeddings for random embeddings within the LSTM stack parser?
(2) Is Table 1 an average across the 17 embeddings detailed in Section 5.1?
(3) What advantages does the multiSkip approach offer compared to learning bilingual embeddings and then applying multiCCA for cross-space projections?
(4) The dictionary extraction methods (from parallel corpora via alignments or Google Translate) may not accurately reflect the challenges of utilizing genuine multilingual lexicons. Was the use of real multilingual dictionaries explored?
