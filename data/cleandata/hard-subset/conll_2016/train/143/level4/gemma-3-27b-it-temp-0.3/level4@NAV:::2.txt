This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for multilingual word embedding estimation – MultiCluster, based on clustering, and MultiCCA, leveraging canonical correlation analysis. Additionally, the authors propose an enhanced supersense similarity measure, improving upon QVEC by integrating CCA in place of correlation and incorporating multilingual evaluation. The methods are evaluated extensively using a web portal created by the authors, demonstrating performance gains over several baselines in certain scenarios.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and represents a significant body of work. The proposed representation learning and evaluation techniques are highly relevant to current research trends, and the thorough documentation is commendable. 

However, my overall impression is that the paper prioritizes breadth over depth. I would have appreciated a more in-depth discussion of the results, particularly regarding the contrasting performance of MultiCluster on the 59- and 12-language setups, and a more detailed analysis of the impact of parameter choices and design decisions within MultiCluster and MultiCCA. While the paper offers substantial practical value – notably the improved QVEC measure and the publicly available web portal – I found the research questions addressed and answered less novel.

Here are some specific suggestions for improvement:

Including the correlation results for monolingual QVEC and QVEC-CCA (as presented in Table 1) would strengthen the claim of QVEC-CCA’s improvement (lines 326-328). 

Minor points:
Line 304: The phrasing "a combination of several cross-lingual word similarity datasets" could be misinterpreted; these datasets differ by language, not by type.
Page 3: Two equations extend beyond the column margin.
Lines 121 and 147 cite Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. in relation to MultiSkip, while Section 2.3 only references Luong et al. Clarification of this correspondence is needed.

While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, several relevant publications could be included:

Regarding multilingual embeddings and clustering:
Chandar et al. (2014), Hill et al. (2014), Lu et al. (2015), and Faruqui & Dyer (2013).

Concerning multilingual training for improved source-language embeddings:
Suster et al. (2016) and Guo et al. (2014).

More broadly, the exploration of translational context, such as in Diab & Resnik (2002), could also be acknowledged.
