

# Peer Review for CONLL

## Summary of Contributions
The paper's most significant contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric for multilingual word embeddings that addresses fundamental shortcomings of previous methods. The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual embeddings, though these are extensions of existing approaches rather than novel methods. The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate future research.

## Strengths

1. **High-impact evaluation framework**: The paper's most substantial contribution is multiQVEC-CCA, which effectively addresses critical limitations in existing evaluation metrics. Previous methods like QVEC suffer from non-invariance to linear transformations and produce unnormalized scores. By employing CCA instead of cumulative dimension-wise correlation, multiQVEC-CCA produces a normalized score in [-1, 1] that is invariant to basis rotation. The empirical results in Table 1 are compelling, showing that multiQVEC-CCA correlates much more strongly with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification) compared to previous metrics (0.386 for cross-lingual word similarity). This represents a significant advance in the field.

2. **Practical resource for the community**: The web portal is a valuable contribution that will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP. The portal enables researchers to easily evaluate their multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, with development and test modes to encourage sound experimentation practices. This addresses a real gap in the field where evaluation practices have been inconsistent.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness of dictionary-based approaches**: The paper convincingly shows that dictionary-based methods can produce high-quality multilingual embeddings without requiring parallel corpora (which are scarce for many languages). The results for 59 languages demonstrate that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics, establishing that dictionary-based approaches can be effective for large-scale multilingual embedding learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the proposed methods**: The core contribution of the paper (the estimation methods) lacks significant novelty. The paper acknowledges that multiCCA is "a simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a straightforward extension of existing clustering approaches. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these specific extensions are significant contributions to the field, particularly given that the evaluation metrics represent the paper's most important contribution.

2. **Questionable quality of the 59-language dataset**: The 59-language set relies heavily on Google Translate for dictionary creation, which introduces potential noise and inaccuracies. The paper states that "dictionaries were formed by translating the 20k most common words in the English monolingual corpus with Google Translate," which may significantly degrade the quality of the embeddings for many languages. This undermines the claim that the methods work well across 59 languages.

3. **Lack of comparison with recent approaches**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent multilingual embedding approaches that have emerged since the cited work (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM, or other contextualized embeddings). This is a significant omission given that these approaches have become the state-of-the-art for multilingual NLP tasks. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess how the proposed methods perform relative to current best practices.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your proposed methods compare to more recent multilingual embedding approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM, XLM-R) that have become the standard in the field? The paper doesn't address these approaches, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of your methods.

2. The paper claims that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available," but for the 59-language set, dictionaries are built using Google Translate. What is the impact of this on the quality of the embeddings, and how does this compare to using dictionaries built from parallel corpora (for the 12-language set)?

3. The paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings by using clusters of translationally equivalent words. Have you considered using more sophisticated clustering approaches (e.g., context-aware clustering) to address this issue, and how would that affect the results?

## Additional Comments

The paper's evaluation of the correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics (Table 1) is particularly strong and well-executed, providing clear evidence for the value of the proposed metrics. The authors should consider addressing the limitations of their evaluation (e.g., the limited number of languages in the 12-language set) in the final version. The web portal is a commendable practical contribution that should be highlighted more prominently in the paper's presentation.
