

# Review of "Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings"

## Summary
This paper proposes methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries (without requiring parallel corpora). The authors introduce two dictionary-based methods—multiCluster and multiCCA—and develop new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) to better assess these embeddings. They also create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel evaluation framework**: The paper's most significant contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of existing evaluation methods and demonstrates superior correlation with downstream task performance compared to previous metrics.
2. **Evaluation portal**: The creation of a web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings is a practical contribution that will substantially benefit the research community.
3. **Dictionary-based methods**: The paper proposes multiCluster and multiCCA as methods for estimating multilingual embeddings using only dictionaries, which is valuable given the scarcity of parallel corpora for many language pairs.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper's most compelling strength is its rigorous analysis of evaluation metrics. The demonstration that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate much more strongly with extrinsic tasks (0.896 and 0.635 for document classification, 0.273 and 0.444 for parsing) compared to existing metrics (0.386 and 0.066 for word similarity, -0.292 for word translation) is a major contribution. This addresses a critical gap in the field, as previous work has relied on metrics that poorly correlate with real-world performance.

2. **Practical value of the evaluation portal**: The web portal is a substantial practical contribution that will facilitate future research. By providing standardized evaluation data, tools, and an interface for uploading embeddings, the authors have created a valuable resource that addresses a real need in the community. The portal's dual-mode operation (development and test) promotes sound experimental practices.

3. **Comprehensive comparison of methods**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of four embedding estimation methods across multiple languages and metrics. The results for 59 languages (where multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster) and 12 languages (where dictionary-based methods compete favorably with parallel-data-based methods) offer valuable insights for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of proposed methods**: The core methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) appear to be incremental extensions of existing work. The paper acknowledges that multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster builds on standard clustering approaches. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how these methods advance beyond prior work in a meaningful way. For instance, the authors note that multiCCA "is a simple extension" but don't provide a clear justification for why this extension is novel or significant.

2. **Insufficient analysis of method limitations**: The paper identifies that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings, but doesn't explore whether this is a fundamental limitation of the approach or if there are ways to mitigate it. The authors also don't thoroughly address the impact of using Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, which introduces potential noise into the data. This is particularly concerning since the paper claims their methods work "well in some settings and evaluation metrics."

3. **Inconsistent presentation of results**: There is a contradiction between the paper's text and Table 3 regarding which method outperforms others. The text states "multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster according to all evaluation metrics" for 59 languages, but Table 3 shows multiCluster outperforming multiCCA in some metrics (e.g., monolingual word similarity: 38.0 vs 43.0). This discrepancy undermines the paper's claims and needs clarification.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that multiCCA is a "novel" method, but describes it as a "simple extension" of existing work. Can you clarify the specific novelty of multiCCA compared to Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and why this extension is significant?

2. The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages. How did you evaluate the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries, and what is the expected impact on the quality of the resulting embeddings? How would results change if higher-quality dictionaries were available?

3. The paper claims multiCCA "consistently outperforms multiCluster" in Table 2, but Table 3 shows multiCluster outperforming multiCCA in some metrics. Can you clarify this discrepancy and provide a more accurate description of the comparative results?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its improved evaluation framework and the creation of a useful evaluation portal. The demonstration that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate much better with downstream tasks than existing metrics is particularly strong. However, the novelty of the proposed embedding estimation methods is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the use of automatically generated dictionaries.

While the paper's contributions to evaluation methodology and community resources are significant, the core estimation methods don't advance the state-of-the-art in a compelling way. With stronger justification for the novelty of the methods and more thorough analysis of their limitations, this paper would be a stronger contribution. As it stands, the paper is of interest to the community primarily for its evaluation framework and portal, rather than for its embedding estimation methods.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses and clarify the results in the author response). The paper's evaluation framework and portal are valuable contributions that would benefit the field, but the limited novelty of the core methods and some methodological issues prevent a stronger recommendation.
