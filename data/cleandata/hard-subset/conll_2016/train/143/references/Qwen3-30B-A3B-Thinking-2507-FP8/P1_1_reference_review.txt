

# Peer Review for CONLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes methods for creating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual corpora and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without requiring parallel corpora). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) two dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA), (2) improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and (3) a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

In my assessment, the most significant contribution is the evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA), which addresses a critical gap in the field. The second most significant contribution is the web portal, which facilitates standardization of evaluation. The embedding methods themselves represent incremental improvements over prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper identifies and solves a fundamental problem in multilingual NLP: previous intrinsic evaluation metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks (Table 1 shows correlations of 0.386 for word similarity vs. 0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA). The authors demonstrate that their new metrics (particularly multiQVEC-CCA) achieve substantially better correlation with extrinsic tasks, which is a major contribution to the field. This is the strongest aspect of the paper.

2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal**: The authors developed a web portal that enables researchers to easily evaluate multilingual embeddings across multiple metrics. This is a valuable community resource that addresses a real need for standardized evaluation in this area. The portal includes both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, making it a comprehensive evaluation tool.

3. **Thorough empirical analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed metrics across multiple languages and tasks. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is particularly compelling, showing that previous metrics are inadequate for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of their proposed metrics (supersense annotation dependency).

4. **Clear demonstration of the problem with existing metrics**: The paper effectively shows that previous intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks (0.386 and 0.066 respectively), while their new metrics correlate much better. This is an important contribution that will help guide future work in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the embedding methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are straightforward extensions of existing approaches (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014; Luong et al., 2015b). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how these methods differ from previous work that has attempted multilingual embeddings. For example, the multiCCA method is described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), which suggests the contribution is incremental rather than novel.

2. **Evaluation metrics' dependency on supersense annotations**: The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics require supersense annotations, which are not consistently available across languages (as acknowledged by the authors). The paper states that "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages and may not be publicly available." This significantly limits the applicability of their evaluation framework to languages without such annotations.

3. **Incomplete comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to a few baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance) but doesn't compare to more recent or state-of-the-art methods for multilingual embeddings. For example, it mentions "Polyglot" by Al-Rfou et al. (2013) but doesn't provide a comparison, making it difficult to assess how their methods perform relative to current approaches.

4. **Limited language coverage in comparison**: When comparing to parallel data-based methods, the authors only use 12 languages (all European), while they train embeddings for 59 languages. This small subset may not be representative of the broader language diversity in their 59-language set, limiting the generalizability of their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your multiCCA method specifically differ from the approach by Hermann and Blunsom (2014), which also proposed a method for more than two languages (though their implementation was limited to bilingual)? The paper mentions that "Hermann and Blunsom (2014) showed that the bicvm method can be extended to more than two languages, but the released software library only supports bilingual embeddings."

2. The authors note that "multiCluster uses a maximum cluster size of 1,000 and 10,000 for the set of 12 and 59 languages, respectively." Why was this threshold chosen, and how does it affect the quality of the embeddings?

3. The paper states that "the mainstream approach of estimating one vector representation per word type... ignores the fact that the same word may have different semantics in different contexts. The multiCluster method exacerbates this problem." However, they don't provide a clear theoretical justification for why multiCCA would be better at handling this issue. Can the authors elaborate on this?

4. The authors acknowledge that their evaluation metrics require supersense annotations, which are not consistently available across languages. What are the plans to address this limitation for languages without such annotations, or to develop alternative evaluation methods that don't require them?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with its improved evaluation methodology (multiQVEC-CCA) and the web portal for standardized evaluation. The demonstration that previous intrinsic metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks is particularly compelling. However, the embedding methods themselves represent incremental improvements rather than significant novel contributions. The limitations of the evaluation metrics (dependency on supersense annotations) and the incomplete comparison with state-of-the-art methods are notable weaknesses.

The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with recent multilingual embedding approaches and a clearer explanation of how their methods differ from existing work. Despite these weaknesses, the evaluation framework and portal represent significant contributions that will benefit the broader community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
