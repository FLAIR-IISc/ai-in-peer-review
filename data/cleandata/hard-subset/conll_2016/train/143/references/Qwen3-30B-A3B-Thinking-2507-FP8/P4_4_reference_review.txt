

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings with Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries, without needing parallel corpora. The authors also develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics, and create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The development of improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address theoretical shortcomings of existing methods and demonstrate stronger correlation with downstream tasks.
2. The creation of a practical web portal that facilitates reproducible evaluation of multilingual embeddings, addressing a clear need in the field.
3. The proposal of dictionary-based embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that work with limited resources.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement in evaluation methodology**: The paper identifies critical theoretical flaws in QVEC (the existing monolingual evaluation metric), particularly its lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric directly addresses these issues by using CCA, resulting in a normalized score in [-1, 1] that is invariant to basis rotation. The experimental results (Table 1) compellingly demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA achieves a correlation of 0.896 with document classification performance, substantially better than existing metrics (maximum 0.386 for word similarity). This is the paper's strongest contribution, as it directly addresses a fundamental limitation in how multilingual embeddings have been evaluated.

2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal**: The web portal addresses a genuine need for standardized, reproducible evaluation in multilingual NLP. By providing a centralized platform for evaluation with both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, the authors significantly lower the barrier to entry for future research in this area. The portal's features (data download, embedding upload, and metric comparison) are well-designed and address practical concerns in the field.

3. **Clear demonstration of evaluation metric improvements**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of how different evaluation metrics correlate with downstream tasks. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is methodologically sound and clearly demonstrates that the proposed metrics are substantially better predictors of downstream task performance than existing methods. This analysis is crucial for establishing the value of the proposed metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the embedding estimation methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are largely extensions of existing approaches. MultiCCA directly builds on Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster resembles previous clustering-based approaches. The paper acknowledges this by stating "we developed multiSkip independently," but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how these methods advance the state of the art beyond incremental improvements. The evaluation (Table 3) shows multiCCA is competitive but not consistently superior to existing methods, which weakens the primary contribution claim.

2. **Overstated claim of "massively multilingual" embeddings**: The paper claims to train embeddings for "59 languages," but the evaluation metrics do not consistently cover all 59 languages. For instance, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA only use English, Italian, and Danish supersense annotations. The claim of "massively multilingual" is thus misleading, as the evaluation is limited to a small subset of the languages for which embeddings were trained.

3. **Insufficient handling of vocabulary coverage differences**: The paper acknowledges that vocabulary coverage differences between embedding methods can bias evaluation results (as noted in the "Results" section). While they report coverage percentages, this is insufficient to fully address the problem. The paper should have provided results using only the intersection of vocabularies covered by all methods, which would have given a more accurate comparison. Without this, the evaluation results (Table 3) may not fairly represent the relative performance of the methods.

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper evaluates on only 12 languages for the main comparison (with parallel data) and 59 languages without parallel data. The results for the 59-language case (Table 2) show that multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, but this is based on a single language set. The paper doesn't demonstrate that these methods generalize well across diverse language families or that they are robust to varying language pairs.

5. **Misleading claim about evaluation metrics**: The paper states that "multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA achieve better correlations with extrinsic tasks" than previous metrics. However, the paper only tests these metrics on two specific extrinsic tasks (document classification and parsing). This does not prove the metrics are better in general, only that they correlate better with these specific tasks. The claim overreaches the evidence provided.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims multiQVEC-CCA "achieves better correlations with extrinsic tasks" than previous metrics. However, the evaluation is limited to two extrinsic tasks. Could the authors provide evidence that these metrics generalize to other downstream tasks beyond document classification and parsing?

2. The 59-language evaluation (Table 2) shows multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster. However, the paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring contextual semantics by using translationally equivalent clusters. Given that this is a known limitation of cluster-based methods, how do the authors justify the use of multiCluster as a primary contribution when it inherently suffers from a fundamental weakness?

3. The paper states that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available." However, the evaluation for the 12-language comparison (Table 3) shows that methods using parallel data (multiSkip and translation-invariance) perform competitively with dictionary-based methods. Given that parallel data is often more readily available than dictionaries, how do the authors position their methods in the practical landscape of multilingual NLP?
