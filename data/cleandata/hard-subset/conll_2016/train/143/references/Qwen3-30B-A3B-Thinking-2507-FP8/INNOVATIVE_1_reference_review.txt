## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to multilingual word embeddings that challenges the prevailing assumption that parallel corpora are necessary. The authors propose two dictionary-based methods—multiCluster and multiCCA—that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not parallel corpora). They also develop a new evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of existing metrics and shows better correlation with downstream tasks. Finally, they create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The paper demonstrates embeddings for 59 languages, significantly expanding the scale of previous work.

## Strengths

1. **Radical paradigm shift in multilingual embedding estimation**: The paper's most significant contribution is its bold departure from the standard approach of requiring parallel corpora for multilingual embeddings. By demonstrating that dictionaries alone can be used to create effective multilingual embeddings (with the ability to incorporate parallel corpora when available), the authors open up new possibilities for low-resource language scenarios. This is a paradigm shift that could substantially lower barriers to multilingual NLP research, particularly for languages with limited parallel data. The paper's claim that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available" is a major conceptual advance.

2. **Novel evaluation metric with strong empirical validation**: The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper correctly identifies limitations in the existing QVEC metric (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores) and provides a principled solution using CCA. The empirical validation showing that multiQVEC-CCA correlates much better with downstream tasks (0.896 vs. 0.386 for word similarity) is compelling evidence of its value. This isn't just an incremental improvement to evaluation—it's a fundamental rethinking of how we assess multilingual embeddings.

3. **Practical impact through community resource**: The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable contribution that will significantly lower the barrier to entry for future research. By providing standardized evaluation metrics and easy-to-use tools, the authors are creating a lasting infrastructure for the field. The scale of 59 languages demonstrated in the paper is impressive and shows the practical applicability of their methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited exploration of when dictionaries suffice**: While the paper demonstrates that dictionary-based methods can work, it doesn't sufficiently explore the conditions under which dictionaries are sufficient versus when parallel corpora would be necessary. A deeper analysis of the trade-offs between dictionary quality and embedding performance would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to only two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), which are somewhat dated. A comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., MUSE, LASER, or other recent multilingual embedding approaches) would better position the work's significance in the current landscape.

3. **Theoretical limitations of multiCluster**: The paper acknowledges that multiCluster's approach of forcing translationally equivalent words to share the same vector might be problematic for words with multiple meanings. However, it doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how this impacts performance across different languages or word types, which is a fundamental limitation of the method.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the proposed methods perform when applied to languages with very limited dictionary resources (e.g., less than 10,000 word pairs)? Would the approach scale to extremely low-resource languages?

2. The paper notes that multiCluster's approach of forcing translationally equivalent words to share the same vector might be problematic for words with multiple meanings. Have you analyzed how this affects performance for specific word types (e.g., polysemous words) across languages?

3. How does the performance of the dictionary-based methods compare to parallel corpus-based methods when the dictionaries are of varying quality (e.g., manually curated vs. automatically generated via Google Translate)?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multilingual NLP by introducing a bold new paradigm for multilingual embedding estimation that reduces reliance on parallel corpora. The theoretical improvements to the evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) are well-motivated and empirically validated, and the practical web portal will benefit the entire community. While the paper has some methodological limitations, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The authors have presented a compelling case for a new direction in multilingual embedding research that could substantially broaden the scope of multilingual NLP applications.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It introduces a significant new concept (dictionary-based multilingual embeddings) that challenges the field's assumptions and has the potential to substantially lower barriers to multilingual NLP research. The evaluation metric improvement is also a major contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. This paper is not just a methodological refinement—it's a conceptual shift that will influence future research in multilingual NLP.
