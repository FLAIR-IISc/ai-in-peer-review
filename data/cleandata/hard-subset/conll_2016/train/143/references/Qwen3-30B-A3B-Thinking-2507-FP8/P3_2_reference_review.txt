

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, along with new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks. The authors also introduce a web portal for standardized evaluation.

From my perspective, the most significant contribution is the evaluation methodology, not the embedding estimation methods. The proposed metrics address a fundamental problem in multilingual NLP: the poor correlation between intrinsic evaluation metrics and downstream task performance. The web portal is a useful practical contribution, while the estimation methods represent incremental improvements over prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Major contribution to evaluation methodology:** The paper correctly identifies a critical weakness in multilingual NLP research—the poor correlation between intrinsic metrics and downstream performance. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric effectively addresses two major flaws in previous metrics (basis dependence and lack of normalization), and the empirical results are compelling. The correlation with document classification (0.896) is dramatically higher than previous metrics (0.386 for cross-lingual word similarity), making this a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal:** The web portal is well-designed and provides a much-needed standardized evaluation framework. It enables researchers to easily evaluate embeddings across multiple metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic), improving reproducibility and fair comparison of methods.

3. **Strong empirical evidence for evaluation metrics:** The correlation analysis in Table 1 is thorough and convincing, demonstrating that the proposed metrics consistently outperform previous ones across multiple downstream tasks. This is a critical contribution that will guide future research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental nature of the estimation methods:** The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are not particularly novel. The paper acknowledges multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a straightforward application of clustering to multilingual embeddings. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how these methods advance the state of the art beyond existing work, particularly given the existence of more recent approaches.

2. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art:** The paper only compares to older methods (multiSkip, translation-invariance) but doesn't include recent transformer-based multilingual approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R), which have become the state of the art for many multilingual tasks. This makes it difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed methods.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper provides empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis explaining why multiCCA outperforms multiCluster or why multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks. Understanding the underlying mechanisms would be valuable for the field.

4. **Coverage issues in evaluation:** While the paper acknowledges coverage differences between methods, their solution (evaluating on the intersection of vocabularies) may not be representative of the full vocabulary. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the intersection subset is representative, potentially biasing results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your proposed methods compare to recent transformer-based multilingual approaches like XLM-R, which have become the state of the art for many multilingual tasks?

2. Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks than other metrics?

3. The paper states that multiCluster's performance degrades with more languages (59 vs 12). Is this primarily due to semantic ambiguity from cluster-based representation, or are there other factors at play?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its new evaluation metrics, which address a critical problem in multilingual NLP research. The web portal is also a useful resource. However, the proposed estimation methods are incremental, and the paper would benefit from comparing to more recent state-of-the-art approaches. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison to recent transformer-based methods.
