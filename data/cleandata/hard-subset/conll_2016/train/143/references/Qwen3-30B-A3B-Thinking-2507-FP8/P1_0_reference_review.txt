

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods—multiCluster and multiCCA—for estimating multilingual word embeddings that require only monolingual corpora and pairwise parallel dictionaries (no parallel corpora needed, though they can be used). The authors train embeddings across 59 languages, develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. As I see them, the paper's strongest contributions are:

1. The development of novel evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics.
2. The creation of a practical web portal that facilitates standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.
3. The demonstration that dictionary-based methods can effectively scale to 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora.

## Strengths

1. **Significant advancement in evaluation methodology**: The paper identifies critical shortcomings in existing evaluation metrics (QVEC), particularly its lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses both issues while achieving substantially better correlations with downstream tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification vs. 0.386 for cross-lingual word similarity). This is a major contribution to the field, as evaluation metrics have been a bottleneck in multilingual NLP research.

2. **Practical resource for the community**: The web portal they developed is a valuable, tangible contribution that will significantly lower the barrier to entry for future research. It provides standardized evaluation across multiple metrics, supports 59 languages, and enables researchers to easily evaluate their methods. This addresses a real need in the community for reliable, reproducible evaluation frameworks.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper demonstrates that multiCCA consistently outperforms other methods across most metrics when trained on 59 languages, and the correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is particularly compelling. The authors show that their proposed metrics correlate much better with downstream performance than existing metrics, which is a critical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core methods**: While the methods are presented as novel, they build directly on existing approaches (e.g., multiCCA extends Faruqui and Dyer 2014, and multiSkip is a variant of existing work). The extension to multilingual settings is described as "simple" (in hindsight), which suggests the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what makes their approach distinct from prior work.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares only to methods from 2015-2016 (multiSkip, translation-invariance) but fails to include more recent approaches like multilingual BERT or other neural approaches to multilingual embeddings. This makes it difficult to assess the paper's standing in the current research landscape. For example, Conneau et al. (2018) showed that multilingual BERT achieves strong performance without requiring parallel data.

3. **Limited exploration of method limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the limitations of their approach. For instance, it doesn't analyze how the quality of the dictionaries affects performance or how the methods handle languages with sparse translation resources. The paper claims their methods work well for 59 languages, but doesn't provide evidence that their approach would scale better than other methods for languages with very few translation pairs.

4. **Narrow downstream task evaluation**: The correlation analysis only evaluates two downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing). To strengthen their claim that their metrics better correlate with downstream performance, they should evaluate on a wider range of tasks (e.g., named entity recognition, machine translation, or sentiment analysis).

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your methods compare to more recent approaches like multilingual BERT or mBERT, which have shown strong performance in multilingual tasks? Have you attempted to compare with these methods?

2. The paper states that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring contextual semantics, yet it still performs reasonably well for 12 languages. Can you elaborate on why this is the case, and what specific limitations this approach has for larger language sets?

3. Your evaluation portal supports 59 languages, but the supersense annotations used for multiQVEC-CCA are limited to English, Italian, and Danish. How would you recommend addressing the lack of consistent supersense annotations across languages, as you note in the paper?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the evaluation of multilingual embeddings, particularly through the development of multiQVEC-CCA and the creation of the evaluation portal. While the core embedding methods are somewhat incremental, the evaluation improvements are significant and will likely have a lasting impact on the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the limitations of the proposed methods and to include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art approaches. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides a useful resource for the community.
