

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods and Improved Evaluation"

## Summary of Contributions
The authors propose two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduce improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and develop a web portal for evaluation. The paper's primary contribution appears to be the evaluation framework, though the authors frame the embedding estimation methods as their key contribution.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a significant gap in evaluation methodology for multilingual embeddings, and the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric shows promising correlation with downstream tasks (Table 1).
2. The web portal is a valuable contribution that will facilitate future research in this area.
3. The authors provide thorough ablation studies comparing their methods against baselines across multiple evaluation metrics.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Ad-hoc threshold selection without justification
The paper states "We fixed τ = 0.1 early on based on manual inspection of the resulting dictionaries" (2.1) without any justification or ablation studies to support this choice. This is a critical parameter for dictionary extraction that directly impacts downstream results. Without systematic exploration of different threshold values, the claimed performance of the methods is not well-founded. The authors should have included ablation studies showing how performance varies with different τ values.

### 2. Inconsistent and problematic evaluation methodology
The paper's evaluation methodology contains significant inconsistencies that undermine the results. For the "monolingual word similarity" metric (Table 3), the authors report scores based solely on the English MEN dataset, despite evaluating embeddings across multiple languages. This is fundamentally flawed because the metric should evaluate how well embeddings capture semantic similarity *within each language*, not just English. The authors should have used language-specific word similarity datasets for each language they evaluate.

### 3. Biased dictionary creation for 59 languages
The paper states: "For the remaining 47 languages, dictionaries were formed by translating the 20k most common words in the English monolingual corpus with Google Translate, ignoring translation pairs with identical surface forms and multi-word translations" (5.2). This approach introduces significant bias and reliability issues. Google Translate is known to have varying quality across language pairs, particularly for low-resource languages. The authors' results for 59 languages are therefore likely compromised by this unreliable data source, which contradicts their claim of "using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries."

### 4. Inconsistent evaluation protocols across language sets
The authors evaluate their methods on two different language sets (12 languages with parallel data and 59 languages without) using different evaluation protocols. For the 12-language set, they use Wiktionary for word translation evaluation, but for the 59-language set, they "use Google Translate to obtain enxx bilingual dictionaries to evaluate the embeddings of 59 languages" (5.2). This means the evaluation data for the 59-language set is generated using the same potentially unreliable method as the training data, creating a significant evaluation leak that invalidates the reported results.

### 5. Unsubstantiated claims about QVEC-CCA improvements
The authors claim that "QVEC-CCA simultaneously addresses both problems" (the two weaknesses of QVEC), but provide no mathematical proof or empirical evidence to support this claim. The paper states that "QVEC-CCA ensures invariance to the matrices bases rotation," but doesn't provide any evidence that this property holds in practice. Without demonstrating that the claimed improvements actually work as intended, this evaluation metric's validity remains questionable.

## Additional Concerns
- The paper lists 59 languages but the set contains duplicate entries (e.g., "sv" appears twice), and the list has 59 entries but the set notation shows 59 items with some repetitions.
- The paper claims "multiQVEC-CCA produces a score in [-1, 1]" but the mathematical formulation shows it's defined as "max v,w r(X>v, S>w)" which is a correlation, so it should be in [-1, 1], but they don't explain why this is an improvement over QVEC.
- The paper mentions "The 12-language set is {bg, cs, da, de, el, en, es, fi, fr, hu, it, sv}" but the list contains 12 items while the set notation shows 12 entries, which is inconsistent with the previous 59-language list.
- The reference for "Universal dependencies 1.1" is incomplete and formatted inconsistently with other references.

## Conclusion
While the paper makes a valuable contribution in creating an evaluation portal and proposing an improved evaluation metric, the methodological flaws in the core experiments and evaluation framework significantly undermine the claimed contributions. The ad-hoc threshold selection, inconsistent evaluation methodology, and problematic dictionary creation for 59 languages introduce serious reliability concerns that cannot be overlooked. The paper would require substantial revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for acceptance.

The authors' key claim that "multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster" (Table 2) is questionable given the methodological issues in the evaluation. Without a sound evaluation methodology, the claimed performance comparisons are not trustworthy.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial methodological flaws that compromise the paper's core claims.
