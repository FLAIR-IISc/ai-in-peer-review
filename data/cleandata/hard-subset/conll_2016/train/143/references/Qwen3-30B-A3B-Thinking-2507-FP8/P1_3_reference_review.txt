

# Peer Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings that only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not full parallel corpora). The authors also develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing metrics, and create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

The main contributions of the paper, ordered by strength, are:

1. The improved evaluation framework (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), which addresses a fundamental flaw in existing evaluation methods by better correlating with downstream tasks.
2. The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which provides a standardized evaluation framework for the community.
3. The dictionary-based embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), which are useful but build on existing approaches.

## Strengths

1. **The evaluation framework is the paper's strongest contribution.** The authors convincingly demonstrate that their new metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) correlate significantly better with downstream tasks than existing metrics. The correlation coefficients (0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA with document classification) are substantially higher than those for previous metrics (0.386 for word similarity). This is a critical contribution to the field, as previous work has struggled to find evaluation metrics that correlate well with downstream task performance.

2. **The comprehensive evaluation across multiple metrics and tasks.** The authors evaluate their methods across a wide range of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, providing a thorough assessment of how well different metrics correlate with downstream performance. This systematic comparison is valuable for the community to understand which evaluation metrics are most useful.

3. **The web portal is a valuable community resource.** The portal provides a standardized way to evaluate multilingual embeddings, complete with development and test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. This will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP by providing a common evaluation framework.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the embedding methods.** The authors acknowledge that multiCCA is a simple extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a straightforward adaptation of clustering approaches. The paper fails to convincingly argue why these methods are novel or significantly better than existing approaches. The improvements over baselines are marginal in many cases, and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish these methods from previous work.

2. **Evaluation framework limitations.** The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics rely on supersense annotations, which are only available for a small number of languages (English, Italian, and Danish in their experiments). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide a clear path forward for applying their metrics to other languages. The suggestion to use universal POS tags instead is mentioned but not explored in depth.

3. **Lack of comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods.** The paper doesn't compare to Artetxe et al. (2018), which introduced a much more effective unsupervised method for multilingual embeddings that has become the new standard. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed methods. The authors claim their methods work for 59 languages, but the evaluation is limited to only 3-12 languages for most tasks.

4. **Vocabulary coverage issues.** The paper acknowledges that different embedding methods have different vocabulary coverage, which can bias evaluation results. While they mention this issue, their solution (repeating evaluation using only the intersection of vocabularies) is not thoroughly implemented in their main results. The paper doesn't provide a convincing argument for why the main results aren't affected by these coverage differences.

5. **Overstated claims about 59-language results.** The paper claims their methods work well for 59 languages, but the evaluation is limited to only 3-12 languages for most tasks. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the methods scale well to many languages, especially given the performance drop observed when moving from 12 to 59 languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to extend your evaluation framework to languages without supersense annotations? The current framework is limited to English, Italian, and Danish, which significantly restricts its applicability.

2. Why didn't you compare your methods to Artetxe et al. (2018), which is now the standard for unsupervised multilingual embeddings? This comparison would be crucial to establish the relative merits of your approach.

3. Can you provide more details on how the web portal will handle vocabulary coverage issues when evaluating embeddings with different coverage, especially for the 59-language experiments?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its improved evaluation framework and web portal. The evaluation metrics are well-motivated and empirically validated, addressing a critical gap in the literature. However, the novelty of the embedding methods is limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing to more recent state-of-the-art methods and addressing the limitations of the evaluation framework. The paper's claims about the 59-language results are not fully supported by the experimental evidence provided. Despite these weaknesses, the evaluation framework and web portal are substantial contributions that will benefit the community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
