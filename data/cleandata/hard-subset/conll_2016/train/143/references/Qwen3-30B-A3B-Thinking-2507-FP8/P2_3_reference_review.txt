

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings: Dictionary-Based Methods and Improved Evaluation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of learning multilingual word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors propose two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without needing parallel corpora). They also introduce improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing methods, and develop a web portal for easy evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

In my assessment, the paper's strongest contributions are:
1. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, a theoretically sound evaluation metric that significantly outperforms existing metrics in correlating with downstream tasks
2. The practical web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will substantially facilitate future research
3. The empirical demonstration that dictionary-based methods can be competitive with parallel data-based methods

## Strengths

1. **Significant Improvement in Evaluation Metrics**: The paper identifies a critical flaw in existing evaluation methods for multilingual embeddings - their poor correlation with downstream tasks. The authors' new metric, multiQVEC-CCA, addresses two major theoretical shortcomings of previous methods (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring) and demonstrates substantially better correlation with extrinsic tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification vs. 0.386 for word similarity). This is a major contribution to the field, as evaluation is fundamental to progress in NLP research.

2. **Practical Contribution with Immediate Impact**: The creation of a web portal that enables researchers to easily evaluate their multilingual embeddings across nine metrics (including both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks) is a highly valuable contribution. The portal addresses a real pain point in the field, as previous work often lacked standardized evaluation frameworks. The portal's development will lower barriers to entry for researchers and enable more replicable, comparable results across studies.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across 59 languages, carefully evaluating both the estimation methods and the evaluation metrics. They address the important issue of vocabulary coverage in evaluations, which is often overlooked in previous work. Their experiments demonstrate that dictionary-based methods (particularly multiCCA) can be competitive with parallel data-based methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance) across multiple metrics, which is significant given that dictionaries are more widely available than parallel corpora for many language pairs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Estimation Methods**: While the paper presents multiCluster and multiCCA as novel, they are largely adaptations of existing approaches (multiCCA extends Faruqui and Dyer 2014, and multiCluster is a straightforward extension of clustering methods). The paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why these specific adaptations are necessary or superior to existing methods. The novelty is primarily in the application to a large number of languages rather than the methods themselves, which weakens the contribution.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed methods. For example, multiCCA is described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of bilingual embeddings, but the paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis to justify why this extension should work better than alternatives. The absence of theoretical grounding makes it difficult to understand why the methods work and how they might be improved.

3. **Overstated Benefits of "Massively Multilingual" Approach**: The paper claims to train embeddings for 59 languages, but doesn't clearly demonstrate the benefits of such a large number of languages. The results show multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, but the paper doesn't address whether including more languages beyond a certain point becomes detrimental to performance. The 59-language results don't provide clear evidence that "massively multilingual" embeddings are substantially better than smaller-scale approaches.

4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations**: While multiQVEC-CCA is theoretically sound, the metric relies on supersense annotations, which are inconsistent across languages and not publicly available for most languages. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't propose a practical solution. This significantly limits the metric's applicability in real-world scenarios where such annotations may not exist.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across evaluation metrics. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why multiCCA is better than multiCluster, especially since multiCluster uses a similar underlying clustering approach. Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical and practical advantages of the projection approach used in multiCCA?

2. The paper states that "most differences in coverage between multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively small." However, in Table 2, the coverage for multiCluster is 70.9% while multiCCA is 69.3% for dependency parsing, which is a difference of 1.6 percentage points. Given that coverage differences can significantly impact evaluation results, could the authors explain why they consider this difference "relatively small" and how they handled coverage differences in their correlation analysis?

3. The paper acknowledges that supersense annotations are inconsistent across languages and not publicly available for many languages. How would the authors recommend adapting the multiQVEC-CCA metric for languages where such annotations don't exist, and how would they evaluate the metric's performance in such cases?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with significant practical contributions, particularly in the development of improved evaluation metrics and the web portal. While the novelty of the estimation methods is somewhat limited, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a critical gap in evaluation methodology. The empirical results are thorough and well-presented, and the web portal will be a valuable resource for the community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical weaknesses and clarify the scope of the "massively multilingual" claim. The paper's practical contributions and improved evaluation framework make it a valuable addition to the literature in multilingual NLP.
