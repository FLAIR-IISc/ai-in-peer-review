

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings"

## Summary
This paper proposes two new methods for multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries. It also introduces two new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors claim their methods work well for 59 languages and that their new evaluation metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than existing metrics.

## Main Contributions
1. A web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings (practical contribution)
2. New evaluation metrics that better correlate with downstream tasks (methodological contribution)
3. Two new dictionary-based embedding estimation methods (methodological contribution)

## Strengths
1. **The evaluation portal is a genuinely useful contribution** that will facilitate future research. It provides a standardized way to evaluate embeddings across multiple metrics, which is valuable for reproducibility.

2. **The correlation analysis (Table 1) shows that their proposed metrics correlate better with extrinsic tasks than previous metrics.** This is a significant finding that could improve how the field evaluates multilingual embeddings.

3. **The multiCCA method shows consistent performance across metrics for 59 languages.** This suggests that their approach has some merit, particularly when compared to their own proposed multiCluster method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous comparison with established baselines.** The authors compare only to two methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance), neither of which represents state-of-the-art. For example, they don't compare to widely adopted approaches like mBERT, XLM-R, or the multilingual FastText embeddings. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to develop "new" methods for multilingual embeddings. The comparison is limited to methods that use parallel data (multiSkip) or translation-invariance (Gardner et al., 2015), which are not the most relevant baselines for their dictionary-based approach.

2. **Unsubstantiated claims about novelty and necessity.** The authors claim their methods "only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries," but they don't provide evidence that these resources are more accessible than parallel corpora for most languages. For many languages, parallel corpora are more readily available than high-quality bilingual dictionaries. Without this evidence, the claim that their approach is more practical or scalable is unsupported.

3. **Questionable novelty of proposed methods.** multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster resembles existing cluster-based approaches (Täckström et al., 2012). The authors don't adequately distinguish their methods from prior work, making it difficult to assess their novelty. The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review and clearer positioning against existing methods.

4. **Inadequate validation of the proposed evaluation metrics.** While Table 1 shows promising correlations, the multiQVEC-CCA metric correlates poorly with dependency parsing (0.273), undermining the claim that it's a significant improvement. The authors don't explain this inconsistency or how to address it. Additionally, their evaluation metrics depend on supersense annotations, which are not consistently available across languages, limiting their general applicability.

5. **Lack of thorough ablation studies and scalability analysis.** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate why specific design choices (like cluster size, projection methods, etc.) are necessary. It also doesn't show how the methods scale to a larger number of languages or how they perform on languages with very limited resources. The claim that the methods work well for 59 languages is insufficient without evidence of robustness across different language families and resource levels.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you choose to compare only to multiSkip and translation-invariance, which are not the most established methods in the field? How do your methods compare to state-of-the-art approaches like mBERT or XLM-R?

2. The paper claims that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available," yet your multiSkip method explicitly uses parallel data. How do your methods compare when parallel data is available versus when it's not, and how does this compare to methods that primarily use parallel data?

3. Your evaluation metrics depend on supersense annotations, which are not consistently available across languages. How do you plan to address this limitation for languages without these annotations?

4. Your multiCCA method consistently achieves better coverage than translation-invariance, but this may confound performance comparisons. How did you address this coverage bias in your results?

5. The correlation between multiQVEC-CCA and dependency parsing is low (0.273). Why is this the case, and what steps would you take to improve this correlation?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a useful contribution with the evaluation portal, but the methodological contributions are questionable. The proposed methods lack sufficient novelty, and the validation against established baselines is inadequate. The paper claims to develop "new" methods for multilingual embeddings but fails to properly position them against the current state of the art. The evaluation metrics show promise, but their inconsistent performance across tasks and dependence on language-specific annotations limits their utility.

The authors present an interesting idea but fail to provide sufficient evidence to support their claims. As a skeptical reviewer, I find the paper's claims about methodological novelty and performance improvement unsupported by rigorous validation against established baselines. The paper would require substantial revision to demonstrate that their methods represent a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. Without this, the paper does not meet the high standards expected for a CoNLL publication.
