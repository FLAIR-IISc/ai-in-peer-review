

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA), develops improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and introduces a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors claim to train embeddings in 59 languages using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without requiring parallel corpora), and demonstrate that their evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream tasks than previous methods.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Improvement in Evaluation Metrics**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which substantially outperforms previous intrinsic evaluation metrics in correlating with downstream task performance (0.896 correlation with document classification vs. 0.635 for multiQVEC and 0.386 for cross-lingual word similarity). This addresses a critical gap in the field, as previous work has lacked robust metrics for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their metric is invariant to linear transformations of embeddings and produces a normalized score, solving key limitations of QVEC.

2. **Valuable Resource for the Community**: The web portal they developed is a major practical contribution that will substantially facilitate future research. It provides a standardized framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings across multiple metrics and languages, addressing the reproducibility issues common in this field. The portal's dual-mode operation (development/test) encourages sound experimental practices, which is particularly important given the field's reliance on multiple evaluation metrics.

3. **Thorough Empirical Validation**: The paper provides extensive experimental results across 59 languages and multiple evaluation metrics, including both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The correlation analysis between evaluation metrics and downstream performance is particularly strong evidence for their proposed metrics' superiority. The comparison across different estimation methods (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, translation-invariance) across multiple languages and metrics demonstrates the robustness of their findings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper fails to compare against recent transformer-based multilingual models (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM, mBERT, XLM-R) that have become the de facto standard in multilingual NLP. These models typically outperform dictionary-based methods and use parallel data differently. Without this comparison, it's unclear how their methods stand relative to the current state of the art, which significantly weakens their contribution claim.

2. **Questionable Language Diversity and Dictionary Quality**: The paper claims to cover 59 languages, but the majority are European languages (e.g., English, German, French, Spanish, Italian, Swedish) with similar linguistic structures. This limits the generalizability of their findings to more linguistically diverse language sets. Additionally, they form dictionaries for 47 languages using Google Translate, which introduces potential errors and biases that aren't adequately addressed or evaluated in the paper.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that multiCluster exacerbates the problem of ignoring context-specific meanings (by using clusters for translationally equivalent words), but doesn't adequately discuss the implications for real-world applications or explore potential mitigations. This is particularly concerning since the paper's main contribution is for "massively multilingual" embeddings where context sensitivity is crucial.

4. **Inconsistent Performance Across Metrics**: The paper shows that the best-performing method varies across different evaluation metrics (Table 3), which is expected but not thoroughly analyzed. The authors don't provide a compelling explanation for why certain methods perform better on specific metrics, which would strengthen their claims about methodological superiority.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your dictionary-based methods compare to multilingual BERT and XLM-R, which have become the standard for multilingual NLP tasks? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the relevance of your work in the current landscape.

2. You form dictionaries for 47 languages using Google Translate. How did you evaluate the quality of these dictionaries, and how might errors in these dictionaries affect the quality of your embeddings? Did you perform any error analysis on the translation pairs?

3. Your 59-language set is predominantly European languages. How do you expect your methods to perform on more linguistically diverse language pairs (e.g., languages with very different writing systems, morphological structures, or syntactic patterns)?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through the development of improved evaluation metrics and a practical web portal for multilingual embedding evaluation. The multiQVEC-CCA metric represents a significant advance in how we assess multilingual embeddings, addressing critical limitations of previous methods. However, the paper's claim of "massively multilingual" embeddings is somewhat undermined by the limited language diversity in their experiments and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art transformer-based methods.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by comparing with recent transformer-based models and providing a more thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach. Despite these weaknesses, the evaluation metrics and web portal are strong contributions that will benefit the community. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the final version.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above)
