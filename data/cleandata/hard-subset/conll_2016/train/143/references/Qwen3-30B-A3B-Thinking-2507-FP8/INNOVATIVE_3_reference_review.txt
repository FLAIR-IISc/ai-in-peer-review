

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not parallel corpora). The authors also introduce multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric for multilingual embeddings that addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous metrics and shows better correlation with downstream tasks. Finally, they create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

## Main Contributions (from my perspective)
1. Development of multiQVEC-CCA as an evaluation metric that significantly better correlates with downstream tasks than existing metrics
2. Demonstration that multilingual embeddings can be trained using only dictionaries for 59 languages
3. Creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings

## Strengths

**1. The development of multiQVEC-CCA is a bold and significant contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field.** The paper identifies a critical flaw in existing evaluation metrics: they correlate poorly with downstream tasks (e.g., word translation correlates -0.292 with dependency parsing, while multiQVEC-CCA correlates 0.896 with document classification). This is not just a minor improvement but a conceptual shift in how we evaluate multilingual embeddings. The authors don't just propose a new metric—they address theoretical shortcomings of previous metrics and provide a statistically sound solution. This is the kind of bold, novel contribution I value most.

**2. The demonstration that multilingual embeddings can be trained using only dictionaries (not parallel corpora) for 59 languages is highly practical and valuable.** This makes multilingual NLP more accessible to researchers without access to parallel data, which is a significant practical contribution. The paper shows that their dictionary-based methods work well across a large number of languages, which is impressive.

**3. The web portal is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate future research.** By providing a standardized evaluation framework with both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, the authors create a resource that will help the community make progress in this area. This is not just a nice-to-have but a necessary tool for the field.

## Weaknesses

**1. The novelty of the embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) is limited.** The paper acknowledges that multiCCA is "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), which is a major weakness for a reviewer who values novelty. The authors don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why these methods should work better than existing approaches, and the methods themselves appear to be practical extensions rather than novel conceptual breakthroughs.

**2. The statistical significance of the correlation improvements is not established.** The paper shows impressive correlation values (multiQVEC-CCA at 0.896 vs. 0.386 for document classification), but doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals to establish whether these differences are statistically significant. Without this, the claimed superiority of their metric is difficult to fully evaluate.

**3. The evaluation is limited to a small subset of the 59 languages.** The results in Tables 2 and 3 primarily focus on English, Italian, and Danish, with limited evaluation across the full 59-language set. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the methods generalize well to all 59 languages, especially those from diverse language families.

**4. The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of their approach.** For example, how well does multiQVEC-CCA work for languages with very different grammatical structures? How does the performance of the embedding methods change as the number of languages increases beyond 59? The paper mentions that the mainstream approach of one vector per word type ignores context, but doesn't explore how context-aware methods might interact with their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that multiCCA is "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this extension is novel and how it differs from other approaches to multilingual embeddings?

2. The correlation between multiQVEC-CCA and downstream tasks is impressive (0.896 for document classification), but the paper doesn't establish statistical significance. Can you provide p-values or confidence intervals to show that this correlation is significantly better than existing metrics?

3. The evaluation of the embedding methods is limited to a small subset of the 59 languages. Can you provide more information about how the performance varies across different language families and whether the results generalize to all 59 languages?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. While the embedding methods themselves aren't particularly novel, the development of multiQVEC-CCA as a superior evaluation metric is a bold and significant contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. The paper's main contribution isn't the methods themselves, but the evaluation metric that shows how to better measure progress in multilingual embeddings. This aligns with my preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. The practical contribution of the web portal further strengthens the paper's value to the community. The weaknesses are important but don't outweigh the paper's significant contribution to the field.
