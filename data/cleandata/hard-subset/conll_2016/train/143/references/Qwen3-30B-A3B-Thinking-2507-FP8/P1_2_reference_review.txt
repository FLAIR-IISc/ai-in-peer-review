

# Peer Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) and introduces new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) for assessing their quality. The authors claim these methods work well across 59 languages using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not requiring parallel corpora). The primary contribution appears to be the new evaluation metrics, which they demonstrate correlate significantly better with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics. The secondary contribution is the web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will facilitate future research in this area.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Improvement in Evaluation Metrics**: The authors identify two fundamental flaws in QVEC (the standard monolingual evaluation metric), namely lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores. Their proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses both issues while producing a normalized score in [-1, 1]. Most importantly, they empirically demonstrate that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate much more strongly with downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics (word similarity and word translation), with correlations of 0.896 and 0.273 versus 0.386 and 0.007, respectively. This is a substantial contribution to the field as it provides a reliable way to assess multilingual embeddings.

2. **Practical Community Resource**: The web portal they've developed is an exceptionally valuable contribution to the NLP community. It enables researchers to easily evaluate their embeddings against a comprehensive suite of metrics, with both development and test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. This addresses a critical need for standardization in multilingual embedding evaluation, which has been lacking in the literature.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The paper doesn't just propose methods but thoroughly validates them through extensive experiments across multiple languages and tasks. The correlation analysis between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks is particularly strong evidence for the value of their new metrics. The authors also provide detailed ablation studies comparing their methods across 59 languages and a smaller set of 12 languages with high-quality parallel data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper only compares with methods from 2012-2016 (Klementiev et al., 2012; Gardner et al., 2015; Luong et al., 2015), missing more recent advances like MUSE (Conneau et al., 2017), LASER (Artetxe et al., 2018), or XLM (Conneau et al., 2019). Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether their methods represent a significant advance over current state-of-the-art in multilingual embeddings.

2. **Questionable Quality of "59 Languages" Data**: The paper claims to train embeddings for 59 languages, but for 47 of these languages, the authors use Google Translate to generate bilingual dictionaries (ignoring translation pairs with identical surface forms and multi-word translations). This creates a significant methodological limitation that should be more clearly acknowledged. The quality of embeddings for these languages is likely substantially lower than for the 12 languages with manually curated dictionaries.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for MultiCCA**: The paper describes multiCCA as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" without providing a strong theoretical foundation for why it works better than multiCluster. While the empirical results show multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, the paper doesn't explain why this is the case beyond noting that multiCluster has "added semantic ambiguity." A deeper theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 of the 59 languages. How does the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries compare to manually curated ones, and how does this affect the quality of the embeddings? Could the authors provide some qualitative examples of the embeddings for languages where Google Translate was used?

2. The paper claims multiCCA outperforms multiCluster across all metrics on 59 languages, but the differences are relatively small (e.g., 43.3 vs 41.5 in multiQVEC-CCA). Are these differences statistically significant, or are they within the margin of error?

3. The authors state that "multiCluster uses a maximum cluster size of 1,000 and 10,000 for the set of 12 and 59 languages, respectively." Why was the cluster size increased for the larger language set, and how did this affect the results?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the evaluation of multilingual word embeddings, with the new metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) representing a significant improvement over existing methods. The web portal is a practical resource that will benefit the community. However, the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods and the questionable quality of the "59 languages" data set are notable weaknesses. With these issues addressed, this paper would make an excellent contribution to CoNLL.

The paper's strengths in evaluation methodology and community resource development outweigh its weaknesses, making it suitable for acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the questions about the quality of the Google Translate dictionaries and the comparison with more recent methods.
