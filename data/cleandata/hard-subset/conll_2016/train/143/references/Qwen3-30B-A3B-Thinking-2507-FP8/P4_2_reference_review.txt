

# Review of "Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings"

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries. The authors demonstrate their methods on 59 languages and develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with extrinsic tasks than previous metrics. They also provide a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

## Main Contributions
1. Development of improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address theoretical shortcomings in existing metrics and show better correlation with downstream tasks.
2. Creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, facilitating future research.
3. Proposal of two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for multilingual embeddings, which require only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement in evaluation metrics**: The authors clearly identify shortcomings in existing evaluation metrics (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores) and address them through multiQVEC-CCA. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides strong empirical evidence that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate significantly better with extrinsic tasks (0.896 correlation for multiQVEC-CCA with document classification) than previous metrics (0.386 for word similarity). This represents a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Practical contribution of the web portal**: The evaluation portal is a valuable resource that will standardize evaluation practices in multilingual NLP research. It enables researchers to easily evaluate their embeddings using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, with development and test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. This addresses a clear gap in the field.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages and metrics**: The paper provides thorough comparisons across 59 languages and multiple evaluation metrics. The results show that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across metrics, and the analysis of why multiCluster performs worse with 59 languages (due to semantic ambiguity from clustering translationally equivalent words) is insightful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the proposed estimation methods**: The paper states that multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a straightforward extension of previous approaches. The paper doesn't clearly articulate what makes these extensions novel or necessary, which weakens the contribution claim. The methods don't significantly advance the state-of-the-art in multilingual embedding estimation.

2. **Insufficient analysis of 59-language results**: While the paper claims to train embeddings for 59 languages, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the embeddings perform across different language families or for low-resource languages. The evaluation is primarily conducted on a subset of 12 languages (for comparison with baselines), and the 59-language results are not deeply analyzed in terms of language-specific performance patterns.

3. **Lack of discussion on dictionary quality impact**: The paper states that parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available, but it doesn't discuss how the quality of the dictionaries affects the embedding quality or how to handle cases where dictionaries are incomplete. This is a significant gap in the paper, as dictionary quality is likely to be a major factor in the performance of the proposed methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the 59-language set includes languages from various language families. How do the results vary across different language families, and are there any patterns in which languages perform better or worse with the proposed methods?

2. The paper states that multiCluster clusters translationally equivalent words. How does the method handle cases where a word has multiple translations in the dictionary (polysemy), and how does this affect the clustering process and final embedding quality?

3. The paper uses English as the reference language for multiCCA. How sensitive are the results to the choice of reference language, and what would happen if a different language were used as the reference (particularly for low-resource languages)?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution through its improved evaluation metrics and web portal, which will significantly facilitate future research in multilingual NLP. However, the novelty of the proposed estimation methods is limited, and the analysis of the 59-language results is insufficient. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough analysis of language-specific performance and a clearer articulation of the novelty of the proposed methods.
