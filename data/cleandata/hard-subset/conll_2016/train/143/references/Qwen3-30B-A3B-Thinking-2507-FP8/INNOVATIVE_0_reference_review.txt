

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings Using Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two novel dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (no parallel corpora needed). It introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks, and provides a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

The primary contribution is the novel evaluation metric multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses fundamental flaws in existing evaluation methods (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores). The secondary contribution is the dictionary-based embedding methods that work without parallel corpora, making multilingual embeddings more accessible for low-resource languages. The web portal is a valuable community resource.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Improvement in Evaluation Metrics**: The paper's most compelling contribution is multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses two critical flaws in QVEC (the monolingual evaluation metric): lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores. The paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA achieves substantially better correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 for document classification, 0.273 for parsing) compared to existing metrics (0.386 and 0.007 for word similarity, 0.066 and -0.292 for word translation). This is a major contribution because evaluation metrics are crucial for driving progress in NLP, and this new metric could become a standard.

2. **Bold Approach to Multilingual Embeddings**: The paper makes a compelling case that multilingual embeddings can be created using only dictionaries (not parallel corpora), which is a significant departure from previous work. This approach opens up possibilities for low-resource language scenarios where parallel data is scarce. The paper demonstrates embeddings for 59 languages using this method, which is a substantial scale.

3. **Valuable Community Resource**: The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a practical contribution that will substantially facilitate future research in this area. It provides a standardized way for researchers to evaluate their methods, promoting replicable results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Demonstration of Practical Utility**: While the paper shows correlations between evaluation metrics and downstream tasks, it doesn't convincingly demonstrate that models using the proposed embeddings outperform existing methods on specific NLP tasks. The paper shows that the evaluation metrics correlate better with downstream tasks, but doesn't provide evidence that the embeddings themselves improve performance on specific applications.

2. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods**: The multiCCA method is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of existing bilingual CCA methods, which suggests it's not particularly novel. The multiCluster method is a straightforward application of clustering to multilingual embeddings. The paper doesn't clearly establish why these approaches are fundamentally new or better than existing methods.

3. **Unaddressed Limitation of multiCluster**: The paper acknowledges that multiCluster forces translationally equivalent words to share the same vector, which could be problematic for polysemous words (words with multiple meanings). However, it doesn't provide strong evidence that this limitation is minor in practice, which is a significant concern for real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with its novel evaluation metric multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses a real problem in multilingual NLP evaluation. The dictionary-based approach to multilingual embeddings is bold and potentially impactful for low-resource languages. While the embedding methods themselves aren't radically novel, the paper's focus on evaluation metrics is a significant contribution that could reshape how multilingual embeddings are assessed.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the evaluation metric is substantial, and the paper's approach to multilingual embeddings using only dictionaries represents a meaningful step toward more accessible multilingual NLP. The web portal is a valuable community resource that will facilitate future research. While the embedding methods aren't radically novel, the paper's focus on evaluation metrics aligns with my preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection.

The weaknesses identified are important but don't undermine the paper's significant contribution to the field. With minor revisions addressing the practical utility demonstration and the novelty of the embedding methods, this paper would be a strong contribution to CoNLL.
