

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. It also introduces two new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) to better assess multilingual embeddings, and develops a web portal for evaluating embeddings using these metrics.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Novel evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) with better correlation to downstream tasks than existing metrics
2. Web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings
3. Dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings

## Strengths
1. **Significant improvement in evaluation metrics**: The authors correctly identify limitations in existing evaluation methods (QVEC is not invariant to linear transformations and has unnormalized scores). Their proposed multiQVEC-CCA addresses these issues and demonstrates significantly better correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification vs. 0.386 for word similarity). This is a well-motivated contribution that could have real impact on the field.

2. **Practical web portal**: The development of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate future research. It provides a standardized framework for comparing methods and makes it easier for researchers to validate their work.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper conducts thorough evaluations across multiple metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic) and multiple languages. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is particularly valuable for understanding which evaluation approaches actually predict downstream performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient validation against established baselines**: The paper lacks rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art multilingual embedding methods. It doesn't compare to established methods like MUSE (Conneau et al., 2017) or LASER (Artetxe et al., 2018), which have become standard in the field. The claim that "the proposed methods work well in some settings" is too vague without clear benchmarks against current best practices.

2. **Overstated methodological novelty**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are presented as novel, but their theoretical foundation is weak. The paper acknowledges that multiCluster exacerbates semantic ambiguity by using clusters of translationally equivalent words, yet presents it as a viable approach without sufficient justification or ablation studies. The marginal performance improvements (e.g., 0.7 in monolingual QVEC-CCA) don't justify the novelty claims.

3. **Questionable dictionary construction method**: The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, which introduces significant noise into the training process. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to measure the impact of this noise on the final embeddings, making it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed methods.

4. **Lack of comparison to established approaches**: The paper fails to position its work within the current landscape of multilingual embeddings. Without comparison to state-of-the-art methods, it's impossible to evaluate the true significance of the proposed approaches. The citation of older work (Klementiev et al., 2012; Hermann and Blunsom, 2014) doesn't adequately represent the current state of the field.

5. **Inconsistent results across metrics**: The paper shows that the ranking of methods varies across different evaluation metrics (Table 3), which is expected since each metric evaluates different aspects of embeddings. However, the paper doesn't adequately explain why their methods should be preferred when they don't consistently outperform baselines across all metrics.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you not compare your methods to established state-of-the-art multilingual embedding methods like MUSE (Conneau et al., 2017) or LASER (Artetxe et al., 2018), which are now standard in the field?
2. How do you justify using Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, and what ablation studies have you performed to measure the impact of this noise on your results?
3. The paper claims that "multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster according to all evaluation metrics," but the performance differences are marginal in most cases (e.g., 0.7 in monolingual QVEC-CCA). How significant are these differences compared to the differences between established methods?

## Overall Recommendation
While the evaluation metric improvements are valuable and the web portal is a useful contribution, the core methodological claims lack sufficient validation against established baselines. The marginal performance improvements of the proposed methods don't justify the novelty claims, especially without comparison to current state-of-the-art approaches. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more rigorous comparison to established methods and ablation studies to measure the impact of dictionary construction noise.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient validation of the core methodological claims. The paper makes an important contribution to evaluation metrics, but the proposed embedding methods don't provide enough evidence of superiority over existing approaches to warrant publication in a venue that prioritizes methodological rigor.
