

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings: Methods and Evaluation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) and introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) for assessing these embeddings. The authors train embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without requiring parallel corpora), and create a web portal to facilitate future research in this area.

The main contributions, ranked by significance, are:
1. The development of improved intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that demonstrate better correlation with downstream tasks than existing metrics.
2. The creation of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will standardize and facilitate future research in this area.
3. The proposal of two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), though these are incremental extensions of prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper's most substantial contribution is the demonstration that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics (word similarity and translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks, and the introduction of multiQVEC-CCA as a superior alternative. The correlation analysis in Table 1 is compelling, showing multiQVEC-CCA has a strong 0.896 correlation with document classification performance. This is a critical insight for the field, as proper evaluation is fundamental to progress in multilingual NLP.

2. **Practical contribution with broad impact**: The web portal (§4) is a valuable resource that will substantially facilitate future research by providing standardized evaluation protocols. The portal's ability to handle 59 languages and multiple evaluation metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic) addresses a real need in the community for reproducible and comparable results.

3. **Thorough experimental analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (59 in total), multiple metrics (nine in total), and two extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing). This breadth of evaluation is commendable and strengthens the paper's claims about the evaluation metrics' effectiveness.

4. **Clear identification of a research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that the existing evaluation metrics used in the literature are inadequate for multilingual embeddings, which is an important contribution to the field's methodological understanding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the embedding estimation methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are incremental extensions of prior work. multiCCA is explicitly described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is similar to existing clustering approaches. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish how these methods advance beyond previous work, which weakens the contribution claim. The results in Table 3 show that multiCCA performs well but not significantly better than existing methods (translation-invariance), and the paper doesn't adequately justify why their approach is superior.

2. **Insufficient justification for the dictionary-based approach**: The paper claims that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available," but it doesn't thoroughly address the limitations of relying on dictionaries. For instance, the paper doesn't discuss how dictionary quality affects embedding quality, or how the methods perform when high-quality dictionaries are unavailable. The use of Google Translate to form dictionaries for 47 languages (§5.2) is a significant limitation that isn't adequately addressed.

3. **Overstated claims about method performance**: The paper states that "the proposed methods work well in some settings and evaluation metrics" (Introduction), but the results in Tables 2 and 3 show only modest performance improvements over baselines. For example, multiCCA only outperforms multiCluster by 8.5 points on multilingual word similarity (66.6 vs 58.1), which is not a substantial difference. The paper should provide more nuanced analysis of when these methods work best.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent methods**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent methods for multilingual embeddings, such as those using unsupervised cross-lingual word alignment (e.g., Artetxe et al., 2018) or multilingual BERT. This omission weakens the claim that the proposed methods are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the evaluation metrics themselves. For instance, the multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics rely on supersense annotations that are only available for a limited number of languages (English, Italian, Danish), which severely restricts their applicability. The paper notes this limitation but doesn't propose a solution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims multiCCA "consistently outperforms multiCluster according to all evaluation metrics" for 59 languages (Table 2). However, the performance differences are often small (e.g., 66.6 vs 58.1 for multilingual word similarity). How significant are these differences, and under what specific conditions would one method be preferred over the other?

2. The paper uses Google Translate to form dictionaries for 47 languages. How does the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries compare to manually curated dictionaries, and what impact does this have on the quality of the resulting embeddings?

3. The paper states that "the mainstream approach of estimating one vector representation per word type... ignores the fact that the same word may have different semantics in different contexts." How does multiCluster specifically address this issue, given that it forces translationally equivalent words to share the same vector?

4. The paper mentions that "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages." What steps could be taken to address this inconsistency for future multiQVEC-CCA evaluations?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the evaluation methodology for multilingual embeddings, which is a significant gap in the literature. The improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) are well-motivated and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The web portal is also a practical and useful resource for the community.

However, the core contribution of the paper is undermined by the lack of novelty in the embedding estimation methods, which are incremental extensions of prior work. The paper would be substantially stronger if it focused more on the evaluation metrics and less on the embedding estimation methods, which don't represent a major advancement.

I recommend a **weak reject** decision. The paper's contribution to evaluation methodology is strong, but the overall work doesn't sufficiently advance the state of the art in multilingual embedding estimation. With significant revisions to better justify the novelty of the methods, address the limitations of the dictionary-based approach, and provide a more nuanced analysis of results, this paper could be a strong contribution.
