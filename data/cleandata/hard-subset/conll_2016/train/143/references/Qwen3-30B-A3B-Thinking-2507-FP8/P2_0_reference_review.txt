

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA), evaluates them across 59 languages, and introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) along with a web portal for evaluation. The authors claim that their methods require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without parallel corpora), and that their evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream tasks than existing approaches.

The main contributions as I see them (ordered by strength):
1. The improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) with strong empirical validation
2. The web portal for easy evaluation of multilingual embeddings
3. The dictionary-based estimation methods (though with limited novelty)

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation of evaluation metrics**: The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction of multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of existing metrics. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification, 0.273 with parsing), significantly outperforming existing metrics (max correlation of 0.386). This is a substantial contribution to the field, as evaluation metrics are crucial for progress in NLP.

2. **Practical contribution with the web portal**: The developed web portal is a valuable resource for the community, enabling easy evaluation of multilingual embeddings across nine metrics with both development and test modes. This will substantially facilitate future research by promoting standardized evaluation practices and reproducibility.

3. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The paper thoroughly evaluates methods across 59 languages and multiple metrics, including both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The results consistently show that multiCCA outperforms multiCluster across most metrics, with multiQVEC-CCA emerging as the best overall evaluation metric. The comparison of dictionary-based methods versus parallel corpus-based methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance) is particularly valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in estimation methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are largely straightforward extensions of existing techniques. multiCCA is described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a variant of the word cluster approach. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how these extensions are novel or significantly better than existing approaches beyond the empirical results. Without strong theoretical justification, this limits the methodological contribution.

2. **Evaluation metrics' limited applicability**: The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics rely on supersense annotations, which are only available for a few languages (English, Italian, Danish in the paper). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a compelling solution. For a metric to be widely adopted, it should work across many languages, not just those with existing annotations. The paper doesn't address how to extend this to languages without supersense resources.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to only two baseline methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance), despite a rich literature on multilingual embeddings. Key works like Klementiev et al. (2012), Hermann and Blunsom (2014), and Guo et al. (2016) are not compared against, making it difficult to assess how competitive the proposed methods are. This weakens the claim of "improved" methods.

4. **Potential bias in 59-language evaluation**: The dictionaries for 47 of the 59 languages were created using Google Translate, which introduces a potential bias. The embeddings may perform well on words Google Translate handles well but poorly on others. The paper doesn't discuss this limitation or how it affects the generalizability of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why this extension works well for multiple languages, beyond the empirical results?

2. The multiQVEC-CCA metric relies on supersense annotations. Given that these are only available for a few languages, how do you propose to extend this metric to languages without such annotations? Would a universal POS tag-based approach (as mentioned in footnote 9) be sufficient?

3. The paper doesn't compare to several recent state-of-the-art methods for multilingual embeddings. Could you provide a comparison with at least two more recent methods (e.g., from the past 2 years) to better establish the relative performance of your methods?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution with its improved evaluation metrics and web portal, which will help advance the field of multilingual NLP. However, the novelty of the estimation methods is limited, and the evaluation of these methods against the current state-of-the-art is incomplete. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent work and a better justification for the proposed methods.

The strengths of the evaluation metrics and the practical web portal make this a solid contribution to the field, but the limited novelty of the estimation methods prevents it from being a groundbreaking paper. With the authors addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison to state-of-the-art methods and the limitations of the evaluation metrics, this paper would be a strong contribution to CoNLL.
