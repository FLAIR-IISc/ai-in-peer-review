

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, without requiring parallel corpora. It introduces new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address theoretical shortcomings of existing intrinsic evaluation methods, and develops a web portal for easy evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

## Main Contributions (from my perspective)
1. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, a novel evaluation metric that addresses fundamental flaws in previous embedding evaluation methods and demonstrates significantly better correlation with downstream tasks
2. The demonstration that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics correlate poorly with downstream performance, which is a critical insight for the field
3. The creation of a practical web portal that facilitates future research in multilingual embeddings

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper's most significant contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses two fundamental weaknesses of QVEC (a previously used evaluation metric): lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring. By replacing cumulative dimension-wise correlation with CCA, they produce a normalized score in [-1, 1] that is invariant to basis rotations. This is a substantive theoretical improvement that could have broader implications for word embedding evaluation beyond multilingual contexts.

2. **Strong empirical evidence for evaluation metric superiority**: The paper provides compelling evidence (Table 1) that multiQVEC-CCA correlates significantly better (0.896) with downstream document classification than any other intrinsic metric. This is a major finding that could shift how the field evaluates multilingual embeddings, as it demonstrates that previous evaluation practices may have been misleading.

3. **Practical resource creation**: The web portal is a valuable contribution that lowers the barrier to entry for researchers in this field. It provides standardized evaluation metrics, development/test datasets, and an easy-to-use interface for comparing embeddings across languages, which will significantly accelerate future research.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty in core methods**: The multiCluster and multiCCA methods are largely extensions of existing bilingual approaches (Faruqui and Dyer 2014, Luong et al. 2015b) to the multilingual setting. The paper does not sufficiently justify why this extension is novel enough to warrant publication, especially given that the authors acknowledge their methods "exacerbate" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings.

2. **Insufficient analysis of methodological limitations**: The paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific meanings but doesn't explore how to address this limitation. The choice to project all languages to English as the anchor language (rather than creating a truly multilingual space) is presented as a simple extension, but the paper doesn't discuss the implications of this choice or how it might limit the model's performance.

3. **Overstated claims about method performance**: The paper claims "the proposed methods work well in some settings and evaluation metrics" without providing sufficient evidence of their superiority over existing methods. The results in Table 3 show that multiCCA consistently outperforms other methods in most evaluations, but this is presented as a general claim rather than a specific finding for the proposed methods.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings. How do you propose to address this limitation in future work, and why does this approach still yield competitive results for 12 languages despite this fundamental limitation?

2. The choice to project all languages to English as the anchor language seems to be a significant design decision. What are the theoretical and practical implications of this choice, and why was this approach selected over creating a truly multilingual vector space?

3. The paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks than existing metrics, but doesn't deeply analyze *why* previous metrics failed to correlate well. Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of the previous metrics that led to this poor correlation?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution through its evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), which addresses a fundamental flaw in the field's evaluation methodology and demonstrates strong empirical correlation with downstream tasks. While the core embedding methods are extensions of existing work, the evaluation framework is novel and impactful. The web portal is a valuable practical contribution that will benefit the research community.

I recommend acceptance because the paper introduces a significant new concept (the improved evaluation metric) that could fundamentally change how multilingual embeddings are evaluated, which is a more important contribution than incremental improvements to embedding methods. The novelty of the evaluation approach outweighs the limitations of the embedding methods themselves, and the authors provide strong empirical evidence for the value of their evaluation framework.

The paper's focus on evaluation rather than just embedding methods is particularly noteworthy and aligns with the need for better evaluation practices in NLP. This is the kind of bold, conceptual contribution that advances the field beyond incremental improvements.
