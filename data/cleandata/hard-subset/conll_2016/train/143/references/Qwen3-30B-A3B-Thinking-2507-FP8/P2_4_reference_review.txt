

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not parallel corpora). It also develops improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) for multilingual embeddings and creates a web portal for easy evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their evaluation metrics correlate significantly better with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel evaluation metrics** (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address fundamental shortcomings of previous evaluation methods
2. **Web portal** for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings
3. **Dictionary-based embedding methods** (multiCluster and multiCCA)

## Strengths

**1. Significant contribution in evaluation metrics (primary strength)**

The paper's strongest contribution is the development and validation of improved evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings. The authors effectively demonstrate that previous intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing), with correlations ranging from 0.007 to 0.386. In contrast, their proposed multiQVEC-CCA achieves correlations of 0.896 and 0.273 respectively. This is a critical contribution because it addresses a major gap in the field: researchers have been using evaluation metrics that don't actually predict performance on downstream tasks. The paper provides compelling evidence through thorough correlation analysis (Table 1) that these new metrics better reflect the quality of embeddings for practical applications.

**2. Practical evaluation portal (secondary strength)**

The creation of a web portal that enables researchers to easily evaluate their multilingual embeddings using a suite of metrics is a highly valuable practical contribution. The portal supports multiple evaluation tasks, provides standardized development/test splits, and allows researchers to upload embeddings and receive immediate evaluation results. This will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP by establishing a common evaluation framework and reducing barriers to entry for researchers new to the field.

**3. Clear demonstration of previous evaluation limitations**

The paper effectively demonstrates why previous evaluation metrics are inadequate. The authors show that metrics like cross-lingual word similarity cannot measure how well embeddings from different languages relate to each other (ii), while word translation metrics don't capture semantic similarity within languages (i). This clear articulation of the limitations of existing methods strengthens the case for their new metrics.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited novelty of estimation methods**

The proposed embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) lack significant novelty compared to existing approaches. multiCCA is explicitly described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is based on clustering translationally equivalent words. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that these methods are substantially better than existing approaches. In fact, Table 3 shows that the translation-invariance method (Gardner et al., 2015) often outperforms their methods on intrinsic metrics, and multiCCA only consistently outperforms multiCluster on the 59-language evaluation. The authors don't provide compelling evidence that these methods represent a significant advance over prior work.

**2. Limited evaluation of the proposed metrics' scope**

While the paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics, it doesn't thoroughly address the limitations of their proposed metrics. The authors acknowledge that their metrics rely on supersense annotations which are inconsistent across languages and not publicly available for all languages. This significantly limits the generalizability of their evaluation method. The paper suggests using universal POS tags instead, but this is presented as an afterthought without proper exploration or validation. This limitation should be addressed to make the evaluation metrics more widely applicable.

**3. Inadequate comparison with recent methods**

The paper doesn't compare their methods with some of the most recent state-of-the-art approaches to multilingual embeddings. For example, it mentions Al-Rfou et al. (2013) but doesn't compare with more recent methods like those using fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2017) or other recent advances in multilingual representation learning. This weakens the paper's claim that their methods represent a significant contribution to the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that multiQVEC-CCA is "invariant to the matrices bases rotation" but doesn't provide evidence or explanation for this claim. Could the authors elaborate on why this is the case?

2. In Table 3, multiCCA outperforms the translation-invariance method on most metrics, but the paper notes that "multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than the translation-invariance method." How much of this performance difference is attributable to coverage differences versus actual quality improvements?

3. The paper states that "most differences in coverage between multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively small," but doesn't provide specific coverage numbers for the 59-language case. Could the authors provide these exact coverage percentages to better understand the comparison?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field through its development of improved evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings and the creation of a practical evaluation portal. However, the proposed estimation methods lack significant novelty compared to existing approaches. The paper's greatest value lies in its thorough evaluation of existing metrics and the introduction of better alternatives that correlate with downstream task performance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations in the evaluation metrics and provide more thorough comparisons with recent methods.

The paper addresses an important problem in multilingual NLP research by demonstrating that previous evaluation metrics don't correlate well with actual downstream performance. The authors provide compelling evidence that their new metrics (particularly multiQVEC-CCA) do correlate better, which is a significant contribution to the field. The evaluation portal will be a valuable resource for future research. While the estimation methods themselves are not groundbreaking, the paper's focus on proper evaluation represents a step forward in the field's methodology.
