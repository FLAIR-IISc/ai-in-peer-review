

# Review for "Multilingual Word Embeddings: A Dictionary-Based Approach with Improved Evaluation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, develops improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and creates a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. 

As a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, I see the most significant contribution as the development of multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric that addresses theoretical shortcomings of existing methods and shows significantly better correlation with downstream tasks. The proposed embedding methods are incremental extensions of prior work, while the evaluation portal is a useful but not novel community resource.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and impactful evaluation metric**: The paper identifies critical limitations of existing evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings (invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores), and proposes multiQVEC-CCA as a principled solution. This is a bold contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. The empirical demonstration that multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification) while existing metrics show weak correlations (0.386 for word similarity) is compelling evidence of its value. This contribution has the potential to reshape how multilingual embeddings are evaluated in the field.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation showing that their evaluation metric outperforms existing methods across multiple tasks. The correlation analysis between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks (Table 1) is particularly convincing, demonstrating that the authors have identified a meaningful problem and provided a robust solution.

3. **Practical resource for the community**: The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable practical contribution that will facilitate future research. By providing a standardized evaluation framework and making training data publicly available, the authors have created a resource that will help the community move beyond ad hoc evaluation practices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental nature of proposed embedding methods**: While the authors claim novelty for multiCluster and multiCCA, these methods are largely straightforward extensions of existing bilingual approaches (e.g., multiCCA is described as "a simple extension (in hindsight) to construct multilingual embeddings for more languages"). The methods don't represent a fundamental breakthrough in the space of multilingual embeddings, and their empirical performance is mixed (multiCluster suffers from semantic ambiguity issues).

2. **Limited scope of evaluation metrics**: The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics require supersense annotations in multiple languages, which are only available for a limited set of languages (English, Italian, Danish in this work). The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages and may not be publicly available." This restricts the broad applicability of their evaluation metric.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: While the paper identifies weaknesses in existing evaluation metrics, it doesn't provide a strong theoretical foundation for why multiQVEC-CCA should work better. The connection between the CCA-based evaluation and downstream task performance is demonstrated empirically but lacks deeper theoretical justification.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you envision the multiQVEC-CCA metric being adapted for languages without available supersense annotations, or for languages where the annotations are inconsistent?

2. Can you provide more insight into why multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster in the 59-language setting, given that multiCluster is based on a more intuitive approach (cluster-based translation equivalence)?

3. The paper notes that "the mainstream approach of estimating one vector representation per word type (rather than word token) ignores the fact that the same word may have different semantics in different contexts." How does multiCCA address this issue, given that it uses a single vector per word (via projection to English space)?

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the proposed embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are incremental, the paper's most significant contribution—the development of multiQVEC-CCA as a principled evaluation metric with strong empirical validation—represents a meaningful advance in the field. The paper demonstrates that existing evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings have fundamental shortcomings, and provides a better alternative that correlates strongly with downstream task performance.

This contribution is particularly valuable because it addresses a critical bottleneck in the field: how to properly evaluate multilingual embeddings. The authors' bold approach to rethinking evaluation metrics, rather than just proposing new embedding methods, is exactly the kind of innovative thinking that advances the field. The paper provides a clear path forward for the community by introducing a more reliable evaluation framework that will improve the quality and comparability of future research in multilingual embeddings.

The minor limitations regarding the scope of the evaluation metrics and the incremental nature of the embedding methods are outweighed by the significant contribution of the evaluation metric, which has the potential to substantially impact how multilingual embeddings are developed and evaluated in the future.
