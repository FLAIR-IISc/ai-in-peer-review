

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks, and develops a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors' strongest contribution is the improved evaluation metrics, which address a critical gap in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology:** The paper's most valuable contribution is demonstrating that multiQVEC-CCA correlates much more strongly with downstream tasks than existing metrics (0.896 vs. 0.386 for document classification). This addresses a major gap in the field, as prior work has lacked reliable evaluation methods for multilingual embeddings. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is compelling and well-executed, showing that previous intrinsic metrics correlate poorly with downstream performance.

2. **Practical resource for the community:** The evaluation portal developed by the authors is a significant practical contribution. It enables researchers to easily evaluate embeddings on multiple metrics with development and test modes, promoting sound experimentation practices. This resource will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual NLP.

3. **Comprehensive scale of evaluation:** The paper evaluates embeddings across 59 languages, which is a significant advancement in scale compared to prior work that typically focuses on 2-3 languages. This demonstrates that the proposed methods work across a diverse language set, making the findings more generalizable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core methods:** The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are straightforward extensions of existing techniques rather than truly novel contributions. MultiCluster is essentially a standard clustering approach based on dictionaries, and multiCCA is a simple extension of the bilingual CCA approach (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014) to multiple languages by projecting into English space. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how these extensions represent a significant advance beyond prior work.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper compares against only two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), while ignoring other recent methods like Polyglot (Al-Rfou et al., 2013) and other dictionary-based approaches. This limited comparison makes it difficult to assess how much the proposed methods advance the state of the art. For instance, the paper mentions Polyglot but doesn't compare against it.

3. **Coverage bias in evaluation:** The evaluation results (Tables 2 and 3) are potentially biased by significant differences in vocabulary coverage between methods (e.g., multiCCA consistently has higher coverage than translation-invariance). While the authors mention this in the supplementary material, they don't sufficiently address it in the main text or provide a fair comparison by restricting to the intersection of vocabularies. This issue is particularly problematic for intrinsic evaluations, which are sensitive to vocabulary coverage.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you explain that multiCluster, which forces different meanings of words to share vectors (a fundamental limitation), performs reasonably well (especially in the 12-language case), despite this limitation?

2. The paper claims that the mainstream approach of one vector per word type ignores context, yet multiCluster exacerbates this problem. Why did you choose this design approach, and how does it align with your stated motivation?

3. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why CCA-based multiQVEC-CCA addresses the two shortcomings of QVEC (invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores)?

## Decision and Recommendations

This paper makes a valuable contribution through its evaluation metrics and web portal, which will be useful to the community. However, the core methodological contributions are limited in novelty, and the evaluation methodology has significant concerns. I recommend a **weak reject**.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Better articulate the novelty of their methods compared to prior work, possibly by conducting more thorough comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods.
2. Address the vocabulary coverage issue more thoroughly in their evaluation, providing results restricted to the intersection of vocabularies.
3. Strengthen the theoretical justification for their evaluation metrics and clarify how their methods advance beyond existing approaches.

These improvements would significantly strengthen the paper and make it more suitable for publication at CoNLL. The evaluation metrics and portal are promising contributions that could be strengthened by better contextualizing them within the existing literature and addressing the evaluation methodology concerns.
