

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings: Methods and Evaluation"

## Summary
The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and develops a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors claim these contributions will facilitate future research in multilingual NLP.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, which addresses theoretical shortcomings in previous evaluation methods
2. A web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings
3. The multiCluster and multiCCA methods for estimating multilingual embeddings

## Strengths
1. The paper demonstrates a clear need for better evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings, as the authors show existing metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks.
2. The web portal is a useful contribution that will facilitate future research in this area.
3. The paper makes a strong case that dictionary-based methods can work well without requiring parallel corpora.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Methodological flaw in dictionary construction (Critical)
The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, but fails to discuss the potential errors in these translations or their impact on results. The authors state: "For the remaining 47 languages, dictionaries were formed by translating the 20k most common words in the English monolingual corpus with Google Translate, ignoring translation pairs with identical surface forms and multi-word translations." This is a significant methodological flaw without justification or error analysis. Google Translate translations are known to contain errors, especially for less common words or morphologically complex languages, which would directly impact the quality of the multilingual embeddings. The paper should have conducted error analysis on these automatically generated dictionaries or at least acknowledged this limitation.

### 2. Inadequate handling of vocabulary coverage differences (Critical)
The paper acknowledges that coverage differences confound performance comparisons (Section 5.2): "multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than the translation-invariance method. For intrinsic measures, this confounds the performance comparison." However, the paper does not properly address this issue. The authors state they "report the coverage of each set of embeddings in square brackets," but this doesn't resolve the confounding factor. The paper should have either:
- Reported results on the intersection of all vocabularies (as they mention doing in supplementary material), or
- Provided statistical tests that account for coverage differences
Without proper handling of this issue, claims about relative performance (e.g., "multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster") are questionable.

### 3. Lack of statistical significance testing (Major)
The paper makes strong claims about method performance ("multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster," "multiSkip ranks last in five evaluations, and never ranks first") but provides no statistical significance testing for these differences. The paper uses Pearson correlations to show metrics correlate with downstream tasks, but fails to provide confidence intervals or significance tests for these correlations. Without statistical tests, it's impossible to determine whether the observed differences are meaningful or simply due to random variation.

### 4. Inconsistent reference formatting (Minor but significant)
The reference list contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine the paper's professionalism:
- Inconsistent use of "et al." vs. full author lists
- Inconsistent citation styles ("Proc. of" vs. "Proc.")
- Unexplained formatting like "Vincze and Zeman.,? \Q2015\E"
- Inconsistent use of italics for conference names

### 5. Unclear theoretical contribution of multiQVEC-CCA
The paper claims multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming of multiQVEC" but doesn't clearly articulate what this shortcoming is. The authors state: "QVEC has been shown to correlate strongly with downstream semantic tasks (Tsvetkov et al., 2015). However, it suffers from two major weaknesses. First, it is not invariant to linear transformations of the embeddings' basis, whereas the bases in word embeddings are generally arbitrary (Szegedy et al., 2014). Second, a sum of correlations produces an unnormalized score: the more dimensions in the embedding matrix the higher the score." The paper should have more clearly articulated how exactly CCA addresses these specific issues.

## Conclusion
While the paper presents a useful web portal and makes some interesting claims about evaluation metrics, the methodological flaws in dictionary construction, inadequate handling of vocabulary coverage differences, and lack of statistical significance testing are too substantial to overlook. These issues fundamentally undermine the paper's main claims about the relative performance of the proposed methods. The formatting inconsistencies further suggest a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a publication at CoNLL.

Given the paper's significant methodological weaknesses, I recommend rejection.

## Questions to Authors
1. How did you measure the quality of the Google Translate-generated dictionaries for the 47 languages, and how did you account for translation errors in your results?
2. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences reported in Tables 2 and 3?
3. Could you clarify the theoretical shortcoming of multiQVEC that multiQVEC-CCA addresses, and how exactly CCA resolves it?
