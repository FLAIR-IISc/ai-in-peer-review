

# Peer Review: "Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two novel dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks, and provides an evaluation web portal. The authors position their work as addressing the need for "massively multilingual" embeddings that can support cross-lingual transfer learning.

The most significant contributions are:
1. The multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous intrinsic metrics and demonstrates superior correlation with extrinsic tasks.
2. The evaluation portal that provides standardized benchmarks for the field.
3. The multiCCA method, which outperforms other approaches on multiple metrics.

## Strengths

1. **Superior Evaluation Framework**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the identification and correction of fundamental flaws in existing evaluation metrics. The authors correctly identify that QVEC's lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring (based on dimensionality) make it unsuitable for comparing models. Their multiQVEC-CCA metric, using CCA to produce a normalized score in [-1, 1], is theoretically sound and demonstrates significantly better correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification vs. 0.635 for multiQVEC, and 0.386 for word similarity). This represents a major advance in the field, as prior work has often relied on metrics that poorly correlate with actual task performance.

2. **Practical Value of the Evaluation Portal**: The authors have created a genuinely useful resource for the community. By providing a standardized evaluation framework with both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, they address a critical pain point in multilingual NLP research—reproducibility and fair comparison. The portal's design (allowing researchers to upload embeddings and evaluate them against multiple metrics) will significantly lower the barrier to entry for new researchers in this space.

3. **Theoretical Soundness of multiCCA**: The multiCCA method is a natural extension of Faruqui and Dyer's bilingual approach that avoids the semantic ambiguity inherent in multiCluster. By projecting all languages into a common English space (using English's large monolingual corpus as a reference), it maintains semantic consistency across languages while avoiding the need for parallel data. The method is theoretically well-motivated and consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading "Massively Multilingual" Claim**: The paper claims to provide embeddings for "59 languages" but fails to adequately address the quality of these embeddings. For 47 of these languages, dictionaries are created using Google Translate, which introduces significant noise and errors. The paper should acknowledge that the embeddings for these languages are likely of lower quality than those for the 12 languages where dictionaries are extracted from high-quality parallel corpora (Europarl). The claim of "massively multilingual" is thus misleading, as the embeddings for most languages are not reliable for practical use.

2. **Underdeveloped Justification for multiCluster**: The authors acknowledge multiCluster's limitations with 59 languages ("the added semantic ambiguity severely hurts performance"), yet they don't adequately explain why this method is worth pursuing at all. The method essentially forces translationally equivalent words to share the same vector regardless of context, which is theoretically problematic. The paper should either provide strong evidence for when this approach might be beneficial (e.g., for low-resource languages with limited monolingual data) or acknowledge that multiCCA is the superior approach and focus on that.

3. **Unfair Comparison with multiSkip**: The paper compares multiSkip (which only uses parallel data) against methods that also leverage monolingual data (multiCluster, multiCCA, and translation-invariance). This is not a fair comparison, as the other methods can learn from vastly larger monolingual corpora. The authors should acknowledge that multiSkip's poor performance is expected when not using monolingual data, and either include a version of multiSkip that incorporates monolingual data or clarify that this comparison is not intended to be fair.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Dictionary Quality**: The paper does not provide any analysis of the quality of the Google Translate-based dictionaries used for 47 languages. For instance, how many of the translation pairs are actually correct? What is the precision/recall of these dictionaries compared to manually created ones? Without this analysis, it's impossible to assess the reliability of the embeddings for these languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available." However, for 47 languages, the dictionaries are created via Google Translate. How did you assess the quality of these dictionaries, and what evidence do you have that the embeddings trained on these dictionaries are of sufficient quality for practical use?

2. The multiCluster method is described as "exacerbating" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings. Given this limitation, what specific use cases would justify using multiCluster over multiCCA, especially since multiCCA consistently outperforms it?

3. The evaluation on 59 languages shows multiCCA outperforming multiCluster, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why multiCCA is so much better. Given that both methods rely on the same dictionary-based clustering, what specific aspect of multiCCA's projection approach leads to better performance?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its improved evaluation framework, which addresses a critical gap in multilingual NLP research. The multiQVEC-CCA metric is theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the evaluation portal will be a useful resource for the community. However, the paper's claim of "massively multilingual" embeddings is overstated due to the reliance on low-quality Google Translate dictionaries for most languages. The authors should revise their claims to more accurately reflect the quality of embeddings across different language sets.

The paper is well-written and technically sound in its core contributions, but the misleading claims about the scope and quality of their embeddings weaken the overall impact. With revisions to address these concerns, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend acceptance with major revisions to clarify the scope and quality of the embeddings.
