

# Peer Review: Multilingual Word Embeddings with Dictionary-Based Methods

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries. It introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks, and develops a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors claim their methods achieve competitive results without requiring parallel corpora.

## Main Contributions
1. **multiQVEC-CCA**: An improved evaluation metric that addresses theoretical shortcomings of previous metrics and shows stronger correlation with downstream tasks (0.896 correlation with document classification).
2. **Web portal**: A practical tool enabling standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, significantly lowering the barrier to entry for future research.
3. **Dictionary-based embedding methods**: Demonstrating that multilingual embeddings can be effectively learned without parallel corpora, using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries.

## Strengths

### 1. Theoretical Improvement to Evaluation Metrics
The identification and resolution of QVEC's fundamental flaws (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring) is a significant contribution. The paper's demonstration that multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with downstream tasks (0.896 vs. 0.635 for multiQVEC and 0.386 for word similarity) provides compelling evidence of the metric's value. This is arguably the paper's strongest contribution, as evaluation methodology has been a critical gap in multilingual NLP research.

### 2. Practical Web Portal for Standardized Evaluation
The development of an accessible evaluation portal is a major practical contribution that addresses a real need in the field. By providing standardized evaluation metrics and data, the authors facilitate more reliable and comparable research in multilingual embeddings. The portal's dual-mode operation (development and test) encourages sound experimental practices, which is particularly valuable for a field with inconsistent evaluation approaches.

### 3. Demonstrating Value of Dictionary-Based Methods
The paper effectively shows that dictionary-based methods can achieve competitive results without requiring parallel corpora, which is valuable for low-resource language settings. The results in Table 3 demonstrate that multiCCA consistently outperforms other methods across multiple metrics, with the dictionary-based approach ranking first in five out of nine evaluations.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods
The paper lacks comparison with more recent, advanced methods for multilingual embeddings (e.g., Artetxe et al., 2018; Lample et al., 2018; Conneau et al., 2018). The baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance) are somewhat dated, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how the proposed methods compare to current SOTA. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the authors' methods represent a meaningful advancement over recent work.

### 2. Misleading Claim About "No Parallel Data Required"
While the paper claims their methods "only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries," the dictionaries for 47 of the 59 languages are created using Google Translate (which relies on parallel data). This is a significant methodological gap—the paper doesn't clearly distinguish between using dictionaries derived from parallel corpora versus using dictionaries obtained through other means. The claim that parallel corpora "are not required" is misleading, as Google Translate itself is built on massive parallel corpora.

### 3. Insufficient Analysis of Methodological Limitations
The paper acknowledges that multiCluster's performance suffers from semantic ambiguity (as it assigns one vector to translationally equivalent words across languages) but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is the primary reason for its lower performance. The explanation is somewhat superficial, and the paper doesn't explore whether multiCluster's limitations could be mitigated through additional techniques.

### 4. Inconsistent Evaluation Protocol
The paper reports coverage percentages in square brackets but doesn't consistently handle vocabulary coverage differences across methods. For instance, in Table 3, coverage varies significantly between methods (e.g., multiCCA: 69.3% vs. translation-invariance: 68.6% for dependency parsing), which could bias results. The paper mentions using vocabulary intersection for some comparisons but doesn't provide the results of this more rigorous evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your methods compare to recent state-of-the-art approaches that use more sophisticated alignment techniques (e.g., Artetxe et al. 2018, Lample et al. 2018)? Have you attempted to reproduce these methods for a fair comparison?

2. Given that you use Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, how do you account for potential errors introduced by this process, and how might these errors impact your results? Could you provide a quantitative estimate of the error rate in these automatically created dictionaries?

3. The paper claims that multiCCA's better performance is due to its "invariance to linear transformations," but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for why this property is critical for multilingual embeddings. Could you elaborate on the theoretical foundation for this claim?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by improving evaluation methodology for multilingual embeddings and providing a practical tool for researchers. The identification of QVEC's limitations and the development of multiQVEC-CCA is particularly significant. However, the paper's claims about "no parallel data required" are misleading, and the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods limits the assessment of the proposed methods' novelty and competitiveness. With these issues addressed, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions (specifically, addressing the misleading claims about parallel data and adding comparisons with recent SOTA methods).
