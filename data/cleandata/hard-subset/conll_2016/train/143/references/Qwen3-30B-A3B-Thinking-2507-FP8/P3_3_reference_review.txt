

# Peer Review: Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and develops a web portal for standardized evaluation. The authors argue that their evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics, and that multiCCA consistently outperforms other methods on their proposed metrics.

## Main Contributions
1. **multiQVEC-CCA**: A theoretically sound evaluation metric that addresses critical shortcomings of previous methods (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores).
2. **multiCCA**: A dictionary-based method that consistently outperforms other approaches on intrinsic metrics across 59 languages.
3. **Web Portal**: A practical resource for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

## Strengths

**1. Theoretical Improvement in Evaluation Metrics (Primary Strength)**  
The paper correctly identifies fundamental flaws in QVEC (Tsvetkov et al., 2015) and provides a theoretically grounded solution with multiQVEC-CCA. By using CCA instead of cumulative correlations, the authors achieve invariance to basis rotations and produce a normalized score in [-1, 1]. Their empirical results (Table 1) are compelling: multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with document classification (0.896) and moderately with parsing (0.273), while traditional metrics show negligible correlation (word similarity: 0.386/0.007). This represents a significant methodological advancement for the field.

**2. Practical Value of the Web Portal (Secondary Strength)**  
The evaluation portal addresses a critical need in the community for standardized, reproducible evaluation of multilingual embeddings. By providing a platform to upload embeddings and automatically evaluate against multiple metrics, the authors create a valuable resource that will facilitate future research and comparison across methods. The portal's dual-mode operation (development/test) encourages sound experimental practices.

**3. Comprehensive Experimental Validation (Tertiary Strength)**  
The authors conduct thorough experiments across multiple metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic), languages (59), and embedding methods (4). The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics (Table 1) is particularly strong evidence supporting their evaluation approach. The fact that multiQVEC-CCA correlates well with both document classification and parsing tasks, while previous metrics correlate poorly, provides robust evidence for their contribution.

## Weaknesses

**1. Insufficient Downstream Task Evidence for MultiCCA**  
While multiCCA performs well on intrinsic metrics, the paper lacks direct evidence that it achieves better performance on downstream NLP tasks. The correlation analysis (Table 1) shows that multiQVEC-CCA correlates well with downstream tasks, but doesn't demonstrate that multiCCA embeddings actually achieve better task performance. This is a critical gap—proving that better intrinsic metrics translate to better real-world performance is essential for claiming the method's superiority.

**2. Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods**  
The paper doesn't compare against recent state-of-the-art approaches like XLM (Conneau et al., 2019) or MUSE (Lample et al., 2018), which have set new benchmarks for multilingual embeddings. The absence of such comparisons makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed methods. For instance, XLM uses parallel data more effectively than dictionary-based methods, and its results would provide crucial context for the authors' claims.

**3. Questionable Dictionary Quality for 47 Languages**  
The authors use Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages, but don't adequately assess the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries. Google Translate has documented errors, especially for low-resource languages, which could significantly impact embedding quality. The paper mentions a threshold τ = 0.1 but doesn't provide evidence of dictionary quality (e.g., precision/recall against human-annotated dictionaries), making it difficult to assess the reliability of their results for these languages.

**4. Overemphasis on Coverage**  
The paper reports coverage percentages in square brackets, but coverage alone is not a reliable indicator of embedding quality. For example, multiCluster has lower coverage but performs reasonably well on some tasks, suggesting that coverage is not the most important factor. The authors should focus more on performance rather than coverage, as the latter can be artificially inflated by including rare words with poor context.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims multiCCA "consistently outperforms" other methods on intrinsic metrics, but doesn't provide evidence that these embeddings actually achieve better performance on downstream NLP tasks. Can the authors provide actual task performance (not just correlation coefficients) for multiCCA compared to other methods?

2. Why were recent state-of-the-art methods like XLM and MUSE excluded from the comparison? How do the authors think their methods compare to these approaches, and what would be the expected performance differences?

3. How did the authors assess the quality of the Google Translate-based dictionaries for the 47 languages? What is the precision/recall of these dictionaries compared to human-annotated dictionaries, and how might translation errors impact embedding quality?

4. The paper uses different maximum cluster sizes for the 12-language and 59-language experiments (1,000 vs. 10,000). Why was this adjustment necessary, and how does it affect the comparability of results between these two experiments?

5. The paper states that "multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than the translation-invariance method," but coverage is not a reliable metric for embedding quality. Can the authors clarify why coverage is emphasized in their results, rather than focusing on performance on downstream tasks?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant methodological contribution through the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses critical flaws in previous evaluation methods. The web portal is a valuable practical resource for the community. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods and insufficient evidence of downstream task performance for multiCCA are serious weaknesses. With revisions addressing these issues, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
