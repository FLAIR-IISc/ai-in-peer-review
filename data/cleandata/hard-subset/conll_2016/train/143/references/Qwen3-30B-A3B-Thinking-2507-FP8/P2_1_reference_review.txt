

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries, without needing parallel corpora. The authors also introduce two new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics, and develop a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. 

The main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1. The proposed evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA in particular) that significantly outperform existing metrics in correlating with downstream task performance
2. The dictionary-based estimation methods that work with just monolingual data and dictionaries
3. The web portal for easy evaluation of multilingual embeddings

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper's most compelling contribution is its thorough analysis of evaluation metrics. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their proposed metrics (especially multiQVEC-CCA) correlate much better with downstream tasks than previous metrics (0.896 vs. 0.386 for document classification). This addresses a critical gap in the field - the lack of good intrinsic evaluation methods for multilingual embeddings. The paper shows that previous metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream performance, which has likely led to misleading conclusions in prior work.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across 59 languages, multiple metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic), and different estimation methods. The comparison between methods is well-justified, and the results are clearly presented. The inclusion of the web portal with downloadable data and evaluation tools is a major practical contribution that will benefit the community.

3. **Clear theoretical motivation**: The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed evaluation metrics, addressing the limitations of QVEC (invariance to basis transformations and unnormalized scores). The extension to multilingual settings is well-motivated and carefully executed. The paper also provides a good discussion of the trade-offs between different methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of proposed methods**: The multiCCA method is presented as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer's bilingual CCA method. While the extension to multiple languages is useful, it lacks significant innovation. The multiCluster method is also quite similar to previous work on word clusters (e.g., Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how these methods advance the state of the art beyond existing approaches.

2. **Insufficient analysis of results**: The paper reports that multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, but provides limited analysis of *why* this is the case. The authors mention that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings, but this claim isn't thoroughly supported with evidence or analysis. More detailed examination of failure cases or qualitative analysis would strengthen the paper.

3. **Limited generalizability of evaluation metrics**: The proposed evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) rely on supersense annotations, which are only available for a limited set of languages (English, Italian, Danish in the paper). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't propose a solution or discuss how this affects the generalizability of their evaluation approach. This significantly limits the applicability of their metrics to many languages.

4. **Comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper doesn't compare to recent high-performing methods like MUSE (Conneau et al., 2017) or LASER (Artetxe et al., 2018), which use more sophisticated approaches (e.g., adversarial training) and have achieved strong results. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess how competitive the proposed methods are with current state-of-the-art.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you elaborate on why the multiCCA method consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics, particularly given that multiCluster uses a more direct approach to aligning translationally equivalent words?

2. The paper mentions that supersense annotations are inconsistent across languages and not publicly available for most languages. What alternative linguistic resources could be used to extend the multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics to other language pairs?

3. How would the authors expect the proposed methods to perform on low-resource languages where dictionaries might be less reliable or available?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing the critical need for better evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings. The proposed metrics significantly outperform existing approaches in correlating with downstream tasks, which is a major strength. However, the novelty of the estimation methods is limited, and the paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of results and comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods. The evaluation metrics represent the most significant contribution of the paper, but their limited applicability due to reliance on supersense annotations is a notable limitation.

The paper is well-written, well-structured, and provides a useful resource for the community (the web portal). With some additional analysis and comparison to recent work, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to evaluation methodology but offers limited novelty in the estimation methods themselves.
