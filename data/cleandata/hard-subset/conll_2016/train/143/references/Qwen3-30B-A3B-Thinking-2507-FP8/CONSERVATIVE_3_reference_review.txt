

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings: A Dictionary-Based Approach"

## Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings, develops new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and creates a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The authors claim these methods require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (not parallel corpora), and that their evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics.

## Strengths

1. **The web portal is a valuable contribution**: The authors have created a practical, well-structured resource that will facilitate future research by providing standardized evaluation metrics, development/test data, and an easy way to evaluate embeddings. This is a genuinely useful tool for the community.

2. **The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics provides important insights**: The paper demonstrates that traditional intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks, while the proposed metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) show better correlations. This is a meaningful contribution to evaluation methodology.

3. **The comprehensive comparison across multiple metrics and languages**: The authors evaluate four embedding methods across multiple languages and multiple evaluation metrics, providing a thorough comparison that helps researchers understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty in the proposed methods**: The authors claim their methods are "novel," but both multiCluster and multiCCA appear to be straightforward extensions of existing approaches rather than significant innovations. MultiCCA is described as a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is essentially a clustering approach that has been used before (e.g., by Klementiev et al., 2012). The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how these methods differ from or improve upon established baselines. This is a major weakness for a paper claiming to advance the state-of-the-art.

2. **Insufficient validation of the proposed evaluation metrics**: While the paper claims multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification), the correlation coefficients are not dramatically higher than existing metrics (e.g., 0.635 for multiQVEC). The evaluation is limited to only three languages (English, Italian, Danish) and a small set of tasks, which doesn't provide strong evidence for generalizability. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the differences are meaningful.

3. **Inconsistent results across evaluation metrics**: As shown in Table 3, the ranking of methods varies across different metrics, undermining the claim that the proposed metrics are superior. If the metrics don't consistently rank methods in the same way, it's hard to argue they're better. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a compelling justification for preferring their metrics.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper doesn't compare with modern transformer-based multilingual approaches (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), which have become the new standard in multilingual NLP. This is a significant oversight, as the proposed methods would need to demonstrate clear advantages over these established baselines to be considered impactful.

5. **Insufficient evidence for practical advantage**: While the paper provides some results, it doesn't clearly demonstrate that the proposed methods offer a practical advantage over established baselines in real-world applications. The extrinsic task results (Table 3) show competitiveness but not clear superiority, and the paper doesn't quantify the magnitude of any improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) specifically differ from and improve upon existing approaches like those of Klementiev et al. (2012) or Guo et al. (2016)? What is the specific theoretical or empirical contribution that makes them novel?

2. The correlations shown in Table 1 (e.g., 0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA vs. document classification) are not dramatically higher than existing metrics. Can the authors provide statistical significance tests to show these differences are meaningful?

3. Why didn't the paper compare with modern transformer-based multilingual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), which have become the new standard in multilingual NLP? How do the proposed methods compare to these state-of-the-art approaches?

4. The paper reports varying coverage percentages across methods (e.g., multiCCA vs. translation-invariance). How did the authors address this potential confounding factor in their evaluation, and why is it acceptable to report results with different coverage?

5. The paper uses only 12 languages for the main comparison and 3 languages for the correlation analysis. How generalizable are these results to a larger set of languages?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a useful web portal and provides some interesting insights about evaluation metrics, but the core contributions lack sufficient novelty and rigorous validation. The proposed methods appear to be incremental rather than transformative, and the evidence supporting their superiority is not compelling. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, stronger validation of the proposed metrics, and clearer articulation of the specific advantages of the proposed approaches over established baselines. As a reviewer who values methodological soundness and established approaches, I find the evidence presented insufficient to justify the claims of novelty and superiority. The paper is better suited as a useful resource (the portal) with some incremental insights about evaluation, rather than as a significant contribution to the field.
