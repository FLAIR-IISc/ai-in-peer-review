

# Peer Review: Multilingual Word Embeddings

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, without requiring parallel corpora. The main contributions are: 1) improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics, 2) demonstration that dictionary-based methods can effectively create multilingual embeddings, and 3) a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

## Strengths

1. **Critical evaluation methodology:** The paper's most significant contribution is its rigorous demonstration that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics (word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing), while their proposed metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) correlate strongly. The correlation analysis in Section 5.1 is methodologically sound and addresses a fundamental problem in the field. The Pearson's correlation coefficients (0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA with document classification) are compelling evidence that current evaluation practices are inadequate.

2. **Practical utility of the evaluation portal:** The web portal provides a much-needed infrastructure for reproducible research in multilingual embeddings. By enabling researchers to easily evaluate embeddings across multiple metrics and languages, it will significantly advance the field by standardizing evaluation practices. This is particularly valuable given the scarcity of consistent evaluation protocols in multilingual NLP.

3. **Demonstration of dictionary-based methods' viability:** The paper successfully shows that multilingual embeddings can be effectively created using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries (not parallel corpora), which is particularly valuable for low-resource languages. The results for 59 languages demonstrate the scalability of these methods, addressing a key limitation of previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core methods:** The multiCCA method is described as a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster builds on established cluster-based approaches. The paper fails to clearly articulate how these methods differ from prior work (e.g., Coulmance et al. 2015, Guo et al. 2016). The claim that their methods "only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries" is misleading, as bilingual dictionaries themselves often require parallel data to construct.

2. **Evaluation metric limitations:** The paper acknowledges that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA require consistent supersense annotations across languages, which are not available for most languages. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative, this would fundamentally change the evaluation from semantic to syntactic properties. The lack of a clear solution for languages without supersense annotations limits the generalizability of their evaluation framework.

3. **Inconsistent performance across metrics:** The results in Table 3 show that the ranking of methods varies significantly across different metrics. For example, multiCCA is best on most metrics but ranks last in word translation. The paper doesn't provide a strong rationale for prioritizing one metric over others, which weakens the claim that their methods are universally superior.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that their methods "only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries" but notes that dictionaries are often derived from parallel corpora (Section 2.1). How do you address the fact that obtaining high-quality bilingual dictionaries for many languages remains challenging, and how does this compare to the resource requirements of parallel corpus-based methods?

2. The paper acknowledges that supersense annotations are inconsistent across languages. Given this limitation, how would you recommend adapting multiQVEC-CCA for languages without consistent supersense annotations, and how would this adaptation affect the metric's validity?

3. The paper shows that multiCluster's performance degrades significantly when scaling to 59 languages compared to 12 languages. Is this degradation primarily due to the semantic ambiguity introduced by cluster-based representation, or are there other factors at play?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the inadequacy of current intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings and providing better alternatives. The web portal is a significant practical contribution that will facilitate future research. While the core methods lack substantial novelty, the evaluation framework and demonstration of dictionary-based methods' viability are important advances. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the limitations of their evaluation metrics and providing clearer justification for their methodological choices.

The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling, particularly the correlation analysis in Section 5.1. With minor revisions addressing the evaluation limitations and methodological clarity, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
