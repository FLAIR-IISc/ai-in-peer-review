

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Estimation and Evaluation"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without parallel corpora). It introduces new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than existing metrics, and develops a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:
1. The evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that significantly outperform existing metrics in correlating with downstream tasks
2. The dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that work without parallel corpora
3. The web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings

## Strengths

1. **Strong evaluation metrics**: The paper's most significant contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses critical limitations of the existing QVEC metric. The authors correctly identify that QVEC is not invariant to linear transformations of embeddings and produces unnormalized scores that prevent comparison across different dimensionality. Their solution using CCA produces a normalized score in [-1, 1] that is invariant to basis rotation. The empirical evidence is compelling, showing that multiQVEC-CCA achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification performance, substantially higher than existing metrics (e.g., 0.386 for word similarity).

2. **Web portal for standardized evaluation**: The authors created a valuable community resource in the form of an evaluation portal. This portal provides a standardized framework for researchers to evaluate multilingual embeddings using both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, with development and test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. The portal will substantially facilitate future research in this area by enabling consistent evaluation across different methods.

3. **Important gap in evaluation methodology**: The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks. The authors provide a clear, data-driven analysis showing that their proposed metrics better predict performance on downstream tasks, which addresses a critical gap in the literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty in estimation methods**: The proposed estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) lack clear novelty compared to existing approaches. The multiCCA method is explicitly described as "a simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is similar to previous clustering-based approaches (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012). The paper fails to articulate what makes these methods distinct from prior work, which weakens the contribution.

2. **Quality concerns with dictionary creation**: The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 of the 59 languages, but doesn't adequately address the quality issues this introduces. The paper states they "ignore translation pairs with identical surface forms and multi-word translations," but doesn't provide details on validation procedures or error rates. This is a significant limitation, as dictionary quality directly impacts embedding quality, and the paper doesn't compare results with manually created dictionaries for these languages.

3. **Limited justification for 59-language scale**: The paper claims to cover 59 languages, but doesn't clearly justify why this scale is meaningful or different from prior work. Al-Rfou et al. (2013) trained embeddings for over 100 languages, and the paper doesn't explain why 59 languages represents a meaningful advancement. The evaluation results (Table 2) don't show a dramatic improvement over baselines, making the scale seem arbitrary rather than significant.

4. **Coverage differences confound results**: The paper notes that coverage differences between methods (e.g., multiCCA has better coverage than translation-invariance) confound performance comparisons. While they mention they provide results on the vocabulary intersection in supplementary material, the main results don't adequately address this issue, potentially biasing the comparison.

5. **Limited discussion of metric limitations**: The authors acknowledge that supersense annotations are inconsistent across languages and may not be publicly available, which limits the applicability of their metrics. However, they don't explore alternative approaches for languages without consistent supersense annotations, leaving a significant gap in the practical utility of their evaluation framework.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages. Can you provide details on how you validated the quality of these automatically generated dictionaries, and what error rates you observed?

2. Given that multiCCA is described as "a simple extension" of prior work, what specific novelty does this method bring compared to existing bilingual embedding approaches?

3. The paper claims to cover 59 languages, but previous work (e.g., Al-Rfou et al., 2013) has covered over 100 languages. What makes the scale of 59 languages significant compared to prior work?

4. How does the paper address the limitation that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA require consistent supersense annotations across languages, which may not be available for many languages?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a strong contribution to evaluation methodology in multilingual NLP through the development of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, which significantly outperform existing metrics in correlating with downstream tasks. The web portal is also a valuable community resource. However, the estimation methods lack clear novelty, and the paper's claims about the scale of 59 languages are not sufficiently justified. The quality concerns with Google Translate-generated dictionaries are a significant weakness that needs to be addressed. While the evaluation metrics represent a meaningful contribution, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of novelty and more thorough discussion of limitations.

The paper provides valuable empirical evidence that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks, which is an important insight for the field. The evaluation metrics and portal will likely have a positive impact on future research in multilingual embeddings. However, the lack of clear novelty in the estimation methods and the limitations in dictionary quality for most languages weaken the overall contribution.
