

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries. The authors introduce two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The main contributions I identify are:

1. **Development of multiQVEC-CCA**: The most significant contribution is the theoretical improvement of QVEC for multilingual evaluation, addressing key limitations of existing metrics and demonstrating better correlation with downstream tasks.

2. **Principled extension of CCA for multilingual embeddings**: The multiCCA method provides a theoretically grounded approach to extending bilingual embedding techniques to multiple languages, which is a meaningful contribution.

3. **Web portal for evaluation**: The creation of an accessible evaluation portal that supports multiple metrics and languages is a valuable practical contribution to the community.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical improvement of evaluation metrics**: The authors' identification of fundamental flaws in QVEC (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores) and their development of multiQVEC-CCA (using CCA to address these issues) is a major strength. Their empirical results showing multiQVEC-CCA achieves a correlation of 0.896 with document classification (vs. 0.386 for word similarity) provide compelling evidence that this metric better captures embedding quality for downstream tasks.

2. **Practical value of the evaluation portal**: The web portal is exceptionally well-designed and addresses a genuine need in the field. It enables standardized, reproducible evaluation of multilingual embeddings across multiple languages and metrics, which will significantly benefit the community by reducing evaluation inconsistencies.

3. **Comprehensive correlation analysis**: The paper's thorough correlation analysis between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks is a strong methodological contribution. By demonstrating that existing metrics correlate poorly with downstream performance while their proposed metrics correlate well, the authors provide a clear roadmap for future research in evaluation.

4. **Clear demonstration of methodological differences**: The results showing that different evaluation metrics capture different aspects of embedding quality (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA excels in document classification while word translation excels in translation tasks) is valuable for understanding what different metrics measure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of estimation methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are not particularly novel. multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a simple application of word clustering to multilingual embeddings. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these extensions are novel contributions rather than incremental improvements.

2. **Questionable dictionary creation method**: For 47 of the 59 languages, the authors use Google Translate to create bilingual dictionaries. This introduces significant noise into the data, and the paper doesn't adequately address how this might impact the quality of the embeddings or the validity of their results. A comparison with higher-quality dictionaries (when available) would have strengthened the paper.

3. **Inconsistent results across metrics**: The paper shows that the ranking of methods varies significantly across different evaluation metrics (e.g., multiCCA outperforms multiCluster in most metrics but not all). This inconsistency undermines the claim that their methods are definitively superior, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain or address this issue.

4. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper doesn't compare their methods to the most recent state-of-the-art multilingual embedding approaches (e.g., those using parallel data or more sophisticated alignment techniques), making it difficult to assess the true value of their contributions.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of using Google Translate for dictionary creation, nor does it address how the quality of embeddings might vary across languages based on dictionary quality.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the quality of embeddings trained with Google Translate dictionaries compare to embeddings trained with high-quality manual dictionaries (when available for the same language pairs)?

2. Why did the authors choose to use Google Translate for dictionary creation for 47 languages rather than other dictionary extraction methods (e.g., fast align or dictionary extraction from parallel corpora)?

3. The results show inconsistent performance across evaluation metrics. How do the authors explain this inconsistency, and what does it imply about the nature of multilingual embeddings?

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its improved evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and the accompanying evaluation portal. However, the novelty of the estimation methods is limited, and the reliance on Google Translate for dictionary creation introduces significant concerns about data quality. The inconsistent results across metrics also weaken the paper's central claim.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions:
1. Address the concerns about dictionary quality by providing a comparison with higher-quality dictionaries when available.
2. Expand the comparison to include state-of-the-art methods from recent literature.
3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the impact of dictionary quality on embedding performance.
4. Clarify the interpretation of the inconsistent results across evaluation metrics.

With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field by providing both a better evaluation framework and a useful community resource.
