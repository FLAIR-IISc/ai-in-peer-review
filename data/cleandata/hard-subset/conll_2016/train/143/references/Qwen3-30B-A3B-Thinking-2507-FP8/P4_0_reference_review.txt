

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings: Methods and Evaluation"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (no parallel corpora needed). It introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks, and develops a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The primary contributions are:

1. Novel evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address theoretical shortcomings of previous intrinsic metrics and demonstrate significantly better correlation with extrinsic tasks.
2. A practical web portal enabling standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings across multiple languages and metrics.
3. The multiCCA method, which consistently outperforms other approaches in multilingual settings.

## Strengths

**1. Strong empirical validation of evaluation metrics:** The paper makes a compelling case that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing). The authors demonstrate through Table 1 that their proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification, substantially higher than previous metrics (max 0.386). This is a significant contribution because reliable evaluation is fundamental to progress in the field, and the paper provides concrete evidence that their metrics better predict real-world performance.

**2. Practical contribution with broad utility:** The web portal they developed (described in Section 4) is a valuable resource for the community. It enables researchers to easily evaluate multilingual embeddings across multiple languages and metrics, with development/test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. The portal addresses a real need in the field by standardizing evaluation procedures, which will facilitate more reliable comparisons between methods and accelerate future research.

**3. Clear demonstration of methodological advantages:** The paper thoroughly evaluates the proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) against baselines across multiple languages and metrics. The results in Tables 2 and 3 show that multiCCA consistently outperforms other methods, particularly when scaling to 59 languages. The paper provides a reasonable explanation for why multiCluster underperforms at scale (semantic ambiguity from cluster-based representations), which strengthens the technical soundness of their claims.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited novelty of core methods:** The core methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are largely adaptations of existing techniques rather than fundamentally new approaches. multiCCA extends the bilingual CCA method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages, and multiCluster builds on established word clustering approaches (Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish these adaptations as novel contributions or explain why these specific implementations represent significant advances over prior work.

**2. Limited applicability of evaluation metrics:** The proposed evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) rely on supersense annotations, which are inconsistent across languages and not publicly available for many languages. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 3.3) but doesn't propose a solution for languages without such annotations. This significantly restricts the applicability of these metrics, undermining the paper's claim that they "will substantially facilitate future research."

**3. Incomplete comparison to recent methods:** The paper compares to multiSkip and translation-invariance but doesn't evaluate against more recent methods for multilingual embeddings, such as those by Al-Rfou et al. (2013) or Guo et al. (2016). This omission weakens the paper's claim that the proposed methods represent state-of-the-art approaches. A comparison to these methods would have provided stronger evidence of the proposed methods' advantages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that multiCCA outperforms multiCluster across all metrics for 59 languages. Can the authors provide more insight into why the cluster-based approach of multiCluster is so detrimental at scale, beyond the semantic ambiguity mentioned? Is there a way to mitigate this issue while retaining the dictionary-based approach?

2. The paper acknowledges that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA rely on supersense annotations, which are limited in availability. What are the authors' plans to address this limitation for languages without such annotations, or to make the evaluation metrics more generally applicable?

3. The paper doesn't compare to recent methods like those by Al-Rfou et al. (2013), which also trained embeddings for many languages. How do the authors' methods compare to these in terms of performance and resource requirements, particularly regarding the need for parallel dictionaries versus parallel corpora?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution through its improved evaluation metrics and web portal, which will benefit the broader NLP community. The empirical validation of the new metrics is particularly strong and addresses a genuine gap in the field. However, the core methods lack sufficient novelty compared to prior work, and the evaluation metrics have significant limitations in their current form. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive comparisons to recent methods and a clearer justification for the novelty of the core techniques. The practical utility of the web portal is a significant strength that outweighs the methodological limitations, making this a solid contribution to the field.
