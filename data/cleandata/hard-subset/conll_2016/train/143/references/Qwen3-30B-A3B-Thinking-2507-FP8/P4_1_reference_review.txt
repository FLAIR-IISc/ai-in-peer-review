

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes methods for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries (without requiring parallel corpora). The authors introduce two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), develop improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and create a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

Main contributions as assessed by me (in order of strength):
1. The evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) that address significant theoretical shortcomings of previous metrics and demonstrate better correlation with downstream tasks
2. The evaluation portal that facilitates standardized, replicable research in multilingual embeddings
3. The dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for multilingual embeddings

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical and empirical contribution of evaluation metrics**: The authors identify two critical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scoring) and provide a theoretically sound solution using CCA. The empirical results in Table 1 are compelling, showing that multiQVEC-CCA achieves strong correlations (0.896) with document classification performance - substantially better than previous metrics. This contribution directly addresses a significant gap in the field, as evaluation metrics have been a weak point in multilingual embedding research.

2. **Practical contribution of the evaluation portal**: The web portal is a substantial practical contribution that will facilitate future research. It provides standardized datasets, evaluation tasks, and an easy-to-use interface for researchers to test their embeddings. This addresses a real need in the field for consistent evaluation practices, which has been a problem in multilingual NLP research.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of how intrinsic metrics correlate with extrinsic tasks. This correlation analysis (Table 1) is valuable for the field as it helps researchers understand which evaluation metrics are most meaningful for downstream applications, potentially guiding future research directions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of proposed estimation methods**: The authors claim multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages, which is accurate. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this from prior work on multilingual embeddings. The multiCluster method is based on cluster-based approaches that have been used in previous work (Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012), and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their specific implementation differs from or improves upon these approaches. The novelty of these methods appears limited.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison**: The paper only compares against two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), which are not the strongest or most recent methods in the field. The authors acknowledge that multiSkip doesn't use monolingual data, but they don't provide a strong justification for this limitation or how their methods would compare to more recent approaches that do leverage monolingual data. This makes it difficult to assess the true performance of their methods.

3. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific meanings but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how this limitation specifically affects performance across different languages or how it might be mitigated. The paper also doesn't discuss computational costs or scalability to larger language sets, which would be important for a method claiming to work across 59 languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors state that multiCCA is a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). How does this differ from the bilingual method, and what specific challenges did the authors need to address to extend it to multiple languages?

2. The paper notes that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific meanings. How does this limitation specifically manifest in the performance differences between multiCluster and multiCCA across the 59 languages, and what potential approaches could address this issue?

3. The evaluation portal is a strong contribution, but it appears to be limited to the specific metrics and languages used in this paper. How do the authors envision this portal being extended to support new metrics or additional languages in the future?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution through its evaluation metrics and web portal, which address real needs in the field. However, the novelty of the proposed estimation methods is limited, and the evaluation lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art approaches. The paper would be strengthened by more thorough comparisons with recent methods and clearer articulation of how their methods differ from prior work. The evaluation metrics and portal are strong contributions that could significantly benefit the field, but the core estimation methods don't represent a major advance over existing approaches.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The evaluation metrics are particularly strong, and the web portal is a practical resource that will facilitate future research.
