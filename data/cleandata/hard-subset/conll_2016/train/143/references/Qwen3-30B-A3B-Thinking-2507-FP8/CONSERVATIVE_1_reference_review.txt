

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods ("multiCluster" and "multiCCA") for estimating multilingual word embeddings, claims to train embeddings for 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics ("multiQVEC" and "multiQVEC-CCA"), and provides a web portal for evaluation. The authors argue their evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream tasks than previous metrics.

## Main Contributions
1. Improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlate with downstream tasks than previous intrinsic metrics
2. A web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings
3. Proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for multilingual embeddings (though these are not truly novel)

## Strengths

1. **The improved evaluation metrics are a significant contribution**. The paper identifies genuine weaknesses in QVEC (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores) and proposes a theoretically sound solution (multiQVEC-CCA) that produces a normalized score in [-1, 1]. The correlation analysis showing that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks (0.896 with document classification, 0.273 with parsing) than previous metrics (word similarity: 0.386/0.007, word translation: 0.066/-0.292) is compelling and addresses a real need in the field.

2. **The web portal is a valuable practical contribution**. Providing a centralized platform for evaluating multilingual embeddings with standardized metrics will substantially facilitate future research in this area. The portal's dual-mode operation (development/test) encourages sound experimentation practices.

3. **The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation**. The authors evaluate across multiple languages (59 languages for their methods, 12 languages for comparison with baselines), multiple metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic), and provide coverage statistics for each method, which is good practice.

## Weaknesses

1. **The core methods lack novelty and are not convincingly demonstrated to be superior**. MultiCCA is a straightforward extension of the bilingual CCA method by Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is similar to existing cluster-based approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Och, 1999). The paper claims "the proposed methods work well" but doesn't demonstrate significant advantages over established methods. Table 3 shows multiCCA performs well on some metrics (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA) but not consistently across all metrics, and it doesn't outperform the translation-invariance method on all tasks. The authors overstate the significance of their methods.

2. **Comparison with strong baselines is insufficient**. The authors compare to only two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), but there are established methods in the literature that should have been included (e.g., Polyglot by Al-Rfou et al., 2013; methods by Klementiev et al., 2012; Guo et al., 2016). The paper acknowledges multiSkip doesn't use monolingual data, but doesn't compare to methods that do, which would have been more meaningful.

3. **The "59 languages" claim is misleading and the quality of resources is questionable**. For 47 of the 59 languages, the authors used Google Translate to create bilingual dictionaries. This is a significant limitation that undermines the validity of results for those languages. The coverage statistics (e.g., multiCluster coverage for dependency parsing: 70.9%) show the embeddings don't actually cover most words in these languages, yet the paper claims to train embeddings for these languages without acknowledging this limitation.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to understand why the proposed methods work (or don't work). For example, how does varying the threshold τ for dictionary extraction affect performance? How does cluster size impact results? Without these analyses, it's hard to assess the robustness of the methods.

5. **The paper overstates the significance of the methods**. The abstract claims the methods "work well in some settings," but the results don't support this claim for a broad range of settings. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their methods outperform established approaches in a way that would justify their use over more established techniques.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you not compare your methods with the Polyglot approach (Al-Rfou et al., 2013), which also trains embeddings for multiple languages (though not in a shared vector space)? How would your methods compare to this established approach?

2. Can you provide an analysis of the quality of the Google Translate-generated dictionaries for the 47 languages? How does the quality of these dictionaries affect the performance of your methods, especially given that coverage is often low (e.g., 70.9% for multiCluster on dependency parsing)?

3. The paper mentions that multiCluster "exacerbates" the problem of ignoring context-specific word meanings, but it doesn't provide evidence that this is a significant issue in practice. Can you provide examples where context-specific meanings significantly impact performance?

## Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the evaluation metric improvement is valuable, the core contribution (the proposed embedding methods) lacks novelty and sufficient validation against established baselines. The paper overstates the significance of their methods, particularly given the questionable quality of resources for many of the 59 languages. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims by comparing against more established methods, providing ablation studies, and addressing the limitations of their dictionary extraction approach before this work would be considered for publication at CoNLL.

The evaluation metric improvement is the strongest contribution, but it's insufficient to outweigh the weaknesses in the proposed methods and the inadequate experimental validation. The paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of their methods' limitations and a more accurate portrayal of their results.
