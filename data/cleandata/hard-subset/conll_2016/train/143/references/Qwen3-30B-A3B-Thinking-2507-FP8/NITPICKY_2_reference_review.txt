

# Review of "Multilingual Word Embeddings via Dictionary-Based Methods"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and develops an evaluation portal. The main contributions appear to be: (1) the proposed estimation methods, (2) the new evaluation metrics, and (3) the evaluation portal.

## Strengths

1. **Evaluation Portal**: The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a genuinely useful contribution that will facilitate future research in this area. The portal's design for encouraging sound experimentation practices (with development/test modes) is particularly commendable.

2. **Correlation Analysis**: The paper's systematic correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics is valuable. The finding that traditional intrinsic metrics (word similarity and translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks, while the proposed metrics correlate better, provides important insights for the field.

3. **Multilingual Coverage**: Training embeddings across 59 languages is a significant achievement, demonstrating the scalability of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Flaws in Formulation**: The paper contains a serious mathematical error in the definition of multiCCA. In section 2.2, the paper states: "ECCA(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enE m(v)". This formula is mathematically incorrect and would produce nonsensical results. The paper uses inconsistent notation for embedding matrices (E_m vs. E m), and the dimensionality of the transformation matrices is not properly explained. This fundamental error undermines the paper's credibility and suggests the authors may have not thoroughly verified their mathematical formulations.

2. **Inconsistent Language Coverage Claims**: The paper claims to train embeddings for "59 languages" (section 1) but then provides two inconsistent language sets: a 59-language set (with 59 languages listed) and a 12-language subset. In section 5.2, it states "We train the proposed dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for 59 languages" but then in Table 3, evaluates only on 12 languages. The paper never clarifies how the 59 languages were selected or why the 12-language set was used for comparison with parallel-data-based methods. This inconsistency makes it impossible to assess the true scope of the work.

3. **Improper Evaluation Metrics**: The paper claims that multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming of multiQVEC" but fails to clearly identify or explain this shortcoming. The theoretical justification for the proposed metric is weak, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that QVEC-CCA is truly superior to QVEC. The paper also inconsistently uses "multiQVEC-CCA" and "multiQVECCCA" in different sections, creating confusion.

4. **Unjustified Threshold Selection**: The paper states "We fixed τ = 0.1 early on based on manual inspection of the resulting dictionaries" (section 2.1) but provides no ablation studies or justification for this choice. The threshold τ significantly impacts dictionary quality, and without proper analysis of how different τ values affect results, the paper's claims about the effectiveness of the methods are questionable.

5. **Technical Errors and Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting issues, including inconsistent terminology (multiQVEC-CCA vs. multiQVECCCA), mathematical notation errors (E m(v) instead of E_m(v)), and an internal IP address (http://128.2.220.95/multilingual) where a proper URL should be. These issues suggest a lack of attention to detail that is particularly concerning for a paper claiming methodological rigor.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify why the paper reports results for 59 languages in section 5.2 but only evaluates on 12 languages in Table 3? How were the 12 languages selected from the 59?

2. Please provide a detailed mathematical justification for the multiCCA transformation formula, including the dimensionality of all matrices involved. The current formulation (ECCA(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enE m(v)) appears to be incorrect.

3. The paper claims that multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming" of multiQVEC but doesn't specify what this shortcoming is. Could you explicitly state this shortcoming and provide evidence that multiQVEC-CCA resolves it?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a useful evaluation portal and provides valuable insights about evaluation metrics, the critical mathematical errors in the proposed methods, inconsistent language coverage claims, and lack of justification for key design choices (such as the τ threshold) undermine the paper's credibility. These methodological flaws, combined with the formatting inconsistencies and theoretical weaknesses in the evaluation metrics, are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper's contributions are overshadowed by these fundamental issues, and the authors would need to thoroughly revise the mathematical formulations and clarify their experimental setup before the paper could be considered acceptable for publication.

The paper's potential value is significant, but the current version contains too many critical errors that prevent readers from trusting the proposed methods or results. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
