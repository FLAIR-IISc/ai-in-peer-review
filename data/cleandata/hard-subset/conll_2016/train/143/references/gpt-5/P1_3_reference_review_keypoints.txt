- Paper proposes two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods: multiCCA and multiCluster, trained with monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries.
- Introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a CCA-based intrinsic evaluation metric claimed to correlate better with downstream tasks than existing metrics.
- Provides a public evaluation portal with datasets and scripts to standardize multilingual embedding benchmarking.
- Experiments cover up to 59 languages and compare against parallel-corpus baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance) on intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.
- Key claimed contribution: multiQVEC-CCA and an empirical study showing stronger correlation with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation.
- Key claimed contribution: scalable, practical multilingual embedding training for many low-resource languages, with coverage analysis.
- Key claimed contribution: release of evaluation resources to support reproducibility and standardized comparisons.
- Strength: multiQVEC-CCA addresses QVEC issues (basis variance and dimensionality) and shows strong correlation with document classification (r=0.896).
- Strength: Methods work with monolingual data and dictionaries, enabling training for 59 languages relevant to low-resource settings.
- Strength: Careful experimental design reporting vocabulary coverage, repeating analyses on intersected vocabularies, and freezing embeddings in extrinsic tasks to isolate effects.
- Strength: Inclusion of comparative baselines spanning dictionary-only, parallel-only, and hybrid approaches.
- Strength: Release of portal, code, and datasets (where licensable) supports reproducibility.
- Weakness: Modest methodological novelty—multiCCA and multiCluster are straightforward extensions of prior bilingual alignment and clustering ideas.
- Weakness: Some baselines may be unfairly constrained (multiSkip omits monolingual data; translation-invariance limited by implementation), complicating comparisons.
- Weakness: Risk of train–test leakage and bias from using Google Translate for training dictionaries and evaluation word translations in many languages.
- Weakness: Correlation study is limited in scope (17 embeddings, three languages, two tasks) and shows weak correlation with parsing (r=0.273), raising generalization concerns.
- Weakness: Anonymity/privacy issues (live IP/URLs) and underspecified experimental details (corpus sources, preprocessing, CCA settings, hyperparameters).
- Question: How was train–test leakage prevented when using Google Translate for both training and evaluation dictionaries in the 59-language setup?
- Question: What CCA implementation/regularization was used for multiCCA, how were OOV dictionary words handled, and were alternatives (PLS/Procrustes) tested?
- Question: How sensitive are results to choosing English as pivot versus other pivots or multiway CCA approaches?
- Question: Can the authors provide detailed monolingual corpus statistics (size, domain, tokenization) and analyze effects of corpus size disparities?
- Question: For multiCluster, how do maximum cluster size and threshold τ affect semantic conflation and cluster purity?
- Overall assessment: Useful contribution to evaluation methodology and scalable multilingual recipes, but limited novelty, some weak/biased baselines, and experimental rigor concerns—borderline accept.
