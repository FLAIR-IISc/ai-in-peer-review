Summary and key contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A careful empirical study of intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings, introducing QVEC-CCA and its multilingual variant (multiQVEC-CCA), and showing substantially higher correlations with two downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual similarity and word translation), albeit on a limited setting.
- Secondary: Two simple dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings across many languages: multiCCA (a pivot-CCA projection into English space) and multiCluster (dictionary-induced cluster IDs trained with skip-gram), evaluated on up to 59 languages.
- Tertiary: An evaluation portal aggregating data and scripts for intrinsic/extrinsic evaluation (although the URL in the submission breaks anonymity and some resources cannot be redistributed).

Strengths
- Thorough evaluation suite and useful correlation analysis. The paper systematically compares four intrinsic metrics against two extrinsic tasks, and provides clear evidence that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with document classification and (to a lesser extent) parsing than word similarity/translation. The critique of QVEC’s basis dependence and dimensionality dependence is valid, and CCA is an appropriate fix.
- Practical, scalable methods and large coverage. Both multiCCA and multiCluster are simple, use only monolingual data plus dictionaries, and scale to 59 languages. The paper reports coverage figures and discusses coverage confounds—a welcome and often ignored issue.
- Clear baselines and breadth of tasks. The comparison includes a translation-invariance method and a skip-gram–style bilingual objective. The suite spans monolingual and cross-lingual intrinsic tasks and two extrinsic tasks, enabling multiple perspectives on performance.
- Implementation detail transparency. The paper specifies windows, epochs, normalization, minimum counts, clustering procedure, and even practical constraints (e.g., Matlab implementation limiting context window), making it easier to attempt replication in principle.

Weaknesses
- Anonymity and formatting issues. The paper includes a live IP URL for the portal and non-redacted descriptions that break double-blind. References contain numerous formatting errors (missing years, malformed entries, stray punctuation, inconsistent capitalization), and footnotes/URLs appear mid-sentence (e.g., “A method for evaluating monolingual word embeddings. 2http://...”). This is not acceptable for a camera-ready standard and complicates verification.
- Unfair/weak baselines and incomplete comparisons. The multiSkip baseline is weakened by excluding monolingual data (unlike the original papers), making the comparison unfavorable. Other strong and popular baselines (e.g., BilBOWA/biVEC variants with monolingual objectives, or orthogonal Procrustes mapping methods) are missing. The translation-invariance baseline is run with a smaller context window “due to computational challenges,” further compromising fairness.
- Questionable claims/assumptions in multiCCA. The claim that the CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” is not generally true with finite data and potentially rank-deficient embeddings; using d×d CCA with inversion is fragile. The pivot-to-English approach is straightforward and not novel; no analysis is provided on error propagation or sensitivity to pivot quality, and no comparison to joint-space CCA across multiple languages.
- Data quality and potential leakage. For 47 languages, training dictionaries are generated via Google Translate, and evaluation for 59 languages also uses Google Translate. This raises serious concerns about train-test contamination and reproducibility (GT changes over time; ToS/licensing; unknown quality across domains). For the 12-language case, evaluation dictionaries come from Wiktionary; again, overlap with training dictionaries extracted from Europarl is possible but unquantified. The threshold τ=0.1 for fast_align-based dictionaries is fixed “based on manual inspection,” without principled tuning or sensitivity analysis.
- Correlation study scope and statistical rigor. Correlations are computed over 17 embedding settings and two extrinsic tasks with only three languages for the correlation analysis. No p-values, confidence intervals, or robustness (e.g., bootstrapping, Spearman vs Pearson) are reported. Several reported differences are small and may not be significant. Extrinsic results lack variance across runs; no significance testing is presented.
- Coverage claims inconsistent. The paper states extrinsic evaluations are “immune” to coverage differences, but then reports coverage percentages for extrinsic tasks. In reality, OOV handling (e.g., UNK vectors) can materially affect downstream performance; the statement should be corrected, and OOV handling must be specified.
- Methodological details missing or arbitrary. Many hyperparameters are fixed without justification (512 dims, 10 epochs, window=5, max cluster sizes 1k/10k), with no ablations. The word translation metric uses a restricted candidate set (Gl2) from the evaluation dataset; this reduces difficulty vs. realistic open-vocab retrieval and is sensitive to hubness, yet no mitigation (e.g., CSLS) is used. MultiCluster’s effect on polysemy is noted but not quantified; no analysis of cluster size distribution or induced ambiguity is provided.

Questions to authors
- CCA invertibility: Under what conditions are your T matrices non-singular in practice? Did you observe rank deficiencies? If so, how did you regularize or pick k? Please justify the matrix inversion step in multiCCA.
- Baseline fairness: Why not include a version of multiSkip that also leverages monolingual corpora (as in prior work), or include stronger/properly configured mappings (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes) and BilBOWA-style baselines with comparable monolingual data?
- Google Translate usage: What steps did you take to avoid train-test contamination when using GT both for training (47 languages) and evaluation (59 languages)? Can you quantify overlap and provide results with strictly disjoint resources? How do you address GT reproducibility and licensing?
- Statistical significance: Can you report confidence intervals or significance tests for the extrinsic results and for the correlation coefficients? How sensitive are the correlations to the choice of the 17 settings?
- OOV handling: How are OOV tokens treated in the extrinsic tasks (parser/classifier)? What proportion of tokens receive UNK vectors per language, and how does this correlate with performance?

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem and offers a useful evaluation insight (multiQVEC-CCA) and practical methods; however, numerous methodological weaknesses, fairness issues in baselines, unaddressed data-quality risks, missing statistical rigor, and serious formatting/anonymity violations collectively undermine confidence in the results. With stronger baselines, cleaner and reproducible data, statistical validation, and corrected presentation, this work could be impactful.
