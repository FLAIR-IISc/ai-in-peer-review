Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
1) A multilingual intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which fixes key theoretical issues in QVEC (basis dependence, unnormalized scores) and extends it to multilingual settings; accompanied by a correlation study showing markedly better alignment with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) Scalable, dictionary-based multilingual embeddings at large scope (59 languages) via two simple approaches—multiCCA (pivoted CCA projection) and multiCluster (dictionary-induced shared clusters)—with broad intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation.
3) A public evaluation portal (with benchmarks and scripts) intended to standardize and accelerate research on multilingual embeddings.

Strengths
- Novel, impactful evaluation contribution with evidence of external validity: The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA is principled (CCA removes basis arbitrariness and produces a normalized score) and arguably the paper’s most original idea. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is compelling: multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with multilingual document classification (0.896), and multiQVEC correlates best with parsing (0.444), while popular metrics (cross-lingual similarity, word translation) correlate poorly or negatively. This directly addresses an ongoing pain point—intrinsic metrics that fail to predict downstream utility.
- Scale and practicality: Training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages with lightweight supervision (monolingual corpora + bilingual dictionaries) demonstrates reach beyond standard bilingual setups. The methods are simple, fast to adopt, and applicable in low-resource settings where parallel corpora are scarce.
- Careful, diverse evaluation: The paper spans intrinsic metrics (monolingual and cross-lingual similarity; word translation; QVEC variants) and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual RCV topic classification; UD parsing), explicitly discussing coverage confounds and reporting coverage alongside scores. This makes the comparisons more interpretable.
- Clear empirical takeaways: With 12 languages, the proposed dictionary-based approaches are competitive or better on five evaluations (including both extrinsic tasks). The translation-invariance baseline excels on some intrinsic scores, while multiSkip (trained only on parallel) underperforms—yielding actionable guidance: monolingual data and simple dictionary supervision remain strong choices.
- Community resource and reproducibility push: The evaluation portal, datasets, and trained models (as claimed) can materially improve comparability and reduce ad hoc evaluation—valuable for the community, even if the methods themselves are simple.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is modest: multiCCA is a straightforward pivoted extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) and is framed as “simple in hindsight”; multiCluster is closely related to prior cross-lingual clustering/tying ideas and, by collapsing translational equivalents to a single type, exacerbates polysemy. The scientific advance on the modeling side is incremental.
- Correlation study scope is narrow and somewhat mixed: The correlation analysis uses 3 languages and 17 models; multiQVEC-CCA is best for document classification but not parsing (where multiQVEC is better). While still useful, the evidence base for broad claims about “better correlation with downstream tasks” is limited and task-dependent.
- Dependence on Google Translate dictionaries for 47 languages (and for evaluation in the 59-language condition): This raises concerns about noise, potential bias, and replicability (proprietary system; quality varies across languages). Using GT both to train and evaluate risks subtle circularity and may overstate performance or correlation.
- Baseline fairness and detail gaps: The multiSkip implementation does not leverage monolingual data unlike Coulmance et al. (2015), making it a weaker point of comparison. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window for computational reasons, and coverage differences remain substantial. These choices hamper a fully fair head-to-head. Some details (e.g., justification for “guaranteed non-singular” CCA projections; sensitivity to τ=0.1 for dictionary extraction) are under-explained.
- Anonymity and presentation: The paper includes a direct IP link to the portal, which appears to violate double-blind policy. There are also minor issues (table numbering mismatches, occasional formatting glitches in references) that can be cleaned up.

Questions to authors
- multiQVEC-CCA: How sensitive are correlations to the choice of linguistic resource and language mix in S? Have you tried UPOS or other universally available tagsets to broaden coverage beyond supersenses?
- Dictionary noise: How robust are multiCCA and multiCluster to noisy or low-coverage dictionaries (especially GT)? Any ablations varying τ or injecting noise to quantify degradation?
- Pivot choice: What happens if English is not the pivot, or if you use multi-view CCA to learn a shared space jointly rather than via pairwise projections to English? Any evidence that the English pivot biases the space toward English semantics?
- Baselines: Can you provide results for a monolingual+parallel variant of multiSkip (à la TransGram), and align context window settings across methods to strengthen fairness?
- Coverage-confound control: For intrinsic metrics, can you add results on vocabulary intersections across methods and report how conclusions change?

Overall assessment
This paper’s strongest and genuinely novel contribution is the evaluation side—multiQVEC-CCA and the meta-evaluation demonstrating superior task correlation, plus the standardized portal. The modeling contributions are incremental but practically useful at scale. Given the clear community value and the novelty in evaluation, I lean positive, provided baseline fairness and anonymity concerns are addressed.
