Summary and claimed contributions
- Primary: Proposal of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA as intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual word embeddings, with a small-scale correlation study suggesting better alignment with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation.
- Secondary: Two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods: a CCA-based pivot-to-English projection (multiCCA) and a clustering approach that ties translationally equivalent words into shared clusters (multiCluster), with experiments on 12 and 59 languages.
- Tertiary: An evaluation portal bundling data, benchmarks, and an interface to score embeddings.

Strengths
- Emphasis on evaluation rigor: The paper clearly articulates limitations of common intrinsic metrics and proposes alternatives (multiQVEC-CCA in particular) that address basis-rotation sensitivity and dimensionality normalization issues in QVEC. The correlation table, while limited, points to systematic weaknesses in word similarity/translation as proxies for downstream utility.
- Breadth and practicality: Training across 59 languages with dictionary-based methods (without requiring parallel corpora) is valuable for low-resource settings. Reporting coverage alongside intrinsic scores is good practice, and the experiments span both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.
- Reproducibility intent: Consolidating datasets, metrics, and an evaluation interface can lower barriers for subsequent work and improve comparability across methods.
- Simple baselines: multiCCA and multiCluster are intuitive, easy to implement, and may serve as strong baselines when parallel data is scarce.

Weaknesses
- Baseline fairness and comparability: The multiSkip baseline is deliberately handicapped (parallel-only) versus published variants that exploit monolingual corpora; translation-invariance uses a different context window due to implementation constraints; and hyperparameters appear unevenly tuned across methods. These choices risk underestimating stronger alternatives and confound conclusions about relative performance. Intersection-vocabulary evaluations are relegated to the supplement and not reported consistently.
- Potential training–evaluation leakage in 59-language setup: For 47 languages, both training dictionaries and evaluation word-translation pairs are derived from Google Translate. This invites circularity and inflates scores on translation-style metrics; even if other metrics are used, the main multilingual evaluation relies on a non-independent supervision source.
- Limited and selective correlation study: Correlations are computed from 17 embedding variants over only three languages (en/da/it), using one document classification task and one parsing setup. The reported 0.896 correlation for document classification seems unusually high given the diversity of metrics and methods; there is no significance testing or robustness checks (e.g., across architectures, languages, or hyperparameters). The parsing setup freezes embeddings and removes POS/morphology, an atypical usage that may distort practical conclusions about downstream utility.
- Modest novelty in methods: multiCCA is essentially a straightforward pivot-CCA extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014); multiCluster closely parallels prior cross-lingual clustering ideas. The truly novel component is the evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and its multilingual instantiation; methodologically, the embedding estimators are incremental.
- Methodological gaps and unclear claims: The CCA mapping is assumed non-singular and inverted to map into English space; with limited dictionary sizes and high-dimensional embeddings, this assumption and the numerical stability of inversion need justification. The clustering approach exacerbates polysemy by collapsing translationally related words across many languages; while the paper observes degradation at 59 languages, there is little analysis (e.g., sensitivity to τ, cluster-size caps, hub nodes, or alternative constraints). Key hyperparameters (τ=0.1, max cluster size, window sizes, dimensionality) lack ablation, and the translation-invariance model is trained under a stricter computational budget, making comparisons uneven.
- Double-blind concerns: The inclusion of a direct web URL (IP address) and mention of a mirrored GitHub backend potentially compromises anonymity.

Questions to authors
- CCA mappings: Why are Tm→m,en and Ten→m,en “guaranteed to be non-singular”? With dictionary sizes smaller than embedding dimension, covariance matrices can be rank-deficient. How do you regularize and ensure numerical stability when inverting Ten→m,en?
- Baselines: Can you include stronger baselines that exploit both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., TransGram/BiLBOWA-style), and align training budgets/hyperparameters (context window, epochs, dimensionality) across methods? If not, please justify why the current setup is fair.
- Coverage control: For all intrinsic metrics, can you report results on the full intersection vocabulary and provide variance across different intersections (e.g., frequency buckets) to mitigate coverage bias?
- Leakage: For the 59-language word translation evaluation, can you replace Google Translate-based test pairs with an independent source (e.g., PanLex, Wiktionary subsets, or manually curated samples)? If not feasible, please remove or clearly separate these results to avoid circularity.
- Sensitivity analyses: Please provide ablations on τ, maximum cluster size, embedding dimensionality, and window size for both multiCluster and multiCCA. How robust are your conclusions?
- Downstream realism: Can you report parsing results in a more typical setting (with POS/morph features and tunable embeddings) and for additional languages, to test whether the observed correlations generalize?
- Anonymity: Will you remove or anonymize the portal/GitHub references to comply with double-blind review?

Additional comments
- The references contain multiple formatting issues; please clean up citations.
- Reporting confidence intervals or statistical tests for correlations and downstream scores would strengthen claims.
