Summary
This paper studies multilingual word embeddings at scale. It proposes two dictionary-based estimation methods: (1) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent word types across languages and trains embeddings over cluster IDs; and (2) multiCCA, which learns independent monolingual embeddings and linearly projects non-English spaces into an English space via CCA. It further introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA—multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics, with QVEC-CCA addressing basis-rotation sensitivity and dimensionality normalization deficiencies of QVEC. The authors release a web portal to standardize evaluation. Extensive experiments across intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) compare the proposed methods against multiSkip and translation-invariance baselines over 59 and 12 languages.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation contribution: QVEC-CCA and its multilingual variant (multiQVEC-CCA) that fix important theoretical flaws of QVEC (basis invariance and normalization), coupled with an empirical correlation study showing better alignment with downstream tasks than standard intrinsic metrics (Table 1).
2) A practical, scalable recipe for multilingual embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries (multiCCA), demonstrated across up to 59 languages and multiple evaluation settings.
3) A standardized evaluation portal and curated benchmarks that facilitate reproducible, comparable research on multilingual embeddings.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful evaluation advance: QVEC-CCA is a principled fix to QVEC’s basis-rotation sensitivity and unnormalized score. The multilingual extension (multiQVEC-CCA) is well-motivated. The correlation analysis (Table 1) shows strong correlation with document classification (0.896) and better correlation than word similarity or word translation for both downstream tasks, addressing the critical question of “do intrinsic metrics reflect extrinsic utility?”
- Practical scalability: The dictionary-based pipeline scales to 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora, and multiCCA achieves consistently strong results and coverage across many metrics (Section 5.2; 59-language setting). This is valuable for low-resource scenarios where parallel data is scarce.
- Thorough, multifaceted evaluation: The paper evaluates on diverse intrinsic tasks (monolingual and cross-lingual similarity, word translation, QVEC/QVEC-CCA) and extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing), explicitly reporting coverage and discussing its confounding effects. This breadth improves credibility.
- Sensible baselines and candid analysis: The paper includes both parallel-based and dictionary-based baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance) and provides reasoned interpretations (e.g., multiSkip underperforms due to ignoring monolingual data; translation-invariance excels on some intrinsic metrics). It also identifies multiCluster’s semantic-ambiguity failure mode in the 59-language setting.
- Infrastructure and reproducibility support: The evaluation portal and release of trained embeddings and code (post-review) meaningfully lower barriers for future work and encourage standardization.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in estimation: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) to a pivot (English) for multiple languages; multiCluster is a natural clustering-plus-skipgram approach. The main novelty is pragmatic scale and evaluation, not algorithmic innovation. This reduces the conceptual contribution on the modeling side.
- Noisy and potentially biased dictionaries and evaluations: For 47 languages, dictionaries are derived from Google Translate (top 20k words), and evaluation for 59 languages also uses Google Translate. This raises concerns about noise, domain mismatch, tokenization/script issues, and bias. There is no intrinsic quality assessment of these dictionaries nor sensitivity analysis to dictionary noise or size.
- Correlation analysis scope is limited: The correlation study uses 17 embedding variants across only three languages (en/it/da). While Table 1 shows promising trends, the small, language-limited sample and absence of confidence intervals/significance tests limit generalizability. Moreover, multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with document classification but modestly with parsing (0.273), suggesting task dependence not fully explored.
- Reproducibility and fairness caveats: Some implementation choices complicate fair comparison. For example, translation-invariance is trained with a smaller context window due to MATLAB constraints (Section 5.2), which may handicap it. The multiSkip baseline omits monolingual data, making it an arguably weak comparator. The choice of τ=0.1 for dictionary extraction is fixed “based on manual inspection” without sensitivity analysis.
- Limits of multiQVEC(-CCA) resources: The multilingual supersense annotations exist for only three languages and are acknowledged to be inconsistent across languages. This restricts “multilingual” intrinsic evaluation breadth and might bias conclusions toward languages with such resources.

Questions to authors
- Dictionary construction and quality: For the Google Translate-based dictionaries, how are multiple translations handled, how are MWEs treated or filtered, and is any POS or frequency filtering applied? Can you report coverage/precision estimates or a small human evaluation for a few languages to quantify noise?
- Robustness of correlations: Can you provide confidence intervals or significance tests for the Pearson correlations in Table 1? How sensitive are the coefficients to random seeds, embedding dimensionality, and choice of languages? Do the trends hold if adding a morphologically rich or typologically distant language?
- Stability and regularization in multiCCA: CCA yields invertible projections, but inverting Ten→m,en could be numerically unstable. Did you use regularized CCA or any shrinkage? Any observed issues with ill-conditioning for low-resource languages?
- Fairness of baselines: Given MATLAB constraints forced a smaller window for translation-invariance, can you comment on its effect or provide a controlled experiment with matched window sizes on a smaller setup? Could you add a stronger multiSkip variant that also uses monolingual data (as in related work) to close the comparison gap?
- Transfer beyond training languages: For the extrinsic tasks, you train and test on the same set of languages. Can you report zero-shot transfer performance (train on a subset, test on held-out languages) to substantiate the multilingual transfer claim?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper’s strongest and most novel contribution is the evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA and the standardized portal) and the careful empirical study across many languages. While the modeling advances are incremental, the practical impact and the evidence that the proposed intrinsic metrics better track downstream utility make this a valuable contribution for the community. Addressing the concerns on dictionary quality, correlation robustness, and baseline fairness would further strengthen the work.
