Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and evaluation. It proposes two simple dictionary-based methods to induce shared spaces across many languages (multiCluster, multiCCA), introduces multilingual correlation-based intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) adapted from QVEC, and provides an evaluation portal with benchmarks. Experiments span up to 59 languages for dictionary-based methods and compare to parallel-corpus-based baselines on a 12-language subset. A correlation analysis relates intrinsic metrics to extrinsic performance on multilingual document classification and dependency parsing.

Main contributions (as assessed here), strongest first:
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA, and an empirical correlation study showing these metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than common alternatives (cross-lingual similarity, word translation).
2) Scalable multilingual embeddings: two dictionary-based approaches (multiCCA, multiCluster) trained over 59 languages, with extensive evaluation; multiCCA is particularly effective.
3) Resource contribution: a public web portal and packaged datasets to standardize and facilitate intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation.

Strengths
- Strong evaluation design linking intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The paper evaluates four intrinsic metrics and two downstream tasks, reports Pearson correlations across 17 distinct embeddings for three languages, and finds multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates substantially better with document classification and parsing than cross-lingual similarity/translation (e.g., 0.896 vs 0.386/0.066 for doc classification). This directly addresses the field’s need for intrinsic metrics that are predictive of utility.
- Practical scalability demonstrated. The dictionary-based methods are trained across 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora, showing a realistic path when only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries are available. This broad coverage is uncommon and useful for many low-resource settings.
- Simple, effective method (multiCCA) with solid empirical performance. The multilingual extension of CCA by projecting all languages into English is conceptually straightforward and yields strong results. In the 59-language setting, multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics reported. In the 12-language setting, dictionary-based methods win on five metrics (including both extrinsic tasks).
- Attention to evaluation pitfalls. The paper explicitly discusses coverage effects on intrinsic metrics, reports coverage percentages alongside scores, and includes an intersection-vocabulary analysis in the supplement. This improves transparency and interpretability.
- Reproducibility resources. Training details (dimension, window sizes, epochs, normalization, frequency thresholds) are given, and the authors provide software, trained embeddings, and an evaluation portal with standard splits for development/test (with license caveats for RCV).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty for embedding induction. MultiCCA is a straightforward extension of prior bilingual CCA-based alignment (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014) by choosing English as a pivot; multiCluster (connected components over bilingual dictionaries followed by skip-gram) is also conceptually simple and exacerbates polysemy. The main scientific novelty lies more in evaluation than in modeling.
- Correlation study scope and statistical rigor are limited. Correlations are computed over 17 systems and only three languages (en/it/da), with one document classification setting and a reduced parsing setting. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported for correlations or for score differences among methods. The 0.896 correlation for doc classification is compelling but may be sensitive to this small design; parsing correlation for multiQVEC-CCA is modest (0.273), and variance is not analyzed.
- Baseline comparability concerns. The implemented multiSkip baseline does not use monolingual data (unlike related work), likely handicapping it. The translation-invariance method uses a smaller context window due to computational constraints, which may understate its potential. These choices reduce the strength of comparative claims. Additionally, dictionaries for 47 languages are obtained via Google Translate both for training and, in part, for evaluation, introducing possible bias and noise not fully quantified.
- Anonymity and presentation issues. The early inclusion of a concrete portal URL appears to violate double-blind reviewing. The references contain numerous formatting inconsistencies and transcription errors, which impede clarity.
- Missing or underdeveloped analysis. Sensitivity to dictionary noise, to the threshold τ used to extract dictionaries (τ fixed at 0.1 based on manual inspection), and to cluster size limits is not reported. The claim that CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” is stated without conditions or justification. The paper acknowledges supersense annotation inconsistency across languages, which undermines scalability of multiQVEC(-CCA) beyond the three languages considered; alternatives (e.g., universal POS) are mentioned but not tested. No ablation on the impact of using only embeddings as features in extrinsic tasks versus more realistic setups is provided.

Questions to authors
- Can you report confidence intervals/significance tests for (a) the correlation coefficients in Table 1 and (b) the method comparisons in Tables 2–3? How stable are correlations under bootstrapping the set of systems or tasks?
- What conditions guarantee the non-singularity of the CCA projection matrices you invert in multiCCA? How do you handle rank deficiency or ill-conditioning in practice?
- How sensitive are multiCCA/multiCluster to the dictionary threshold τ, dictionary size/quality, and translation noise (especially from Google Translate)? Please provide a sensitivity analysis.
- For fairness, can you include a multiSkip variant that also leverages monolingual data (as in related work), and/or re-run the translation-invariance baseline with larger windows using a more scalable implementation?
- Could you quantify the impact of cluster size limits and polysemy on multiCluster performance (e.g., results by average ambiguity per cluster)?
- How will the evaluation portal comply with anonymity during review and ensure long-term availability? Can you replace proprietary Google Translate resources in training/evaluation with publicly shareable alternatives?

Additional comments
- The correlation analysis is valuable; summarizing the intersection-vocabulary results from the supplement in the main text would strengthen claims about coverage effects.
- Consider adding an extrinsic cross-lingual transfer setup (train on subset of languages, test zero-shot on others) to better reflect the motivations in the introduction.
- The references need careful proofreading and standardization.
