- Paper proposes two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods: multiCCA (pivot-to-English CCA extension) and multiCluster (cross-lingual clustering + skip-gram).
- Paper proposes two intrinsic evaluation measures: multiQVEC (multilingual QVEC) and multiQVEC-CCA (CCA-based, rotation-invariant variant).
- Paper releases a web portal and packaged resources to standardize multilingual embedding data and evaluation.
- Primary claimed contribution: QVEC-CCA as an intrinsic metric that correlates better with downstream tasks than common word similarity/translation measures.
- Secondary claimed contribution: multiCCA as a simple, scalable multilingual extension of CCA with broad language coverage and competitive performance.
- Tertiary claimed contribution: standardized evaluation portal and release of trained embeddings and splits.
- Strength: multiCCA is practical, easy to implement, scalable, and yields strong coverage across many languages without parallel corpora.
- Strength: thoughtful critique of QVEC and introduction of QVEC-CCA addressing basis dependence and normalization, showing high correlation with document classification.
- Strength: broad evaluation across up to 59 languages, multiple intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, and explicit reporting of coverage and intersections.
- Strength: transparent baseline choices, clear reporting of training details and computational challenges.
- Strength: release of portal and resources likely to improve reproducibility and community impact.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—approaches largely extend existing methods and may be more engineering than new modeling.
- Weakness: possible training/evaluation leakage because many dictionaries and evaluation pairs were derived from Google Translate; overlap could inflate results.
- Weakness: correlation study is small (17 systems, 3 languages) and may be biased toward semantic tasks, weakening generality of claims.
- Weakness: theoretical/ numerical robustness of multiCCA mapping is underexplored—CCA rank, conditioning, regularization, and inversion stability are not detailed.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons may be unfair or incomplete (multiSkip omits monolingual data; missing strong monolingual+parallel baselines and orthogonal Procrustes).
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of multiCluster’s coverage-quality trade-offs and failure modes (polysemy, large connected components), with no explored mitigations.
- Question: quantify overlap between training dictionaries and evaluation pairs for the 59-language translation task and report disjoint-pair accuracies if overlap exists.
- Question: report effective CCA dimensionality, use of regularization/truncation, and numerical stability of T^{-1}_{en→m} across languages.
- Question: provide sensitivity/ablation studies for dictionary size, noise, and pivot choice (English vs. other) for both multiCCA and multiCluster.
- Question: include stronger baselines that combine monolingual and parallel data (e.g., BilBOWA/TransGram) and a simple orthogonal Procrustes mapping for comparison.
- Question: for parsing, test embedding fine-tuning or added POS features to see if trends hold in more practical settings.
- Additional: portal URL and some data references break anonymity—replace with anonymized links for double-blind review.
- Additional: report statistical significance for key comparisons (document classification, parsing).
- Additional: analyze hubness/anisotropy effects and whether post-processing (mean centering, whitening) alters results.
