- Paper studies scalable multilingual embeddings and proposes two dictionary-based methods: multiCluster and multiCCA.  
- Introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA intrinsic evaluation metrics to fix QVEC’s rotation and normalization issues, and releases a web portal for standardized evaluation.  
- Main contribution 1: QVEC-CCA and multiQVEC-CCA that correct theoretical flaws of QVEC and correlate better with downstream tasks.  
- Main contribution 2: A practical, scalable multilingual embedding recipe (multiCCA) using monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, demonstrated on up to 59 languages.  
- Main contribution 3: Standardized evaluation portal and curated benchmarks to improve reproducibility and comparability.  
- Strength: QVEC-CCA is a principled fix with strong empirical correlation to document classification (0.896) and better alignment with downstream utility than common intrinsic metrics.  
- Strength: The dictionary-based pipeline scales to 59 languages and is useful for low-resource scenarios without parallel corpora.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation across diverse intrinsic and extrinsic tasks with explicit reporting of coverage and its effects.  
- Strength: Sensible baselines and candid analysis, including identification of multiCluster’s semantic-ambiguity failure mode.  
- Strength: Release of code, trained embeddings, and an evaluation portal enhances reproducibility and future work.  
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—multiCCA extends prior work pragmatically and multiCluster is a natural approach, so modeling contributions are incremental.  
- Weakness: Dictionaries derived from Google Translate for many languages raise concerns about noise, domain mismatch, tokenization, and bias; no quality or sensitivity analysis provided.  
- Weakness: Correlation analysis is limited to 17 embedding variants across three languages (en/it/da) without confidence intervals or significance testing, limiting generalizability.  
- Weakness: Some baseline comparisons may be unfair due to implementation constraints (e.g., translation-invariance window size, multiSkip omits monolingual data), and fixed hyperparameters lack sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Multilingual QVEC resources are limited to three inconsistent languages, restricting the breadth of multilingual intrinsic evaluation.  
- Question: How were Google Translate dictionaries constructed (handling multiple translations, MWEs, POS/frequency filtering), and can the authors provide coverage/precision estimates or small human evaluations?  
- Question: Can the authors provide confidence intervals/significance tests for correlations, and test sensitivity to seeds, dimensionality, and typologically diverse languages?  
- Question: Was regularized CCA or shrinkage used to address potential numerical instability in multiCCA, and were ill-conditioning issues observed?  
- Question: Can the authors control for baseline fairness (e.g., match context windows, include monolingual data for multiSkip) or provide controlled experiments?  
- Question: Can the authors report zero-shot transfer (train on subset, test on held-out languages) to support multilingual transfer claims?  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept—evaluation advance and scale are valuable despite incremental modeling novelty; addressing dictionary quality, correlation robustness, and baseline fairness would strengthen the work.
