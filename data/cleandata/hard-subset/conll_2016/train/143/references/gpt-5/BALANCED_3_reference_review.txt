Summary
The paper studies multilingual word embeddings and makes three main contributions: (1) two simple dictionary-based estimation methods—multiCluster (cluster translational equivalents and learn shared embeddings) and multiCCA (project monolingual spaces into a shared English space via CCA); (2) intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings—multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA (a CCA-based variant that fixes shortcomings of QVEC)—and an empirical correlation analysis showing they align better with extrinsic task performance than commonly used intrinsic metrics; and (3) an evaluation portal bundling data, metrics, and upload/evaluate functionality. Experiments cover 12 and 59 languages, and include cross-lingual similarity, word translation, (multi)QVEC(-CCA), document classification, and dependency parsing.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA and the correlation study across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This is the intellectually strongest and most broadly useful piece.
2) Large-scale multilingual embeddings trained via simple dictionary-based methods, with broad evaluation and useful insights (e.g., multiCCA > multiCluster at scale; coverage effects).
3) Engineering artifact: a public evaluation portal consolidating datasets and metrics.

Strengths
- Empirical insight on evaluation: The correlation analysis convincingly shows that widely used intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual similarity, word translation) correlate poorly with downstream performance, while multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better (e.g., 0.896 with document classification). This is valuable guidance for the community.
- Well-motivated fix to QVEC: multiQVEC-CCA addresses two real issues—basis dependence and dimensionality sensitivity—in a principled way using CCA, and extends to multilingual settings. The conceptual step is clean and justified.
- Breadth and scale of evaluation: The paper evaluates across 9 metrics and up to 59 languages, includes two extrinsic tasks, and carefully reports coverage alongside scores. The analysis that multiCluster struggles at 59 languages due to semantic conflation is insightful.
- Simple, effective methods: multiCCA is a straightforward, practical way to align many languages without parallel corpora; multiCluster is a clear baseline for dictionary-driven sharing. Together they provide a useful empirical yardstick.
- Community resource: The portal (plus data and trained models) is a meaningful contribution that can raise the bar on comparability and reproducibility, if maintained.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a direct, almost trivial extension of bilingual CCA to a multilingual setting by choosing English as the hub space; multiCluster exacerbates sense conflation by sharing vectors across connected components. The estimation methods themselves are incremental relative to prior work.
- Baseline unfairness/limitations: The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual corpora (unlike published variants), likely handicapping it; the translation-invariance baseline is constrained by an implementation with a small context window. This weakens comparative claims. Stronger, better-tuned baselines (e.g., recent bilingual/multilingual skip-gram variants with monolingual+parallel) are needed.
- Correlation study scope and bias: Correlations are computed over 17 embeddings with 12 coming from one method family (multiCluster), which can bias results; no confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. The reported 0.896 correlation may overstate robustness given the small, non-diverse sample (3 languages only).
- Potential train/test contamination in 59-language setting: Both training dictionaries and evaluation dictionaries come from Google Translate for the 59-language experiments. Even if not identical, source overlap risks inflating word-translation and cross-lingual similarity scores. The paper does not detail procedures to ensure disjointness or control leakage.
- Incomplete methodological details and assumptions: The multiCCA derivation assumes invertible CCA projection matrices and full-rank mappings for d-dimensional embeddings; this may fail when dictionary size < d or when cross-covariance is low-rank, leading to numerical instability. The monolingual corpora used per language (sources, sizes, preprocessing) are under-specified, hampering reproducibility and interpretation.

Questions to Authors
- multiCCA stability: How do you ensure T_en→m,en is invertible when the bilingual dictionary size is smaller than d or when cross-covariance is rank-deficient? Did you reduce dimensionality to the number of reliable canonical components or regularize the inverse? Any observed numerical issues?
- Dictionary overlap: For the 59-language setup, what steps guarantee no overlap between training dictionaries (Google Translate for top 20k English words) and evaluation dictionaries (also Google Translate)? Can you quantify overlap (types/pairs) if any, and re-report translation/similarity results with strict disjointness?
- Baseline tuning: Can you provide a multiSkip variant that leverages monolingual corpora as in published work (and/or other competitive multilingual methods) to ensure fairness? Similarly, can you re-run translation-invariance with a larger effective context (or argue that window=3 is sufficient)?
- Correlation robustness: Please report confidence intervals or significance for the correlation coefficients; re-run the analysis with a more diverse set of embedding families and more languages; and de-bias by ensuring roughly balanced counts per method family.
- Monolingual corpora and preprocessing: What are the sources and sizes per language, tokenization/normalization steps, and OOV handling? These are important for replicability and for understanding coverage differences.
- Extrinsic tasks: Could you add an extrinsic transfer setting that includes standard features (e.g., POS for parsing) to assess incremental utility of embeddings in a realistic pipeline, and/or add another task (e.g., NER, XNLI, or MT lexicon induction) to broaden conclusions?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject
Rationale: The paper’s core scientific contribution is primarily on evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA and the correlation study), which is useful. However, the proposed estimation methods are modestly novel; important baseline choices and the potential training/evaluation dictionary leakage limit the strength of empirical claims; and the correlation analysis, while promising, lacks robustness analysis and statistical support. With stronger baselines, leakage controls, clearer methodological details (especially for CCA), and more robust correlation evidence, this work could make a solid contribution. The portal resource is valuable, and I encourage resubmission after addressing these concerns.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines and ablations: include competitive multilingual methods that use both monolingual and parallel data; run ablations for dictionary size/quality and for the English-hub choice in multiCCA.
- Ensure clean evaluation: enforce strict train/dev/test disjointness for dictionaries; report overlap statistics; consider human-curated test sets where possible.
- Improve statistical rigor: add CIs/significance for correlations and downstream results; expand the set of embeddings and languages used in the correlation study.
- Clarify multiCCA math and implementation: handle low-rank cases (reduced CCA dimensionality, regularization), and report sensitivity to dictionary size.
- Expand extrinsic evaluation: include at least one additional downstream task and a realistic parsing setting to complement the embedding-only scenario.
- Provide full data/computation details and ensure the portal is anonymized for review and sustainable post-publication.
