Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation framework: The paper proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA for intrinsic evaluation of multilingual embeddings and claims stronger correlation with downstream tasks than commonly used word similarity/translation scores. This is the clearest conceptual contribution.
2) Empirical suite and portal: A broad experimental suite across several tasks/languages and a web portal to standardize evaluation and datasets.
3) Estimation methods: Two dictionary-based methods for multilingual embeddings, multiCCA (a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA into an English pivot space) and multiCluster (cluster-by-dictionary then monolingual training).

Strengths
- Clear need and scope: The paper targets an important gap—consistent, reproducible evaluation of multilingual word embeddings—and provides a diverse set of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The emphasis on correlations to downstream tasks is welcome.
- Simple, effective evaluation idea: QVEC-CCA addresses meaningful issues with QVEC (basis invariance, dimensionality normalization). Extending both to multi-lingual settings is a practical step that will likely be used by others.
- Scale of experiments: Training and evaluating embeddings for up to 59 languages is non-trivial. Reporting coverage alongside scores is a good practice often neglected in the literature.
- Honest discussion of coverage and confounds: The authors highlight how intrinsic evaluations can be biased by coverage differences and attempt to mitigate this (intersection analysis, reporting coverage).
- Practicality: Focusing on dictionary-based approaches is pragmatic for low-resource settings; the multiCCA “pivot to English” is simple to implement and can be useful in practice.

Weaknesses
- Anonymity and reproducibility issues: The paper contains an explicit non-anonymized IP to a portal, while simultaneously stating that GitHub URLs are redacted—this violates double-blind policy. Further, substantial portions of training/evaluation rely on Google Translate dictionaries; these cannot typically be redistributed, contradicting the claim that resources can be downloaded from the portal. This undermines replicability.
- Methodological clarity and correctness (multiCCA): The claim that CCA projection matrices Tm→m,en and Ten→m,en are “guaranteed to be non-singular” is generally false unless strong rank conditions hold. Using T−1en→m,en is ill-defined if Ten→m,en is not full rank; no regularization or dimensionality choices are specified for CCA. Please clarify the exact CCA setup (regularization, retained canonical dimensionality, whitening, orthogonality constraints) and how invertibility is ensured.
- Baseline fairness and configuration: The multiSkip implementation does not use monolingual data (unlike prior work it cites), making it a weaker baseline. Translation-invariance is constrained to window size 3 due to computational issues, likely handicapping it versus others set to 5. These choices can materially bias the comparative ranking. Missing are other strong, simple baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mapping to English space). Ablations on window size/epochs are absent.
- Evaluation protocol limitations and inconsistencies: 
  - Correlations in Table 1 are computed over only 17 points per pair, across just three languages (en/da/it), yet the text elsewhere emphasizes 7-language RCV and 12-language UD. No confidence intervals or statistical tests are reported; r=0.896 with n=17 is high and sensitive to outliers. The mismatch between the 7-language vs 3-language setups is confusing.
  - Word translation evaluation restricts candidates to the test list (Gl2), which can inflate performance and hide hubness issues; modern retrieval corrections (e.g., CSLS) are not considered.
  - For 59 languages, both training and evaluation rely on Google Translate dictionaries (and identical-form pairs are removed), raising quality/coverage biases—especially for related languages that share orthography. This makes the “gold” itself noisy and non-standard.
  - Table numbering/text inconsistencies: The text references Table 2 for 59-language results, but only Table 3 appears. Some footnotes are mangled, and equations/notation are inconsistent (e.g., QVEC alignment constraints appear one-sided; original QVEC uses one-to-one matching).
- Missing critical details:
  - Document classification: model architecture, training regime, preprocessing, per-language scores, and variance are not specified. The paper states training on 7 languages for the main task, but the correlation study uses only 3; splits are unclear.
  - Dependency parsing: hyperparameters for the stackLSTM, number of epochs, dev selection, OOV handling, and whether subwording is used are unspecified. Freezing embeddings is stated, but handling of unseen tokens is not.
  - MultiCluster: the τ=0.1 threshold and max cluster sizes (1k/10k) are chosen ad hoc without sensitivity analysis. Connected-component clustering over noisy bilingual edges can create huge, semantically incoherent clusters; this is acknowledged but not rigorously analyzed.
- Technical inaccuracies and formatting:
  - Skip-gram is described with “noise contrastive estimation” whereas word2vec commonly uses negative sampling (related but distinct).
  - Equations and notation in §3.3 are inconsistent (e.g., summation indices and alignment constraints). 
  - References contain numerous formatting errors, missing venues/years, duplicated author names, and placeholder markers (e.g., “?\Q2015\E”).

Questions to authors
- multiCCA: What exact CCA variant is used (regularized CCA? number of canonical dimensions retained)? Under what conditions are T matrices invertible in your implementation, and what do you do when they are not? Why not map all languages into a shared canonical space directly instead of composing T−1en→m,enTm→m,en?
- Baseline fairness: Can you provide results for multiSkip that also leverages monolingual data (as in Coulmance/Guo) and for translation-invariance with the same window size as other methods? If computationally infeasible, please justify or downweight those comparisons.
- Correlation study: Please report exact n, confidence intervals, and p-values for the correlations; include scatter plots. Why only en/da/it for correlations when the paper otherwise uses 7/12 languages? Are the conclusions stable across more languages?
- Word translation evaluation: Why restrict candidate sets to the test vocabulary? Can you report results with full-vocabulary retrieval and hubness corrections (e.g., CSLS) and include bilingual lexicon induction accuracy comparisons?
- Resources/portal: How will you ensure anonymity and reproducibility given reliance on Google Translate dictionaries? Which artifacts are actually downloadable? Can you provide a blinded archive of all training/evaluation data used (except RCV) and scripts?
- MultiCluster sensitivity: Please include ablations over τ and max cluster size, and statistics on cluster size distribution and polysemy effects.

Overall recommendation
Despite meaningful ideas (multiQVEC-CCA) and an ambitious experimental scope, the paper contains multiple methodological, fairness, and presentation issues (including anonymity violations) that, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the results. I recommend rejection in the current form; a revision addressing the above points could be impactful.
