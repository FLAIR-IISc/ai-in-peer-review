Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
- Evaluation contribution: The paper introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, extensions of QVEC for multilingual settings, with QVEC-CCA addressing basis-variance and scaling issues. A correlation study suggests these intrinsic metrics correlate better with selected downstream tasks than word similarity/translation.
- Resources and infrastructure: The authors provide an evaluation portal, datasets, and trained embeddings, aiming to standardize evaluation and aid reproducibility.
- Methods: Two dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation approaches are proposed: a straightforward hub-based extension of CCA (multiCCA) and a cluster-then-embed approach (multiCluster). Empirically, multiCCA tends to outperform multiCluster; both are compared against multiSkip and a translation-invariance factorization baseline.

Strengths
- Emphasis on evaluation rigor: Addressing known deficiencies of QVEC and proposing QVEC-CCA is useful and methodologically sound. The correlation analysis, albeit limited (see weaknesses), is a step toward grounding intrinsic metrics in downstream utility.
- Broad empirical scope and engineering effort: Training embeddings for up to 59 languages and releasing a portal lowers barriers for future work. Even if not novel scientifically, this is valuable community infrastructure.
- Clear, replicable experimental setup: Hyperparameters, training details, and coverage reporting are provided, and the authors acknowledge coverage confounds and attempt to mitigate them. This transparency is appreciated.
- Simple, resource-lean methods: Relying on monolingual corpora and dictionaries makes the approaches applicable when parallel data are scarce. Showing dictionary-based methods can be competitive in some settings is informative.
- Diagnostic insights: The observation that multiCluster exacerbates semantic conflation as the number of languages grows is a useful negative result for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in methods: multiCCA is essentially a direct, hub-based generalization of a known bilingual CCA mapping; multiCluster is a straightforward connected-component clustering on bilingual dictionaries followed by monolingual skip-gram. The methodological contribution is incremental, and much of the value comes from engineering and evaluation rather than algorithmic innovation.
- Questionable evaluation design and potential leakage: For many languages, both training dictionaries and evaluation dictionaries come from Google Translate. This risks data leakage (training and test signal derived from the same system) and inflates word translation performance. The word translation task uses a restricted candidate set (Gl2) rather than full-vocabulary retrieval, which can further bias results. The alignment threshold τ is set by manual inspection and not tuned/validated.
- Correlation study is small-scale and not representative: Only three languages, 17 runs, and two downstream tasks are considered. The parsing setup removes POS/morphology and freezes embeddings—this is not representative of current practice and may distort which intrinsic metrics correlate with realistic downstream performance. Notably, multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with document classification (0.896) but weakly with parsing (0.273), calling into question generality.
- Baseline fairness and tuning issues: The multiSkip implementation explicitly does not use monolingual data, unlike related prior work, which handicaps it; the translation-invariance baseline is constrained to window size 3 due to a specific implementation, likely under-tuning it. As a result, conclusions about relative method quality are hard to trust. Stronger bilingual/multilingual baselines that exploit both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., TransGram/BiSkip/BiBOWA-style) should be included under comparable training regimes.
- Coverage confounds remain: Although the paper reports coverage percentages and notes the issue, most intrinsic metric comparisons are still on different subsets across methods. The “intersection-vocabulary” analysis is relegated to supplementary material and described as non-representative. This weakens claims about superiority of any method on intrinsic metrics.
- Reliance on scarce/inconsistent linguistic resources: multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA depend on supersense annotations that are inconsistent across languages (acknowledged by the authors); thus the proposed intrinsic metrics are not easily scalable or comparable for many languages. The suggestion to use universal POS as an alternative is not tested.

Questions to authors
- How do you prevent train–test leakage when both training and evaluation dictionaries come from Google Translate? Can you re-run word translation evaluations with gold dictionaries not used in training, and with full-vocabulary nearest neighbor retrieval (and report P@k) to avoid candidate-set bias?
- Can you strengthen baselines to use monolingual corpora where appropriate (e.g., Coulmance et al. 2015, Gouws et al. 2014 variants) and retune the translation-invariance model with comparable capacity and window sizes? If the Matlab implementation is limiting, can you provide an alternative or a fairer comparison?
- Please provide ablations: impact of (i) embedding dimensionality, (ii) window size, (iii) dictionary size/quality and τ, and (iv) choice of English as hub in multiCCA (does using a different hub or joint Procrustes alignment alter results?).
- For the correlation study, can you (i) add more language pairs and tasks, (ii) include more realistic parsing setups (with POS/morphology and fine-tuned embeddings), and (iii) test whether multiQVEC-CCA correlates under those settings?
- For multiCluster, can you quantify the effect of cluster size constraints and ambiguity (e.g., sense purity measures) and explore simple de-ambiguation strategies (e.g., word-sense clustering before cross-lingual merging)?

Overall, the paper offers useful evaluation tools and engineering artifacts, but the methodological novelty is limited and the empirical comparisons are undermined by baseline weaknesses, potential leakage, and limited external validity of the correlation study. Strengthening the baselines and evaluation protocols would substantially improve the scientific contribution.
