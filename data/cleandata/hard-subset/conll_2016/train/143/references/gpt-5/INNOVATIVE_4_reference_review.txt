Summary and main contributions
- A principled, multilingual intrinsic evaluation: multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA, which fixes key flaws of QVEC (basis dependence and dimensionality bias) and extends it to multilingual settings via supersense-annotated linguistic matrices. A correlation study shows these metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than commonly used word similarity and word translation metrics.
- Scalable dictionary-based multilingual embeddings: two simple methods, multiCCA (project monolingual embeddings into a shared English space via CCA) and multiCluster (cluster translation equivalents and train on cluster IDs), trained for up to 59 languages using only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries.
- Community infrastructure: a web portal with data, trained models, and a battery of intrinsic/extrinsic evaluations to standardize benchmarking.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty in evaluation: multiQVEC-CCA is a clear, elegant fix to QVEC’s theoretical issues (basis invariance and normalization), and the multilingual extension is timely. The paper’s correlation analysis convincingly argues that widely used intrinsic metrics (word similarity/translation) can be poor proxies for downstream utility, advancing the discussion on how we should evaluate multilingual embeddings.
- Practical impact and scale: training dictionary-based embeddings for 59 languages without parallel corpora is valuable, especially for low-resource scenarios. The comparison between multiCCA and multiCluster at different scales provides useful guidance (e.g., multiCCA’s robustness and better coverage; multiCluster’s sense conflation issues).
- Breadth of evaluation: intrinsic (mono- and cross-lingual similarity, translation, QVEC variants) and extrinsic (multilingual document classification, dependency parsing) with explicit attention to coverage effects. The coverage reporting and intersection-based analysis reflect careful experimental hygiene.
- Infrastructure for the community: the evaluation portal lowers barriers to replication and fosters standardization. This is likely to accelerate progress and improve comparability of future work.
- Clear analysis-driven insights: observations such as multiCCA outperforming multiCluster as languages increase, and translation-invariance excelling on certain intrinsic metrics but with lower coverage, are actionable takeaways.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in the embedding methods: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer (bilingual CCA to English hub space) and multiCluster is a simple cluster-then-embed approach. The core technical innovation lies more in evaluation than modeling.
- Scope of the correlation study: correlations are estimated from 17 models across three languages (en/da/it). While results are suggestive (e.g., very high correlation of multiQVEC-CCA with document classification), external validity remains uncertain, and correlations with parsing are more modest. Confidence intervals/significance and robustness across more languages/tasks would strengthen claims.
- Dictionary quality and evaluation coupling: for 47 languages, dictionaries and some evaluation sets are derived from Google Translate. This introduces noise and potential train–test coupling (especially for 59-language word translation), risking biased estimates. An ablation on dictionary noise/coverage or human-vetted subsets would help.
- Baselines not maximally competitive: the multiSkip variant is trained only on parallel data, whereas prior work often combines monolingual and parallel signals; translation-invariance uses a constrained window due to tooling. Missing or limited comparisons to other scalable cross-lingual methods that leverage both signals (e.g., BilBOWA/TransGram under comparable settings) reduce the strength of the empirical ranking.
- Dependency on supersense resources: multiQVEC(-CCA) currently relies on supersense annotations available (and consistent) only for a few languages, limiting applicability. While the paper acknowledges this and suggests POS as an alternative, the current multilingual metric is effectively tri-lingual, which complicates claims of broad multilingual evaluation.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is multiQVEC-CCA to the choice of linguistic resource? Have you tried POS-based linguistic matrices (universal POS) or other semantic inventories, and how do correlations with parsing/classification change?
- Can you provide confidence intervals and significance tests for the reported correlations (and Spearman vs. Pearson), and a breakdown by language to assess robustness?
- For the 59-language setup, can you quantify dictionary noise (e.g., precision/recall against a gold lexicon for a subset) and its impact on both training and evaluation? Any ablation on dictionary size/threshold τ?
- In multiCCA, did you consider non-English hubs or multi-hub CCA (e.g., Procrustes across multiple pivots)? Any cases where an English hub underperforms?
- Will the portal support vocabulary-intersection evaluation by default to mitigate coverage bias, and report both full-coverage and intersection-based scores?

Overall assessment
This paper’s most significant and novel contribution is the evaluation angle: multiQVEC-CCA and the empirical demonstration that it correlates with downstream tasks better than entrenched intrinsic metrics. Coupled with the public portal, this is a meaningful step toward more reliable, community-standard evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The embedding methods themselves are incremental, but the scale (59 languages) and analysis yield useful practical insights. Given the strong emphasis on novelty and field-shaping contributions, I lean toward acceptance despite methodological simplicity and some experimental limitations.
