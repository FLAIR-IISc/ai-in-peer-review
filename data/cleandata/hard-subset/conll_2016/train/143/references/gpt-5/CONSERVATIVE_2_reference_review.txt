Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- A practical, dictionary-driven recipe for multilingual word embeddings: (1) multiCCA, a straightforward pivot-based extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) that projects monolingual embeddings for many languages into an English space via CCA; and (2) multiCluster, which builds translation-equivalence clusters and trains skip-gram on cluster IDs.
- An intrinsic evaluation variant, QVEC-CCA, addressing rotation invariance and dimensionality normalization of QVEC, and its multilingual versions (multiQVEC, multiQVEC-CCA).
- An evaluation suite/portal and accompanying benchmarks; experiments across 59 and 12 language subsets; correlation study between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics; downstream tests on multilingual document classification and parsing.

Strengths
- Scope and practicality: Training embeddings for 59 languages with light supervision (monolingual corpora + bilingual dictionaries) is useful. The methods are simple, reproducible in principle, and do not rely on heavy parallel data (helpful for low-resource scenarios).
- Evaluation breadth and transparency: The paper evaluates across multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, explicitly discusses coverage effects (often ignored), and reports correlations between intrinsic and downstream performance. The attempt to standardize evaluation via a portal and data splits is valuable for the community.
- QVEC-CCA formulation: Replacing QVEC’s sum of per-dimension correlations with CCA addresses known issues (basis rotation and dimensionality dependence). This is a sound methodological correction that yields stronger correlations (at least for document classification in the reported setting).
- Empirical findings of interest: For 59 languages, multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster, reinforcing that type-level clustering over many languages exacerbates polysemy. On 12 languages, dictionary-based methods are competitive or best on several metrics and tasks, suggesting the dictionary route is a strong baseline against parallel-only training.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a direct, pivot-based extension of bilingual CCA; multiCluster resembles prior cross-lingual clustering approaches (e.g., Och, Täckström et al.) coupled with skip-gram. The contributions are largely incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Questionable baseline fairness and tuning: The multiSkip implementation intentionally omits monolingual data, while related prior work uses it; the translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window for computational reasons. These choices likely handicap baselines and confound comparisons. Stronger concurrent baselines (e.g., other bilingual/multilingual projection or joint-training methods using both mono and parallel data) are missing or under-tuned.
- Correlation analysis is underpowered and potentially biased: Correlations are computed from 17 embedding variants across only three languages (en/da/it), with no significance testing or confidence intervals. The strong 0.896 correlation for multiQVEC-CCA on document classification may not generalize. The extrinsic setups are also atypical (e.g., delexicalized parsing with frozen embeddings), limiting external validity.
- Data quality and potential evaluation leakage: For 47 languages, the bilingual dictionaries are obtained via Google Translate; the 59-language word translation evaluation also uses Google Translate. This risks training–test contamination and biases scores toward methods that rely on those dictionaries. There is no analysis of dictionary noise or its impact; no ablations on dictionary precision/recall or threshold τ beyond a fixed choice “based on manual inspection.”
- Reproducibility and anonymity concerns: The portal/IP is exposed in the text, conflicting with double-blind review. Some resources (RCV) cannot be redistributed. The paper states software is available but redacts links, making it hard to verify claims; several formatting glitches (stray footnotes, inconsistent tables) further hinder clarity.

Questions to authors
- Baselines/tuning: Can you provide a fair version of multiSkip that also leverages monolingual corpora (as in Coulmance et al., Guo et al.) and retune translation-invariance with comparable context windows? How do results change under equalized training data and hyperparameter budgets?
- Correlation robustness: What are the confidence intervals and p-values for the reported correlations? Do the correlations hold when expanding to more languages/tasks or when using realistic parsing setups (POS/morphology enabled, embeddings fine-tuned)?
- Data leakage: For the 59-language setting, how do you ensure the Google Translate–based evaluation dictionaries are disjoint from the training dictionaries? Can you report results using independent gold dictionaries for a subset of languages to quantify the bias?
- Dictionary quality and τ: Please provide precision/recall or noise estimates for the dictionaries (Europarl-derived and Google Translate). How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to τ and dictionary noise? Any ablations varying τ or dictionary size?
- multiCCA details: Are the CCA projections constrained (e.g., orthogonal) and is T_en→m invertible in practice for all languages? How do you handle hubness and mean/variance normalization across languages before/after projection? Any evidence that using a non-English pivot (or multi-pivot) alters results?

Additional comments/suggestions
- Include stronger and more recent baselines (if within timeframe), or at least strengthen existing ones with monolingual data. Report variance over multiple runs.
- Provide matched-vocabulary evaluations and intersection-only scores more prominently; add coverage-normalized analyses.
- Clarify the downstream models (architectures, training sizes, hyperparameters) and justify the delexicalized/frozen-embedding setup.
- Fix anonymity and formatting issues; ensure the portal and materials can be accessed in an anonymized manner for review.

Overall, while the work is useful and carefully executed in parts, the methodological novelty is modest, some comparisons are not fully controlled, and the correlation claims rest on limited evidence. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen baselines, address leakage, and bolster the correlation study.
