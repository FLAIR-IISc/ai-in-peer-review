Summary and main contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
- Evaluation contribution: The paper proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, intrinsic metrics for multilingual embeddings that better correlate with downstream tasks than commonly used word similarity/translation metrics. QVEC-CCA fixes basis-invariance and normalization issues of QVEC. A correlation study across several embeddings supports the claim.
- Practical multilingual embedding estimation: Two simple, dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual embeddings: (i) multiCCA (a pragmatic extension of bilingual CCA by projecting all languages into an English space), and (ii) multiCluster (clustering dictionary-connected words across languages and training skip-gram on cluster IDs). Embeddings are trained for up to 59 languages.
- Community resources: An evaluation portal with datasets, code, and a suite of intrinsic/extrinsic metrics to standardize and facilitate future work.

Strengths
- Clear evaluation insight with explicit empirical support: The correlation analysis shows that standard intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity, word translation) correlate poorly with downstream tasks, whereas multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better (e.g., 0.896 with document classification for multiQVEC-CCA, 0.635–0.444 for multiQVEC). The rationale for QVEC-CCA (basis invariance, normalization) is sound and addresses known deficiencies of QVEC.
- Breadth and relevance: Training and evaluating embeddings across many languages (59 total; 12 with parallel corpora) and on multiple downstream tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) increases the ecological validity. Reporting coverage alongside intrinsic scores is thoughtful given known biases.
- Simple, scalable, and useful methods: multiCCA is a lightweight extension of prior CCA-based bilingual mapping that requires only monolingual embeddings plus dictionaries, avoids parallel corpora, and achieves strong coverage and competitive performance. This is practical for low-resource and broad-coverage settings.
- Reproducibility and community impact: The provided portal, code, and benchmarks (with dev/test splits) will likely accelerate research and make results more comparable. Even with licensing constraints on Reuters, the rest of the pipeline is well described.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) by choosing a pivot (English) and projecting all others into that space; multiCluster closely resembles prior cross-lingual clustering ideas (e.g., Och 1999; Täckström et al. 2012). The contribution here is more in scale and evaluation than in algorithmic innovation.
- Baseline fairness and strength: The multiSkip baseline is handicapped (trained only on parallel data, unlike e.g., TransGram or Guo et al., which leverage monolingual corpora). The translation-invariance implementation is constrained (context window = 3 due to computational limits). Absence of stronger, resource-matched baselines (e.g., BilBOWA/TransGram-style with monolingual+parallel, or better-tuned skip-gram variants) weakens comparative claims.
- Noise and bias in dictionaries/evaluation: For 47 languages, bilingual dictionaries are from Google Translate; multiword translations are dropped, and identical surface forms ignored. Evaluation for the 59-language setting also uses GT-derived dictionaries. This introduces unknown noise and potential bias, making it hard to interpret absolute performance and cross-method differences.
- Correlation study scope and robustness: The correlation analysis uses 17 embeddings and only three languages (en/it/da). One downstream task (parsing) correlates weakly with multiQVEC-CCA (0.273). Confidence intervals or significance tests are not reported, so the robustness of the high 0.896 correlation is unclear. It would help to replicate over more languages and methods.
- Coverage confounds remain: Although coverage is reported and an intersection analysis is mentioned, main intrinsic comparisons are still affected by differing vocabularies. Given that coverage can substantially skew similarity/translation metrics, more extensive intersection or stratified analyses would strengthen conclusions.

Questions to authors
- Can you include stronger, resource-matched baselines that exploit both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., TransGram/BilBOWA-like) and/or a variant of your multiSkip that also uses monolingual corpora, to make comparisons more balanced?
- How sensitive is multiCCA to the choice of pivot language and to dictionary quality? Have you tried different pivots or noisy subsets to quantify robustness?
- Can you quantify the noise in Google Translate dictionaries (e.g., manual spot checks, duplicate/multiword handling) and its effect on both intrinsic scores and extrinsic performance?
- Please report confidence intervals or statistical tests for the correlation coefficients and downstream metrics. How stable are correlations across random seeds or different train/dev splits?
- For the portal, will you host standardized bilingual dictionaries and scripts to regenerate them (to mitigate license/proprietary constraints) and ensure long-term accessibility beyond the current IP?

Assessment against CoNLL criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The evaluation metric contribution (QVEC-CCA and its multilingual extension) is the most original; the embedding methods are practical extensions of known ideas.
- Quality/Soundness: Generally solid, with thorough experiments across tasks and languages. Some comparative limitations (baselines, dictionary noise) and limited robustness analyses for correlations.
- Clarity: Clear and well organized; motivations and methods are easy to follow. Good discussion of coverage caveats.
- Significance: High for evaluation practice and resources; moderate for methods. The portal and metrics can have lasting impact.
- Resources/Reproducibility: Strong, though Reuters licensing limits full replication; nonetheless, code, datasets (where possible), and portal are valuable.

Overall: A strong evaluation/resource paper with practical methods; I lean toward acceptance if framed primarily as an evaluation and resources contribution, with tempered claims about methodological novelty and comparative superiority.
