Summary and main contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and presents simple dictionary-based methods to estimate them, along with new intrinsic evaluation metrics and an evaluation portal.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation methodology: introduction of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics derived from QVEC, and a correlation study showing stronger alignment with downstream tasks than common intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation).
2) Estimation methods and large-scale embeddings: two dictionary-based approaches (multiCCA and multiCluster) for training multilingual embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, with results covering up to 59 languages and extrinsic evaluations on document classification and dependency parsing.
3) Practical resources: a web portal with datasets, trained embeddings, and standardized evaluation to support reproducibility and comparison.

Strengths
- Strong evaluation methodology contribution supported by evidence: The paper motivates limitations of common intrinsic metrics and proposes multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA. The correlation analysis (Table 1) across 17 embeddings shows substantially higher correlations with extrinsic tasks, especially for document classification (multiQVEC-CCA 0.896 vs word similarity 0.386, word translation 0.066). This directly addresses a common gap in embedding research—poor intrinsic–extrinsic alignment.
- Scalable and simple multilingual estimation: MultiCCA is a minimal extension of bilingual CCA to a multilingual setting via an English pivot, requires only monolingual data plus pairwise dictionaries, and scales to 59 languages. The design choice (linear projections into English space) is clear and computationally efficient, enabling broad coverage without parallel corpora.
- Comprehensive empirical study with extrinsic tasks: The paper evaluates multiple methods (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, translation-invariance) on two extrinsic tasks across several languages, deliberately constraining parsers/classifiers to rely on embeddings. This provides a clearer view of embedding utility than purely intrinsic tests.
- Transparent handling of coverage and confounds: The paper explicitly reports coverage percentages and discusses biases arising from differing vocabularies across embeddings, and provides intersection-based comparisons in supplementary material.
- Useful infrastructure and benchmarks: The evaluation portal and data packaging (with dev/test splits) facilitate reproducibility and standardization in a fragmented area.

Weaknesses
- Baseline limitations and potential unfairness: The multiSkip baseline is trained only on parallel data and does not use monolingual corpora, unlike related work (e.g., BilBOWA/other skip-gram variants that combine mono + parallel). This likely underestimates its performance and may bias comparisons, especially on extrinsic tasks where monolingual context is important.
- Dictionary quality and evaluation circularity: For 47 languages, training dictionaries are derived from Google Translate; for 59-language evaluation, word translation “gold” also uses Google Translate. This introduces noise and potential bias (and a risk of overfitting to the same source of translations), making the word translation results hard to interpret. A cleaner setup would use independently curated lexicons or parallel corpora-derived dictionaries for evaluation.
- Theoretical clarity of multiCCA projections: The claim that learned CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” is not generally true when the dimensionality exceeds the rank implied by the number of dictionary pairs or when canonical correlations are zero. In practice, CCA often requires regularization or dimensionality reduction to ensure invertibility. The paper does not specify how invertibility is enforced or how d is chosen relative to the dictionary size per language.
- Limited linguistic resource coverage for multiQVEC(-CCA): The multilingual supersense resource covers only English, Danish, and Italian. While the correlation evidence is valuable, the metric’s applicability to other languages and tagging schemes remains constrained. The paper acknowledges this but it still limits generality.
- Blinding and presentation issues: The portal URL and GitHub references undermine double-blind reviewing. There are several formatting and reference inconsistencies (e.g., broken citations, hyphenation, missing table cross-references). While not affecting technical content, they slightly hinder clarity.
- Cluster conflation in multiCluster: The connected-component clustering over dictionary graphs can create large, semantically heterogeneous clusters due to polysemy and many-to-many translations, which likely explains its degradation at 59 languages. Although the paper acknowledges this, additional strategies (e.g., sense-aware clustering or edge weighting/pruning) are not explored.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- MultiCCA invertibility: What safeguards ensure that Ten→m,en is invertible for all languages (e.g., dimensionality reduction to the number of nonzero canonical correlations, regularization)? What fraction of languages required rank reduction, and how was d selected relative to dictionary sizes?
- Sensitivity to dictionary size/quality: How does performance vary with dictionary size per language (ablation with varying numbers of translation pairs)? Are the results robust to noise injected in dictionaries, especially those from Google Translate?
- Evaluation circularity: For the 59-language word translation evaluation, did you test with an evaluation dictionary independent of the training dictionaries’ source to avoid bias? If not, can you provide an alternative evaluation (e.g., Wiktionary subsets or parallel-derived dictionaries) on a subset of languages?
- BilBOWA/related baselines: Can you include or discuss comparisons to bilingual/multilingual methods that leverage both monolingual and parallel data without word alignments (e.g., Gouws et al., 2014), to contextualize multiSkip’s performance?
- multiQVEC-CCA details: Are the CCA projections computed anew per embedding model to handle basis invariance? How do results vary with embedding dimensionality (since QVEC-CCA is dimension-normalized), and did you evaluate stability across different d?

Overall, the paper offers a solid evaluation contribution, practical methods with broad coverage, and useful resources. Addressing baseline fairness, dictionary/evaluation biases, and clarifying CCA technical details would strengthen the work.
