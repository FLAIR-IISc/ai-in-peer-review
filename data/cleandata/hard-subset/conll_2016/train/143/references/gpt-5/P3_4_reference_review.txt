Summary and main contributions
- Primary: An evaluation contribution introducing multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual extensions of QVEC with a principled fix (CCA) for basis invariance and dimensionality normalization. The paper provides correlation analyses showing these metrics align better with downstream performance than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- Secondary: Two simple, dictionary-based approaches for massively multilingual word embeddings: multiCCA (pivoting through English with CCA projections) and multiCluster (building multilingual clusters and training skip-gram on cluster IDs), yielding models for up to 59 languages.
- Tertiary: A public evaluation portal and benchmark suite for intrinsic and extrinsic assessment across multiple languages.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated evaluation advance. QVEC-CCA addresses well-known weaknesses of QVEC (basis dependence and unnormalized scores). Extending to the multilingual case and demonstrating stronger correlations with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation is valuable. The correlation experiment is carefully framed (multiple intrinsic metrics vs two extrinsic tasks) and is an important service to the community.
- Pragmatic, scalable methods. Both multiCCA and multiCluster use only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, enabling coverage across 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora. The methods are easy to reproduce and deploy.
- Solid suite of evaluations. The paper evaluates on nine metrics, including two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and multilingual dependency parsing) and reports coverage explicitly, discussing the bias induced by varying vocabulary coverage. It is laudable that extrinsic evaluations force embeddings to carry the burden by removing other features in parsing.
- Honest analysis of method behavior. The authors analyze why multiCluster degrades with 59 languages (semantic conflation via cross-lingual clustering) and why multiSkip underperforms (no monolingual data), and they discuss coverage confounds and intersection-based re-evaluations.
- Community impact via portal and datasets. Standardized data, metrics, and an upload-and-evaluate portal will likely accelerate research and increase comparability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of embedding estimation. multiCCA is a straightforward pivot-based extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s bilingual CCA; multiCluster is a simple connected-component clustering of dictionary neighbors followed by monolingual skip-gram. The methodological contribution is incremental; the paper’s main impact is evaluation rather than modeling innovation.
- Potential evaluation leakage and bias. For many languages, Google Translate is used both to construct training dictionaries and to build test lexicons for word translation (and even for coverage estimation). This risks overlap and inflating performance, and it introduces a system-dependent bias and unknown noise profile. The paper should quantify the overlap and perform held-out or disjoint dictionary evaluations.
- Correlation analysis scope is narrow. The claim that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with downstream tasks is based on only three languages (en/da/it), 17 embedding variants, and two downstream tasks. Moreover, multiQVEC relies on multilingual supersense resources that only exist for a few languages. The generality of the correlation result is therefore limited; alternative linguistic properties (e.g., universal POS, morphological features) are suggested but not evaluated.
- Comparisons to baselines are not fully fair. The multiSkip baseline does not use monolingual corpora, unlike the other methods and unlike related methods (e.g., TransGram, BilBOWA) that combine monolingual and parallel signals. This likely depresses its performance and obscures conclusions about the merits of dictionary vs parallel training. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to computational limits, which may also understate its potential.
- Pivot-to-English design and potential distortions. Projecting all languages into the English space may introduce pivot bias and reduce cross-lingual symmetry. CCA choices (whitening/regularization) and matrix inversion (T_en→m,en inverse) could be numerically unstable. Alternatives such as generalized CCA (multi-view CCA) or mapping all languages to a jointly learned shared space are not explored.
- Extrinsic tasks are limited and potentially unbalanced. Document classification covers seven Europarl languages; parsing 12 UD languages; results are averaged without per-language analysis. The parsing setup deliberately removes POS/morph features to isolate embeddings, but this may yield a task that is less representative of practical systems and can change method rankings.

Questions to authors
- Dictionary/evaluation leakage: For the 59-language condition, what proportion of evaluation pairs (word translation) overlap with or are trivially derivable from the training dictionaries? Can you rerun with strictly disjoint dictionaries (e.g., split English headwords) and report the drop?
- CCA details: How are CCA projections regularized/whitened? Are the learned projections constrained to be orthogonal? Did you observe numerical issues when inverting T_en→m,en? Please report condition numbers and whether lower-dimensional projections (d’ < d) improve stability.
- Pivot bias: Did you try multi-view CCA or learning a joint shared space without privileging English? Any evidence that using a pivot with less data (e.g., non-English) degrades performance?
- Hubness/nearest neighbor effects: For word translation evaluation, did you assess hubness or try alternative retrieval criteria (e.g., mean-centered cosine, inverted softmax)? How sensitive are the results?
- Sensitivity to dictionary noise and τ: You fix τ=0.1 early on for fast_align-based dictionaries. How sensitive are multiCluster and multiCCA to dictionary precision/recall trade-offs and to noisy machine-translated dictionaries?
- Extrinsic analyses: Can you provide per-language results and variance across seeds? In document classification, how is class imbalance handled across languages? In parsing, how is language weighting handled during training?
- Resource longevity: The portal is referenced via an IP. Will there be a stable, citable URL and archival copy of benchmarks and code to ensure reproducibility?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from including a baseline that combines monolingual and parallel signals in the skip-gram objective (e.g., TransGram/BiSkip variants) to strengthen comparative claims.
- Consider reporting results with vocabulary intersection for intrinsic metrics in the main text, not only in supplementary, to mitigate coverage confounds.
- Clarify monolingual corpora sources and sizes per language for the 59-language setup.
