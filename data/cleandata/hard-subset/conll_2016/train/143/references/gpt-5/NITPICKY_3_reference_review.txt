Summary and main contributions
- Practical framework for multilingual word embeddings with two dictionary-based methods: (a) multiCCA, a pivot-based CCA alignment of independently trained monolingual embeddings into English space; (b) multiCluster, clustering translationally linked surface forms across languages and training skipgram over cluster IDs. These are simple, scalable, and do not require parallel corpora.
- Evaluation methodology: extensions of QVEC to multilingual settings (multiQVEC) and a basis-invariant variant using CCA (multiQVEC-CCA), and a correlation study versus two downstream tasks.
- Breadth and tooling: trained embeddings for up to 59 languages; a web portal with datasets and automated evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear, scalable baselines that are easy to reproduce. The decomposition of the problem into monolingual training + lightweight alignment (multiCCA) is simple and enables broad coverage (59 languages) with only dictionaries. The multiCluster construction is also conceptually straightforward and easy to implement.
- Evaluation awareness and rigor. The paper discusses coverage effects in intrinsic evaluation and reports coverage alongside scores, plus attempts an intersection-based control. This is better than most comparable work.
- Improved intrinsic metrics. The move from QVEC to QVEC-CCA addresses two genuine shortcomings (basis dependence and non-comparability across dimensionalities), and the multilingual extension is practically useful. The correlation study suggests substantially better correlation with extrinsic tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- Breadth of results. The paper evaluates multiple families of methods (dictionary-based, parallel-based) across several metrics and tasks, and provides multilingual downstream evaluations (doc classification, parsing).
- Reusable infrastructure. A public portal and packaged resources, if actually accessible as claimed, could have high community value.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. multiCCA is a straightforward pivot-language extension of existing bilingual CCA alignment (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014). multiCluster effectively replaces tokens with translation-cluster IDs and trains skipgram—closely related to prior cross-lingual clustering and cluster-based distributional representations (e.g., Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012). The conceptual advances are incremental rather than novel algorithms.
- Potential evaluation leakage and fairness concerns. For 47 of the 59 languages, both training dictionaries and evaluation for word translation rely on Google Translate; this risks circularity and overestimation of performance on dictionary-based metrics. Additionally, the multiSkip baseline is handicapped by excluding monolingual corpora, unlike comparable prior methods, leading to an unfair comparison. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller window size (3 vs 5), likely weakening its results.
- Correlation study is underpowered and inconsistently reported. Correlations are computed over only 17 embedding variants for 3 languages, yet reported coefficients are very high (e.g., 0.896). No confidence intervals/p-values are provided; no robustness checks (e.g., leave-one-method-out, Spearman vs Pearson) are reported. Reported negative correlation of word translation with parsing (−0.292) raises questions about stability. The rationale for which languages and tasks are included in the correlation study is only partially justified.
- MultiCluster design risks pathological clusters; hyperparameters seem ad hoc. Clusters are connected components over bilingual edges; with noisy dictionaries and many languages, components can become very large (max sizes allowed: 1k or 10k), exacerbating polysemy and semantic drift. The fixed threshold τ=0.1 for dictionary extraction is chosen by manual inspection and not validated. No analysis of cluster size distribution, error propagation, or sensitivity is provided.
- Numerous presentation, formatting, and double-blind issues. The paper contains many typographical errors, broken equations, truncated sentences, inconsistent references, and stray footnotes. The web portal is identified by a raw IP address, violating double-blind. A promised Table 2 is referenced but not shown. Reference formatting is inconsistent and often malformed. Some mathematical claims are imprecise (e.g., CCA projections “guaranteed to be non-singular” is not generally true; the proposed inverse mapping Ten→m,en−1 is unclear and may be ill-conditioned).
- Missing experimental details and ablations. Key training details are absent (tokenization, casing, vocabulary selection, negative sampling rate, optimizer settings, corpus sizes by language). No ablation on pivot choice for multiCCA (why English only?), no analysis of dictionary noise or size, and no significance testing across methods.

Questions to authors
- multiCCA mapping: Please clarify the exact CCA solution and mapping. Why is ECCA(m,v) = Ten→m,en−1 Tm→m,en Em(v) preferred over directly projecting Em into the English canonical subspace via the canonical weights? Under what conditions is Ten→m,en invertible in practice, and how do you regularize if it is ill-conditioned?
- Train/test leakage: For the 59-language setting, do training dictionaries and evaluation dictionaries (word translation) overlap or derive from the same source (Google Translate)? If yes, can you provide an evaluation that avoids such overlap (e.g., held-out pairs or independent resources)?
- Baselines: Why use different context window sizes across methods (3 for translation-invariance vs 5 elsewhere)? Can you rerun with matched hyperparameters and with multiSkip that includes monolingual data, to ensure fairness?
- Correlation study: Please report sample sizes per metric, confidence intervals/p-values, and robustness checks (e.g., Spearman, removing each method family in turn). How sensitive are the correlations to the choice of languages and tasks?
- MultiCluster analysis: Can you report the cluster size distribution, proportion of giant components, and performance as a function of max cluster size and τ? How often do clusters mix unrelated senses due to noisy dictionary links?

Overall, while the paper is practical and the evaluation contributions are useful, the limited novelty, potential evaluation leakage, underpowered correlation analysis, uneven baselines, and substantial presentation issues collectively reduce confidence. I recommend rejection in its current form; a revised version addressing the methodological and evaluation concerns and fixing the presentation could be valuable.
