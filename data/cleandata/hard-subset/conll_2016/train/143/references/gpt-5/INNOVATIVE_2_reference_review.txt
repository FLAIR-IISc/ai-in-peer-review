Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles massively multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. The primary contributions, in order of perceived significance, are:
1) An intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, that fixes key shortcomings of QVEC (basis dependence and dimensionality bias) and extends it to multilingual settings; supported by a correlation study showing stronger alignment with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) A practical infrastructure contribution: a public evaluation portal and standardized multilingual benchmarks enabling consistent, replicable intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation across languages and methods.
3) Two simple dictionary-based estimation methods for multilingual embeddings, multiCCA (a CCA-based projection to an English pivot space) and multiCluster (cluster-then-embed), with trained embeddings for 59 languages.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty in evaluation: Proposing multiQVEC-CCA to address basis invariance and normalization is a meaningful step forward. The correlation analysis (even if modest in scale) convincingly indicates that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with extrinsic performance than cross-lingual word similarity or word translation—useful guidance for the community.
- Ambition and scope: Training and evaluating embeddings across 59 languages using only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries is bold and practically valuable. It demonstrates scalability and provides a shared resource.
- Useful infrastructure: The evaluation portal and curated datasets are a strong community service and likely to accelerate progress by lowering the barrier to consistent, comparable evaluation.
- Clear, pragmatic methods: The multiCCA pivot approach is simple, easy to reproduce, and effective; the paper offers a transparent recipe for practitioners with limited parallel data.
- Thoughtful discussion of coverage confounds: Reporting coverage and noting vocabulary-intersection evaluations shows care in experimental design and interpretation.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty for embedding learning: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA using an English pivot; multiCluster is a simple dictionary-induced clustering that exacerbates polysemy. Compared to recent joint training methods that integrate monolingual and bilingual signals, these are incremental in modeling terms.
- Small-scale correlation study: Correlations are computed over 17 systems and only three languages; one intrinsic metric (multiQVEC-CCA) correlates strongly with document classification but not parsing, and vice versa for multiQVEC. Confidence intervals/significance are not reported, making it hard to gauge robustness.
- Baseline fairness and tuning: The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual data (unlike comparable prior work), likely handicapping it. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to tooling constraints, and it is unclear if its hyperparameters were tuned comparably. These choices may affect the ranking in Table 3.
- Dictionary and evaluation bias: For 59 languages, Google Translate is used both to build training dictionaries and to evaluate (word translation), risking bias and overestimation. The quality of GT dictionaries varies widely across low-resource languages, and no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Limited and atypical extrinsic settings: Only two tasks (RCV topic classification in 7 languages; UD v1.1 parsing in 12 languages), with embeddings frozen and strong features removed. While this isolates embedding quality, it is not representative of modern pipelines and may understate or distort practical utility.
- Anonymity/reproducibility concerns: The portal is referenced via a fixed IP and mentions a mirrored GitHub backend, which can compromise double-blindness. Also, RCV licensing prevents redistribution, limiting full reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Correlation analysis: Please provide confidence intervals and significance tests for the reported Pearson correlations, and include Spearman as a robustness check. How sensitive are the correlations to the particular set of languages/systems?
- CCA details: What preprocessing (centering/whitening), dimensionality choices, and regularization (if any) are used? You state the projections are guaranteed non-singular—under what conditions, and how do you handle rank-deficient monolingual spaces or small dictionaries?
- Pivot choice: Why fix English as the pivot? Have you evaluated alternative hubs (e.g., high-resource non-English) or multi-pivot variants? How sensitive is performance to the pivot?
- Dictionary construction: For Europarl-derived dictionaries, τ = 0.1 was fixed via manual inspection. Can you provide a sensitivity analysis over τ and its effect on both methods? For the 59-language setting, do you ensure that evaluation pairs are disjoint from training dictionaries to avoid leakage?
- Baseline parity: Can you augment multiSkip with monolingual data (as in related work) and retune translation-invariance with a more flexible implementation to ensure a fairer comparison?
- Coverage control: Beyond reporting coverage, could you provide intersection-vocabulary results for all intrinsic metrics (including 59-language settings) in the appendix to further decouple coverage confounds?
- Portal and anonymity: How will you ensure sustained availability, versioning, and blind evaluation (e.g., hidden test sets, anonymized submissions)? Post-publication, will code/data be fully released (except restricted corpora), and will you document exact preprocessing to ensure reproducibility?

Overall, despite some methodological incrementalism and evaluation caveats, the paper opens a valuable new direction on intrinsic evaluation for multilingual embeddings and provides community infrastructure and large-scale resources. These are significant contributions likely to have practical impact.
