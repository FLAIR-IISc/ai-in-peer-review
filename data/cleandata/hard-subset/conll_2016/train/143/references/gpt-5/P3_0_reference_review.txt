Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings trained with only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, and proposes:
1) Two dictionary-based estimation methods for multilingual embeddings:
   - multiCCA: a simple pivot-based extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s CCA approach that projects each language into an English embedding space.
   - multiCluster: forms cross-lingual clusters via bilingual dictionaries, replaces tokens by cluster IDs in monolingual corpora, and trains skip-gram on the concatenated cluster sequence.
2) Two intrinsic evaluation measures for multilingual embeddings:
   - multiQVEC: multilingual extension of QVEC using supersense annotations.
   - multiQVEC-CCA: a CCA-based variant addressing QVEC’s rotation non-invariance and unnormalized scoring; reported to correlate better with downstream tasks.
3) A web portal to standardize data and evaluation for multilingual embeddings.

Main contributions as I see them
- Primary: QVEC-CCA (and its multilingual variant) as an intrinsic evaluation that correlates better with extrinsic tasks than widely used word similarity/translation measures.
- Secondary: A practical, simple multilingual extension of CCA (multiCCA) that achieves strong coverage and competitive performance across 12–59 languages without parallel corpora.
- Tertiary: A standardized evaluation portal and release of trained embeddings and data splits.

Strengths
- Clear, practical methodological advances: multiCCA is a straightforward, scalable way to produce multilingual embeddings from monolingual vectors and bilingual lexicons. Its pivot-to-English mapping is simple to implement and consistently yields better coverage and competitive quality, especially across many languages.
- Thoughtful critique and improvement of intrinsic evaluation: The paper convincingly identifies shortcomings of QVEC (basis dependence, dimensionality effects) and proposes QVEC-CCA, which is basis-invariant, normalized, and empirically shows markedly better correlation with downstream document classification (r=0.896) and better than alternatives for parsing (r=0.444 for multiQVEC).
- Broad and careful evaluation: The study spans up to 59 languages, uses both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks (document classification in 7 languages, dependency parsing in 12), and discusses coverage effects (reporting coverage and intersection analyses). The observation that word similarity/translation correlate poorly with downstream tasks is important for the community.
- Transparent baselines and design trade-offs: The paper includes both dictionary-based (multiCCA, multiCluster) and parallel-based baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance), clarifies training constraints (e.g., window sizes, SGD epochs), and openly reports computational challenges (translation-invariance window size).
- Community impact: The evaluation portal and packaged resources (trained embeddings, corpora, standardized splits) will improve reproducibility and comparability across future work.

Weaknesses
- Novelty of embedding methods is limited: multiCCA is a direct pivot-based extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014); multiCluster resembles earlier cross-lingual clustering approaches (e.g., Och 1999; Täckström et al. 2012). The technical novelty lies more in engineering choices than in new modeling ideas. This is acceptable if accompanied by strong empirical insight, but the paper should better position its novelty relative to TransGram/BilBOWA-style methods and recent bilingual projection lines.
- Potential leakage and evaluation bias in 59-language setup: For 47 languages the training dictionaries come from Google Translate; word translation evaluation for 59 languages is also constructed using Google Translate. It is unclear whether the eval pairs overlap with the training dictionaries, which can inflate scores. The paper should explicitly ensure disjointness or quantify the overlap and its effect.
- Limited and potentially unbalanced correlation study: The correlation analysis uses 17 systems over 3 languages; this is a small sample, and systems are not independent (e.g., many are variants of similar objectives). Moreover, multiQVEC(-CCA) uses semantic supersenses, while the strongest correlation is with document classification (a semantic task), making the result somewhat tautological; the weaker correlation with parsing (0.273 for multiQVEC-CCA) reflects this. Stronger claims about “better intrinsic metrics” need broader languages, tasks (e.g., POS, NER), and models.
- Theoretical details and robustness of multiCCA mapping: The paper asserts the CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” and uses T^{-1}_{en→m}T_{m→en}E_m; but in practice CCA projections have rank at most the number of dictionary pairs and can be ill-conditioned. Regularization, dimensionality selection, and numerical stability are not discussed. An ablation on dictionary size/quality vs. performance would strengthen the claims.
- Baseline fairness and scope: The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual data, diverging from Coulmance et al. and Guo et al., and underperforms consistently. Including stronger, monolingual+parallel baselines (e.g., BilBOWA/TransGram) or an off-the-shelf bilingual projection with orthogonal Procrustes would provide a more balanced comparison. Also, translation-invariance was constrained by implementation limits (small window), which may handicap it.
- Multilingual coverage vs. quality trade-offs insufficiently analyzed: multiCluster’s performance degrades at 59 languages due to polysemy conflation and large connected components, but the paper does not explore mitigations (e.g., one-to-one dictionary pruning via matching, sense-aware clustering, degree thresholds) or quantify cluster purity and its relationship to performance.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify overlap between training dictionaries and evaluation pairs in the 59-language word translation task when both are derived from Google Translate? If there is overlap, can you re-report translation accuracy on disjoint pairs?
- For multiCCA, what is the effective CCA dimensionality relative to d and dictionary size? Do you use regularization or truncated projections? How stable is the inversion T^{-1}_{en→m} numerically across languages?
- How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary quality/size? Please provide ablations varying dictionary size, noise level, and pivot choice (English vs. other).
- Could you include a stronger baseline that uses both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., BilBOWA/TransGram) and a simple orthogonal Procrustes mapping baseline, to better contextualize multiCCA?
- For parsing, have you tried allowing fine-tuning of embeddings or adding POS features to gauge whether the trends hold when models are closer to practical settings?

Additional comments
- The portal URL and some data references compromise anonymity. Please ensure compliance with double-blind reviewing (use anonymized links).
- Reporting statistical significance for key comparisons (e.g., doc classification, parsing) would help.
- It would be useful to analyze hubness/anisotropy effects in the shared space and whether whitening or post-processing (e.g., mean centering) changes results.
