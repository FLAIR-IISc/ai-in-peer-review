Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies estimation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. It proposes two simple dictionary-based methods: (1) multiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into a shared space via CCA using bilingual dictionaries (pivoting on English), and (2) multiCluster, which forms cross-lingual clusters of translationally equivalent words and trains skip-gram on cluster IDs. It trains embeddings for 59 languages (dictionary-only) and for 12 languages (with parallel corpora for baselines).
- It adapts QVEC to the multilingual setting (multiQVEC) and introduces QVEC-CCA/multiQVEC-CCA to address QVEC’s basis-variance and dimensionality issues. It performs a correlation study between intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing).
- It builds an evaluation portal and releases data/splits, with a focus on reproducibility.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) An evaluation contribution: QVEC-CCA and its multilingual variant, with a correlation analysis showing better alignment with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (e.g., cross-lingual similarity and word translation).
2) A resource/practical contribution: wide-coverage multilingual embeddings (59 languages), standardized dev/test splits, and an evaluation portal.
3) A methodological contribution: a straightforward but effective extension of bilingual CCA to multilingual (multiCCA), and a simple clustering-based approach (multiCluster).

Strengths
- Evaluation methodology and insight: The QVEC-CCA idea is well-motivated (basis-invariance, normalization) and the multilingual extension is useful. The correlation study (Table 1) provides evidence that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation (e.g., 0.896 with document classification for multiQVEC-CCA vs 0.386/0.066 for similarity/translation), offering actionable guidance to the community on intrinsic evaluation choices.
- Scale and reproducibility: Training across 59 languages and providing standardized dev/test splits and an evaluation portal significantly lower the barrier for future work. The paper discusses coverage effects explicitly and reports coverage alongside scores, a valuable and often overlooked detail that improves fairness of comparisons.
- Simple, scalable methods with competitive performance: multiCCA is elegant and easy to apply (requiring only monolingual embeddings and dictionaries) and performs strongly across many metrics, particularly at larger scale (59 languages), where it consistently outperforms multiCluster. For 12 languages, dictionary-based methods are competitive with or superior to parallel-based baselines on several metrics, including both extrinsic tasks (Table 3).
- Clear experimental design choices to isolate embedding utility: For parsing, disabling fine-tuning and other features forces the models to rely directly on pretrained embeddings, clarifying the contribution of the embeddings to downstream performance.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a direct, almost immediate extension of prior bilingual CCA work (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014) by choosing English as a pivot space; multiCluster is also conceptually straightforward. The paper’s main intellectual advance lies more in evaluation/resources than in algorithmic innovation.
- Potentially unfair/limited baselines and incomplete comparisons: The multiSkip implementation excludes monolingual data (unlike some prior variants), likely handicapping it; the translation-invariance baseline has a smaller window due to tooling constraints. The paper acknowledges these issues, but the comparative landscape would be stronger with matched training regimes or additional dictionary-based mapping baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mappings). This complicates claims about superiority of the proposed methods.
- Reliance on Google Translate dictionaries (training and evaluation) for many languages: For 47 languages, the dictionaries are machine-generated (top-20k English words), and Google Translate is also used for evaluation in the 59-language setting. This raises concerns about noise, bias, and potential circularity (train/test contamination in spirit if not in data), and the paper does not quantify dictionary quality or robustness to noise.
- Limited scope and strength of the correlation study: Correlations are computed over 17 embeddings and for three languages (en/da/it). While the directional findings are plausible and useful, the sample is small; effect sizes for parsing are modest (e.g., 0.273 for multiQVEC-CCA), and the paper does not report statistical significance/confidence intervals or robustness across more languages/tasks.
- Multilingual design choices are under-analyzed: The English-pivot approach could induce hubness or typological bias. There is no exploration of alternative pivots, multi-pivot strategies, or joint projections. MultiCluster’s degradation with 59 languages is expected (polysemy amplified by clustering), but there is little ablation/analysis (e.g., effect of cluster size thresholds, dictionary precision/recall τ, or sense-splitting) to understand failure modes.
- Minor but notable issues with anonymity/clarity: The paper includes an IP address for the portal and references to (partly redacted) code, possibly compromising double-blind review. The references and formatting have several inconsistencies.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is multiCCA to dictionary size/quality and to the choice of pivot language? Can you report performance as a function of dictionary coverage/noise, and compare English vs. non-English pivots or multi-pivot approaches?
- For the 59-language experiments, can you quantify the noise in Google Translate dictionaries and analyze its impact (e.g., by perturbing or filtering the dictionaries), and address the use of Google Translate for evaluation (risk of bias)?
- Can you strengthen baselines by including a dictionary-based orthogonal Procrustes mapping, and a multiSkip variant that also leverages monolingual data (as in related work), to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons?
- What dimensionality is used by CCA? You state T matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular”; in practice, CCA often returns reduced-rank projections. Please clarify how you handle rank deficiencies and how this interacts with the inversion in ECCA(m, v).
- Can you provide confidence intervals/significance tests for the reported correlations and extrinsic task differences, and expand the correlation study to more languages/tasks if possible?

Overall, I find the evaluation and resources to be the strongest and most impactful aspects, with methods that are simple and practical but only moderately novel. With clarifications and stronger baselines/analyses, this work would be a solid contribution.
