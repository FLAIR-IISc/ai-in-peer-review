Summary and main contributions
- Primary: Introduction of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA as intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual word embeddings, with a clear theoretical improvement over QVEC (basis invariance and normalization) and empirical evidence that these metrics correlate better with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than commonly used word similarity and word translation evaluations.
- Secondary: A broad empirical study and publicly accessible evaluation suite/portal covering multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, enabling standardized, replicable evaluation across many languages and models.
- Tertiary: Two dictionary-based methods for estimating multilingual embeddings across many languages—multiCCA (a CCA-based projection of monolingual spaces into a shared English space) and multiCluster (shared cluster ID embeddings)—and an empirical comparison against multiSkip and translation-invariance baselines, including training for 59 languages and a focused 12-language setting.

Strengths
- Strong evaluation framing and evidence linking intrinsic to extrinsic performance: The paper systematically compares four intrinsic metrics against two extrinsic tasks and reports Pearson correlations. multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA show substantially higher correlations (e.g., 0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA vs. 0.386 for word similarity on document classification), directly supporting the claim that these metrics are more predictive of downstream utility than traditional similarity/translation tests. This meets a clear community need for better intrinsic metrics.
- Sound improvement over QVEC: The move from dimension-wise alignment to CCA solves QVEC’s basis dependence and unnormalized scoring problems. The definition of QVEC-CCA is concise and technically sound, and the multilingual extension is straightforward via multilingual supersense resources. These properties make the metric both theoretically cleaner and practically comparable across embedding dimensionalities.
- Breadth and scale: The work trains and evaluates embeddings across up to 59 languages and multiple tasks, including extrinsic ones (multilingual document classification and parsing). The 12-language controlled setting enables more equitable comparisons with parallel-data methods. Reporting coverage alongside intrinsic scores addresses a common evaluation pitfall and improves transparency.
- Practical, resource-lean estimation methods: The two dictionary-based methods require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, avoiding reliance on large parallel corpora. The multiCCA pivot-to-English approach is simple, efficient, and empirically strong in many settings; multiCluster is competitive in smaller language sets.
- Community infrastructure: The evaluation portal and standardized datasets (where licensing allows) lower barriers to fair comparison, likely improving reproducibility and comparability of future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in estimation methods: multiCCA is a direct extension of prior bilingual CCA-based alignment (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014) by projecting into English. multiCluster is a straightforward cluster-sharing variant that exacerbates polysemy by tying translationally equivalent words across many languages to a single vector. The empirical study is valuable, but the algorithmic novelty of the embedding methods is modest.
- Potential training/evaluation leakage and bias for 59-language word translation: For 47 languages, Google Translate (GT) is used to build training dictionaries; for the 59-language word translation evaluation, GT-derived dictionaries are also used. This risks circularity and inflates performance or alters relative comparisons in ways not reflective of generalization beyond the GT lexicon. A strict separation or alternative evaluation resource would strengthen claims.
- Baseline fairness and confounds: The multiSkip baseline is trained without monolingual corpora, unlike related prior work, which likely depresses its performance relative to dictionary-based methods that exploit large monolingual data. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to implementation constraints, potentially handicapping it. These discrepancies make some cross-method conclusions less decisive.
- Correlation analysis scope: The correlation study is limited to three languages (en, it, da) and two downstream tasks. While the results are indicative, it remains unclear how robust the correlations are across other languages, domains, and tasks. Notably, correlations with parsing are weaker and mixed (e.g., 0.273 for multiQVEC-CCA), suggesting metric-task dependence that warrants broader validation.
- Methodological details that could be strengthened: The claim that the CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” and the use of T−1en→m,en would benefit from clearer justification (e.g., dimensionality choices, regularization, handling rank-deficiency). The threshold τ for dictionary extraction (0.1) is fixed “based on manual inspection”; sensitivity analysis or validation would increase confidence. The English pivot choice introduces asymmetry and possible hubness/anchor bias; alternatives are not explored.

Questions to authors
- Dictionaries and evaluation: For the 59-language setting, do the GT-derived evaluation dictionaries overlap with GT-derived training dictionaries? If so, can you quantify the overlap and its impact, or provide results using a disjoint evaluation resource to avoid leakage?
- Baseline parity: Why not include a version of multiSkip (or TransGram-like) that also uses monolingual data, to match resource usage and allow a fairer comparison? Similarly, can the translation-invariance baseline be run with comparable window sizes and tuned hyperparameters?
- CCA projections: What dimensionalities are used in CCA projections relative to the original embedding size? How do you ensure invertibility and handle potential rank deficiency/noise (e.g., regularized CCA)? Please clarify numerical stability and implementation details.
- Pivot choice: How sensitive is multiCCA to the choice of pivot language? Have you tried alternative pivots (e.g., high-resource non-English) or joint alignment (e.g., Procrustes to a learned hub) to reduce English-centric bias?
- Dictionary noise and τ: How sensitive are multiCluster and multiCCA to the alignment-derived dictionary threshold τ and to noise in GT dictionaries? Any ablation on dictionary coverage/precision trade-offs?
- Supersense coverage: Given inconsistencies across languages, can you provide additional evidence that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate with non-semantic downstream tasks (e.g., syntax-heavy tasks) or alternative linguistic resources (e.g., universal POS)?
