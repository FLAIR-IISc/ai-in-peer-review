Summary and main contributions
- Empirical evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings: The paper adapts QVEC to the multilingual setting (multiQVEC) and proposes QVEC-CCA/multiQVEC-CCA to address QVEC’s basis-rotation sensitivity and dimensionality dependence. It also studies correlations between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic performance, and releases an evaluation portal and benchmarks.
- Scalable dictionary-based multilingual embeddings: The paper proposes two simple methods—multiCCA (pivoting via CCA to English) and multiCluster (clustering translational equivalents then training skip-gram on cluster IDs)—and trains embeddings for up to 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora.
- Comparative study across multiple metrics and data regimes: It compares the two proposed methods to a skip-gram-based parallel method (multiSkip) and a translation-invariance matrix factorization baseline, across 12- and 59-language settings, multiple intrinsic tasks, and two downstream tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, useful evaluation advances: The move from QVEC to QVEC-CCA resolves real theoretical issues (rotation invariance, comparability across dimensionalities), and the multilingual extensions are natural and valuable. The correlation analysis, though modest in scope, is an important step toward validating intrinsic metrics; notably, multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with extrinsic tasks than word similarity/translation.
- Practical impact and reproducibility: Training 59-language embeddings using only monolingual corpora and dictionaries is a substantial engineering contribution. The evaluation portal, datasets, and trained models lower the barrier to entry and can catalyze future work.
- Simple, effective baselines: MultiCCA is a straightforward, elegant extension of bilingual CCA. The approach leverages high-quality monolingual embeddings and bilingual dictionaries to align into a common space. It performs strongly across many metrics, especially in the 59-language setting, with good coverage.
- Nuanced evaluation and transparency: Reporting coverage alongside intrinsic scores and discussing how coverage can bias results is exemplary. The paper also reports both monolingual and cross-lingual similarity, word translation, QVEC variants, and two downstream tasks, giving a multi-faceted picture.
- Insightful qualitative interpretation: The observation that multiCluster exacerbates polysemy by collapsing translational equivalents is helpful to understand why it degrades when scaling to many languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in embedding methods: MultiCCA is largely a direct extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s bilingual CCA by using English as a pivot; multiCluster is a straightforward clustering-plus-skip-gram pipeline. The primary methodological novelty lies more in evaluation than in estimation.
- Correlation study is underpowered and narrow: The intrinsic–extrinsic correlation analysis uses 17 embedding variants and only three languages (en, it, da), with two downstream tasks. Pearson correlations on such small samples can be unstable; the mixed pattern (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA very high for document classification but low for parsing) suggests task-dependence not fully explored. Confidence intervals/significance are not reported.
- Potential evaluation bias/circularity: For the 59-language setup, Google Translate is used both to construct training dictionaries (for 47 languages) and to build test dictionaries for word translation evaluation. This risks inflating performance of methods that directly exploit the same resource. An evaluation using independent dictionaries or gold lexicons would strengthen claims.
- Baseline fairness and configuration concerns: The multiSkip implementation intentionally omits monolingual data, while cited contemporaneous methods do leverage it. This likely handicaps multiSkip and weakens comparative conclusions. The translation-invariance baseline is trained with a smaller context window due to computational limits, which may also disadvantage it. Ablations to match training budgets/context windows would be fairer.
- Resource and coverage limitations for multiQVEC(-CCA): The multilingual supersense resource is available only for en/da/it, limiting applicability and possibly biasing the correlation study. The paper acknowledges inconsistencies across languages; however, the reliance on supersenses constrains generality. Moreover, QVEC-CCA is defined as a single canonical correlation; summing multiple canonical correlations may provide a more stable signal.
- Insufficient analysis of sensitivity and design choices: Key hyperparameters (e.g., alignment threshold τ=0.1, cluster size limits, CCA regularization) lack sensitivity analysis. The claim that CCA projections are “guaranteed to be non-singular” in practice merits clarification (e.g., dimensionality reduction, regularized CCA, numerical stability).

Questions to authors
- Correlation analysis: Can you report confidence intervals or significance tests for the correlation coefficients in Table 1? How robust are the correlations to (i) using Spearman vs Pearson; (ii) leave-one-condition-out resampling; (iii) adding more languages/tasks?
- QVEC-CCA design: Why use only the first canonical correlation rather than the sum (or average) of the top k canonical correlations? Did you try variants and how did they correlate with downstream tasks?
- Dictionary/evaluation bias: For the 59-language word translation evaluation, how much overlap exists between training and test pairs when both are sourced from Google Translate? Can you replicate the ranking with an independent lexicon for a subset of languages?
- Baseline parity: Can you provide results for a multiSkip variant that also uses monolingual corpora (as in Coulmance/Guo), and for translation-invariance with matched context windows/training budget? This would help isolate algorithmic vs data differences.
- CCA details: Do you use regularized CCA and dimensionality reduction to handle rank deficiencies? How do you ensure invertibility and numerical stability when computing T−1 en→m,en?
- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive are multiCluster and multiCCA to τ, cluster size caps, embedding dimensionality, and normalization? Any evidence that the conclusions hold across settings?

Additional comments
- Please add statistical significance for downstream differences (especially Table 3), or at least multiple runs with variance.
- Consider an ablation where the 59-language training uses a smaller, noisier dictionary to quantify robustness to dictionary noise.
- The delexicalized parsing setup highlights the embeddings’ contribution but is atypical; consider also allowing fine-tuning or adding POS to assess practical end-task utility.
- The portal is a valuable community asset; documenting versioning of datasets/metrics and ensuring long-term availability would increase its impact.
