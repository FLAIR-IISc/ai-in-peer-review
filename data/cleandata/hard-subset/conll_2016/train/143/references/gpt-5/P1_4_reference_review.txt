Summary and main contributions
This paper studies estimation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. It proposes two dictionary-based methods to induce a shared multilingual space across many languages without requiring parallel corpora: (1) multiCCA, which trains monolingual embeddings and aligns each non-English space to English via CCA-based linear projections; and (2) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent word types across languages and trains a single embedding per cluster on concatenated monolingual corpora. The paper also introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, intrinsic evaluation metrics extending QVEC to multiple languages and, via CCA, addressing basis-variance and dimensionality normalization. Finally, the authors present an evaluation portal and report extensive comparisons across up to 59 languages, including extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing), and show that multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates better with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation metrics.

As I see it, the key contributions, in order of strength, are:
1) An evaluation framework (multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA) for multilingual embeddings that correlates better with downstream performance than commonly used intrinsic tasks, plus a suite of tasks and an evaluation portal.
2) A simple, scalable dictionary-based alignment method (multiCCA) that produces competitive multilingual embeddings over 59 languages, often outperforming prior baselines in extrinsic tasks.
3) A careful empirical comparison across many languages and metrics, including analysis of coverage effects, and an accessible resource bundle (data, trained embeddings).

Strengths
1) Evaluation innovation with demonstrated utility: The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA is technically motivated (CCA resolves basis invariance and dimensionality scaling issues in QVEC) and empirically supported. The correlation analysis (Table 1) shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates strongly with multilingual document classification (r=0.896) and multiQVEC correlates better with parsing (r=0.444), while word similarity and word translation correlate poorly. This is a valuable contribution for the community to move beyond entrenched but weakly predictive intrinsic metrics.

2) Scale and practicality: Training and evaluating embeddings over 59 languages with only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries (many extracted automatically) addresses a realistic low-resource constraint. The resource-light multiCCA approach is appealing in settings where parallel corpora are unavailable. The results indicate multiCCA is consistently better than multiCluster at 59 languages across most metrics, suggesting robustness and scalability.

3) Thorough experimental setup and transparency around coverage: The paper reports both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, details training setups, and explicitly tracks coverage—an often-overlooked confound in intrinsic evaluations. Reporting coverage and discussing intersection-based evaluation helps interpret results more fairly.

4) Useful resources: The evaluation portal, code, trained embeddings, and compiled datasets (where licenses permit) can significantly lower the barrier to entry for future research, fostering comparability and reproducibility.

5) Simple, effective baseline (multiCCA): The extension of bilingual CCA to a multilingual space via a pivot (English) is elegant and competitive. It is easy to implement, easy to scale, and empirically strong in many settings.

Weaknesses
1) Correlation study scope and potential confounds: The correlation analysis uses only 17 embedding variants across 3 languages and 2 extrinsic tasks. While trends are suggestive, the sample is small, task diversity limited, and confidence intervals absent. Moreover, embeddings for extrinsic tasks are frozen and models are delexicalized, which isolates the embedding signal but may not reflect typical downstream usage (where fine-tuning or POS features are included). The strong correlation claims should be tempered or further substantiated.

2) Baseline fairness and strength: The multiSkip baseline omits monolingual data, unlike prior art it is derived from, likely handicapping it relative to dictionary-based approaches that exploit large monolingual corpora. The translation-invariance method is trained with a small context window (due to tooling constraints), potentially weakening it. These choices create an uneven comparison; a stronger suite of baselines would bolster claims.

3) Dictionary and evaluation circularity risks: For 47 languages, training dictionaries come from Google Translate applied to the top 20k English words; evaluation for the 59-language setting also uses Google Translate for word translation. This risks overlap or systematic bias toward dictionary-aligned methods and may inflate performance on translation-style metrics. Clear separation of training vs. evaluation lexicons and an analysis of dictionary noise sensitivity would strengthen credibility.

4) MultiCluster’s semantic conflation: While acknowledged, the one-vector-per-cluster design exacerbates polysemy across 59 languages and underperforms substantially. More analysis on when multiCluster helps (e.g., closely related languages, smaller language sets) and whether subword/contextual signals could mitigate conflation would add insight.

5) Anonymity and reproducibility issues: The paper contains a non-anonymized URL early on, then later states URLs are redacted. References also show formatting problems. Additionally, RCV licensing prevents full replication of document classification results. While common, these issues detract from double-blindness and full reproducibility.

Questions to authors
1) Correlation robustness: Can you report confidence intervals or significance testing for the correlations in Table 1? How stable are these correlations across different random seeds, subsets of languages, or additional downstream tasks?

2) Train/eval dictionary split: For word translation evaluations, how did you ensure no overlap with training dictionaries? If overlap exists, can you provide results with strict separation?

3) Baseline strengthening: Can you include a bilingual/multilingual baseline that leverages both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., a version of multiSkip augmented with monolingual corpora) to better match the data used by the proposed methods?

4) Pivot choice and joint alignment: You project all languages into the English space. How sensitive are results to the pivot language? Have you tried a joint (multi-way) CCA or mapping to an averaged/common latent space rather than chaining through English?

5) Dictionary noise analysis: For languages where dictionaries were auto-generated, what is the estimated noise level? Can you quantify multiCCA’s performance as a function of dictionary size/quality?

6) Coverage-normalized intrinsic scores: In addition to reporting coverage, can you provide intrinsic results on the intersection vocabulary for key metrics in the main text (not only supplementary), to facilitate fairer comparisons?

Overall, this is a solid paper with an impactful evaluation contribution (multiQVEC-CCA), a practical multilingual embedding method (multiCCA), and broad empirical evidence. Addressing the fairness and robustness concerns would significantly strengthen the work. I lean positive.
