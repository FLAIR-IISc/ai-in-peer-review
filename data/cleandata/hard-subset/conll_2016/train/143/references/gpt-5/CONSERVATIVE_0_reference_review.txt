Summary and contributions
- Primary: An evaluation contribution. The paper adapts QVEC to the multilingual setting and proposes QVEC-CCA/multiQVEC-CCA to address basis-variance and normalization issues of QVEC, and empirically studies correlations between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks.
- Secondary: Two dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation methods: a straightforward CCA pivoting into English space (multiCCA) and a clustering approach that ties translation equivalents (multiCluster). The methods are scaled to 59 languages given bilingual dictionaries.
- Tertiary: A web evaluation portal that packages datasets and metrics for multilingual embedding evaluation.

Strengths
- Evaluation focus and evidence on metric-task correlation. The correlation study, despite being limited in scope, is valuable: traditional intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity and word translation) show weak/negative correlations with downstream tasks, while multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better (e.g., 0.896 with document classification; 0.444 with parsing for multiQVEC). Highlighting this misalignment is practically important for the community.
- Practical, competitive baselines from simple methods. The multiCCA pivot (an extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s CCA) is a simple, established technique and nonetheless performs competitively, particularly at larger multilingual scales (authors report it consistently surpasses multiCluster on 59 languages). That a basic linear alignment baseline remains strong is a useful finding for practitioners.
- Breadth of evaluation. The paper evaluates on multiple intrinsic metrics (monolingual and cross-lingual similarity, translation, QVEC variants) and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual doc classification and parsing). Reporting coverage and acknowledging its confounding effect is responsible and improves interpretability.
- Resource contribution. The portal and collected datasets (to the extent they can be shared) can help standardize future comparisons and lower barriers to replication. This is a community service.

Weaknesses
- Limited rigor and scope in correlation claims. The correlation analysis is based on 17 embedding configurations over 3 languages (en/da/it). There is no uncertainty reporting (CIs), no robustness checks (across seeds/hyperparameters), and no extension beyond these languages or tasks. The striking 0.896 correlation for multiQVEC-CCA with document classification raises concerns about overfitting to the small design space. It is unclear if the correlations hold for other language families, tasks (e.g., NER, QA), or larger embedding/model variations.
- Potential circularity and data quality concerns in 59-language experiments. For 47 languages, Google Translate is used to build bilingual dictionaries for training; for evaluation on word translation, Google Translate pairs are used again. This risks evaluation inflation and data leakage. Moreover, GT dictionary quality varies by language and domain; no noise analysis is provided. The lack of independent gold lexicons for most languages weakens the 59-language claims.
- Baseline fairness and comparability issues. The multiSkip baseline is implemented without monolingual data (unlike prior work), handicapping it in a setting where monolingual corpora are abundant. The translation-invariance method is constrained to a smaller context window due to tooling limits. Hyperparameters are not consistently matched across methods (e.g., window sizes), and coverage differences complicate intrinsic comparisons despite the authors’ caveats. A stronger bilingual/multilingual baseline that uses both monolingual and parallel data (e.g., TransGram/Joint methods) is missing.
- Limited extrinsic setup and ecological validity. For parsing, POS/morph features are removed and embeddings are frozen, which isolates the embedding effect but does not reflect modern practice. The experiments are multilingual but not cross-lingual transfer (train on one/few languages, test on unseen languages), which is a central use case for multilingual embeddings. The doc classification setup similarly aggregates training across languages rather than testing zero-shot transfer.
- Methodological novelty is modest. MultiCCA is a direct extension of bilingual CCA using English as a pivot; multiCluster is a straightforward cluster-then-embed approach known to exacerbate polysemy. The paper’s empirical findings are more compelling than the methodological innovations. Given CoNLL standards, stronger novelty or deeper analysis would be expected.

Questions to authors
- Correlation analysis:
  - Please report confidence intervals and significance for the correlation coefficients. How sensitive are the results to the set of embeddings, random seeds, and hyperparameters?
  - Can you replicate the correlation trends across additional languages and tasks (e.g., NER, POS, XNLI), and with a larger number of embedding variants?
  - Did you control for coverage in extrinsic tasks (e.g., OOV handling) when computing correlations?

- Data and training details:
  - What monolingual corpora were used per language (size, source), and how were they standardized across methods?
  - For the 59-language setup, can you provide an evaluation that does not rely on Google Translate for both training dictionaries and test pairs (e.g., Wiktionary subsets, PanLex, or human-labeled samples)?
  - How sensitive are multiCluster/multiCCA to the dictionary extraction threshold τ and to cluster size caps? Any ablations?

- Baselines:
  - Can you include a variant of multiSkip (or another joint model) that uses both monolingual and parallel data, to match prior art?
  - Can you harmonize window sizes and other hyperparameters across methods, or provide ablations to quantify their effect?

- Extrinsic tasks:
  - Can you add zero-shot transfer experiments (train on source language(s), test on target without labels) for both parsing and classification?
  - For parsing, can you report results with standard features and fine-tuned embeddings to assess practical impact alongside the controlled setting?

Overall, this paper’s main value lies in its evaluation contributions and broad, if imperfect, empirical sweep. The methodological proposals are incremental, and several experimental choices limit the strength of the claims. Strengthening baseline fairness, broadening and de-biasing the correlation analysis, and adding true cross-lingual transfer experiments would significantly improve the paper.
