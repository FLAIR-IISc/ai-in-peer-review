Summary and claimed contributions
The paper introduces two dictionary-based methods for learning massively multilingual word embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel corpora, proposes multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and releases an evaluation portal. Experiments cover up to 59 languages, investigate correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, and compare against multiSkip and translation-invariance baselines.

Contributions as assessed (in order of strength):
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA, a theoretically motivated intrinsic metric addressing QVEC’s lack of basis invariance and normalization, and an empirical correlation study showing higher correlations with downstream tasks than standard word similarity/translation metrics.
2) Large-scale multilingual training with lightweight supervision: empirically demonstrating dictionary-based embeddings across 59 languages, with competitive downstream performance relative to parallel-based baselines.
3) Practical infrastructure: an evaluation portal and packaged datasets supporting standardized, replicable assessment.

Strengths
- Conceptually sound improvement to intrinsic evaluation. The paper clearly identifies two limitations of QVEC (basis-dependence and dimensionality bias) and addresses both via CCA. The resulting multiQVEC-CCA produces bounded, comparable scores and is invariant to linear transformations. The correlation study indicates substantially higher correlation with document classification (0.896) and better or comparable correlation with parsing versus standard intrinsic metrics, providing evidence that the proposed metric is more predictive of downstream utility.

- Broad and careful evaluation. The work spans nine metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic), reports vocabulary coverage alongside scores, and discusses the confounding effects of differential coverage. The extrinsic setups deliberately remove other features to isolate the contribution of embeddings, which strengthens causal interpretation of differences among methods.

- Scalable, resource-efficient multilingual methods. Both multiCCA and multiCluster require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, enabling coverage of 59 languages. This is practically important for low-resource settings and is demonstrated in experiments that show multiCCA consistently outperforming multiCluster in the 59-language setup.

- Reproducibility and facilitation of future work. The provision of an evaluation portal, downloadable training/evaluation data (with noted licensing exceptions), and implementations for multiple methods lowers barriers for standardized comparisons and replication.

- Clear presentation of methods and experimental design. The paper defines the embedding objective, the construction of clusters, the CCA extension to a multilingual pivot space, and the evaluation metrics with sufficient mathematical detail to understand and reimplement.

Weaknesses
- Limited algorithmic novelty. MultiCCA is a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA by projecting all languages into the English space; multiCluster is a simple connected-component clustering over dictionary graphs followed by monolingual training on cluster IDs. While practically useful, the methodological innovations are incremental relative to prior bilingual embedding work.

- Potentially unfair baseline configurations. The multiSkip baseline is trained only on parallel data (omitting monolingual corpora used by related methods), which likely handicaps it. The translation-invariance baseline is restricted to a smaller context window due to computational constraints. These choices may depress baseline performance, complicating claims of superiority.

- Supervision and evaluation biases for 59 languages. For 47 languages, training dictionaries come from Google Translate; word translation evaluation for the 59-language condition also uses Google Translate. This risks evaluation bias toward the supervision source, and conflates embedding quality with MT dictionary quality. No analysis quantifies dictionary noise or its effect on results.

- Correlation study scope and statistical support. The correlation results are based on three languages and 17 models (102 points). While indicative, the sample is small, the tasks are limited (one classification, one parsing), and no statistical significance or confidence intervals over correlations are reported. The very high correlation (0.896) for document classification could be sensitive to this setup.

- Technical details and stability concerns. The paper states that CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” and uses T_en→m,en^{-1} in multiCCA. In practice, numerical stability and near-singularity can arise, especially with high dimensions and noisy dictionaries. The approach to regularization, dimensionality selection for CCA, and conditioning is not described. For multiCluster, cluster size capping is mentioned but not detailed (e.g., how merges are constrained), and the impact of long chaining via dictionary edges on semantic drift is not quantified.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- MultiCCA stability: What dimensionality is used for CCA projections relative to the original embedding dimension? Are projections regularized or reduced rank? How is invertibility ensured in practice, and what are the condition numbers observed? Did you compare using only forward projections into English space (without matrix inversion) versus the T^{-1} construction?

- Baselines: Can you provide multiSkip results with monolingual data (as in related works) and translation-invariance with a larger window or a more efficient implementation, to control for configuration disadvantages? If not, can you quantify sensitivity of these baselines to window size and monolingual data on a smaller subset?

- Dictionary quality: For the 59-language setting, do you have estimates of precision/recall or noise rates for Google Translate dictionaries? How sensitive are multiCluster/multiCCA to dictionary noise (e.g., via ablations varying τ, or injecting noise)?

- Correlation analysis: Can you report confidence intervals or significance tests for the reported correlations, and replicate the analysis on additional languages or tasks to test robustness?

- MultiCluster details: How is the maximum cluster size enforced when connected components exceed the cap? Are hubs or long chains split, and according to what heuristic? What proportion of tokens fall into very large clusters, and how does that affect performance?

- Pivot choice: Did you experiment with non-English pivots or joint multilingual CCA (e.g., Procrustes alignment via a shared latent space) to reduce pivot-language bias?
