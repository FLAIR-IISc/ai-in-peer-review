Summary and key contributions
- A new intrinsic evaluation metric for multilingual embeddings, multiQVEC-CCA, which fixes theoretical issues of QVEC (basis dependence and lack of normalization) via CCA and extends it to multilingual settings. The paper also introduces multiQVEC, a multilingual variant of QVEC.
- A standardized, easy-to-use evaluation suite and web portal that aggregates data and scripts for intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation of multilingual embeddings, encouraging replication and comparability.
- Two simple dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual embeddings (multiCCA as a pivot-CCA extension; multiCluster as dictionary-induced cluster sharing), and large-scale training over 59 languages without requiring parallel corpora, plus a broad empirical comparison including downstream parsing and document classification.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty in evaluation: multiQVEC-CCA is a clear, principled improvement over QVEC (basis invariance, normalized scores), and the paper provides an “evaluation of evaluation” showing substantially stronger correlations with downstream tasks than widely used word similarity/translation metrics. This is a valuable direction-setting contribution for the community.
- Community impact via infrastructure: the evaluation portal and curated benchmarks (with development/test splits) will lower barriers to entry and improve rigor and comparability in multilingual embedding research. This is high-value, even if methods evolve.
- Pragmatic scalability: training embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries is useful, especially for low-resource settings where parallel data is sparse. The empirical breadth (59 languages; and 12-language parallel setting) is commendable.
- Thorough, multi-faceted evaluation: the paper crosses intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, reports coverage, and discusses confounds (e.g., coverage effects), which strengthens the credibility of findings. The decision to “stress-test” embeddings by removing POS/morphology in parsing is a thoughtful design to isolate embedding quality.
- Clarity and simplicity: multiCCA is an elegant, minimal extension of an existing bilingual technique into a practical multilingual pipeline (pivoting into English), and multiCluster offers a straightforward baseline that is competitive in some settings. The empirical finding that dictionary-based methods beat a parallel-only skipgram baseline on several metrics is informative.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is limited: multiCCA is essentially pivot-based bilingual CCA; multiCluster is a simple cluster-sharing strategy that amplifies polysemy. The most substantial novelty resides in evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA) and infrastructure more than in embedding estimation methods themselves.
- Potential evaluation leakage/noise: for the 59-language condition, Google Translate is used both to construct training dictionaries and to evaluate translation accuracy. This risks circularity and overestimation, and translation quality for many low-resource languages is unknown. Even with coverage reporting, this could bias conclusions.
- Baseline parity and fairness: the multiSkip implementation uses only parallel data (unlike related work that combines monolingual+parallel), likely handicapping this baseline. The translation-invariance baseline is used with a smaller context window for computational reasons, which may also disadvantage it. This makes some rankings harder to interpret.
- Limited scope and size of the correlation study: correlations are computed over 17 embedding variants and only three languages (en/it/da). While trends are promising, the sample size and language diversity are limited for strong generalization claims. Confidence intervals/significance are not reported.
- Multilingual linguistic resource dependence: multiQVEC(-CCA) depends on supersense tag resources that exist for few languages and are not fully consistent cross-linguistically. This limits the immediate applicability of the proposed intrinsic metrics to the broader 59-language setting (acknowledged by the authors).

Questions to authors
- On potential leakage: For the 59-language setting, can you quantify the overlap between Google Translate dictionary entries used in training and those used for evaluation? Could you provide results using disjoint splits (or alternative evaluation sources) to deconfound?
- Correlation analysis: Please report the exact N used for each correlation, confidence intervals, and p-values. Did you also check Spearman correlations? How sensitive are the results to the choice of languages/embeddings in the 17-model pool?
- Resource dependence: Beyond supersenses, can multiQVEC(-CCA) be instantiated with multilingual resources that scale better (e.g., Universal POS, UD features, or multilingual lexicons), and if so, what are the observed correlations with downstream tasks?
- Pivot and linearity assumptions: How sensitive is multiCCA to the pivot choice (English) and to linearity? Did you attempt multi-view CCA or joint projections to a shared latent space that do not privilege a single language?
- Fairness of baselines: Can you provide results for multiSkip (or similar) trained with both monolingual and parallel data, matching the training signal used by your methods? Likewise, is it feasible to re-run the translation-invariance baseline with comparable context settings?
- Noise analysis: For the 59-language dictionaries derived from MT, can you report dictionary precision/recall estimates (perhaps via manual checks for a subset) and analyze how noise impacts multiCCA vs multiCluster?
- Coverage effects: For key intrinsic metrics with large coverage disparities, could you add intersection-vocabulary evaluations in the main text (not only supplementary) to help readers gauge robustness?
- Reproducibility and anonymity: The paper references a public IP for the portal, which may compromise anonymity. Will code, data, and trained models be released post-acceptance under stable URLs, and can you ensure anonymized access during review?

Overall, I find the evaluation contributions (multiQVEC-CCA and the public portal) novel and impactful for the field, and I’m willing to overlook the modest methodological novelty of the embedding methods. I lean toward acceptance based on the bold and useful reframing of how we evaluate multilingual embeddings and the practical resources provided.
