Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: (a) multiCCA, a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s bilingual CCA that maps each non-English space into English; and (b) multiCluster, which builds cross-lingual clusters via dictionaries and trains one embedding per cluster using monolingual corpora.
- It introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation measures extending QVEC; QVEC-CCA addresses basis-variance and normalization issues. The authors show higher correlation with two downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- It conducts an empirical study across multiple metrics and tasks, trains embeddings for 59 languages (largely via automatically-derived dictionaries), and releases an evaluation portal to facilitate standardized evaluation.

Main contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) An evaluation contribution: multiQVEC-CCA and the correlation study suggesting it is a stronger intrinsic proxy than word similarity or word translation. The portal and data packaging further support evaluation reproducibility.
2) A simple, scalable dictionary-based multilingual mapping (multiCCA) demonstrating competitive performance and better coverage when extended to many languages.
3) A large multilingual embedding resource (59 languages) trained with light supervision.

Strengths
- Evaluation rigor and transparency: The paper evaluates with both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, reports coverage, and openly discusses the pitfalls of intrinsic evaluation with differing vocabularies. The correlation analysis, while limited (see below), is a useful step toward grounding intrinsic metrics in downstream relevance.
- Methodical improvement to QVEC: QVEC-CCA directly addresses two known issues (basis dependence and dimensionality scaling) in QVEC. Empirically, multiQVEC(-CCA) correlate better with downstream document classification and parsing than word similarity or word translation. This contributes to the community’s evaluation methodology.
- Practical scalability and simplicity: The multiCCA approach is computationally light, easy to implement, and scales to many languages by choosing a pivot (English). Results show it is competitive or superior in several settings, especially at larger scale, and attains strong coverage.
- Resource and tooling contribution: Training on 59 languages and providing an evaluation portal (with data, metrics, and upload functionality) should lower the barrier for future comparisons and promote replicability.
- Clear discussion of design tradeoffs: The paper acknowledges clustering-induced ambiguity in multiCluster and provides coverage-normalized comparisons, which improves interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and technical depth: multiCCA is a direct and expected multilingual extension of a known bilingual CCA approach; multiCluster is a straightforward cluster-and-embed pipeline. There is limited algorithmic novelty beyond composition and scaling choices.
- Questionable supervision quality for the 59-language setting: Many training/evaluation dictionaries are derived from Google Translate (top-20k English words) or fast_align with a fixed τ=0.1 based on manual inspection. The paper does not quantify dictionary noise, nor analyze sensitivity to dictionary quality/size. Using machine translations both for training and evaluation risks bias and data contamination; it also complicates interpretation of the 59-language intrinsic results.
- Baseline fairness and strength: The multiSkip implementation excludes monolingual data while other methods benefit from abundant monolingual corpora; translation-invariance is constrained to a smaller context window due to computational limits. These choices likely handicap the baselines. Stronger mapping baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mappings) and versions of multiSkip that leverage monolingual data would make comparisons more convincing.
- Limited extrinsic diversity and scope: Despite the “massively multilingual” claim, extrinsic evaluations cover only European languages (RCV7 and UD v1.1 subsets). No extrinsic results are reported for the 59-language setting, which undermines claims of broad multilingual utility, especially for non-Indo-European and morphologically rich languages.
- Correlation study limitations: Correlations are computed over 17 embeddings with two downstream tasks in three languages; no statistical significance analysis, CIs, or robustness checks are provided. multiQVEC(-CCA) relies on supersense resources available only for a few languages and acknowledged as inconsistent, which weakens the generality of the proposed metrics as “multilingual” evaluation tools.
- Sparse analysis and ablations: Key choices (e.g., dictionary threshold τ, cluster size caps, use of connected components potentially causing chaining/hubness, choice of English as pivot) are not systematically ablated. Error analyses and sensitivity to dictionary noise, corpus size, or projection quality are lacking.

Questions to authors
1) How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary size and noise? Please provide ablations varying dictionary size, noise level (e.g., injected noise), and τ, with effects on both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
2) How do results change if multiSkip is given access to monolingual corpora (as in TransGram/Guo et al.) or if you include an orthogonal Procrustes mapping baseline? This would address fairness and strengthen conclusions about relative merit.
3) For the 59-language setting, can you quantify Google Translate dictionary quality (precision/recall against a gold subset) and discuss train/eval contamination risks when Google is used for both training and evaluation? Any intrinsic results using independently curated gold dictionaries for a subset?
4) Can you report statistical significance or confidence intervals for the reported correlations and downstream differences? Are the correlations stable across different language subsets and additional downstream tasks?
5) Does selecting English as the pivot systematically disadvantage distant languages or different scripts? Have you tried alternative pivots or a joint CCA over multiple languages to reduce pivot bias?
6) For multiCluster, how often do connected components create large, semantically heterogeneous clusters across many languages? Can you provide statistics on cluster sizes and an analysis linking cluster size/heterogeneity to performance drops?

Additional comments (minor)
- Some references and anonymization details (portal URL) appear to risk double-blindness; please ensure compliance.
- Clarify coverage computation and OOV handling in extrinsic tasks.
