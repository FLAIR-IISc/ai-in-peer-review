Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies multilingual word embeddings and contributes along three axes: (1) two dictionary-based methods to learn shared embeddings for many languages (multiCCA and multiCluster), trained with monolingual data and pairwise bilingual dictionaries; (2) intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings, extending QVEC with a CCA-based variant (multiQVEC-CCA) and showing higher correlation with extrinsic tasks than popular alternatives; and (3) a public evaluation portal with datasets and scripts to facilitate standardized benchmarking. Experiments span up to 59 languages and compare against parallel-corpus baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance) on intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

Main contributions (as I see them), ordered by significance
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA and the empirical correlation study showing it correlates better with downstream tasks than word similarity and word translation. This addresses a pressing need for more meaningful intrinsic evaluation.
2) Broad multilingual experiments: training and evaluating multilingual embeddings for up to 59 languages with practical, low-resource methods, and quantifying coverage effects.
3) The evaluation portal and packaged resources that can standardize and accelerate research (though ancillary to the core scientific contributions).

Strengths
- Clear advance in evaluation: The CCA-based intrinsic metric (multiQVEC-CCA) fixes two known issues of QVEC (basis variance and dimensionality dependence) and, in a controlled comparison across 17 embedding variants, correlates strongly with document classification (r=0.896) and better than word similarity/translation on both extrinsic tasks. This is valuable for the community, which has lacked intrinsic metrics that reliably predict downstream utility.
- Practical multilingual scope: Methods are designed to work with monolingual corpora and dictionaries, enabling training for 59 languages. This is compelling for low-resource scenarios where parallel corpora are scarce, and the paper reports consistent improvements of multiCCA over multiCluster across many metrics in the 59-language setup.
- Thoughtful experimental design details: The paper explicitly reports vocabulary coverage and discusses how differing coverage can bias intrinsic evaluations, repeating some analyses on vocabulary intersections. Extrinsic tasks (cross-lingual document classification and dependency parsing) are set up to isolate embeddings’ contribution by minimizing other features and freezing embeddings.
- Comparative baselines including parallel-only methods: The inclusion of translation-invariance and a skipgram-based bilingual extension provides a range of contrasting approaches (dictionary-only vs. parallel-only vs. hybrid).
- Reproducibility support: The portal, datasets (where licensing permits), and code help ensure replicability and future benchmarking.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), anchoring in English and applying CCA projections; multiCluster is effectively clustering via dictionary graph components and tying embeddings. The technical novelty is modest relative to prior bilingual embedding alignment and clustering work.
- Potentially unfair or weak baselines: The multiSkip implementation explicitly omits monolingual data, handicapping its performance relative to published variants that leverage both mono- and parallel data. The translation-invariance baseline is constrained by a small window (due to MATLAB implementation), which may depress its results. These choices make it hard to draw strong conclusions about relative merits.
- Evaluation data quality and leakage risks: For 47 languages, training dictionaries come from Google Translate; for the 59-language evaluation, en–xx word translation also uses Google Translate. This risks train–test contamination and biases the word translation evaluation toward dictionary-based methods. Similarly, for the 12-language setup, bilingual dictionaries are extracted from the same parallel data used for baselines, which may introduce coupling if not carefully split.
- Correlation study is small and narrow: The intrinsic–extrinsic correlation analysis relies on 17 embeddings across three languages and two downstream tasks. While results are promising, the sample is limited; correlations (especially the very high r=0.896) may not generalize across more languages/tasks. Moreover, multiQVEC-CCA correlates poorly with parsing (r=0.273), so the claim of broad predictive power should be tempered.
- Anonymity and clarity issues: The paper includes a live IP address for the portal and only later redacts some URLs, which violates double-blind policy. Some experimental details are underspecified (e.g., monolingual corpus sources/sizes and preprocessing/tokenization per language; exact CCA settings/regularization; sensitivity to dictionary threshold τ and cluster size caps).

Questions to authors
- How do you prevent train–test leakage in word translation evaluations when both training and evaluation dictionaries come from Google Translate (59 languages)? Were the evaluation pairs disjoint from training dictionaries?
- For multiCCA, what CCA implementation and regularization were used? How are OOV dictionary words handled in computing projections? Did you try shrinkage/PLS or orthogonal Procrustes alignment, and how do results compare?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of pivot language (English)? If you pivot via a high-resource non-English language or learn a shared latent space via multiway CCA, do results change?
- Can you provide details of monolingual corpora per language (size, domain, tokenization, normalization)? How do corpus size disparities affect performance?
- For multiCluster, how does the maximum cluster size and τ threshold affect performance and semantic conflation? Any analysis of cluster purity?

Overall assessment
This paper makes a useful contribution to evaluation methodology and offers a practical, scalable recipe for multilingual embeddings. However, the methodological novelty on the embedding side is limited, some baselines are handicapped, and parts of the evaluation risk leakage or lack detail. I lean toward a borderline accept if the venue values shared evaluation resources and improved intrinsic metrics; otherwise, a weak reject based on novelty and experimental rigor concerns.
