Summary and main contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and their evaluation. It proposes two simple, dictionary-based approaches to induce shared embedding spaces across many languages: (i) multiCCA, which projects independently trained monolingual embeddings into an English space using CCA; and (ii) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent word types across languages and trains skipgram on sequences of cluster IDs. The paper further introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics that align embeddings to manually crafted linguistic property matrices and, in the CCA variant, address QVEC’s basis-dependence and dimensionality confound. Extensive experiments compare the proposed approaches to parallel-corpus baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), including two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing). The authors also release an evaluation portal with datasets and scripts.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation framework: multiQVEC-CCA (and multiQVEC), plus a correlation study showing substantially better correlation with downstream tasks than common intrinsic metrics; and an open portal for standardized, reproducible evaluation.
2) Scalable multilingual embedding estimation via a simple CCA-based projection into English and a clustering-based approach, applied to 59 languages with broad empirical comparison.
3) Analysis of coverage effects and a careful discussion of intrinsic vs. extrinsic evaluation.

Strengths
- Strong evaluation contribution with demonstrated utility. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA addresses two known QVEC shortcomings (basis dependence and dimensionality sensitivity) in a principled way via CCA. The correlation analysis (17 systems across 2 extrinsic tasks) shows multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better than cross-lingual similarity and word translation (e.g., 0.896 correlation with document classification), providing actionable guidance for the community.
- Broad multilingual scope and reproducibility. Training embeddings for 59 languages and providing a public evaluation portal (with datasets, scripts, and upload functionality) is valuable infrastructure that will likely outlast any single modeling idea. Reporting coverage and re-evaluating on vocabulary intersections shows welcome care in experimental methodology.
- Simple, scalable methods with competitive results. multiCCA is a straightforward, effective extension of bilingual CCA to many languages via an English pivot. It consistently achieves strong coverage and competitive performance, notably outperforming multiCluster at 59 languages and matching or exceeding parallel-based baselines in several settings, including both extrinsic tasks at 12 languages where dictionary-based methods often win.
- Thoughtful experimental design. The mix of intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, coverage analysis, and discussion of the ambiguity introduced by clustering translational equivalents indicate a mature understanding of pitfalls in embedding evaluation. Freezing embeddings and removing POS/morphology in parsing isolates embedding quality.
- Clarity and accessibility. The paper explains methods cleanly, states goals and evaluation desiderata, and reports enough hyperparameters to facilitate replication. The discussion of limitations of conventional intrinsic metrics is persuasive.

Weaknesses
- Baseline fairness and strength. The multiSkip implementation does not use monolingual data, unlike closely related work, which materially disadvantages it. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to computational constraints, which may depress its performance. These choices complicate claims about relative strength.
- Potential train–test bias in 59-language evaluations. For 47 languages, Google Translate dictionaries are used both to train (for multiCCA/multiCluster) and to evaluate word translation, raising concerns about circularity and optimistic results on the translation metric. The paper partially mitigates this for the 12-language subset (Wiktionary), but the broader claims for 59 languages may be inflated on dictionary-based metrics.
- Limited novelty in estimation methods. multiCCA is a direct, intuitive extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to a multilingual setting via a single pivot; multiCluster resembles prior cross-lingual clustering approaches. Their practical value is clear, but the conceptual novelty is modest relative to prior art.
- Scope of correlation study. Correlations are computed over 17 systems in 3 languages (en/it/da) and 2 downstream tasks. While the trends are strong, the sample size, language coverage, and task diversity are limited. No statistical significance (e.g., confidence intervals/p-values) is reported for the correlations. It would help to assess robustness across random seeds and additional tasks/languages.
- Reliance on limited manually annotated linguistic resources. multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA require supersense annotations in only three languages; the authors note inconsistencies and availability issues. This limits immediate applicability and introduces potential bias in multilingual evaluation to languages with such resources.

Questions to authors
- Correlation robustness: How sensitive are the correlation results to random initialization, different monolingual corpora, and alternative downstream tasks (e.g., NER/POS or zero-shot transfer)? Can you report confidence intervals or significance tests for the reported correlation coefficients?
- Dictionary effects: For the 59-language setup, can you quantify how often the evaluation dictionaries overlap with training dictionaries and whether results change using independent evaluation dictionaries (or subsets disjoint from training pairs)?
- Noise sensitivity: How does performance of multiCCA degrade with dictionary noise and size? Any ablations varying dictionary coverage/quality or the threshold τ for dictionary extraction?
- Pivot choice: Why use English as the sole pivot? Have you tried averaging projections from multiple pivot languages or a joint CCA to reduce hubness/anisotropy and potential English-specific biases?
- Baselines: Can you strengthen multiSkip with monolingual corpora (as in prior work) and run translation-invariance with a matched window size on a subset to better isolate architectural differences?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept, leaning accept. The evaluation contributions (multiQVEC-CCA and the portal) and the careful empirical study are impactful and of clear community value, even if the modeling novelty is modest and some baselines could be stronger. With clarifications and minor additional experiments, this would be a solid contribution.
