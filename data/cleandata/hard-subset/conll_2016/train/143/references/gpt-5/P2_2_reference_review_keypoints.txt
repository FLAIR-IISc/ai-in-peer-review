- Proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA as intrinsic metrics that better correlate with downstream tasks than standard word similarity/translation metrics; QVEC-CCA fixes basis-invariance and normalization issues.
- Presents two simple, dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual embeddings: multiCCA (project all languages into English) and multiCluster (cluster dictionary-linked words and train skip-gram on cluster IDs), training up to 59 languages.
- Provides an evaluation portal with datasets, code, and intrinsic/extrinsic metrics to standardize future work.

- Empirical correlation analysis shows standard intrinsic metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks while multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better (e.g., 0.896 with document classification).
- Broad evaluation across many languages (59) and downstream tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) with thoughtful reporting of coverage.
- multiCCA is a simple, scalable, practical method requiring only monolingual embeddings plus dictionaries, useful for low-resource settings.
- Strong reproducibility and community impact via released portal, code, and benchmarks (with some licensing caveats).

- Limited methodological novelty: methods are pragmatic extensions of existing ideas rather than new algorithms.
- Baseline comparisons are unfair/weak (multiSkip limited to parallel data; absent stronger, resource-matched baselines like TransGram/BilBOWA).
- Bilingual dictionaries (mostly from Google Translate) introduce unknown noise/bias (multiword translations dropped, identical surface forms ignored).
- Correlation study scope is limited (17 embeddings, only en/it/da; parsing correlation weak) and lacks confidence intervals or significance testing.
- Coverage differences still confound intrinsic comparisons; more intersection/stratified analyses needed.

- Request to include stronger, resource-matched baselines that use both monolingual and parallel data.
- Ask for sensitivity analysis of multiCCA to pivot language choice and dictionary quality (robustness tests).
- Request quantification of noise in Google Translate dictionaries and its effect on results.
- Ask for confidence intervals/statistical tests for correlation coefficients and downstream metrics and stability checks across seeds/splits.
- Ask whether the portal will host standardized bilingual dictionaries or scripts to regenerate them to address licensing/accessibility.

- Originality: Moderate — most original contribution is the QVEC-CCA evaluation metric; methods are extensions of prior work.
- Quality/Soundness: Generally solid experiments, but comparative limitations and limited robustness analyses.
- Clarity: Clear and well organized with good discussion of coverage caveats.
- Significance: High for evaluation practice and resources; moderate for the embedding methods.
- Resources/Reproducibility: Strong overall, though Reuters licensing limits full replication.

- Overall recommendation: Strong evaluation/resource paper; lean toward acceptance if framed primarily as an evaluation and resources contribution with tempered claims about methodological novelty.
