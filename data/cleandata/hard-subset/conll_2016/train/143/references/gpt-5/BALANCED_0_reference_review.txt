Summary
The paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and presents two dictionary-based estimation methods—multiCluster and multiCCA—that require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. It further proposes intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings, adapting QVEC to multilingual settings (multiQVEC) and introducing a CCA-based variant (multiQVEC-CCA) that is invariant to basis rotations and comparable across dimensions. The authors train embeddings for up to 59 languages and compare four estimation methods (multiCluster, multiCCA, multiSkip, translation-invariance) across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. They also release an evaluation portal to facilitate standardized benchmarking.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA and the multilingual adaptation of QVEC (multiQVEC), with evidence that these correlate better with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation).
2) Scaled multilingual embeddings: practical, dictionary-based approaches (especially multiCCA) that scale to 59 languages with competitive results versus parallel-based methods in selected settings.
3) Infrastructure: an evaluation portal and standardized datasets to support reproducible, comparable assessments of multilingual embeddings.

Strengths
- Clear, useful evaluation advances. The paper convincingly identifies limitations of QVEC (basis dependence, dimensionality bias) and addresses them via CCA. The reported correlations (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA correlates 0.896 with cross-lingual document classification) are substantially stronger than those for word similarity/translation, supporting the claim that the proposed metrics are more diagnostic for downstream performance.
- Practical, scalable methodology. multiCCA is a conceptually simple extension of bilingual CCA that leverages English as an anchor, and it performs strongly across many metrics with better coverage than some baselines. The 59-language training demonstrates feasibility at scale with modest supervision.
- Thorough and balanced comparisons. The paper evaluates four families of methods across multiple intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, reports coverage effects, and acknowledges confounds (e.g., vocabulary intersection). This breadth increases confidence in the conclusions about when dictionary-based vs. parallel-based approaches are preferable.
- Valuable community resource. The evaluation portal, standard splits, and trained models (as stated) will reduce friction and improve reproducibility. Even if the methods are incremental, the infrastructure contribution is significant for the community.
- Insightful analysis of ambiguity. The observation that multiCluster exacerbates type-level ambiguity when clustering translationally equivalent words is important and borne out in the 59-language results, offering guidance for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) to a shared space via English, and multiCluster is a simple clustering-plus-skipgram pipeline. While pragmatic, the estimation innovations are incremental, and the most novel aspects of the paper lie in the evaluation methodology and infrastructure.
- Potential leakage/confirmation bias in 59-language evaluation. For 47 languages, Google Translate is used both to construct training dictionaries and to build evaluation dictionaries for word translation. This risks overlap or systematic bias and may inflate performance (even if involuntarily). A held-out or independently sourced evaluation lexicon is needed to ensure clean measurement.
- Baseline fairness and breadth. The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual data, unlike related works (e.g., BilBOWA, TransGram), potentially handicapping it. Some strong mapping baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes/CCA variants with joint refinement) are missing. This limits how definitive the comparative claims can be.
- Correlation analysis scope and statistics. The correlation study uses 17 embedding variants and two extrinsic tasks, with uneven language coverage (en/da/it) and restricted feature sets (e.g., parsing without POS/morphology and frozen embeddings). While this isolates embedding contributions, it also makes the downstream tasks atypical. Statistical significance, confidence intervals, or robustness checks (e.g., Spearman, bootstrap CIs) are not reported, which weakens the strength of the correlation claims.
- Anonymity and reproducibility issues. The submission includes a concrete URL and mentions resource restrictions (RCV licensing). The portal and software links are redacted in places; however, reliance on restricted data and third-party services (Google Translate) without ensuring open equivalents complicates full reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- For the 59-language setting, did you ensure that evaluation translation pairs do not overlap with training dictionary pairs? If not, can you quantify overlap and re-run with held-out pairs or an independent lexicon?
- How sensitive are multiCCA results to the choice of pivot language? Did you try anchoring to a language with smaller monolingual corpora or using joint multi-CCA rather than pairwise-to-English?
- For multiCluster, how often do connected components create large, semantically heterogeneous clusters via chaining? Could you report cluster size distributions and an ablation with stricter pruning or bilingual “hub” constraints?
- Can you provide statistical significance analyses for the reported correlation coefficients (e.g., CIs, p-values, Spearman vs. Pearson) and for differences among methods on extrinsic tasks?
- Would adding monolingual data to multiSkip (to align with prior art) change the relative ranking? If available, please include these baselines.
- For extrinsic tasks, can you add experiments with standard feature sets (e.g., POS for parsing, or end-to-end models fine-tuning embeddings) to show that the correlation results hold in more realistic settings?

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The methodological novelty is modest, but the evaluation advances (multiQVEC-CCA), the careful correlation study, and the infrastructure contribution are valuable. Addressing the potential training/evaluation leakage, adding stronger/ fairer baselines, and providing statistical robustness would strengthen the paper further.

Suggestions for improvement
- Ensure clean separation between training and evaluation dictionaries for large-scale settings; consider alternative evaluation lexicons (e.g., Wiktionary, PanLex) and report overlap statistics.
- Include stronger mapping baselines and monolingual-augmented multiSkip to solidify comparative claims.
- Report statistical confidence for correlations and task scores; consider additional downstream tasks (e.g., NER) and more languages where feasible.
- Mitigate multiCluster ambiguity via sense-aware clustering or soft alignment; report qualitative error analyses.
- Clarify anonymization and ensure that sufficient artifacts are openly available for replication without restricted licenses.
