Summary and contributions
This submission studies multilingual word embeddings with an emphasis on dictionary-based estimation and intrinsic evaluation. The main contributions, in order of significance as I see them, are:
1) An intrinsic evaluation framework (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) extending QVEC to multilingual settings, with a small correlation study suggesting better alignment with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation.
2) Two simple dictionary-based estimation methods for multilingual embeddings (multiCCA, a hub-language CCA projection; multiCluster, a cluster-ID skip-gram training scheme), and empirical comparisons across multiple metrics.
3) An evaluation portal (datasets, scripts, upload/evaluate), intended to standardize benchmarking.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus on massively multilingual embeddings and transfer: The motivation is well articulated, and the paper targets practical settings where parallel data are scarce but dictionaries are accessible.
- Simple, reproducible estimation methods: multiCCA is a minimal, scalable hub-based extension of prior bilingual CCA. multiCluster is conceptually straightforward. Both are easy to implement and deploy, which is valuable for the community.
- Attempt to standardize evaluation: The paper highlights serious pitfalls (coverage confounds) and provides a suite of intrinsic/extrinsic tasks. Reporting coverage alongside scores is good practice.
- Correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics: Even with a small study, showing that word similarity/translation correlate weakly with downstream tasks while multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better is useful and timely.
- Breadth: Experiments span up to 59 languages and include two downstream tasks (document classification, dependency parsing), giving some breadth of evidence.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: multiSkip is handicapped (trained only on parallel text), unlike prior TransGram/BiSkip variants that also use monolingual data; unsurprisingly it underperforms. The translation-invariance baseline is trained with a different (smaller) context window due to tooling limits. Hyperparameters appear unevenly tuned (e.g., one size d=512 for all, distinct window sizes, no per-method tuning/search). These choices undermine conclusions about relative method quality.
- Questionable train–test independence for dictionary-based evaluation: For 59 languages, both training dictionaries and evaluation pairs are from Google Translate. This risks leakage/overlap and inflates word translation scores. No statement that training and evaluation pairs are disjoint; no noise analysis of MT dictionaries; no sensitivity analysis to τ or dictionary noise. For 12 languages, Europarl is used to extract both alignments and dictionaries; again, unclear separation of resources across train/dev/test.
- multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA limitations and methodology ambiguities: Metrics rely on multilingual supersense resources available only for en/da/it, limiting generality. The correlation study uses only 17 embedding variants (very small N) across three languages, with Pearson correlations reported but no confidence intervals, significance tests, or robustness checks (e.g., Spearman). The QVEC-CCA description appears to use only the leading canonical correlation; this is fragile and may not capture broader alignment (typical CCA-based similarities often aggregate multiple canonical correlations).
- CCA projection details are under-justified: The claim that Tm→m,en and Ten→m,en are “guaranteed to be non-singular” is dubious in practice; CCA projections can be rank-deficient without regularization. The proposed ECCA uses T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v); the inversion may be ill-conditioned. No regularization, numerical stability, or conditioning analysis is provided.
- multiCluster design exposed to severe polysemy over-merging: Creating connected components over all pairwise dictionaries with τ fixed at 0.1 (chosen by manual inspection) risks giant clusters and hubness; only a hard cap on cluster size is used. No ablation on τ, no analysis of cluster purity, and no discussion of how dictionary noise or graph connectivity affects semantics. This likely explains the big drop with 59 languages but is not deeply analyzed.
- Incomplete data documentation: The monolingual corpora sources/sizes per language for the 59-language setting are not described. It is unclear how tokenization/normalization were handled across scripts. This impedes reproducibility and interpretation of coverage/performance.
- Formatting and double-blind issues: Numerous formatting artifacts (broken footnotes, hyphenation “we there- fore”, footnote text in body: “A method for evaluating monolingual word embeddings. 2http://128.2.220.95/multilingual”), inconsistent/malformed references (e.g., “Vincze and Zeman.,? \Q2015\E”, “Al.Rfou.”), and missing/renumbered tables (text references Table 2 but only Table 3 shown). The IP address for the portal violates double-blind. Language codes include deprecated “iw” (should be “he”). These detract from professionalism and anonymity.
- Evaluation design choices: The word translation metric uses a small candidate set Gl2 from the evaluation list rather than full-vocabulary retrieval, which may inflate scores and reduce ecological validity. Coverage effects are acknowledged but only partially mitigated; “intersection-of-vocabularies” results are relegated to supplementary material and not visible.

Questions to authors
1) Train/eval separation: For both the 12-language and 59-language settings, are evaluation pairs (word translation) disjoint from any training dictionaries/alignments? Please quantify overlap and report performance when removing overlaps.
2) CCA details: Are the projection matrices regularized? What fraction of singular values are near-zero? How stable is T−1en→m,en? Please report conditioning, any whitening, and whether you aggregate multiple canonical correlations for multiQVEC-CCA.
3) Hyperparameters/fairness: Why constrain multiSkip to parallel-only if published variants use monolingual data? Can you re-run baselines with matched access to monolingual corpora and matched window sizes, or at least provide a hyperparameter sweep per method?
4) Data: What are the monolingual corpora per language (source, size, preprocessing)? For scripts with different tokenization conventions, how were tokens normalized?
5) Clustering: Provide statistics on cluster size distribution, purity, and sensitivity to τ. How often do giant connected components arise at 59 languages?
6) Correlation analysis: Please add confidence intervals (via bootstrap) and Spearman correlations; with N=17, how robust are the 0.896/0.635 values?

Overall recommendation
While the topic and goals are important, the paper has substantial methodological and presentation issues (fairness, possible leakage, incomplete data details, fragile CCA justification, and significant formatting/double-blind violations). Despite promising ideas (especially the evaluation angle), I recommend rejection in the current form. A revised version addressing the above concerns could be impactful.
