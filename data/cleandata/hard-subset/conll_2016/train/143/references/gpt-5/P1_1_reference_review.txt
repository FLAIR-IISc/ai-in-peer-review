Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An evaluation contribution. The paper introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a basis-invariant, dimension-normalized intrinsic metric derived from CCA, and its multilingual variant using supersense resources. A thorough correlation study shows multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates with extrinsic tasks (doc classification and parsing) substantially better than commonly used intrinsic metrics (cross-lingual word similarity, word translation).
- Secondary: A practical resource contribution. A public evaluation portal (plus datasets/scripts) enabling standardized, multi-metric assessment of multilingual embeddings and reporting coverage, which helps address common pitfalls in intrinsic evaluation.
- Tertiary: Two simple dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual embeddings: multiCCA (a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA by projecting non-English languages into an English space) and multiCluster (clustering translational equivalents and training skip-gram on cluster IDs). Experiments across up to 59 languages compare these with translation-invariance (Gardner et al., 2015) and a skip-gram parallel-data-only baseline.

Strengths
- Strong evaluation insight (“evaluate the evaluations”). The correlation analysis (17 systems over en/da/it) convincingly shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates best with document classification (r=0.896) and multiQVEC correlates best with parsing (r=0.444), while word similarity and word translation correlate poorly (near zero or negative). This is an actionable result for the community and addresses a long-standing gap.
- Practical breadth and utility. Training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages and providing an evaluation portal is valuable. The paper explicitly tackles coverage biases by reporting coverage alongside intrinsic scores and discussing intersection-based evaluation, which improves transparency and replicability.
- Simple, effective, and easy to reproduce methods. MultiCCA is an elegant extension of bilingual CCA using English as a pivot and achieves strong performance and coverage, frequently outperforming baselines on extrinsic evaluations (e.g., on 12 languages, dictionary-based methods win both extrinsic tasks). MultiCluster is a useful contrastive design that helps understand the effects of semantic conflation.
- Careful experimental design choices for extrinsic tasks. By fixing pretrained embeddings and removing POS/morphological features in parsing, the study isolates the contribution of embeddings. The multilingual doc classification setup across seven languages further demonstrates cross-lingual transfer utility.
- Honest discussion of limitations. The paper notes the semantic conflation drawback of cluster-based embeddings, training-data constraints for translation-invariance (window size), and coverage confounds in intrinsic metrics.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty for multiCCA. The core algorithm is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages by anchoring on English and composing CCA projections. The authors themselves characterize it as simple in hindsight. The contribution here is more engineering/evaluation than algorithmic innovation.
- Baseline fairness and strength. The multiSkip baseline excludes monolingual data, unlike related prior work (Coulmance; Guo), making it systematically weaker. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to computational constraints, likely undercutting performance. Stronger baselines (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes mapping from bilingual dictionaries, multiway CCA/joint mapping) are missing and would sharpen conclusions.
- Scope and stability of correlation study. Correlations are computed over 17 systems and only three languages (en/da/it), and multiQVEC(-CCA) relies on supersense resources available exactly for these languages. It is unclear how well the correlations generalize to other languages, resources, or tasks. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported for the correlations.
- Dictionary quality and potential coupling. For 47 languages, training dictionaries come from Google Translate; for 59-language evaluation, the word translation evaluation also uses Google Translate. This raises concerns about noise in supervision and potential coupling between training and evaluation resources. The paper does not quantify dictionary noise or its effect (e.g., ablation vs Wiktionary/Europarl-derived lexicons).
- Technical clarity and correctness. The multiCCA mapping uses the inverse of one CCA projection and claims the CCA matrices are guaranteed non-singular. With high-dimensional embeddings and small dictionaries, CCA solutions may be low-rank; numerical stability and invertibility are not guaranteed and should be discussed (e.g., regularization, pseudoinverses). Some presentation issues remain (e.g., reference to Table 2 but showing Table 3; minor inconsistencies).
- Anonymity breach and significance. The introduction includes a raw IP address for the portal, which could compromise double-blind review. Across results, there is no reporting of statistical significance or variance for extrinsic results.

Questions to authors
- MultiCCA mapping: Under what conditions are Ten→m,en and Tm→m,en invertible? If the bilingual lexicon is small relative to 512-d embeddings, what rank do you observe and how do you handle rank deficiency (regularization, dimensionality truncation, pseudoinverses)? Please clarify the exact CCA formulation and implementation details.
- Baselines: Why not include an orthogonal Procrustes mapping baseline (monolingual embeddings + linear map from bilingual lexicon), which is a widely used and strong approach? Could you also report a version of multiSkip that leverages monolingual data, to make it comparable to prior art?
- Correlation robustness: Can you report confidence intervals and significance for the Pearson correlations, and repeat the study on a different language triad to assess robustness? How sensitive are the correlations to the choice of linguistic resource (e.g., using universal POS tag distributions instead of supersenses)?
- Dictionaries: Can you quantify noise in Google Translate dictionaries and its impact (e.g., by varying dictionary size/quality, or comparing against Wiktionary-based lexicons)? For the 59-language word translation evaluation that also uses Google Translate, how do you ensure no overlap or leakage from training dictionaries?
- Portal and coverage: Does the portal standardize coverage handling (e.g., mandatory intersection evaluation option) and provide variance estimates for extrinsic tasks? Will you release scripts to reproduce all training/evaluation runs including seeds and preprocessing?

Overall, this is a solid and useful evaluation-centric paper with broad practical impact. The methodological novelty is modest, but the empirical insights, resources, and standardization value are significant. Addressing baseline strength, CCA details, and correlation robustness would further strengthen the submission.
