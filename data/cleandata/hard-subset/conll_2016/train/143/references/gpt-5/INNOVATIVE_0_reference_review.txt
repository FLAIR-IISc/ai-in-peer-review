Summary and contributions
- Primary: An intrinsic evaluation metric for multilingual word embeddings, multiQVEC-CCA, that fixes key issues in QVEC (basis-rotation sensitivity and dimensionality bias) via CCA, and its multilingual variant. The paper shows substantially better correlations with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
- Secondary: A simple, scalable, dictionary-only approach (multiCCA) to learn a shared multilingual space across up to 59 languages by projecting monolingual embeddings into an English pivot via CCA; plus a clustering-based variant (multiCluster).
- Tertiary: A public evaluation portal and benchmark suite covering intrinsic and extrinsic tasks to standardize comparison, alongside broad empirical results across many languages.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance in evaluation: multiQVEC-CCA is a clean, well-motivated fix to QVEC’s known flaws (rotation invariance, normalization), and the multilingual extension is timely. The correlation study is compelling: the proposed metrics correlate better with downstream tasks (e.g., doc classification 0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA; parsing 0.444 for multiQVEC) than word similarity or word-translation, which often guide current practice.
- Scalable multilingual coverage with minimal supervision: multiCCA leverages only monolingual corpora and pairwise dictionaries to reach 59 languages. The simplicity and practicality (pivoting to English via CCA) make it easy to reproduce and extend, and it yields strong coverage and competitive performance across diverse evaluations.
- Broad, carefully assembled evaluation: The paper evaluates on 9 metrics spanning intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, reports coverage explicitly, and discusses vocabulary-coverage confounds. It also includes a 12-language setting to compare against parallel-corpus baselines, and a 59-language setting showcasing scale.
- Useful community resource: The evaluation portal, data packaging, and standardized splits will likely have outsized impact by encouraging consistent, replicable multilingual evaluation. This is valuable infrastructure for the field.
- Insightful analysis: The paper diagnoses why multiCluster degrades with many languages (amplified ambiguity), clarifies the role of coverage, and surfaces limits of popular baselines (e.g., monolingual-signal deprivation in multiSkip).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of embedding methods: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer’s bilingual CCA to a multilingual pivot space; multiCluster is a naive cluster-sharing scheme that ignores polysemy. The methodological boldness is in evaluation, not representation learning.
- Baseline fairness and settings: The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual data (unlike Coulmance/Guo) and the translation-invariance model uses a smaller window for computational reasons. These choices likely handicap those baselines and cloud comparative conclusions.
- Reliance on English pivot and noisy dictionaries: The approach assumes English-centric pivoting and uses Google Translate to build dictionaries for many languages. This raises questions about bias, noise, and reproducibility (and potential circularity when evaluation dictionaries also come from GT for 59 languages).
- Scope and robustness of correlation study: The correlation analysis involves 17 embedding variants across three languages; while suggestive, it remains limited in size and language diversity. The parsing correlation for multiQVEC-CCA is modest (0.273), and the two proposed metrics correlate differently across tasks.
- Dependence on supervised linguistic resources: multiQVEC(-CCA) needs supersense tag matrices; cross-lingual inconsistencies and limited availability constrain the languages where it’s applicable. The paper notes this but the practical cost is real.

Questions to authors
- Pivot dependence: How do results change if the pivot is not English (e.g., high-resource non-English pivot), or if you use a joint multilingual CCA rather than per-language-to-English projections?
- Mapping constraints: Did you try orthogonal Procrustes mappings (as in cross-lingual alignment work) versus unconstrained CCA? Are the learned transforms well-conditioned, and how sensitive is performance to dictionary size/noise?
- Dictionary noise analysis: For the 59-language setup, what is the impact of GT dictionary noise on multiCCA and on evaluation? Any filtering or confidence thresholds beyond excluding identical/multiword translations?
- Coverage vs performance: For intrinsic metrics, beyond reporting coverage, did you evaluate on intersection vocabularies systematically across all settings? How do the main conclusions change under this control?
- multiQVEC-CCA construction: How is the multilingual linguistic matrix built/aligned across languages when supersense inventories differ? Could universal POS tags be used at scale, and would correlations hold?

Overall assessment
This paper’s most valuable and novel contribution is the evaluation advance: multiQVEC-CCA and the empirical evidence that it correlates better with downstream tasks than widely used intrinsic metrics. The multilingual scaling of a dictionary-only pipeline and the evaluation portal are strong practical contributions. The representation-learning methods themselves are incremental, but the paper opens a useful direction by re-centering how we evaluate multilingual embeddings at scale. I am inclined to accept on the strength of the evaluation contribution and the community impact of the benchmarks/portal, despite baseline-setting caveats and modest novelty in the embedding models.
