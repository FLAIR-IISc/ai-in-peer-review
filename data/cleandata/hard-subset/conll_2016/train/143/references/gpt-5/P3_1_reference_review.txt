Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual word embeddings and proposes evaluation metrics intended to better predict downstream performance.

Key contributions (as I see them):
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. Adapting QVEC to a multilingual setting and replacing dimension-wise alignment with CCA addresses basis-variance and dimensionality normalization, and the correlation study shows these metrics predict downstream performance better than commonly used intrinsic metrics.
2) Scalable dictionary-based embedding estimation: multiCCA (projecting multiple languages into an English space via CCA) and multiCluster (graph clustering of translational equivalents with monolingual skipgram over cluster IDs). The methods require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries and are applied to 59 languages.
3) An evaluation suite/portal aggregating resources, metrics, and scripts, enabling reproducible, multi-metric comparisons.

Strengths
- Evaluation clarity and impact: The correlation analysis across four intrinsic and two extrinsic metrics (albeit on 17 systems) is valuable. It substantiates that cross-lingual word similarity and word translation are poor proxies for downstream tasks, while multiQVEC (best for parsing) and multiQVEC-CCA (best for document classification) correlate much better. This is a concrete, actionable insight likely to influence future evaluation practice.
- Simple, scalable methods with broad coverage: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of CCA-based bilingual alignment to a hub-language setup and achieves strong coverage and performance across 59 languages with minimal supervision (dictionaries). The multiCluster approach is a clever, easily implemented way to inject cross-lingual constraints and leverage monolingual data.
- Careful experimental design and reporting: The paper evaluates on diverse metrics (word similarity, translation, QVEC variants, two downstream tasks) and discusses coverage effects and their confounds for intrinsic evaluation. The authors also report when their own methods underperform, and provide insight (e.g., semantic ambiguity in multiCluster with many languages).
- Useful resources and tooling: The portal and released benchmarks can standardize multilingual embedding evaluation. Even though some resources (RCV) are restricted, much of the setup is reproducible and will lower barriers for future work.
- Comparative baselines from both dictionary and parallel paradigms: Including translation-invariance (matrix factorization) and a skipgram-based parallel approach provides a more complete picture than many earlier bilingual-only studies.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of methods: multiCCA is an intuitive hub-language extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is a classic “hard sharing via translation clusters” approach. Their value is in engineering and scale rather than algorithmic novelty. A discussion of why linear mappings suffice across 59 languages, and where they break down, is thin.
- Evaluation biases from supervision sources: For 47 languages, Google Translate provides both training dictionaries (for clustering/projection) and, for some metrics, evaluation pairs. This risks circularity and bias, especially for the 59-language word translation evaluation. The paper acknowledges dictionary noise but lacks robustness analyses (e.g., varying dictionary size/quality).
- Baseline comparability: The multiSkip baseline is trained only on parallel corpora, unlike related work that augments with monolingual data. The translation-invariance baseline is constrained by an implementation that limits window size, potentially handicapping it. This raises fairness questions in head-to-head comparisons.
- Intrinsic metrics depend on scarce resources: multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA require multilingual supersense annotations, currently available for a few languages and not fully consistent across them. This limits scalability and may skew correlation results toward semantics-heavy tasks (document classification) rather than syntax (parsing).
- Correlation analysis scope: Correlations are estimated using 17 embedding variants over 3 languages for extrinsic tasks. While helpful, this sample size and language set are small. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported, and parsing correlations for multiQVEC-CCA remain modest.
- Modeling limitations and missing ablations: multiCluster’s hard clustering exacerbates polysemy, especially with 59 languages; a soft/shared-parameter variant or subword-aware model might mitigate this. The paper does not analyze sensitivity to τ for dictionary extraction, cluster size caps, embedding dimensionality, or monolingual corpus size per language.

Questions to Authors
- multiCCA formulation: You define ECCA(m, v) = T^{-1}_{en→m,en} T_{m→m,en} E^m(v). Why not directly use a projection learned from m into the English space (i.e., a single W_{m→en})? Please explain the stability and empirical advantages of the T^{-1}T composition versus a direct projection.
- Dictionary noise and sensitivity: How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary size/quality? Can you report performance as a function of dictionary size (e.g., 1k/5k/20k seed pairs) and translation threshold τ? Any results with induced vs curated dictionaries?
- Baseline fairness: Can you provide results for a parallel-plus-monolingual multiSkip (as in Coulmance et al.; Guo et al.) and for translation-invariance with larger windows or optimized hyperparameters to better match your settings?
- Evaluation independence: For the 59-language setting, how do you avoid overlap between Google Translate-generated training dictionaries and evaluation pairs? If not avoided, can you provide alternative evaluations (e.g., Wikipedia interlanguage links, PanLex) to ensure independence?
- Robustness and variability: What is the variance across random seeds for the downstream tasks, and are differences statistically significant? Also, how do results change when allowing the parser to finetune embeddings or when reintroducing POS features?

Additional comments
- Consider subword/character modeling to improve coverage and morphology handling, particularly for low-resource or morphologically rich languages.
- It would be helpful to report monolingual corpus sizes per language and the number of dictionary pairs used per language, to contextualize coverage and performance.
- The portal is a strong contribution; please ensure long-term availability and document versioning of datasets/metrics.
