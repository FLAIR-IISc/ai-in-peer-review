Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings: (i) multiCluster, which forms cross-lingual connected-component clusters via dictionaries and learns a single embedding per cluster using monolingual corpora; and (ii) multiCCA, a straightforward extension of CCA-based bilingual alignment that projects all languages into an English embedding space. The paper also introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics derived from QVEC, and reports correlations between these metrics and downstream tasks. Experiments compare the proposed methods against a skipgram-based parallel method (multiSkip) and a translation-invariance matrix factorization, training up to 59 languages. A web portal for evaluation is advertised.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation contribution: multiQVEC-CCA (and multiQVEC) and a correlation study showing higher correlation with downstream tasks than standard (cross-lingual) similarity/translation metrics.
2) Engineering/scale: large-scale training and evaluation of multilingual embeddings (up to 59 languages) using simple dictionary-based methods.
3) Resource/service: an evaluation portal aggregating datasets and metrics.

Strengths
- Empirical breadth and careful reporting of coverage. The paper trains and evaluates across many languages and multiple metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic). It explicitly reports coverage percentages and discusses vocabulary intersection issues—often glossed over in this literature.
- Useful evaluation insights. The correlation analysis (albeit limited; see weaknesses) is valuable: multiQVEC-CCA shows very high correlation with multilingual document classification (0.896), and multiQVEC with parsing (0.444), while cross-lingual similarity and word translation correlate poorly. This cautions against over-relying on popular intrinsic metrics.
- Simple, practical methods. multiCCA is easy to implement and—given strong monolingual embeddings—performs robustly across many metrics with good coverage. The cluster-based approach is a reasonable baseline for resource-scarce settings.
- Reproducibility intent. The paper provides (redacted) software artifacts and a portal, and makes clear which resources are restricted (RCV), signaling an intent to support future work.

Weaknesses
- Double-blind violation and formatting issues. The portal’s public URL (http://128.2.220.95/multilingual) reveals identity/affiliation and violates anonymity. The paper contains numerous formatting and citation problems (broken footnotes, hyphenation, malformed references with backslashes/question marks, “indeces”, misaligned footnotes), which undermines professionalism and readability.
- Methodological novelty is limited. multiCCA is a direct extension of prior bilingual CCA alignment (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014) by pivoting through English; multiCluster is very close to prior cross-lingual clustering (e.g., Och, 1999; Täckström et al., 2012) plus skipgram. The main novelty ends up being the evaluation metrics/portal rather than embedding methods.
- Potential circularity in training/evaluation dictionaries for 59 languages. For 47 languages, training uses Google Translate to create en–xx dictionaries from the top 20k English words; evaluation on word translation for the 59-language setting also uses Google Translate dictionaries. This risks overlap and inflation of results. The paper does not quantify overlap or control for leakage.
- Incomparable and weak baselines. The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual data (unlike prior work), likely handicapping it. The translation-invariance method is trained with a smaller context window (3) than others (5) due to computational constraints, further skewing comparisons. Window size and NCE settings differ across methods; the fairness of comparisons is questionable.
- Insufficient statistical rigor in the correlation study. Correlations are computed over 17 embedding variants and 3 languages (en/da/it), with no confidence intervals or significance tests reported. The sample size is small; negative/near-zero correlations (e.g., -0.292 for word translation vs parsing) are reported without uncertainty. Additionally, extrinsic metrics are aggregated across languages, potentially masking per-language variance.

Additional methodological concerns
- CCA invertibility claim: The paper asserts the CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” and uses Ten→m,en^{-1}. In practice, CCA often yields rank-deficient transforms (especially with dimensionality reduction), and numerical stability of inverses is not guaranteed. No regularization details are given.
- multiCluster details underspecified: How are clusters capped at 1,000/10,000—are large components split, pruned, or truncated? What is the impact on connectivity/quality? Transitive closure over noisy dictionaries likely induces polysemy and bloated clusters.
- Hyperparameter choices and tuning: 512-dim, 10 epochs, τ=0.1 for dictionary extraction “based on manual inspection” with no tuning/ablation. No sensitivity analyses are provided.
- Claim that extrinsic evaluations are immune to coverage is too strong: unseen words still affect model inputs; freezing embeddings and removing POS/morphology is an atypical setting that may not reflect realistic downstream usage.

Questions to Authors
1) Google Translate overlap: What fraction of evaluation pairs for the 59-language word translation task also appear in the training dictionaries? Please quantify overlap and re-run with disjoint dictionaries.
2) Correlation significance: Report 95% CIs or p-values for the correlations in Table 1, and provide per-language breakdowns for extrinsic tasks to rule out aggregation artifacts.
3) CCA mapping: Under what conditions are Ten→m,en and Tm→m,en non-singular in your implementation? Do you regularize CCA? Why not map directly into English space via the learned English-side projection instead of inverting Ten→m,en?
4) multiCluster capping: How do you enforce maximum cluster sizes (1,000/10,000)? What happens to nodes in oversized components? Provide statistics on cluster size distributions and ablations varying τ and the cap.
5) Baseline parity: Can you retrain multiSkip with monolingual data (as in prior art) and rerun translation-invariance with the same window size to ensure fair comparisons?
6) Anonymity: Please remove or anonymize the portal URL for the camera-ready to comply with double-blind policy.

Recommendation
Despite valuable evaluation insights and broad experimentation, the accumulation of methodological and presentation issues (anonymity, baseline fairness, dictionary circularity, limited novelty, statistical rigor) leads me to recommend rejection at this time.
