Summary and claimed contributions
The paper studies multilingual word embeddings learned from monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, and proposes two estimation methods: multiCluster (cluster translational equivalents, then learn one vector per cluster) and multiCCA (project monolingual embeddings into a shared English space via CCA). It further proposes intrinsic evaluation metrics—multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA—intended to correlate better with downstream tasks than common similarity/translation metrics, and provides an evaluation portal. Experiments cover up to 59 languages, with comparisons to multiSkip and translation-invariance baselines, and a correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength)
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA (and its multilingual variant) addressing basis-variance and dimensionality issues of QVEC, with evidence of stronger correlation to downstream tasks than word similarity/translation. This is the most conceptually novel and broadly useful aspect.
2) Resources and infrastructure: a public portal and packaged benchmarks to evaluate multilingual embeddings across intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, encouraging replicability and standardization.
3) Estimation methods: a simple, scalable multiCCA pivoting through English via CCA and a dictionary-based clustering approach (multiCluster). While effective, novelty is moderate given prior CCA-based bilingual mappings and clustering/skip-gram ideas.

Strengths
- Clear, principled evaluation advance: Moving from sum-of-correlations (QVEC) to CCA directly addresses basis invariance and dimensionality scaling. The multilingual extension is useful, and the correlation table shows multiQVEC(-CCA) outperforming common intrinsic metrics in predicting downstream document classification and (to a lesser extent) parsing.
- Breadth and practicality: Training embeddings for 59 languages with only dictionaries and monolingual data is valuable. The study carefully reports coverage and discusses its impact on intrinsic evaluation bias, which many works ignore.
- Solid baselines and analysis: The paper compares dictionary- and parallel-based methods, and analyzes why multiCluster degrades with many languages (sense conflation), which is insightful.
- Infrastructure for the community: The portal and packaged datasets (plus code) can standardize evaluation and lower barriers to entry.
- Clarity and reproducibility orientation: Methods are well-described, with reasonable hyperparameter disclosures and attention to evaluation pitfalls.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of correlation study: The intrinsic–extrinsic correlation is estimated on only three languages (en/it/da) and 17 models, many of which are variants of a single method. This limits generality. Moreover, only Pearson correlations are reported; Spearman or robust measures and confidence intervals would strengthen claims.
- Baseline comparability concerns: The multiSkip implementation omits monolingual corpora (unlike related work), likely handicapping it. The translation-invariance baseline uses a smaller context window due to computational limits. These differences cloud fairness of the comparative results.
- Reliance on noisy and pivot-biased resources: For 47 languages, dictionaries come from Google Translate; no robustness analysis to dictionary noise, polysemy, or domain mismatch is provided. The multiCCA pivot through English may bias the space toward English semantics; the effect is not quantified.
- Evaluation limitations: 
  - Word translation is evaluated by ranking within the evaluation set’s vocabulary, not the full target vocabulary, making the task easier and less comparable to standard retrieval setups.
  - The extrinsic parsing setup delexicalizes other features to isolate embeddings; while methodologically clean, it may under-represent utility in realistic pipelines, and the extrinsic evaluations are limited to 12 languages, not the full 59.
  - multiQVEC(-CCA) currently relies on supersense resources in only three languages with acknowledged inconsistencies; general applicability remains uncertain.
- Methodological details needing clarification: The claim that CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular” and the use of T_en→m,en^{-1} deserve justification; CCA can yield degenerate directions if data are low-rank or regularized. The impact of cluster construction choices (τ, max cluster size) and connected-component chaining on noise propagation is not ablated.

Questions to Authors
1) CCA invertibility: Under what conditions are Ten→m,en and Tm→m,en guaranteed non-singular? Did you observe rank deficiencies in practice, and if so, how did you regularize or handle them?
2) Baseline parity: Can you align training regimes by allowing multiSkip to use monolingual corpora (as in Coulmance/Guo), and match context windows for translation-invariance? If not, can you quantify the sensitivity of these baselines to such choices?
3) Dictionary noise/pivot bias: How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary quality? Can you provide ablations varying dictionary size/noise or non-English pivots (e.g., de, es) to assess pivot bias?
4) Word translation evaluation: What happens when retrieval is over the full target vocabulary (or a large open vocabulary) rather than the evaluation set only? Does the ranking of methods change?
5) Coverage-controlled comparisons: You partially address coverage by reporting percentages. Can you provide results on strict vocabulary intersections for all intrinsic metrics, and report confidence intervals?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Please ensure double-blind compliance: the hard-coded portal URL appears de-anonymizing; consider anonymized URLs or deferred release.
- Consider reporting Spearman’s ρ and CIs for correlations, and adding more families of models (e.g., different dims, orthogonal Procrustes variants) to improve external validity.
- Add ablations on τ for dictionary edge selection and on cluster-size limits to quantify multiCluster’s noise vs. ambiguity trade-off.
- Explore using universal POS tag inventories for multiQVEC(-CCA) as a syntactic counterpart, and harmonize supersense inventories across languages to broaden applicability.
- Clarify whether the document classification correlation (0.896) is robust across random seeds and alternative classifiers.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The evaluation advances (multiQVEC-CCA and the multilingual evaluation suite) and the practical, scalable multiCCA approach are valuable to the community, despite moderate methodological novelty and some evaluation limitations. Addressing fairness, robustness, and clarity issues would strengthen the paper further.
