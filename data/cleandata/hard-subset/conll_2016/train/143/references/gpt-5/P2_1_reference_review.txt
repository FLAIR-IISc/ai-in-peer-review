Summary and main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An evaluation contribution. The paper proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics derived from QVEC, with QVEC-CCA fixing two key theoretical flaws (basis dependence and dimensionality bias). Empirically, these metrics correlate substantially better with downstream tasks than commonly used intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation), e.g., up to 0.896 correlation with multilingual document classification (Table 1).
- Secondary: Practical multilingual embedding estimation at scale. Two dictionary-based methods—multiCCA (a simple, effective extension of CCA projections to a shared English space) and multiCluster (cluster IDs trained with skip-gram)—are implemented and evaluated across 59 languages, with thorough comparisons on 12 languages where parallel data is available. The study provides concrete guidance on trade-offs (e.g., multiCCA’s superior coverage and robustness vs. multiCluster’s susceptibility to semantic conflation as the number of languages grows).
- Tertiary: Community resource. A web portal with standardized datasets and automated evaluation across intrinsic/extrinsic metrics, plus code/hyperparameter details.

Strengths
- Strong, well-justified evaluation advance: The critique of QVEC (basis sensitivity, unnormalized sum) is correct, and the CCA fix is principled. The multilingual extension via supersense tag matrices is clearly described. The correlation analysis (17 embeddings × 6 metrics) is a concrete step toward “evaluating the evaluators,” showing multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA are better proxies for real downstream performance than popular alternatives. This addresses a pervasive pain point in the literature.
- Solid empirical evidence and breadth: Experiments span 59 languages (dictionary-based) and a 12-language subset (with strong parallel corpora) across nine metrics, including two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) where embeddings are deliberately the only token features. This breadth adds credibility and practical relevance.
- Simple, scalable, and effective multiCCA: Although conceptually straightforward (pairwise CCA into an English pivot space), multiCCA performs consistently well, often with the best coverage and competitive accuracy, especially at larger language scales where multiCluster suffers from semantic conflation.
- Thoughtful analysis of coverage confounds: The paper explicitly tracks and reports coverage and even provides intersection-based comparisons in supplementary material. This careful treatment of a common pitfall in intrinsic evaluation improves interpretability.
- Reproducibility and community impact: Hyperparameters and training details are specified (e.g., d=512, window sizes, tau=0.1), and code/portal are provided (subject to anonymization). The portal can standardize future comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the estimation methods: multiCCA is a direct, albeit useful, extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) to a multilingual space via a pivot; multiCluster is straightforward clustering of translation equivalents plus skip-gram. The contributions here are more in the engineering and broad evaluation than in algorithmic innovation.
- Potentially unfair or weak baselines: The multiSkip variant excludes monolingual data, whereas comparable works (Coulmance et al., Guo et al.) leverage monolingual contexts; this likely depresses its performance. The translation-invariance method is trained with a smaller context window due to computational constraints, which may disadvantage it. This complicates the strength of comparative claims.
- Correlation study scope and statistical reporting: The correlation claims rest on 17 embedding variants and two extrinsic tasks (one classification setup in en/it/da and one parsing setup in en/it/da). Confidence intervals or significance tests for correlation coefficients are not reported, and the results are mixed across tasks (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA: 0.896 with classification but only 0.273 with parsing; multiQVEC: 0.635 and 0.444 respectively). This limits generality and may indicate that different extrinsic tasks prefer different intrinsic views.
- Training/evaluation dependence in 59-language translation metric: For the 59-language setup, both the training dictionaries and the translation evaluation rely on Google Translate (with different extraction procedures but same source). This risks target leakage or domain alignment that inflates apparent translation performance and should be carefully controlled or at least analyzed.
- Linguistic resource limitations for multiQVEC(-CCA): The multilingual supersense matrix is currently only for en/da/it, with noted inconsistencies across languages. This limits the breadth of intrinsic evaluation and may bias correlation results toward languages where those resources exist. Guidance for extending to consistent, broader multilingual tagsets is helpful but does not fully resolve this limitation.

Questions to authors
- Can you report confidence intervals or p-values for the Pearson correlations in Table 1? Are the differences between multiQVEC(-CCA) and other intrinsic metrics statistically significant?
- How sensitive is multiCCA to the choice of pivot language? Did you try a joint multi-view CCA or averaging projections from multiple pivots? Any empirical evidence on pivot selection (e.g., high-resource vs. related-language pivot)?
- For fairness, can you include a multiSkip variant that leverages monolingual corpora (as in Trans-gram/bilingual skip-gram), or cite comparable results? Similarly, can you re-run the translation-invariance baseline with a larger context window (or report sensitivity) to address the current computationally induced limitation?
- In the 59-language word translation evaluation, can you quantify or mitigate training–evaluation dependence from Google Translate (e.g., by using a disjoint seed list, different time snapshots, or an alternative dictionary source)?
- For multiCluster, did you explore sense disambiguation or subword/character features to reduce semantic conflation within large multilingual clusters? Any analysis of how cluster size thresholds affect performance?

Overall assessment
- Significance: High for evaluation practice; moderate for methods. The paper advances the state of evaluation for multilingual embeddings and provides broad empirical baselines/resources. The estimation methods are practical but not highly novel.
- Soundness: Generally solid, with careful reporting and sensible setups, though some baseline choices and statistical reporting should be strengthened.
- Reproducibility/clarity: Good; code/portal and hyperparameters provided, clear descriptions.
- I lean positive based on the evaluation contributions, empirical breadth, and practical impact, while encouraging stronger baselines and statistical rigor in the final version.
