- Contribution: Proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, adapting QVEC with CCA to address basis-variance and dimensionality issues and better predict downstream performance.
- Contribution: Introduces scalable dictionary-based embedding methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) that use monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries and are applied to 59 languages.
- Contribution: Releases an evaluation suite/portal aggregating resources, metrics, and scripts for reproducible multi-metric comparisons.

- Strength: Correlation analysis shows multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA predict downstream tasks better than standard intrinsic metrics like cross-lingual word similarity and translation.
- Strength: Methods are simple, scalable, and achieve broad coverage with minimal supervision (dictionaries), with multiCluster a clever way to enforce cross-lingual constraints.
- Strength: Careful experimental design and transparent reporting, including diverse metrics, coverage analysis, and candid discussion of when methods underperform.
- Strength: The released portal and benchmarks can standardize multilingual embedding evaluation and lower barriers for future work.
- Strength: Comparison includes both dictionary-based and parallel-based baselines for a more complete evaluation.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—multiCCA and multiCluster are intuitive extensions/known ideas, with thin discussion of where linear mappings fail across many languages.
- Weakness: Potential evaluation bias from using Google Translate for training dictionaries and some evaluation pairs, risking circularity and lack of robustness analyses.
- Weakness: Baseline comparability concerns—multiSkip lacks monolingual augmentation and translation-invariance baseline is constrained, possibly making comparisons unfair.
- Weakness: multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA depend on scarce/inconsistent multilingual supersense annotations, limiting scalability and possibly biasing toward semantics-heavy tasks.
- Weakness: Correlation analysis is limited in scope (17 systems, 3 languages), lacks confidence intervals/significance tests, and shows modest parsing correlations for multiQVEC-CCA.
- Weakness: Modeling and ablation gaps—hard clustering worsens polysemy and the paper omits sensitivity analyses for dictionary size/threshold τ, cluster caps, embedding dimensionality, and corpus size.

- Question: Why use the T^{-1}_{en→m,en} T_{m→m,en} composition in multiCCA instead of a direct projection W_{m→en}; what stability/empirical benefits justify this choice?
- Question: How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary size/quality—can you report performance for varying seed sizes (e.g., 1k/5k/20k) and τ, and compare induced vs curated dictionaries?
- Question: Can you provide fairer baselines: a multiSkip augmented with monolingual data and translation-invariance with larger windows/optimized hyperparameters?
- Question: How do you avoid overlap between Google Translate-generated training dictionaries and evaluation pairs for the 59-language setting; can you provide independent evaluations (e.g., Wikipedia interlanguage links, PanLex)?
- Question: What is the variance across random seeds and are downstream differences statistically significant; how do results change if embeddings are fine-tuned or POS features reintroduced?

- Comment: Consider adding subword/character modeling to handle morphology and improve low-resource language performance.
- Comment: Report monolingual corpus sizes and number of dictionary pairs per language to contextualize coverage and results.
- Comment: Ensure the portal’s long-term availability and dataset/metric versioning is documented.
