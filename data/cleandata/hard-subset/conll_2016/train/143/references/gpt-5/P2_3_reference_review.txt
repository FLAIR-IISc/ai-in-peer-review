Summary and contributions
- This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings and introduces two simple, dictionary-based estimation methods: (1) multiCCA, a straightforward extension of bilingual CCA that projects monolingual spaces into an English pivot space; and (2) multiCluster, which creates cross-lingual clusters via dictionaries and trains a single embedding per cluster on concatenated monolingual corpora. The authors train embeddings for up to 59 languages.
- The paper also proposes multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, intrinsic evaluation metrics extending QVEC to multilingual settings and addressing basis-rotation and normalization issues via CCA. It presents a correlation study of intrinsic metrics vs extrinsic performance (document classification and dependency parsing).
- Finally, it offers an evaluation portal (and data/resources) to facilitate standardized evaluation.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation framework: multiQVEC-CCA and the empirical correlation study showing these metrics correlate better with downstream tasks than commonly used alternatives. This is both novel and practically important.
2) Resource contribution: trained multilingual embeddings for 59 languages and an evaluation portal with benchmarks, which will likely have community impact.
3) Methods: multiCCA and multiCluster. While simple and mostly incremental, they are pragmatic and scale to many languages; multiCCA performs strongly and efficiently.

Strengths
- Strong focus on evaluation quality with explicit correlation to downstream tasks. The study comparing four intrinsic metrics with two extrinsic tasks (17 embedding variants) is valuable. The finding that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate better than word similarity and word translation helps the field move beyond weak intrinsic proxies. The use of CCA addresses QVEC’s theoretical shortcomings (basis dependence and lack of normalization), which is well-motivated.
- Practical, scalable methods with broad coverage. The dictionary-based approaches require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, enabling coverage of 59 languages. The pivot-based multiCCA is computationally light, conceptually clean, and achieves strong performance across several metrics. The paper carefully reports coverage and discusses its impact, an important and often overlooked issue.
- Solid extrinsic evaluations across languages and tasks. The paper includes multilingual document classification (7 languages) and UD parsing (12 languages), removes confounding lexical features, and freezes embeddings to isolate their contribution. Results show dictionary-based methods are competitive and often best in extrinsic metrics, supporting the utility of the proposed approaches.
- Community resource and reproducibility. The evaluation portal, data packaging, and trained embeddings (plus explicit dev/test splits) will facilitate replicable research and standardization. This is a meaningful service contribution.
- Clear exposition and sensible baselines. The paper situates its methods against multiSkip and translation-invariance. The design choices (e.g., using English as a pivot for multiCCA) are well justified.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. multiCCA is a direct, albeit useful, extension of prior bilingual CCA work; multiCluster is a simple clustering plus skip-gram pipeline that can exacerbate polysemy. While practicality matters, the core algorithmic contributions are incremental. The paper’s main novelty lies more in evaluation than modeling.
- Fairness and robustness of comparisons. The multiSkip implementation uses only parallel data, whereas related prior work commonly leverages both parallel and monolingual corpora. This likely handicaps multiSkip and complicates claims about relative performance. Similarly, translation-invariance uses a smaller context window due to computational constraints, potentially affecting results.
- Potential circularity and quality issues in 59-language setup. For 47 languages, dictionaries are derived from Google Translate for training, and evaluation for word translation in the 59-language set also uses Google Translate. Even if not exactly the same pairs, this risks source overlap and does not reflect realistic lexicon induction. Dictionary quality and coverage vary across languages; the impact on embedding quality is not thoroughly analyzed.
- Correlation study scope and methodology. The correlation analysis uses 17 points with an imbalanced composition (12 multiCluster variants, 1 multiCCA, 1 multiSkip, 2 translation-invariance), which can bias correlations toward variation within a single family. Only three languages are used for this analysis (EN/IT/DA), and Pearson correlations are reported without confidence intervals or Spearman alternatives. Although the trends are plausible, the evidence would be stronger with more families, languages, and robustness checks.
- Evaluation design details. Some intrinsic evaluations rank translations within a limited set (test vocabulary), which can inflate accuracy relative to full-vocabulary retrieval. The paper notes coverage effects and provides partial remedies, but more systematic intersection-based comparisons and analysis of frequency effects would improve rigor. For multiCluster, analysis of cluster semantic coherence and the impact of the max cluster size would be helpful.

Questions to authors
- In the 59-language setting, do the Google Translate dictionaries used for training overlap with those used for evaluation (word translation)? Can you quantify overlap and assess potential inflation? Would Wiktionary- or PanLex-derived evaluation reduce this concern?
- For the correlation study, can you provide Spearman correlations, confidence intervals (e.g., via bootstrap), and results when balancing the sample across method families (or removing multiple multiCluster variants)? How sensitive are the results to these design choices?
- How does multiCCA perform when pivoting through a different language (e.g., DE, ES) or when projecting all languages into a learned shared space rather than EN? Any degradation for non-Indo-European languages?
- Can you report ablations on dictionary size/quality and monolingual corpus size for multiCCA to characterize data requirements?
- For document classification and parsing, can you quantify how much performance is lost by removing POS/morphological features, to contextualize the absolute scores?

Additional comments
- The portal URL appears in the text; please ensure compliance with double-blind reviewing (masking or anonymous links).
- Consider releasing scripts to regenerate GT-based dictionaries to improve reproducibility given API variability.
- Minor: clarify the word translation evaluation protocol (candidate set size, tie-breaking).

Overall evaluation
This paper meets CoNLL criteria well on significance (especially via evaluation metrics and resources), soundness (methods and experiments are reasonable), and clarity. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the evaluation contribution and practical scaling to many languages are valuable. I recommend acceptance (weak accept) primarily for the evaluation advances (multiQVEC-CCA with correlation evidence) and the resource/portal that will benefit the community.
