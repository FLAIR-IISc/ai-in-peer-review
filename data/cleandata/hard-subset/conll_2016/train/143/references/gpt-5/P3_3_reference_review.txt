Summary
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings. It introduces two simple, dictionary-based methods for learning shared spaces across many languages (multiCluster and multiCCA), proposes intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) intended to better correlate with downstream performance, and provides a public evaluation portal. Experiments compare four embedding estimation methods over 12–59 languages, on intrinsic (similarity, translation, QVEC variants) and extrinsic (document classification, dependency parsing) benchmarks, and analyze correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA, a rotation/scale-invariant intrinsic metric aligned to interpretable linguistic features, and a correlation study showing it (and multiQVEC) aligns better with downstream tasks than common alternatives.
2) Scalable multilingual embeddings with minimal supervision: pragmatic dictionary-based approaches (multiCCA and multiCluster) that extend to 59 languages, with comprehensive comparative evaluation.
3) Community resources: an evaluation portal, code, and datasets to standardize and facilitate multilingual embedding evaluation.

Strengths
- Evaluation insight and methodological clarity: The critique of QVEC’s basis dependence and dimensionality bias is correct and well articulated; multiQVEC-CCA is a principled fix via CCA. The correlation analysis (albeit limited; see weaknesses) provides valuable evidence that common intrinsic metrics (word similarity/translation) can be weak predictors of downstream performance.
- Simple, scalable methods with strong empirical showing: multiCCA is an elegant, low-friction extension of bilingual CCA to the multilingual setting via an English pivot. Results indicate competitive or superior performance to more complex methods on several metrics, especially extrinsic ones, and better coverage than translation-invariance.
- Breadth of evaluation and careful reporting: The paper evaluates both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, includes a 12-language and a 59-language setting, and explicitly reports coverage and discusses its confounds. The inclusion of a non-English pair (da–it) in translation is a good sanity check for the pivot strategy.
- Reproducibility and practical impact: A unified portal, released models (with the caveat of RCV licensing), and clear training details lower the barrier for future work and increase comparability across papers.
- Useful negative result: multiCluster’s degradation at 59 languages highlights the dangers of excessive sense conflation when clustering across many languages—an important lesson for the community.

Weaknesses
- Baseline fairness and comparability: The multiSkip baseline was implemented without monolingual data, unlike related work; the translation-invariance method used a smaller context window due to tooling constraints. These decisions likely handicap the baselines and blur conclusions about relative quality. A stronger reproduction (or acknowledgment with adjusted claims) is needed.
- Evaluation independence and noise: For 59 languages, both training dictionaries and evaluation word translation pairs come from Google Translate. This risks leakage and optimistic scores, and the MT quality varies by language family and morphology. The paper should quantify overlap between training/evaluation pairs, or ensure disjoint splits, and discuss how noise in GT dictionaries affects multiCCA/multiCluster.
- Limited scope and stability of correlation analysis: The correlation study uses only three languages, a small set of 17 embeddings from closely related methods, and reports a single Pearson coefficient without CIs or significance tests. The very high doc classification correlation (0.896) could reflect shared artifacts. It remains unclear if multiQVEC-CCA generalizes across languages, corpora, embedding dimensionalities, and tasks. Also, using only the first canonical correlation may be brittle; summing the top-k correlations could be more stable.
- Methodological limitations not fully addressed:
  - multiCluster: connected components over bilingual edges will over-merge senses across languages, leading to very large, semantically heterogeneous clusters; the max cluster cap does not solve the underlying issue. An analysis of cluster purity and its effect on performance is missing.
  - multiCCA: mapping all languages into English via non-orthogonal projections and matrix inversion can distort monolingual geometry and propagate noise; pivot bias toward English is not analyzed. Joint or orthogonal Procrustes mappings are a common alternative.
- Downstream task design: The extrinsic tasks freeze embeddings and remove other features, which isolates embedding quality but under-represents practical usage. The document classification setup trains and tests on the same set of languages (multilingual supervised), not a true cross-lingual transfer setting. Broader downstream coverage (e.g., zero-shot transfer, POS/NER, bilingual lexicon induction) and any 59-language extrinsic results would strengthen the conclusions.

Questions to authors
- How much overlap exists between GT-derived training dictionaries and GT-derived evaluation pairs in the 59-language word translation metric? Can you provide leakage-free splits and report performance gaps?
- For multiCCA, have you tried orthogonality constraints (e.g., Procrustes) or summing the top-k canonical correlations in multiQVEC-CCA? How do results and correlations change?
- Can you report per-language or per-pair results (especially non-English pairs) to assess the effect of the English pivot on non-English language pairs?
- How sensitive are multiCCA/multiCluster to dictionary size/noise and the alignment threshold τ? Any ablations on dictionary coverage vs. performance?
- For the correlation analysis, can you provide confidence intervals (e.g., bootstrap) and test whether correlations hold across different embedding dimensionalities and corpora?

Additional comments
- The portal is valuable; please comment on maintenance/longevity plans. Given RCV licensing, consider adding alternative open corpora for document classification.
- Given the coverage confound, providing intersection-only scores in the main text (not only supplement) would aid fair comparison.
- The discussion would benefit from related alignment methods (orthogonal mapping/Procrustes) and hubness mitigation, even if contemporaneous.

Overall, this is a solid and useful contribution, particularly on evaluation methodology and scalable, simple multilingual embeddings. Addressing baseline fairness and evaluation leakage would significantly strengthen the paper.
