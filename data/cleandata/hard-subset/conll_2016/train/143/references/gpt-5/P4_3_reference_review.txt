Summary and main contributions
This paper studies massively multilingual word embeddings built from monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, and proposes two simple estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA). It also introduces multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal. Experiments compare multiple embedding estimation methods across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks in settings with 12 and 59 languages and analyze correlations between intrinsic metrics and downstream performance.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA (a CCA-based, basis-invariant, normalized intrinsic metric) and its multilingual extension, plus a correlation analysis showing higher agreement with downstream tasks than commonly used word similarity/translation metrics.
2) Empirical study at scale: a systematic comparison of four multilingual embedding approaches (multiCCA, multiCluster, a skip-gram parallel-only variant, and translation invariance) over multiple tasks, languages (12 and 59), and metrics, with attention to coverage effects.
3) Practical resources: an evaluation portal and trained models/data to facilitate standardized, reproducible benchmarking.

The estimation methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) are straightforward extensions of prior bilingual work; novelty lies more in the breadth of the evaluation and the intrinsic metric.

Strengths
- Clear, useful intrinsic metric with better downstream correlation: The paper identifies two key shortcomings of QVEC (basis dependence and dimensionality bias) and addresses both via CCA, yielding multiQVEC-CCA. The reported Pearson correlations (e.g., 0.896 with document classification) substantially exceed those of word similarity/translation, supporting the claim that multiQVEC(-CCA) better reflects downstream utility. The metric is computationally light and multilingual by construction.
- Thorough, transparent experimental design: The study evaluates four estimation methods across nine metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic) and two language regimes (12 and 59). Important training/evaluation details are provided (dimensionality, context windows, epochs, clustering threshold τ, coverage reporting). The paper explicitly discusses coverage confounds and reports coverage percentages, with intersection analyses in supplementary material.
- Scalable, resource-light embedding estimation: The dictionary-based methods only require monolingual data plus pairwise dictionaries (no parallel corpora), enabling training for 59 languages. multiCCA, in particular, shows consistently strong results and better coverage than the translation-invariance baseline, and is competitive on extrinsic tasks.
- Practical impact via standardization: The evaluation portal, along with downloadable code and models, lowers the barrier for future work and encourages consistent, comparable results across methods and languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in estimation: multiCCA is a direct extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) using English as a pivot, and multiCluster reduces to clustering translationally linked types and training skip-gram on cluster IDs. No new learning objective or modeling principle is introduced; contributions here are mainly pragmatic/scaling.
- Correlation study scope and generality: The analysis uses 17 embeddings and only three languages (en/it/da) for intrinsic–extrinsic correlations. While results are suggestive, the sample is small and the downstream setups are constrained (e.g., parsing with no POS/morphology and frozen embeddings), which may limit generalizability. Notably, multiQVEC-CCA correlates well with document classification but only modestly with parsing (0.273).
- Potential bias and reproducibility concerns in 59-language setup: For many languages, both training and evaluation dictionaries rely on Google Translate. This raises questions about independence between training and test resources (possible leakage) and about reproducibility/licensing if such dictionaries cannot be redistributed. In contrast, the 12-language setting uses Europarl/Wiktionary with clearer provenance.
- Unproven assumptions and numerical aspects in multiCCA: The paper states the CCA projection matrices are “guaranteed to be non-singular,” but full-rank conditions depend on dictionary size/rank; this may not always hold. The composition ECCA(m, v) = T^{-1}_{en→m,en} T_{m→m,en} E^m(v) could be numerically unstable; no analysis of stability, regularization, or sensitivity to pivot choice is provided.
- Limited analysis of clustering side-effects: The paper acknowledges that multiCluster exacerbates polysemy/ambiguity and caps cluster sizes, but provides no quantitative analysis of cluster distributions, polysemy rates, or sensitivity to τ and size caps. This limits diagnostic insight into when/why multiCluster degrades with 59 languages.

Questions to authors (for clarification)
- CCA rank and stability: Under what conditions are Tm→m,en and Ten→m,en non-singular in practice? Do you regularize CCA, reduce dimensionality to the empirical rank, or apply whitening? How sensitive are results to dictionary size/rank?
- Pivot sensitivity: Why fix English as the pivot? Have you tested alternative pivots or multi-pivot schemes, and how do results change for distant language pairs?
- Dictionary independence: For the 59-language experiments, are training and evaluation dictionaries independent when both are derived from Google Translate? Can you quantify potential overlap/leakage and its impact on word translation scores? Can you release (or regenerate) these dictionaries for reproducibility?
- Cluster diagnostics: Please report cluster size histograms, rate of cross-lingual polysemy in clusters, and sensitivity to τ/max size. Does filtering noisy dictionary links improve multiCluster at 59 languages?
- Correlation robustness: Can you expand the correlation study to more languages and more diverse embeddings (e.g., varying dimensionality, training corpora), and report confidence intervals or statistical tests on correlation differences?
- Downstream breadth: Beyond RCV doc classification and POS-less parsing, can you include additional tasks (e.g., NER, POS-tagging with standard features) to assess the metrics’ predictive power in more typical settings?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound in its core methods and evaluations, and offers valuable standardization and insight into intrinsic metrics. The main novelty lies in evaluation methodology and scale, while the proposed estimation methods are incremental.
