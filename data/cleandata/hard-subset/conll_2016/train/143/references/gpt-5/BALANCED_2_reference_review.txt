Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes simple, dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual word embeddings and new intrinsic evaluation metrics designed to correlate with downstream performance.

- Contribution 1: Two estimation methods for multilingual embeddings that do not require parallel corpora: multiCCA (a CCA-based projection from monolingual spaces into a shared English space) and multiCluster (dictionary-induced clustering followed by monolingual training on cluster IDs). Embeddings are trained for up to 59 languages.
- Contribution 2: New intrinsic evaluation metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, extending QVEC to multilingual settings and addressing QVEC’s basis-variance and dimensionality issues via CCA. The paper provides a correlation analysis showing these metrics correlate better with document classification and dependency parsing than word similarity/translation.
- Contribution 3: A web portal and benchmark suite for evaluating multilingual embeddings across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, plus released resources.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable estimation without parallel corpora: multiCCA is a clean, scalable way to map monolingual embeddings into a shared space using only bilingual lexicons, demonstrated at 59-language scale. This is useful where parallel corpora are scarce, and the paper reports consistently strong performance and coverage for multiCCA.
- Better-motivated intrinsic metrics with empirical validation: The paper clearly articulates QVEC’s shortcomings and proposes QVEC-CCA as a basis-invariant, normalized alternative. The multilingual extensions are natural, and the reported correlations (multiQVEC-CCA for document classification, multiQVEC for parsing) are notably stronger than word similarity or word translation, offering the community more predictive intrinsic metrics.
- Breadth of evaluation and attention to coverage: The study spans multiple intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks and reports coverage, discussing the confound of OOV handling. The supplementary intersection analysis addresses this partially, which is a thoughtful experimental detail often overlooked.
- Community impact via resources: The evaluation portal, datasets, trained embeddings, and standardized evaluation recipes can materially improve comparability and reproducibility in multilingual embedding research.
- Insight on clustering across many languages: The empirical observation that hard cross-lingual clustering exacerbates polysemy and degrades quality as the number of languages grows is informative and useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of estimation methods: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui & Dyer (2014) to a multilingual setting by using English as a pivot and composing CCA projections; multiCluster is a simple connected-components clustering over dictionary edges. The methodological contribution is modest, with little theoretical analysis or new modeling ideas beyond engineering choices.
- Small-scale correlation study and limited linguistic diversity: The correlation analysis involves 17 models across three (closely related) languages and two downstream tasks, which may be underpowered. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. This raises questions about generalizability, especially beyond European languages and to other tasks (e.g., NER, QA).
- Potentially unfair or limited baselines: The multiSkip baseline is trained only on parallel data (omitting monolingual corpora used in prior work), possibly handicapping it. The translation-invariance baseline uses a small context window due to computational constraints, which may not be optimal. These choices could affect rankings and weaken comparative claims.
- Use of Google Translate for training and evaluation in the 59-language setup: Dictionaries for 47 languages and the word-translation evaluation are both derived from Google Translate, raising concerns about bias (training–test coupling), licensing, and replicability. The extent of overlap between training lexicons and test pairs is unclear.
- Extrinsic setup limits external validity: For parsing and classification, embeddings are frozen and other linguistic features are removed; while this isolates embedding quality, it may not reflect typical downstream usage where fine-tuning and richer features are beneficial. Moreover, no extrinsic evaluation is reported for the 59-language scenario.
- Methodological clarifications missing: The multiCCA projection composition (using T−1_en→m,en T_m→m,en) is unusual relative to the simpler “project into English” approach; more derivation or justification is needed. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., dictionary threshold τ, cluster size limits, pivot choice) are not reported.

Questions to authors
- multiCCA formulation: Why is ECCA(m,v) defined via T−1_en→m,en T_m→m,en Em(v) rather than directly projecting into English with a single learned mapping? Please provide a derivation and discuss numerical stability and conditioning of the inverse.
- Training–test leakage: For the 59-language setting, what overlap exists between Google-Translate-derived training dictionaries and the Google-Translate-based evaluation pairs? Can you report results with disjoint vocabularies to rule out bias?
- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive are results to (a) the dictionary threshold τ for multiCluster, (b) maximum cluster size, and (c) the choice of pivot language in multiCCA? Have you explored a multi-pivot or joint CCA across multiple languages?
- Baseline configuration: Can you provide stronger baselines by (a) using monolingual data with multiSkip (as in prior work) and (b) matching window sizes/hyperparameters for translation-invariance to reduce potential handicaps?
- Correlation study robustness: Can you expand the correlation analysis to more languages, more diverse tasks, and report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap CIs) to substantiate the claimed superiority of multiQVEC(-CCA)?
- Resource reliance: Given that supersense annotations are scarce and inconsistent across languages, do you have evidence that multiQVEC-CCA with universal POS/morphological features yields similar correlations? Any plans to release an aligned, multilingual supersense resource?

Additional comments and suggestions for improvement
- Include an ablation separating effects of dictionary size/quality on multiCCA performance, especially with noisy MT-derived lexicons.
- Report extrinsic results for the 59-language set (even on a subset of tasks) to support claims about scalability beyond intrinsic metrics.
- Consider evaluating more modern downstream tasks and settings where embeddings are fine-tuned, to assess robustness of the intrinsic–extrinsic correlation.
- Ensure long-term availability of the portal by mirroring resources and providing an offline evaluation toolkit.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. The methodological novelty is limited, but the work offers practical, scalable multilingual embeddings, more predictive intrinsic metrics, and community resources likely to have positive impact. Strengthening baselines and expanding the correlation study would solidify the paper.
