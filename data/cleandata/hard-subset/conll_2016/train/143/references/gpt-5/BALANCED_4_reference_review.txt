Summary and contributions
This paper studies multilingual word embeddings and presents: 
1) Two dictionary-based estimation methods: multiCCA (a CCA-based projection into an English pivot space) and multiCluster (cluster-and-share embeddings across translation-equivalence classes), trained for up to 59 languages using monolingual data plus bilingual dictionaries or parallel text when available.
2) New intrinsic evaluation measures for multilingual embeddings: multiQVEC (an extension of QVEC) and multiQVEC-CCA (a CCA-based, basis-invariant alternative), along with an analysis of their correlation with downstream tasks.
3) An evaluation portal and released resources (embeddings, datasets) to facilitate standardized, replicable evaluation.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
- An empirical framework and resources (embeddings for 59 languages, evaluation portal) with a systematic comparison of estimation methods across multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
- A principled intrinsic metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that improves over QVEC and shows stronger correlation with downstream performance than commonly used similarity/translation metrics.
- A simple but effective multilingual extension of bilingual CCA (multiCCA).

Strengths
- Breadth and resource value: Training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages, plus providing an evaluation portal, is a substantial service to the community. The breadth enables insights into scalability (e.g., multiCluster’s degradation with many languages) and encourages replicability and future benchmarking.
- Evaluation design and analysis: The paper compares four families of methods and reports on nine metrics including two extrinsic tasks. The authors explicitly discuss coverage effects, provide intersection-vocabulary analyses in the supplement, and investigate correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The finding that multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA correlate better with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation is practically important.
- Clear methodological exposition: The multiCCA extension is well motivated and easy to reproduce; multiCluster is clearly described and helps probe the cost of forced cross-lingual sharing. The paper situates baselines fairly and explains training details and constraints.
- Empirical takeaways with actionable guidance: For 12 languages, dictionary-based methods are competitive or superior on several metrics, including both extrinsic tasks, while translation-invariance excels on translation and similarity. For 59 languages, multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster, highlighting the polysemy/ambiguity cost of cluster sharing. These are useful, non-trivial observations for practitioners.
- Improved intrinsic metric: QVEC-CCA addresses two weaknesses of QVEC (basis dependence and dimensionality dependence), and the multilingual variants are carefully constructed from supersense annotations.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCCA is a straightforward pivot-space extension of prior bilingual CCA; multiCluster is a simplistic cluster-sharing scheme that amplifies polysemy. The novelty lies more in scale and evaluation than in modeling. Strengthen by adding more sophisticated multilingual alignment (e.g., joint multi-CCA, shared latent spaces without a single pivot) or context-sensitive variants.
- Fairness of baselines and confounds: The multiSkip implementation uses only parallel data (no monolingual), while other methods benefit from large monolingual corpora; translation-invariance uses a smaller context window due to computational limits. These discrepancies can disadvantage baselines. A stronger comparison would either equip multiSkip with monolingual signals (e.g., BilBOWA-style) or ablate monolingual data for other methods; and harmonize hyperparameters where feasible.
- Dictionary and evaluation quality: For 47 languages, dictionaries are built with Google Translate; evaluation for 59 languages also uses Google Translate where Wiktionary is unavailable. This introduces noise and potential circularity (training and test artifacts from the same MT system). The reliability of translation evaluations and cross-lingual alignment learned from these dictionaries is uncertain, particularly for low-resource languages. An analysis of robustness to dictionary noise/size and alternate sources (e.g., PanLex, Wikidata) would strengthen claims.
- Correlation study scope: The intrinsic–extrinsic correlation is computed over 17 embeddings and only three languages (en, it, da), with two extrinsic tasks. While the results are suggestive, they may not generalize across languages or tasks. Notably, multiQVEC-CCA correlates very strongly with document classification (0.896) but weakly with parsing (0.273). More languages, tasks (e.g., NER, POS, XNLI), and significance/confidence intervals would bolster conclusions.
- Extrinsic setups are atypical: For parsing, POS/morphological features are removed and embeddings are frozen, forcing reliance on embeddings. While this isolates embedding quality, it diverges from realistic usage, potentially changing method rankings. Reporting auxiliary results with standard feature sets and fine-tuning would increase external validity.

Questions to authors
- Baseline parity: Can you provide results where multiSkip (or a BilBOWA-like model) also leverages monolingual corpora, and harmonize context windows and training steps across methods?
- Dictionary sensitivity: How do multiCCA and multiCluster performance vary with dictionary size/precision? Could you report ablations varying τ and dictionary noise, and alternative dictionary sources (PanLex/Wikidata) for a subset of languages?
- Pivot choice: How sensitive is multiCCA to the English pivot? If you pivot via another high-resource language (e.g., Spanish), do downstream results change materially?
- Correlation robustness: Can you extend the correlation analysis to more languages and additional extrinsic tasks, and report confidence intervals/significance? Any insight into why multiQVEC-CCA correlates much less with parsing?
- Evaluation leakage: For languages where Google Translate is used to construct both training dictionaries and test word-translation pairs, how do you mitigate bias? Can you provide a human-verified subset to validate the translation metric?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The methodological novelty is modest, but the empirical study, improved intrinsic metric with demonstrated correlation advantages, and the valuable resources/portal collectively make a strong case for impact. Addressing fairness of baselines, dictionary quality, and expanding the correlation study would further strengthen the work.
