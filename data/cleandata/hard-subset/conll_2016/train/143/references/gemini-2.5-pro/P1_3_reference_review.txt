### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a comprehensive study on creating and evaluating massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors introduce two novel dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, capable of generating shared embeddings for 59 languages. A significant portion of the work is dedicated to evaluation methodology. The authors propose multiQVEC-CCA, an intrinsic evaluation metric that addresses theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and demonstrates a much stronger correlation with performance on downstream tasks. The work is supported by extensive experiments comparing the proposed methods and metrics against strong baselines on a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. Finally, the authors contribute a public web portal for standardized evaluation, along with the trained embeddings and datasets, to facilitate future research.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A principled evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The development of multiQVEC-CCA is a strong theoretical contribution, and the empirical analysis showing its superior correlation with extrinsic task performance (Table 1) provides a crucial, data-backed recommendation for how the field should evaluate multilingual representations going forward.
2.  **Significant and highly valuable resources for the community.** The release of an easy-to-use evaluation portal, benchmark datasets, and pre-trained embeddings for 59 languages represents a major practical contribution that will lower the barrier to entry, promote reproducibility, and accelerate progress in multilingual NLP.
3.  **Effective and scalable dictionary-based embedding methods.** While simple, the proposed multiCCA and multiCluster methods demonstrate that high-quality multilingual embeddings can be learned for a very large number of languages without requiring parallel corpora, a resource that is scarce for most language pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough and insightful evaluation.** The paper's greatest strength is its focus on rigorous evaluation. The "meta-evaluation" that assesses the correlation between intrinsic metrics and extrinsic tasks (Sec 5.1) is particularly valuable. It convincingly argues that the proposed multiQVEC-CCA is a more reliable proxy for downstream utility than commonly used metrics like word similarity or translation. The breadth of evaluation, covering nine different metrics across two language sets (12 and 59), is commendable.
2.  **High potential for community impact.** The development of the evaluation portal is an outstanding contribution. By providing a standardized platform for benchmarking, it addresses a major need in the field and will likely become a go-to resource for researchers. Releasing the 59-language embeddings further enhances the paper's impact, providing a useful off-the-shelf tool for multilingual applications.
3.  **Scalability and practicality of proposed methods.** The paper makes a strong case for dictionary-based methods as a practical path toward *massively* multilingual embeddings. By successfully training embeddings for 59 languages, the authors demonstrate the scalability of their approach, which relies on more widely available resources (monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries) than methods requiring large parallel corpora.
4.  **Clarity and organization.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivations are clear, the methods are explained concisely, and the experimental results are presented and analyzed effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unfair comparison to the multiSkip baseline.** My primary concern is the implementation of the multiSkip baseline. In footnote 4, the authors state that their version is trained *only* on parallel corpora, whereas the original works it is based on (e.g., Guo et al., 2016) also leverage monolingual corpora. This puts the baseline at a significant disadvantage, as it cannot learn from the much larger monolingual datasets that the authors' own methods rely on. Consequently, the poor performance of multiSkip in Table 3 may not be representative of methods that use parallel data, weakening the claim that the proposed dictionary-based methods are superior.
2.  **Limited novelty of the multiCCA method.** The paper candidly describes multiCCA as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of previous bilingual CCA-based work. The approach consists of repeatedly applying a known technique to project various languages into a single pivot language space (English). While its effectiveness and scalability are well demonstrated, the conceptual novelty of the method itself is modest.
3.  **Limited language diversity in the evaluation of new metrics.** The core claim that multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA correlate well with downstream tasks is supported by experiments on only three languages: English, Italian, and Danish. While these results are very promising, they are all for well-resourced European languages. The generalizability of this finding to more typologically diverse languages is not established, which is a limitation given the paper's focus on "massively multilingual" embeddings.
4.  **The confounding effect of vocabulary coverage.** The authors correctly identify that differences in vocabulary coverage across embedding sets complicate the interpretation of intrinsic evaluation scores (Table 3). Although this is a general problem in the field, it is particularly pronounced here, with the translation-invariance method covering less than a third of the vocabulary on some tasks. While the authors mention an analysis on the vocabulary intersection in the supplementary material, this issue is significant enough that it deserves a more central discussion, as it directly impacts the comparability of the reported scores.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the decision to implement the multiSkip baseline without using monolingual data, contrary to the original papers? Given that this is a key point of comparison, a stronger baseline would make your results more convincing. How do you believe the results in Table 3 would change if multiSkip was trained with both parallel and monolingual data?
2.  The multiCCA method uses English as the central pivot language. This choice could potentially introduce an English-centric bias into the shared embedding space. Did you investigate the effects of this choice, for instance, by using a different pivot language or by attempting to create a more neutral, non-pivoted shared space?
3.  You hypothesize that the performance drop of multiCluster from 12 to 59 languages is due to increased semantic ambiguity from larger clusters. This is a very interesting finding. Could you provide a more quantitative analysis to support this? For instance, is there a clear correlation between average cluster size and performance degradation on a held-out task?
