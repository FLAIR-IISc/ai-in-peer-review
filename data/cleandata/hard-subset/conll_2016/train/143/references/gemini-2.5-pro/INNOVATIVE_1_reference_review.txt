**Summary and Contributions**

The paper's primary contribution is a new framework for evaluating multilingual word embeddings, which represents a significant step forward for the field. This framework includes a novel intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which corrects fundamental theoretical flaws (basis invariance, score normalization) in prior work and, crucially, is shown to correlate much more strongly with downstream task performance than existing standard metrics. This contribution is instantiated in a publicly available web portal, a commendable and highly valuable resource that will lower the barrier to entry and promote standardized, reproducible research.

A secondary, but still important, contribution is the exploration of simple, dictionary-based methods for creating embeddings on a massive scale (59 languages). This demonstrates the feasibility of building shared semantic spaces for a large and diverse set of languages without the need for scarce parallel corpora, pushing the community towards a truly "massively multilingual" vision.

**Strengths**

1.  **A Bold and Necessary Rethink of Evaluation:** The paper's most significant strength is its critical take on existing evaluation practices. For too long, the community has relied on intrinsic metrics like word similarity that, as the authors convincingly show, have poor correlation with how these embeddings actually perform on downstream tasks. The proposal of multiQVEC-CCA is not merely an incremental improvement; it is a principled and methodologically sound fix to the flaws of its predecessor (QVEC). The reported correlation of 0.896 with document classification is compelling evidence that this metric provides a far more reliable signal of embedding quality. This is precisely the kind of foundational work that enables more ambitious and creative future research.

2.  **Ambitious Scale and an Outstanding Community Resource:** The authors' ambition to tackle 59 languages simultaneously is a welcome departure from the vast body of work that focuses only on bilingual or small-scale multilingual settings. This "massively multilingual" framing is a bold and important direction for the field. The creation of the evaluation portal is an exemplary contribution. By providing data, standardized tasks, and an easy-to-use interface, the authors are not just publishing a paper; they are building infrastructure that will benefit the entire research community. This commitment to reproducibility and shared progress is highly laudable.

3.  **Elegant and Conceptually Interesting Methods:** I particularly value the conceptual simplicity of the `multiCluster` method. The idea of identifying translational equivalence classes, replacing words with cluster IDs, and running a standard monolingual tool like `skipgram` is refreshingly direct and intuitive. While it has obvious limitations regarding ambiguity (which the authors acknowledge), it serves as a powerful and easily understood baseline for what is possible with minimal machinery. This kind of bold, simple approach is often more inspiring than a complex, overwrought model. The focus on dictionary-based methods is also a strategic and important choice, as it dramatically expands the number of languages that can be brought into the multilingual fold.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Embedding Models:** While I appreciate the simplicity of the proposed embedding methods, their conceptual novelty is a clear weakness. The authors themselves describe `multiCCA` as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of prior work. The `multiCluster` idea, while elegantly implemented, is also closely related to a long line of research in cross-lingual word clustering. The main novelty here is the application at an unprecedented scale, not the core algorithmic ideas themselves. Consequently, the paper's primary contribution lies firmly in the evaluation space, not in model development.

2.  **Confounding Factors in Experimental Comparison:** The experimental results in Table 3 do not paint a clear picture of the superiority of the proposed methods. The performance ranking changes depending on the evaluation metric, with the `translation-invariance` baseline outperforming the proposed methods on several intrinsic tasks. More troublingly, the large and inconsistent differences in vocabulary coverage across models (e.g., 53.2% for `multiCCA` vs 30.3% for `translation-invariance` on word translation) are a significant confounding variable. An evaluation on a smaller vocabulary where all models have coverage may not be representative, but it is necessary for a fair comparison. While the authors acknowledge this issue, its presence in the main results table weakens the conclusions that can be drawn about the relative quality of the embedding methods.

3.  **Dependency on Limited or Proprietary Resources:** The work's scalability is somewhat undermined by its dependencies. The 59-language experiments rely on dictionaries from Google Translate, a black-box commercial system, which complicates full reproducibility and analysis. More importantly, the paper's star contribution, the `multiQVEC-CCA` metric, is itself hamstrung by its reliance on manually annotated supersense tag resources, which exist for only a tiny fraction of the world's languages. This severely limits the metric's applicability in the very "massively multilingual" setting the paper champions. The authors' suggestion to use universal POS tags is a good one, but without empirical validation, `multiQVEC-CCA` remains a powerful tool for a very small club of languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The `multiCluster` approach is an intriguing idea that appears to suffer from sense ambiguity at large scale. Have you considered hybrid models that might mitigate this? For example, one could use the cluster IDs to initialize embeddings that are then fine-tuned on monolingual corpora, allowing words within a cluster (e.g., the English "bank" and German "Bank") to diverge and capture their language-specific nuances.

2.  The correlation results in Table 1 are the strongest argument for your new evaluation metric. However, the correlation with dependency parsing (0.273) is markedly lower than with document classification (0.896). What is your interpretation of this gap? Does it imply that `multiQVEC-CCA`, being based on supersense tags, is primarily a measure of semantic quality and may not be the best intrinsic proxy for tasks requiring more syntactic information?

3.  Given that the availability of supersense tags is a major bottleneck for your evaluation framework, have you performed any preliminary experiments using more widely available resources, such as universal POS tags, as you suggest in footnote 9? Demonstrating that a similar correlation holds for a syntax-oriented QVEC-CCA would significantly strengthen your claim of providing a general-purpose evaluation framework for the massively multilingual setting.
