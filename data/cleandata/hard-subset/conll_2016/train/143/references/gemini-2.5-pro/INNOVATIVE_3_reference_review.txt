**Summary and Contributions:**

This paper addresses the challenge of creating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, and demonstrate their scalability by training embeddings for 59 languages. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to the critical and often-overlooked problem of evaluation. The authors identify theoretical flaws in the existing `QVEC` metric, propose a superior alternative named `multiQVEC-CCA`, and validate it by showing a much stronger correlation with downstream task performance compared to previous intrinsic metrics. Finally, to facilitate future research, the authors introduce a public web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:
1.  The development and validation of `multiQVEC-CCA`, a novel intrinsic evaluation metric that addresses key theoretical shortcomings of prior work and demonstrates a substantially higher correlation with extrinsic task performance. This is a significant step forward for the methodology of the field.
2.  The creation of a public evaluation portal with standardized datasets and benchmarks. This is an invaluable piece of community infrastructure that will enable more rigorous and reproducible research on this topic.
3.  The proposal of two simple and highly scalable dictionary-based methods (`multiCluster` and `multiCCA`) that can generate embeddings for dozens of languages without requiring parallel corpora, a key practical advantage.

**Strengths:**

1.  **A Principled and Impactful Contribution to Evaluation:** The paper's most impressive contribution is its focus on improving evaluation methodology. The critique of `QVEC` (its lack of invariance to basis rotation and its unnormalized score) is sharp and accurate. The proposed solution, `QVEC-CCA`, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis, is an elegant and theoretically sound fix. The empirical results in Table 1, showing that `multiQVEC-CCA`'s correlation with document classification accuracy is 0.896 (compared to 0.386 for word similarity), provide compelling evidence that this is a far more reliable intrinsic proxy for downstream performance than what has been commonly used. This work has the potential to steer future research in more productive directions.

2.  **Boldness in Approach and Ambition:** The paper tackles the problem at a truly "massive" scale, moving beyond the typical bilingual or small-scale multilingual setups to 59 languages. This is commendable. Furthermore, the `multiCluster` method represents a bold conceptual leap. The idea of collapsing all words in a translation set into a single point in the vector space is a radical simplification. While its empirical performance is mixed, I highly value this kind of creative and audacious thinking that challenges standard assumptions.

3.  **Significant Community Benefit:** The development of the evaluation portal is an outstanding service to the research community. Standardizing evaluation is crucial for making genuine progress, and by providing data, metrics, and an easy-to-use interface, the authors are removing significant barriers to entry and enabling fair comparison. This type of contribution can often have a more lasting impact than any single model.

4.  **Pragmatism and Scalability:** The proposed dictionary-based methods are appealing for their simplicity and minimal data requirements. By relying only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries (which, as the authors show, can be generated automatically), their methods are applicable to a far wider range of languages than methods requiring large parallel corpora. The successful training of 59-language embeddings is a testament to this scalability.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Inconclusive Empirical Comparison of Methods:** The experimental results in Table 3 do not establish a clear superiority for the proposed embedding methods. The baseline `translation-invariance` method outperforms the proposed `multiCCA` on several intrinsic metrics, including the authors' own `multiQVEC-CCA`. While `multiCCA` performs well on the extrinsic tasks, the overall picture is murky, making it difficult to draw strong conclusions about which method is best. The authors rightly point out that differing vocabulary coverage confounds these comparisons, but this ultimately weakens the paper's claims about its proposed models.

2.  **Conceptual Limitations of `multiCluster`:** The `multiCluster` method, while conceptually interesting, suffers from a critical flaw: it forces all words in a translational cluster to share a single representation, thereby conflating their meanings. This completely ignores polysemy and subtle semantic differences between languages (e.g., Russian "mir" meaning both "world" and "peace"). The authors acknowledge that this "severely hurts the performance" in the 59-language setting, which calls into question the method's practical utility beyond being a simple baseline.

3.  **Incremental Novelty of `multiCCA`:** The `multiCCA` method is presented as a straightforward extension of a prior bilingual method (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). While applying it at scale is an achievement, the core technical idea is not a significant departure from existing work. The hub-and-spoke architecture, which pivots through English, is a pragmatic but potentially suboptimal design. It may introduce an English-centric bias and could be less effective for language pairs that are distant from English but close to each other.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The correlation study in Table 1 is very promising but is based on only three languages (English, Italian, Danish). How confident are you that these strong correlations will hold for more typologically diverse language families, where linguistic phenomena might not align as neatly?

2.  The `multiCCA` method relies on a single pivot language (English). Have you considered or can you comment on alternative architectures, such as a graph-based approach where projections are learned between all available language pairs and propagated, to avoid the potential information bottleneck and bias of a single hub?

3.  Given that vocabulary coverage appears to be a major confounding variable in your intrinsic evaluations, could you elaborate on the comparison conducted on the intersection of vocabularies (mentioned in the supplementary material)? While this subset might be biased, seeing those results would help disentangle performance from coverage effects. Does `multiCCA` consistently outperform `translation-invariance` in that controlled setting?
