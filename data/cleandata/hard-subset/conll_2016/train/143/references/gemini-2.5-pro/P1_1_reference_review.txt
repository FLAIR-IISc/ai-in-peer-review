**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based methods for training embeddings: multiCluster, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters before training, and multiCCA, which learns projections from monolingual embedding spaces into a shared pivot space (English). They apply these methods to create embeddings for up to 59 languages. A significant part of the paper is dedicated to evaluation methodology. The authors identify theoretical flaws in the QVEC metric and propose multiQVEC-CCA, a CCA-based alternative that is invariant to basis rotation and produces a normalized score. They conduct a thorough correlation analysis showing their proposed metric is a better predictor of downstream task performance than existing intrinsic metrics. Finally, they introduce a web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation and reproducible research in this area.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A principled and empirically validated intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA).** The careful analysis of existing metrics and the proposal of a theoretically sound and practically superior alternative is a major contribution that could have a lasting impact on how the community evaluates cross-lingual representations.
2.  **Demonstration of simple, scalable dictionary-based methods for massively multilingual embeddings.** The paper successfully scales embedding training to 59 languages using methods that rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, which are more widely available than parallel text. The finding that these simpler methods are competitive with, and sometimes superior to, more complex parallel-data-based methods is an important result.
3.  **A valuable community resource in the form of an evaluation portal.** By providing data, benchmarks, and an easy-to-use evaluation service, the authors have made a significant practical contribution that will foster future research.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong and insightful evaluation methodology.** The highlight of this paper is the work on evaluation. The critique of QVEC is sharp and accurate, and the proposed QVEC-CCA solution is elegant. The correlation analysis in Section 5.1 provides compelling evidence that multiQVEC-CCA is a far more reliable proxy for extrinsic performance than commonly used metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy. This addresses a critical need in the field for better intrinsic evaluation.
2.  **Impressive scale and practical methods.** The paper truly delivers on the "massively multilingual" promise by training embeddings for 59 languages. The proposed methods, particularly multiCCA, are simple and scalable, relying on more accessible resources (dictionaries) than large parallel corpora. This makes the approach practical for a wider range of the world's languages.
3.  **Thorough and careful experimental design.** The authors compare their methods against relevant baselines across a diverse suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The experiments are well-designed, and the paper shows a high degree of scientific rigor. For instance, the discussion and handling of vocabulary coverage differences during evaluation (final paragraph of §5.2) is a thoughtful detail that strengthens the validity of the results.
4.  **Significant contribution to reproducible research.** The development of the web portal is an excellent contribution to the community. Standardizing evaluation is crucial for making progress in a field, and this portal provides the infrastructure to do so.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited technical novelty of the embedding methods.** The proposed embedding methods, while effective, are presented as straightforward extensions of prior work. MultiCCA is an iterative application of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014) using a pivot language, and multiCluster is a clever but simple combination of graph clustering and standard skip-gram training. The primary contribution here is in the application and scaling, not in a fundamental algorithmic advance.
2.  **Unaddressed drawbacks of the pivot-based approach.** The multiCCA method, which performs best overall, relies entirely on English as a pivot space. This design may introduce an English-centric bias and could lead to degraded performance for language pairs that do not involve English, as information is passed through a potentially noisy pivot. The paper does not investigate or discuss the implications of this architectural choice.
3.  **Mixed results that lack a clear conclusion.** The evaluation results in Table 3 for the 12-language setting are inconsistent, with different methods excelling on different tasks. For example, multiCluster is best for parsing, while multiCCA is best for document classification, and the translation-invariance baseline is best on several intrinsic metrics. This makes it difficult to declare a clear winner or provide guidance on when to use which method. A deeper analysis of *why* certain models are better suited for specific tasks (e.g., syntactic vs. semantic) would greatly strengthen the paper's impact.
4.  **Practical limitations of the proposed evaluation metric.** While multiQVEC-CCA is shown to be a superior metric, its reliance on manually created supersense-tagged resources limits its current applicability to just three languages (English, Danish, Italian). This is a significant bottleneck that prevents it from being used to evaluate the very "massively multilingual" spaces the paper advocates for.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The multiCCA method's reliance on a single pivot language (English) seems like a potential limitation. Have you considered non-pivot-based architectures, for example, by jointly optimizing all projections toward a common latent space? How do you expect the choice of pivot might influence the quality of the resulting embeddings, especially for typologically distant languages?
2.  Your `multiSkip` baseline performs surprisingly poorly. In footnote 4, you state that your implementation uses only parallel corpora, whereas the original work by Coulmance et al. and Guo et al. also leverages monolingual data. This seems to put your implementation at a significant disadvantage. Could this design choice be the primary reason for its poor performance, and would including monolingual data make it a much more competitive baseline?
3.  The strong performance of `multiCluster` on dependency parsing (Table 3) is intriguing, especially given its tendency to conflate senses and its weaker performance elsewhere. Do you have a hypothesis for this? Could the hard clustering of translational equivalents enforce a syntactic consistency that is particularly beneficial for parsing models?
