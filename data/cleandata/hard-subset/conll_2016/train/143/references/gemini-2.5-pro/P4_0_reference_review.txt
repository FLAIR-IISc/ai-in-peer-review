### Summary

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based methods for estimating embeddings: `multiCluster`, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters and trains embeddings on these clusters, and `multiCCA`, which learns projections from monolingual embedding spaces into a shared space (English) using Canonical Correlation Analysis. A significant portion of the work is dedicated to evaluation methodology. The authors introduce `multiQVEC-CCA`, an intrinsic evaluation metric that improves upon the existing QVEC method by ensuring invariance to linear transformations and providing a normalized score. An empirical study shows that `multiQVEC-CCA` correlates better with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors present a public web portal to standardize the evaluation of multilingual embeddings, providing data, metrics, and an upload facility for researchers. Experiments are conducted on up to 59 languages, comparing the proposed methods against baselines and analyzing the effectiveness of the evaluation metrics.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, with strong theoretical and empirical justification.** The paper clearly identifies two fundamental weaknesses in the prior QVEC metric (lack of rotational invariance and an unnormalized score) and proposes a technically sound solution using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). The empirical analysis demonstrating that this new metric has a substantially higher correlation with downstream task performance (particularly document classification) than other intrinsic metrics is a significant finding for the field.
2.  **The development of a public, standardized evaluation platform for multilingual embeddings.** By creating a web portal that provides benchmark datasets, standardized evaluation scripts for a suite of metrics, and a facility for researchers to test their own embeddings, this work makes a substantial practical contribution to the community. This resource has the potential to improve the reproducibility and comparability of future research in this area.
3.  **Two scalable, dictionary-based methods for creating embeddings for a large number of languages.** The `multiCCA` and `multiCluster` methods leverage only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, resources that are more widely available than the parallel corpora required by many alternative methods. The successful application of these methods to generate embeddings for 59 languages demonstrates their scalability and practical utility for building "massively multilingual" resources.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong focus on principled evaluation.** The paper's most significant strength is its thorough and critical treatment of evaluation. The proposal of `multiQVEC-CCA` is well-motivated, addressing clear theoretical flaws in prior work. The experimental validation of evaluation metrics via correlation with extrinsic tasks (Table 1) is a rigorous approach that provides convincing evidence for the utility of the new metric and highlights the shortcomings of widely used metrics like word translation and cross-lingual similarity.
2.  **High potential for community impact.** The creation of the evaluation portal is a commendable effort that directly addresses the challenges of reproducibility and standardized comparison in NLP research. Providing open access to training data, evaluation datasets, and a suite of metrics in an easy-to-use platform will lower the barrier to entry for new researchers and help unify the evaluation practices in this subfield.
3.  **Comprehensive and large-scale experiments.** The empirical evaluation is extensive, covering two proposed methods and two baselines across nine different evaluation tasks (both intrinsic and extrinsic) and two language sets (12 and 59 languages). This breadth allows for a nuanced analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of different embedding techniques and provides a robust testbed for the proposed evaluation metrics.
4.  **Demonstrated scalability to many languages.** A key challenge in multilingual NLP is scaling methods beyond a handful of high-resource languages. By successfully training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages using dictionary-based methods, the paper makes a concrete step towards truly "massively multilingual" representations and demonstrates the practicality of its proposed techniques.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited technical novelty of the embedding estimation methods.** The proposed `multiCluster` and `multiCCA` methods are primarily incremental extensions of existing ideas. `multiCluster` is a fairly direct combination of cross-lingual word clustering and standard monolingual embedding training. `multiCCA` is presented by the authors as a "simple extension" of the bilingual CCA projection method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). While their application and evaluation in a massively multilingual setting is valuable, the core methodological contribution is not a significant departure from prior work.
2.  **Inconsistent model performance and confounding variables.** The experimental results in Table 3 do not point to a single superior method. The proposed dictionary-based methods excel on extrinsic tasks, while the translation-invariance baseline performs best on several intrinsic tasks. This makes it difficult to draw a definitive conclusion. Furthermore, the large variance in vocabulary coverage across models is a significant confounding factor. For instance, the translation-invariance method has substantially lower coverage than `multiCCA`, making a direct comparison of intrinsic scores potentially misleading, an issue the authors acknowledge but cannot fully resolve.
3.  **The proposed best-practice metric relies on limited resources.** The `multiQVEC-CCA` metric, which is shown to be the most reliable intrinsic measure, depends on the availability of manually annotated linguistic resources (specifically, supersense-tagged data). As the paper notes, such resources are available for only a very small number of languages, which severely limits the metric's applicability for evaluating embeddings in low-resource settings—precisely where multilingual models are often most needed.
4.  **Weak implementation of a key baseline.** The `multiSkip` baseline, intended to represent parallel-corpus-based methods, was implemented to use *only* parallel data and not additional monolingual data, contrary to the stronger models it is compared against (e.g., Guo et al., 2016). This design choice likely handicaps its performance, potentially creating an unfair comparison that inflates the relative performance of the proposed dictionary-based methods. Using a more faithful and stronger implementation of this baseline would have provided a more convincing evaluation.
