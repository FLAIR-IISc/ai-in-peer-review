### **Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The work presents three main contributions. First, it proposes two novel dictionary-based methods for estimating embeddings across many languages: multiCluster, which groups translation equivalents into clusters before training, and multiCCA, which learns projections from monolingual spaces into a shared English space. Second, it identifies theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC intrinsic evaluation metric (e.g., lack of rotational invariance) and proposes an improved version, multiQVEC-CCA, demonstrating through a correlation study that it is a better predictor of downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics. Third, it introduces a public web portal that serves as a benchmark for evaluating multilingual embeddings, providing data, standardized tasks, and an upload/evaluation service to facilitate future research. The authors test their methods on up to 59 languages and compare them against two baselines on a smaller set of 12 languages.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Principled and Empirically Validated Evaluation Framework for Multilingual Embeddings:** The most significant contribution is the critical analysis of existing intrinsic evaluation methods and the development of multiQVEC-CCA. By identifying the theoretical weaknesses of QVEC (sensitivity to basis rotation, unnormalized score) and proposing a CCA-based solution, the authors provide a more robust metric. Crucially, they validate this claim with a correlation analysis (Table 1) showing their proposed metric correlates much more strongly with extrinsic task performance (especially document classification) than standard metrics like word similarity or translation. This provides the community with a more reliable tool for intrinsic evaluation.

2.  **A Valuable Public Resource for Reproducibility and Benchmarking:** The development of the web portal for standardized evaluation is a major practical contribution. By providing datasets, evaluation scripts, and a common platform, the authors significantly lower the barrier for future work in this area, promoting replicability and enabling fair comparison between different embedding estimation techniques. This kind of infrastructure work is vital for advancing the field.

3.  **Effective Dictionary-Based Methods for Large-Scale Multilingual Embeddings:** The paper introduces two methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) that scale to a large number of languages (59 in their experiments) using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, which are more widely available than parallel corpora. The experiments show these methods are competitive, often outperforming parallel-data baselines on extrinsic tasks like parsing and document classification.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodologically Sound and Important Work on Evaluation:** The paper's focus on *how* to evaluate embeddings is a primary strength. Too often, new models are proposed with evaluation as an afterthought. Here, the authors dedicate a significant portion of the paper to improving the evaluation methodology itself. The justification for moving from QVEC to QVEC-CCA is clear, principled, and convincing. The correlation study provides strong empirical evidence to back up their theoretical arguments.

2.  **Ambitious Scale and Thorough Experimentation:** The authors live up to their "massively multilingual" claim by training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages. The experimental design is comprehensive, comparing four different estimation methods across a suite of nine distinct evaluation metrics, spanning both intrinsic (e.g., word similarity, QVEC-CCA) and extrinsic (parsing, document classification) tasks. This thoroughness provides a nuanced view of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

3.  **High Potential for Community Impact:** The release of the evaluation portal, along with the data and benchmarks, is a substantial contribution that will likely have a lasting impact. It provides a focal point for research in this area, making it easier for others to build on this work and compare new methods against established baselines on a level playing field.

4.  **Clarity and Honesty in Reporting:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors are transparent about the limitations of their work, such as the fact that their multiSkip implementation does not use monolingual data (unlike some prior work) and the confounding factor of vocabulary coverage in intrinsic evaluations. They make a good-faith effort to address the coverage issue by re-running experiments on the intersection of vocabularies.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Proposed Estimation Methods:** The core ideas behind multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively straightforward extensions of existing concepts. MultiCluster is based on the intuitive idea of merging translationally equivalent words, and multiCCA is presented as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). While these methods are shown to be effective, they are not major algorithmic innovations in themselves. The paper's strength lies more in the evaluation and large-scale application than in the novelty of the models.

2.  **Inconclusive and Potentially Contradictory Results in Method Comparison:** The results in Table 3 do not point to a single superior method. While the proposed dictionary-based methods excel on the extrinsic tasks, the translation-invariance baseline performs best on several intrinsic tasks, including the authors' own proposed multiQVEC-CCA. This raises a difficult question: If multiQVEC-CCA is the best intrinsic proxy for downstream performance (as argued in Section 5.1), why does a different method (translation-invariance) win on that metric, while the proposed methods win on the actual downstream tasks? This tension is not fully resolved and slightly undermines the otherwise strong narrative about the new evaluation metric.

3.  **Reliance on English as a Central Hub:** The multiCCA method is architecturally dependent on a single hub language (English) into which all other languages are projected. The paper does not discuss the potential typological biases or limitations of this hub-and-spoke model. This approach might implicitly force the semantic spaces of other languages to conform to the structure of English, potentially disadvantaging languages that are structurally distant from it.

4.  **Scalability Limitation of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** A key weakness of the proposed multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics is their reliance on manually-annotated linguistic resources (supersense tags), which are only available for a handful of languages. This creates a disconnect between the paper's goal of "massively multilingual" embeddings (59 languages) and its best evaluation metric, which can only be applied to a small subset (3 languages). The authors acknowledge this in a footnote but it remains a significant practical limitation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The results in Table 3 show that the translation-invariance method outperforms all others on the multiQVEC-CCA metric, yet your proposed methods (multiCluster/multiCCA) perform better on the extrinsic dependency parsing and document classification tasks. Given your argument that multiQVEC-CCA is a better correlate with downstream performance, this seems contradictory. Could you elaborate on why this discrepancy might be occurring? Does this suggest a limitation in multiQVEC-CCA or a specific property of the translation-invariance method that makes it well-suited for this particular intrinsic task but less so for the chosen extrinsic ones?

2.  The multiCCA approach relies on a single hub language (English). Have you considered the potential biases this introduces? How might the results change if a different language family (e.g., Romance or Slavic) was used as the hub, and could the framework be extended to a more decentralized model without a single privileged language?
