### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the important and growing area of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based methods (`multiCluster` and `multiCCA`) for inducing a shared embedding space for a large number of languages (59 in this work). These methods are resource-light, requiring only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, in contrast to methods that rely on scarcer parallel corpora. A second, and perhaps more significant, part of the paper is a rigorous focus on evaluation. The authors adapt an existing intrinsic metric (QVEC) for the multilingual setting and propose a new, theoretically grounded variant, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which is invariant to basis rotation. They conduct a meta-evaluation showing that their proposed intrinsic metrics correlate much more strongly with downstream task performance than commonly used metrics like word similarity and translation. Finally, they provide a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation for future research.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A significant advancement in the evaluation of multilingual embeddings.** The proposal of `multiQVEC-CCA` and the thorough correlation analysis demonstrating its superiority over existing intrinsic metrics is a major contribution. This work provides a path forward for more reliable and meaningful intrinsic evaluation, which is crucial for rapid model development.
2.  **Demonstration of effective and scalable dictionary-based methods.** The paper successfully shows that simple, dictionary-based methods like `multiCCA` can be scaled to dozens of languages and produce embeddings that are highly effective on downstream extrinsic tasks, often outperforming more complex models that require parallel data.
3.  **A valuable resource for the community.** The public evaluation portal, along with the benchmark data, is a commendable contribution that will lower the barrier to entry and promote reproducible research in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's strongest point is its treatment of evaluation. The authors rightly identify the weakness of existing intrinsic metrics and perform a meta-analysis (Table 1) to quantify this. The development of `multiQVEC-CCA` addresses fundamental flaws in QVEC (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) and the empirical result showing its high correlation (0.896) with document classification is compelling. This is a foundational contribution that could have a lasting impact on how the field evaluates multilingual representations.
2.  **Scalability and Practicality:** The work tackles a truly "massively multilingual" setting (59 languages), which is a step beyond much of the prior work focused on bilingual or few-language scenarios. The proposed methods, particularly `multiCCA`, are not only scalable but also practical, as they rely on bilingual dictionaries (even machine-translated ones) which are far more readily available than large, clean parallel corpora for most of the world's languages.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The authors provide a broad comparison across four different embedding methods, nine distinct evaluation metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic), and two language sets (12 and 59). This comprehensive setup provides a clear picture of the trade-offs between different approaches. The finding that dictionary-based methods can outperform parallel-data methods on extrinsic tasks like parsing and document classification is a key result.
4.  **Contribution of a Community Resource:** The creation of an open evaluation portal is an excellent service to the research community. By providing standardized data splits, evaluation scripts, and a simple interface, the authors are actively fostering better and more comparable research practices.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty of `multiCCA`:** The `multiCCA` method is presented as the clear winner of the two proposed approaches. However, as the authors note, it is a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The core technical innovation is modest. The chosen star-topology, with English as the pivot, is a straightforward but potentially problematic design. It implicitly centers the multilingual space around English, which may disadvantage languages typologically distant from English or introduce an English-centric bias. The implications of this architectural choice are not discussed.
2.  **Under-powered Baseline Comparison:** The `multiSkip` baseline is significantly handicapped by the authors' implementation choice to train it *only* on parallel corpora, excluding the large monolingual corpora used by other methods. The original papers this method is based on (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016) explicitly leverage both data types. This makes `multiSkip` a strawman argument, and its poor performance is unsurprising. A fairer comparison would require using monolingual data for all skip-gram-based models, which might have changed the relative rankings.
3.  **Confounding Effects of Vocabulary Coverage:** The authors acknowledge that differences in vocabulary coverage make direct comparison of intrinsic scores difficult, which is a critical issue. For example, in Table 3, the `translation-invariance` model has dramatically lower coverage on several tasks (e.g., 23% vs. 71% for monolingual similarity). While a comparison on the vocabulary intersection is mentioned as being in the supplementary material, this is a crucial analysis that should be in the main paper. Without it, the reader cannot be certain that the superior performance of `translation-invariance` on several intrinsic tasks isn't an artifact of it being evaluated on an "easier" subset of high-frequency words. The reason for its low coverage is also not explained.
4.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Results:** The results in Table 3 are mixed, with no single model winning across the board. `Translation-invariance` excels on several intrinsic metrics, while the proposed dictionary-based methods win on the extrinsic tasks. The paper reports this pattern but offers little explanation for *why* this might be the case. What properties are being captured by the `translation-invariance` objective that align well with intrinsic similarity but less so with downstream tasks? A deeper qualitative or quantitative analysis into what these different models are learning would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `multiCCA` approach relies on a single pivot language (English). Did you investigate the sensitivity of the results to this choice? For instance, how do results change if a different pivot language (e.g., German or French) is used? Could this pivot-based design introduce systematic biases that hurt performance on language pairs that do not include English?
2.  Could you please clarify why the `multiSkip` baseline was implemented without access to monolingual data, contrary to the cited original work? Given that this likely explains its poor performance, how does this affect your conclusions about the relative strengths of dictionary-based vs. parallel-corpus-based methods?
3.  The `translation-invariance` method shows very low vocabulary coverage on some tasks. Is this an inherent limitation of the method, or an artifact of the specific implementation or data used? Given its strong performance on the subset it does cover, understanding this limitation is crucial.
