### Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces methods for creating "massively multilingual" word embeddings, trained on up to 59 languages using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A new framework for the intrinsic evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The authors identify and correct fundamental theoretical flaws (basis dependence, lack of normalization) in the existing QVEC metric, proposing multiQVEC-CCA. They convincingly demonstrate that their proposed metric correlates much more strongly with downstream task performance than prior intrinsic metrics. This contribution is supported by the release of an open evaluation portal, a significant service to the community that will facilitate standardized and reproducible research.

2.  The proposal of two simple, dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings. The first, multiCluster, is a novel and surprisingly effective approach that clusters translationally equivalent words and learns embeddings over these multilingual clusters. The second, multiCCA, is a pragmatic hub-and-spoke extension of prior work on bilingual CCA. The authors demonstrate that these simple dictionary-based methods can be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, more complex models that require parallel corpora.

### Strengths
1.  **Significant Advance in Evaluation Methodology:** The strongest contribution of this paper is the work on evaluation. The critique of QVEC is sharp, well-reasoned, and the proposed QVEC-CCA solution is theoretically sound, addressing key issues of rotational invariance and normalization. The subsequent empirical validation in Table 1 is compelling; achieving a Pearson correlation of nearly 0.9 with a downstream task is a remarkable result and strongly suggests that multiQVEC-CCA is a far more reliable proxy for extrinsic performance than existing metrics like word similarity or translation. The accompanying web portal is a superb piece of research infrastructure that lowers the barrier to entry and encourages good scientific practice. This is exactly the kind of work that can shape the direction of a subfield.

2.  **Bold and Deceptively Simple `multiCluster` Method:** The multiCluster approach is a prime example of a bold, simple idea that yields surprisingly strong results. The concept of collapsing all translational equivalents into a single representation is, on its face, a radical simplification that should suffer from immense semantic ambiguity. Yet, against this intuition, it emerges as the top-performing method on both extrinsic tasks in the 12-language comparison (Table 3). This is a fascinating result that challenges the community's assumptions. While the paper doesn't fully explore *why* this simplistic model works so well, its effectiveness alone makes it a novel and valuable contribution worthy of further investigation. I value this kind of counter-intuitive finding highly.

3.  **Ambitious Scale and Practicality:** The paper tackles the problem of creating embeddings at a truly "massive" scale (59 languages), moving beyond the small-scale bilingual or pentalingual settings common in prior work. By focusing on methods that only require bilingual dictionaries (which can be easily obtained via tools like Google Translate), the authors provide a practical and scalable recipe for extending distributional semantics to a much wider range of languages, including those with fewer parallel resources. The release of the 59-language embeddings is in itself a valuable resource for the community.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of the `multiCCA` Method:** While the multiCCA method is pragmatic and performs well, its conceptual novelty is limited. The authors themselves describe it as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The contribution here lies more in the "hub-and-spoke" framework for applying it multilingually rather than in the core technique itself. As such, it feels more like a strong, well-engineered baseline than a groundbreaking new approach.

2.  **Inconclusive and Confounded Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation of the embedding methods in Table 3 presents a somewhat confusing picture, with different methods winning on different metrics. More troublingly, the comparison is confounded by the significant variance in vocabulary coverage across methods. For instance, the translation-invariance method shows very strong performance on intrinsic tasks but has drastically lower coverage than other methods. As the authors correctly note, this means scores are not directly comparable, as they are computed over different subsets of test items. While the paper includes a supplementary analysis on the vocabulary intersection, this key methodological weakness muddies the primary conclusion of which embedding method is "best."

3.  **Lack of Analysis on the Success of `multiCluster`:** The paper reports the surprising success of multiCluster on extrinsic tasks but offers little insight or analysis as to why. Given the method's inherent flaw of conflating different word senses into a single cluster representation, its strong performance is a puzzle. Is this because the downstream tasks chosen (document classification, dependency parsing) do not require fine-grained semantics? Or does the forced sharing of a vector act as a powerful form of regularization? A deeper discussion of this counter-intuitive result would have significantly strengthened the paper.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The most intriguing result is the strong performance of the conceptually simple `multiCluster` method on extrinsic tasks, despite the obvious problem of semantic ambiguity it introduces. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for why this method works so well? Have you performed any analysis (e.g., on specific phenomena in the parsing task) that might shed light on where its strengths lie and where its weaknesses become more apparent?

2.  The evaluation portal is an excellent contribution. Could you briefly comment on the plans for its long-term maintenance and potential for expansion? For instance, will it be possible for the community to contribute new evaluation tasks or datasets directly via the linked GitHub repository?
