### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors propose two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, that primarily rely on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, making them scalable to many languages without requiring parallel text. To address the lack of standardized evaluation, the paper introduces a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which improves upon prior work (QVEC) by being invariant to linear transformations and normalized. A key finding is that this new metric, along with its predecessor multiQVEC, correlates much more strongly with downstream task performance (document classification, dependency parsing) than commonly used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and word translation. Finally, the authors present a public web portal for standardized evaluation, providing data, trained models, and a platform for researchers to test their own embeddings. The work includes experiments training embeddings for up to 59 languages and evaluating them across a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's strongest contribution is its rigorous focus on evaluation. It introduces multiQVEC-CCA, an improved intrinsic metric, and empirically demonstrates through correlation analysis that it is a better proxy for downstream performance than existing standard metrics. This is a significant finding for the field. The creation of an easy-to-use public evaluation portal that bundles datasets and metrics is a highly valuable resource that will facilitate reproducible research and lower the barrier to entry for new work in this area.
2.  **Scalable, dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings.** The paper proposes two methods, multiCCA and multiCluster, for learning embeddings across many languages. These methods are notable for their ability to scale to dozens of languages using widely available resources (monolingual text and dictionaries) instead of scarce parallel corpora. The experiments demonstrate that these methods are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, more complex methods that require parallel data.
3.  **The release of pre-trained embeddings for 59 languages.** This is a practical and valuable resource for the NLP community, enabling researchers to apply multilingual representations in a wide range of downstream tasks and languages, particularly those for which such resources were not previously available.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a critical need for better evaluation.** The paper makes a compelling case that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings (e.g., cross-lingual word similarity) are poor predictors of performance on downstream tasks. By introducing multiQVEC-CCA and providing a thorough correlation analysis (Table 1), the authors provide a much-needed course correction for the field. The finding that multiQVEC-CCA has a correlation of 0.896 with document classification is particularly striking.
2.  **Excellent contribution to community infrastructure.** The development and release of the evaluation portal is a standout feature of this work. By providing a centralized platform for benchmarking, along with the associated datasets, the authors are making a substantial contribution to the reproducibility and standardization of research in this domain. This kind of infrastructure work is often overlooked but is crucial for scientific progress.
3.  **Scalability to a large number of languages.** Much prior work has focused on the bilingual or few-language case. This paper's ambition to create embeddings for 59 languages is commendable and pushes the boundaries of the field. The proposed dictionary-based methods are well-suited for this "massively multilingual" setting, as they do not depend on parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for language coverage.
4.  **Thorough and rigorous experimentation.** The authors evaluate their methods and baselines across a wide array of nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The experimental design is sound, and the paper includes careful analysis, such as acknowledging and addressing the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation scores. This demonstrates a high level of scientific rigor.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty of the proposed estimation methods.** While effective, the proposed methods are methodologically straightforward. The authors themselves describe multiCCA as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of existing bilingual CCA-based methods. It essentially applies a known technique in a one-vs-all fashion against a pivot language. MultiCluster is more novel, but its performance is shown to be inconsistent and degrades significantly in the 59-language setting due to semantic ambiguity. The primary value of the paper lies more in its evaluation framework and empirical results than in deep methodological innovation.
2.  **Undeclared reliance on a pivot language and potential biases.** The multiCCA method relies on English as a central pivot language, projecting all other languages into the pre-trained English vector space. This design choice has potential drawbacks that are not discussed. The resulting multilingual space may be biased towards English-centric semantics, and the quality of embeddings for any given language is contingent on the quality of its bilingual dictionary with English. An analysis of the effect of the pivot language choice would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Inconclusive comparison of embedding methods.** The results in Table 3 do not present a clear winner. The proposed multiCCA method performs well on extrinsic tasks, but the translation-invariance baseline is superior on several intrinsic metrics, including word translation and multiQVEC-CCA. MultiCluster, the other proposed method, is the top performer on dependency parsing but struggles elsewhere. This makes it difficult to draw a strong conclusion about which method is definitively better, diluting the paper's message regarding its proposed estimation techniques.
4.  **Potentially weakened baseline comparison.** The authors state that their implementation of the multiSkip baseline does not use monolingual data, unlike the work it is based on (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016). This puts the baseline at a significant disadvantage, as it cannot learn from the large monolingual corpora that all other methods leverage. This may explain its poor performance across most tasks and weakens the claims of superiority for the proposed methods.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the multiSkip baseline: Could you clarify the reasoning for implementing it without leveraging monolingual data, contrary to prior work? Given that this likely hampers its performance, how would you expect the comparison in Table 3 to change if multiSkip were allowed to train on the same monolingual corpora as the other methods?
2.  Regarding the pivot-based approach in multiCCA: Have you investigated the impact of the choice of pivot language? How might the quality of the multilingual space be affected if a language other than English was used as the pivot, or if a non-pivot, all-pairs approach was taken? Does this approach disadvantage language pairs that do not include English?
3.  The performance of multiCluster declines sharply when scaling from 12 to 59 languages, which you attribute to increased semantic ambiguity from clustering translation equivalents. Does this suggest a fundamental scalability limitation of the method? Could you elaborate on potential ways to mitigate this, for example, by incorporating a form of word sense disambiguation prior to clustering?
