### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings, i.e., shared vector spaces for a large number of languages.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are, in order of significance:

1.  **A rigorous evaluation methodology for multilingual embeddings:** The authors identify key theoretical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric (variance to basis rotation, lack of normalization) and propose an improved, CCA-based version, multiQVEC-CCA, that resolves these issues. Crucially, they provide strong empirical evidence (Table 1) that their proposed intrinsic metrics correlate much better with downstream task performance than commonly used metrics like word similarity and translation.
2.  **A valuable public resource for the community:** The paper introduces a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. By providing data, standardized test sets, and an easy-to-use interface, this portal significantly lowers the barrier for future research, promotes reproducibility, and facilitates fair comparison between different methods.
3.  **A large-scale empirical study of multilingual embedding techniques:** The paper introduces two scalable, dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and compares them against two parallel-corpus-based methods across an impressive 59 languages. While the novelty of the methods themselves is modest, their application and evaluation at this scale provide a valuable benchmark and new insights into the trade-offs between different approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong focus on evaluation:** The most significant strength of this paper is its critical and principled approach to evaluation. The NLP community often relies on intrinsic metrics (like word similarity) that have a tenuous connection to real-world performance. This paper's correlation analysis (Sec 5.1) is a compelling demonstration that existing metrics are poor proxies for downstream utility. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA is not just a new metric but a well-motivated improvement over prior work, addressing clear mathematical shortcomings of QVEC. This contribution has the potential to guide future research in the area toward more meaningful objectives.
2.  **Excellent contribution to the community:** The development of the evaluation portal is a standout contribution. Such resources are vital for scientific progress. By centralizing datasets and evaluation scripts, the authors are fostering a more robust and reproducible research ecosystem for this important subfield. This goes beyond a single-paper contribution and provides lasting value.
3.  **Impressive scale and ambition:** The experiments are conducted on a "massively multilingual" scale (up to 59 languages), which is a significant step up from the bilingual or few-language settings common in prior work. This large-scale experiment provides a much more demanding and realistic testbed for the proposed methods and yields more generalizable conclusions.
4.  **Thoroughness of empirical comparison:** The authors compare four different methods across a suite of nine distinct evaluation metrics, including two extrinsic tasks. This multifaceted evaluation provides a nuanced view of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, highlighting that no single method dominates across all criteria. The reporting of vocabulary coverage is also a good practice that adds transparency to the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of proposed estimation methods:** The paper frames multiCluster and multiCCA as key contributions, but their algorithmic novelty is limited. The authors themselves describe multiCCA as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of a prior bilingual method. MultiCluster is an intuitive approach based on graph connected components. While their application to a large number of languages is valuable, they feel more like well-executed and scalable baselines rather than fundamentally new techniques.
2.  **Inconsistent results and lack of clear take-home message for methods:** The results in Table 3, which compares all four methods, are messy and do not clearly support the superiority of the proposed methods. The dictionary-based methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) are outperformed by the translation-invariance baseline on four of the nine intrinsic metrics. Furthermore, multiCluster is the best on dependency parsing, while it ties with multiCCA on document classification. This inconsistency makes it difficult to draw a clear conclusion about which method to prefer. The paper acknowledges the inconsistency but offers little analysis or intuition as to *why* certain methods excel on certain tasks (e.g., why might a clustering approach be better for a syntactic task like parsing?).
3.  **Confounding effect of vocabulary coverage is not fully addressed:** The authors correctly identify that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias intrinsic evaluations. Their solution is to report coverage and provide a re-evaluation on the vocabulary intersection in the supplementary material. However, this is a critical confounding variable. For a truly fair comparison, the results on the vocabulary intersection should have been presented and analyzed in the main paper, as this is the only setting where the intrinsic scores are directly comparable. Hiding these results in the supplement weakens the main comparison.
4.  **The `multiSkip` baseline may be unfairly disadvantaged:** The authors note in a footnote that their implementation of `multiSkip` is trained only on parallel corpora, whereas the original methods it is based on (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016) also use monolingual corpora. This is a significant omission, as it denies the baseline access to the vast amount of monolingual data that the dictionary-based methods leverage. This likely explains its poor performance and makes it a somewhat weak baseline for comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance of the four estimation methods in Table 3 is highly variable across tasks. Could you offer more analysis or intuition for these results? For instance, why do you think the `multiCluster` method, which conflates many words into a single representation, performs best on the syntactically-driven dependency parsing task? Conversely, why does the `translation-invariance` method dominate several of the lexical semantic intrinsic tasks?
2.  Given the significant confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic metrics, would it not be more principled to present the results on the intersection of vocabularies in the main paper's Table 3, rather than in the supplement? How much do the rankings and conclusions change when this stricter comparison is made?
3.  The `multiSkip` baseline was implemented without using monolingual data, which deviates from the original work it is based on. How much of its poor performance do you attribute to this design choice, and do you believe a more faithful implementation would be more competitive with your proposed dictionary-based methods?
