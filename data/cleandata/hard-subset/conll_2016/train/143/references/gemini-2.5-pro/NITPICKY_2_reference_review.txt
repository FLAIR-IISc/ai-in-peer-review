**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents an extensive empirical study of methods for creating multilingual word embeddings. The primary contributions, as I see them, are:
1.  An empirical comparison of four distinct methods for training multilingual embeddings across a large number of languages (up to 59). This includes two dictionary-based methods proposed by the authors (multiCluster, multiCCA) and two existing methods based on parallel data.
2.  A critical analysis of evaluation metrics. The authors propose a new intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical limitations of prior work. They provide an important correlation analysis between several intrinsic metrics and two downstream tasks.
3.  The development and release of a public web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, which is a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths**

1.  **Ambitious Scale and Scope:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of creating embeddings for a massive number of languages (59). The breadth of the experiments, comparing four different methods on nine evaluation metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic), is commendable and provides a valuable point of reference for future work.
2.  **Focus on Robust Evaluation:** A major strength is the careful consideration of how to evaluate multilingual embeddings. The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA addresses valid and important theoretical weaknesses (basis invariance, normalization) in the existing QVEC metric. The correlation analysis in §5.1, which evaluates the evaluation metrics themselves, is a significant and necessary step for the field, highlighting the poor correlation of some popular intrinsic metrics with downstream performance.
3.  **Community Resource:** The creation of an easy-to-use web portal for evaluation, along with the release of data and models, is a substantial service to the research community. This lowers the barrier to entry and promotes standardized, reproducible research in this area.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper's ambition is a strength, its execution is marred by a significant number of minor, yet collectively substantial, flaws in methodology, evaluation, and presentation.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** Several key methodological choices are poorly justified or arbitrary.
    *   In multiCluster (Footnote 3), the dictionary extraction threshold `τ` was "fixed... based on manual inspection." This is not a reproducible or principled approach.
    *   In multiCCA (§2.2), the projection matrices `Tm→m,en` are claimed to be "guaranteed to be non-singular," but this is not generally true for CCA projections. Furthermore, the final embedding formula `ECCA(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v)` is unconventional. Why is the inverse of one projection matrix used to transform the output of another? This requires much stronger justification, especially concerning numerical stability and its advantages over a standard CCA projection. The phrasing "a simple extension (in hindsight)" also downplays the novelty and is overly informal.
    *   Hyperparameters are inconsistent across compared methods. For instance, the translation-invariance method uses a window size of 3 while others use 5, with the justification being a limitation of the provided implementation, not a principled choice. This undermines the fairness of the comparison.

2.  **Confounded Evaluation and Overstated Claims:** The experimental comparison is fundamentally flawed due to the issue of vocabulary coverage.
    *   The authors correctly identify that comparing scores computed on different subsets of a test set is problematic, but they relegate the properly controlled comparison (on the intersection of vocabularies) to supplementary material. This is a critical flaw. The main results tables (e.g., Table 3) are therefore misleading, as performance differences are confounded with coverage differences. This is particularly salient in the comparison between multiCCA and translation-invariance, where coverages differ dramatically (e.g., 71.0% vs 23.0% on monolingual word similarity).
    *   The claim that extrinsic evaluations are "immune to this problem" is an oversimplification. A model with lower coverage will map more out-of-vocabulary words to an `<UNK>` vector, which will undoubtedly impact downstream performance.
    *   The crucial correlation analysis in §5.1 is based on an opaque set of "17 different multilingual embeddings," with insufficient detail on how these variants were generated. This makes the reported correlations difficult to interpret or reproduce.

3.  **Inconsistent Results and Weak Conclusions:** The results presented in Table 3 do not support a clear conclusion about which method is superior. The authors concede that "The ranking of the four estimation methods is not consistent across all evaluation metrics." While honest, this weakens the paper's impact. One proposed method (multiCluster) wins on parsing, another (multiCCA) does not win on any task in that table, and a baseline (translation-invariance) is best on four intrinsic tasks. The paper proposes new methods but fails to demonstrate their consistent superiority.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Polish:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of formatting errors and a general lack of polish that undermines confidence in the work.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, with stray question marks, backslashes, and misplaced author/year fields (e.g., Agic et al., 2015; Al.Rfou. et al., 2013).
    *   There are rendering errors in the main text (e.g., `7→` instead of `\mapsto` in §2).
    *   The description of QVEC-CCA is too brief. The formula `maxv,w r(X >v,S>w)` represents only the first canonical correlation. It is unclear if the final score is this single value or some combination of all canonical correlations. This is a critical missing detail for a central contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding multiQVEC-CCA: Is the final score the value of the first canonical correlation only, or a function of multiple canonical correlations? Please clarify the exact computation.
2.  Regarding multiCCA: Could you please provide a formal justification for the specific projection formulation `T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v)`? What are the theoretical and practical advantages of this approach compared to using a standard projection `Tm→m,enEm(v)`?
3.  Regarding the evaluation in Table 3: Could you please present the results for the intrinsic tasks computed on the intersection of the vocabularies covered by all four methods? This would allow for a fair and direct comparison of the models' quality, disentangled from their coverage.
