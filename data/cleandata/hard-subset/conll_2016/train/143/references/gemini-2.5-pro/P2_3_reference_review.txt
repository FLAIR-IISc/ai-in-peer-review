### Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors present three main contributions:
1.  **Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's strongest contribution is in the area of evaluation. It introduces multiQVEC-CCA, an intrinsic evaluation metric that remedies theoretical flaws in the prior QVEC metric (namely, lack of invariance to basis rotation and score normalization). Critically, the authors conduct a meta-evaluation, showing that their proposed intrinsic metrics (multiQVEC and particularly multiQVEC-CCA) correlate much more strongly with performance on downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than existing intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation.
2.  **Community Resource:** The paper introduces a public web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. This is a significant practical contribution that facilitates reproducible research by providing benchmark data, evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparing different embedding methods on a common set of tasks.
3.  **Embedding Estimation Methods:** The authors propose two novel, dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings: multiCluster (which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters and learns embeddings for the clusters) and multiCCA (which learns projections from various monolingual embedding spaces into a single shared space, pivoted on English). These methods are shown to be effective, particularly in a massively multilingual setting (59 languages) where parallel corpora are scarce.

### Strengths
1.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation:** The highlight of this paper is its focus on *how* to evaluate multilingual embeddings. The analysis in Section 5.1 and Table 1 is extremely valuable. It provides strong empirical evidence that commonly used intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation) are poor proxies for downstream task performance. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric, with a correlation of 0.896 on document classification, is a substantial step forward. This critical analysis of evaluation itself is a major service to the community.
2.  **Valuable Public Resource:** The development of the evaluation portal is a commendable effort. By centralizing datasets, evaluation metrics, and providing a simple interface for testing new embeddings, the authors lower the barrier for future research and promote much-needed standardization in a rapidly growing subfield. This kind of contribution often has a long-lasting impact.
3.  **Impressive Scale:** The paper tackles a truly "massively multilingual" scenario, training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages. This goes far beyond the typical bilingual or few-language setups seen in prior work and demonstrates the scalability and practicality of the proposed dictionary-based methods, which do not rely on scarce parallel corpora.
4.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments. They compare their proposed methods against relevant baselines that use different data sources (dictionaries vs. parallel text). The evaluation suite is diverse, including four intrinsic metrics and two different extrinsic tasks (one semantic, one syntactic). The discussion of potential biases due to vocabulary coverage further demonstrates a careful and thoughtful approach to the experiments.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While the proposed embedding methods are effective, their conceptual novelty is somewhat limited.
    *   **multiCCA** is described by the authors as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The main novelty lies in the practical choice of using English as a pivot language, which is a sensible engineering decision but not a deep methodological advance.
    *   **multiCluster** is a more original idea, but its performance is shown to be poor in the 59-language setting (Table 2), and it is only "competitive" in the 12-language setting (Table 3). The paper itself diagnoses the core problem (exacerbating semantic ambiguity by collapsing types into a single vector), which makes it a less compelling contribution.
2.  **Inconclusive Results for Method Comparison:** The results in Table 3, comparing the four estimation methods on 12 languages, are quite mixed. No single method is a clear winner across the board. The proposed dictionary-based methods win on the extrinsic tasks and some intrinsic ones, while the `translation-invariance` baseline wins on several other intrinsic tasks. While this complexity reflects reality, it weakens the paper's narrative that the proposed methods are superior. The takeaway is less of a clear victory and more of a complex trade-off, which is not articulated as a primary conclusion.
3.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The paper's best-performing evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, relies on the availability of manually created linguistic resources (supersense-tagged corpora). The authors themselves note this is a disadvantage, as such resources exist for only a handful of languages (they use English, Danish, and Italian). This limitation is in tension with the paper's broader goal of "massively multilingual" NLP, as the best metric cannot be applied to the vast majority of the 59 languages studied. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative, this is not explored empirically.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Given the mixed results in Table 3, what is the practical recommendation for a researcher choosing an embedding method? Do your experiments suggest certain methods are better suited for semantic tasks (like doc classification) while others are better for more syntactic tasks or for achieving high alignment on intrinsic metrics? A more explicit discussion of these trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
2.  The strong performance of multiQVEC-CCA is a key result, but its reliance on supersense tags limits its applicability. How confident are you that a similar correlation with downstream tasks would hold if using a more widely available but syntactically-focused resource like Universal POS tags, as you suggest? This seems critical for the metric to be truly useful in a massively multilingual context.
3.  For the 59-language experiments, dictionaries were created using Google Translate. This introduces a potential confound where embedding quality may be heavily dependent on the quality of a commercial, black-box translation system for a given language pair. Have you performed any analysis to see if there is a correlation between presumed translation quality and downstream performance for different languages?
