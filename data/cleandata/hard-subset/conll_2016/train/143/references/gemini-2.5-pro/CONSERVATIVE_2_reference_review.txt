### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of multilingual word embeddings. The authors present two dictionary-based methods for training embeddings on a large scale (up to 59 languages), propose an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, and introduce a web portal for standardized evaluation.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An Evaluation Portal and Standardized Benchmarks:** The most significant contribution is the development of a public web portal that provides standardized data, evaluation metrics, and a platform for comparing different multilingual embedding methods. This is a valuable engineering contribution that can foster reproducible research in the community.
2.  **A Comprehensive Empirical Comparison:** The paper provides a systematic evaluation of four different multilingual embedding methods across a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This broad comparison is useful for understanding the relative merits of different approaches.
3.  **A Methodological Refinement of an Intrinsic Metric:** The paper proposes QVEC-CCA, a principled modification of the existing QVEC metric that addresses its lack of rotational invariance and provides a normalized score. This is a solid, incremental improvement on prior work.

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable Community Resource:** The creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation is a commendable effort. By providing shared datasets and a common evaluation harness, this work directly addresses the challenges of reproducibility and fair comparison in this area. This is a strong, practical contribution that will likely benefit future research.
2.  **Breadth of Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. The authors evaluate multiple methods on a diverse set of nine metrics, including two downstream NLP tasks (document classification and dependency parsing). The scale of the experiments, particularly the training of embeddings for 59 languages, is also impressive. This extensive empirical analysis provides a useful point of reference.
3.  **Methodologically Sound Metric Improvement:** The proposed QVEC-CCA metric is a well-justified improvement over QVEC. Identifying and correcting the flaws in the original metric (namely, its sensitivity to the basis of the embedding space and its unnormalized nature) demonstrates strong methodological rigor. Using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) is an elegant and appropriate solution to these problems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Proposed Embedding Methods:** The two proposed methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, are presented as novel contributions, but they appear to be straightforward implementations or extensions of existing ideas. `multiCCA` is a direct, hub-and-spoke extension of the established bilingual CCA projection method (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). `multiCluster` is a simple, pre-processing clustering step that collapses all words in a translation graph into a single representation. This latter approach is methodologically questionable, as it aggressively discards information about polysemy and semantic nuance between languages. The paper lacks a strong justification for why these simple methods should be considered significant advances over prior, more sophisticated models.
2.  **Inconclusive Experimental Results:** The empirical results do not provide compelling evidence for the superiority of the proposed methods. In the 12-language comparison (Table 3), there is no clear winner. The established `translation-invariance` baseline performs best on four of the nine metrics, including the proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric. The paper's own methods win on several tasks, but the overall picture is one of a trade-off, not a clear improvement. This ambiguity undermines the central claim that the proposed methods are a significant contribution.
3.  **Severe Practical Limitations of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** While `multiQVEC-CCA` is theoretically sound, its practical application is severely limited by its reliance on parallel, manually-annotated linguistic resources (i.e., supersense-tagged corpora). The authors could only apply it to three languages (English, Danish, Italian), which is a far cry from the "massively multilingual" setting of 59 languages that the paper otherwise focuses on. This data dependency raises serious questions about the metric's utility as a general-purpose evaluation tool for multilingual embeddings. A metric that cannot be applied to the vast majority of languages under consideration is of limited practical value.
4.  **Unconvincing Correlation Analysis:** The claim that the proposed intrinsic metrics correlate better with downstream tasks is based on a limited experiment with only three languages. Furthermore, the results in Table 1 are not uniformly strong. While `multiQVEC-CCA` shows a very high correlation with document classification (0.896), its correlation with dependency parsing is low (0.273). This inconsistency suggests that the metric may not be a reliable predictor of performance across different types of downstream tasks, and its strong performance on one task may be an artifact of the specific experimental setup.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `multiCluster` method conflates all words within a translation cluster into a single vector, an assumption that seems particularly problematic for polysemous words. Could you discuss the limitations of this design choice and quantify its impact on words with multiple distinct meanings that translate differently across languages?
2.  The `multiCCA` model relies on English as a central hub. How sensitive is the model's performance to this choice? Have you explored the effects of using a different hub language or a non-hub-based architecture where projections are learned between all available language pairs?
3.  Given the acknowledged data scarcity for `multiQVEC-CCA`, the paper suggests using universal POS tags as an alternative linguistic resource. Have you performed any preliminary experiments to validate this idea? Specifically, does a POS-based version of QVEC-CCA still show a meaningful correlation with semantic- and syntactic-driven downstream tasks?
