### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for creating vector-space word embeddings for a large number of languages (up to 59). It also proposes two new intrinsic evaluation metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, which are extensions of a prior monolingual metric (QVEC), and shows that they correlate better with downstream tasks than existing metrics. Finally, the authors present a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The paper evaluates the proposed methods against two baselines on a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A comprehensive meta-evaluation of intrinsic metrics for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's strongest contribution is the systematic analysis in §5.1, which demonstrates that commonly used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy have poor correlation with downstream task performance. The proposed multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics are shown to be more reliable proxies for extrinsic performance, which is a valuable finding for the community.
2.  **A public evaluation portal and benchmark dataset.** The creation of an easy-to-use platform for standardized evaluation, along with benchmark data, is a significant practical contribution that lowers the barrier to entry and facilitates reproducible research in this area.
3.  **An empirical study of several multilingual embedding methods.** The paper presents a large-scale empirical comparison of four different methods for learning multilingual embeddings across two sets of languages (12 and 59), providing insights into their relative performance on various tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Scale and Ambition:** The effort to train and evaluate embeddings across 59 languages is commendable. This is a significant step up from the typically bilingual or few-language settings seen in prior work.
2.  **Focus on Evaluation:** The paper rightly identifies the need for better evaluation practices. The critical analysis of existing intrinsic metrics and the proposal of better-correlated alternatives (multiQVEC-CCA) is a valuable and much-needed contribution. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides strong evidence for this claim.
3.  **Community Resource:** The development of a web portal for standardized evaluation is an excellent contribution that will undoubtedly benefit future research by promoting rigor and comparability across different studies.

### Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of methodological and experimental design flaws, as well as a lack of precision in its presentation.

1.  **Questionable Methodological Novelty and Description:**
    *   The proposed `multiCCA` method is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)". Its novelty is therefore limited. Furthermore, the choice of English as the sole pivot language is not well-justified and may introduce significant anglocentric bias into the shared embedding space. The claim that the projection matrices are "guaranteed to be non-singular" is asserted without proof or citation, which is concerning given that the method relies on taking the inverse of one of these matrices.
    *   The `multiCluster` method, which collapses all words in a translational cluster into a single vector, is a drastic oversimplification that completely ignores polysemy. The authors themselves admit this "severely hurts the performance" when scaling to 59 languages, which calls into question its utility as a proposed method.

2.  **Flawed Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   The comparison between methods in §5.2 is not conducted on a level playing field. The `multiSkip` baseline is handicapped by only using parallel data, a design choice the authors acknowledge is suboptimal. The `translation-invariance` baseline is run with a smaller context window (3 vs. 5) due to "computational challenging," which is not a valid justification for an unfair comparison. These inconsistencies confound the results and weaken any claims about the relative superiority of the proposed methods.
    *   The issue of vocabulary coverage is a major confounder in the intrinsic evaluations (Table 3). For example, comparing a score of 51.0 with 23.0% coverage (translation-invariance) to 43.0 with 71.0% coverage (multiCCA) is meaningless. While the authors acknowledge this and state that a controlled analysis on the vocabulary intersection is in the supplementary material, this is a critical detail that belongs in the main paper. Without it, the results in Table 3 are difficult to interpret.

3.  **Inconsistent and Unreliable Data Sources:**
    *   For 47 of the 59 languages, the bilingual dictionaries were generated using Google Translate. This introduces a powerful, proprietary, and uncontrolled system as a core component of the methodology. The quality of these machine-translated dictionaries is unknown and likely inconsistent, making any results derived from them suspect and difficult to reproduce.
    *   The threshold `τ` for dictionary extraction from parallel corpora was "fixed τ = 0.1 early on based on manual inspection." This is an unscientific and unprincipled approach to a critical hyperparameter.

4.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   The setup for the correlation experiment (§5.1) is opaque. The paper states that 17 embeddings were trained, including "12 multiCluster embeddings," without explaining what differentiates these 12 variants. This disproportionate representation of one method may have skewed the correlation analysis.
    *   The description of the QVEC objective function contains an apparent typo (`X∑i=1 S∑j=1`), which hinders understanding.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted (e.g., `Agić et al.,? \Q2015\E`), suggesting a lack of care in the final preparation of the manuscript. The use of a raw IP address for the evaluation portal URL is unprofessional and unreliable for a long-term resource.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide a theoretical or empirical justification for the claim that the projection matrix `Ten→m,en` in your multiCCA method is "guaranteed to be non-singular"? What are the consequences if it is singular or near-singular for some language pairs?
2.  The correlation experiment in §5.1 is based on 17 embeddings, 12 of which are variants of `multiCluster`. Could you clarify what these 12 variants are and address the concern that this over-representation might bias the correlation results in favor of methods with similar properties?
3.  Given the significant confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation scores, could you present the results for Table 3 evaluated strictly on the intersection of the vocabularies covered by all four methods? This seems essential for a fair comparison.
4.  How do you ensure that the use of Google Translate for generating dictionaries for 47 languages does not introduce uncontrollable artifacts that invalidate the comparison between `multiCluster` and `multiCCA` in the 59-language experiment?
