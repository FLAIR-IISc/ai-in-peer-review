**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A critical analysis of intrinsic evaluation metrics for word embeddings. The authors adapt the monolingual QVEC metric to the multilingual setting and propose an improved version, QVEC-CCA, which corrects for basis-invariance and normalization issues. They provide an empirical analysis showing that their proposed metrics correlate better with downstream task performance than commonly used metrics like word similarity and word translation.
2.  The proposal of two dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings: multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent words before training, and multiCCA, a straightforward extension of a bilingual CCA-based method to the multilingual case via a pivot language (English).
3.  The creation of a large-scale resource, including embeddings for 59 languages and a web portal for standardized evaluation, intended to facilitate future research in this area.

**Strengths**

1.  The most significant contribution of this paper is its focus on the proper evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The authors correctly identify fundamental flaws in the QVEC metric (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) and propose a methodologically sound improvement using Canonical Correlation Analysis (QVEC-CCA).
2.  The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides valuable, if preliminary, evidence that commonly used intrinsic evaluation metrics (word similarity, word translation) may not be reliable proxies for performance on downstream applications. This is an important finding that encourages more rigorous evaluation practices in the community.
3.  The scale of the endeavor is commendable. Training embeddings for 59 languages and developing a public evaluation portal are significant engineering efforts that provide a useful service to the research community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The novelty of the proposed embedding estimation methods is limited. The multiCCA method is described by the authors as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The multiCluster approach is a simple heuristic of collapsing words into clusters, which the authors themselves admit "severely hurts the performance" when scaled to a large number of languages due to semantic ambiguity. These methods do not represent a significant advance over more established, jointly-trained models.
2.  The empirical evaluation of the estimation methods is inconclusive and fails to provide strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed approaches. In Table 3, the results are inconsistent across the nine evaluation metrics. The established translation-invariance baseline (Gardner et al., 2015) performs best on four intrinsic tasks, while the proposed multiCCA method is not the top performer on either of the extrinsic tasks. The fact that different methods excel on different tasks suggests that the paper has not identified a single, robustly superior method, which undermines the primary motivation for proposing new ones. The discussion is further complicated by significant differences in vocabulary coverage, making direct comparisons difficult.
3.  The practical utility of the proposed evaluation metrics (multiQVEC, multiQVEC-CCA) is severely constrained by their reliance on manually annotated supersense tag data, which is only available for three of the 59 languages considered. While theoretically appealing, a metric that cannot be applied to low-resource languages is of limited use for "massively multilingual" evaluation. Furthermore, the empirical support is mixed; multiQVEC-CCA shows a very weak correlation with the dependency parsing task (0.273), which casts doubt on its general applicability as a proxy for syntactic performance.
4.  The experimental methodology for the 59-language embeddings has a significant weakness: the bilingual dictionaries for 47 of these languages were generated using Google Translate. This introduces a powerful, proprietary black-box system as a component in the data creation pipeline. The quality of these dictionaries is unknown and uncontrolled, which introduces a major confounding variable and undermines the scientific rigor and replicability of the large-scale experiments.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the inconsistent results in Table 3, where no single method is a clear winner, what is the primary argument for adopting multiCCA over existing, more established methods like translation-invariance?
2.  The paper argues that multiQVEC-CCA is a theoretical improvement over multiQVEC. How do you account for the empirical result in Table 1 that multiQVEC-CCA correlates much more weakly with dependency parsing performance (0.273) than the supposedly flawed multiQVEC (0.444)? Does this suggest that the metric is biased towards tasks that are more semantic in nature, like document classification?
3.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use Google Translate for generating the majority of your bilingual dictionaries? How can the community be confident in the quality of the resulting embeddings or the fairness of the comparisons when the primary cross-lingual signal for 47 languages comes from a complex, uncontrolled external tool?
