### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of "massively multilingual" word embeddings, presenting work on models for 59 languages. The authors propose two new dictionary-based methods for learning embeddings: `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`. They also introduce two new intrinsic evaluation metrics, `multiQVEC` and `multiQVEC-CCA`, which are multilingual extensions of the existing QVEC metric, and argue these new metrics correlate better with downstream task performance than prior intrinsic evaluations. Finally, the paper introduces a web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, providing data, benchmarks, and an upload-and-evaluate service.

### Contributions

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A valuable resource contribution for the community.** The paper's primary contribution is the creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. By providing data, standard splits, and a common evaluation platform, this work significantly facilitates reproducible research and lowers the barrier to entry for future work in this area.
2.  **An extensive empirical study of multilingual embeddings.** The authors conduct a large-scale evaluation, training embeddings for up to 59 languages and testing them on a wide suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This provides a useful set of benchmarks for the field.
3.  **A principled improvement to an existing evaluation metric.** The proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric identifies and addresses clear theoretical weaknesses in the prior QVEC metric (namely, its lack of rotational invariance and its unnormalized score), offering a more methodologically sound approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Community Resource.** The development of the evaluation portal is a strong contribution. Standardized, accessible, and reproducible evaluation is critical for scientific progress. This portal, along with the curated datasets, represents a substantial service to the multilingual NLP community.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Metric Improvement.** The analysis of QVEC's shortcomings is insightful, and the use of Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) in `multiQVEC-CCA` is a well-motivated and elegant solution to the identified problems of rotational variance and score normalization.
3.  **Thorough Correlation Analysis.** The experiment presented in Table 1, correlating intrinsic metrics with extrinsic task performance, is a valuable contribution. It provides compelling evidence that the proposed QVEC-based metrics are more predictive of downstream utility than commonly used metrics like word similarity and word translation, lending credibility to their use.
4.  **Large-Scale Experimentation.** The ambition to train and evaluate embeddings for 59 languages is commendable. This scale pushes beyond the typical bilingual or few-language setting and provides useful data points on how different methods behave as the number of languages increases.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty.** The proposed embedding estimation methods are largely derivative of existing work. `multiCCA` is a straightforward hub-and-spoke extension of the bilingual CCA method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), a standard approach whose limitations (e.g., dependence on a single pivot language) are not discussed. The `multiCluster` method is a simplistic approach based on merging translationally equivalent words; the authors themselves note that its performance is "severely hurt" by semantic ambiguity in the large-scale setting, which undermines its value as a contribution.
2.  **Inconclusive Experimental Results for Embedding Methods.** The experimental results do not clearly establish the superiority of the proposed methods. In the 12-language comparison (Table 3), the baseline "translation-invariance" method outperforms the proposed methods on four of the nine intrinsic metrics, including the authors' own `multiQVEC-CCA`. While `multiCCA` performs well on the extrinsic tasks, the overall picture is mixed and does not support a strong claim of advancing the state of the art in embedding estimation. The claim that the proposed methods "work well in some settings" is a weak conclusion.
3.  **Mismatch Between Metric Proposal and its Application.** A central contribution is the multilingual `multiQVEC-CCA` metric, yet its practical utility is severely constrained by its reliance on supersense-tagged corpora, which are available for only three of the 59 languages studied. This limitation is significant. When evaluating the 59-language models, the authors revert to using monolingual QVEC on English data, which contradicts the paper's multilingual focus and highlights the impracticality of the proposed metric for "massively multilingual" evaluation at present.
4.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparisons.** The baselines used for comparison may have been disadvantaged. The `multiSkip` model was implemented without using monolingual data, contrary to related work, which likely hindered its performance. The `translation-invariance` model was trained with a smaller context window than other methods due to computational issues. These discrepancies confound the comparison and weaken the conclusions that can be drawn about the relative performance of the different methods.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `multiCCA` method uses English as a pivot language. This is a common design choice, but it can introduce biases, potentially performing better for languages related to English and propagating errors from the pivot. Have you investigated the impact of the choice of pivot language, or considered non-pivotal architectures for extending CCA to the multilingual setting?
2.  Given that `multiCluster` performs poorly at scale due to semantic ambiguity, what is the rationale for presenting it as a primary contribution, especially when the paper's focus is on "massively multilingual" embeddings?
3.  The practicality of your proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric seems limited by data availability, forcing you to use a monolingual English version for the 59-language experiment. Do you see a viable path forward for making this metric genuinely multilingual at a large scale, or should it be considered a high-quality metric for low-resource language settings where such annotations can be created?
