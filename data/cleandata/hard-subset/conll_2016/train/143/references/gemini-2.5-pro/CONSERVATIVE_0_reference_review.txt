**Summary of the paper, and list of the main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of creating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The authors present two new dictionary-based methods for estimating embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA), adapt and improve upon an existing intrinsic evaluation metric (proposing multiQVEC-CCA), and develop a web portal to standardize the evaluation of future work in this area.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  The proposal of multiQVEC-CCA, a methodologically sound intrinsic evaluation metric for word embeddings. The authors provide a clear critique of the prior QVEC method, identifying its lack of invariance to basis transformations and its unnormalized score. The proposed use of CCA is a well-founded solution. Crucially, the authors validate this new metric by showing it has a substantially higher correlation with downstream task performance than previously used intrinsic metrics.
2.  The development of a public web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. By providing data, standardized test sets, and an upload-and-evaluate service, this resource is a valuable practical contribution that could facilitate more rigorous and comparable research in the community.
3.  An empirical comparison of several methods for learning multilingual embeddings, including two newly proposed methods (multiCCA and multiCluster).

**Strengths**

1.  The work on evaluation metrics is the strongest part of this paper. The analysis in Section 5.1, which evaluates the evaluation metrics themselves, is a laudable example of rigorous scientific practice. The finding that multiQVEC-CCA correlates with document classification performance with a coefficient of 0.896, while standard metrics like word similarity and word translation show much weaker correlations (0.386 and 0.066, respectively), provides strong evidence for the utility of the proposed metric.
2.  The methodological improvement of QVEC to create QVEC-CCA is well-motivated and technically sound. Addressing the theoretical shortcomings of an existing method (arbitrariness of basis, unnormalized score) with a standard technique like CCA is a solid and defensible contribution.
3.  The paper includes a comparison against appropriate and strong baselines, particularly the translation-invariance method of Gardner et al. (2015). The inclusion of two extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) provides a practical grounding for the evaluation that goes beyond purely intrinsic measures.
4.  The creation of the evaluation portal is a commendable service to the research community. Such resources are crucial for ensuring reproducibility and establishing standardized benchmarks, which helps mature a research area.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness of this submission is that the proposed estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, do not demonstrate a clear and consistent superiority over established baselines. In the key results in Table 3, the baseline translation-invariance method outperforms all other methods on four of the nine evaluation metrics. The proposed multiCCA method, while performing well on extrinsic tasks, is outperformed by the baseline on monolingual word similarity, monolingual QVEC-CCA, and multiQVEC-CCA. A new method should provide a more compelling case for its adoption; the current results are too mixed to be conclusive.
2.  The novelty of the proposed estimation methods is limited. The multiCCA method is described as a "simple extension" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014), primarily differing in its use of a single pivot language. While pragmatic, this is an incremental step rather than a significant conceptual advance. The multiCluster method is conceptually simple but appears to be a flawed approach, as its performance degrades severely when scaling from 12 to 59 languages, likely due to the conflation of distinct word senses into single cluster representations. This approach seems too simplistic to be considered a competitive method.
3.  The experimental comparison is confounded by significant differences in vocabulary coverage between the methods, particularly between the proposed methods and the translation-invariance baseline. For instance, in Table 3, multiCCA has 71.0% coverage on the monolingual word similarity task, while the translation-invariance method has only 23.0%. The authors acknowledge this issue and state that an analysis on the vocabulary intersection is provided in supplementary material. However, this is a critical methodological point that should be front and center, as comparing scores computed on different subsets of test data can be highly misleading. The main results in the paper remain difficult to interpret because of this confound.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the mixed results in Table 3, where the translation-invariance baseline is highly competitive and outperforms the proposed methods on several intrinsic metrics, could you provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs? Under what specific conditions or for what types of tasks would you recommend using multiCCA over the translation-invariance method?
2.  The performance of multiCluster drops significantly when moving from 12 to 59 languages. You suggest this is due to increased semantic ambiguity in the clusters. Does this imply that the method is only suitable for small sets of closely-related languages? Have you considered alternative clustering schemes that might be more robust to ambiguity at a larger scale?
