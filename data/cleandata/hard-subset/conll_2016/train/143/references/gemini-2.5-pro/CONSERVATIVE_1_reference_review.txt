### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of estimating and evaluating massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors make three main contributions. First, they propose two novel dictionary-based methods for training embeddings across many languages: `multiCluster`, which groups translationally equivalent words into clusters before training, and `multiCCA`, which extends a bilingual CCA-based approach by projecting multiple languages into a single pivot (English) vector space. Second, they propose an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which addresses theoretical shortcomings of the existing QVEC metric (e.g., lack of rotational invariance) and demonstrate its stronger correlation with downstream task performance compared to other intrinsic metrics. Third, they introduce a web portal to serve as a standardized platform for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

### Contributions
In my view, the primary contributions of this work are, in order of significance:
1.  **An improved intrinsic evaluation metric (`multiQVEC-CCA`) and its validation.** The paper provides a clear and methodologically sound critique of the existing QVEC metric and proposes a principled solution using CCA. The correlation analysis presented in Table 1, which shows a strong relationship between `multiQVEC-CCA` and document classification performance, is a valuable finding for the community.
2.  **The creation of a public evaluation portal.** A centralized and easy-to-use resource for benchmarking multilingual embeddings is a welcome and practical contribution that facilitates reproducibility and standardized comparison, which is crucial for rigorous scientific progress.
3.  **The proposal of two dictionary-based embedding methods.** The paper presents `multiCluster` and `multiCCA` as methods scalable to dozens of languages. While the scale is impressive, the methods themselves are largely extensions or combinations of established techniques.

### Strengths
1.  **Methodologically Sound Improvement to Evaluation.** The main strength of this paper lies in its contribution to evaluation methodology. The authors correctly identify significant theoretical flaws in the QVEC metric (namely, its sensitivity to basis rotation and its unnormalized scoring). The proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` is a well-justified fix that leverages a standard statistical technique (CCA) to resolve these issues. This is a solid, incremental contribution that improves upon existing work.
2.  **Valuable Community Resource.** The development of a web portal for standardized evaluation is a commendable effort. By providing data, evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparison, the authors are helping to establish the kind of rigorous and reproducible evaluation practices that the field needs. This is a strong practical contribution.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup.** The authors evaluate their methods on a wide variety of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks (nine in total) and conduct experiments on both a 12-language set and a large-scale 59-language set. This breadth of evaluation provides a multifaceted view of the performance of the different embedding models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty and Inconclusive Performance of Proposed Embedding Methods.** The proposed embedding methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, are not particularly novel. `multiCluster` is a straightforward heuristic of collapsing word types into clusters, a method which the authors themselves admit is problematic due to increased semantic ambiguity. `multiCCA` is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of an existing bilingual method. More importantly, the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate their superiority. In the 12-language comparison (Table 3), the established `translation-invariance` baseline performs best on four of the nine evaluation metrics, including several intrinsic tasks. The proposed methods each win on some tasks, but there is no clear evidence that they represent a significant advance over existing approaches. The claims of success feel overstated given the mixed results.
2.  **Flawed Baseline Comparison.** The comparison against the `multiSkip` baseline is methodologically questionable. The authors state that their implementation "is only trained on parallel corpora," whereas the methods it is meant to represent (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015) also leverage large monolingual corpora. This is a critical omission that significantly handicaps the baseline, as it cannot learn from the rich contextual information in monolingual data that all other methods use. This unfair comparison makes the authors' methods appear better by comparison and undermines the validity of the experimental conclusions. For a rigorous comparison, baselines must be implemented to their full strength.
3.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` Metric.** While `multiQVEC-CCA` is a theoretical improvement, its utility is severely constrained by its reliance on parallel, manually-created linguistic resources (supersense tags), which are only available for three languages in this study. This makes it unsuitable for the "massively multilingual" settings the paper aims to address. The suggestion to use universal POS tags as an alternative is speculative and is not empirically validated in the paper. As it stands, the proposed metric is a niche tool rather than a general-purpose solution for multilingual evaluation.
4.  **Inconsistent Correlation Results.** The justification for `multiQVEC-CCA` rests on its correlation with downstream performance. However, Table 1 shows a more complex picture than is presented. While its correlation with document classification is very high (0.896), its correlation with dependency parsing (0.273) is mediocre and notably lower than that of the original `multiQVEC` metric (0.444). This suggests `multiQVEC-CCA` may be biased towards capturing topical-semantic information over syntactic information, a critical nuance that is not discussed.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please justify the decision to implement the `multiSkip` baseline without access to monolingual data, a choice that deviates from the established literature and likely hinders its performance? How might the results in Table 3 change if `multiSkip` were allowed to train on the same monolingual corpora as the other methods?
2.  The correlation results for your proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric are strong for document classification but weak for dependency parsing, where the original `multiQVEC` actually shows a stronger correlation. What does this suggest about the specific linguistic properties captured by your new metric, and how does it affect its claim as a general-purpose evaluation tool for multilingual embeddings?
