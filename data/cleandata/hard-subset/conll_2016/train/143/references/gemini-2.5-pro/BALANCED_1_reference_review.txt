### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The authors propose two methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, for learning embeddings across dozens of languages using only monolinggual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, avoiding the need for large parallel corpora. The paper's more significant contribution lies in its thorough investigation of evaluation methodologies. It identifies weaknesses in existing intrinsic evaluation metrics, proposes an improved metric (`multiQVEC-CCA`) based on Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), and demonstrates that its proposed metrics correlate much better with performance on downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than commonly used metrics like word similarity and translation accuracy. Finally, the paper introduces a public web portal to facilitate standardized and reproducible evaluation of multilingual embeddings, a valuable practical contribution to the research community.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A rigorous evaluation of evaluation metrics:** The paper provides a critical analysis of how multilingual embeddings are evaluated. It introduces `multiQVEC-CCA`, an improved intrinsic metric that is invariant to basis rotation and normalized. Crucially, it presents strong empirical evidence (Table 1) that this and the related `multiQVEC` metric have a much higher correlation with downstream task performance than prior standard intrinsic metrics, providing the community with a more reliable and efficient way to assess embedding quality.
2.  **A valuable public resource for the community:** The creation of an easy-to-use web portal for standardized evaluation is a significant practical contribution. By providing data, benchmarks, and a platform for evaluating new embeddings, this resource will lower the barrier to entry, encourage reproducible research, and help standardize the field's experimental practices.
3.  **Scalable dictionary-based embedding methods:** The paper proposes two methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, that can generate embeddings for a large number of languages (59 in this work) without requiring parallel corpora. While the methods are extensions of prior work, their application and evaluation at this scale provide a useful baseline for future research in truly "massively multilingual" settings.

### Strengths

1.  **Focus on Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The paper's greatest strength is its emphasis on evaluation. The community often relies on intrinsic metrics like word similarity without rigorously checking if they are predictive of real-world utility. The correlation analysis in §5.1 is a clear and compelling demonstration that popular metrics can be misleading. By proposing and validating metrics that better predict downstream performance, this work makes a fundamental contribution to how the field should approach the problem. The identification and correction of theoretical flaws in the original QVEC (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) further strengthens this contribution.
2.  **Significant Community Contribution via the Evaluation Portal:** The development and release of the evaluation portal is an excellent contribution. Such infrastructure is vital for the progress of a research area. It enables fair comparisons, ensures reproducibility, and saves other researchers significant effort in setting up complex evaluation pipelines. This practical tool, along with the release of data and models, substantially increases the paper's impact and utility for the community.
3.  **Comprehensive and Large-Scale Experiments:** The experimental setup is thorough and well-designed. The authors evaluate four different embedding estimation methods across a diverse suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. Crucially, they conduct experiments at two scales: a 12-language setting to compare against parallel-data baselines and a 59-language setting to demonstrate the scalability of their dictionary-based methods. This breadth provides a robust picture of the relative merits of different approaches and evaluation tasks. The authors' careful consideration of confounding factors, such as vocabulary coverage, also demonstrates a high degree of experimental rigor.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of Proposed Embedding Methods:** The primary weakness is the incremental nature of the proposed estimation methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`. `multiCluster`—grouping words by translation and running a monolingual model—is an intuitive and straightforward technique. `multiCCA` is self-described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of prior work by Faruqui and Dyer (2014). While their application to 59 languages is novel and valuable as a proof-of-concept, the methodological innovation is limited. The paper's framing would be stronger if it presented the evaluation framework as the core contribution and the models as case studies within that framework.
2.  **Inconclusive Model Performance:** The experimental results do not yield a clear "winner" among the embedding techniques. On the 12-language benchmark (Table 3), the proposed `multiCluster` and `multiCCA` methods excel on some tasks (especially extrinsic), but the `translation-invariance` baseline is superior on several key intrinsic tasks (word translation, multiQVEC-CCA). This makes it difficult to draw a strong conclusion about the superiority of the proposed methods. While this complex result is itself an interesting finding about the state of the art, it weakens the paper's claims about its own proposed models.
3.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed Evaluation Metrics:** While `multiQVEC` and `multiQVEC-CCA` show strong correlation with extrinsic tasks, their utility is constrained by the availability of the required linguistic resources (i.e., supersense-tagged data). In this work, they are demonstrated on only three languages (English, Italian, Danish). This limitation is at odds with the paper's goal of "massively multilingual" NLP covering 59 languages. The authors briefly mention this in a footnote and suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative, but this is a significant caveat that deserves more prominent discussion. The limited applicability of the paper's best-performing intrinsic metrics is a practical weakness.
4.  **Potentially Unfair `multiSkip` Baseline:** The `multiSkip` baseline is implemented to use only parallel corpora, whereas the cited works (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016) also leverage monolingual data. The authors state this difference explicitly, but it makes the comparison potentially unfair, as the baseline is deprived of the large monolingual corpora that all other methods use. This may explain its consistently poor performance and weakens the conclusions drawn about the relative merits of dictionary-based versus parallel-data-based approaches.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `multiSkip` baseline performs quite poorly. You note that your implementation, unlike others, does not use monolingual data. Could you comment on why this design choice was made for the comparison? How do you expect a `multiSkip` model that also leverages monolingual corpora would perform, and would this change your conclusions about the effectiveness of parallel-data methods?
2.  The performance of `multiCluster` degrades significantly when moving from 12 to 59 languages, which you attribute to "added semantic ambiguity." Could you elaborate on this? Does this suggest a fundamental scaling problem with this approach, or could it be mitigated with different hyperparameter choices, such as a much smaller maximum cluster size?
3.  Given the reliance of your best intrinsic metrics (`multiQVEC-CCA`) on limited linguistic resources, what is your recommendation for evaluating embeddings for the majority of the 59 languages you target, for which supersense annotations do not exist? How confident are you that the alternative you propose (using universal POS tags) would yield similarly high correlations with downstream tasks?
