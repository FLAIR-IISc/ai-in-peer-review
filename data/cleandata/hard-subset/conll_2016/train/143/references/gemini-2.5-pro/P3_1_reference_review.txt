### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the important and growing challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings across a large number of languages ("massively multilingual"). The authors' main contributions, in order of significance, are:

1.  **A new evaluation methodology and community resource:** The paper proposes multiQVEC-CCA, a novel intrinsic evaluation metric that remedies theoretical flaws (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized scores) in the prior QVEC metric. Crucially, they demonstrate empirically that their new metric, along with its predecessor multiQVEC, correlates significantly better with downstream task performance than commonly used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation. This is a major finding. Coupled with this, the development of a public web portal for standardized evaluation is a substantial contribution that will foster reproducible research in the field.
2.  **Scalable dictionary-based embedding methods:** The paper introduces two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning multilingual embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, which are more widely available than large parallel corpora. They demonstrate the scalability of these methods by training and releasing embeddings for 59 languages. While methodologically straightforward, the practical demonstration at this scale is a valuable contribution.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison:** The paper provides a thorough comparison of their proposed methods against two parallel-corpus-based baselines (multiSkip, translation-invariance) across a suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks. This empirical analysis provides useful insights into the relative strengths of dictionary-based vs. parallel text-based approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Evaluation is the core strength:** The most significant contribution of this work is its focus on rigorous and meaningful evaluation. Identifying the theoretical weaknesses of QVEC and proposing a well-motivated fix using CCA is a strong methodological improvement. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that the new metrics are more reliable proxies for downstream performance than existing ones. This helps address a persistent problem in the representation learning literature, where intrinsic metrics often fail to predict extrinsic utility.
2.  **Excellent contribution to the community:** The creation of the evaluation portal is an outstanding service to the research community. Standardizing evaluation protocols and providing easy access to benchmarks and data is critical for making progress in a field. This lowers the barrier to entry for new researchers and ensures that future methods can be compared on a level playing field. This aspect alone makes the paper a strong candidate for acceptance.
3.  **Practicality and Scalability:** The proposed methods, particularly multiCCA, are simple, effective, and rely on more accessible resources (dictionaries, which can even be generated by machine translation) than parallel corpora. This pragmatic approach allows them to successfully scale to 59 languages, a significant increase over most prior work that focused on bilingual or few-language settings. The release of these embeddings is also a valuable resource.
4.  **Thorough and honest analysis:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering multiple intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The authors are transparent about the limitations of their work, for example, acknowledging the semantic ambiguity problem in multiCluster, the coverage differences between methods, and the fact that their multiSkip baseline does not use monolingual data. This self-awareness and careful analysis strengthen the paper's conclusions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the embedding estimation methods:** The primary weakness is that the proposed estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, are not particularly novel. MultiCluster is a form of hard clustering based on translation equivalence, an intuitive idea with a significant and acknowledged flaw: it conflates all words in a translation cluster into a single representation, exacerbating polysemy issues. MultiCCA is described by the authors themselves as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The contribution here lies in the application and scaling of these ideas, rather than in fundamental methodological innovation.
2.  **Potentially unfair baseline comparison:** The multiSkip baseline is at a considerable disadvantage because it is trained only on parallel corpora, whereas the other methods leverage large monolingual corpora. The authors note this distinction in a footnote, but it significantly impacts the interpretation of the results in Table 3. Methods like those of Coulmance et al. (2015) and Guo et al. (2016), which the authors cite, explicitly combine parallel and monolingual objectives. A stronger and fairer comparison would have been against a multiSkip implementation that also incorporated a monolingual skip-gram objective, which would likely have improved its performance substantially. As it stands, the paper compares strong dictionary-based methods to a weak parallel-corpus-based baseline.
3.  **Reliance on a single pivot language:** The multiCCA method is architecturally dependent on a single high-resource pivot language (English) to create the shared space. This introduces a potential bias, as the structure of the final embedding space is determined entirely by the English monolingual embeddings. The paper does not discuss the implications of this design choice or explore alternatives, such as averaging projections from multiple pivots or using a more decentralized graph-based approach to align spaces. This limits the generality of the method for language sets that do not include English or another single dominant language.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the multiSkip baseline: Could you elaborate on the decision not to incorporate monolingual data in the multiSkip objective? Given that related work demonstrates the value of combining monolingual and parallel signals, using a monolingual-only baseline seems to weaken the claim about the relative performance of dictionary-based versus parallel-corpus-based approaches. How do you expect the results would change with a more competitive multiSkip baseline?
2.  Regarding the multiCCA pivot: The method relies entirely on projecting into the pre-trained English space. Have you considered the potential bias this introduces, where phenomena not well-represented in English might be poorly modeled for other languages? Did you experiment with alternative strategies, such as creating a shared space that is a synthesis of all languages, rather than simply adopting one? For instance, one could learn all pairwise projections and use them to find a single consensus projection for each language.
