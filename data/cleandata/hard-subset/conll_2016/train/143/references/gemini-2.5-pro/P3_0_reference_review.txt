### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the important and growing area of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors make three primary contributions. First, they introduce a comprehensive framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings, proposing an improved intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which corrects theoretical flaws in prior work. They provide strong empirical evidence that this new metric correlates significantly better with downstream task performance than other common intrinsic metrics. Second, they present two novel, dictionary-based methods for training embeddings (multiCluster and multiCCA) and demonstrate their scalability by training embeddings for 59 languages. Crucially, they show these dictionary-based methods are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, methods that require more resource-intensive parallel corpora. Third, the authors have developed and released a public web portal for standardized evaluation, a major contribution that provides the community with shared data, metrics, and a benchmark for future research, fostering reproducibility and accessibility.

From my perspective, the contributions in order of strength are:
1.  **The evaluation framework and community resource:** The well-motivated multiQVEC-CCA metric, its empirical validation against downstream tasks, and the public evaluation portal together represent a significant and lasting contribution to the field.
2.  **The demonstration of effective, scalable, dictionary-based methods:** Showing that relatively simple, dictionary-based methods can outperform parallel-corpus-based methods on extrinsic tasks is an important practical result, given the wider availability of dictionaries.
3.  **The analysis and insights from the comprehensive experiments:** The paper offers valuable insights into the trade-offs between different modeling and evaluation choices, such as the scalability limitations of the multiCluster approach and the importance of monolingual data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Major advance in evaluation methodology:** The paper's focus on evaluation is a significant strength. The proposal of multiQVEC-CCA is well-grounded, addressing clear theoretical shortcomings in QVEC (invariance to basis rotation and normalization). The correlation analysis in Table 1 is the paper's most compelling result, demonstrating that the proposed metrics are far more predictive of downstream performance than widely used metrics like word similarity. This finding helps steer the community towards more meaningful intrinsic evaluation.

2.  **Exceptional contribution of a public resource:** The creation of the evaluation portal is a standout contribution that elevates the paper beyond a typical research submission. By providing data, standardized evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparison, the authors have created a valuable resource that will undoubtedly facilitate and standardize future research in this area. This is a commendable service to the community.

3.  **Strong empirical results for scalable methods:** The paper successfully demonstrates that multilingual embeddings can be built for a large number of languages (59) using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. The key finding in Table 3—that the proposed dictionary-based methods outperform parallel-text-based baselines on both extrinsic tasks (parsing and document classification)—is highly impactful. It challenges the assumption that large parallel corpora are a prerequisite for high-quality multilingual representations.

4.  **Rigor and thoughtfulness in experimental design:** The experiments are carefully designed and analyzed. The authors make the sound choice to isolate the contribution of word embeddings in extrinsic tasks by removing other features. They also thoughtfully identify and discuss the potential biasing effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation metrics, reporting coverage statistics and performing follow-up analysis. This level of rigor strengthens the paper's conclusions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear formulation of the multiCCA method:** The projection for multiCCA is defined as `E(m, v) = T−1_en→m,en * T_m→m,en * Em(v)`. This is a non-standard application of CCA projections. A standard approach would be to simply use the projection into the shared (English) space, i.e., `T_m→m,en * Em(v)`. The paper provides no motivation or explanation for the additional multiplication by the inverse matrix `T−1_en→m,en`. This lack of clarity about a core proposed method is a significant weakness that needs to be addressed.

2.  **Limited applicability of the best intrinsic metric:** The paper's best-performing intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, relies on multilingual supersense tag annotations, which the authors could only source for three languages. This presents a practical limitation, as the metric cannot be readily applied to evaluate the "massively multilingual" embeddings the paper aims to create. While the authors suggest alternatives like using universal POS tags (footnote 9), this is not demonstrated. This limitation undermines the metric's utility for the very problem the paper focuses on.

3.  **Potentially unfair comparison to the multiSkip baseline:** The multiSkip baseline is implemented to use only parallel corpora, whereas the works it is based on (e.g., Guo et al., 2016) also leverage monolingual data. As the authors' own results show the importance of large monolingual corpora, this design choice severely handicaps the baseline and makes its poor performance unsurprising. A more informative comparison would have involved a stronger multiSkip implementation that also incorporates monolingual data, providing a fairer assessment of the underlying model architecture.

4.  **Incremental novelty of the estimation methods:** The proposed estimation methods, while effective, are extensions of existing ideas. The authors themselves describe multiCCA as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" that uses a standard hub-and-spoke model. MultiCluster is more novel but is shown to perform poorly in the main 59-language setting. Therefore, the primary contribution of this part of the work lies more in the successful engineering and scaling of these methods rather than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the mathematical formulation of the multiCCA projection in §2.2? Specifically, what is the intuition behind pre-multiplying the standard projection `T_m→m,en * Em(v)` by the inverse matrix `T−1_en→m,en`? Is this a typo, or does this two-step transformation serve a specific purpose?

2.  The multiSkip baseline was disadvantaged by not using monolingual data. Could you speculate on how the rankings in Table 3 might change if multiSkip were implemented in a way that combines monolingual and bilingual objectives, as done in the work you build upon?

3.  Given the scarcity of multilingual supersense resources, have you explored creating a version of multiQVEC-CCA using more widely available annotations, such as universal POS tags? How might the correlation of such a "syntactic" QVEC-CCA metric compare to the current "semantic" version, particularly for a syntax-heavy task like dependency parsing?
