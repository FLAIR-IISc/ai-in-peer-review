**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning embeddings from monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, scaling up to 59 languages. For evaluation, the paper introduces multiQVEC-CCA, an intrinsic metric that improves upon a prior method (QVEC) by addressing theoretical flaws related to rotational invariance and normalization. The authors demonstrate that their proposed metric correlates better with performance on downstream tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than existing intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation tasks. Finally, to facilitate future research, the paper introduces a public web portal that provides data, trained embeddings, and a standardized suite of evaluation metrics. The experimental section includes a correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics and a comparative evaluation of the proposed methods against two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance) on sets of 12 and 59 languages.

**Contributions**

1.  **A standardized evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The primary contribution is the development of a comprehensive framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings. This includes a new intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which is shown to be more theoretically sound (rotationally invariant) and empirically better correlated with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics. The framework is operationalized through a public web portal, providing datasets and evaluation tools, which constitutes a significant resource for the community.
2.  **Scalable, dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings.** The paper proposes two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, that learn embeddings using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries, making them applicable to a large number of languages where parallel corpora are not available. The multiCCA method, in particular, is shown to be effective and robust when scaled to 59 languages.
3.  **An extensive empirical comparison of multilingual embedding methods.** The paper presents a large-scale evaluation of four distinct methods across nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks for both a 12-language and a 59-language setting. This provides a valuable benchmark and set of findings on the relative strengths of different approaches (dictionary-based vs. parallel text-based) across various evaluation criteria.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant contribution to evaluation methodology.** The paper makes a compelling case for the need for better intrinsic evaluation metrics. The analysis of the shortcomings of QVEC (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) is clear, and the proposed QVEC-CCA method provides an elegant and theoretically sound solution using canonical correlation analysis. The empirical evidence presented in Table 1, showing that multiQVEC-CCA has a much higher correlation (0.896) with document classification performance than prior metrics like word similarity (0.386) and word translation (0.066), strongly supports its utility.
2.  **Valuable community resource.** The development of the web portal for standardized evaluation is a major strength. By providing access to training data, evaluation scripts, and a platform for uploading and testing new embeddings, the authors are directly addressing issues of reproducibility and comparability that often hinder progress in this area. This is a practical and impactful contribution that will likely facilitate future research.
3.  **Demonstrated scalability to a large number of languages.** A key goal stated in the introduction is to develop methods for "massively multilingual" embeddings. The authors successfully demonstrate this by training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages. The results in Table 2 show that their multiCCA method performs consistently well in this large-scale setting, which is a non-trivial achievement and highlights the practicality of using dictionary-based approaches where parallel data is scarce.
4.  **Thorough experimental design.** The paper's experimental setup is comprehensive. It evaluates multiple methods on a diverse suite of nine tasks, including both intrinsic and extrinsic measures. The authors are careful in their analysis, for example, by reporting vocabulary coverage alongside performance metrics and discussing the potential biases this can introduce in intrinsic evaluations. This level of rigor strengthens the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty of the proposed embedding methods.** The multiCCA method is described by the authors as a "simple extension" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The multiCluster method, while conceptually novel, performs poorly in the 59-language setting, which undermines the primary motivation for a massively multilingual method. While simplicity and effectiveness are desirable, the core technical novelty of the embedding *estimation* part of the paper is incremental.
2.  **Potentially weakened baseline comparisons.** The comparison with baselines may not be entirely equitable.
    *   The authors' implementation of the multiSkip baseline does not use monolingual data, which is a key component of the cited works (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016). This is noted in a footnote but significantly disadvantages the baseline, as it cannot leverage the large amounts of monolingual data available. This might lead to an overestimation of the relative performance of the proposed methods.
    *   The translation-invariance method was trained with a smaller context window (3 vs. 5 for other methods) due to computational constraints. This hyperparameter difference could also negatively affect its performance, confounding the comparison.
3.  **Limited applicability of the proposed evaluation metric.** The main proposed metric, multiQVEC-CCA, relies on multilingual supersense tag annotations. The authors acknowledge that these are only available for a few languages (English, Danish, Italian in this work) and can be inconsistent. This practical limitation restricts the metric's widespread use for the very "massively multilingual" settings the paper advocates for. While an alternative (universal POS tags) is mentioned in a footnote, no experiments are presented to validate its effectiveness or correlation with semantic downstream tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiSkip baseline was implemented without monolingual data, differing from the original works you cite. Could you comment on how the results in Table 3 might change if multiSkip were allowed to leverage the same monolingual corpora as your proposed methods?
2.  Your proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric relies on supersense annotations, which are not widely available. You suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic tasks. Have you performed any analysis to see how well a POS-based QVEC-CCA correlates with syntactic tasks like dependency parsing? Such a result would significantly broaden the applicability of your proposed evaluation method.
3.  The multiCCA method anchors the multilingual space to the English vector space. Does this introduce an English-centric bias, potentially degrading performance for language pairs that are typologically distant from English? Have you explored using a different pivot language or a method that creates a more symmetric, language-agnostic shared space?
