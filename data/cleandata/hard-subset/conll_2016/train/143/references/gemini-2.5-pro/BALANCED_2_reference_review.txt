### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of creating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors make three primary contributions. First, and most significantly, they present a rigorous evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings. This includes a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and is shown to correlate much more strongly with downstream task performance than commonly used metrics. They also developed and released a public web portal to standardize the evaluation process, a valuable service for the community. Second, the paper proposes two simple, dictionary-based methods for training embeddings, multiCluster and multiCCA, which do not require parallel corpora and are scaled to 59 languages. Third, the authors provide a comprehensive empirical study comparing their methods against baselines that use parallel corpora, evaluating on a suite of nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Contribution to Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's most impressive contribution is in the area of evaluation. The authors provide a clear and insightful critique of the QVEC metric, pointing out its lack of invariance to basis rotations and its unnormalized scoring. Their proposed multiQVEC-CCA method, based on Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), elegantly resolves both issues. The correlation analysis presented in Table 1 is compelling, demonstrating that multiQVEC-CCA has a much higher correlation with performance on a document classification task (0.896) than standard metrics like word similarity (0.386) and word translation (0.066). This finding is crucial as it provides the community with a more reliable and theoretically grounded intrinsic metric for measuring the quality of multilingual embeddings.

2.  **Excellent and Valuable Community Resource:** The development of the web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a major strength. By providing standardized datasets, evaluation scripts, and an easy-to-use interface for uploading and testing new embeddings, the authors are promoting reproducible research and facilitating more rigorous comparisons in the field. This kind of infrastructure is essential for scientific progress and represents a significant service to the research community.

3.  **Thorough and Scalable Empirical Analysis:** The paper presents an extensive set of experiments. The methods are evaluated at a large scale (59 languages), demonstrating their applicability beyond the common bilingual or small multilingual setups. The controlled comparison on 12 languages against strong baselines across nine different metrics provides a nuanced view of the methods' performance. The results are strong for the proposed dictionary-based methods, which achieve the best performance on both extrinsic tasks (dependency parsing and document classification), arguably the most important indicators of real-world utility. The authors also show good scientific practice by acknowledging and analyzing the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation metrics.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The `multiSkip` baseline is implemented using only parallel corpora. The authors note in a footnote that this differs from related work (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015) which also leverages monolingual data. Since monolingual corpora are typically orders of magnitude larger than parallel corpora, this design choice severely disadvantages the `multiSkip` baseline, as it cannot learn from the rich contextual information available in the monolingual data that all other methods use. This likely contributes to its poor performance across most tasks and may overstate the relative strength of the proposed methods.

2.  **Modest Novelty of the Proposed Embedding Methods:** The two proposed estimation methods, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, are effective and scalable but are arguably straightforward extensions of existing ideas rather than major methodological innovations. `multiCCA` extends bilingual CCA to a multilingual setting via a pivot language, a well-established strategy. `multiCluster` uses dictionaries to merge words into cross-lingual clusters before training, a simple and intuitive approach. While the engineering and application of these methods at scale is a valuable contribution, the core novelty of the paper lies more in its evaluation framework than in these specific embedding techniques.

3.  **Limited Applicability of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The new multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA metrics depend on the availability of manually created linguistic resources (specifically, supersense tag annotations) to construct the linguistic feature matrix. In this work, these resources were only available for three languages (English, Danish, Italian). This limits the metric's immediate use for the "massively multilingual" settings the paper advocates for. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as a substitute for syntactic evaluation, this does not address the need for a broadly applicable *semantic* evaluation metric, which was the original goal.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the `multiSkip` baseline: Could you expand on the rationale for implementing it using only parallel data? Given that this is a significant disadvantage, how might its performance change if it were trained on both monolingual and parallel data, and how would this affect the conclusions drawn from Table 3?

2.  The `multiCCA` model relies on English as a single pivot language. Did you investigate the sensitivity of the results to this choice? How might the quality of embeddings be affected for language families that are typologically distant from English?

3.  The strong performance of `multiQVEC-CCA` is a key result, but its reliance on supersense-tagged data currently limits its scope to a few languages. Beyond using POS tags for syntax, do you have concrete proposals for how the required semantic feature matrix could be created or bootstrapped for a much larger set of languages, thereby making the evaluation metric as scalable as the embedding methods themselves?
