### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings, where words from many languages are represented in a single shared vector space. The authors make three main contributions. First, they propose two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, that create such embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, demonstrating their scalability by training embeddings for 59 languages. Second, they identify theoretical flaws in the QVEC intrinsic evaluation metric and propose an improved version, multiQVEC-CCA, which is invariant to basis rotation and produces a normalized score. They provide a compelling correlation analysis showing that their new metric, along with a multilingual extension of QVEC, is a better predictor of downstream task performance than more traditional metrics like word similarity. Third, they introduce a web portal to serve as a standardized platform for evaluating multilingual embeddings, releasing their data and benchmarks to facilitate future research.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work, in order of importance, are:

1.  **A comprehensive and principled framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings.** The paper's most significant contribution is its work on evaluation. The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA, backed by a strong theoretical motivation and empirical correlation analysis (Table 1), provides the community with a much-needed, more reliable intrinsic evaluation metric. This, combined with the public evaluation portal, substantially lowers the barrier to rigorous, comparable, and replicable research in this area.
2.  **Scalable dictionary-based methods and large-scale multilingual embeddings.** The paper proposes two accessible methods for creating embeddings for a large number of languages without requiring parallel corpora, a scarce resource for many language pairs. While multiCCA is an extension of prior work, its application to 59 languages is a valuable demonstration of scalability. The resulting 59-language embeddings are themselves a useful resource for the community.
3.  **A thorough empirical comparison of different embedding techniques.** The paper presents a broad experimental comparison of four different methods across nine distinct evaluation metrics, including two extrinsic tasks. This provides a nuanced view of the trade-offs between different approaches (e.g., dictionary-based vs. parallel-corpus-based) and different evaluation criteria.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Contribution to Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's most compelling strength is its focus on improving evaluation. The authors clearly articulate the theoretical weaknesses of the existing QVEC metric (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) and propose a principled fix using CCA. The subsequent correlation analysis (Table 1) provides strong empirical evidence that multiQVEC-CCA (and to a lesser extent, multiQVEC) is a far better proxy for downstream task performance than existing intrinsic metrics like cross-lingual word similarity and word translation. This is a significant finding that can guide future research toward more meaningful intrinsic evaluations.
2.  **Ambitious Scale and Thorough Experimentation:** The work is impressive in its scope. Training and evaluating embeddings across 59 languages is a substantial undertaking. The experimental setup is comprehensive, comparing four distinct methods on a diverse suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. This breadth allows for a detailed analysis of how different methods perform under different evaluation paradigms, moving beyond a single-metric comparison.
3.  **High-Impact Community Resources:** The development and release of the evaluation portal is an outstanding contribution. By providing a centralized place for standardized evaluation, along with benchmark datasets and models, the authors are fostering reproducibility and lowering the barrier to entry for new research. Such resources are invaluable for making sustained progress in a field.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The methods are explained concisely, and the experimental results are presented in a structured and understandable manner.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty of the Proposed Methods:** The core embedding methods, while effective, are not profoundly novel. MultiCCA is presented as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). MultiCluster is a conceptually straightforward approach whose main drawback—conflating senses of all words in a translation cluster—is shown to severely harm its performance at the 59-language scale. The contribution here lies more in the scaling and application of these ideas rather than in a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.
2.  **Inconsistent Results and Confounding Factors in Comparison:** The experimental results in Table 3 do not point to a single superior method. The best-performing model is highly dependent on the chosen evaluation metric, making it difficult to draw a clear conclusion. More problematically, the comparison is confounded by large differences in vocabulary coverage between methods. For instance, the translation-invariance method performs exceptionally well on several intrinsic tasks but covers a much smaller vocabulary than multiCCA. The authors rightly acknowledge this issue and mention an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies in supplementary material, but this confounding variable weakens the direct comparability of the headline results presented in the main paper.
3.  **Practical Limitations of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** While multiQVEC-CCA is theoretically and empirically strong, its reliance on manually annotated supersense tag data limits its applicability. Such resources are only available for a handful of languages, which is at odds with the paper's goal of "massively multilingual" NLP. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic tasks, the metric's utility for semantic evaluation in low-resource languages remains constrained by data availability.
4.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The multiSkip baseline is implemented using only parallel data, whereas the authors note in a footnote that related prior work also leverages monolingual data. Since this baseline consistently ranks last or near-last, training it on only a fraction of the available data (parallel corpora are much smaller than monolingual corpora) may represent a weak comparison point. A stronger baseline implementation could have altered the relative standing of the proposed dictionary-based methods.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The multiCCA approach uses English as a single pivot language, projecting all other languages into its space. How might this choice of a single, typologically specific pivot affect the quality of the embeddings for languages that are distant from English? Have you considered alternative topologies, such as a multi-pivot approach or one that doesn't rely on a pivot at all, and what trade-offs would you expect?
2.  The multiSkip baseline performs poorly across most tasks. The paper notes that your implementation differs from prior work by not using monolingual data. How much of its poor performance do you attribute to this implementation choice? If it were trained on the large monolingual corpora as well, would you still expect the dictionary-based methods like multiCCA to be superior, particularly on the extrinsic tasks?
3.  Your analysis of evaluation on the intersection of vocabularies is relegated to supplementary material. Given the large coverage disparities in Table 3, could you briefly summarize the key changes in relative performance when evaluation is restricted to the vocabulary covered by all four methods? Specifically, how does the gap between multiCCA and the translation-invariance method change on the intrinsic tasks where translation-invariance initially appears much stronger?
