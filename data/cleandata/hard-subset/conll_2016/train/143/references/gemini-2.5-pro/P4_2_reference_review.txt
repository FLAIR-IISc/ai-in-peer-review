### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of multilingual word embeddings, which represent words from multiple languages in a single shared vector space. The authors propose two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning these embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, removing the need for scarcer parallel corpora. They train embeddings for 59 languages using these methods. A significant portion of the work is dedicated to evaluation methodology. The authors identify theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC intrinsic evaluation metric (namely, its sensitivity to basis rotation and lack of normalization) and propose an improved version, multiQVEC-CCA, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis to resolve these issues. Through extensive experiments, they demonstrate that their proposed metric correlates better with downstream task performance (document classification and dependency parsing) than other common intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors introduce a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings for the research community.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A theoretically-grounded and empirically-validated intrinsic evaluation metric for multilingual word embeddings (multiQVEC-CCA).** The paper provides a clear critique of the existing QVEC metric and proposes a technically sound improvement using CCA that makes the metric invariant to linear transformations and normalizes its score. The empirical correlation analysis (Table 1) provides strong evidence that this new metric is a more reliable proxy for performance on extrinsic tasks than previously used intrinsic measures.
2.  **A comprehensive, large-scale empirical comparison of dictionary-based and parallel-corpus-based methods for learning multilingual embeddings.** The paper evaluates four different methods across nine distinct intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks, on both a 12-language and a massive 59-language scale. This rigorous evaluation provides valuable insights into the relative merits and trade-offs of different approaches, particularly highlighting the effectiveness of dictionary-based methods in low-resource scenarios.
3.  **The creation of public resources to advance research in multilingual embeddings.** The authors have developed and released a web portal for standardized evaluation, along with trained 59-language embedding models and evaluation datasets. This infrastructure lowers the barrier to entry and promotes reproducible research in the area.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Focus on Evaluation Methodology.** The paper's most significant strength lies in its contribution to the evaluation of word embeddings. The authors clearly identify two major technical weaknesses in the QVEC metric: its lack of invariance to the embeddings' basis and its unnormalized score, which prevents comparison across different dimensionalities. Their proposed solution, multiQVEC-CCA, is a principled fix that directly addresses both issues. The correlation analysis in Table 1, which shows multiQVEC-CCA achieving a Pearson's correlation of 0.896 with document classification, provides compelling evidence that this new metric is a more faithful intrinsic measure of embedding quality for downstream tasks than word similarity or word translation tasks.
2.  **Thorough and Extensive Experimentation.** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The comparison spans four distinct estimation methods, nine different evaluation metrics (including two extrinsic tasks), and two setups of different scale (12 and 59 languages). This breadth allows for a nuanced analysis of the models' performance. For example, the results demonstrate that the proposed dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are highly competitive, often outperforming the parallel-corpus-based baselines on several tasks, including both extrinsic evaluations (Table 3). This validates their utility, especially for scaling to many languages where parallel text is unavailable.
3.  **Practicality and Scalability of Proposed Methods.** The multiCCA and multiCluster methods rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. This is a significant practical advantage over methods requiring large parallel corpora, as dictionaries are more widely available or can be automatically generated (as the authors do via Google Translate for 47 languages). The successful training of embeddings for 59 languages demonstrates the scalability of this approach and makes a tangible step towards "massively multilingual" representations.
4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility and Community.** The development of the evaluation portal is a commendable contribution. By providing a platform for standardized testing and releasing their data and models, the authors are actively facilitating future research. This helps address the common problem of inconsistent evaluation practices in the field and enables more reliable comparisons of new methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Embedding Estimation Methods.** The primary methods proposed, multiCluster and multiCCA, are incremental extensions of existing ideas. The authors describe multiCCA as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of the bilingual CCA approach of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), where a pivot language (English) is used to create a shared space. The multiCluster method, which merges translation pairs into single units before training monolingual embeddings, is an intuitive approach related to prior work on cross-lingual word clustering. While these methods are shown to be effective, their conceptual novelty is modest.
2.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison with Baselines.** The experimental comparison between the proposed methods and the `multiSkip` baseline may be confounded by the training data used. The `multiSkip` implementation is trained only on parallel corpora, whereas the other methods leverage much larger monolingual corpora. The authors acknowledge that this limits `multiSkip`'s ability to learn from diverse contexts. A more direct comparison would have involved a `multiSkip` model that was also augmented with monolingual data, as suggested by the original work it is based on (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015). Without this, it is difficult to conclude whether the dictionary-based approach is superior or if the performance gap is simply due to the amount of training data.
3.  **Unaddressed Limitations of a Pivot-Based Architecture.** The multiCCA method relies entirely on projecting all languages into the vector space of a single pivot language, English. While pragmatic, this design has potential drawbacks that are not discussed. For instance, it may introduce an English-centric bias, potentially performing sub-optimally for language pairs that are typologically distant from English but close to each other. Furthermore, any errors or peculiarities of the English embedding space are propagated to all other languages.
4.  **Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage.** The paper rightly notes that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias the results of intrinsic evaluations. While the authors state that an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies is provided in supplementary material, this is a critical detail for interpreting the main results in Table 3. The translation-invariance method, for example, consistently has much lower coverage than multiCCA. This makes a direct comparison of their scores on tasks like word similarity challenging, as the scores are computed on different sets of word pairs, and the translation-invariance method may be evaluated on an easier subset of more frequent words.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your implementation of the `multiSkip` baseline does not use monolingual data, which seems to put it at a disadvantage compared to both your proposed methods and the original methods it is based on. Could you clarify the rationale for this choice? How would you expect the results in Table 3 to change if `multiSkip` were trained on the same large monolingual corpora as the other methods?
2.  The `multiCCA` model architecture is pivot-based, using English as the hub. Have you considered or can you comment on alternative architectures, such as a joint optimization that does not privilege any single language? What are the potential limitations of relying on a single pivot language, particularly for capturing semantic relationships between non-English languages?
