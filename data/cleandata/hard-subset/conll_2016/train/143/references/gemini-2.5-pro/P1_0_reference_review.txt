### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The authors make three main contributions. First, they propose two new methods for learning multilingual embeddings, `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, which are scalable and rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, not parallel text. They use these to train embeddings for 59 languages. Second, they introduce a new intrinsic evaluation metric, `multiQVEC-CCA`, which improves upon prior work (QVEC) by being invariant to linear transformations and normalized. Crucially, they demonstrate that this new metric correlates much more strongly with performance on downstream tasks than existing intrinsic metrics. Third, they introduce a web portal providing data, code, and a standardized evaluation suite to facilitate future research in this area.

As a reviewer, I see the primary contributions of this work as:

1.  **A superior evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction and rigorous validation of `multiQVEC-CCA`. The correlation analysis in Table 1, showing its strong relationship to extrinsic task performance, is a very important result for the field. This, combined with the public evaluation portal, provides the community with a much-needed toolkit for reliable and reproducible evaluation.
2.  **Simple, scalable, and effective methods for multilingual embedding.** The paper presents two practical methods, with `multiCCA` standing out as a strong and straightforward approach that scales to dozens of languages. It provides a valuable baseline for future work, especially in settings where only dictionaries are available.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical study.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across a variety of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, comparing their proposed methods against relevant baselines. The scale of the experiments (up to 59 languages) is impressive.

### Strengths

1.  **Advances in Evaluation Methodology:** The paper makes a compelling case that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics like word similarity and word translation are poor proxies for downstream performance. The proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric is theoretically well-motivated, addressing clear flaws in the original QVEC, and the empirical evidence for its superiority (Table 1) is a standout result. This is a significant step forward for the field.
2.  **Excellent Resource for the Community:** The creation of an easy-to-use web portal for standardized evaluation is an outstanding contribution. By providing data, standard splits, and a platform for comparing different embedding methods, this work will foster reproducibility and accelerate progress in the area. This kind of infrastructure development is invaluable.
3.  **Scalability to a Large Number of Languages:** Many papers focus on bilingual or few-language settings. This work's ambition to tackle a "massively multilingual" setting with 59 languages is commendable and highly relevant. The methods are designed for this scale, relying on more widely available resources (dictionaries) than parallel corpora.
4.  **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The experimental setup is described in detail, and the authors are transparent about potential methodological issues, such as the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation scores, which they handle thoughtfully.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The `multiSkip` baseline appears to be at a significant disadvantage. As noted in Footnote 4, the authors' implementation uses only parallel corpora, whereas their proposed methods and the `translation-invariance` baseline leverage large monolingogual corpora. The original work on similar models (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015) uses both data types. This likely explains `multiSkip`'s poor performance and weakens the claim that the proposed dictionary-based methods are superior to parallel-data methods. The comparison would be much more convincing if all methods had access to the same data sources.
2.  **Inconsistent Results and Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The results of the 12-language comparison (Table 3) are quite mixed, with no single method dominating. `multiCluster` wins on parsing, `multiCCA` on document classification, and `translation-invariance` on several intrinsic tasks. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it offered deeper insights into *why* these methods have different strengths. For example, why would the conceptually simple `multiCluster` be best for a syntactically-driven task like parsing? A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs would provide clearer guidance for practitioners.
3.  **Limited Practicality of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** While `multiQVEC-CCA` is shown to be an excellent metric, its current implementation relies on multilingual supersense annotations, which the authors could only find for three languages (English, Danish, Italian). This limits its immediate use as a general-purpose evaluation tool for the "massively multilingual" settings the paper advocates for. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic evaluation, the flagship semantic metric's reliance on a scarce resource is a notable limitation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the `multiSkip` baseline: Can you elaborate on the decision to not use monolingual data in your implementation, especially when other methods did? Given that this is a key difference from prior work and likely explains its poor performance, how would you expect the results in Table 3 to change if `multiSkip` were trained on both parallel and monolingual data?
2.  The `multiCCA` method uses English as a central pivot language. Have you considered the potential effects of this hub-and-spoke model? Does it introduce an English-centric bias, or does performance degrade for languages that are typologically distant from English?
3.  The strong performance of `multiCluster` on dependency parsing (Table 3) is surprising, as collapsing translationally equivalent words into a single vector might be expected to lose subtle, language-specific syntactic information. Do you have a hypothesis that explains this result?
