### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of creating and evaluating massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors' primary contribution, in my view, is the development of an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, **multiQVEC-CCA**, which remedies theoretical flaws in a prior metric (QVEC) and is shown to correlate significantly better with downstream task performance than other intrinsic metrics. A secondary contribution is the introduction and large-scale evaluation of two dictionary-based embedding methods, **multiCluster** and **multiCCA**, which are tested on up to 59 languages. Finally, the authors present a web portal for standardized evaluation, which serves as a useful, albeit non-scientific, resource contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Well-Validated Evaluation Metric:** The paper's strongest contribution is the development of multiQVEC-CCA. The authors clearly identify two major weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric: its lack of invariance to linear transformations and its unnormalized score. The proposed solution, using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), is an elegant and theoretically sound fix for both issues. The empirical validation in Section 5.1 is compelling; the high Pearson correlation of multiQVEC-CCA with document classification performance (0.896) provides strong evidence that this new metric is a much better proxy for downstream utility than existing intrinsic measures like word similarity or translation.

2.  **Ambitious Scale of Experiments:** The effort to train and evaluate embeddings across 59 languages is commendable. This scale moves beyond typical bilingual or small-scale multilingual studies and provides valuable data points on the performance of dictionary-based methods in a "massively multilingual" setting. The public release of these embeddings and evaluation scripts is a valuable service to the community.

3.  **Thoughtful Analysis of Evaluation Pitfalls:** The authors demonstrate a nuanced understanding of evaluation challenges. The discussion surrounding the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic evaluation scores is particularly insightful. While relegating the intersection-based analysis to supplementary material is a minor weakness, acknowledging the problem and reporting coverage percentages in the main tables is good practice that strengthens the credibility of the analysis.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Methodological Inconsistencies in Comparative Evaluation:** The experimental design for comparing the four embedding estimation methods is undermined by several inconsistencies, making the conclusions in Section 5.2 difficult to trust.
    *   The authors' **multiSkip** model is trained only on parallel corpora, explicitly excluding the monolingual data that the original methods (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016) leverage. This makes for an unfair and weakened baseline, likely explaining its poor performance.
    *   The **translation-invariance** method was trained with a context window of size 3, while all skipgram-based methods used a window of 5. The justification of computational cost (footnote 12) does not negate the fact that this is a significant confounding variable that directly impacts performance and makes a head-to-head comparison invalid.
    *   The experimental setup for the correlation analysis in Section 5.1 is opaque and seemingly unbalanced. The authors state they used "12 multiCluster embeddings, 1 multiCCA embeddings, 1 multiSkip embeddings, 2 translation-invariance embeddings". The rationale for this highly skewed distribution is never explained, and it could potentially bias the correlation results.

2.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Rigor:** Several claims and methodological details are imprecise or insufficiently justified.
    *   In Footnote 3, the dictionary extraction threshold τ was fixed "based on manual inspection". This is not a reproducible or principled approach to hyperparameter tuning.
    *   In Section 2.2, the authors claim the projection matrices are "guaranteed to be non-singular" but provide no explanation or proof for this assertion. While likely true if the source matrices are full-rank, it requires justification.
    *   The description of the **multiCCA** method as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" is overly informal. More importantly, the choice to pivot all languages through English is a critical design decision with potential drawbacks (e.g., error propagation, non-optimality for language pairs not involving English) that are not discussed.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor but unprofessional errors.
    *   The evaluation portal is linked via a raw IP address (`http://128.2.220.95/multilingual`). This is not a stable, permanent URI and raises concerns about the longevity of the resource. It also presents a potential security risk and breaks the anonymity of the review process if server logs are inspected.
    *   The reference section contains numerous and obvious BibTeX processing errors (e.g., `Agirre et al\.,? 2009`, `Al.Rfou. et al\.,? \Q2013\E`, an incomplete entry for `Agic et al., 2015`, double commas). This suggests a lack of careful proofreading and detracts from the overall quality of the submission.
    *   The use of `7→` instead of the correct `↦` for mapping notation in Section 2 is a minor but noticeable typographical error.

While the paper presents a valuable new evaluation metric and a large-scale empirical study, the significant methodological flaws in the comparative experiments and the general lack of polish in the presentation lead me to recommend rejection. The core claims comparing embedding methods are not well-supported by the current experimental design.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the experimental design for the correlation analysis in Section 5.1? Specifically, why were 12 variants of multiCluster used against only 1-2 variants of the other methods, and how were these variants generated?

2.  Could you justify the inconsistent experimental settings used for comparing embedding methods in Section 5.2? In particular, can a fair comparison be made when your multiSkip implementation is denied access to monolingual data and the translation-invariance method is trained with a different context window size?

3.  In your multiCCA method, you state that the projection matrices are "guaranteed to be non-singular." Could you briefly provide the reasoning or conditions under which this holds true?

4.  Regarding the evaluation portal, are there plans to host it at a permanent, stable URL rather than a raw IP address? Will the numerous formatting and BibTeX errors in the references be corrected in a future version?
