### Summary of the paper and contributions

This paper tackles the challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors propose two novel methods for training multilingual embeddings that rely on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, thus avoiding the need for large parallel corpora. The paper's primary contributions, in my view, are:

1.  **A new, principled intrinsic evaluation metric and a public evaluation framework.** The paper introduces `multiQVEC-CCA`, a significant improvement over the existing `QVEC` metric that addresses its theoretical shortcomings (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized scores). Crucially, the authors provide strong empirical evidence that their proposed metrics correlate much better with downstream task performance than widely used alternatives like word similarity. The accompanying web portal for standardized evaluation is a major service to the community.
2.  **Scalable, dictionary-based methods for "massively multilingual" embeddings.** The paper introduces `multiCluster` and `multiCCA`, demonstrating their ability to generate embeddings for 59 languages. `multiCCA`, in particular, proves to be a practical and effective method that leverages a pivot language to create a shared space.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of multilingual embedding techniques.** The work presents a broad-scale evaluation of four distinct methods across nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks for both 12- and 59-language settings. This provides valuable insights into the relative merits of dictionary-based vs. parallel-corpus-based approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent contribution to evaluation methodology.** The paper’s most significant strength is its rigorous focus on evaluation. The critique of existing intrinsic metrics and the development of `multiQVEC-CCA` is a key insight. By demonstrating its superior correlation with extrinsic task performance (Table 1), the authors make a compelling case for its adoption. This work helps move the field towards more reliable and meaningful intrinsic evaluation.
2.  **Impressive scale and thoroughness of experiments.** The experiments are conducted on an ambitious scale, creating embeddings for 59 languages. The comparison across four methods and nine distinct tasks (including parsing and document classification) is extensive. This breadth provides a nuanced and well-supported analysis, lending significant weight to the paper's conclusions. The careful handling of issues like vocabulary coverage further enhances the quality of the evaluation.
3.  **High potential for community impact.** The creation of a public web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, along with the release of data and models, is a substantial contribution. This will facilitate reproducible research, standardize evaluation, and lower the barrier for future work in this area. Such resources are invaluable for driving progress in the field.
4.  **Practicality of the proposed methods.** The dictionary-based approaches, especially `multiCCA`, are highly practical. They do not depend on scarce parallel corpora, and the paper shows that dictionaries can be effectively created using tools like Google Translate. This makes the approach applicable to a far wider range of languages than methods that rely strictly on parallel text, which is a crucial step towards truly "massively multilingual" NLP.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the embedding estimation methods.** The proposed methods are incremental extensions of prior work. `multiCCA` is explicitly described as a "simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), with the main novelty being its application in a pivot-based multilingual setup. `multiCluster` is an intuitive but somewhat simplistic approach whose conceptual flaw (conflating distinct words in a cluster into a single vector) is confirmed by its weaker performance in the 59-language experiment. The paper's core strength is more in its evaluation and analysis than in developing fundamentally new embedding techniques.
2.  **Insufficient justification for the `multiCCA` projection formulation.** The projection formula for non-pivot languages, `E(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v)`, is non-obvious and lacks a clear justification. This operation involves projecting from a language's space to the CCA-induced shared space, and then mapping it back into the pivot language's original space via the inverse of the pivot's projection matrix. The rationale for this specific chain of transformations over simpler alternatives (e.g., using the shared CCA space as the final embedding space for all languages) is not explained. This is a critical detail of the method that needs more elaboration.
3.  **Potentially unfair comparison to baselines.** The `multiSkip` baseline implementation is noted to use *only* parallel data, whereas the work it is based on (e.g., Guo et al., 2016) also leverages monolingual data. This likely puts it at a severe disadvantage, as it cannot learn from the large monolingual corpora that the other methods use, possibly explaining its consistently poor performance. This may not be a fair "apples-to-apples" comparison and could overstate the relative strength of the proposed methods.
4.  **Unexplored biases of the pivot-language approach.** The `multiCCA` method relies on a single pivot language (English) to bridge all other languages. This hub-and-spoke architecture forces all cross-lingual relationships to be mediated through English, which may introduce significant biases and inadequately model relationships between language pairs that are typologically distant from English but close to each other. The potential drawbacks and limitations of this pivot-centric design are not discussed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed motivation for the specific projection formula used in `multiCCA`? Did you experiment with using the shared latent space from CCA directly as the final multilingual space, rather than projecting back into the original English space? How did that compare?
2.  The `multiSkip` baseline was at a disadvantage by not using monolingual data, unlike the other methods. How much do you think this contributed to its weaker performance? Would a `multiSkip` model that also leverages monolingual corpora (as in the original papers) be a more competitive baseline?
3.  The `multiCCA` model architecture is centered on English as a pivot. Have you analyzed the effect this has on the geometric relationships between non-pivot languages? For instance, is the vector similarity between French and Spanish words overly influenced by their respective similarities to their English translations?
