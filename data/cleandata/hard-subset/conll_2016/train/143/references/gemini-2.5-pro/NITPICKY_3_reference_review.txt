**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of estimating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors' primary contributions, as I see them, are twofold:
1.  **A new evaluation methodology and community resource:** The paper proposes an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical shortcomings (basis-invariance, normalization) of the prior QVEC metric. Crucially, they demonstrate that this new metric, along with multiQVEC, correlates more strongly with downstream task performance than previous intrinsic metrics. This is accompanied by a public web portal for standardized evaluation, a significant practical contribution that could foster more rigorous and reproducible research in the area.
2.  **An empirical study of multilingual embedding methods:** The paper introduces two dictionary-based embedding methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and compares them against two baselines across a suite of nine evaluation metrics and on two different language sets (12 and 59 languages). The scale of this empirical evaluation is extensive.

While the authors also list their proposed embedding methods as a key contribution, I view them as more incremental extensions of prior work. The true strength of the paper lies in its careful consideration of evaluation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Evaluation:** The paper's most significant strength is its focus on improving the evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The critique of QVEC is well-founded, and the proposed QVEC-CCA is a theoretically sound improvement. The correlation analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence that the proposed intrinsic metrics are more indicative of downstream performance than widely used metrics like word similarity and translation, which is an important finding for the field.
2.  **Valuable Community Resource:** The creation of an open evaluation portal is a commendable effort. By providing standardized data, metrics, and an easy-to-use interface, this resource has the potential to significantly benefit the community by streamlining the evaluation process and improving the comparability of results across different studies.
3.  **Experimental Scale and Breadth:** The ambition to train and evaluate embeddings on 59 languages is impressive and directly addresses the "massively multilingual" goal. The comparative analysis on the 12-language set is comprehensive, covering four distinct methods and a diverse set of nine intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the paper's strengths, particularly in its contributions to evaluation, it is undermined by a significant number of methodological and experimental design flaws, as well as a lack of precision in its presentation.

1.  **Uncontrolled Experimental Variables:** The comparison between methods is not on equal footing. Most notably, the translation-invariance method was trained with a context window of 3, while skipgram-based methods used a window of 5. The authors relegate the justification for this discrepancy—"computationally challenging"—to a footnote (12). This is an uncontrolled variable that directly impacts the information available to the models and renders the comparisons in Table 3 scientifically questionable. A rigorous comparison would require consistent hyperparameters or a thorough analysis of their impact.
2.  **Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage:** The paper correctly identifies that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias intrinsic evaluation scores. However, it fails to address this critical issue adequately in the main text. For instance, in Table 3, the translation-invariance method achieves a much higher monolingual word similarity score (51.0) than multiCCA (43.0), but its vocabulary coverage is drastically lower (23.0% vs. 71.0%). This strongly suggests the superior score is an artifact of evaluating on a small, likely easier, subset of words. The authors state that a comparison on the vocabulary intersection is in the supplementary material, but this analysis is fundamental to the paper's claims and must be presented and discussed in the main paper. As it stands, the intrinsic evaluation results in Table 3 are difficult to interpret and potentially misleading.
3.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:**
    *   **multiCCA:** The formulation `ECCA(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v)` is presented without sufficient justification. It is described as a "simple extension (in hindsight)," but this does not excuse the lack of a clear explanation for this specific sequence of transformations. Why is this indirect projection through a shared space preferable to a direct one? The assertion that the projection matrix `Ten→m,en` is "guaranteed to be non-singular" is stated without proof or conditions.
    *   **multiCluster:** The threshold `τ` for dictionary extraction was set "based on manual inspection" (footnote 3). This is an ad-hoc, unscientific approach that hinders reproducibility. A sensitivity analysis or a more principled method for hyperparameter selection is expected.
    *   **Data Provenance:** The use of Google Translate for generating dictionaries for 47 languages introduces a dependency on a proprietary, black-box system. This not only affects reproducibility as the service changes over time but also acts as a potential confounder: the quality of the resulting embeddings is intertwined with the quality of Google's translation system. This limitation is not discussed.
4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its credibility. The bibliography is riddled with formatting errors (e.g., `Agirre et al\.,? 2009, Agirre et al\.,`), suggesting a lack of careful proofreading. Several crucial details, such as the context window discrepancy and the vocabulary intersection analysis, are either relegated to footnotes or supplementary material when they should be central to the experimental discussion. This makes the paper difficult to follow and assess.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed theoretical or empirical justification for the specific projection formulation used in multiCCA? What are the conditions under which the matrix `Ten→m,en` is guaranteed to be invertible?
2.  Given the significant confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic metrics, can you please integrate the results on the intersection of vocabularies into the main paper and explicitly discuss how these controlled results affect your conclusions about the relative performance of the embedding methods in Table 3?
3.  How can you be confident in the fairness of your comparison in Table 3 when the translation-invariance method used a smaller context window (3) than the other methods (5)? Can you provide evidence that this did not systematically disadvantage the model?
