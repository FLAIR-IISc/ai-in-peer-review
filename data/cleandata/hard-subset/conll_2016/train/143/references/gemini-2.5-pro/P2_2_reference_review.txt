### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of creating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The authors make three primary contributions, which I see as follows:

1.  **A new intrinsic evaluation metric and a rigorous meta-evaluation:** The paper introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a principled improvement over the existing QVEC metric that is invariant to linear transformations and produces a normalized score. Critically, the authors conduct a correlation study (Table 1) showing that their proposed metric, along with its predecessor multiQVEC, is substantially more predictive of downstream task performance (document classification, dependency parsing) than commonly used metrics like word similarity and word translation. This is a significant finding that provides guidance for future research in the area.
2.  **A valuable public resource for the community:** The authors have developed and released a web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings. By providing data, benchmarks, and an easy-to-use upload interface, this portal significantly lowers the barrier to entry for new research, promoting reproducibility and comparability of results. The release of embeddings for 59 languages is also a major contribution.
3.  **Two simple, scalable, dictionary-based embedding methods:** The paper proposes multiCluster and multiCCA, two methods for learning multilingual embeddings that require only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, making them applicable to a large number of languages where parallel corpora are scarce. The methods are shown to be competitive, particularly on extrinsic tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Emphasis on Principled Evaluation:** The paper's most significant strength is its focus on *how* to evaluate multilingual embeddings. The critique of existing intrinsic metrics (low correlation with extrinsic tasks) is well-motivated, and the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses clear theoretical shortcomings of QVEC (basis variance, unnormalized score). The correlation analysis in §5.1 provides strong empirical evidence that the new metrics are more reliable indicators of downstream utility. This is a foundational contribution that could have a lasting impact on how the community assesses progress.
2.  **Excellent Community-Oriented Resources:** The creation of the evaluation portal is a major service. It has the potential to unify a fragmented evaluation landscape and accelerate research by allowing for rapid, standardized comparison of new methods. Releasing pre-trained embeddings for 59 languages is also highly valuable. Such contributions are crucial for the health and progress of the field.
3.  **Comprehensive and Large-Scale Experiments:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation. They test their methods on a massive scale (59 languages) and in a more controlled 12-language setting against strong baselines that use parallel corpora. The evaluation suite is diverse, including four intrinsic metrics and two challenging extrinsic tasks (parsing and document classification), providing a well-rounded view of model performance.
4.  **Clarity of Proposed Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods are simple, intuitive, and clearly explained. multiCCA's approach of using English as a pivot space is a pragmatic and scalable solution for extending a bilingual technique to a massively multilingual setting. While not deeply novel from a theoretical standpoint, their simplicity and scalability are strong practical advantages.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Inconclusive Performance of Proposed Models:** The primary weakness is that the experimental results for the proposed embedding methods are mixed and do not demonstrate a clear superiority over the baselines. In the 12-language comparison (Table 3), the translation-invariance method outperforms multiCCA and multiCluster on four of the nine metrics, including key intrinsic tasks like word translation and monolingual/multilingual QVEC-CCA. While the proposed methods perform best on the two extrinsic tasks, the overall picture is not one of a new state-of-the-art method, which tempers the impact of that contribution.
2.  **Vocabulary Coverage as a Major Confounding Variable:** The experimental results in Table 3 are difficult to interpret due to the large and inconsistent vocabulary coverage across methods (the numbers in square brackets). For instance, the translation-invariance method achieves the highest score on monolingual word similarity (51.0) but with a dramatically lower coverage (23.0%) than multiCCA (43.0 score with 71.0% coverage). This raises the concern that its high score may be an artifact of only providing embeddings for a small, potentially easier subset of words (e.g., very high-frequency words). While the authors acknowledge this and mention an analysis on the intersection of vocabularies in supplementary material, this issue is significant enough to undermine the direct comparability of the results presented in the main paper.
3.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Embedding Methods:** Both multiCluster and multiCCA are straightforward extensions of existing ideas. multiCluster is a pipeline of graph clustering and standard skip-gram training. multiCCA is a "one-vs-all" extension of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The novelty lies in the application and scaling, not in the core learning algorithm. This is a minor weakness, as the paper's main contributions are clearly in evaluation and resources, but it's worth noting that the modeling aspect is more incremental than groundbreaking.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance of the translation-invariance baseline is very strong on several intrinsic metrics (word translation, QVEC-CCA), yet it is weaker on the extrinsic tasks where your dictionary-based methods excel. How do you interpret this discrepancy? Does it suggest that even the improved intrinsic metrics like multiQVEC-CCA are still failing to capture qualities essential for complex downstream tasks like parsing?
2.  Regarding the vocabulary coverage issue in Table 3: Could you elaborate on the results from the evaluation on the intersection of vocabularies? Specifically, does the translation-invariance method's advantage on intrinsic tasks hold when it is forced to evaluate on the same (and presumably more difficult) set of words as the other methods? A brief summary of these findings in the final version could significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
3.  The multiCCA method relies on English as a central pivot language. Have you analyzed the potential drawbacks of this hub-and-spoke model? For example, how does it affect the quality of embeddings for a language pair that is typologically distant from English (e.g., Finnish-Hungarian) compared to a direct mapping?
