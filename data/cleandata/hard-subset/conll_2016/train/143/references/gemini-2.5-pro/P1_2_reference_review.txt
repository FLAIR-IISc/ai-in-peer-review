### Summary of the paper
This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings, i.e., shared vector spaces for a large number of languages. The authors make three main contributions. First, they propose two novel dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning embeddings from monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, applying them to 59 languages. Second, they propose a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which improves upon the existing QVEC method by being invariant to linear transformations and providing a normalized score. They demonstrate through a meta-evaluation that their proposed metric, along with its predecessor multiQVEC, correlates better with downstream task performance than other common intrinsic metrics like word similarity and word translation. Third, they introduce a web-based evaluation portal to standardize and facilitate future research in this area, providing data, benchmarks, and an interface for evaluating new embeddings.

### Main Contributions (Reviewer's View)
1.  **A rigorous framework for evaluating multilingual embeddings.** The paper's most significant contribution is its work on evaluation. The development of multiQVEC-CCA addresses clear theoretical shortcomings of a prior method (QVEC). More importantly, the correlation analysis in Section 5.1 provides strong evidence that this new family of metrics is more indicative of downstream utility than widely used alternatives. This finding has the potential to guide future research toward more meaningful intrinsic evaluation.
2.  **A valuable public resource for the community.** The creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation is a substantial service to the research community. By providing data, pre-trained models, and a common set of evaluation scripts, it lowers the barrier to entry and promotes reproducible research in a complex area. This is a strong practical contribution.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical study of multilingual embedding methods at scale.** The paper presents a broad comparison of four different embedding techniques across two settings (12 and 59 languages) and nine evaluation tasks. While the proposed embedding methods themselves are relatively straightforward extensions of prior work, the scale of the experiments and the detailed reporting (including coverage statistics) provide a valuable snapshot of the current landscape and a useful benchmark for future methods.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong and impactful work on evaluation methodology.** The paper correctly identifies weaknesses in existing intrinsic evaluation metrics (poor correlation with extrinsic tasks) and in a recent proposal (QVEC's lack of rotational invariance and score normalization). The proposed multiQVEC-CCA is a principled and well-motivated solution. The meta-evaluation experiment (Table 1) is compelling and provides clear, quantitative support for the claim that multiQVEC-CCA is a more reliable proxy for downstream performance, particularly for document classification (r=0.896). This is a very strong result.
2.  **Excellent contribution to research infrastructure.** The development of the evaluation portal is a significant strength. Such resources are crucial for driving a field forward, ensuring that new methods are compared on a level playing field and that results are reproducible. The authors' commitment to making their data, models, and evaluation tools publicly available is commendable.
3.  **Large-scale experiments and honest reporting.** The ambition to train and evaluate embeddings for 59 languages is impressive and pushes the boundary of what is typically seen in this area. The authors are commendably transparent about their results, especially in Table 3, where no single method emerges as a clear winner across all tasks. They also thoughtfully address the confounding issue of vocabulary coverage in their analysis, which demonstrates methodological rigor.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited novelty in the proposed embedding methods.** The proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, are conceptually simple. MultiCluster is an intuitive approach based on connected components, but as the authors acknowledge, it conflates word senses and performs poorly at the 59-language scale. MultiCCA is a direct "hub-and-spoke" extension of a bilingual CCA method, projecting all languages into a single pivot space (English). While effective, this approach is not new in principle and has well-known potential drawbacks (e.g., quality degradation for languages typologically distant from the hub, propagation of errors from the hub's embeddings) which are not discussed.
2.  **Lack of deep analysis of the experimental results.** The results in Table 3, which compares all four methods on 12 languages, are quite mixed. For instance, the proposed dictionary-based methods win on both extrinsic tasks, while the translation-invariance method performs best on several intrinsic tasks. The paper presents these results but offers little insight into *why* these patterns emerge. Why does multiCluster, despite its semantic ambiguity, achieve the highest parsing score? Why is there such a disconnect between intrinsic and extrinsic performance for the translation-invariance method? A deeper qualitative or quantitative analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper's conclusions about the relative merits of these methods.
3.  **Practical limitations of the proposed evaluation metric.** While multiQVEC-CCA is shown to be effective, its reliance on manually created supersense-tagged resources limits its applicability to a very small number of languages (English, Danish, Italian in this paper). This is a significant practical limitation for a metric intended to evaluate "massively multilingual" embeddings. The authors acknowledge this in a footnote and suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic evaluation, but this point deserves more prominent discussion in the main body of the paper, as it tempers the immediate impact of the proposed metric.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The multiCCA method relies on English as a "hub" language. Could you comment on the potential risks of this design? For example, does performance on a language pair like Danish-Italian suffer from being mediated through English? Did you consider or experiment with alternative topologies, such as chaining projections or learning a single global projection?
2.  Your implementation of the multiSkip baseline uses only parallel data, whereas the original papers also leverage monolingual data. This seems to put the method at a distinct disadvantage, especially concerning vocabulary coverage and learning from broader contexts. Could you clarify the rationale behind this implementation choice? Was it to draw a sharper contrast between methods that require parallel data and those that do not?
3.  The strong performance of multiCluster on dependency parsing (Table 3) is surprising, given that the method conflates distinct words into a single vector. Can you offer any intuition for this result? Could it be that for syntax, this high-level clustering acts as a useful form of abstraction, similar to traditional word clustering features?
