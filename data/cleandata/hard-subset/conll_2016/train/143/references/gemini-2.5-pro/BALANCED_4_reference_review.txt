**Summary of the paper:**
The paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating word embeddings for a large number of languages. The authors propose two new methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, enabling them to train embeddings for 59 languages. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to evaluation methodology. The authors identify theoretical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric and propose an improved version, multiQVEC-CCA, which is invariant to linear transformations and normalized. They empirically validate this new metric by showing it has a much stronger correlation with performance on downstream tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than previously used intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors introduce a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation and reproducible research in this area.

**Main Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is the proposal and validation of a new intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA. By identifying and fixing flaws in prior work (QVEC), and then demonstrating through a correlation analysis that the new metric is a much better proxy for extrinsic task performance, the authors provide a valuable new tool for the community.
2.  A second major contribution is the creation and release of a public evaluation portal. This resource, which provides benchmark datasets and standardized evaluation tools, significantly lowers the barrier for future research and promotes reproducibility, addressing a key challenge in the field.
3.  The paper introduces two scalable, dictionary-based methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) and demonstrates their effectiveness by training and evaluating embeddings across 59 languages, a larger scale than much prior work.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significant Advancement in Evaluation Methodology:** The paper makes a compelling case against existing intrinsic evaluation metrics like word similarity, showing their poor correlation with downstream performance (Table 1). The proposed multiQVEC-CCA is theoretically well-motivated, addressing key issues of basis invariance and normalization in the prior QVEC metric. The empirical validation showing a strong correlation with extrinsic tasks (0.896 with document classification) is a powerful result that provides the community with a more reliable and efficient way to measure progress.
2.  **High-Impact Community Resource:** The development of the evaluation portal is a standout feature of this work. By providing data, standard evaluation splits, and an easy-to-use interface for scoring new embeddings, the authors are making a substantial contribution to the research infrastructure of multilingual NLP. This will foster more direct and fair comparisons between methods and accelerate progress in the field. This alone is a strong reason for acceptance.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their proposed methods against relevant baselines. The experiments are extensive, covering nine different evaluation metrics (both intrinsic and extrinsic) and are conducted at two different scales (a 12-language set with parallel data and a 59-language set with dictionaries). This breadth of evaluation provides a nuanced view of the performance of different approaches and lends strong support to the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Confounding Vocabulary Coverage in Evaluation:** A major weakness in the experimental comparison is the significant variance in vocabulary coverage between the different methods reported in Table 3. For instance, the translation-invariance method often has the lowest coverage but achieves the highest scores on several intrinsic tasks. This makes direct comparison difficult, as models might perform better simply by avoiding harder, less frequent words present in the test sets. While the authors mention an analysis on the vocabulary intersection in the supplement, its absence from the main paper weakens the claims about the relative performance of the methods, particularly for the extrinsic tasks where coverage could be a decisive factor.
2.  **Limited Novelty of Proposed Embedding Methods:** The proposed embedding methods, multiCCA and multiCluster, are primarily practical extensions of existing ideas. The paper itself describes multiCCA as a "simple extension" of a bilingual technique to a multilingual pivot-based setup, and multiCluster is an intuitive combination of graph-based clustering and a standard skip-gram model. While their application at a massive scale is valuable, the paper lacks a core algorithmic innovation in the embedding creation process itself.
3.  **Restricted Applicability of the Proposed Evaluation Metric:** The paper's flagship contribution, the multiQVEC-CCA metric, relies on the availability of supersense-tagged corpora. Currently, these resources are noted to be available for only three European languages (English, Danish, Italian). This limits the metric's utility in the "massively multilingual" settings that the paper champions. The correlation analysis that validates the metric is also confined to this small, typologically homogeneous language set, leaving its generalizability to more diverse languages an open question.
4.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The authors note that their implementation of the multiSkip baseline uses only parallel data, whereas the original work it builds upon also leverages monolingual data. As parallel corpora are typically much smaller than monolingual ones, this likely puts the baseline at a significant disadvantage and may inflate the relative performance of the proposed methods. A more faithful implementation of the baseline would be needed for a truly fair comparison.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Regarding the large differences in coverage in Table 3: Could you please summarize the key findings from your supplementary analysis on the intersection of vocabularies? Does controlling for coverage substantially change the relative ranking of the methods, especially the comparison between multiCCA and translation-invariance on the extrinsic parsing and classification tasks?
2.  The multiSkip baseline was implemented without using additional monolingual data, differing from the cited original work. Could you comment on why this choice was made and how you believe a more faithful implementation that leverages large monolingual corpora might have performed in your experiments?
3.  The strong correlation results for multiQVEC-CCA are very encouraging but are derived from only three related European languages. What is your assessment of how this metric, and its correlation with downstream tasks, would generalize to more typologically diverse languages for which supersense annotations might become available in the future?
