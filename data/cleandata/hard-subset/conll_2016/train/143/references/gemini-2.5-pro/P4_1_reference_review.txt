### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the creation and evaluation of massively multilingual word embeddings. It makes three primary contributions. First, it proposes two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning embeddings from monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, demonstrating their scalability by training embeddings for 59 languages. Second, it introduces new intrinsic evaluation metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, which are multilingual extensions of the QVEC metric. The paper identifies theoretical flaws in the original QVEC (non-invariance to linear transformations, unnormalized score) and proposes QVEC-CCA as a technically sound alternative using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). The authors demonstrate that these new metrics correlate more strongly with downstream task performance than existing intrinsic metrics like word similarity and word translation. Third, the paper presents a public web portal designed to facilitate standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, providing datasets, benchmarks, and an upload-and-evaluate service for the community.

### Contributions
1.  **A comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's most significant contribution is its systematic approach to evaluation. It introduces multiQVEC-CCA, a novel intrinsic metric that addresses key technical limitations of its predecessor (QVEC). Crucially, the authors provide empirical evidence (Table 1) showing that their proposed metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) have substantially higher correlation with performance on extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than commonly used intrinsic metrics. This work provides the community with both a theoretically motivated metric and empirical validation of its utility.
2.  **Scalable, dictionary-based methods for creating massively multilingual embeddings.** The paper proposes two methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that leverage only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries, bypassing the need for large parallel corpora which are not available for most language pairs. The successful application of these methods to generate embeddings for 59 languages is a valuable practical contribution and demonstrates a viable path toward broad-coverage multilingual representations.
3.  **A public resource for standardized evaluation.** The development of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a valuable service to the research community. By providing standardized data, metrics, and an easy-to-use evaluation interface, this resource promotes reproducibility and lowers the barrier for future research in this area.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Focus on Principled Evaluation.** The paper's rigorous treatment of evaluation is its greatest strength. Instead of merely presenting new models and reporting scores, it critically examines the evaluation metrics themselves. The correlation analysis in §5.1 provides compelling evidence that existing intrinsic metrics like cross-lingual word similarity and word translation are poor proxies for downstream performance, while the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric is a significantly better predictor (e.g., 0.896 correlation with document classification). This finding is important for the field and helps steer future work toward more meaningful evaluation practices.
2.  **Technical Improvement of Evaluation Metrics.** The paper clearly articulates two major flaws in the QVEC metric: its lack of invariance to the embeddings' basis and its unnormalized score, which prevents comparison across different dimensionalities. The proposed QVEC-CCA method, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis, elegantly solves both issues. This is a clear, technically sound improvement that makes the metric more robust and general.
3.  **Demonstrated Scalability to a Large Number of Languages.** A key challenge in multilingual NLP is scaling beyond a handful of high-resource languages. By training and evaluating embeddings for 59 languages using dictionary-based methods, the authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach on a massive scale. This is a significant step forward from typical bilingual or few-language studies.
4.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup.** The experiments compare four distinct embedding estimation methods across nine different evaluation metrics, including two extrinsic tasks. This broad comparison, conducted on both a 12-language set (allowing for comparison with parallel-data methods) and a 59-language set, provides a rich and nuanced view of the performance landscape for multilingual embeddings.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Embedding Estimation Methods.** The proposed estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, appear to be incremental extensions of prior work. The paper itself describes multiCCA as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014). The multiCluster approach, which groups translation equivalents into a single node and runs a monolingual algorithm, is intuitive but conceptually straightforward. While their application at a massive scale is a contribution, the methods themselves lack significant technical novelty.
2.  **Inconsistent Performance of Proposed Methods.** The experimental results in Table 3 do not present a clear winner among the embedding estimation methods. The proposed multiCluster method performs best on dependency parsing, but the proposed multiCCA method does not. Conversely, the baseline `translation-invariance` method outperforms both proposed methods on four of the seven intrinsic metrics. This mixed result weakens the claim that the proposed *estimation methods* are superior, shifting the paper's main strength to its contributions in evaluation.
3.  **Confounding Effect of Vocabulary Coverage.** The paper correctly identifies that differences in vocabulary coverage can bias intrinsic evaluation scores. For example, in Table 3's monolingual word similarity task, the `translation-invariance` method scores highest (51.0) but has the lowest coverage (23.0%), while multiCCA scores lower (43.0) with much higher coverage (71.0%). It is difficult to conclude that one is superior to the other under these conditions. While the authors mention an analysis on the vocabulary intersection is provided in supplementary material, this is a critical issue that significantly impacts the interpretation of the main results and should have been addressed more centrally.
4.  **Dependence on Proprietary Tools for Data Creation.** The 59-language experiments rely on dictionaries created using Google Translate. This introduces a dependency on a closed-source, commercial system whose output can change over time, potentially hindering exact reproducibility. The quality of these automatically generated dictionaries is a critical but unanalyzed variable that directly impacts the quality of the resulting embeddings.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The `multiSkip` baseline is noted to be trained only on parallel corpora, whereas the original works it is based on (e.g., Guo et al., 2016) also use monolingual corpora. This seems to place the baseline at a significant disadvantage, as it cannot leverage the large amounts of monolingual data available to the other methods. Could you clarify the reasoning for this implementation choice and speculate on how the performance of this baseline might change if it were augmented with monolingual data, creating a more direct comparison?
2.  The results in Table 3 show that `multiCluster` is the best method for dependency parsing, while `multiCCA` performs better on tasks like cross-lingual word similarity. This suggests the methods may capture different types of linguistic information. Do you have an intuition as to why this might be? For example, does the clustering approach in `multiCluster` better preserve structural or syntactic regularities useful for parsing?
3.  The multiQVEC-CCA metric relies on the availability of multilingual supersense annotations, which are scarce. The paper suggests that for syntactic tasks, a linguistic matrix could be constructed from universal POS tags. Have you performed any experiments to validate this suggestion, for instance by checking if a POS-based QVEC-CCA correlates well with dependency parsing performance? This would strengthen the case for the metric's general applicability.
