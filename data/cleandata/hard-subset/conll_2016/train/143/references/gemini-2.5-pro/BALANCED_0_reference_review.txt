### Review

**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper addresses the important and growing area of massively multilingual word embeddings. The authors present two dictionary-based methods for training embeddings across many languages, an improved intrinsic evaluation metric, and a public web portal to facilitate standardized evaluation.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1.  A significant advancement in the methodology for evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The paper identifies critical theoretical flaws (lack of basis invariance, unnormalized scores) in the existing QVEC metric and proposes QVEC-CCA as a robust alternative. Crucially, the authors provide strong empirical evidence (Table 1) that their proposed metrics correlate much more strongly with downstream task performance than widely-used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation.
2.  The development and release of a public evaluation portal. This is a highly valuable practical contribution to the research community. By providing standardized datasets, evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparison, this portal lowers the barrier to entry and promotes reproducible, comparable research in the field.
3.  Two simple and scalable dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for creating multilingual embeddings. These methods are demonstrated to work for up to 59 languages and serve as effective baselines, with multiCCA in particular showing strong performance on extrinsic tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong and Critical Focus on Evaluation:** The paper's most impressive strength is its deep engagement with the problem of evaluation. The analysis of QVEC's shortcomings is sharp, and the proposed QVEC-CCA solution is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The correlation study in §5.1 is a key result that convincingly argues for the adoption of better intrinsic metrics that are more predictive of extrinsic utility. This careful work on evaluation methodology is essential for moving the field forward in a principled way.
2.  **Excellent Contribution to the Community:** The creation of an open evaluation portal is an outstanding contribution. It addresses a major bottleneck in the field: the difficulty of performing standardized, replicable evaluations. Such resources are critical for fostering a healthy research ecosystem and can have a more lasting impact than any single model. This is a commendable effort that will benefit many researchers.
3.  **Comprehensive and Large-Scale Experiments:** The scale of the experiments is impressive, covering up to 59 languages and comparing four distinct methods across a suite of nine different intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The authors demonstrate a commitment to thoroughness, for instance by discussing and attempting to mitigate the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on intrinsic scores. This comprehensive evaluation provides a solid empirical grounding for the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed Embedding Methods:** The proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, are presented as primary contributions but are methodologically incremental. The authors rightly describe multiCCA as a "simple extension" of prior work, and multiCluster is an intuitive but straightforward application of graph clustering. While their effectiveness as baselines is well-demonstrated, they do not represent a major conceptual advance in representation learning. The paper would be stronger if it framed its core contribution more explicitly around the evaluation methodology and resources, with the methods serving as a case study.
2.  **Unexamined Limitations of the Pivot-Based Approach:** The multiCCA method relies entirely on English as a pivot language, projecting all other languages into a shared space defined by the initial English embeddings. This design choice has potential drawbacks that are not discussed, such as propagating English-specific linguistic biases or accumulating errors in a hub-and-spoke model. It is unclear how the choice of pivot language might affect the quality and geometric properties of the resulting multilingual space.
3.  **Tension Between Evaluation Metric and Application Scale:** While multiQVEC-CCA is shown to be an excellent evaluation metric, its reliance on manually annotated linguistic resources (supersense tags) restricts its applicability to a very small number of languages (three in this paper). This creates a disconnect: the paper's best proposed metric cannot be used to evaluate the "massively multilingual" 59-language embeddings that are one of its main outputs. While the authors suggest using Universal POS tags as an alternative, this is not explored or validated, leaving a gap between the proposed solution and the target problem scale.
4.  **Inconsistent Results and Confounding Factors:** The results in Table 3 do not point to a single superior method. The translation-invariance baseline outperforms the proposed methods on several intrinsic tasks, while multiCCA and multiCluster perform better on extrinsic tasks. This makes it difficult to draw a clear conclusion about which method is best overall. Moreover, the large differences in vocabulary coverage between methods severely confound the comparison of intrinsic scores, a point the authors acknowledge but which remains a significant caveat for interpreting the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method is pivot-based. Did you experiment with using other well-resourced languages from the 12-language set as the pivot? How sensitive are the results, particularly on extrinsic tasks, to the choice of the pivot language?
2.  In your results (Table 3), your proposed dictionary-based methods excel on extrinsic tasks (parsing, doc classification), whereas the translation-invariance method performs best on several intrinsic tasks (word translation, multiQVEC-CCA). How do you interpret this divergence? Does it imply that different architectures are optimized for different linguistic properties (e.g., syntactic vs. semantic), and that our evaluation suite needs to be even more diverse to capture this?
3.  You rightly point out that the reliance on supersense tags limits the applicability of multiQVEC-CCA. Could you elaborate on the feasibility of using a resource like Universal POS tags to create a more widely applicable correlation-based metric? What kind of performance would you expect such a metric to have in predicting performance on semantic-heavy downstream tasks like document classification?
