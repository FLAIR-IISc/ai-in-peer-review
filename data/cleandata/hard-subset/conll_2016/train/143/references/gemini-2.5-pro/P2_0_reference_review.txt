### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The authors present contributions in three main areas:

1.  **New Evaluation Metrics:** The paper's primary contribution is the development of a new intrinsic evaluation metric, **multiQVEC-CCA**. It identifies two key theoretical flaws in the existing QVEC metric (lack of invariance to linear transformations and an unnormalized score) and proposes a solution using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). Crucially, the authors demonstrate through a rigorous correlation study (Table 1) that their proposed metric, along with multiQVEC, correlates much more strongly with downstream task performance than previously used intrinsic metrics like word similarity and translation. This is a significant contribution that can help guide future research in the field more effectively.
2.  **Resources for the Community:** The second major contribution is the creation of substantial resources. This includes training and releasing embeddings for 59 languages, which is a commendable engineering effort. More importantly, they have developed an easy-to-use web portal for uploading and evaluating multilingual embeddings on a standardized suite of tasks. This portal, along with the associated benchmarks, significantly lowers the barrier to entry for new research and promotes reproducible science.
3.  **Scalable Embedding Methods:** The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods, **multiCluster** and **multiCCA**, for training embeddings at scale. While these methods are conceptually straightforward extensions of existing ideas, their value is demonstrated in their ability to scale to 59 languages using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, a more accessible resource than large parallel corpora.

### Strengths

1.  **Rigorous and Impactful Work on Evaluation:** The most compelling part of this paper is its focus on *how* to evaluate multilingual embeddings. The critique of existing intrinsic metrics is well-founded, and the proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` is an elegant and theoretically sound improvement. The experiment in §5.1, which computes correlations between four intrinsic and two extrinsic metrics over 17 different embedding models, is thorough and provides strong evidence for the superiority of their proposed metrics (e.g., a correlation of 0.896 for `multiQVEC-CCA` with document classification, compared to 0.386 for word similarity). This work provides a clear path forward for more reliable intrinsic evaluation.

2.  **Impressive Scale and Valuable Community Resources:** The scale of the experiments—training embeddings for 59 languages—is a significant strength. This moves beyond the typical bilingual or few-language setting and tackles the "massively multilingual" problem head-on. The public release of these embeddings, coupled with the evaluation portal, is a substantial service to the NLP community that will facilitate future research and fair comparisons.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper evaluates the proposed and baseline methods across a diverse set of nine different metrics, including both intrinsic (word similarity, translation, QVEC variants) and extrinsic (document classification, dependency parsing) tasks. The authors also demonstrate good scientific practice by carefully considering and reporting vocabulary coverage as a potential confounding factor, and even performing analysis on the intersection of vocabularies to ensure fair comparison.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed embedding estimation methods, `multiCCA` and `multiCluster`, are not significant methodological breakthroughs. The authors themselves describe `multiCCA` as a "simple extension (in hindsight)" of prior work, using a standard pivot-based projection strategy. `multiCluster` is an intuitive approach, but its core idea of merging translation pairs into single units is related to earlier work in cross-lingual clustering. The paper's strength lies in the application and evaluation of these methods at scale, not in their algorithmic novelty.

2.  **Inconclusive Results on Method Comparison:** The results in Table 3 do not present a clear case for the general superiority of the proposed methods. While a proposed method (`multiCluster`) performs best on the two extrinsic tasks, the `translation-invariance` baseline outperforms all other methods on four of the seven intrinsic tasks, including the paper's own proposed `multiQVEC-CCA` metric. The authors acknowledge that the "ranking... is not consistent," which leaves the reader without a clear takeaway regarding which method to prefer. This suggests that the performance is highly task- and metric-dependent.

3.  **Unexamined Reliance on a Pivot Language:** The `multiCCA` method is entirely dependent on a single high-resource pivot language (English) to create the shared space. This is a practical design choice, but it comes with potential drawbacks that are not discussed. For instance, information may be lost when projecting from a non-pivot language into the English space, and this may disproportionately affect languages that are typologically distant from English. The quality of embeddings for a language pair like Finnish-Hungarian could be degraded by forcing them through an English pivot.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The comparison in Table 3 is inconclusive. Could you elaborate on the qualitative differences and trade-offs between your best-performing method on extrinsic tasks (`multiCluster` or `multiCCA` in different settings) and the `translation-invariance` method, which performed best on your own `multiQVEC-CCA` metric? Are there specific linguistic properties that one method captures better than the other, which would explain this divergence?

2.  Your `multiSkip` baseline performs poorly across the board. In Footnote 4, you mention that your implementation only uses parallel corpora, while related work also uses monolingual data. Could this implementation choice be putting `multiSkip` at an unfair disadvantage, especially since all other methods leverage large monolingual corpora?

3.  The `multiCCA` method pivots through English. Have you considered the potential biases this introduces? For example, does this architecture perform worse on language pairs that are typologically distant from English compared to pairs where one language is English? Would a multi-pivot or non-pivot approach be feasible at this scale?
