**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings. The authors present three main contributions. First, they propose two new intrinsic evaluation metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, which adapt and improve upon the existing QVEC metric by addressing its theoretical shortcomings (e.g., basis variance) and extending it to a multilingual setting. They provide an empirical analysis showing these metrics correlate better with downstream task performance than standard intrinsic evaluations. Second, the authors develop a public web portal to serve as a standardized benchmark for evaluating multilingual embeddings, a valuable community resource. Third, they introduce two dictionary-based methods for training embeddings, multiCluster and multiCCA, which are scalable to a large number of languages (demonstrated on 59) and do not require parallel corpora.

**Strengths**

1.  **Improved Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's strongest contribution is its focus on improving the evaluation of multilingual embeddings. The authors provide a clear and valid critique of the original QVEC metric, identifying its lack of invariance to linear transformations and its unnormalized scoring. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA, which uses Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA), is a theoretically sound and elegant solution to these problems. The correlation analysis in Table 1, showing multiQVEC-CCA's strong correlation (0.896) with document classification, provides compelling evidence that this line of work is a step towards more meaningful intrinsic evaluation.

2.  **A Valuable Community Resource:** The creation of a web portal for standardized evaluation is a commendable and significant contribution. If maintained, such a resource could greatly benefit the field by facilitating reproducible research, lowering the barrier to entry for evaluation, and helping to consolidate community efforts around a common set of benchmarks. This practical contribution has the potential for high impact.

3.  **Scalability of Proposed Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods are simple and rely only on monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries. This makes them highly scalable, as demonstrated by the successful training of embeddings for 59 languages. This is a practical advantage over methods that depend on parallel corpora, which are a scarce resource for most of the world's languages.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Methodological and Technical Obscurity:** The paper lacks precision in describing key technical details, raising concerns about reproducibility and correctness.
    *   The formulation for multiCCA embeddings is non-standard and poorly explained. The equation `ECCA(m, v) = T−1en→m,enTm→m,enEm(v)` is puzzling. Standard CCA-based approaches project vectors from a source space into a shared space; the use of an inverted projection matrix `T−1en→m,en` is not justified and its purpose is entirely unclear, especially given the claim that the English space itself serves as the target space.
    *   The QVEC-CCA metric is defined as `CCA(X>,S>)`, but CCA produces a vector of canonical correlations, not a single value. The paper should specify if it is using the first correlation coefficient (`ρ₁`) or some aggregation.
    *   Key hyperparameter choices are arbitrary. For instance, the dictionary extraction threshold `τ = 0.1` was set based on "manual inspection" (Footnote 3), a non-rigorous and irreproducible process.

2.  **Flawed and Unfair Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental setup appears to systematically disadvantage the baseline methods, calling the validity of the results into question.
    *   The `translation-invariance` baseline was trained with a smaller context window (size 3) than the proposed methods (size 5) due to "computational challenges" (Footnote 12). This is not a scientifically valid reason for using a potentially suboptimal hyperparameter setting for a key baseline, and it may artificially deflate its performance.
    *   The authors' implementation of `multiSkip` is intentionally hobbled by not using monolingual data, a deviation from prior work which they themselves acknowledge as a major limitation. It is unclear why a known-to-be-weaker version of a baseline was used.
    *   The vocabulary coverage of the `translation-invariance` method is drastically lower than the other methods (e.g., 23.0% vs. 71.0% for multiCCA on one task). While the authors commendably report this, comparing scores across such different test sets is deeply problematic. The fairer comparison on the vocabulary intersection is relegated to supplementary material, but this should be a primary result.

3.  **Pervasive Lack of Polish and Attention to Detail:** The manuscript is marred by numerous small errors that collectively suggest a lack of care and undermine confidence in the work.
    *   The evaluation portal is hosted on a raw IP address (http://128.2.220.95/multilingual). This is unprofessional and provides no guarantee of persistence, which is essential for a resource intended for community use.
    *   The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Several entries are malformed (e.g., Agić et al., 2015), contain BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `\Q2015\E`), or use incorrect keys (e.g., `Agirre et al\.,?`). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   There are minor formatting issues throughout the text, such as an improper line break in "therefore" (end of §1).

4.  **Contradiction in Scope:** There is a mismatch between the paper's "massively multilingual" framing (59 languages) and the limited applicability of its main evaluation contribution. The proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric relies on supersense-tagged resources that are only available for 3 languages (EN, IT, DA), a fact acknowledged in Footnote 9. This severely restricts the metric's utility in the very setting the paper champions.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please provide a clear derivation and justification for the multiCCA projection formula, particularly the role of the `T−1en→m,en` matrix. How does this achieve the stated goal of projecting into the English embedding space?
2.  Can you justify running the `translation-invariance` baseline with a smaller context window? Furthermore, given the large coverage discrepancies, could you please present the results evaluated on the intersection of vocabularies in the main paper to enable a more direct and fair comparison?
3.  The correlation analysis in §5.1 is based on "17 different multilingual embeddings". Please specify what these 17 variations are (e.g., hyperparameter settings) to make this experiment and its conclusions interpretable.
4.  What are your concrete plans to ensure the long-term stability and accessibility of the evaluation portal beyond the temporary IP address provided?
