### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of creating and evaluating "massively multilingual" word embeddings, i.e., shared vector spaces for a large number of languages. The authors' primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A significant advance in evaluation methodology for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's strongest contribution is its rigorous analysis of intrinsic evaluation metrics. It introduces multiQVEC-CCA, an improved, basis-invariant version of QVEC, and demonstrates through correlation analysis that it is a much better predictor of downstream performance than widely used metrics like word similarity and word translation. The creation of a public evaluation portal is a major part of this contribution, promising to standardize and accelerate research in the area.
2.  **Two pragmatic, dictionary-based methods for learning multilingual embeddings at scale.** The paper proposes multiCluster and multiCCA. Of these, multiCCA stands out as a simple, effective, and scalable hub-and-spoke model that projects monolingual spaces into a common pivot space using CCA. This approach bypasses the need for large parallel corpora, enabling the authors to train embeddings for 59 languages.
3.  **The release of valuable large-scale resources.** The 59-language embeddings themselves are a useful resource for the community, enabling transfer learning and cross-lingual applications for many lower-resource languages.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent focus on the problem of evaluation.** The field often defaults to intrinsic metrics like word similarity without rigorously questioning their validity. This paper's core strength is its systematic evaluation *of the evaluation metrics themselves*. Table 1 provides compelling evidence that popular metrics correlate poorly with extrinsic task performance, a crucial finding for the community. The proposal of multiQVEC-CCA, which corrects theoretical flaws (basis-invariance, score normalization) in prior work (QVEC), is a well-motivated and impactful contribution.
2.  **Scalability and practicality of the proposed methods.** The multiCCA method is particularly noteworthy for its practicality. By relying only on bilingual dictionaries (which can be generated automatically, e.g., via Google Translate as done here) and a pivot language, it offers a feasible path to creating embeddings for dozens or even hundreds of languages. This contrasts sharply with methods that require large, curated parallel corpora, which are unavailable for most language pairs. The strong performance of this relatively simple method on extrinsic tasks demonstrates its effectiveness.
3.  **Commitment to reproducible research and community building.** The development of the web portal for standardized evaluation is a commendable effort that goes beyond a typical research paper. By providing data, benchmarks, and an upload-and-evaluate service, the authors are establishing infrastructure that can significantly benefit the field, improve the reliability of reported results, and lower the barrier for future work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty of the embedding methods is somewhat limited.** The paper is transparent that multiCCA is a "simple extension" of prior bilingual work (Faruqui and Dyer, 2014). While its application and scaling to 59 languages are novel and impressive, the core technical idea is incremental. Similarly, the multiCluster approach of merging words based on a translation graph is conceptually straightforward. The paper's main value lies more in the evaluation and application of these ideas at scale rather than in groundbreaking algorithmic innovation.
2.  **Experimental comparisons are confounded by large differences in vocabulary coverage.** The authors correctly identify this issue, but it remains a significant weakness in the experimental results presented in Table 3. The translation-invariance baseline, for example, has dramatically lower coverage than other methods on several tasks (e.g., 31.3% on doc classification vs. 62.8% for multiCCA). This makes a direct comparison of scores highly problematic. The method's high score on word translation (63.9) might be an artifact of it only covering a small, "easy" subset of word pairs for which it has high confidence. While this is discussed and addressed in supplementary material, it weakens the main conclusions drawn from Table 3.
3.  **The applicability of the proposed best metric (multiQVEC-CCA) is limited.** The paper's primary evaluation contribution, multiQVEC-CCA, is shown to work well for English, Italian, and Danish. However, it depends on the existence of manually-annotated linguistic resources (supersense tags), which are not available for the vast majority of the 59 languages for which embeddings are built. This creates a disconnect: the paper provides a tool for building massively multilingual embeddings but proposes an evaluation metric that cannot be applied in that massively multilingual setting. The suggestion to use universal POS tags is an interesting idea but remains unevaluated.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The hub-and-spoke architecture of multiCCA makes the choice of the pivot language (English) very important. All other languages are projected into this space. Have you analyzed the impact of this choice? For example, how would the geometry of the space change if a morphologically rich language was chosen as the pivot? Could errors or biases in the English embeddings propagate to all other languages?
2.  Table 1 shows a surprising *negative* correlation (-0.292) between the word translation task and dependency parsing performance. This is counter-intuitive, as one might expect that a space where translations are close would be good for cross-lingual structure transfer. Do you have a hypothesis for why this might be the case?
3.  The multiCluster method's performance degrades significantly when moving from 12 to 59 languages, which you attribute to increased semantic ambiguity. Could this approach be improved with a "soft" representation, where a word's embedding is a mixture of its cluster's vector and a language-specific deviation vector, potentially mitigating the ambiguity issue?
