## Peer Review for CoNLL Submission: "Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings"

**Summary:**

This paper presents two dictionary-based methods – multiCluster and multiCCA – for generating word embeddings across 59 languages. It also introduces new evaluation metrics, multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, designed to better assess the quality of these multilingual embeddings, and provides a publicly accessible web portal for evaluating arbitrary multilingual embeddings. The core idea is to leverage bilingual dictionaries to bridge semantic spaces across numerous languages, avoiding reliance on parallel corpora (though they can be incorporated).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The most significant contribution is the creation of the multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA metrics and the associated evaluation portal. Existing evaluation methods for multilingual embeddings are limited. This work provides a valuable tool and benchmark for the community.
2. **Scalable Dictionary-Based Embedding Approach:** The paper demonstrates a viable approach for generating multilingual embeddings for a large number of languages (59) using only bilingual dictionaries, which are more readily available than parallel corpora.
3. **Empirical Analysis of Evaluation Metrics:** The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics is insightful, demonstrating the shortcomings of existing metrics and validating the proposed ones.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Need:**  Multilingual embeddings are crucial for various NLP tasks in a low-resource setting. Scaling these to a large number of languages is a significant challenge, and this work tackles it directly.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides extensive experimental results, comparing different methods and evaluation metrics. The correlation analysis is particularly convincing in demonstrating the superiority of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations strengthens the findings.
3. **Practical Impact via Public Resources:** The web portal is a huge asset to the community. Providing the data, code, and evaluation tools promotes reproducibility and further research in the field.  This is a considerable contribution beyond just the algorithmic advancements.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanations of the methods and metrics are clear and concise.
5. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison against several existing methods (multiSkip, translation-invariance) provides a strong baseline for evaluating the proposed approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** While the scaling to 59 languages is noteworthy, the underlying methods (multiCCA and multiCluster) build directly on existing techniques (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014 and clustering approaches respectively). The core algorithmic innovation is relatively incremental. The multiCCA method feels like a straightforward extension of existing CCA techniques.
2. **Dictionary Quality and Bias:**  The reliance on bilingual dictionaries, especially those automatically generated from Google Translate for the 47 less-resourced languages, introduces potential noise and bias. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of this data quality issue isn't thoroughly investigated. The threshold τ=0.1 feels arbitrary and its impact wasn't sufficiently explored.
3. **Extrinsic Task Selection:** The choice of document classification and dependency parsing as extrinsic tasks is somewhat limited. These tasks don’t fully capture the breadth of potential applications for multilingual embeddings. Exploring tasks like cross-lingual information retrieval or machine translation would provide a more complete evaluation.
4. **Coverage Concerns:** The varying coverage of the embeddings across languages, particularly due to dictionary availability, raises concerns about the fairness of comparisons. While the paper attempts to mitigate this in the correlation analysis, it remains a potential confound.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality of the automatically generated dictionaries (especially those from Google Translate)?  What steps were taken to filter or validate these dictionaries?
2. **Threshold Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the threshold τ used for dictionary creation? Did you experiment with different values of τ, and what impact did they have on the performance of the methods?
3. **Scalability Bottlenecks:** What are the major computational bottlenecks when scaling the methods to even more languages?  Are there any foreseeable limits to the scalability of the proposed approach?
4. **Extrinsic Task Justification:** Why were document classification and dependency parsing specifically chosen as the extrinsic tasks? What considerations guided this selection, and how representative are these tasks of the broader potential applications of multilingual embeddings?
5. **MultiCCA and English Dependence**: The reliance on English as the hub language for multiCCA seems potentially limiting. Were other hub languages considered and why was English chosen? Does the choice of hub language significantly affect performance?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of dictionary-based approaches in general, especially concerning polysemy and contextual information.
* The supplementary material should include detailed statistics about the data used, including dictionary size and coverage for each language pair.
* The URLs to the code and data on the evaluation portal should be made publicly available in the final version of the paper.
