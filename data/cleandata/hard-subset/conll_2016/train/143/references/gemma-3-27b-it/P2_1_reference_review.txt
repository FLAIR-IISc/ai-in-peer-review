## Review for CoNLL Paper

**Summary:**

This paper investigates methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA). It also introduces improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) for these embeddings, addressing shortcomings of existing methods, and provides a publicly available evaluation portal. The core goal is to facilitate research in multilingual NLP by providing tools and benchmarks for evaluating embeddings across a large number of languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of the multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA metrics *and* the accompanying evaluation portal represent the most significant contribution. This provides the community with much-needed tools to reliably assess multilingual embeddings and compare different methods. The correlation analysis demonstrating the superiority of these metrics over existing ones is particularly valuable.
2. **Practical Dictionary-Based Methods:** While not radically novel in concept, the practical implementation and scaling of multiCCA and multiCluster to 59 languages is a solid engineering achievement. The demonstration of reasonable performance across this many languages is useful.
3. **Empirical Baseline & Data:** Providing embeddings trained on a large number of languages, along with the associated data and code, serves as a valuable baseline for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Relevance:** The paper addresses a key challenge in multilingual NLP – the need for robust methods and reliable evaluation of multilingual word embeddings. The potential applications (machine translation, transfer learning) are well-articulated.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, employing both intrinsic *and* extrinsic metrics.  The thorough correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic measures is a standout feature, convincingly demonstrating the value of the proposed evaluation metrics.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of the evaluation portal, code, and pre-trained embeddings is a major benefit to the research community. This promotes reproducibility and facilitates further progress in the field.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The technical details of the methods are explained clearly. The inclusion of tables summarizing results is helpful.
5. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Metrics:** The authors correctly identify and address weaknesses in prior evaluation methods (QVEC), leading to improvements in their proposed metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Methods:** The core technical ideas behind multiCluster and multiCCA are based on established techniques (clustering and CCA). The primary novelty lies in their application and scaling to a large number of languages, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. This doesn't negate their value, but it's important to acknowledge.
2. **Dependency on Dictionary Quality:** The performance of the dictionary-based methods is highly sensitive to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't fully explore the impact of dictionary noise or the limitations of using Google Translate for dictionary creation for many languages. The coverage issue is a real limitation.
3. **Extrinsic Task Performance:** While the correlation analysis shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates well with extrinsic tasks, the actual performance on the downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) is not particularly impressive. It’s difficult to ascertain if the observed gains are substantial in a real-world application setting.
4. **Overly Optimistic Self-Comparison:** The claims of superiority in Tables 2 & 3 are somewhat muted by the caveats about coverage and vocabulary overlap.  More direct comparisons with a wider range of existing methods *using a consistent evaluation setup* would strengthen the conclusions.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results of multiCCA and multiCluster to the quality of the input dictionaries? Could you provide an analysis of the impact of dictionary noise or errors on performance?
2.  You mention the limitations of using Google Translate for dictionary creation. Have you explored alternative methods for constructing dictionaries for low-resource languages, and could you discuss the trade-offs between these methods?
3.  Regarding the extrinsic task results:  How do the performance gains on document classification and dependency parsing compare to baseline systems that *do not* utilize pre-trained word embeddings?  This would help contextualize the impact of the multilingual embeddings.
4.  You note a difference in correlation with downstream tasks between multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. Can you explain the theoretical reasons why multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with document classification while multiQVEC correlates better with dependency parsing?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The URL for the evaluation portal should be checked to ensure it is publicly accessible during the review period.
*   The supplementary material referenced in the paper should be available for reviewers.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of multilingual NLP. While the proposed methods aren't groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation framework and the release of valuable resources make this paper worthy of acceptance. The strengths of the work – the rigorous evaluation, public availability of tools, and clear presentation – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions above.
