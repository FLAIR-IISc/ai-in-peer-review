This paper presents two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating multilingual word embeddings, alongside a novel evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, and a web portal for evaluating such embeddings. The primary contribution is the extensive empirical evaluation of these methods across a significant number of languages (59), coupled with the provision of the data, code, and evaluation portal to facilitate future research. A secondary contribution lies in the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric, which addresses perceived shortcomings in existing evaluation methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper’s most significant strength is the breadth of its empirical evaluation. Assessing methods across 59 languages is a substantial undertaking and provides valuable insights into the generalizability of the proposed approaches. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, while not entirely novel, is commendable.
2. **Resource Provision:** The release of the data, code, and the evaluation portal significantly enhances the reproducibility and accessibility of the research. This is a critical benefit to the community, lowering the barrier to entry for researchers working in multilingual embeddings.
3. **Addressing Evaluation Challenges:** Recognizing the limitations of existing evaluation metrics and proposing multiQVEC-CCA represents a thoughtful contribution. The authors’ analysis of the shortcomings of QVEC, and the attempt to address these, is valuable. The correlation analysis (Table 1) provides evidence, although not conclusive, that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methods:** While the multiCluster and multiCCA methods are presented as novel, they largely represent straightforward extensions of existing techniques (CCA, skipgram, clustering). The conceptual leap is minimal, and the justification for these specific extensions isn't strongly motivated. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, essentially framing the methods as a vehicle for evaluation rather than groundbreaking advances.
2. **Inconsistent Results & Limited Analysis:** The results (Table 3) show an inconsistent ranking of methods across different evaluation metrics. While the authors note this, they offer a surprisingly superficial analysis of *why* these discrepancies occur. A deeper dive into the characteristics of the metrics and the specific strengths and weaknesses of each method is needed. For example, the coverage issue is acknowledged but only partially addressed.
3. **Overreliance on English as a Hub:** The decision to use English as the central vector space for multiCCA feels arbitrary and potentially limiting. The rationale ("since English typically offers the largest corpora") feels pragmatic rather than theoretically justified. The impact of this design choice on the quality of embeddings for non-English languages isn't adequately explored.
4. **Minor Formatting & Detail Issues:** Numerous minor issues detract from the paper's polish. Footnotes are used inconsistently (e.g., defining a method in a footnote, citing a URL with a number), and there are grammatical errors ("we show that the proposed methods work well in some settings and evaluation metrics" – should be “on some…”). The description of the dictionary creation process (using Google Translate) is concerning; the quality of those dictionaries is not discussed, and reliance on automated translation introduces significant noise. The implementation details could also be more rigorous (e.g., fixed τ = 0.1 based on "manual inspection" lacks sufficient justification).
5. **Insufficient Justification for Parameter Choices:** The choice of a maximum cluster size of 1,000 (and 10,000) for multiCluster is not motivated. An ablation study or sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of this parameter would strengthen the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing English as the hub language in multiCCA? Have you explored alternative hub languages, and if so, what were the results?
2.  The paper mentions that “the ranking of the four estimation methods is not consistent across all evaluation metrics.” Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of *why* this is the case? Are there specific characteristics of the languages or datasets that explain these discrepancies?
3.  The dictionary creation process using Google Translate raises concerns about data quality. How did you assess the quality of these automatically translated dictionaries? What steps were taken to mitigate potential noise introduced by translation errors?
4.  What justification is there for the choice of a context window size of 3 for the translation-invariance method and 5 for the skipgram-based methods? Was this choice tuned or simply selected arbitrarily?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper's length is appropriate, but tighter editing could improve clarity and conciseness.
*   The reference list is extensive, but formatting inconsistencies are present.
*   The URL for the evaluation portal should be checked for accessibility.
* The sentence "In a postprocessing step, all vectors are normalized to unit length" should specify *when* this normalization is performed relative to other operations.



Despite the extensive empirical work and the valuable resources provided, the lack of significant methodological novelty and the presence of numerous minor flaws lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the paper is not without merit, the cumulative effect of these issues undermines its overall impact and quality. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, and with a stronger focus on the analysis of the results, would be necessary for reconsideration.
