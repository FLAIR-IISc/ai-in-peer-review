This paper presents two dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, alongside a refined evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA) and a publicly available evaluation portal. The core contribution lies in the development and empirical evaluation of these methods for handling a relatively large number of languages (59) based solely on bilingual dictionaries and monolingual data, a significant step beyond the typical bilingual-only approaches. A second notable contribution is the improved evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, addressing shortcomings of existing metrics and demonstrating better correlation with downstream tasks. Finally, the evaluation portal itself is a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Scalability to a Large Number of Languages:** The primary strength is the demonstrated ability to generate multilingual embeddings for 59 languages using only bilingual dictionaries and monolingual corpora. This is a significant advancement, as many existing methods struggle with scalability beyond a few languages. This broader coverage has the potential to unlock more effective cross-lingual transfer learning and resource-poor language processing.
2. **Improved Evaluation Metric:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA represents a significant improvement over existing intrinsic evaluation metrics. The theoretical justification for addressing the limitations of QVEC, combined with the empirical evidence showing stronger correlation with downstream tasks, provides a compelling argument for its adoption. This is crucial for reliable comparison of different multilingual embedding methods.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The creation and release of the evaluation portal is a valuable contribution to the community. Providing a standardized platform for evaluating multilingual embeddings will facilitate reproducible research and accelerate progress in the field. The availability of the data and trained embeddings further enhances this impact.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing the proposed methods with baselines across multiple evaluation metrics and downstream tasks. The ablation studies and analysis of results provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The careful consideration of vocabulary coverage during evaluation is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** While the application of multiCluster and multiCCA to a large number of languages is significant, the underlying techniques themselves (clustering and CCA) are well-established in the literature. The paper doesn't present fundamentally new algorithmic breakthroughs in embedding estimation. The novelty is more in the *scale* and integration of these existing methods rather than the methods themselves.
2. **Dictionary Quality Dependence:** The performance of the methods is highly dependent on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges the use of Google Translate for generating dictionaries for some languages, which introduces potential noise and inaccuracies.  A more detailed analysis of the impact of dictionary quality on the resulting embeddings would be beneficial.
3. **MultiSkip Baseline Weakness:** The description of multiSkip highlights its limitation of *only* utilizing parallel data and not monolingual data. This severely disadvantages the baseline and might skew the comparison towards methods that *do* leverage monolingual data (i.e., the proposed methods).  A stronger baseline incorporating monolingual data, even if it’s a more recent or complex method, would provide a more robust evaluation.
4. **Downstream Task Selection:** The choice of document classification and dependency parsing as downstream tasks is somewhat limited. Including tasks that more directly rely on semantic understanding (e.g., cross-lingual information retrieval, machine translation) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the embeddings' utility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of dictionary quality (specifically, the automatically translated dictionaries) on the performance of your methods?  Were any filtering or post-processing steps applied to these dictionaries?
2.  You mention that multiSkip is limited to parallel data. Could you briefly discuss why you chose this particular implementation of multiSkip and not a more competitive baseline incorporating monolingual data?
3.  What are the computational resources required to train the embeddings for 59 languages, and how does this scale with the number of languages?  Could you provide some insights into the efficiency of your methods?
4.  Regarding the evaluation portal, will there be provisions for users to submit their own evaluation datasets and metrics, or is it currently limited to the datasets provided in the paper?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The extensive experimental results are well-organized and easy to interpret.
*   The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the field.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the potential biases introduced by using English as the central language in the multiCCA method.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations in the novelty of the core methods, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. The scalability to a large number of languages, the improved evaluation metric, and the publicly available resources make it a worthwhile addition to the CoNLL conference. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
