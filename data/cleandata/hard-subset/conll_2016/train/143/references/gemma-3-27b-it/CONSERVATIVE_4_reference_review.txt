This paper explores methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings using dictionary-based approaches and proposes new evaluation metrics. The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of *multiCCA*, a dictionary-based method for extending bilingual embedding techniques to multiple languages. A secondary contribution is the proposal of *multiQVEC-CCA*, an improved evaluation metric for multilingual embeddings intended to address shortcomings of the existing *multiQVEC* metric. A third, supporting contribution is the creation of a web portal to facilitate evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough evaluation of their proposed methods against several baselines, utilizing both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. This is a significant strength, as it attempts to provide a holistic assessment of the embeddings' quality. The inclusion of a web portal to facilitate further evaluation by the community is commendable.
2. **MultiCCA shows promise:** While the gains aren’t overwhelming, *multiCCA* consistently outperforms *multiCluster* across the reported metrics. This suggests a potentially viable direction for multilingual embedding estimation, particularly when leveraging readily available bilingual dictionaries.
3. **Addressing limitations in Evaluation:** The authors correctly identify shortcomings in existing multilingual embedding evaluation metrics, particularly *QVEC*, and propose a refined version, *multiQVEC-CCA*. While the actual improvement needs further scrutiny (see weaknesses), the recognition of the issue is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Substantial Novelty:** The core ideas—dictionary-based embedding alignment and leveraging monolingual data—are not novel. The paper extends existing techniques (Faruqui & Dyer, Gardner et al.) rather than presenting fundamentally new approaches. The claims of "proposing two new methods" feel overstated; *multiCluster* is a rather straightforward combination of existing techniques, and *multiCCA* is a direct extension of a previously published method.
2. **Modest Performance Gains & Reliance on Hyperparameters:** The performance improvements achieved by *multiCCA* over baselines are often marginal, and the reported results are sensitive to parameter choices (e.g., the threshold τ in dictionary creation). The sensitivity to hyperparameters is not adequately discussed. More rigorous hyperparameter optimization and reporting are needed to establish the robustness of the findings.
3. **Evaluation Concerns & Data Quality:** The reliance on automatically generated dictionaries (using Google Translate for the 59-language setup) raises concerns about data quality. The authors acknowledge this, but the impact on the results isn’t fully addressed. Furthermore, the intrinsic evaluations are susceptible to vocabulary coverage issues, as the authors themselves note, and the solutions proposed are not entirely convincing. The dependence on supersense tagging, with its own limitations and potential for cross-lingual inconsistency, is also a concern.
4. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the paper compares to several baselines, some potentially stronger alternatives are missing. For example, more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual embedding methods utilizing adversarial learning or transformer architectures are not considered. The lack of comparison to these methods weakens the claims of advancement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the dictionary creation using Google Translate: How was the quality of the automatically translated dictionaries assessed? Were any filtering or quality control measures employed beyond the simple surface form check?
2.  The paper mentions that multiSkip doesn't utilize monolingual data. Given the benefits of monolingual data demonstrated in other work, could you justify this design choice and discuss potential avenues for incorporating monolingual information into the multiSkip approach?
3. The improvement provided by multiQVEC-CCA over multiQVEC is only shown through correlation with downstream tasks. Could you provide examples illustrating the practical differences in the embeddings resulting from using multiQVEC-CCA versus multiQVEC, beyond these correlation metrics?
4. How robust are the performance gains of multiCCA to variations in the size and quality of the bilingual dictionaries? Could you present results showing the performance of multiCCA with varying dictionary sizes or quality levels?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material referenced in the paper is essential for a full assessment and should be readily available during the review process.
*   The web portal is a valuable resource, but its long-term maintenance and accessibility should be considered.
