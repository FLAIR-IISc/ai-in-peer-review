This paper investigates methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a refined evaluation methodology (multiQVEC-CCA). A key contribution is the provision of a publicly available evaluation portal. However, while the intention is laudable, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating substantial advancements over established techniques.

The most notable contribution lies in the creation and release of the evaluation portal and associated benchmarks. This resource will undoubtedly be valuable to the community, facilitating standardized comparison of multilingual embedding methods. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, aiming to address theoretical shortcomings of QVEC, is a positive step, though its practical benefit remains to be fully substantiated. Finally, the systematic experimentation across 59 languages, even with varying data quality, represents a significant undertaking.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. Firstly, the proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, build upon established techniques (CCA, skip-gram) without introducing truly novel algorithmic ideas. While the extension to a massively multilingual setting is non-trivial, the core concepts lack originality. Secondly, the experimental results are often inconclusive or show marginal improvements. The authors demonstrate correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, but these correlations aren't strong enough to justify the complexity of the proposed methods. In several cases, baseline methods perform comparably or even better. The substantial differences in data quality (dictionaries extracted from Google Translate versus parallel corpora) create significant confounding factors, making meaningful comparison difficult. Thirdly, the paper lacks a sufficiently rigorous comparison to strong baselines, particularly more recent and sophisticated approaches to multilingual embedding learning. For example, the absence of comparison to methods leveraging cross-lingual language model pre-training (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) is a significant omission. These models are state-of-the-art and should serve as a crucial point of comparison. The reliance on somewhat dated baselines (e.g., translation-invariance) diminishes the perceived impact of the work. Finally, the paper’s discussion of the limitations of intrinsic evaluation is well-taken, but the proposed multiQVEC-CCA doesn’t demonstrably resolve all the stated issues and its improvement over QVEC isn’t always compelling.

Specifically, the observation that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster is interesting, but the reasons for this difference aren’t thoroughly investigated. It is stated that multiCluster suffers from semantic ambiguity due to clustering, but this claim isn't supported by detailed analysis. The limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the sensitivity of results to the dictionary quality further weaken the conclusions.

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  How sensitive are the results to the threshold (τ) used in creating the dictionaries from parallel corpora? Was a more extensive hyperparameter search performed than simply a manual inspection to set τ = 0.1?
2.  The paper states that the Google Translate-derived dictionaries are of lower quality. How was this assessed, and how does this impact the validity of the comparisons when using these dictionaries?
3.  Why were more recent and powerful multilingual embedding methods (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) not included in the baseline comparison?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of multiCCA, and the reasons why it outperforms multiCluster?
5. What specific steps have been taken to ensure the reproducibility of the experiments, beyond providing the code and data?

Additional Comments:
The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from a degree of self-promotion regarding the significance of the contributions. The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. The statement that QVEC-CCA addresses “fundamental flaws” of QVEC appears overstated.
