This paper presents methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings using dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA) and introduces new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC/CCA) alongside a public evaluation portal. The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of these methods across a large number of languages (59). A secondary contribution lies in the proposed evaluation metrics, aiming to address shortcomings of existing intrinsic evaluation techniques. The provision of a publicly available evaluation portal, while useful, is a tertiary contribution, contingent on the core methods being sound.

**Strengths:**

1. **Scale of Multilingual Coverage:** The work’s most significant aspect is the attempt to create embeddings for 59 languages. While multilingual embedding research is not new, extending it to this many languages is a notable undertaking, providing a valuable resource for the community.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a broad evaluation, including both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The inclusion of the multilingual RCV corpus and dependency parsing provides reasonable baselines for assessing downstream performance. The effort to correlate intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is also commendable.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The provision of the evaluation portal and the trained embeddings (presumably – this isn’t explicitly stated but implied) are a benefit to the research community. This facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Rigor & Detail:** The description of multiCluster is surprisingly vague. The critical parameter τ (threshold for dictionary creation) is chosen based on "manual inspection", lacking a systematic justification. Furthermore, the concatenated corpus approach within multiCluster raises concerns about data imbalances and potential bias towards languages with larger corpora. The explanation of how clusters are formed is also unclear – are clusters language-specific, or truly multilingual? The use of a fixed cluster size is arbitrary and not justified.
2. **Limited Novelty of Methods:** While the multiCCA method is presented as an extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), the extension – projecting all languages into the English space – is a conceptually simple choice, lacking substantial algorithmic innovation. The multiSkip method, being a standard extension of existing work, adds minimal novelty. The reliance on pre-existing methods weakens the claim of significant technical contribution.
3. **Evaluation Metric Concerns:** The improvements reported for multiQVEC-CCA are promising, but the explanation of the core theoretical improvement – addressing the basis invariance of QVEC – is insufficient. The mathematical formulation is presented without adequate motivation or explanation of why this solves the identified problem. Furthermore, the heavy reliance on supersense tagging, with acknowledged inconsistencies across languages, undermines the robustness of the evaluation. The coverage percentages reported in the results section highlight substantial disparities in vocabulary coverage which heavily influences evaluation scores.
4. **Inconsistent Reporting and Reproducibility:** The paper states trained embeddings are available, but lacks a direct link within the paper. Several experimental details are missing (e.g. the specific SGD optimizer used, learning rate, batch size). The reliance on “supplementary material” for detailed results, such as the correlation matrices, is undesirable. The claim that multiCCA achieves “identical results” to skipgram in English tasks is concerning and requires further clarification – why is this the case, and is it a desirable property?
5. **Overstated Claims:** The phrasing "substantially facilitate future research" regarding the evaluation portal feels overstated. While useful, the portal's impact hinges on the validity of the methods and metrics it employs, which are themselves subject to concerns.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of τ in the multiCluster method, including a description of the “manual inspection” process?
2. How are clusters formed in multiCluster? Are they language-specific, or truly multilingual, and how is the cluster ID assignment handled?
3. Why was the English embedding space chosen as the central space in multiCCA, and have you experimented with other language choices?
4. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical improvements in multiQVEC-CCA, specifically how it addresses the basis invariance issue of QVEC? A more intuitive explanation would be appreciated.
5. Please provide a direct link to the trained embeddings and the source code for the evaluation metrics.
6. Can you elaborate on why multiCCA produces identical results to skipgram in English tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from a lack of precision in methodological details and a tendency towards overstated claims. The large scale of the multilingual coverage is a valuable asset, but the underlying methods and evaluation require more rigor and justification to warrant acceptance. The reliance on supplementary material for key results is suboptimal.
