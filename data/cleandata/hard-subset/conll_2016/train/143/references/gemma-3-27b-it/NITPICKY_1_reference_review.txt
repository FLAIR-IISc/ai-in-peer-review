This paper presents methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, focusing on dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA), a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), and a publicly available evaluation portal. The primary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation framework, including both novel metrics and a publicly accessible resource. A secondary contribution is the exploration of dictionary-based methods for multilingual embedding, though the core ideas build heavily on existing work. A tertiary, less significant contribution, is the proposed multiCCA method itself, which is a fairly straightforward extension of existing bilingual CCA techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The most significant strength is the creation of a thorough evaluation suite. The authors recognize the limitations of existing intrinsic metrics and propose multiQVEC-CCA, addressing a theoretical shortcoming of QVEC. The accompanying evaluation portal is a valuable resource for the community, facilitating reproducible research.
2. **Large-Scale Experimentation:** Training embeddings for 59 languages is a substantial undertaking. This scale provides a meaningful testbed for the proposed methods and evaluation metrics. The authors demonstrate a commitment to releasing the data and models for reproducibility.
3. **Identification of Evaluation Metric Issues:** The paper correctly identifies issues with commonly used intrinsic evaluation metrics, specifically their limited correlation with downstream tasks. This critique is valuable and motivates the development of multiQVEC-CCA.
4. **Clear Presentation of Methods:** While not novel in concept, the descriptions of multiCluster and multiCCA are clear and well-structured. The paper appropriately positions these methods within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methods:** The proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, lack substantial novelty. MultiCluster is a compositional approach built on existing clustering and monolingual embedding techniques. MultiCCA is a direct extension of existing bilingual CCA methods. The paper doesn’t present any truly innovative algorithmic ideas.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices lack strong justification. For example, the fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary creation in multiCluster is chosen based on “manual inspection” without a rigorous analysis. Similarly, the choice of 512 embedding dimensions is arbitrary.
3. **Inconsistent Results & Overstated Claims:** The results reveal that the methods do not consistently outperform baselines across all metrics, yet the authors present the methods as superior. The claim that multiCCA “consistently outperforms multiCluster” is weakened by the fact that the performance difference is often small, and dependent on the evaluation metric. The conclusion overstates the impact of the proposed work.
4. **Evaluation Setup Concerns:** The evaluation data for the intrinsic metrics (word similarity, word translation) is not consistently covered by all embeddings, potentially biasing the results. The authors acknowledge this but the mitigation strategy (using the intersection of vocabularies) is not fully explained, and may lead to a non-representative subset. The reliance on Google Translate for dictionary creation for many of the 59 languages introduces a potential source of noise and bias.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any detailed error analysis. It’s unclear *why* certain methods perform better in specific scenarios or which types of words/relationships are challenging for the proposed embeddings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the dictionary creation process using Google Translate, including the specific prompts used and any filtering/post-processing steps applied? What steps were taken to mitigate potential bias introduced by the translation process?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing τ = 0.1 for dictionary creation? Have you experimented with different threshold values and analyzed their impact on performance?
3.  The paper mentions a performance gap between the extrinsic tasks when using multiCCA. Is the difference statistically significant? If so, can you explain why the improvements in multiCCA performance are limited?
4.  The statement that the training data for multiSkip only consists of parallel data seems to contradict the statement that “parallel corpora are not required but can be used when available.” Please clarify this.
5.  While the evaluation portal is a valuable contribution, what measures are in place to ensure the long-term maintainability and accessibility of the portal and the associated data?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., some use “et al.”, others don’t).
*   The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational cost of each method.
*   The authors mention a GitHub repository mirroring the backend of the evaluation portal, but the URL is redacted. It should be provided during the rebuttal period.



Despite the valuable contribution of the evaluation framework, the lack of novelty in the proposed methods and the weaknesses in the experimental setup and analysis lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper presents incremental improvements and a useful resource, but falls short of the originality and rigor expected for publication at CoNLL.
