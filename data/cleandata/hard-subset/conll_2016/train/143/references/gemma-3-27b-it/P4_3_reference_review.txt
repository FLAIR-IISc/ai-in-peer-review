This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, along with new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal for evaluating such embeddings. The core contribution lies in the development and evaluation of a comprehensive framework for multilingual embedding research, combining methods, metrics, and infrastructure. A secondary contribution is the proposed multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metric, designed to address shortcomings in existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Framework:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in multilingual NLP: creating and evaluating word embeddings across many languages. The combination of novel methods for estimation, improved evaluation metrics, and a publicly available evaluation portal constitutes a substantial contribution. This provides a valuable resource for the research community and facilitates further progress in the field.
2. **Novel Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The identification of limitations in the original QVEC metric and the development of multiQVEC-CCA to address those limitations is a strong point. The authors demonstrate that this new metric correlates better with downstream tasks, suggesting its utility as a more reliable evaluation tool.
3. **Extensive Experiments & Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, comparing their proposed methods against baselines across a range of evaluation metrics and tasks. The detailed analysis of these results (including correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of the evaluation portal and the trained embeddings makes this work highly reproducible and accessible. This commitment to open science is commendable and will undoubtedly benefit other researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are presented as novel, they are largely extensions of existing techniques (clustering and CCA respectively). The core ideas lack significant novelty, and the performance gains relative to existing methods are not always substantial.
2. **Dependency on Dictionary Quality:** The performance of multiCluster and multiCCA is heavily dependent on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used. The paper acknowledges this, but the evaluation doesn't sufficiently explore the impact of dictionary noise or sparsity on performance. Specifically, the Google Translate approach to dictionary creation for the 47 languages raises concerns about potential biases and inaccuracies.
3. **Extrinsic Task Limitations:** While the extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) provide some validation, the removal of other features (POS tags, morphology) to isolate the contribution of the embeddings is a significant limitation. This artificial setup may not accurately reflect how embeddings would be used in a real-world application, potentially overstating their relative importance.
4. **Coverage Issues:** The evaluation results are significantly impacted by the coverage of the embeddings. The paper attempts to address this by reporting coverage percentages, but this doesn’t fully mitigate the issue. Discrepancies in coverage across methods could bias the results and make comparisons difficult.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the methods to dictionary quality, particularly for the 47 languages where dictionaries were created using Google Translate? Were any measures taken to assess and mitigate potential biases in these dictionaries?
2. The decision to remove POS and morphology features for the extrinsic tasks seems drastic. Could you provide a more detailed justification for this choice? Were any experiments conducted *with* these features included to provide a baseline comparison?
3. The paper mentions that the evaluation portal allows users to upload arbitrary embeddings. How is the quality control handled to ensure the embeddings uploaded are valid and comparable?
4. Regarding multiQVEC-CCA, what is the computational cost associated with this metric compared to multiQVEC? Is the improved correlation worth the potentially higher computational expense?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is somewhat limited, particularly concerning recent advances in multilingual embedding techniques beyond dictionary-based approaches.
* The formatting of table 3 is a bit difficult to read; consider using a more concise representation.
* The URLs to the software repositories are redacted, which is understandable, but it hinders immediate reproducibility during the review process.
