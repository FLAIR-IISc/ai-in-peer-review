This paper investigates methods for learning massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a refined evaluation methodology (multiQVEC-CCA). The primary contribution is the comprehensive evaluation framework, including both novel metrics and a publicly available evaluation portal. A secondary contribution lies in the application of existing and proposed methods to a relatively large set of languages (59), establishing a benchmark for future work. A third, less significant contribution, is the introduction of multiCluster and multiCCA, which, while not groundbreaking, provide viable baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of multiQVEC-CCA and the accompanying evaluation portal are valuable assets to the multilingual NLP community. The correlation analysis demonstrating the superior predictive power of multiQVEC/CCA compared to prior intrinsic metrics is compelling. This addresses a critical need for more reliable evaluation methods in this research area.
2. **Large-Scale Experimentation:**  Training embeddings for 59 languages is a substantial undertaking and provides a broader empirical basis than many previous studies limited to a handful of languages. This broader scope is important for assessing the generalizability of the proposed methods.
3. **Rigorous Baseline Comparison:** The inclusion of several existing methods (multiSkip, translation-invariance) provides a solid foundation for comparison, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the performance of multiCluster and multiCCA. The authors clearly articulate the distinctions between these approaches, which is crucial for a fair assessment.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of the code, trained embeddings, and the evaluation portal significantly enhance the reproducibility and accessibility of the research, promoting further advancements in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** MultiCluster and multiCCA, while sound, build heavily on existing techniques (clustering and CCA, respectively). The paper does not present a fundamentally new approach to learning multilingual embeddings. The improvements, if any, over existing bilingual extensions of these methods are not convincingly demonstrated given the complexity of the experimental setup.
2. **Dictionary Quality Concerns:** The reliance on automatically translated dictionaries for a significant portion of the languages raises concerns about the quality and accuracy of the training data. The fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary creation appears somewhat arbitrary and its impact on performance is not thoroughly investigated. The performance degradation observed in the 59-language setup, compared to the 12-language setup with parallel data, suggests that dictionary quality is a limiting factor.
3. **Inconsistency in Results and Interpretation:** The results demonstrate that different evaluation metrics favor different methods. While the authors acknowledge this, the lack of a clear unifying interpretation of these findings weakens the overall conclusions. The claim that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster isn't entirely supported when considering the extrinsic evaluations.  The reliance on averaged scores across languages obscures potential language-specific performance variations.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies examining the impact of individual design choices, such as the cluster size in multiCluster or the projection dimensionality in multiCCA. Such analyses would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the threshold τ=0.1 for dictionary creation was fixed based on manual inspection. Could you provide details regarding this inspection, including the number of dictionaries reviewed and the criteria used to determine the optimal threshold?
2.  The results suggest a discrepancy between the performance of the methods according to intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. Can you elaborate on the potential reasons for this discrepancy, and how the choice of downstream tasks might influence the evaluation results?
3.  Given the impact of dictionary quality, have you explored techniques for filtering or weighting dictionary entries based on their confidence or reliability (e.g., using translation probabilities)?
4.  The paper focuses on vector space models, which have inherent limitations in capturing compositional semantics. Have you considered exploring alternative approaches, such as graph-based or knowledge-infused embeddings, to address this limitation?
5.  Could you provide more detailed information about the computational resources required to train and evaluate the embeddings, especially for the 59-language setup?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material is essential for understanding the experimental setup and results. The authors should ensure that all code and data are readily accessible and well-documented. The statement that “Although omitting other features (e.g., parts of speech) hurts the performance of the parser, it emphasizes the contribution of the word embeddings being studied” is somewhat unconvincing; a more robust analysis would involve comparing performance with and without these features for all methods.
