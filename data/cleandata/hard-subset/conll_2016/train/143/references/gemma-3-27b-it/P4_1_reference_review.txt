This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, along with new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal for evaluating embeddings. The primary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation framework, encompassing both new metrics and a publicly available portal, which should facilitate future research in multilingual embeddings. A secondary contribution is the exploration of dictionary-based methods for scaling to a large number of languages (59 in this work). The proposed methods are reasonably well-motivated, building upon existing techniques like CCA and clustering.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, addressing shortcomings of existing evaluation metrics, is a strong point. The demonstrated correlation with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) lends credibility to these metrics and provides a valuable tool for the community. The public web portal further enhances this contribution, streamlining the evaluation process for other researchers. This is a significant practical benefit.
2. **Scaling to a Large Number of Languages:**  The work successfully extends existing methods to handle a substantially larger number of languages (59) than typical multilingual embedding studies. This addresses a key challenge in the field and opens up possibilities for wider application of multilingual NLP techniques. While the proposed methods aren't necessarily groundbreaking in their core ideas, the demonstration of their feasibility and performance at this scale is valuable.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a substantial experimental section with comparisons to several baselines. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations provides a well-rounded assessment of the proposed methods. The analysis of correlations between intrinsic metrics and downstream tasks is particularly insightful, highlighting the importance of selecting appropriate evaluation criteria.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods are incremental improvements over existing techniques. MultiCluster leverages clustering and monolingual embeddings, concepts already well-established. MultiCCA extends bilingual CCA to multiple languages in a relatively straightforward manner. The core algorithmic innovations are limited, and the performance gains over baselines, while present, are not dramatic.
2. **Data Dependence and Dictionary Quality:** The performance of dictionary-based methods is inherently tied to the quality and coverage of the bilingual dictionaries used. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of dictionary quality on results isn’t fully explored. The use of Google Translate for dictionary creation for a substantial number of languages raises concerns about potential noise and biases in the resulting embeddings. The 0.1 threshold for dictionary creation also appears somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from more justification.
3. **Coverage Issues in Evaluation:** The evaluation results are impacted by differences in vocabulary coverage between the different embedding methods. The authors attempt to address this by reporting coverage percentages and, in some cases, evaluating on the intersection of vocabularies, but the potential for bias remains.  A more robust evaluation strategy might involve careful selection of a common vocabulary or the use of techniques for handling out-of-vocabulary words.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding dictionary creation: What steps were taken to assess the quality of the dictionaries generated using Google Translate? Were any filtering or post-processing steps applied to mitigate potential errors or biases?
2.  The paper mentions that multiCCA projects embeddings to the English vector space. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing English as the central language? Were other languages considered, and what were the potential tradeoffs?
3.  The coverage issue in evaluation is noted. Could you perform a more thorough analysis of the impact of vocabulary coverage on the reported results, perhaps by reporting performance on different subsets of the vocabulary with varying levels of coverage?
4. The supplementary material lists the Pearson correlation coefficients of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. It would be helpful to see statistical significance tests associated with these correlations to determine whether the observed relationships are reliable.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The manuscript is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of URLs to code and data is commendable.
*   The reference list is extensive.
*   Minor: The phrasing "although extrinsic evaluations are immune to this problem" seems inaccurate given that out-of-vocabulary words still affect performance on downstream tasks.
