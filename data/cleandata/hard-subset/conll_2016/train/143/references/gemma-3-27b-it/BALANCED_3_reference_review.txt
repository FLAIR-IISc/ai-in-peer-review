This paper presents two methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, alongside a novel evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal. The core idea is to leverage bilingual dictionaries and monolingual data to bridge the gap between languages.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Evaluation Metrics:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA, addressing theoretical shortcomings of existing metrics like multiQVEC, and demonstrating improved correlation with downstream tasks is the strongest contribution. This provides the community with a more reliable tool for evaluating multilingual embeddings.
2. **Public Evaluation Portal:** Creating a user-friendly web portal with benchmarks for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a significant contribution. This facilitates reproducibility and accelerates research in the field by providing a centralized resource.
3. **Multilingual Embedding Methods:** While less novel than the evaluation components, the proposed dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) provide practical approaches to constructing embeddings for a large number of languages, particularly valuable where parallel data is scarce.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Statement:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of massively multilingual embeddings and the limitations of existing approaches. The need for better evaluation methods is convincingly argued.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed methods and metrics using both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks across a significant number of languages. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is particularly insightful.
3. **Practical Impact:** The availability of the evaluation portal and the code will be extremely valuable to researchers working in this area. The authors have provided a tangible resource that will likely be widely adopted.
4. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Work:**  The authors directly address the shortcomings of QVEC and provide a compelling justification for multiQVEC-CCA. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the field.
5. **Detailed Methodology:** The descriptions of the estimation methods, evaluation metrics, and experimental setup are clear and detailed, allowing for easy replication.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods build on existing techniques (CCA, clustering, skip-gram) and don't introduce fundamentally new algorithmic ideas.  The performance gains are primarily observed with multiCCA, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain *why* multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, beyond stating that it projects into the English vector space.
2. **Data Dependency & Dictionary Quality:** The performance of the methods relies heavily on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of dictionary noise isn't thoroughly investigated.  The reliance on Google Translate for dictionary creation for many languages is a potential weakness.
3. **Coverage Issues:** The coverage of the embeddings varies significantly across languages. While the paper acknowledges this and attempts to mitigate it in the evaluation, it still introduces a potential bias in the results. The use of a fixed vocabulary size for clustering might hinder performance in languages with rich morphology.
4. **Extrinsic Task Setup:** The extrinsic task evaluation (document classification and dependency parsing) removes other features, forcing the models to rely *solely* on the word embeddings. This is a strong constraint and might not reflect real-world scenarios where embeddings are often used in conjunction with other features.
5. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, it lacks a detailed analysis of the specific cases where the methods fail or perform poorly. This would provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons why multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster, particularly concerning the projection into the English vector space? What are the potential drawbacks of this approach?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used? Did you perform any analysis to assess the impact of dictionary noise or inaccuracies on the performance of the methods?
3.  Regarding the extrinsic task evaluation, could you provide results with other features included to demonstrate the performance of the embeddings in a more realistic setting?
4.  Can you discuss the computational cost of the different methods, especially multiCCA, as the number of languages increases?
5.  The paper mentions using a fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary creation. How was this threshold chosen and what impact does it have on the performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work in the context of massively multilingual embeddings, especially recent advances.
*   Consider providing ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components of the methods.
*   The code and data availability is excellent.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes valuable contributions to the field of multilingual embeddings. The novel evaluation metrics and the publicly available evaluation portal are particularly noteworthy. While the proposed embedding methods themselves are not groundbreaking, they offer practical approaches and are well-evaluated. The weaknesses are primarily related to data dependency, coverage, and the limitations of the extrinsic task setup. Despite these minor concerns, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above.
