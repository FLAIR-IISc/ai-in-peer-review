## Review for CoNLL Paper: Estimating Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of creating word embeddings for a large number of languages (59 in this case) using dictionary-based methods. The authors propose two new methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and adapt an existing evaluation metric (QVEC) to the multilingual setting, creating multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA. A key contribution is the release of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **The Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of the evaluation portal, along with the thorough suite of intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics (including the improved multiQVEC-CCA), is the most significant contribution. This will undoubtedly be a valuable resource for the research community.
2. **MultiCCA Method:** The multiCCA method appears to be a sensible and effective extension of existing bilingual CCA approaches to a multilingual setting.  Its consistent performance across various evaluation tasks suggests a solid contribution.
3. **Empirical Baseline & Analysis:** The comprehensive experiments, covering a large number of languages and evaluation metrics, provide a useful empirical baseline and insightful analysis of the performance of different multilingual embedding methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Impact via Evaluation Portal:** The evaluation portal is *extremely* valuable. Evaluating multilingual embeddings is notoriously difficult, and having a standardized, publicly available platform streamlines this process significantly. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations is commendable.
2. **Sound Methodology in MultiCCA:** Extending CCA in this way is a logical and well-motivated approach. Leveraging English as a central vector space is a pragmatic solution for handling many languages, especially given the availability of resources for English. The results demonstrate that this approach works well.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a strong commitment to evaluation, employing a diverse set of metrics and conducting a thorough correlation analysis to assess the validity of the intrinsic evaluations.  The effort to address the limitations of QVEC with multiQVEC-CCA is also appreciated.
4. **Scale of Experimentation:** Training embeddings for 59 languages and evaluating across multiple metrics is a substantial undertaking. This large-scale evaluation provides valuable insights into the generalizability of the proposed methods.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methods and experimental setup.  The logical flow makes it easy to follow the authors' reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of MultiCluster:**  While functional, the multiCluster method feels less innovative than multiCCA.  The reliance on clustering translationally equivalent words may introduce semantic ambiguity and appears to underperform consistently. The core idea isn't significantly different from existing clustering-based approaches.
2. **Dictionary Quality Dependence:** The performance of both multiCCA and multiCluster is heavily reliant on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used.  The authors acknowledge this but the impact of noisy or incomplete dictionaries isn't fully explored.  The methodology for creating dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages without existing dictionaries is a potential weakness, as machine translation quality varies.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are comprehensive, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of *why* certain methods perform better or worse in specific scenarios.  Exploring error cases and identifying the types of linguistic phenomena that challenge the models would be valuable.
4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to other methods (multiSkip, translation-invariance) feels somewhat limited. A more direct comparison to recent, more sophisticated multilingual embedding approaches (e.g., those based on transformers) would be beneficial to contextualize the contributions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries? Did you perform any analysis to assess the impact of dictionary noise or incompleteness on the performance of multiCCA and multiCluster?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the limitations of using Google Translate to create dictionaries, and how this might affect the performance of your models, especially for the 47 languages where you didn't have existing dictionaries?
3.  The paper mentions that multiCCA projects into the English vector space.  Have you considered projecting into a different, potentially more “central” language, and how might this affect performance?
4.  Are there plans to continuously update and maintain the evaluation portal with new languages, datasets, and metrics? How will the community be involved in this process?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The code and data availability is excellent.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, but could benefit from some minor edits for clarity and conciseness.
*   The authors should consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the different methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the multiCluster method lacks significant novelty, the combination of a well-motivated and effective method (multiCCA), a comprehensive evaluation framework (including the invaluable evaluation portal), and a thorough empirical study makes this a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual NLP. The evaluation portal alone makes this work particularly impactful.
