This paper presents two methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings, an adaptation of QVEC for multilingual evaluation (multiQVEC, multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluating embeddings. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the development and release of the evaluation portal alongside a comprehensive evaluation suite. The proposed embedding methods, while functional, lack substantial novelty. The multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metrics are a reasonable extension of existing work, but their impact hinges heavily on the quality and availability of supersense tag annotations – a limitation acknowledged by the authors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The authors provide a valuable resource in the form of the web portal and accompanying datasets. This will undoubtedly benefit future research in multilingual embeddings by providing a standardized platform for evaluation and comparison. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations is commendable.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experiments are relatively extensive, covering 59 languages for the embedding methods and 12 for comparison with baseline methods. The reported correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, while not overwhelmingly strong, are a useful addition.
3. **Clear Presentation of Methods:** The core ideas behind multiCluster and multiCCA are explained clearly and concisely, with appropriate references to related work. The mathematical formulations are accessible.
4. **Identification of QVEC Limitations:** The paper correctly points out the limitations of the original QVEC metric – invariance to linear transformations and lack of normalization – and attempts to address these issues with multiQVEC-CCA.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** MultiCluster is essentially an application of existing clustering techniques to the multilingual embedding space. MultiCCA is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) and doesn’t introduce a significantly new algorithmic approach. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate substantial improvement over existing methods, particularly when considering the computational cost and complexity.
2. **Data Dependency of Evaluation:** The performance of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA is highly dependent on the quality and availability of supersense tag annotations, which are not consistently available across all languages. The reliance on English supersense tags for multiple languages is a significant limitation and could introduce bias. The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a critical weakness.
3. **Inconsistent Results & Coverage Issues:** The experimental results are somewhat fragmented. The disparity in coverage between the methods (especially with the 59-language setup) makes direct comparison difficult. The authors attempt to address this by analyzing a subset with consistent coverage, but the conclusions drawn from this subset are not particularly strong.
4. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components of the proposed methods. For example, the impact of the threshold *τ* in the dictionary creation process (multiCluster) is not thoroughly investigated. Similarly, the choice of projection matrices in multiCCA could benefit from further analysis.
5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies and typos are present throughout the paper. Specifically, the second author’s name is formatted slightly differently on lines 5 and 7 of page 2. There are also instances of hyphenation issues ("there-fore" on line 5 of page 1). These minor errors detract from the paper’s overall polish.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of multiCCA and multiCluster compared to existing methods, particularly for large-scale datasets?
2. What steps could be taken to mitigate the dependence of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA on high-quality supersense tag annotations? Could you explore alternative linguistic resources or cross-lingual projection techniques?
3.  The paragraph describing the motivation for using English as the central language in multiCCA states “…since English typically offers the largest corpora and wide availability of bilingual dictionaries.” Can you provide evidence (e.g., statistics on corpus size and dictionary coverage) to support this claim?
4.  The phrase "the mainstream approach of estimating one vector representation per word type" feels somewhat dismissive. Could you elaborate on the potential advantages and disadvantages of token-level versus type-level embeddings in the context of multilingual representation learning?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The URLs to the software libraries are redacted. Please ensure these are properly included in the final version of the paper.
*   The citation formatting is inconsistent. Please standardize the style throughout the paper.
*   The paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods and a clearer articulation of future research directions.



Given the relatively limited novelty of the core methods, the inconsistencies in the experimental results, and the reliance on potentially biased evaluation metrics, I recommend **rejection**. While the evaluation portal is a valuable contribution, it alone does not justify acceptance of this paper, particularly given the shortcomings in the methodological aspects.
