This paper presents two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, along with new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal for evaluating such embeddings. The primary contribution of this work is the development and evaluation of multiQVEC-CCA as a more robust intrinsic evaluation metric for multilingual embeddings, demonstrating improved correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing metrics. A secondary contribution lies in the creation of a publicly available web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which will likely be a valuable resource for the research community. The proposed embedding methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are less novel, building on existing techniques but extending them to a larger number of languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Effective Evaluation Metric:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and its demonstrated superior correlation with downstream tasks is a significant strength. The paper convincingly argues that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics are flawed and that multiQVEC-CCA addresses these shortcomings. The empirical results strongly support this claim, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
2. **Publicly Available Evaluation Portal:** The creation of a user-friendly web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a highly valuable resource. This will greatly facilitate research in this area by providing a standardized and accessible platform for comparing different methods.  The inclusion of downloadable data and the possibility of registering new evaluation datasets are commendable features.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation of the proposed methods and evaluation metrics.  The use of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks, across multiple languages, provides a robust assessment of performance. The detailed reporting of results and statistical correlations strengthens the validity of the conclusions.
4. **Large-Scale Multilingual Embeddings:** Training embeddings for 59 languages is a substantial undertaking and a valuable contribution in itself. This scale allows for a more comprehensive assessment of the methods and their generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA extend existing techniques to more languages, they lack significant methodological novelty. The ideas are based on well-established approaches (clustering and CCA), and the improvements are incremental rather than groundbreaking. The performance of these methods, while reasonable, isn't dramatically superior to existing approaches as demonstrated in the results.
2. **Dependence on Dictionary Quality:** The performance of both multiCluster and multiCCA heavily relies on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries.  The paper acknowledges this, but the process of creating dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages with limited resources raises concerns about potential noise and inaccuracies. This could significantly impact the quality of the resulting embeddings.
3. **Coverage Issues and Vocabulary Discrepancies:** The paper acknowledges coverage issues, particularly for the intrinsic evaluations. The problem of differing vocabularies across embeddings makes comparison difficult, and the attempt to address this by using the intersection of vocabularies might introduce bias. While the authors attempt to address this, it remains a concern, particularly when comparing across methods.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper provides extensive quantitative results, it lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of error cases. Understanding *why* certain methods fail in specific scenarios would provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to select the threshold τ = 0.1 for dictionary creation from FastAlign? Was this threshold optimized, and if so, how?
2.  For the 47 languages where dictionaries were created using Google Translate, did you perform any manual validation or quality control to assess the accuracy of the translations? If so, what was the process?
3.  The paper states that "multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage than the translation-invariance method."  How does this difference in coverage affect the results, and what steps were taken to mitigate this effect during the evaluation?
4.  The intrinsic evaluation results in Table 3 show some variability.  Could you provide a statistical significance analysis to determine whether the observed differences in performance are statistically significant?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are extensive and well-organized.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections could benefit from further clarification (e.g., the explanation of QVEC-CCA).
*   The provided URLs to the software and data are helpful for reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the creation of the evaluation portal are significant achievements. While the proposed embedding methods lack substantial novelty, the thorough evaluation and clear presentation of results make this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the questions raised above and potentially providing a more in-depth discussion of the limitations related to dictionary quality and coverage.
