## CoNLL Review: Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings

**Summary:** This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The authors also introduce improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and provide a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The core aim is to facilitate research in massively multilingual NLP by providing tools and benchmarks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of the multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA metrics and the accompanying evaluation portal represent the most significant contribution. The detailed analysis demonstrating a better correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing intrinsic metrics is compelling.
2. **Extensive Multilingual Embedding Experiment:** Training embeddings for 59 languages and making them available is a valuable resource for the community, providing a broad base for comparison and further research.
3. **Practical Dictionary-Based Approaches:** While not fundamentally novel, the application of multiCluster and multiCCA to a large-scale multilingual setting, coupled with a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrates their potential and provides a solid baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for massively multilingual embeddings and their potential benefits for tasks like machine translation and transfer learning. The introduction effectively sets the stage.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, covering both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The correlation analysis between intrinsic metrics and downstream task performance is particularly insightful and strongly supports the value of their proposed metrics. The use of multiple languages for extrinsic evaluation is commendable.
3. **Community Resource:** The provided web portal and publicly available embeddings are incredibly valuable resources that will likely stimulate further research in this area.  The attention to reproducibility is appreciated (data availability, code mention).
4. **Thorough Experimentation:**  The experimentation is well-described and includes a fair comparison to several baselines. The attempt to account for coverage biases in the evaluation is also a strength.
5. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Metrics:**  The authors correctly identify and address theoretical shortcomings in QVEC, demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:**  While the application to a large number of languages is important, multiCluster and multiCCA build directly on existing work (CCA, clustering approaches) without introducing substantial algorithmic innovation. The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations and potential improvements to these core methods.
2. **Dictionary Quality Impact:** The reliance on automatically translated dictionaries for many languages (47/59) is a potential weakness. The authors acknowledge this but don't fully explore the impact of dictionary quality on the resulting embeddings.  An analysis correlating dictionary precision/recall with embedding performance would be beneficial.
3. **Scaling Issues & Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t adequately discuss the computational challenges associated with scaling these methods to a large number of languages. Details on training time and resource requirements would be helpful for potential users.
4. **Coverage vs. Performance Trade-off:** The issue of vocabulary coverage is acknowledged, but the solutions (e.g., evaluating only on the intersection of vocabularies) feel somewhat ad-hoc.  A more systematic exploration of the trade-off between coverage and performance would be valuable.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:**  The results are presented primarily as aggregate performance numbers. An error analysis, even at a qualitative level, would provide more insight into the strengths and weaknesses of each method and metric.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality (precision/recall) of the automatically generated dictionaries and their correlation with the performance of your embeddings in different languages?
2. **Computational Scalability:**  What were the computational costs (training time, memory usage) associated with training the embeddings for 59 languages? What strategies could be employed to improve scalability for even larger numbers of languages?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the performance of multiCluster and multiCCA to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., cluster size, threshold τ, embedding dimension)? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization?
4. **Low-Resource Languages:** How do the embeddings perform for languages with very limited parallel or dictionary resources? Are there any strategies to improve performance in these scenarios?
5. **Qualitative Examples:** Can you provide some qualitative examples of how the learned embeddings capture translational equivalence or semantic similarity for specific word pairs in different languages?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and organized.
* The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
* The provided URLs for the evaluation portal and data download are excellent.
* The use of a Github mirror for the backend is a great idea for transparency and reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual NLP. While the proposed estimation methods are not groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation framework, the large-scale multilingual experiment, and the publicly available resources represent a significant advancement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
