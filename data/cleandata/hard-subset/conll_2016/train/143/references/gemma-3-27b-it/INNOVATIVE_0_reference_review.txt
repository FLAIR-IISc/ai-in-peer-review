This paper tackles the important problem of massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a refined evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA). The primary contribution, in my view, is the thorough evaluation setup—the multiQVEC-CCA metric and the accompanying web portal—which will be a valuable resource for the community. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility of dictionary-based methods for scaling to 59 languages, albeit with varying success. Finally, while not entirely novel, the multiCCA approach represents a sensible extension of prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **High-Impact Evaluation Resource:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the accompanying web portal are excellent. The paper convincingly demonstrates that existing intrinsic evaluation methods correlate poorly with downstream tasks, and multiQVEC-CCA offers a significantly improved alternative.  Providing a standardized and accessible evaluation platform is a major service to the field and will undoubtedly accelerate research. The fact that the authors are releasing data and code strengthens this point.
2. **Scalability Demonstration:** Successfully training embeddings for 59 languages, even with the limitations outlined below, is a noteworthy achievement. This demonstrates the potential of dictionary-based methods to scale beyond bilingual or small multilingual settings, offering a viable alternative to resource-intensive parallel corpus-based approaches. The focus on leveraging widely available bilingual dictionaries is a practical strength.
3. **Clear Presentation and Thorough Experimentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations. The authors thoughtfully address potential biases in evaluation (e.g., vocabulary coverage) and attempt to mitigate them.
4. **Addressing a Gap in Existing Work:** The paper directly confronts the limitations of prior multilingual embedding methods, particularly concerning evaluation.  The critique of QVEC is well-justified, and the proposed multiQVEC-CCA tackles the identified shortcomings effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While multiCCA is a reasonable extension of existing work, it lacks a genuinely groundbreaking idea.  It's a sensible application of CCA, but doesn't present a fundamentally new approach to learning multilingual representations. MultiCluster, while conceptually interesting, suffers significantly in performance, suggesting the chosen clustering approach isn’t optimal.
2. **Performance Gaps and Data Quality Concerns:** The performance of multiCluster is notably lower than multiCCA and the baseline methods, particularly on extrinsic tasks. The reliance on automatically translated dictionaries for the 47 languages raises concerns about the quality of the training data, potentially impacting the overall results. While acknowledged, the impact of this lower-quality data isn’t fully investigated.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the individual contributions of different components of multiCCA. For example, how does performance change when using different monolingual embeddings as the basis?  Exploring these sensitivities would provide deeper insights into the method's behavior.
4. **Extensive Use of Supersense Tags:** The exclusive reliance on supersense tags for QVEC/QVEC-CCA limits the applicability of the evaluation metrics to languages with good supersense tag resources. This creates a potential bias and reduces the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality of the automatically translated dictionaries used for the 47 languages? Specifically, what steps were taken to ensure reasonable translation accuracy, and what is the estimated level of noise in these dictionaries?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of multiCCA to the choice of the "anchor" language (English in this case)? Have you experimented with using other languages as the anchor?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training the embeddings using each of the four methods? A comparison of training times and resource requirements would be valuable.
4.  Regarding the underperformance of MultiCluster, were there attempts to optimize the cluster size parameter beyond the two values reported, or to explore alternative clustering algorithms?
