This paper proposes two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, alongside an adaptation of QVEC (multiQVEC) and a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) for these embeddings. A web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is also presented.

The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings, encompassing both novel intrinsic metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a publicly accessible web portal. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their proposed evaluation metrics better correlate with downstream tasks compared to existing methods. This is a significant step towards establishing reliable benchmarks for the field. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of dictionary-based methods for multilingual embedding creation, specifically multiCCA, which consistently outperforms the other proposed methods across a range of evaluation metrics. The authors' extensive experimental evaluation across 59 languages is also valuable, providing insights into the performance of these methods in a truly multilingual setting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Well-Justified Evaluation Metrics:** The introduction of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA addresses theoretical shortcomings of existing intrinsic evaluation methods like QVEC and offers improved correlation with downstream tasks. The clear explanation of these improvements and the experimental results supporting their superiority are compelling. This significantly advances the ability to assess the quality of multilingual embeddings.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation across a large number of languages (59) and various evaluation metrics (intrinsic and extrinsic). This breadth of evaluation is a strong point, providing robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The inclusion of both monolingual and multilingual tasks adds to the credibility of the findings.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of both the trained embeddings and the evaluation portal is a valuable contribution to the research community. It facilitates reproducibility and enables other researchers to build upon this work easily. This commitment to open science is commendable.
4. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed methods, experimental setup, and evaluation procedures. This allows for easy understanding and replication of the results. The attention to detail in addressing potential biases (e.g., vocabulary coverage) further strengthens the credibility of the study.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While multiCCA demonstrates good performance, the core idea of extending CCA to multiple languages is relatively straightforward and lacks substantial novelty. The method builds directly upon existing work (Faruqui & Dyer, 2014) with a simple generalization. The theoretical contribution of the embedding methods themselves is not as strong as the evaluation framework.
2. **Dictionary Creation Process:** The method for creating dictionaries for languages beyond the 12 with parallel data relies on Google Translate, which introduces potential noise and bias. The authors acknowledge this, but the impact of this on the results is not fully quantified. This could significantly affect the performance of the methods for those languages.
3. **Performance Gap Compared to State-of-the-Art:** While the proposed methods perform well, the reported performance on downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) doesn't necessarily reach state-of-the-art levels. It's not entirely clear if this is due to the embeddings themselves, or other factors in the downstream task setups. A more direct comparison to current best-performing systems would be helpful.
4. **Dependence on English as Pivot:** The multiCCA method relies on English as a pivot language, which could introduce biases and limitations. The assumption that English offers the largest corpora and widest availability of dictionaries may not hold true for all language combinations, and this dependency could hinder performance in certain scenarios.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of using Google Translate for dictionary creation on the performance of your methods for the 47 languages where parallel data is unavailable? Specifically, could you quantify the error rate or quality of these automatically generated dictionaries?
2. Why was the maximum cluster size set to 1,000 for the 12-language setup and 10,000 for the 59-language setup? What is the rationale behind this difference, and how sensitive are the results to this parameter?
3. Could you discuss potential strategies for mitigating the dependency on English as a pivot language in multiCCA? Are there alternative approaches that could avoid this limitation?
4.  Regarding the performance on downstream tasks, could you provide a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art methods for multilingual document classification and dependency parsing, specifically highlighting the contributions of your embeddings to any observed improvements or limitations?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The supplementary material would benefit from including the raw correlation values mentioned in Table 1.
