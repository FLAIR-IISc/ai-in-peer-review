## Review for CoNLL Paper: Estimating Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings

**Summary:** This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for creating word embeddings across 59 languages, along with a novel evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal. The core idea is to leverage bilingual dictionaries to align embeddings learned from monolingual data, aiming for a scalable approach to multilingual representation learning.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development of the multiQVEC-CCA metric *and* the accompanying public evaluation portal are the strongest contributions.  These resources address a clear need in the field – standardized, robust evaluation of multilingual embeddings – and lower the barrier to entry for researchers.
2. **Scalable Dictionary-Based Methods:** The proposed multiCCA and multiCluster methods provide practical approaches for generating embeddings for a large number of languages, relying on relatively accessible resources (dictionaries) rather than parallel corpora. This is valuable for low-resource languages.
3. **Empirical Analysis & Baselines:** The thorough experimentation comparing the proposed methods to established baselines, along with the correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics, provides useful insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical gap in the field:**  Multilingual embedding research often lacks standardized evaluation. The portal and multiQVEC-CCA directly tackle this, allowing for reproducible and comparable results. The correlation analysis (Table 1) powerfully demonstrates the shortcomings of existing intrinsic metrics and validates the improved correlation of multiQVEC-CCA with downstream tasks.
2. **Practicality and Scalability:**  The reliance on bilingual dictionaries, rather than parallel corpora, is a significant advantage.  Parallel data is expensive and difficult to obtain, particularly for a large number of languages.  The ability to scale to 59 languages is impressive.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper presents a well-designed experimental setup with a range of evaluation metrics and languages, lending credibility to the findings. The reporting of coverage percentages is crucial for interpreting the results.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed methods and experimental setup. The equations are clearly presented and explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** While functional, the proposed multiCCA and multiCluster methods build heavily on existing techniques (Faruqui & Dyer 2014, Mikolov et al. 2013a).  The core innovation isn’t necessarily in *what* is being done, but *how* it’s being applied at scale and evaluated.  This is not a fatal flaw, but it diminishes the impact of the technical contributions.
2. **Performance Discrepancy & Coverage:** The performance gap between multiCCA and multiCluster is noticeable, especially in the 59-language setting.  The paper doesn’t fully delve into *why* multiCluster struggles, beyond mentioning semantic ambiguity. A deeper analysis of this issue would be valuable. The fact that coverage differs significantly, and can influence the results, is also a concern.
3. **Dictionary Quality Dependence:** The performance of the methods is inherently tied to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges the reliance on Google Translate for some languages, which introduces potential noise and bias. A sensitivity analysis on dictionary quality would strengthen the work.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents overall scores, but lacks a detailed analysis of specific error cases.  Examining *which* translation pairs are poorly represented could offer insights for improving the methods.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why multiCluster underperforms compared to multiCCA, especially when scaling to 59 languages? Is the issue solely semantic ambiguity, or are there other factors at play (e.g., cluster size, dictionary quality)?
2. The paper mentions the inherent limitation of evaluating using coverage as it may influence results. Beyond reporting coverage, were any analyses performed to mitigate the impact of coverage differences during evaluation (e.g. using only the intersection of vocabularies, weighting scores)?
3. Given the reliance on Google Translate for some dictionaries, how sensitive are the results to dictionary quality? Have you explored alternative dictionary sources or methods for assessing dictionary reliability?
4. Could you elaborate on the computational resources required for training and evaluating the embeddings, particularly for the 59-language setup? This would help assess the practical feasibility of the methods for researchers with limited resources.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The links to the software and datasets in the paper are currently placeholders and should be updated in the final version.
*   The reference formatting is slightly inconsistent.  Please ensure a consistent style throughout.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embedding research through the development of a comprehensive evaluation framework and practical methods for creating embeddings for a large number of languages. While the core methods themselves may not be groundbreaking, the resources and insights provided are significant and will benefit the community. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.
