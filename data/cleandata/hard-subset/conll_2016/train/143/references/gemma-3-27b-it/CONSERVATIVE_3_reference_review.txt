This paper investigates methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings using dictionary-based approaches (multiCluster and multiCCA) and proposes new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC/CCA). The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of these dictionary-based methods for generating embeddings across a substantial number of languages (59), alongside a publicly available evaluation portal. A secondary contribution lies in the refinement of the QVEC evaluation metric to better suit multilingual embedding assessment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Contribution: The Evaluation Portal.** The creation of a publicly accessible evaluation portal is a significant practical contribution. Evaluating multilingual embeddings is complex and resource-intensive. Providing a standardized platform with readily available data and metrics will genuinely benefit the research community. This lowers the barrier to entry for researchers working in this area and facilitates reproducible results.
2. **Large-Scale Experimentation:** The work attempts to scale dictionary-based methods to a large number of languages (59). While the performance isn’t uniformly strong, the effort to train and evaluate embeddings across such a diverse set is commendable and provides a valuable dataset for future comparisons.
3. **Improved Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** Addressing the limitations of the original QVEC metric and proposing multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses issues of basis invariance and normalization, is a positive step. The reported correlations with downstream tasks suggest it's a more robust evaluation tool.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Novelty is Limited:** The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) largely build upon existing techniques (clustering and CCA, respectively). The core ideas aren’t groundbreaking, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over simpler baselines or existing methods beyond the scale of the experiment. The adaptation of established techniques to a larger language set doesn’t automatically constitute significant innovation.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While baselines are included, the comparison feels incomplete. The paper states that multiSkip only uses parallel corpora, but doesn’t fully articulate why this is a limitation. A more thorough comparison against strong, state-of-the-art methods—especially those leveraging monolingual data as well—is necessary to demonstrate the value of the proposed approaches. The fact that dictionary-based methods outperform parallel-data methods in some cases is not surprising, given the scarcity and noise inherent in parallel corpora.
3. **Evaluation Concerns & Reporting:** The extensive use of Google Translate for dictionary creation in the 59-language setup is concerning. The quality of machine-translated dictionaries can significantly impact embedding quality and introduces systematic bias. The report acknowledges coverage disparities in evaluation, but the proposed solutions (using intersection of vocabularies) feel ad hoc. The discussion of results lacks sufficient statistical significance testing; reporting only correlation coefficients without p-values weakens the conclusions.
4. **Limited Analysis of Method Failures:** While results are presented, there’s a lack of in-depth analysis of *why* certain methods perform poorly in specific scenarios. Understanding the limitations of each approach is crucial for guiding future research. The observation that multiCluster suffers from semantic ambiguity due to clustering isn't fully explored regarding mitigation strategies.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of τ = 0.1 in the dictionary creation process? What impact does this parameter have on the resulting embeddings?
2.  Given the concerns about the quality of machine-translated dictionaries, what steps were taken to mitigate their influence on the results?
3.  Could you provide p-values for the correlation coefficients reported in Table 1?
4.  The paper states that multiSkip "only learns from monolingual contexts observed in parallel corpora." Is this intentional, and if so, what is the rationale behind this design choice? Does incorporating monolingual data into multiSkip improve its performance?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dimensionality (512) for the embeddings?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. However, the overall impact is diminished by the incremental nature of the technical contributions and the limitations in the experimental setup. The evaluation portal is the strongest aspect of this submission.
