This paper tackles the problem of learning massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) alongside a refined evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA) and a publicly available evaluation portal. The primary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation setup – a portal with benchmarks and metrics – which will undoubtedly benefit the community. A secondary contribution is the exploration of dictionary-based methods for scaling multilingual embeddings to a large number of languages, pushing beyond pairwise approaches. The proposed methods themselves, while not groundbreaking, represent a practical application of existing techniques to a challenging scenario.

**Strengths:**

1. **Community Resource:** The development and release of the evaluation portal is a significant strength.  Reproducible evaluation is a major bottleneck in multilingual embedding research, and providing a standardized platform with multiple metrics is a valuable contribution, even if the methods themselves aren't revolutionary.  The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks is particularly commendable.
2. **Scaling to Many Languages:**  Successfully applying and evaluating dictionary-based methods across 59 languages demonstrates a practical approach to building massively multilingual embeddings. The paper acknowledges and addresses the challenges of data availability and dictionary quality, which is important.
3. **Improved Evaluation Metric:** MultiQVEC-CCA appears to be a sensible improvement over QVEC, addressing identified shortcomings and showing better correlation with downstream tasks.  While the improvements may not be dramatic, refining existing evaluation methods is valuable.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a reasonable set of experiments, comparing their methods against baselines and analyzing correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations. The ablation studies (reported in the supplementary material) add further confidence to the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Incrementalism:** The proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, largely build upon existing techniques (CCA, skipgram).  While the adaptation to a multilingual, dictionary-based setting is well-executed, the core ideas lack significant novelty.  The paper doesn’t present a truly *new* algorithmic approach to learning multilingual embeddings.
2. **Dictionary Quality & Data Bias:** The reliance on Google Translate for creating dictionaries for many languages introduces a potential source of bias and noise. While acknowledged, the extent of its impact isn't fully investigated. The differing quality and coverage of dictionaries across languages could significantly skew the results.
3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on aggregate performance. A more detailed analysis of which languages or word types pose the greatest challenges for each method would provide valuable insights. Identifying specific failure cases could guide future research.
4. **Over-reliance on English:**  The choice of English as the central space in multiCCA introduces a potential bias toward English semantics. Exploring alternative anchoring languages or more sophisticated alignment strategies could improve performance and reduce bias.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the impact of Google Translate-derived dictionaries on the overall performance? Did you perform any robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of the results to dictionary quality?
2.  You mention that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* this is the case, specifically in relation to the cluster size parameter in multiCluster?
3.  Given the bias potentially introduced by using English as the central space in multiCCA, have you considered exploring other languages as the anchor, or employing techniques to mitigate this bias?
4.  The results section mentions an extensive supplementary material. Please ensure that all critical details required for reproducibility are included in that material.



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of a web portal is a significant step toward facilitating research in this area. While the core methodological contributions are incremental, the comprehensive evaluation framework and the practical application to a large number of languages make this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance, primarily due to the value of the evaluation resources provided.
