## Peer Review for CoNLL Submission: "Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings"

**Summary:**

This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating word embeddings across a large number of languages (59 in total). The authors also introduce improved evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) for multilingual embeddings and provide a web portal to facilitate research in this area. The core idea revolves around leveraging bilingual dictionaries and monolingual corpora to align embeddings and bridge languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA *and* the accompanying evaluation portal represent the most significant contribution. Prior work lacked robust evaluation, and this provides a valuable resource for the community. The correlation analysis demonstrating these metrics' better alignment with downstream tasks is compelling.
2. **Scalable Dictionary-Based Approach:**  Demonstrating the feasibility of dictionary-based methods for *massively* multilingual embeddings (59 languages) is a valuable practical contribution. While the methods themselves aren't entirely novel, extending them to this scale and providing a working implementation is useful.
3. **Empirical Baseline & Dataset:** The comprehensive experiments and the public release of training data and embeddings establish a strong baseline for future research in massively multilingual word representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of massively multilingual embeddings for machine translation, transfer learning, and other multilingual NLP tasks.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors go beyond standard intrinsic evaluations and include extrinsic tasks (document classification, dependency parsing), strengthening the validity of their findings. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic scores is particularly insightful.
3. **Resourceful Contribution:** The release of the evaluation portal, data, and trained embeddings is a significant contribution to the research community, fostering reproducibility and further investigation.
4. **Well-Defined Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup appears well-controlled, with clear descriptions of the data, methods, and evaluation metrics. The use of coverage as a consideration in evaluation is commendable.
5. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for readers to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:**  MultiCluster and multiCCA build upon existing techniques (CCA, skipgram, clustering). While scaling them to 59 languages is non-trivial, the fundamental innovation in the *methods themselves* is limited. The paper acknowledges this to some extent but perhaps downplays it.
2. **Dictionary Quality & Creation:** The method of creating bilingual dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages raises concerns about data quality.  While acknowledged, the potential impact on downstream performance isn’t thoroughly analyzed. This is a key limitation, especially when comparing to methods utilizing parallel corpora.
3. **MultiCluster Performance:** The consistently poorer performance of multiCluster compared to multiCCA, particularly at scale, suggests a potential issue with the clustering approach. The paper briefly mentions semantic ambiguity as a possible reason, but a deeper analysis of cluster quality or alternative clustering strategies would be beneficial.
4. **Computational Cost/Scalability:**  The paper doesn’t provide much detail regarding the computational resources required to train the embeddings, especially for the 59-language setup.  A discussion of scalability limitations would be useful.
5. **Over-reliance on English as Pivot:** Using English embeddings as the central vector space for projection (multiCCA) could introduce biases or limitations. Exploring alternative pivot languages or truly multilingual approaches (without a single central space) could be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality:**  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality of the automatically created dictionaries (e.g., precision/recall compared to human-annotated dictionaries)? How sensitive are your results to the quality of these dictionaries?
2. **MultiCluster Analysis:**  Can you provide further insights into why multiCluster performs so poorly at scale? Have you investigated alternative clustering strategies or quality metrics for the clusters?
3. **Scalability & Resources:** What were the computational resources (CPU, GPU, memory) required to train the embeddings for 59 languages?  How does training time scale with the number of languages?
4. **Pivot Language Impact:** Have you experimented with using other languages as the pivot language in multiCCA? Do you observe significant performance differences?
5. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide examples of translationally equivalent word pairs that your methods *fail* to align correctly?  What types of errors are most common?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work on zero-shot cross-lingual transfer, as this is a direct application of multilingual embeddings.
*   The supplementary material should include more detailed information about the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings and hardware configurations.
*   It's unclear how much manual effort went into setting the threshold τ for dictionary creation. This deserves clarification.
