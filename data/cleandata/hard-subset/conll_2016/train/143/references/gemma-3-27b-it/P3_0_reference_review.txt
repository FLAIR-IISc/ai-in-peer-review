## Review for CoNLL Paper: Estimating Massively Multilingual Word Embeddings

**Summary:**

This paper investigates methods for creating word embeddings across a large number of languages (59) using only monolingual data and pairwise dictionaries. It proposes two dictionary-based methods – multiCluster and multiCCA – and critically, introduces a refined evaluation framework, multiQVEC-CCA, building upon existing intrinsic metrics. Alongside, the authors provide a valuable resource – a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the associated evaluation portal are the most significant contribution.  Existing evaluation methods for multilingual embeddings are lacking, and a standardized, accessible evaluation suite is *crucial* for driving progress in the field.  The analysis correlating intrinsic metrics with downstream tasks is also strong.
2. **Extensive Experimental Setup:** Training and evaluating embeddings across 59 languages is a substantial undertaking. This provides a valuable benchmark and a significant dataset for future researchers.  The breadth of languages addressed is commendable.
3. **MultiCCA Method:** While not dramatically novel, the extension of CCA to a massively multilingual setting (projecting all languages into an English space) is a sensible and practical approach. It demonstrates good performance, particularly in the larger language setting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Need:** Multilingual embeddings are essential for numerous NLP applications, and the reliance on parallel corpora is a significant bottleneck. This work directly tackles this issue by focusing on dictionary-based approaches.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the limitations of existing evaluation metrics and proactively propose a more robust alternative. The correlation analysis with downstream tasks provides compelling evidence for the superiority of multiQVEC-CCA.
3. **Resource Availability:** The web portal and publicly released code/data are invaluable for the research community. This promotes reproducibility and accelerates future work.  The careful documentation of the training process (e.g., dictionary creation threshold) is also appreciated.
4. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The paper includes a wide range of experiments, comparing multiple methods on various intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The inclusion of coverage metrics and the attempt to address vocabulary mismatches in evaluation are commendable.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and evaluation procedures.  The motivations are clearly stated, and the related work section provides a good overview of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** MultiCluster, while a reasonable approach, feels somewhat straightforward. The idea of clustering translation equivalents and then training a shared embedding isn’t groundbreaking. The main novelty lies in scaling this to 59 languages.
2. **Dependence on English as Pivot:** The reliance on English as the central vector space in multiCCA introduces potential biases. While acknowledged, the implications of this aren't deeply explored.  The performance relative to other pivot languages isn’t investigated.
3. **Dictionary Quality:** The method for creating dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages without Europarl data is concerning.  Google Translate quality varies significantly across language pairs, and this introduces significant noise into the embedding space.  A sensitivity analysis of the dictionary quality (e.g., different thresholds or alternative translation sources) would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit from more targeted ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the methods. For example, what's the impact of the cluster size in multiCluster?  What happens if you use a different dimensionality for the embeddings?
5. **Downstream Task Selection:** While the inclusion of document classification and dependency parsing is good, the choices feel somewhat standard. Investigating tasks more directly reliant on cross-lingual transfer (e.g., cross-lingual information retrieval) might provide stronger evidence of the embeddings' effectiveness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Pivot Language Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the multiCCA results to the choice of English as the pivot language? Have you experimented with other potential pivot languages, and if so, what were the results?
2. **Dictionary Quality Analysis:** Can you provide more detail on the quality of the dictionaries created using Google Translate?  Have you performed any manual validation of these dictionaries, and if so, what was the error rate?
3. **Computational Cost:**  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the different methods, especially as the number of languages increases?
4. **MultiCluster Cluster Size:** How was the maximum cluster size (1000/10000) determined?  Did you experiment with different cluster sizes, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
5. **Handling of Polysemy:** How do these methods handle polysemy? Does training on multiple languages exacerbate the issue of multiple senses being conflated into a single vector?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of potential biases inherent in the data and methods.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the computational resources used for training.
*   The supplementary material should include more detailed results, including the full correlation matrix and additional ablation studies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. While the core methods aren’t radically novel, the comprehensive evaluation framework and the large-scale experimental setup are highly valuable. The publicly available resources will be a significant asset to the research community.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
