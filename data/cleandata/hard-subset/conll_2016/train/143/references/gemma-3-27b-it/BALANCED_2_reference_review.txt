This paper presents two methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings using dictionary information, along with a novel evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a web portal for evaluating embeddings. The primary contribution of this work lies in the comprehensive evaluation framework and the associated publicly available resources. While the proposed embedding methods themselves offer incremental improvements, the thorough evaluation and accessibility tools significantly benefit the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The adaptation of QVEC to a multilingual setting (multiQVEC & multiQVEC-CCA) and the demonstration of its better correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing intrinsic metrics is a strong point. The authors convincingly argue for the need for better intrinsic evaluation methods, and their proposed metric addresses weaknesses in previous approaches.
2. **Publicly Available Resources:** The development of a web portal and release of data/code are excellent contributions. This directly facilitates reproducibility and further research in the field.  The portal makes it easier for researchers to compare their methods against baselines and utilize a standardized evaluation setup.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experiments cover a wide range of languages (59 for embedding training, 12 for comparisons to parallel data baselines) and evaluation tasks (word similarity, translation, document classification, dependency parsing). This breadth provides a more robust assessment of the proposed methods.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The introduction clearly outlines the challenges and opportunities in multilingual embeddings, effectively motivating the work.  The focus on leveraging dictionary information is a sensible approach, especially for low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are well-described, their novelty appears incremental. MultiCluster is a relatively straightforward extension of clustering techniques, and multiCCA builds upon existing CCA methods.  The gains over simpler baselines aren’t always substantial.
2. **Dictionary Quality Dependency:** The performance of multiCluster and multiCCA is heavily reliant on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The authors acknowledge this and mention using Google Translate for some languages, which introduces potential noise and biases. The impact of dictionary quality isn’t thoroughly investigated, and results from the 47 languages using translated dictionaries should be treated with caution.
3. **Scaling Issues & Computational Cost:**  Training embeddings for 59 languages is computationally expensive. While the authors provide details on the training process, the resource requirements may limit the accessibility of their approach for researchers with limited computational resources.  A discussion of computational efficiency and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
4. **Potential Bias in Extrinsic Task Evaluation:** The extrinsic tasks involve removing other features. While this highlights the contribution of the embeddings themselves, it might not accurately reflect how these embeddings would be used in a real-world NLP pipeline where they would typically be combined with other features. This is a common practice in embedding evaluation, but it’s worth acknowledging the potential bias.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results of multiCluster and multiCCA to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries? Could you provide an ablation study or error analysis to assess the impact of dictionary errors on performance?
2. What were the main challenges encountered when scaling the training process to 59 languages? Can you provide more details on the computational resources required and potential strategies for optimizing performance?
3.  You mention that QVEC and QVEC-CCA require supersense annotations. Given the lack of consistent supersense annotations across languages, do you foresee a future where these metrics can leverage alternative linguistic resources (e.g., Universal Dependencies) to improve cross-lingual applicability?
4.  The documentation of the web portal is not accessible from the paper. Can you provide a working link?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   The supplementary material is crucial for understanding the full scope of the experiments and results.
*   Consider expanding the related work section to include more recent advancements in multilingual embedding techniques (e.g., using transformers).



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. While the proposed embedding methods are not groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation framework, publicly available resources, and thorough experimental analysis make this work a significant addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the reviewer's questions and concerns.
