This paper explores methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings using dictionary-based approaches, introduces new evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA), and provides a web portal for evaluating such embeddings. The most substantial contribution is the creation and release of the evaluation portal and associated benchmark datasets. The proposed estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) themselves represent incremental work building on existing techniques. The introduction of multiQVEC-CCA is a worthwhile refinement of existing evaluation methodologies, albeit one whose impact appears modest based on the reported results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Resource: The Evaluation Portal.** The availability of a centralized resource for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a significant benefit to the research community. The provision of benchmark datasets alongside the portal will facilitate reproducible research and allow for fair comparison of future methods. This lowers the barrier to entry for researchers in the field.
2. **Improved Evaluation Metric:** The proposed multiQVEC-CCA addresses a theoretical shortcoming of QVEC and demonstrates better correlation with downstream tasks (specifically document classification). While the improvement isn’t dramatic, any refinement of evaluation metrics is positive, particularly given the importance of reliable evaluation in this area.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a relatively thorough empirical evaluation, covering a wide range of languages and tasks. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations is commendable, and the attempt to address vocabulary coverage issues in the intrinsic evaluations is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Methodology:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods lack substantial novelty. MultiCluster is a fairly straightforward extension of clustering techniques, and multiCCA builds directly on existing CCA-based approaches. The paper doesn’t present compelling evidence that these methods significantly outperform established baselines, particularly when considering the computational cost. The performance gains, when observed, are often marginal and task-dependent.
2. **Limited Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices are made without sufficient justification. For example, the threshold τ = 0.1 in dictionary creation seems arbitrarily chosen and the potential impact of this parameter is not investigated. Similarly, the selection of a fixed embedding dimension (512) without a sensitivity analysis feels somewhat arbitrary.
3. **Overstated Claims Regarding Evaluation Metric Improvement:** While multiQVEC-CCA shows a higher correlation with document classification, the gains over multiQVEC are small. The paper overstates the significance of this improvement, implying a substantial advancement in evaluation methodology when the difference is relatively minor.  The results in Table 1 demonstrate that multiQVEC-CCA is only marginally better than multiQVEC on the document classification task; it performs *worse* on dependency parsing.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to existing baselines is limited. Newer and more sophisticated multilingual embedding techniques (e.g., those based on transformers like mBERT or XLM-RoBERTa) are not considered, making it difficult to assess the true competitiveness of the proposed methods. The authors briefly acknowledge the existence of such methods but do not provide a rationale for their exclusion.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the dictionary-based methods "only require monolingual data and pairwise parallel dictionaries." However, the dictionary creation process for the 47 languages without parallel corpora relies on Google Translate. How does this reliance on a machine translation system impact the quality and reliability of the embeddings for those languages? What steps were taken to mitigate potential errors introduced by the machine translation process?
2.  The results indicate that multiCCA generally outperforms multiCluster. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the reasons for this performance difference? Are there specific linguistic characteristics or data conditions under which multiCluster might be preferred?
3.  The selection of the supersense tag annotations for evaluation is limited to English, Italian and Danish. Why were these languages chosen, and how might the results differ if supersense annotations were available for a wider range of languages?
4.   Regarding the statement that "the ranking of the four estimation methods is not consistent across all evaluation metrics," could you elaborate on why this inconsistency is not concerning? Does this suggest that no single evaluation metric provides a complete picture of embedding quality?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The code and data associated with the evaluation portal would greatly benefit from more detailed documentation. The reliance on Google Translate for dictionary creation in many languages warrants further investigation and discussion. The relatively modest improvements offered by the proposed methods and evaluation metric, combined with the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art baselines, limit the overall impact of this work.
