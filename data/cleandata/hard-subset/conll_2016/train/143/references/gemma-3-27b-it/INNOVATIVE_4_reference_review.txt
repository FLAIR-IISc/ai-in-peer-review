This paper tackles the important problem of learning massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a refined evaluation suite (multiQVEC/CCA). The primary contribution, in my view, is the creation and release of a comprehensive evaluation framework, including both new metrics *and* a web portal, for multilingual embeddings. While the proposed embedding methods themselves aren’t revolutionary, the readily available resources for evaluating others are a significant boon to the field. A secondary contribution is the identification and addressing of shortcomings in existing evaluation metrics like QVEC, leading to the more robust multiQVEC-CCA.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Impact of Evaluation Resources:** The publicly available evaluation portal and benchmarks are extremely valuable. Evaluating multilingual embeddings is currently cumbersome, requiring substantial engineering effort. This work directly lowers that barrier and will accelerate research in the area. The clear demonstration of shortcomings in prior evaluation methods further strengthens this contribution.
2. **Novelty of MultiQVEC-CCA:** While QVEC existed, extending it to a multilingual setting *and* addressing its theoretical weaknesses with CCA is a genuinely novel and well-justified improvement. The correlation analysis demonstrating its superior link to downstream tasks provides strong support for its validity.
3. **Scale of Multilingual Experimentation:** Training and evaluating embeddings on 59 languages is ambitious and showcases a considerable engineering effort. This breadth is important for assessing the generalizability of the proposed methods and evaluation techniques.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the proposed methods and experimental setup. The authors are upfront about limitations and provide helpful contextualization within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** MultiCluster and multiCCA, while sound approaches, build heavily on existing techniques (CCA, skipgram). The core ideas aren’t paradigm-shifting and feel more like competent adaptations than fundamental breakthroughs. The performance gains, while present, aren’t dramatic enough to warrant strong excitement about the methods themselves.
2. **Reliance on Google Translate for Dictionary Creation:** The use of Google Translate to generate dictionaries for languages lacking parallel corpora raises concerns about the quality and potential biases introduced into the training data. While acknowledged, the impact of this isn't thoroughly investigated. The performance difference between the 12-language set (using Europarl) and the 59-language set is likely heavily influenced by this.
3. **Incomplete Ablation Studies:** While the paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, it lacks a deeper ablation study to understand the contribution of individual components within multiCCA and multiCluster. For instance, what is the impact of cluster size in multiCluster? How sensitive is multiCCA to the choice of initial English embedding space?
4. **Downstream Task Selection:** The choice of document classification and dependency parsing as downstream tasks is somewhat limited. Including more diverse tasks (e.g., cross-lingual information retrieval, question answering) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the embeddings’ quality.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the Google Translate dictionaries: Can you provide more analysis on the quality of these dictionaries? Did you attempt any filtering or post-processing to mitigate potential translation errors or biases?
2.  For multiCluster, how does performance change with different cluster size parameters? Are there diminishing returns to increasing cluster size?
3.  You mention that QVEC and QVEC-CCA rely on supersense tags. How sensitive are these metrics to the specific choice of linguistic resource? Could they be adapted to use other types of linguistic annotations?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train and evaluate the embeddings, particularly for the 59-language setup? This would help assess the accessibility of the proposed methods for researchers with limited resources.

**Additional Comments:**

The URL to the evaluation portal is redacted in the submission. Please ensure this is included in the final version. The supplementary material should also include the Pearson correlation matrix mentioned in the results section. While the paper is well-written, tightening the language in a few places would improve clarity. For example, several sentences are unnecessarily verbose.
