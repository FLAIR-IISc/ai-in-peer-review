This paper presents methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, along with a new evaluation suite. The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of `multiCCA`, a dictionary-based method for extending bilingual embedding techniques to multiple languages. A secondary contribution is the `multiQVEC-CCA` evaluation metric, designed to address shortcomings of existing intrinsic evaluation methods. Finally, the authors provide a web portal to facilitate evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors undertake a relatively comprehensive evaluation, exploring multiple intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The creation of the evaluation portal is a commendable effort and a valuable resource for the community. This facilitates reproducibility and comparison of future work.
2. **Practical Approach:** Focusing on dictionary-based methods is a pragmatic approach given the scarcity of large parallel corpora for many language pairs. The scalability to 59 languages demonstrates the method’s potential.
3. **Identified Limitations of Existing Evaluation:** The paper clearly articulates weaknesses in prior intrinsic evaluation methods like QVEC and provides a theoretically motivated improvement in `multiQVEC-CCA`. The demonstrated correlation with downstream tasks suggests its utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Novelty in Method:** While `multiCCA` is a reasonable extension of existing bilingual CCA techniques, the core idea feels incremental. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a *significant* methodological leap beyond prior work, particularly considering the relatively limited performance gains observed. The description lacks mathematical rigor - the extension from bilingual to multilingual is presented almost as a triviality.
2. **Data Quality Concerns & Dictionary Creation:** The methodology for creating dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages is concerning. The resulting dictionary quality is likely to be highly variable and introduce noise into the embeddings. This is acknowledged but not sufficiently addressed. The fixed threshold of τ = 0.1 seems arbitrarily chosen and lacks justification. The impact of this dictionary quality on results is not thoroughly investigated.
3. **Inconsistent Results & Lack of Clear Winners:** The results section is difficult to interpret. There is no single method consistently outperforming others across all tasks. The analysis of why certain methods excel on certain tasks is superficial. The presentation of results in tables without statistical significance testing further weakens the conclusions. The coverage percentages are presented but not systematically incorporated into the analysis (e.g., comparing performance on the *intersection* of covered vocabulary).
4. **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks crucial ablation studies. For example, the impact of the monolingual corpus size, the choice of embedding dimensionality, and the quality of bilingual dictionaries on the performance of `multiCCA` is not explored. This hinders understanding the factors driving performance.
5. **Formatting and Writing Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies are present. For example, inconsistent use of italics and boldface, and inconsistent referencing (e.g., referencing (Luong et al., 2015b) repeatedly without consistent formatting). The writing, while generally clear, suffers from occasional awkward phrasing and redundancy. (e.g. repeating the goals of evaluation multiple times).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality of the Google Translate-derived dictionaries? What steps were taken to mitigate potential errors?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the threshold τ in the dictionary creation process? Have you experimented with different values, and what impact did they have?
3.  Please provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences observed in the experiments.
4.  Can you elaborate on the theoretical justification for the simplicity of extending CCA to multiple languages? What are the potential limitations of this approach, and how might they be addressed?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with each of the methods, and how do they scale with the number of languages?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   The inclusion of URLs within the text is discouraged.
*   The "Related Work" section could be more focused and less descriptive. It reads like a list of papers rather than a critical analysis of existing approaches.
*   The supplementary material should include a detailed breakdown of the data used for each experiment.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some valuable contributions, particularly the evaluation suite, the paper suffers from a lack of methodological novelty, data quality concerns, and a somewhat inconclusive experimental analysis. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall presentation. While the work is not entirely without merit, I recommend **rejection** due to the cumulative effect of these weaknesses. A major revision addressing the points raised above would be necessary for reconsideration.
