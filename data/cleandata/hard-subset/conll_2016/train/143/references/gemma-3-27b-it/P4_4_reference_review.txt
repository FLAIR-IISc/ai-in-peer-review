This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, a novel adaptation of QVEC for evaluating these embeddings (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. The primary contribution is the development and evaluation of multiQVEC-CCA as an intrinsic evaluation metric, demonstrating its stronger correlation with downstream tasks compared to existing metrics. A secondary contribution lies in the provision of a comprehensive evaluation framework and benchmark dataset via the web portal, facilitating future research. The proposed embedding methods, while functional, offer less novelty.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The development of multiQVEC-CCA and the demonstration of its improved correlation with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) is a significant contribution. The authors convincingly argue that existing intrinsic metrics are inadequate, and their proposed method addresses key shortcomings of QVEC, resulting in a more reliable evaluation tool. The detailed explanation of the theoretical improvements and empirical validation makes this a compelling aspect of the paper.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The creation of a publicly available web portal with a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is a valuable resource for the research community.  Providing a standardized evaluation setup, benchmark datasets, and a platform for sharing embeddings promotes reproducibility and accelerates progress in the field. The detailed description of the portal's functionalities adds to its utility.
3. **Extensive Experiments & Data:** The experiments are well-designed, covering a substantial number of languages (59 for embedding training, 12 for baselines) and a variety of evaluation metrics. The use of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations adds robustness to the findings. The availability of data and code through the portal further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of massively multilingual word embeddings for applications like machine translation and transfer learning. The motivation for developing better evaluation methods is well-explained, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods, while sound, build directly upon existing techniques (clustering and CCA respectively). The extensions to handle multiple languages are relatively straightforward and lack significant algorithmic innovation. The performance gains demonstrated are not substantial enough to establish these methods as state-of-the-art.
2. **Dictionary Quality Dependence:** The performance of multiCluster and multiCCA is heavily reliant on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges the use of Google Translate for dictionary creation in many languages, which introduces potential noise and bias. A more thorough analysis of the impact of dictionary quality on the results is needed.
3. **Coverage Issues & Vocabulary Restrictions:** The evaluation results are affected by varying coverage across different embedding methods. The attempt to address this by restricting the evaluation to the intersection of vocabularies, while reasonable, may not fully resolve the issue and could potentially introduce bias.  The paper doesn’t fully explore the consequences of evaluating on different vocabularies.
4. **Incomplete Comparison with Strong Baselines:** While the paper compares with several baselines, it doesn’t comprehensively compare against other recent and strong multilingual embedding methods beyond those explicitly chosen. A more thorough comparison against methods utilizing parallel corpora more extensively might provide a clearer picture of the proposed methods’ relative strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of dictionary quality (especially for those created using Google Translate) on the performance of multiCluster and multiCCA?
2. The paper states that multiCCA consistently achieves better coverage. How does this increased coverage contribute to its performance gains in the evaluation tasks? Is the performance gain solely attributable to coverage, or are there other factors at play?
3. You mention the limitations of using supersense tags for languages other than English, Italian, and Danish. Have you explored alternative cross-lingual resources or methods for creating more consistent annotations across languages for use with multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA?
4. The table showing correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics only provides single correlation coefficients. Could you provide the p-values associated with these correlations to assess their statistical significance?
