## Peer Review for CoNLL - Multilingual Word Embeddings Paper

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper investigates methods for learning massively multilingual word embeddings using only monolingual data and pairwise bilingual dictionaries. The authors propose two novel dictionary-based approaches, multiCluster and multiCCA, and evaluate them alongside existing baselines.  Crucially, they also contribute a refined evaluation framework, multiQVEC-CCA, addressing shortcomings in existing intrinsic evaluation metrics and providing a publicly available evaluation portal to facilitate future research.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework & Portal:** The development of multiQVEC-CCA, and more importantly, the accompanying publicly available evaluation portal are the strongest contributions.  A standardized, accessible, and well-justified evaluation suite is desperately needed in this area and significantly lowers the barrier to entry for researchers.
2. **MultiQVEC-CCA Metric:** The theoretical justification and empirical validation of multiQVEC-CCA as a more robust intrinsic evaluation metric is a valuable addition. Identifying and addressing weaknesses in existing evaluation methodologies is important for progress.
3. **Empirical Study of Dictionary-Based Methods:** The extensive empirical evaluation of multiCluster and multiCCA across 59 languages provides a useful benchmark and demonstrates the practical challenges of scaling dictionary-based methods to a large number of languages.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Need:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem – building massively multilingual embeddings.  The focus on leveraging readily available monolingual data and dictionaries is pragmatic and addresses a limitation of methods requiring parallel corpora.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a commitment to rigorous evaluation, not only proposing a new metric but also conducting correlation analyses with downstream tasks. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations strengthens the findings. The extensive range of languages (59) is also commendable.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:**  The release of the evaluation portal, data, and code is a significant contribution to the community and will undoubtedly accelerate research in this area.  This promotes reproducibility and allows others to build upon the work.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the proposed methods and experimental setup. The inclusion of related work provides good context.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the methods, specifically highlighting the impact of semantic ambiguity in multiCluster, which demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While multiCCA and multiCluster are well-described and implemented, the core ideas – leveraging CCA and clustering – are not fundamentally new. The primary innovation lies in their application to the massively multilingual setting and the comprehensive evaluation, not the underlying algorithms themselves. This is not a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Dictionary Quality Concerns:** The reliance on automatically generated dictionaries (using Google Translate for the 47 languages without Europarl data) introduces a potential source of noise and bias. The authors acknowledge this but a more thorough analysis of the impact of dictionary quality would be beneficial.  The fixed threshold of τ=0.1 also seems somewhat arbitrary.
3. **Scalability Concerns Not Fully Addressed:** While the paper demonstrates the feasibility of scaling to 59 languages, the computational cost and potential bottlenecks associated with multiCCA and multiCluster are not fully discussed.  A more detailed analysis of scalability with increasing numbers of languages, particularly concerning memory requirements and training time, would be valuable.
4. **Inconsistent Performance Trends:** The authors note that the best method varies across evaluation metrics, but the explanation for this inconsistency is somewhat lacking.  A deeper investigation into *why* certain methods excel on specific tasks could yield valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality:** Could you provide more details on the quality of the dictionaries generated using Google Translate? What metrics were used to assess their accuracy, and how might dictionary noise impact the performance of your methods?
2. **Scalability Analysis:** Could you provide more details about the computational resources (memory, CPU time) required to train the embeddings for 59 languages? How does the training time scale with the number of languages and the size of the monolingual corpora?
3. **τ Threshold Selection:** What experiments were conducted to determine the threshold τ=0.1?  Would a more adaptive threshold, perhaps based on dictionary size or language pair, improve performance?
4. **Impact of Cluster Size:** For multiCluster, how sensitive is the performance to the maximum cluster size?  Did you explore alternative strategies for determining the optimal cluster size?
5. **Extrinsic Task Feature Ablation:** You remove other features for the extrinsic tasks to emphasize the embeddings. Have you tried *combining* the embeddings with those features? Would that significantly improve performance, and if so, what does that tell us about the embeddings’ standalone usefulness?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the potential for bias in the training data and its impact on the learned embeddings.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the data sources (corpora, dictionaries) used for each language.
* It’s great to see the code and data being made available, but providing more detailed documentation for the evaluation portal would further enhance its usability.




Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of multilingual NLP. The strong focus on evaluation and the release of valuable resources make it a particularly impactful work.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its long-term impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the authors’ responses to the questions raised.
