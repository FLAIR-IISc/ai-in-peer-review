This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings, along with a refined evaluation framework (multiQVEC-CCA) and a publicly available evaluation portal. The core contribution lies in the practical implementation and evaluation of multilingual embeddings across a significant number of languages (59), facilitated by the evaluation portal, and the proposed improvement to an existing evaluation metric. The methods themselves build upon established techniques, but their extension to a large-scale multilingual setting and thorough evaluation are valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Large-Scale Evaluation & Public Resource:** The most significant strength is the evaluation of multilingual embeddings across 59 languages and the release of a web portal. This is a substantial contribution to the field, providing a much-needed resource for researchers and a standardized benchmark for comparing methods. The availability of data and code further enhances its value.
2. **Improved Evaluation Metric (multiQVEC-CCA):** The identification of shortcomings in QVEC and the development of multiQVEC-CCA, addressing those flaws, is a valuable contribution. The paper demonstrates that multiQVEC-CCA correlates better with downstream tasks, making it a more reliable evaluation tool.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis:** The paper presents a thorough empirical analysis, comparing different estimation methods using multiple evaluation metrics and downstream tasks. This provides valuable insights into the performance of each method and helps identify their strengths and weaknesses. The inclusion of correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations is particularly useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methods:** The proposed methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, are extensions of existing techniques (clustering and CCA, respectively) and don’t introduce fundamentally new algorithmic concepts. While scaling these methods to 59 languages is a non-trivial undertaking, the core ideas lack substantial novelty.
2. **Dictionary Quality & Google Translate Reliance:** The creation of dictionaries for the 47 languages relying on Google Translate is a potential weakness. The quality of machine-translated dictionaries can vary significantly and introduce noise into the embeddings.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of dictionary quality would be beneficial.
3. **Coverage Issues and Vocabulary Restriction:** The variations in vocabulary coverage between the different methods and their impact on intrinsic evaluation scores are a valid concern, as highlighted by the authors. While the authors attempt to address this by reporting coverage and analyzing a subset with identical vocabularies, the results might still be biased. Further investigation into the impact of coverage on the observed correlations is needed. The evaluation using a restricted vocabulary doesn’t fully mitigate the problem.
4. **Limited Analysis of MultiCluster Performance:** The paper suggests that semantic ambiguity introduced by clustering negatively affects multiCluster’s performance. However, a more in-depth analysis of *why* this happens and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., different clustering algorithms, cluster size adjustment) would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the quality of the dictionaries created using Google Translate? Specifically, what steps were taken to ensure reasonable quality, and what is the estimated level of noise in these dictionaries?
2.  Regarding the coverage issue: What is the distribution of vocabulary coverage across the 59 languages? Is the coverage significantly lower for certain language families or typological groups?
3.  The paper mentions a trade-off between dictionary recall and precision when creating dictionaries from parallel corpora (τ = 0.1). Was this value optimized, and if so, how? Would exploring different values of τ affect the results?
4.  For multiCluster, could you elaborate on the factors contributing to the observed performance degradation due to semantic ambiguity? Were different clustering algorithms or cluster size adjustment strategies considered?



**Additional Comments:**

* The references are formatted correctly but quite extensive.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The use of a web portal is commendable and will undoubtedly be valuable to the community.
* A discussion of the computational resources required to train and evaluate the embeddings would be helpful.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual word embeddings, primarily through its extensive evaluation across a large number of languages and the provision of a publicly available evaluation portal. While the core methods themselves lack significant novelty, the practical implementation and thorough evaluation are commendable. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The potential impact of this work on facilitating future research in multilingual NLP is substantial.
