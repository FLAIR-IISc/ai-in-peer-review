## Review for CoNLL Paper

**Summary:**

This paper investigates methods for estimating massively multilingual word embeddings using dictionary-based approaches. The authors propose two novel methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, and adapt an existing evaluation metric (QVEC) to the multilingual setting (multiQVEC, multiQVEC-CCA). Crucially, they also provide a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, alongside benchmark datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by significance):**

1. **The publicly available evaluation portal and benchmarks:** This is the most significant contribution. The lack of standardized evaluation tools hinders progress in multilingual embedding research. Providing a platform and datasets will be invaluable to the community.
2. **MultiQVEC-CCA:** The improved evaluation metric addresses theoretical shortcomings of QVEC and demonstrates better correlation with downstream tasks, offering a more reliable way to assess multilingual embeddings.
3. **Empirical exploration of dictionary-based methods:** While the methods themselves aren’t groundbreaking, the extensive experiments across 59 languages and comparison with baselines provide a valuable empirical analysis of the feasibility and performance of dictionary-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant practical impact:** The evaluation portal is a substantial contribution that directly addresses a bottleneck in the field.  The availability of standardized benchmarks and tools will accelerate research.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a thorough evaluation strategy, utilizing both intrinsic *and* extrinsic metrics. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic scores strengthens the validity of their findings. The attempt to control for vocabulary coverage in the evaluation is also commendable.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:**  The experiments are conducted across a large number of languages (59), providing broad coverage and demonstrating the scalability of the proposed methods.  The comparison against multiple baselines provides a good point of reference.
4. **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally clearly written and easy to follow, even with the technical details of the methods. The motivation is well-established, and the contributions are clearly articulated.
5. **Addressing limitations of existing evaluation metrics:** Identifying and addressing the flaws of QVEC is a valuable contribution to the field of embedding evaluation. MultiQVEC-CCA provides a more robust assessment of embedding quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Methods:** The proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods build heavily on existing techniques (clustering, CCA) and don’t introduce fundamentally new algorithmic ideas. While the application to a massively multilingual setting is a step forward, the core methods themselves lack substantial novelty.
2. **Performance of Proposed Methods:**  The results presented show that multiCCA performs well, but only occasionally outperforms the baselines significantly.  The reliance on English as the "hub" language in multiCCA might introduce biases. A deeper analysis of why multiCCA succeeds (or fails) in different language settings is needed.
3. **Dictionary Quality Dependency:** The performance of dictionary-based methods is inherently limited by the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges the use of Google Translate for constructing dictionaries for some languages, which could introduce noise and bias. A sensitivity analysis regarding dictionary quality would be valuable.
4. **Scaling Challenges:** While the paper demonstrates scalability to 59 languages, the computational cost of training and evaluating these embeddings is likely substantial. The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the computational resources required, which could be a barrier to adoption for researchers with limited resources.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality:**  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used, particularly those generated using Google Translate? Did you perform any error analysis on the translated dictionaries?
2. **English as Hub:**  Why was English chosen as the hub language in multiCCA? Did you experiment with other languages as hubs and observe any significant differences in performance?
3. **Computational Cost:** Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (CPU, memory, training time) required to train and evaluate the embeddings for 59 languages?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide examples of word pairs where the embeddings fail to capture translational equivalence, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?
5. **Alternative Evaluation Metrics:**  Are there other intrinsic evaluation metrics beyond QVEC/multiQVEC that you considered, and if so, why were they not included in the evaluation suite?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods and potential avenues for future research.
* The inclusion of error bars or statistical significance tests in the results tables would strengthen the conclusions.
* It would be useful to see a comparison of the performance of the proposed methods on different types of languages (e.g., typologically similar vs. dissimilar).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a valuable contribution to the field of multilingual embedding research. While the proposed methods are not groundbreaking, the provision of a publicly available evaluation portal and the development of a more robust evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) are significant achievements. The comprehensive experimental analysis provides useful insights into the performance of dictionary-based methods. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The evaluation portal alone justifies acceptance.
