## Peer Review for CoNLL – Multilingual Word Embeddings Paper

**Summary:**

This paper presents two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual word embeddings, along with novel evaluation metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) and a public evaluation portal. The authors evaluate these methods across 59 languages, demonstrating the correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics and benchmarking several embedding estimation techniques.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of the evaluation portal, alongside the proposed metrics (multiQVEC/CCA) which show improved correlation with downstream tasks, is the most significant contribution.  This provides a valuable resource for the community and addresses a clear need for standardized multilingual embedding evaluation.
2. **Empirical Study of Dictionary-Based Methods:** The large-scale empirical study applying dictionary-based methods to a substantial number of languages (59) is a valuable contribution, providing insight into their performance and limitations.
3. **MultiQVEC-CCA Metric:** While building on QVEC, the proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric represents a theoretically sound improvement addressing limitations of the original.


**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Utility of Evaluation Portal:** The provision of a readily accessible web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a substantial strength.  This lowers the barrier to entry for researchers and facilitates reproducible research in the field. The inclusion of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation tasks is commendable.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across a large number of languages and with several different metrics. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of different evaluation approaches.
3. **Improved Evaluation Metric:** MultiQVEC-CCA offers a theoretically motivated improvement over QVEC, addressing concerns about basis invariance and normalization.  The empirical results support the claim that it correlates better with downstream tasks than existing metrics.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup. The organization of the paper is logical.
5. **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge potential biases in the evaluation (e.g., vocabulary coverage) and attempt to mitigate them, demonstrating a thoughtful approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Embedding Methods:** While the multiCCA and multiCluster methods are presented, their core ideas (CCA, clustering) are not novel in themselves. The primary contribution is the *application* of these techniques to a multilingual setting and at scale, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but the paper should more clearly emphasize the contribution of the *scale* and thorough evaluation.
2. **Dictionary Quality Concerns:**  The creation of dictionaries from Google Translate for the 59-language setup raises concerns about the quality and potential biases of those dictionaries. While acknowledged, the impact of this on the results isn't fully explored.  Specifically, the sensitivity of the methods to dictionary quality warrants more investigation.
3. **Extrinsic Task Choice & Simplification:** The chosen extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) are somewhat limited in scope. Furthermore, the removal of features *other* than word embeddings in the parsing task, while intended to isolate the embedding quality, feels artificially restrictive and may not reflect real-world parsing scenarios. It limits the generalizability of the conclusions regarding the embeddings' usefulness.
4. **Incomplete Ablation Studies:** While comparisons are made to baselines, more detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of specific design choices (e.g., cluster size in multiCluster, threshold τ in dictionary construction) would strengthen the analysis.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dictionary Quality:** Can you provide more details on the quality of the automatically generated dictionaries (e.g., precision/recall estimates)? How sensitive are your results to variations in dictionary quality? Did you perform any error analysis of the automatically generated dictionaries?
2. **Extrinsic Task Rationale:**  Why were these specific extrinsic tasks chosen?  Could you elaborate on the rationale for removing all features *except* the word embeddings in the dependency parsing task?  Do you anticipate different results if other features were included?
3. **Scalability:** While the methods were applied to 59 languages, what are the computational limitations of scaling this further (e.g., to 100+ languages)? What are the main bottlenecks?
4. **MultiCCA’s Reliance on English:** The multiCCA method relies on English as the central vector space. Does this introduce any biases or limitations? Have you experimented with using other languages as the central vector space, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper refers to a GitHub repository for the evaluation portal and code. Please ensure the repository is publicly accessible and well-documented.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential ethical implications of using machine translation for dictionary creation, particularly concerning potential biases.
*   The supplementary material mentioned in the paper (e.g., correlation values) should be readily available and clearly organized.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of multilingual embeddings. The development of the evaluation portal and the rigorous empirical analysis are particularly valuable. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and its impact.
