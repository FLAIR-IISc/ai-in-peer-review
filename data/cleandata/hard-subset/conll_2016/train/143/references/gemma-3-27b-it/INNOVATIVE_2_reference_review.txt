This paper tackles the problem of massively multilingual word embeddings, proposing two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a refined evaluation suite (multiQVEC/CCA). The primary contribution lies in the creation and release of a comprehensive evaluation framework – including metrics and a web portal – for multilingual embeddings. While the embedding methods themselves aren’t groundbreaking, the rigorous evaluation and publicly available resources represent a valuable contribution to the field. A secondary contribution is the adaptation and improvement of the QVEC evaluation metric for multilingual settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Evaluation Focus:** The paper prioritizes evaluation, which is often a weak point in multilingual embedding research. The development of multiQVEC-CCA and demonstration of its correlation with downstream tasks is a significant improvement over existing intrinsic metrics. The authors convincingly argue for the inadequacy of previous methods and present a compelling case for their new metric.
2. **Publicly Available Resources:** The provided web portal and accompanying datasets are *highly* valuable. Enabling researchers to easily benchmark and compare their methods will accelerate progress in the field. This focus on reproducibility and community benefit is commendable. This is a crucial contribution, as the field desperately needs standardized benchmarks.
3. **Broad Language Coverage:** Training embeddings on 59 languages is ambitious and provides a substantial resource for the community. While the data collection process has caveats (see weaknesses), the sheer scale is noteworthy.
4. **Clear Presentation and Thorough Experiments:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The experimental setup is described in detail, and the results are presented clearly, allowing for easy replication and analysis. The ablation studies and correlation analysis provide valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Embedding Methods:**  MultiCluster and multiCCA, while sound, are extensions of existing techniques. They lack a truly novel algorithmic approach. The methods build on established CCA and clustering techniques; the primary innovation is applying them to a larger multilingual setting.
2. **Dictionary Creation Concerns:** The method for creating bilingual dictionaries for the 59 languages (using Google Translate) raises concerns about quality and potential biases. While acknowledged, the potential impact on embedding quality isn't fully investigated. Relying on machine translation for dictionary creation introduces significant noise.
3. **Coverage Issues & Extrinsic Task Reliance:** While the authors attempt to address coverage disparities in evaluation, the reliance on extrinsic tasks to demonstrate the utility of the embeddings masks potential shortcomings in intrinsic evaluations.  Performance on downstream tasks might be driven by factors other than the quality of the embeddings themselves. The reliance on only two extrinsic tasks is also a limitation.
4. **Limited Analysis of Method Failures:** The paper largely presents positive results. A more in-depth analysis of *why* certain methods perform poorly in specific scenarios (e.g., for low-resource languages) would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by using Google Translate to create the bilingual dictionaries, and how these biases might affect the performance of the embeddings in downstream tasks?
2.  The paper highlights the correlation between multiQVEC-CCA and downstream tasks. However, correlation doesn't imply causation. Can you discuss potential confounding factors that might explain this correlation?
3.   What are the computational costs associated with training and evaluating the embeddings using the proposed methods and evaluation framework, particularly for a large number of languages? Is the web portal scalable to even larger datasets?
4.   Given the limitations of the 59-language dataset creation, do you have plans to incorporate higher-quality dictionary resources as they become available?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's focus on evaluation and accessibility is refreshing.  The authors should be commended for providing a valuable resource for the multilingual NLP community. While the embedding methods themselves aren't revolutionary, the paper makes a significant contribution by lowering the barrier to entry for researchers working in this area. I recommend acceptance, prioritizing the value of the evaluation framework and resources over the incremental advances in embedding techniques.
