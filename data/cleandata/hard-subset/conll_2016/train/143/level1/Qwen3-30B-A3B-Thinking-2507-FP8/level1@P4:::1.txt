

Summary: This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating word embeddings across more than fifty languages in a single shared space. The authors propose two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for embedding estimation, a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), and a web portal for evaluation.

Main Contributions (as I see them):
1. The most significant contribution is the development and validation of multiQVEC-CCA as an evaluation metric that correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics.
2. The creation of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings, which provides a standardized framework for researchers.
3. The estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), though these appear to be incremental improvements over existing work.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a substantial contribution with the development of multiQVEC-CCA as an evaluation metric. The authors provide clear experimental evidence that this metric correlates significantly better with downstream tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than previous intrinsic metrics. The correlation coefficients reported (0.896 for multiQVEC-CCA with document classification) are substantially higher than those for previous metrics (e.g., cross-lingual word similarity at 0.386), establishing a new standard for evaluating multilingual embeddings. This addresses a critical gap in the field, as evaluation metrics are essential for comparing different approaches.

2. The web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is a practical and valuable contribution. The portal provides a standardized framework for researchers to evaluate their methods, which will facilitate more reproducible and comparable research. The authors have made the data and code publicly available, enhancing the usability of this resource. This addresses a real need in the field, as researchers currently have to develop their own evaluation frameworks.

3. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple languages (59 languages) and multiple evaluation metrics. The authors compare their methods against several baselines and show consistent performance for multiCCA across different metrics, adding credibility to their claims. The evaluation covers both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, which is important for establishing the utility of the embeddings.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) is limited. The paper acknowledges that multiCCA is "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of previous work, and multiCluster is a variant of clustering approaches. The authors do not clearly articulate how these methods differ from or improve upon existing approaches, making the contribution seem incremental rather than transformative. The methods build on Faruqui and Dyer (2014) and Coulmance et al. (2015), but the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight the novel aspects.

2. The evaluation of the estimation methods is inconsistent. The paper shows that multiCCA outperforms multiCluster, but the comparison with other methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance) is inconsistent across different metrics. The authors note that "the ranking of the four estimation methods is not consistent across all evaluation metrics," which suggests that no single method is clearly superior across all tasks. This undermines the significance of these methods as a contribution.

3. The multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA evaluation metrics have limitations. They rely on supersense annotations, which are only available for a small number of languages (English, Italian, and Danish in the experiments). The authors acknowledge that "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages and may not be publicly available," which limits the applicability of their evaluation method to a small subset of languages.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that multiCCA is a "simple extension" of previous work. Could you clarify exactly how it differs from the approach of Hermann and Blunsom (2014), which the authors cite as having "shown that the bicvm method can be extended to more than two languages"?
2. The authors claim that multiSkip "does not make use of monolingual data" but instead "only learns from monolingual contexts observed in parallel corpora." However, the multiSkip method described appears to use monolingual data from the parallel corpus. Could you clarify how this differs from using monolingual data directly?
3. The correlation between multiQVEC-CCA and dependency parsing (0.273) is relatively low compared to document classification (0.896). What are the authors' thoughts on why this correlation is lower, and what might be done to improve it?
