

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that do not require parallel data, and a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics. They also present a web portal for evaluation.

The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:
1. The development of multiQVEC-CCA, a significantly improved intrinsic evaluation metric that better correlates with downstream tasks
2. The creation of a comprehensive evaluation portal that will facilitate future research in multilingual embeddings
3. The multiCCA method for estimating multilingual embeddings that demonstrates strong performance across multiple metrics

## Strengths

1. **Evaluation Metric Innovation:** The paper's most significant contribution is the multiQVEC-CCA metric. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this metric correlates much better with downstream tasks (document classification: 0.896; dependency parsing: 0.273) than existing metrics like cross-lingual word similarity (0.386 and 0.007) and word translation (0.066 and -0.292). This addresses a critical gap in the field where intrinsic metrics have not been properly validated against extrinsic performance, making the paper highly valuable for future research.

2. **Evaluation Portal:** The web portal they developed is a practical and much-needed resource. It standardizes evaluation across multiple metrics and languages, enabling more replicable and comparable research. This is a significant contribution to the community, as inconsistent evaluation practices have hindered progress in multilingual NLP.

3. **Strong Performance of multiCCA:** The multiCCA method consistently outperforms multiCluster across all evaluation metrics for 59 languages. The paper provides thorough evaluation of this method, demonstrating its robustness and practical utility for building large-scale multilingual embeddings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Proposed Methods:** The multiCCA method is a straightforward extension of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to multiple languages, and the multiCluster method resembles previous work on word clustering for cross-lingual embeddings (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012). The paper fails to adequately distinguish these methods from prior work, particularly in the related work section. The authors should provide a more detailed comparison to demonstrate the incremental contribution of their specific extensions.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Dictionary Quality:** For 47 of the 59 languages, the authors rely on Google Translate to create bilingual dictionaries. This approach introduces potential noise and errors that could significantly impact embedding quality. The paper lacks analysis of dictionary quality (e.g., precision/recall of Google Translate-based translations) or how these errors might propagate to the embeddings. Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess the true quality of the embeddings for these languages.

3. **Polysemy Handling Limitation:** The multiCluster method uses a single vector per cluster of translationally equivalent words, which inherently exacerbates the polysemy problem (same word form with different meanings across languages). While the paper mentions this limitation, it doesn't thoroughly analyze how this affects the embeddings' performance on downstream tasks or compare this to methods that handle polysemy better (e.g., by using context-sensitive embeddings). This is a significant weakness given that polysemy is a well-known challenge in multilingual embeddings.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of multiCCA compare to methods that use parallel data when the same amount of parallel data is available? The paper compares multiCCA to parallel-data-based methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance), but it's unclear how much parallel data was used for each method.

2. The paper uses Google Translate to create dictionaries for 47 languages. Could the authors provide an analysis of the quality of these dictionaries (e.g., precision/recall compared to manually curated dictionaries) and discuss how translation errors might have affected the embeddings?

3. The multiCluster method's cluster-based approach creates a significant polysemy problem. How did the authors analyze this issue in their evaluation, and what evidence do they have that this limitation didn't disproportionately affect the performance of multiCluster compared to multiCCA?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a more reliable intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that better correlates with downstream tasks, and by providing a useful evaluation portal. While the estimation methods themselves are not particularly novel, the evaluation framework they propose represents a significant step forward in the field. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to prior work, analysis of dictionary quality, and discussion of polysemy limitations. Despite these weaknesses, the paper's contributions to evaluation practices are substantial and will likely have a positive impact on future research in multilingual NLP.
