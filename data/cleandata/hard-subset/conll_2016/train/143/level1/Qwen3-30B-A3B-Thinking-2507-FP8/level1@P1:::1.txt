

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across more than 50 languages. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) two dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for estimating multilingual embeddings that require only monolingual data and pairwise dictionaries, (2) a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics, and (3) a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

In my assessment, the primary contribution is the evaluation method multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses a fundamental limitation in previous evaluation approaches. The second most significant contribution is the web portal, which provides a practical infrastructure for the community. The estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are the least novel contribution, as the extensions from bilingual to multilingual settings are relatively straightforward.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for multiQVEC-CCA's superiority**: The paper provides compelling evidence that multiQVEC-CCA correlates significantly better with downstream tasks (document classification: 0.896, dependency parsing: 0.273) than previous intrinsic metrics (word similarity: 0.386/0.007, word translation: 0.066/-0.292). This is a major contribution to the field, as evaluation metrics are crucial for driving research forward. The authors' correlation analysis is thorough and well-designed, using multiple languages and tasks to validate their claims.

2. **Practical infrastructure for the community**: The web portal is a significant contribution that will substantially facilitate future research in multilingual embeddings. The portal enables researchers to easily evaluate their methods using a suite of intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, with development and test modes to encourage sound experimental practices. This addresses a genuine need in the field for standardized evaluation infrastructure.

3. **Demonstration of dictionary-based methods' viability**: The paper convincingly shows that dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) can be competitive with parallel-data-based methods (multiSkip and translation-invariance), especially when considering scalability across many languages. This is particularly valuable given the practical difficulty of obtaining parallel data for all language pairs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the estimation methods**: The authors claim multiCluster and multiCCA as primary contributions, but these are largely incremental extensions of existing work. multiCCA is described as "a simple extension (in hindsight)" of Faruqui and Dyer (2014), and multiCluster is essentially a word clustering approach that has been used before. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate these methods from previous work, such as the clustering-based approaches in Haghighi et al. (2008) or Täckström et al. (2012).

2. **Questionable significance of the 59-language results**: The paper claims to train embeddings for 59 languages but only evaluates them using intrinsic metrics without demonstrating their utility on downstream tasks. This is problematic because the authors themselves show that intrinsic metrics correlate poorly with downstream tasks (except for their new metrics). The 59-language results (Table 2) are therefore less meaningful than the paper suggests, as they don't demonstrate practical utility.

3. **Insufficient comparison with related work on evaluation**: The paper doesn't adequately explain how their web portal differs from existing evaluation tools in the field. For example, Tsvetkov et al. (2015) already proposed a monolingual evaluation method, and the authors don't clarify how their portal improves upon what's already available for monolingual evaluation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related evaluation infrastructure.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim multiQVEC-CCA "addresses a theoretical shortcoming of multiQVEC" but don't fully explain what this shortcoming is. Could they clarify this point more explicitly, especially regarding the invariance to linear transformations and the unnormalized score issue?

2. The paper states that "most differences in coverage between multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively small" but doesn't explain why. Given that multiCluster forces translationally equivalent words to share the same vector, one might expect larger coverage differences. Could the authors elaborate on this?

3. The authors claim that multiCCA "consistently outperforms multiCluster according to all evaluation metrics" for the 59-language setting, but don't provide statistical significance tests. Are these differences statistically significant?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their evaluation metrics, particularly regarding the need for supersense annotations in multiple languages. The authors note that "supersense annotations exist for other languages, but the annotations are inconsistent across languages and may not be publicly available," which is a significant limitation for the practical adoption of their evaluation method.

The comparison between dictionary-based and parallel-data-based methods is somewhat limited, as the authors only evaluate on 12 languages for the parallel-data methods. It would be more convincing if they could demonstrate similar results on a larger number of languages using parallel data.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with a strong contribution to evaluation methodology, though the claims about the novelty of the estimation methods are overstated. The web portal is a valuable resource for the community, and the evaluation metric shows clear promise for improving the field.
