

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) new dictionary-based methods for embedding estimation (multiCluster and multiCCA) that require only monolingual data and dictionaries (not parallel data); (2) a new evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that correlates better with downstream tasks than previous metrics; and (3) a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. In my assessment, the most significant contribution is the evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA), followed by the web portal resource, with the embedding estimation methods being the least novel.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement in evaluation methodology**: The paper makes a compelling case that existing intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings (word similarity, word translation) correlate poorly with downstream performance (document classification and parsing), while their proposed multiQVEC-CCA metric correlates strongly (0.896 with document classification). This addresses a critical gap in the field, as evaluation has been a weak point in multilingual NLP research. The authors provide rigorous empirical evidence through correlation analysis across multiple metrics, demonstrating that multiQVEC-CCA is not just an incremental improvement but a substantial step forward in how we assess multilingual embeddings.

2. **Valuable community resource**: The web portal they developed is a practical, well-executed contribution that will significantly benefit the research community. It provides standardized evaluation across multiple languages, makes evaluation more accessible, and encourages replicable research. This is particularly valuable given the complexity of multilingual evaluation, as it removes a major barrier to entry for researchers working in this area.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness at scale**: The paper successfully applies their methods to 59 languages, which represents a substantial scale-up from previous work. The results show that multiCCA consistently outperforms multiCluster across all metrics in the 59-language setting, and the methods work well when evaluated on real downstream tasks (document classification and parsing). This demonstrates practical applicability beyond the research setting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in estimation methods**: The proposed methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) are largely incremental extensions of existing approaches. multiCCA is explicitly described as "a simple extension" of Faruqui and Dyer's bilingual method, and multiCluster builds on prior work like Coulmance et al. (2015). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how these are novel beyond the scale (59 languages) or the specific implementation details. The authors claim their methods "work well," but don't demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods beyond the scale of languages.

2. **Evaluation method limitations not sufficiently addressed**: The multiQVEC-CCA method requires supersense annotations, which are only available for a limited number of languages (English, Italian, and Danish in their experiments). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't propose a compelling solution for languages without these annotations. This significantly limits the applicability of their evaluation method to the broader multilingual community. The claim that "future multilingual supersense annotation efforts should use the same set of supersense tags" is not a sufficient solution to this problem.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares only to two baselines (multiSkip and translation-invariance), but doesn't compare to more recent or relevant state-of-the-art methods for multilingual embeddings. For example, methods like those by Al-Rfou et al. (2013) or Guo et al. (2016) are mentioned but not properly compared. This weakens the claim that their methods "work well" and makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and effectiveness of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that multiQVEC-CCA relies on supersense annotations for multiple languages. How do you plan to address the limitation that this requires linguistic resources for multiple languages, which are not widely available? Could you use alternative linguistic resources (e.g., universal POS tags) to extend this evaluation to more languages?

2. The paper claims that "most differences in coverage between multiCluster and multiCCA are relatively small," but doesn't provide evidence that this small difference is the primary reason for the performance gap. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the performance difference between these methods, particularly whether the gap persists when vocabulary coverage is normalized?

3. The paper states that multiSkip "does not make use of monolingual data," but this contradicts the description of multiSkip as an extension of Luong et al. (2015b), which does use monolingual data. Could you clarify the implementation of multiSkip in your experiments and explain why it differs from the original method?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field through its evaluation methodology and web portal, which will benefit the community. However, the novelty of the embedding estimation methods is limited, and the evaluation method's dependency on specific linguistic resources is a significant limitation that needs addressing. With stronger comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and more thorough discussion of the evaluation limitations, this work would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
