Summary:
- Proposes dictionary-based multiCluster and multiCCA to produce multilingual embeddings for 59 languages, introduces multiQVEC-CCA intrinsic metric, and releases an evaluation web portal.

Main contributions:
- multiQVEC-CCA: a rotation-invariant, normalized intrinsic metric that correlates much better with downstream tasks than prior metrics.
- Scalable dictionary-based embedding methods (multiCluster, multiCCA) using monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries; multiCCA projects languages into an English pivot space and is robust.
- Public release of datasets, code, trained embeddings, and a web portal for standardized, reproducible evaluation.

Strengths:
- Strong evaluation methodology: multiQVEC-CCA shows high correlation with extrinsic tasks (e.g., Pearson 0.896 for document classification).
- Practical scalability: methods work across 59 languages using widely available resources instead of scarce parallel corpora.
- Rigorous experimental design that separates metric validation from method comparison and examines confounds like vocabulary coverage.
- Major reproducibility contribution via an open evaluation portal and released resources.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: multiCluster and multiCCA are incremental extensions rather than major conceptual advances.
- Anglocentric bias risk: multiCCA’s hub-and-spoke design depends on English as a pivot and on English-centric dictionaries.
- Evaluation coverage limited: multiQVEC-CCA depends on supersense-tagged corpora available for only three languages, limiting applicability across all 59 languages.
- Mixed empirical outcomes: no single estimation method dominates across tasks; vocabulary coverage issues complicate conclusions.

Questions to authors:
- Have you explored non-pivot architectures or measured sensitivity to the choice of pivot language?
- Would stronger baselines that combine monolingual and parallel data (as in prior work) alter the comparative results?
- Why does multiCluster perform so well on dependency parsing despite conflating word senses, and can you hypothesize this mismatch between semantic/structural task performance?
