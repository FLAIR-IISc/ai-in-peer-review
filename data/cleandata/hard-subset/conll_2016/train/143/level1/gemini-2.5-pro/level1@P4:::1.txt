**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the estimation and evaluation of word embeddings in a shared vector space across a large number of languages. The authors introduce two methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for estimating multilingual embeddings using only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, thereby avoiding the need for parallel text. They apply these methods to create embeddings for 59 languages. For evaluation, the paper identifies theoretical weaknesses in the existing QVEC metric and proposes an improved, correlation-based metric named multiQVEC-CCA. Experiments show that this new metric correlates more strongly with performance on downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification, dependency parsing) than previously used intrinsic metrics. To facilitate future research, the authors also present a web portal for the standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings, along with open-source releases of their methods and data.

**Contributions**

1.  **A new intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) and a systematic analysis demonstrating its stronger correlation with extrinsic task performance compared to existing intrinsic metrics.** This is a significant contribution, providing the community with a more reliable and theoretically sound proxy for evaluating the utility of multilingual embeddings without the computational expense of running full downstream tasks.
2.  **Two scalable methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) for creating multilingual embeddings that do not require parallel corpora.** While multiCCA is an extension of prior bilingual work, its application and evaluation in a massively multilingual setting (59 languages) is novel. These methods provide strong dictionary-based baselines that are shown to be competitive with methods requiring parallel data.
3.  **The creation of a public evaluation portal and associated resources.** This is a valuable contribution to the research community that promotes standardized, replicable evaluation and lowers the barrier to entry for work in this area.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant methodological contribution to evaluation.** The paper's primary strength lies in the introduction and validation of multiQVEC-CCA. The authors clearly articulate the theoretical flaws of the prior QVEC metric (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized score) and propose a principled solution using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). The empirical validation in §5.1, which shows substantially higher correlation with downstream tasks than standard metrics like word similarity and translation, is convincing. A reliable intrinsic metric is a major asset for the field, enabling faster and more efficient model development.
2.  **Ambitious scale and thoroughness of experiments.** The paper tackles the "massively multilingual" problem by training embeddings for 59 languages, a scale that surpasses much of the prior work. The experimental design is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods against relevant baselines on a suite of nine different evaluation metrics, including two extrinsic tasks. This broad evaluation provides a clear view of the relative performance of different approaches.
3.  **Clarity and practical utility.** The paper is well-written, and the proposed estimation methods are conceptually simple and clearly explained. The focus on dictionary-based methods is practical, as bilingual dictionaries are more widely available than large, high-quality parallel corpora, particularly for lower-resource languages. The release of code, trained embeddings, and an evaluation portal greatly enhances the paper's impact and utility.
4.  **Sound and nuanced analysis of results.** The authors provide a detailed discussion of their results, correctly noting that no single estimation method excels across all metrics. They offer plausible explanations for performance differences (e.g., the effect of semantic ambiguity in multiCluster, the data limitations of multiSkip) and directly address confounding factors such as vocabulary coverage.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty of the embedding estimation methods.** The two proposed estimation techniques are straightforward extensions of existing concepts. The multiCluster approach is based on the established idea of cross-lingual word clustering, while multiCCA is a direct extension of the bilingual CCA method of Faruqui and Dyer (2014) to a multilingual setting via a pivot language. While their application at this scale is novel, the core technical contribution is incremental.
2.  **Dependence of the proposed evaluation metric on scarce resources.** The multiQVEC-CCA metric relies on manually created linguistic resources (supersense tags), which are only available for a small number of languages (three in this work). This limits the metric's direct applicability for evaluating embeddings in the "massively multilingual" setting that the paper advocates for. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative, this is not empirically demonstrated. The strong correlation results are based on a 3-language experiment, and it remains an open question how this would generalize if resources for more languages were incorporated.
3.  **Potentially disadvantageous comparison to baselines.** The multiSkip baseline, as implemented, uses only parallel data, whereas all other methods leverage large monolingual corpora. This places multiSkip at a significant data disadvantage, as the authors acknowledge. The comparison would be more robust if it included a stronger version of this baseline that also incorporates monolingual data, as has been done in related work.
4.  **Unexplored assumptions in the multiCCA method.** The multiCCA method relies on projecting all other languages into a single pivot language space (English). This choice is not justified or analyzed. The quality of embeddings for a non-English language pair (e.g., French-German) is entirely dependent on the quality of their respective projections to English, which may not be optimal. The paper does not explore the impact of this pivot choice or consider alternatives, such as a multi-way projection or a different pivot language.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method uses English as a pivot space. Could you comment on the potential drawbacks of this approach, particularly for language pairs that are typologically distant from English but related to each other? Have you considered alternatives, such as creating an average pivot space or using a graph-based approach to combine pairwise projections?
2.  Your proposed multiQVEC-CCA evaluation relies on supersense tags, which are available for only 3 of the 59 languages. You suggest that universal POS tags could be used for syntactic evaluation. Could this metric be extended to cover more languages for semantic evaluation by using automatically induced resources (e.g., topics from LDA) or resources mapped through a knowledge base like BabelNet to construct the required "linguistic matrix"?
3.  Your implementation of the multiSkip baseline does not leverage monolingual data, which likely contributes to its relatively poor performance. Could you quantify the size difference between the parallel data used for multiSkip and the monolingual corpora used by the other methods? Could you also elaborate on the decision not to include a stronger version of this baseline that also makes use of monolingual data?
