### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the estimation and evaluation of word embeddings in a shared vector space across a large number of languages. The authors propose two methods for estimating such embeddings, multiCluster and multiCCA, which are notable for requiring only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, thus avoiding the need for scarce parallel text. They demonstrate the scalability of these methods by training embeddings for 59 languages. On the evaluation front, the paper introduces a new intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which improves upon the existing QVEC metric by addressing key theoretical weaknesses (invariance to linear transformations and normalization). Crucially, this new metric is shown to have a much stronger correlation with downstream task performance than other common intrinsic metrics. Finally, the authors support the research community by releasing their code, data, and a public web portal for standardized evaluation of multilingual embeddings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:
1.  **A new, theoretically sound intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) accompanied by a rigorous empirical validation.** The paper provides compelling evidence (Table 1) that this metric is a significantly better proxy for downstream task performance than existing intrinsic metrics, addressing a major challenge in word embedding research.
2.  **The development and release of a public evaluation portal and associated resources.** This is a substantial practical contribution that will lower the barrier for future research, encourage reproducible experiments, and help standardize evaluation in this subfield.
3.  **Two simple and scalable methods for creating massively multilingual word embeddings without parallel data.** While extending existing ideas, multiCCA and multiCluster are shown to be effective and applicable to a far wider range of languages than methods reliant on parallel corpora.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Advance in Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's most impactful contribution is its work on evaluation. The authors clearly identify critical flaws in the QVEC metric (lack of rotational invariance, unnormalized scores) and propose an elegant solution using Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). The correlation analysis in §5.1, showing that multiQVEC-CCA has a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification performance, is striking. This provides the community with a computationally efficient intrinsic metric that can be more confidently used as a proxy for extrinsic utility.
2.  **Scalability and Practicality of Proposed Methods:** The reliance on dictionaries rather than parallel corpora is a major practical advantage, making the proposed methods applicable to a much larger set of the world's languages. The successful creation of embeddings for 59 languages is a strong demonstration of this scalability. The multiCCA approach, in particular, which pivots through a high-resource language like English, is an elegant and effective strategy for massively multilingual learning.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The paper features a thorough empirical investigation. It wisely separates the evaluation of the *metrics* from the evaluation of the *methods*. The comparison in §5.2 includes strong, recent baselines and a diverse set of nine evaluation metrics, spanning intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The authors also show good experimental practice by acknowledging and addressing the confounding issue of vocabulary coverage in their comparisons.
4.  **High Value to the Research Community:** The release of an easy-to-use web portal for evaluation is an outstanding contribution. By providing standardized data splits, evaluation scripts, and a common platform, the authors are actively fostering better and more comparable research in this area. Paired with open-source code and pre-trained models, this work provides a complete and highly valuable toolkit for other researchers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unfair Comparison to Parallel-Data Baselines:** The multiSkip baseline, a key competitor, was implemented to use only parallel data. However, as the authors note in footnote 4, the works it is based on (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015) also leverage monolingual data. The paper itself later uses this implementation choice to explain multiSkip's poor performance. This constitutes an unfair comparison and weakens the claims of competitiveness against state-of-the-art parallel-data methods. A true "apples-to-apples" comparison would require training all models on the same union of monolingual and parallel data.
2.  **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** The core ideas behind the two proposed estimation methods are extensions of previous work. MultiCCA is explicitly framed as a "simple extension" of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014). MultiCluster builds on well-established ideas of using bilingual lexicons to link monolingual spaces via clustering. While the application and scaling to a massively multilingual setting are valuable engineering contributions, the core algorithmic novelty is modest.
3.  **Inconsistent Results and Unclear Takeaway for multiCluster:** In the 12-language experiments (Table 3), multiCluster is a very strong performer, winning three of the nine evaluations, including the dependency parsing task. This seems to contradict the conclusion drawn from the 59-language setting, where its performance is dismissed as being "severely hurt" by semantic ambiguity. This discrepancy is not fully reconciled, making it difficult to understand the conditions under which multiCluster is or is not a good choice.
4.  **Practical Limitations of the multiQVEC-CCA Metric:** While multiQVEC-CCA is shown to be an excellent metric, its applicability is currently constrained to the few languages (English, Italian, Danish) for which consistent, manually-annotated linguistic resources like supersense tags are available. This reliance limits its generality as a universal evaluation tool. While the authors suggest using universal POS tags as an alternative for syntactic evaluation, this idea is not explored empirically.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the multiSkip baseline: could you elaborate on the decision to exclude monolingual data from its training? Given that your proposed methods leverage large monolingual corpora, a fairer comparison would allow all methods to access this resource. How would you expect the results in Table 3 to change if multiSkip were trained with the addition of monolingual data, as in the original works?
2.  The performance of multiCluster is surprisingly strong in the 12-language setting, yet it is framed as the weaker of the two proposed methods. Could the performance degradation in the 59-language setting be mitigated by methods other than simply abandoning the approach, for instance by using a more sophisticated clustering algorithm or stricter constraints on cluster size and purity?
3.  Given that the applicability of your excellent multiQVEC-CCA metric is limited by the availability of linguistic resources, have you considered validating it with more widely available resources, such as universal POS tags, to see if it maintains a high correlation with syntax-focused downstream tasks like dependency parsing?
