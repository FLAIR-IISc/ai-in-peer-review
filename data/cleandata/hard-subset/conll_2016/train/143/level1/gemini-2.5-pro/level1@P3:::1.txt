**Summary**

This paper introduces two dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, for learning word embeddings in a shared space for a large number of languages (up to 59). These methods notably do not require parallel corpora. The paper also makes a significant contribution to the evaluation of such embeddings. It proposes multiQVEC-CCA, an intrinsic evaluation metric that addresses theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and demonstrates a substantially higher correlation with downstream extrinsic tasks (document classification and dependency parsing) than other common intrinsic metrics. The authors conduct a large-scale empirical comparison of their methods against two baselines across nine different evaluation metrics. Finally, they contribute a valuable public resource: an evaluation web portal, along with open-source code and pre-trained embeddings.

**Contributions**

1.  **A new intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that is a significantly better proxy for downstream task performance than existing metrics.** The correlation analysis in Table 1 is the paper's most impactful result, showing that metrics like word translation can be misleading, while the proposed method correlates strongly with extrinsic performance. This provides the community with a more reliable and efficient way to measure progress in multilingual representation learning.
2.  **A comprehensive, large-scale empirical study and a valuable set of public resources.** The paper presents experiments on up to 59 languages, comparing four methods on a diverse suite of nine intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The release of an easy-to-use evaluation web portal, code, and pre-trained models is a major service that will foster reproducible research and lower the barrier to entry in this area.
3.  **Two simple, scalable, dictionary-based methods for creating multilingual embeddings.** The proposed multiCCA and multiCluster methods demonstrate that effective multilingual embeddings can be created without parallel corpora, which are unavailable for most language pairs. MultiCCA, in particular, proves to be a strong and robust method across a wide range of tasks and languages.

**Strengths**

1.  **High-impact work on evaluation methodology.** The central strength of this paper is its rigorous treatment of evaluation. By demonstrating the poor correlation of popular intrinsic metrics with downstream tasks (Table 1) and proposing a superior alternative, the paper addresses a critical methodological weakness in the field. The finding that multiQVEC-CCA achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification accuracy is particularly compelling and will likely influence how future work in this area is evaluated.
2.  **Impressive scale and contribution to the community.** The ambition to train and evaluate embeddings for 59 languages is commendable and provides a unique large-scale perspective. The development and release of the evaluation portal is an outstanding contribution. It standardizes the evaluation process, promotes reproducibility, and will undoubtedly accelerate research by allowing for rapid and fair comparison of new methods.
3.  **Thorough and well-structured experimental design.** The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of different embedding techniques. The experiments are well-designed, covering both intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, and analyzing performance in both a 12-language setting (where parallel data is available) and a massively multilingual 59-language setting. The analysis is clear and provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between different methods.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty of the proposed estimation methods is limited.** Both multiCluster and multiCCA are built on well-established ideas. MultiCCA is a straightforward, multi-language extension of the bilingual CCA method from Faruqui and Dyer (2014), a point the authors themselves acknowledge. MultiCluster is conceptually similar to prior work on cross-lingual word clustering. While the application and scaling of these methods are valuable, the core methodological contribution in terms of novel embedding *algorithms* is incremental. The paper's primary strength lies elsewhere.
2.  **Comparisons to baselines may be unfair or confounded.**
    a. The `multiSkip` baseline is significantly handicapped by being trained only on parallel data, excluding the large monolingual corpora that all other methods leverage. This is not representative of state-of-the-art bilingual models (e.g., Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016), which use both data types. Consequently, its poor performance is unsurprising and its inclusion as a baseline is questionable.
    b. The results in Table 3 are confounded by dramatic differences in vocabulary coverage between methods (e.g., 23.0% for translation-invariance vs. 71.0% for multiCCA on the monolingual similarity task). A high-scoring model with low coverage may simply be succeeding on frequent, "easy" words. An analysis on the intersection of vocabularies is essential for a fair comparison and should be included in the main paper, not just supplementary material.
3.  **The proposed evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, has limited language coverage.** A key contribution, multiQVEC-CCA, relies on manually created supersense tag annotations, which the authors could only find for three European languages (English, Danish, Italian). This limits the metric's utility for the very "massively multilingual" settings the paper advocates for. While the authors suggest alternatives like universal POS tags for syntactic evaluation, this is not demonstrated. This dependency on rare resources is a significant practical limitation for a metric intended for broad multilingual evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method pivots through English. What is the sensitivity of the results to this choice? Have you explored using a different pivot language, or perhaps a method that doesn't rely on a single pivot, such as averaging projections across multiple pairwise CCA models?
2.  Could you comment on your choice to implement a `multiSkip` baseline that uses only parallel data? Given that related and contemporary methods use both parallel and monolingual data for stronger performance, how would you expect the results in Table 3 to change if `multiSkip` were allowed to train on monolingual data as well?
3.  Given that the applicability of multiQVEC-CCA is limited by the availability of supersense resources, could you elaborate on the feasibility of your suggestion to use universal POS tags instead? What would the linguistic property matrix `S` look like in that case, and do you believe it could still serve as a reliable proxy for semantic quality on downstream tasks like document classification?
