**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for creating and evaluating word embeddings in a shared vector space across a large number of languages. The authors introduce two novel dictionary-based methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, which do not require parallel corpora and are used to train embeddings for 59 languages. A major contribution is in the area of evaluation: the paper proposes a new intrinsic metric, multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses theoretical flaws in prior work (QVEC) and is shown to correlate significantly better with downstream task performance than existing intrinsic metrics. To support the community, the authors also introduce an open-source web portal for standardized evaluation, providing data, code, and a platform for comparing different embedding methods.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A principled and empirically validated evaluation framework for multilingual embeddings.** The paper's most significant contribution is its focus on evaluation. The authors propose multiQVEC-CCA, an improved intrinsic metric that is invariant to basis rotation and produces a normalized score. Crucially, they demonstrate through a rigorous correlation analysis that their proposed metrics (multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA) are far better predictors of performance on extrinsic tasks (parsing and document classification) than widely used metrics like word translation and similarity. This work provides the community with a much-needed tool for more reliable intrinsic evaluation.

2.  **Scalable methods for creating massively multilingual embeddings.** The paper introduces two methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) that leverage only monolingual corpora and bilingual dictionaries, bypassing the need for scarce parallel text. This design choice enables them to scale impressively to 59 languages. The multiCCA method, which projects other languages into a pivot English space, proves particularly effective and robust.

3.  **A valuable public resource for reproducible research.** The development and release of a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings is an outstanding contribution to the field. By providing standardized datasets, evaluation scripts, and a platform for comparison, the authors are fostering transparency and reproducibility, which will undoubtedly accelerate future research in this area.

**Strengths**

1.  **High-impact work on evaluation methodology.** The paper makes a compelling case that popular intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings correlate poorly with extrinsic task performance. The introduction and validation of multiQVEC-CCA (Table 1 shows a Pearson correlation of 0.896 with document classification) is a substantial step forward. This provides researchers with a more trustworthy and efficient way to measure progress without having to run expensive downstream evaluations for every experiment.

2.  **Demonstrated scalability and practical value.** By successfully training embeddings for 59 languages, the paper moves beyond the typical bilingual or few-language setting. The methods are practical, relying on widely available resources (monolingual text and dictionaries, which can be created automatically) rather than curated parallel corpora, making them applicable to a much wider range of the world's languages.

3.  **Thorough and rigorous experimental design.** The experiments are well-structured, separating the validation of the evaluation metrics from the comparison of the embedding estimation methods. The authors are careful in their analysis, for instance by acknowledging and investigating the confounding effect of vocabulary coverage on evaluation scores. The comparison between dictionary-based and parallel-text-based methods on a 12-language subset provides valuable insights into the trade-offs of different approaches.

4.  **Exceptional contribution to the research community.** The release of the evaluation portal, code, and trained embeddings is a major strength. This commitment to open and reproducible science lowers the barrier to entry for other researchers and helps establish a common ground for future work, which is invaluable for a growing subfield.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty of the proposed estimation methods is limited.** The core ideas behind multiCluster (grouping translations and training) and multiCCA (using CCA in a pivot-based structure) are arguably incremental extensions of previous work. The paper itself describes the multiCCA extension as "simple (in hindsight)". While the application and scaling of these methods are novel and impressive, the underlying algorithms do not represent a major conceptual leap. The paper's primary novelty lies in the evaluation framework rather than the embedding techniques themselves.

2.  **The multiCCA model relies on a single pivot language.** The choice to use English as a central "hub" into which all other languages are projected may introduce an anglocentric bias. The quality of embeddings for any non-English language pair (e.g., Italian-Danish) is entirely dependent on their respective projections to English. This may be suboptimal compared to direct alignment or a model that learns a more language-neutral central space. The approach is also limited by the availability of high-quality dictionaries to and from English.

3.  **The proposed evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) has limited language coverage.** While multiQVEC-CCA shows excellent properties, its reliance on manually created supersense-tagged corpora restricts its use to the three languages for which this resource was available (English, Danish, Italian). This limits its direct utility for evaluating the full 59-language embedding space the authors created. The suggestion to use universal POS tags as an alternative is not empirically tested, and it is unclear if a syntax-based resource could replicate the strong correlation observed with the semantics-rich supersense tags.

4.  **The comparison of estimation methods yields mixed results.** In the 12-language experiment (Table 3), the proposed methods do not emerge as the clear winners across the board. The translation-invariance baseline performs best on four intrinsic tasks, while the proposed multiCluster and multiCCA methods excel on different tasks. The surprisingly strong performance of multiCluster on parsing, despite its acknowledged weakness of conflating word senses, is counter-intuitive and not fully explained. These mixed results, combined with the vocabulary coverage issues, make it difficult to draw a single, strong conclusion about the superiority of any one estimation method.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The multiCCA method's hub-and-spoke architecture is a pragmatic choice, but could introduce biases. Have you considered or experimented with other architectures, for example, one that learns a joint projection for all languages into a shared latent space without privileging a pivot language? How sensitive is the model to the choice of the pivot?

2.  Your `multiSkip` baseline performs quite poorly, which you attribute to its lack of access to monolingual data. The papers you build on (Coulmance et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2016) describe methods that do leverage monolingual data alongside parallel data. Would a baseline that more faithfully implements these stronger models provide a more competitive comparison and potentially alter the conclusions in Table 3?

3.  The multiCluster method's top performance on dependency parsing is intriguing, especially given the argument that it suffers from semantic ambiguity by collapsing translations into a single vector. Do you have a hypothesis for why this seemingly coarse representation is so beneficial for a structural task like parsing, while it is outperformed by multiCCA on a semantic task like document classification?
