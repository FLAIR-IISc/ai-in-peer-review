- Paper proposes methods and resources for massively multilingual word embeddings without parallel corpora and an intrinsic evaluation metric that better correlates with downstream tasks.
- Contribution: multiQVEC-CCA — fixes QVEC’s basis-dependence and normalization issues, extends to multilingual setting, and correlates better with document classification and parsing than common alternatives.
- Contribution: multiCCA — a scalable CCA-based method aligning monolingual embeddings to an English hub, producing embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries, compared to multiCluster, multiSkip, and translation-invariance baselines.
- Contribution: Resource/infrastructure release — trained embeddings for up to 59 languages, standardized datasets, and a web evaluation portal supporting intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.
- Strength: Strong empirical evaluation linking intrinsic metrics to extrinsic tasks across 17 embedding variants, showing multiQVEC(-CCA) better predicts document classification and parsing performance.
- Strength: multiCCA is conceptually simple, scalable, and effective across many languages, demonstrating practicality for low-resource scenarios without parallel corpora.
- Strength: Thorough multi-faceted experiments covering 9 metrics, multiple data regimes (12-lang parallel, 59-lang dictionary-only), coverage reporting, and intersection analysis to control confounds.
- Strength: Released resources, code, and a web portal with separate dev/test splits promote reproducibility and future work.
- Strength: Clear motivation and methodological exposition, with precise critique of QVEC and justification for CCA (basis invariance, normalization).
- Weakness: Correlation study limited to three languages (en, da, it) and 17 embedding instances; high correlations may not generalize across languages, tasks, or richer pipelines.
- Weakness: multiQVEC(-CCA) depends on supervised supersense lexica, restricting multilingual applicability and risking annotation inconsistencies and bias toward resource-rich languages.
- Weakness: Baseline comparisons may be unfair or under-tuned (multiSkip without monolingual data, translation-invariance with smaller context windows), with full intersection results relegated to supplementary material.
- Weakness: Training/evaluation dictionaries for 47 languages come from Google Translate, introducing unknown noise/bias and potential entanglement of training/evaluation artifacts without sufficient sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: Extrinsic setups (e.g., frozen embeddings, removed POS/morphology in parsing) isolate embedding effects but may not reflect real-world pipelines and could alter method rankings.
- Question: How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary noise and size; can you show performance vs. dictionary coverage or noise-injection curves?
- Question: Does choosing English as the hub matter; have alternative hubs or joint multi-CCA been tried to reduce hub bias or degradation for distant languages?
- Question: Can you report confidence intervals for correlations and evaluate multiQVEC-CCA on additional extrinsic tasks (POS, NER, semantic tasks) and typologically distant languages?
- Question: Can the portal incorporate language-agnostic linguistic property matrices (e.g., universal POS distributions) to extend multiQVEC-CCA beyond supersenses?
- Question: Will the authors re-run multiSkip with monolingual data and equalize context windows across methods to ensure fair baseline comparisons?
- Overall assessment: Impactful and well-presented work combining a useful intrinsic metric (multiQVEC-CCA), a practical multilingual estimator (multiCCA), and valuable resources; limitations in baseline parity, resource dependence, and breadth of correlation study do not outweigh its significance.
