Summary
This paper proposes two dictionary- and monolingual-corpus-based methods for inducing massively multilingual word embeddings without parallel corpora: (1) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent word types across languages and learns a single embedding per cluster from concatenated monolingual corpora; and (2) multiCCA, which learns monolingual embeddings and uses bilingual CCA to project each non-English space linearly into an English pivot space. The paper also introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual extensions of QVEC, to intrinsically evaluate embeddings against supersense annotations; multiQVEC-CCA addresses basis-variance and normalization issues in QVEC via CCA. The authors evaluate on multiple intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification, dependency parsing), analyze correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics, and release a web portal for standardized evaluation.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An evaluation contribution: multiQVEC-CCA, a principled, basis-invariant, normalized intrinsic metric that correlates more strongly with downstream tasks than standard word similarity/translation metrics in the reported setting.
2) A practical engineering contribution: scalable, dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation (multiCCA and multiCluster) covering 59 languages, with an evaluation portal and public resources to enable replicable comparisons.
3) A methodological baseline: a simple yet surprisingly strong multilingual CCA approach by pivoting through English; and an analysis that exposes weaknesses in commonly used intrinsic evaluations.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated evaluation advance. The argument against basis-sensitive, unnormalized QVEC is sound, and multiQVEC-CCA is a principled fix. The correlation analysis, albeit limited in scope, indicates substantially higher correlation to document classification (0.896) than widely used intrinsic metrics. This is a useful community contribution, independent of the particular embedding methods.
- Strong practical value and breadth. Training and reporting for 59 languages with consistent pipelines, and releasing a portal and benchmarks, address a real need in multilingual embedding research. The effort to standardize dev/test splits and to measure coverage alongside scores shows care for reproducibility and fairer comparisons.
- Sensible, simple algorithms with good performance/coverage trade-offs. MultiCCA is simple to implement (linear projections into a pivot) yet performs robustly across tasks and offers better coverage than the translation-invariance baseline; multiCluster is competitive in the 12-language regime and illustrates the polysemy/merging trade-offs that arise with hard cross-lingual clustering.
- Honest assessment of baselines and confounds. The paper is transparent about multiSkip lacking monolingual training, window-size constraints for translation-invariance, and coverage biases in intrinsic evaluation; it tries to mitigate with coverage reporting and shared-vocabulary analyses.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and potential circularity in the correlation study. The reported correlations are computed over 17 embeddings and only three languages (en/it/da). This small sample and language set raise concerns about statistical power and generality. Moreover, multiQVEC(-CCA) relies on supersense lexica in the same languages used for extrinsic tasks, potentially inflating correlations. A broader language/sample study (or confidence intervals/significance tests) is needed to substantiate the claim of superiority of multiQVEC-CCA.
- Dependence on English pivot and linguistic resources. MultiCCA maps all languages into the English embedding space; performance may degrade for distant languages or where bilingual dictionaries are noisy. multiQVEC(-CCA) requires supersense resources that are scarce and inconsistent across languages; this undermines its practicality for “massively multilingual” evaluation and biases toward languages with rich resources. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer a concrete path to resource-lean variants (e.g., POS/universal properties) or quantify sensitivity to resource quality.
- Questionable evaluation data quality for the 59-language setting. Dictionaries derived from Google Translate are used both for training (47 languages) and for evaluating word translation in the 59-language set. Even if not identical extractions, this introduces a risk of overlap and biases toward the training signal, weakening the independence of evaluation. No audit of overlap or sensitivity analysis to dictionary noise/thresholds (τ=0.1) is provided.
- multiCluster’s modeling assumptions and error propagation. Using connected components of dictionary graphs to enforce shared type-level embeddings across potentially polysemous words and across many languages causes semantic conflation, especially as the graph densifies with 59 languages. The paper observes performance degradation but provides no analysis or mitigation (e.g., soft clustering, sense induction, degree caps, or using alignment probabilities to weight rather than hard-merge).
- Baseline comparability and hyperparameter asymmetries. The translation-invariance method uses a smaller window (3) for computational reasons, likely handicapping it against methods trained with window 5. multiSkip is trained without monolingual data, making it weaker than published TransGram/BiBOWA variants. The paper partially discusses this but the headline comparisons remain hard to interpret without matched-capacity baselines or ablations.
- Insufficient analysis of design choices. Key choices lack ablations: pivot language selection (why not de, es, or a joint multilingual space without a single pivot?), dictionary threshold τ, cluster size caps, embedding dimensionality, and the amount/quality of monolingual data per language. Given the focus on “massively multilingual,” understanding robustness to resource noise and language distance is important.

Questions to Authors
- Correlation analysis: Please report confidence intervals and significance for the correlations in Table 1. How do results change when adding more languages/embeddings or when using different extrinsic tasks (e.g., NER, POS, MT features)?
- Resource overlap: For the 59-language evaluation, what fraction of word pairs in the Google Translate-based training dictionaries overlap with those in the evaluation word-translation lists? Can you re-run with held-out pairs or a different evaluation source to ensure independence?
- multiQVEC(-CCA) construction: How exactly is the multilingual linguistic matrix built across en/da/it? Are properties shared across languages or language-specific? How are lexical items aligned across languages (surface identity, dictionary, lemmas)? Do you frequency-weight words or control for coverage biases?
- Pivot and projections: Why choose English exclusively as pivot? Did you try an orthogonal Procrustes mapping and/or enforcing orthogonality for better length/structure preservation? What happens if you average projections from multiple pivots or learn a joint multilingual CCA with shared latent space?
- Dictionary noise and τ: How sensitive are multiCluster/multiCCA to dictionary precision/recall (τ) and to alignment noise? Can you provide performance as a function of τ and of dictionary size per language?
- Soft vs hard clustering: Did you try weighting contexts by translation probabilities instead of hard connected components? Alternatively, can you limit cross-language edges or perform community detection to reduce conflation?

Overall evaluation
This paper provides valuable evaluation methodology and resources, and presents simple, effective multilingual embedding baselines. The main novelty lies in the evaluation (multiQVEC-CCA) and the scale/engineering rather than in the modeling. Addressing the concerns about correlation generality, evaluation independence, and baseline parity would significantly strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance for its evaluation contribution and practical impact, contingent on clarifying the above points.
