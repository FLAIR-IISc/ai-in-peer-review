Summary
The paper proposes dictionary-based methods for learning massively multilingual word embeddings without parallel corpora (multiCluster and multiCCA), introduces an intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) designed to better correlate with downstream tasks, and releases an evaluation portal and resources. Experiments across up to 59 languages, multiple intrinsic metrics, and two extrinsic tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) suggest that multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates more reliably with downstream performance than commonly used intrinsic metrics.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
- A principled intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) that addresses QVEC’s basis-variance and normalization issues and shows markedly higher correlation with downstream tasks (e.g., 0.896 with document classification), plus its multilingual adaptation.
- A comprehensive, standardized evaluation framework and portal, with datasets, coverage reporting, and cross-metric analysis to facilitate reproducible multilingual embedding research.
- Scalable, dictionary-based estimation of multilingual embeddings (multiCCA, multiCluster) that avoids parallel corpora and achieves competitive extrinsic performance across many languages.

Strengths
- Clear evidence that intrinsic metrics matter: multiQVEC(-CCA) correlates substantially better with downstream tasks than word similarity/translation (e.g., multiQVEC-CCA: 0.896 vs 0.386 for word similarity with document classification; word translation even negative with parsing at -0.292). This is a valuable community signal.
- Breadth and rigor: evaluations span 59 languages (intrinsic), 12-language and 7-language extrinsic settings, four estimation methods, and nine metrics, with attention to coverage biases (reporting coverage and intersection analyses).
- Practicality and scalability: multiCCA (simple CCA projection into an English space) consistently outperforms multiCluster at 59 languages and yields strong coverage; both methods avoid dependence on parallel data, lowering resource barriers.
- Methodological clarity: the critique of QVEC’s basis dependence and unnormalized scores is well-argued, and CCA-based fix is theoretically sound (basis invariance, bounded correlation).
- Reproducibility: datasets, trained models, and an evaluation portal lower friction for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of estimation methods: multiCCA is a straightforward extension of prior bilingual CCA with English as a hub; multiCluster aggregates translational equivalents, increasing polysemy. Gains seem more due to scope and engineering than algorithmic innovation.
- Dictionary quality and evaluation leakage risks: for 47 languages, dictionaries come from Google Translate; evaluation for 59 languages also uses GT-derived pairs, risking bias and noisy supervision.
- Extrinsic evaluations are narrow and somewhat artificial: document classification and delexicalized, frozen-embedding parsing may not reflect typical usage; no MT, NER, or sequence labeling; language coverage for extrinsic tasks is limited.
- “Multilingual” intrinsic metrics rely on supersense resources available for only a few languages (en, da, it), with noted inconsistencies, constraining generality.
- Some experimental choices seem ad hoc (e.g., τ=0.1, cluster size caps) and significance reporting is missing; correlation analysis is based on 17 models only.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Are the correlation improvements statistically significant? Please report confidence intervals and Spearman/Kendall variants.
- How sensitive is multiCCA to the choice of pivot language? Do non-English pivots change results?
- Can you provide a multiSkip baseline that also leverages monolingual data to ensure fairness?
- What safeguards mitigate potential leakage from Google Translate into both training dictionaries and evaluation sets?
- Could the portal include standardized multilingual supersense/POS resources to broaden multiQVEC(-CCA) applicability?
