- Paper proposes two dictionary-based multilingual embedding methods: multiCluster (clusters translational equivalents) and multiCCA (projects monolingual embeddings into English via CCA).
- Introduces multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA as multilingual intrinsic evaluation metrics, with multiQVEC-CCA addressing QVEC’s basis and dimensionality issues via CCA.
- Releases a public evaluation portal, datasets, and code, and trains embeddings for up to 59 languages.
- Main contribution: multiQVEC-CCA is a principled intrinsic metric with substantially higher correlation to downstream tasks than common metrics.
- Main contribution: Scalable dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation (multiCCA, multiCluster) that works without parallel data and covers many languages.
- Main contribution: Public evaluation resources to improve replicability and comparability.
- Strength: multiQVEC-CCA shows high correlation with document classification (0.896) and multiQVEC with parsing (0.444), outperforming word similarity and word translation metrics.
- Strength: multiCCA is simple, scalable, and achieves strong coverage and competitive performance across 59 languages.
- Strength: Thorough evaluation across intrinsic and extrinsic tasks, plus vocabulary coverage and analysis of confounds.
- Strength: Clear analysis of baselines highlighting when translation-invariance or multiSkip perform better or worse and diagnosing method-specific failure modes.
- Strength: Community resource (portal and code) likely to lower barriers and standardize evaluation.
- Weakness: multiCCA’s methodological novelty is limited — straightforward extension of bilingual CCA to English-centric multilingual projections.
- Weakness: Use of Google Translate for many dictionaries (and for evaluation) risks training–test contamination, bias, reproducibility, and licensing concerns.
- Weakness: Intrinsic supervision for multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA is limited to English, Italian, and Danish, constraining true multilingual validation.
- Weakness: The multiSkip baseline may be unfairly implemented without monolingual data, underestimating parallel-based approaches.
- Weakness: Some reported close scores lack statistical testing and robustness checks (variance, significance, confidence intervals).
- Question: Are Google Translate dictionaries used for training disjoint from those used for evaluation, and how sensitive are results to overlap?
- Question: How robust are correlation findings to other downstream tasks/languages (e.g., more UD languages, NER, POS)?
- Question: How do multiCluster’s cluster size thresholds and dictionary noise affect performance; could soft/sense-aware clustering help?
- Question: Did authors try hubs other than English or multi-hub projections, and does that help low-resource languages?
- Question: Can authors provide ablations on dictionary size/quality (Europarl vs. GT) and alignment threshold τ to show performance–noise trade-offs?
- Additional comment: Report variance and significance measures and consider Spearman correlations for robustness.
- Additional comment: Include monolingual-only baselines to quantify the benefit of multilingual sharing.
- Additional comment: Clarify preprocessing (tokenization, lowercasing, homographs) and rationale for excluding identical GT translations that may remove valid cognates.
