Summary and contributions
This paper proposes methods and resources for massively multilingual word embeddings without requiring parallel corpora, and introduces intrinsic evaluation metrics that better correlate with downstream tasks.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) An improved intrinsic evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, that addresses two theoretical flaws of QVEC (basis dependence and unnormalized scores), extends it to a multilingual setting, and is empirically shown to correlate better with downstream tasks (document classification and parsing) than widely used alternatives (word similarity and word translation).
2) A practical, scalable estimation method (multiCCA) that aligns independent monolingual embeddings into a shared space via CCA to an English hub, enabling embeddings for 59 languages using only monolingual data and bilingual dictionaries; along with a comprehensive empirical comparison against multiCluster, multiSkip, and translation-invariance methods.
3) Resource and infrastructure contributions: trained embeddings for up to 59 languages, standardized datasets, and a web evaluation portal supporting both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics.

Strengths
- Strong empirical framing of evaluation: The paper explicitly studies correlations between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics across 17 embedding variants, showing multiQVEC and especially multiQVEC-CCA correlate substantially better with document classification and parsing than word similarity or word translation. This addresses a long-standing gap—intrinsic metrics with weak predictive value—and provides actionable guidance to the community.
- Simple, effective, and scalable multilingual estimation: multiCCA is conceptually straightforward (linear projections into an English space) yet performs well across many settings, achieving good coverage and competitive extrinsic performance without parallel corpora. The 59-language training demonstrates scalability and practicality for low-parallel or low-resource scenarios.
- Thorough, multi-faceted evaluation: The study spans 9 metrics (two extrinsic) and multiple data regimes (12-language parallel and 59-language dictionary-only), with clear reporting of coverage and an intersection analysis to partially control for coverage confounds. The analysis of multiCluster’s degradation with many languages (due to semantic conflation in clusters) is insightful.
- Resource contribution and reproducibility: The web portal, released code/embeddings, and standardized dev/test splits will facilitate replication and future work. The deliberate separation of development and test in the portal is good practice and uncommon in this subarea.
- Methodological clarity and motivation: The limitations of QVEC are precisely articulated, and the move to CCA is well justified (basis invariance, normalized score). The paper is well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Limited generality of the correlation study: Correlation analyses use only three languages (en, da, it) and 17 embedding instances. The extraordinarily high correlation (0.896) of multiQVEC-CCA with document classification may not generalize to other language families, tasks (e.g., NER, POS), or settings with richer features. Parsing correlations are more modest (0.273), suggesting variability across tasks. Broader languages/tasks and statistical uncertainty (e.g., confidence intervals) would strengthen claims.
- Dependence on supervised linguistic resources for multiQVEC(-CCA): Relying on supersense tag lexica restricts multilingual applicability, currently to a small set of languages, and may introduce annotation inconsistencies noted by the authors. While the metric is valuable, its multilingual scope is limited by resource availability, potentially biasing conclusions toward languages with such resources.
- Baseline fairness and tuning: The multiSkip baseline is implemented without monolingual data (unlike Coulmance/Guo variants), making it comparatively weak. The translation-invariance method uses a smaller context window (due to tooling constraints) and shows lower coverage, complicating fair comparisons. While an intersection analysis is mentioned, full results are relegated to supplementary material; more rigorous hyperparameter tuning and harmonization across methods would increase confidence in relative rankings.
- Evaluation data quality and potential bias: For 47 of the 59 languages, bilingual dictionaries are derived from Google Translate, both for training and (partly) evaluation. This introduces unknown noise and potential biases (e.g., unequal quality across languages) and may entangle training/evaluation artifacts. There is limited error analysis or sensitivity analysis to dictionary noise/coverage.
- Extrinsic setup may not reflect real-world usage: For parsing, all POS/morphological features are removed and embeddings are frozen, which cleanly isolates embedding contributions but underestimates practical performance and may change method rankings relative to realistic pipelines. Similar concerns apply to the document classification setup (limited domains/languages).

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are multiCCA and multiCluster to dictionary noise and size? Can you provide performance curves vs. dictionary coverage or noise injection experiments to estimate robustness?
- Does multiCCA’s choice of English as the hub matter? Have you tried alternative hubs or joint multi-CCA to reduce hub bias? Any evidence for hub-induced anisotropy or degradation for distant languages?
- For multiQVEC-CCA, could you report confidence intervals for correlations and include additional extrinsic tasks (e.g., POS, NER, semantic tasks) to assess generality? How does it fare with typologically distant languages?
- Can the evaluation portal incorporate language-agnostic linguistic property matrices (e.g., universal POS tag distributions) to extend multiQVEC-CCA beyond supersenses?
- For baseline fairness: could you re-run multiSkip with monolingual data (as in Coulmance/Guo) and equalize context windows across methods to verify rankings?

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem with a solid combination of methodological contribution (multiQVEC-CCA), practical multilingual estimation (multiCCA), and valuable resources. Despite some limitations in baseline parity, resource dependence for intrinsic metrics, and the breadth of the correlation study, the work is impactful, clearly presented, and likely to influence both evaluation practices and multilingual embedding construction. It meets CoNLL criteria well in terms of novelty (especially in evaluation), significance, clarity, and reproducibility.
