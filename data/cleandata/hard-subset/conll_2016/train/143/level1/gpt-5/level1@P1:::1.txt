Summary and Contributions
The paper presents methods to learn massively multilingual word embeddings and to evaluate them intrinsically with better correlation to downstream tasks. Two estimation methods use only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries: (1) multiCluster, which clusters translational equivalents and learns a single vector per cluster from concatenated monolingual data; (2) multiCCA, which learns monolingual embeddings per language and projects all non-English spaces into the English space via CCA. The authors also propose multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA, multilingual extensions of QVEC, with the latter addressing basis-invariance and dimensionality-normalization shortcomings of QVEC via CCA. They release a web evaluation portal and train embeddings for up to 59 languages, comparing against multiSkip (parallel only) and translation-invariance (matrix factorization).

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) A principled intrinsic evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) with substantially higher correlation to downstream tasks than commonly used metrics (word similarity, word translation), demonstrated via a correlation study over 17 embedding variants.
2) Scalable, dictionary-based multilingual embedding estimation (multiCCA, multiCluster) that does not require parallel data, with state-of-the-art or competitive performance on several intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations, and coverage across 59 languages.
3) A public evaluation portal with standardized datasets and code, facilitating replicable research.

Strengths
- Strong, well-motivated intrinsic metric with empirical validation: The correlation study (Table 1) shows multiQVEC-CCA correlates with multilingual document classification at 0.896 and multiQVEC correlates best with multilingual parsing (0.444), both clearly outperforming cross-lingual word similarity (0.386/0.007) and word translation (0.066/−0.292). This is a meaningful advance because it helps the community choose intrinsic metrics that better predict downstream utility.
- Practical, scalable estimation without parallel corpora: multiCCA is simple yet effective and attains strong results with broad language coverage (59 languages). On 12-language experiments (Table 3), dictionary-based methods are best on 5/9 evaluations, including both extrinsic tasks (multiCluster best or tied best for parsing and document classification), while multiCCA consistently achieves higher coverage.
- Thorough, multi-faceted evaluation: The paper evaluates across intrinsic (mono- and cross-lingual similarity, word translation, QVEC/QVEC-CCA) and extrinsic (multilingual document classification, parsing) tasks, reports vocabulary coverage, and discusses confounds due to different coverage. The correlation analysis is particularly useful and relatively rare in the literature.
- Clear positioning against baselines with insights: Translation-invariance is sometimes best on specific intrinsic metrics, multiSkip lags likely due to not using monolingual data (acknowledged), and multiCluster degrades with many languages because cluster-level ambiguity increases—offering diagnostic insights into method behavior.
- Community resource: The evaluation portal, datasets, and open-source releases (even if redacted in the review) will materially lower barriers to entry and improve comparability in this area.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in multiCCA: The extension from bilingual CCA to a multilingual setting by projecting all languages into English is straightforward. While effective, its methodological novelty is modest. The core technical advance of the paper is thus more in evaluation than in estimation.
- Dictionary quality and potential contamination: For 47/59 languages, dictionaries are created with Google Translate, and for 59-language evaluation the word translation metric is also derived via Google Translate. This raises concerns about training–test contamination and evaluation bias (even if word sets differ), as well as reproducibility and licensing. The paper should clarify disjointness and quantify sensitivity to dictionary noise.
- Limited linguistic diversity in intrinsic supervision: multiQVEC/multiQVEC-CCA rely on supersense annotations in only English, Italian, and Danish, which constrains true “multilingual” validation of the metric and may bias the correlation analysis toward these languages. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer a broader semantic annotation alternative or a syntactic analogue beyond suggesting universal POS as future work.
- Fairness of the multiSkip baseline: The implementation used here does not leverage monolingual corpora, whereas closely related prior work (e.g., BilBOWA, Trans-gram) does. This likely underestimates the performance of parallel-based approaches and obscures the trade-off between dictionary-based and parallel-based methods.
- Reporting and statistical rigor: Some results with close scores (e.g., multiCluster vs. multiCCA in document classification) would benefit from significance testing and variance across runs. The correlation analysis (17 models) is informative, but robustness checks (e.g., bootstrapped confidence intervals, Spearman vs. Pearson, different languages/tasks) would strengthen claims.

Questions to Authors
- Can you confirm that the Google Translate dictionaries used for training in the 59-language setting are disjoint from those used for evaluation (word translation), and quantify any overlap? If overlap exists, how sensitive are results to removing intersecting pairs?
- How robust are the correlation findings to alternative downstream tasks or languages (e.g., adding more UD languages, NER, POS tagging)? Do the relative correlations of multiQVEC and multiQVEC-CCA persist?
- For multiCluster, how do cluster size thresholds (1k/10k) and dictionary noise affect performance? Could soft-clustering or sense-aware clustering mitigate the semantic ambiguity issue observed with 59 languages?
- For multiCCA, did you try using a hub language other than English or multi-hub projections (e.g., averaging projections from several high-resource languages)? Any effect on low-resource languages or coverage?
- Can you provide ablations on dictionary size/quality (Europarl vs. GT) and alignment threshold τ for dictionary extraction to illustrate performance–noise trade-offs?

Additional Comments
- Please report variance and significance where feasible, and consider Spearman correlations for robustness.
- It would be helpful to include comparisons with monolingual-only baselines (per language) to quantify the benefit of multilingual sharing.
- Clarify preprocessing (tokenization, lowercasing, handling of homographs and identical surface forms) and the rationale for excluding identical GT translations, which may discard valid cognates/loanwords.
