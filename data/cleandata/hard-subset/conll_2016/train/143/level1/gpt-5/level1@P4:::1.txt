Summary
The paper proposes two dictionary-based methods for inducing shared multilingual word embeddings without requiring parallel corpora: (1) multiCluster, which clusters translationally equivalent words across languages and trains skip-gram embeddings over multilingual cluster IDs; and (2) multiCCA, which learns monolingual embeddings independently and uses CCA with bilingual dictionaries to project all languages into an English pivot space. The paper also introduces intrinsic evaluation metrics for multilingual embeddings: multiQVEC (a multilingual extension of QVEC) and multiQVEC-CCA, which addresses QVEC’s basis-variance and dimensionality issues via CCA. The authors conduct correlation analyses showing that the proposed metrics correlate better with two downstream tasks (multilingual document classification and dependency parsing) than widely used intrinsic metrics. Finally, they present a web portal and release trained embeddings for up to 59 languages.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) An intrinsic evaluation method (multiQVEC-CCA) that is basis-invariant and yields stronger empirical correlations with downstream tasks than prior intrinsic metrics; accompanied by a multilingual extension (multiQVEC).
2) A simple, scalable, dictionary-based multilingual embedding method (multiCCA) that projects monolingual embeddings into a shared space, demonstrating competitive performance across 59 languages.
3) Public infrastructure: trained embeddings, datasets, and a portal that standardizes multilingual embedding evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated evaluation advance: The multiQVEC-CCA metric directly addresses QVEC’s sensitivity to basis rotations and dimensionality, and the correlation study (17 models, two downstream tasks) shows substantially higher correlations than cross-lingual similarity and word translation (e.g., 0.896 with document classification). This is a meaningful step toward more predictive intrinsic evaluation.
- Scalable multilingual embedding induction without parallel corpora: MultiCCA leverages only monolingual corpora plus bilingual dictionaries, scales to 59 languages, and outperforms multiCluster consistently in that large-scale setting. This is practical for low-resource scenarios lacking parallel data.
- Comprehensive and transparent empirical study: The paper evaluates across multiple intrinsic metrics and two extrinsic tasks, reports coverage alongside scores, and discusses confounds arising from unequal coverage and vocabulary intersections, improving interpretability of results.
- Useful community resources: The evaluation portal, datasets, and released embeddings (with development/test splits) can standardize comparisons and lower barriers for future work, increasing the impact beyond the specific methods.
- Solid baselines and analysis: The paper contrasts dictionary-based approaches with parallel-only (multiSkip) and matrix factorization (translation-invariance) baselines, and analyzes when each method performs best; e.g., translation-invariance excels on some intrinsic metrics, while dictionary-based methods often lead on extrinsic tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and potential biases in correlation analysis: Correlation experiments are restricted to three languages (en, it, da) and two tasks; supersense resources exist only for a few languages and may not be consistent. This limits generalizability of the strong correlation claims, especially for parsing where multiQVEC-CCA correlates weakly (0.273).
- Baseline comparability concerns: The multiSkip implementation excludes monolingual data, unlike some prior art, likely weakening its performance. For translation-invariance, a smaller context window is used due to computational constraints. These choices may disadvantage baselines relative to proposed methods.
- Reliance on machine translation dictionaries and constrained candidate sets: For 59-language evaluations, Google Translate-derived dictionaries are used both for training (dictionaries) and evaluation (word translation), which may be noisy and difficult to reproduce; the word translation metric restricts candidates to dataset vocabularies, potentially inflating accuracy.
- Theoretical clarity of multiCCA mapping: The derivation assumes CCA projection matrices are non-singular and uses an inverse in constructing the multilingual mapping into English space. CCA typically yields projections onto lower-dimensional correlated subspaces; the conditions under which the inverses exist and are numerically stable are not fully justified.
- Method novelty: MultiCluster is a straightforward clustering-and-skip-gram approach that can exacerbate polysemy by conflating many-to-many translations; while its large-scale empirical contrast with multiCCA is informative, the methodological novelty is modest. The multilingual extension of CCA via an English pivot is simple, though practically valuable.

Questions to Authors
- multiCCA mapping: Please clarify the dimensionality and rank assumptions ensuring Tm→m,en and Ten→m,en are invertible. If CCA projects to a subspace, what is inverted, and how do you ensure numerical stability?
- Pivot sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice of pivot language? Have you tried using a different pivot or a joint alignment into a learned latent space?
- Baseline settings: Can you provide results for multiSkip augmented with monolingual data (as in related work) and for translation-invariance with larger windows or optimized hyperparameters, to strengthen baseline competitiveness?
- Dictionary quality: For the 59-language setting, what is the coverage and noise level of Google Translate-derived dictionaries? Do results change materially with alternative dictionary sources or thresholds?
- Word translation evaluation: What happens if candidate sets include the full target vocabulary (or a larger, more realistic set), and/or if hubness mitigation (e.g., CSLS) is applied?

Additional comments
- Reporting variance: Including standard deviations over multiple training runs would help quantify the robustness of correlations and extrinsic results.
- Polysemy handling: Given multiCluster’s degradation at 59 languages, have you considered sense-aware clustering or soft alignment to mitigate semantic conflation?
- Portal longevity: Please comment on the stability and long-term hosting of the evaluation portal and any mirrors to ensure reproducibility.
