This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings. The authors propose two dictionary-based estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) and a new evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), alongside an evaluation portal.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Multilingual Embedding Estimation Methods:** The multiCCA method, which projects embeddings into a shared English space, and multiCluster, which utilizes clustering based on bilingual dictionaries, are the primary contributions. While dictionary-based methods aren’t entirely novel, extending them to a 59-language scale and the specific implementation details (especially multiCCA’s projection approach) are valuable.
2. **Improved Evaluation Metric:** The multiQVEC-CCA metric addresses theoretical shortcomings of the existing multiQVEC metric and demonstrably correlates better with downstream tasks. This is a significant contribution, as reliable evaluation is crucial for progress in multilingual embedding research.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The development and release of the web portal, benchmarks, and code are a valuable contribution to the community, facilitating further research and reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods across a range of intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The correlation analysis (Table 1) is particularly convincing, showing that multiQVEC-CCA outperforms other metrics in predicting downstream performance.
2. **Scalability to a Large Number of Languages:** The work successfully scales to 59 languages, a significant achievement given the challenges of data acquisition and computational complexity. This makes the proposed methods potentially useful for a wider range of applications than previous approaches.
3. **Well-Defined Methodology:** The experimental setup is clearly described, with details on data sources, training parameters, and evaluation metrics. The authors acknowledge potential biases in the evaluation process and attempt to mitigate them (e.g., reporting coverage percentages).
4. **Practical Impact:** The open-source release of code and the evaluation portal will lower the barrier to entry for researchers working in this area and promote reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While the multiCCA and multiCluster methods are well-executed, the underlying concepts (dictionary-based alignment, CCA) are not entirely novel. The main contribution lies in the specific implementation and scaling to a large number of languages.
2. **Dependence on Dictionary Quality:** The performance of multiCluster and multiCCA is heavily reliant on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t sufficiently discuss the potential impact of noisy or incomplete dictionaries. The method for generating dictionaries from Google Translate for the 47 languages is particularly concerning and could introduce significant errors.
3. **Overemphasis on English as the Hub Language:** The multiCCA method’s reliance on English as the central vector space may introduce biases and limit the transferability of embeddings to languages that are very different from English. Exploring alternative hub languages or a more distributed approach might be beneficial.
4. **Downstream Task Selection:** The choice of document classification and dependency parsing as downstream tasks is somewhat limited. Including tasks that require more complex cross-lingual reasoning (e.g., machine translation, cross-lingual information retrieval) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of dictionary quality on the performance of multiCluster and multiCCA, particularly for the 47 languages where dictionaries were generated using Google Translate? Do you have any strategies for mitigating the effects of noisy dictionaries?
2. Did you consider alternative hub languages for the multiCCA method, or explore a more distributed approach that doesn’t rely on a single central vector space? If so, what were the results?
3. What are the computational resources required to run the evaluation portal and train the multilingual embeddings? Is it accessible to researchers with limited computational resources?
4. In Table 3, you mention that "the gap reduces to 0.7 in monolingual QVEC-CCA and 2.5 in multiQVEC-CCA" when evaluating on the intersection of vocabularies. Can you provide those exact numbers and potentially include that comparison in the main table?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, but some sections (e.g., the mathematical formulation of QVEC-CCA) could benefit from further clarification.
*   The reference list is extensive and includes relevant work in the field.
*   The authors should address the potential limitations of their approach in the discussion section, particularly regarding dictionary quality and the reliance on English as a hub language.
