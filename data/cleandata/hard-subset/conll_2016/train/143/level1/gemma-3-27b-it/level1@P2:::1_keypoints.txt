- Proposes two dictionary-based estimation methods: multiCCA (projects embeddings into shared English space) and multiCluster (clusters via bilingual dictionaries), scaled to 59 languages.
- Introduces multiQVEC-CCA, an improved evaluation metric that fixes theoretical issues of multiQVEC and correlates better with downstream tasks.
- Releases a public web portal, benchmarks, and code to support reproducibility and community use.
- Provides a strong empirical evaluation showing multiQVEC-CCA predicts downstream performance better (Table 1 correlation analysis).
- Demonstrates scalability to 59 languages, addressing data and computational challenges.
- Presents a clear experimental setup with detailed data sources, training parameters, and evaluation metrics, reporting coverage percentages to mitigate bias.
- Offers practical impact through open-source resources that lower barriers for further research.
- Estimation methods have limited novelty since they extend known dictionary-based alignment and CCA ideas; main novelty is implementation and scaling.
- Method performance depends heavily on bilingual dictionary quality, with concern about noisy/incomplete dictionaries (notably those generated via Google Translate).
- multiCCA’s reliance on English as a hub may introduce bias and limit transferability for typologically distant languages.
- Downstream evaluation is limited to document classification and dependency parsing; lacks tasks requiring complex cross-lingual reasoning (e.g., MT, CLIR).
- Requests elaboration on the impact of dictionary quality for the 47 Google-Translate-derived languages and mitigation strategies for noisy dictionaries.
- Asks whether alternative hub languages or distributed approaches were considered for multiCCA and what results were obtained.
- Requests details on computational resources needed to run the portal and train embeddings and whether the setup is accessible to low-resource researchers.
- Asks for exact numbers referenced in Table 3 about gaps on the intersection of vocabularies and suggests including that comparison in the main table.
- Notes the paper is well-written but the mathematical formulation of QVEC-CCA could use clearer explanation.
- Highlights the reference list is extensive and relevant.
- Advises authors to discuss limitations explicitly, especially concerning dictionary quality and the English hub assumption.
