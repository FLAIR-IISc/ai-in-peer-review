This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The authors propose two estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, leveraging dictionaries and monolingual data, and a new evaluation metric, multiQVEC-CCA, claiming improved correlation with downstream tasks. They also provide a web portal and open-source releases to facilitate further research.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **MultiQVEC-CCA and its evaluation:** The most significant contribution is the development and demonstration of multiQVEC-CCA as a more robust evaluation metric for multilingual embeddings. The authors identify shortcomings in existing metrics (QVEC) and offer a theoretically sound and empirically validated improvement. The correlation analysis convincingly shows its better alignment with downstream task performance compared to other intrinsic metrics.
2. **Comprehensive Multilingual Embedding Estimation:** The work provides a practical and scalable approach to estimating embeddings for a large number of languages (59), which significantly expands the scale of previous work in this area. While the estimation methods themselves aren't radically novel, the ability to apply them to such a large multilingual setup is valuable.
3. **Public Resources (Portal & Data):** The creation of a web portal and open-source release of methods and data represents a useful contribution to the research community, lowering the barrier to entry for researchers in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Validation of MultiQVEC-CCA:** The correlation analysis in Section 5.1 provides compelling evidence for the superiority of multiQVEC-CCA over existing intrinsic evaluation metrics. The results are clearly presented and statistically significant, bolstering the claim that this metric more accurately reflects downstream task performance.
2. **Large-Scale Multilingual Setup:** The study's scope – encompassing 59 languages – is a significant accomplishment. This scale provides a broader perspective on the challenges and opportunities of multilingual embedding research compared to studies focused on a limited number of languages.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors employ a variety of evaluation metrics – both intrinsic and extrinsic – providing a multifaceted assessment of their methods. This thoroughness strengthens the reliability of the findings.
4. **Practical Resources:** The web portal and open-source releases are a valuable asset to the NLP community. They facilitate reproducibility, encourage further research, and enable broader adoption of the proposed methods.
5. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of multilingual embeddings and motivates the research with relevant examples like improved machine translation and transfer learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** The proposed estimation methods, multiCluster and multiCCA, build upon existing techniques (CCA, skipgram). While their application to a large-scale multilingual setup is valuable, the core ideas lack significant innovation.  The comparison to other estimation methods (multiSkip, translation-invariance) doesn’t demonstrate substantial improvements over established techniques.
2. **Data Quality Concerns (Dictionaries):** The creation of dictionaries for 47 languages using Google Translate raises concerns about data quality and potential biases. While acknowledged, the impact of these potentially noisy dictionaries on the embedding quality isn’t fully investigated. The threshold τ=0.1 for dictionary creation appears somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from more robust justification or experimentation.
3. **Coverage Issues and Vocabulary Discrepancies:** The reported coverage percentages in Table 3 highlight a potential issue: the different methods cover varying portions of the vocabulary. The attempt to address this by using the intersection of vocabularies feels like a potentially restrictive solution, as it discards a lot of information. The discussion of this issue doesn’t fully articulate its potential impact on the results.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the correlation analysis provides a high-level overview of performance, the paper lacks a deeper qualitative analysis of error cases or specific languages where the methods struggle. This would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
5. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choices of parameters like the cluster size in multiCluster and the context window size are not extensively explored or justified. The impact of these parameters on the results isn’t clearly articulated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the dictionary creation using Google Translate: What steps were taken to mitigate the potential biases introduced by machine translation? Were any filtering or post-editing procedures applied to improve the quality of the generated dictionaries?
2.  The evaluation results show varying coverage percentages across methods. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of words that are covered by different methods? Are there systematic differences in the vocabulary covered that might explain the observed performance differences?
3.  The paper mentions that multiCCA projects embeddings into the English vector space. How sensitive are the results to the choice of English as the pivot language? Would using a different pivot language (e.g., a language with larger parallel corpora) significantly alter the performance?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of each of the proposed methods? How does the runtime scale with the number of languages and the size of the corpora?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The code and data availability is commendable and encourages reproducibility.
*   Consider providing more detailed ablation studies on the components of multiCCA and multiCluster.
*   The citation of "Luong et al., 2015a" appears to be missing from the references section.
