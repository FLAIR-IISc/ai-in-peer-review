This paper introduces methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages. The core contributions are two embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) based on dictionaries and monolingual data, a refined evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA) building on previous work, and a web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** The development and release of both novel evaluation metrics (multiQVEC-CCA) and an accompanying web portal represent a significant contribution. The authors acknowledge limitations of existing evaluation methods and address them, improving correlation with downstream tasks (as shown in Table 1). Providing a publicly accessible evaluation tool will undoubtedly benefit the community.
2. **Scalability to Many Languages:** The ability to estimate embeddings for 59 languages, using only dictionaries and monolingual data, is a strong point. Previous work often focused on limited bilingual scenarios. This broader coverage enables research in languages with limited parallel corpora.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing the proposed methods against established baselines across various intrinsic and extrinsic tasks. The inclusion of results for both 12 and 59 languages offers valuable insights into the performance scaling of the proposed methods.  The clear presentation of coverage percentages alongside evaluation scores is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** While multiCluster and multiCCA are extensions to existing techniques (CCA and clustering-based approaches), the core ideas aren’t fundamentally novel. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of these methods over existing approaches, particularly when considering the complexity involved.  The performance gains observed are often modest, and the dependence on the quality of bilingual dictionaries is a potential limitation not fully addressed.
2. **Dependence on Dictionary Quality:** The performance of multiCluster and multiCCA is heavily reliant on the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used. The paper mentions using Google Translate for dictionary creation for many languages, which introduces potential noise and bias. This dependency isn't adequately addressed in the analysis, and the sensitivity of the results to dictionary quality requires further investigation.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components within multiCluster and multiCCA.  For example, exploring the impact of different clustering algorithms or CCA parameters could provide insights into the effectiveness of each method and guide future improvements. The impact of monolingual corpus size on performance isn't discussed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the bilingual dictionaries used, particularly for the 47 languages where dictionaries were generated using Google Translate? Could you provide a correlation between dictionary quality (e.g., precision/recall) and embedding performance?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the multilingual embeddings, particularly for the multiCCA method? How does this scale with the number of languages?
3. The paper mentions that QVEC-CCA addresses theoretical shortcomings of QVEC. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of these shortcomings and why QVEC-CCA is a more robust evaluation metric?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 3 could be improved for readability.
*   The paper references a GitHub repository for code and data, but the URL has been redacted. Providing this link during the author response would be beneficial.
*   Consider including a discussion of the limitations of using supersense tags for cross-lingual evaluation, particularly regarding potential inconsistencies across languages.
