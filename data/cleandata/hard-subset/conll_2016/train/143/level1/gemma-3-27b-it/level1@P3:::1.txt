This paper presents methods for estimating and evaluating multilingual word embeddings across 59 languages, without relying on parallel corpora for estimation. The core contributions are two embedding estimation methods (multiCluster and multiCCA), a refined evaluation metric (multiQVEC-CCA), and a publicly available evaluation portal. While the scale of the multilingual setup and the portal are valuable, the paper's strengths are somewhat overshadowed by methodological shortcomings and a lack of compelling comparative results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multilingual Evaluation Portal:** The most significant contribution is the creation and release of the web portal for evaluating multilingual embeddings. This resource will undoubtedly be valuable to the research community, providing a standardized platform for comparing methods and tracking progress. The associated benchmarks are also beneficial.
2. **MultiQVEC-CCA:** The proposed refinement to QVEC, multiQVEC-CCA, addresses a theoretical flaw in the original metric (lack of invariance to linear transformations and unnormalized scores) and demonstrably correlates better with downstream tasks than other intrinsic metrics. This is a solid contribution to the evaluation of multilingual embeddings.
3. **Scalable Dictionary-Based Estimation:** While not groundbreaking in its core ideas, the application of dictionary-based methods (multiCluster and multiCCA) to a relatively large number of languages (59) is a noteworthy effort. The scaling presents practical challenges and highlights the limitations of relying solely on dictionaries.

**Strengths:**

1. **Large-Scale Multilingual Setup:** The work tackles a complex problem - multilingual embedding estimation - at a significant scale. Working with 59 languages, even with the limitations of dictionary-based methods, pushes the boundaries of previous work which often focused on bilingual scenarios. This breadth is valuable.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper employs a suite of both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics, offering a relatively comprehensive assessment of the proposed methods. The correlation analysis between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics is a particularly strong aspect, clearly demonstrating the superiority of multiQVEC-CCA.
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The release of the evaluation portal, the code, and the trained embeddings promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research. This openness is commendable and will maximize the impact of the work.
4. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of massively multilingual embeddings for tasks like machine translation and transfer learning. The motivation is well-grounded in the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Estimation Methods:** Both multiCluster and multiCCA build on existing techniques (clustering and CCA, respectively). While the extension to multiple languages is a practical consideration, the core algorithmic ideas lack significant novelty. The implementation details, while clearly explained, don't present substantial innovations.
2. **Performance Concerns & Baseline Comparison:** The experimental results are not entirely convincing. While multiCCA performs relatively well, the performance gains over baselines are not dramatic, especially given the effort invested in scaling up the method to 59 languages. The reported improvements on downstream tasks are modest. The choice of baselines, particularly multiSkip, appears weak, potentially inflating the apparent benefits of the proposed methods. A more robust comparison against state-of-the-art methods trained on parallel data would be crucial.
3. **Dictionary Quality & Data Bias:** The reliance on dictionaries, particularly those generated through Google Translate for the 47 languages without direct parallel corpora, raises concerns about data quality and potential biases. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't adequately address how these biases might affect the results. The use of a fixed threshold (τ = 0.1) for dictionary creation seems arbitrary and might not be optimal for all language pairs.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the methods struggle and *why* would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, analyzing the performance on different language families or identifying specific types of words that are poorly represented could guide further research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the dictionary creation process using Google Translate, what steps were taken to assess and mitigate potential biases introduced by the translation service?
2.  The paper states that multiSkip only utilizes parallel data. Is it possible to augment multiSkip with monolingual data, and if so, how would that impact its performance relative to multiCCA and multiCluster?
3.  The correlation coefficients in Table 1 are relatively low. What is your interpretation of these results, and what factors might contribute to the weak correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation metrics?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train the multilingual embeddings, particularly for multiCCA and multiSkip, to facilitate reproducibility and comparison with other methods?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations of some of the technical details.
*   The supplementary material is essential for understanding the full scope of the experiments and should be thoroughly reviewed.
*   The web portal URL should remain active and well-maintained to maximize its usefulness to the community.
