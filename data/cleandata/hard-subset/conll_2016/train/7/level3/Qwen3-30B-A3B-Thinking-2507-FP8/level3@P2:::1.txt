

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 (unlike previous methods that use E(R) = 0). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) PoP enables PPMI weighting on random projection-based semantic spaces, which previous methods like RI cannot do; (2) PoP provides a computationally efficient approach to semantic space construction, training in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days; (3) PoP-constructed spaces combined with PPMI achieve competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks (0.75 average score on MEN).

## Strengths
1. **Computational efficiency**: The claim that PoP enables semantic space construction in seconds/minutes (vs. hours/days for neural methods) is a significant practical contribution, especially for resource-constrained applications. The authors provide evidence of this efficiency through their experimental setup.

2. **Novel technical insight**: The insight that using random projection matrices with E(R) > 0 enables subsequent PPMI weighting (unlike previous methods) is a technically interesting contribution that addresses a limitation in the existing random projection literature.

3. **Competitive performance**: The reported 0.75 average score on MEN is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches, demonstrating that the method can achieve good performance without neural network training.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of direct comparison to related work**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper fails to provide direct comparisons with other relevant methods (e.g., RI, other random projection techniques, or count-based models with PPMI), making it impossible to assess whether PoP actually outperforms existing approaches.

2. **Unjustified use of Kendall's τb**: The authors use Kendall's τb for similarity measurement but fail to explain why it's superior to the standard Spearman correlation used in semantic relatedness evaluations. They also don't justify why they don't use Spearman for their method, given they use it for evaluation.

3. **Inadequate evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to the MEN dataset without comparison to standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). The paper also lacks comparisons with other dimensionality reduction techniques that can be used with PPMI.

4. **Figure 1 error**: The paper claims to show three methods in Figure 1 (count-based, RI, PoP), but only two lines are visible, suggesting a missing line or plotting error that undermines the evaluation.

5. **Methodological inconsistency**: The paper states that PoP-constructed spaces are "non-Gaussian" but then uses Pearson's correlation after PPMI transformation (which assumes Gaussianity) without explaining the transition from non-Gaussian to Gaussian-like distributions.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why is the transformability with PPMI important for semantic space construction? What specific advantages does it provide beyond the performance results reported in the paper?

2. Given that Spearman's ρ is the standard metric for semantic relatedness tasks, why not use it for similarity measurement in the PoP method, rather than introducing Kendall's τb without justification?

3. The paper evaluates only on the MEN dataset. Why not include standard semantic relatedness benchmarks like WordSim-353 or SimLex-999 to better demonstrate the method's generality?

4. Figure 1 appears to show only two lines, but the paper claims three methods are compared. Please clarify and correct this issue in the camera-ready version.

## Additional Comments
The paper contains a minor typo ("large extend" should be "large extent") and makes an unsubstantiated claim that "the need for such transformations arises from the heavy-tailed distributions" without providing evidence for this assertion. The methodological justification for using PoP instead of existing techniques is insufficiently developed. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of why their approach is necessary when existing methods like PPMI-weighted count-based models with SVD are already effective.
