

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 (unlike previous methods with E(R) = 0). The key innovation is that PoP-constructed spaces can undergo weighting transformations like PPMI, which previous random projection methods cannot. The authors claim PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN relatedness test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural network approaches.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A methodological insight: Demonstrating that random projection matrices with E(R) > 0 enable subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI, which is not possible with standard random projection methods
2. Practical efficiency: Showing that PoP+PPMI can achieve competitive performance with dramatically reduced computational requirements compared to neural network approaches
3. Empirical validation: Providing evidence that PoP-constructed spaces can be effectively transformed using standard weighting techniques

Strengths:
1. The computational efficiency claim is well-supported with clear evidence. The paper demonstrates that PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days required for neural networks, which is a significant practical advantage for resource-constrained applications.

2. The theoretical insight about the importance of E(R) > 0 for enabling PPMI transformations is novel and well-explained. The authors clearly articulate why previous random projection methods (with E(R) = 0) couldn't use PPMI, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

3. The reported performance (0.75 on MEN) is competitive with state-of-the-art neural methods, which is impressive for a method that doesn't require training. This demonstrates that the method can achieve high quality without the computational burden of neural networks.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks direct comparisons to relevant baselines. It doesn't compare to standard count-based methods with PPMI (e.g., standard co-occurrence matrices with PPMI and SVD), which would better demonstrate the novelty and advantages of PoP. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI transformations, but don't show how it compares to existing approaches that already use PPMI.

2. There's an inconsistency between the evaluation metric (Spearman's ρ) and the similarity measure used in the model (Kendall's τb). The authors use Spearman's ρ for evaluation but justify using Kendall's τb for similarity computation without addressing why this is appropriate. This inconsistency needs clear justification.

3. The evaluation uses MEN relatedness test, which is not a standard benchmark in semantic similarity assessment. The paper should include results on more established benchmarks like WordSim353, SimLex-999, or RareWords to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. Figure 1 is problematic as only two lines are visible (RI and count-based), but the authors claim to show PoP as well. This makes it difficult to assess their claim that PoP outperforms the other methods.

5. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the experimental setup (e.g., exact dimensionality values, s parameters) to reproduce the results, which is a significant weakness for a methodological paper.

Questions to Authors:
1. Why was Kendall's τb used for similarity computation when Spearman's ρ was used for evaluation? What evidence supports that τb is superior in this context?

2. Can you provide direct comparisons between PoP+PPMI and standard count-based models with PPMI (e.g., using SVD truncation)? This would better demonstrate the novelty and advantages of PoP.

3. Please clarify whether the reported 0.75 MEN score is the mean across runs or the best single run, and provide standard deviations.

4. Why was MEN chosen as the primary evaluation dataset instead of more standard benchmarks like WordSim353 or SimLex-999?

5. Please fix Figure 1 to properly show all three lines (count-based, RI, PoP) as claimed in the text.
