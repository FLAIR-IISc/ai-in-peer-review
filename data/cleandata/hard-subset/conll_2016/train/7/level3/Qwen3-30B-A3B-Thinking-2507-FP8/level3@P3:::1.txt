

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive elements (E(R) > 0) rather than the typical E(R) = 0. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting after projection, which is not possible with existing random projection methods. The main contributions as I see them are: (1) a novel random projection technique that preserves the ability to apply weighting methods like PPMI after projection; (2) empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive semantic relatedness performance (0.75 on MEN) with significantly lower computational requirements; and (3) analysis of PoP's behavior with respect to key parameters like dimensionality and sparsity.

## Strengths
1. **Computational efficiency claim is compelling**: The paper's claim that PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days represents a significant practical advantage. The authors provide reasonable justification for this claim by noting the low dimensionality of PoP vectors (a few hundred dimensions) and the fast application of PPMI to these vectors, which is substantially faster than applying PPMI to high-dimensional count-based models. This efficiency gain could be highly valuable for resource-constrained applications.

2. **Novel technical contribution**: The core innovation of enabling PPMI weighting after projection is technically sound and addresses a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods. The authors correctly identify that methods like RI with E(R) = 0 cannot be transformed using PPMI due to zero sums, while PoP with E(R) > 0 avoids this issue. This is a clear technical contribution that has practical implications.

3. **Competitive results**: The reported MEN score of 0.75 is comparable to state-of-the-art neural methods (as claimed), which is impressive given the computational efficiency claims. The paper demonstrates that the method can achieve high performance without neural network training, which is a notable achievement for a non-neural method.

## Weaknesses
1. **Major lack of direct comparison to related work**: The paper fails to provide direct comparisons with other methods that could achieve similar results with comparable efficiency. For example, there's no comparison with other count-based methods that use PPMI followed by dimensionality reduction (e.g., SVD truncation), which are known to be efficient and perform well. The comparison to RI is insufficient since RI is explicitly designed to preserve distances rather than improve discriminative power.

2. **Unclear motivation for PPMI transformability**: The paper repeatedly emphasizes the importance of being able to apply PPMI after projection but never clearly explains why this is a critical advantage. The authors state that "most importantly, since E(R) > 0, weighting methods such as PPMI can be applied to PoP-constructed spaces," but they don't demonstrate that this capability leads to significantly better performance than other approaches that don't have this property.

3. **Methodological inconsistency with correlation measures**: The paper uses Kendall's τb for similarity computation in PoP spaces but evaluates using Spearman's ρ, which is standard in semantic tasks. The authors don't justify why they use τb for computation but ρ for evaluation, and they don't explain why τb is appropriate for their non-Gaussian spaces when Spearman is the established standard for semantic similarity tasks.

4. **Inadequate evaluation benchmarking**: The evaluation is limited to the MEN test set, which is not the standard benchmark for semantic relatedness. The paper should include standard benchmarks like WordSim-353, SimLex-999, or RG-65 to properly establish the method's performance. The lack of these standard comparisons makes it difficult to assess the true quality of the results.

5. **Figure 1 issue**: Figure 1 shows only two lines (count-based and RI) but claims to show three (including PoP), suggesting a missing line. This undermines the credibility of the empirical claims.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims PoP-constructed models can be "efficiently transferred onto spaces that are discriminative for semantic similarity assessments" using PPMI, but doesn't demonstrate that this is actually more efficient than standard PPMI+SVD approaches. Can the authors provide quantitative comparisons of computational time between PoP+PPMI and traditional count-based+PPMI+SVD?

2. The paper uses Kendall's τb for similarity computation but evaluates with Spearman's ρ. Can the authors clarify why they chose τb over the standard Spearman correlation for their similarity measure, and why they don't use Spearman for both computation and evaluation?

3. The paper states that "PoP-constructed models display a better performance than RI on tasks of semantic similarity assessments" without PPMI weighting, but Figure 1 shows PoP outperforming RI only for small context windows. Can the authors clarify the conditions under which PoP outperforms RI without PPMI, and why this advantage disappears with larger context windows?
