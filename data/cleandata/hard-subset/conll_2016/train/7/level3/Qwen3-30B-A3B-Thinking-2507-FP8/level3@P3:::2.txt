

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing semantic spaces where the projection matrix has a positive expected value (E(R) > 0). The authors claim this enables the application of PPMI weighting to the resulting low-dimensional embeddings, which would otherwise be impossible with standard random projection methods that use matrices with E(R) = 0. They report that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 average Spearman's ρ on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art methods but with significantly reduced computational requirements.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)
1. Proposal of PoP as a random projection method with E(R) > 0 enabling PPMI application
2. Demonstration that PoP-constructed spaces can be transformed using PPMI for improved semantic similarity assessment
3. Claim of computational efficiency through low-dimensional embeddings

## Strengths
1. The core idea of using a positive projection matrix to enable PPMI weighting is conceptually interesting, though the paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from existing methods.
2. The empirical results on MEN (0.75 average ρ) are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches, suggesting potential utility.
3. The paper provides useful empirical analysis of parameter effects (dimensionality, non-zero elements), demonstrating reasonable behavior with increasing model size.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clarity and poor writing**: The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous language errors ("To a large extend" Line 86, "such PPMI" Line 142), poor argument structure, and unsupported statements ("in addition" Line 114, "Since" Line 137, "thus" Line 154). The rationale for PoP is unclear to non-experts, making it hard to assess the method's merits.

2. **Insufficient differentiation from prior work**: The paper fails to clearly distinguish PoP from existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI). It claims PoP enables PPMI weighting, but doesn't adequately explain why previous methods couldn't do this (e.g., why can't RI be combined with PPMI by simply shifting values to be positive?). The novelty claim is unsubstantiated.

3. **Insufficient evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MEN), with no comparison to standard benchmarks like WordSim-353 or SimLex-999. This makes it impossible to assess the method's generalizability or relative performance across different semantic tasks. The paper also doesn't compare against other PPMI-based methods, making the claimed "computational efficiency" difficult to verify.

4. **Lack of theoretical grounding**: The authors admit they cannot provide mathematical justification for the error bounds (δ) of PoP, relying solely on empirical results. This is problematic for a method claiming to be based on random projections, where theoretical guarantees are typically expected. The paper fails to establish how PoP differs mathematically from existing methods.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper claims PoP is "highly scalable and computationally efficient" without providing computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons. The claim that "models can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" is unsupported by any timing data.

## Questions to Authors
1. How exactly does PoP differ from previous random projection methods in terms of mathematical formulation and implementation? Please provide a clear comparison with RI, RRI, ISA, and RMI.

2. The paper claims PoP enables PPMI weighting, but previous methods could also apply PPMI by shifting values to be positive. What makes PoP's approach superior to simply adding a constant to RI vectors before applying PPMI?

3. Why was the evaluation limited to the MEN dataset? Please provide results on at least one additional standard benchmark (e.g., WordSim-353) to demonstrate generalizability.

4. Please provide detailed runtime measurements showing the claimed computational efficiency of PoP compared to standard count-based models with PPMI and matrix factorization.

5. What is the theoretical basis for the distribution choice in Equation 2? Why is α = 0.5 specifically chosen, and how does this choice affect the method's performance?

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting idea that warrants further investigation, but the current version is fundamentally flawed by its lack of clarity, insufficient evaluation, and failure to properly differentiate from prior work. The authors should substantially improve the presentation and clarity before resubmission, provide a thorough comparison with existing methods, expand the evaluation to multiple standard benchmarks, and provide concrete evidence supporting their computational efficiency claims. As written, the paper does not make a compelling case for the novelty or advantages of PoP.
