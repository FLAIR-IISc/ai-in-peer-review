

**Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"**

**Summary & Contributions:**  
This paper proposes "positive-only projection" (PoP), a random projection method using non-negative matrices (E(R) > 0) to construct semantic spaces. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel dimensionality reduction technique enabling PPMI weighting on low-dimensional embeddings, (2) empirical claims of competitive performance (0.75 MEN score) with significantly faster training than neural networks, and (3) the use of Kendall's τb for similarity computation in non-Gaussian spaces. The authors frame PoP as addressing limitations of standard random projections (e.g., RI) that cannot use PPMI due to zero-mean projections.

**Strengths:**  
1. The computational efficiency claim (training in seconds vs. hours for neural networks) is compelling if verified, offering a practical alternative for resource-constrained settings.  
2. The observation that PoP preserves non-Gaussian distributional properties (unlike standard random projections) is theoretically interesting for semantic similarity tasks.  
3. The demonstration of PPMI applicability to PoP-constructed spaces (unlike RI) addresses a genuine limitation in existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical lack of comparison**: The paper omits direct comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., GloVe, FastText) and related work (e.g., RI variants), making the "competitive performance" claim unsubstantiated.  
2. **Methodological inconsistency**: The authors use Spearman's ρ for evaluation (standard in semantic tasks) but justify Kendall's τb as the similarity measure without explaining why it supersedes Spearman (which is directly used in evaluation).  
3. **Insufficient evaluation**: The MEN test alone is inadequate; standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) are missing, and Figure 1 shows only two visible lines (third line missing), undermining results.  
4. **Unjustified claims**: The motivation for PPMI transformability is unclear—why is this "important" when PPMI is typically applied to count-based models before dimensionality reduction?  
5. **Technical flaws**: Kendall's τb definition is nonstandard (omits τb normalization), and "large extend" is a typo.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Why is PPMI transformability a key contribution when PPMI is traditionally applied *before* dimensionality reduction in count-based models?  
2. How does PoP compare to neural methods (e.g., FastText) on standard benchmarks beyond MEN?  
3. Why was Spearman's ρ used for evaluation but not for similarity computation (τb), given its dominance in semantic tasks?
