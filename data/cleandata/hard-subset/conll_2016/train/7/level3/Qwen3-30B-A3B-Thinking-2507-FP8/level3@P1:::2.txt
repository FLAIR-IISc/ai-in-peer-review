

This paper proposes a novel technique called "Positive-only Projection" (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0, rather than the typical E(R) = 0 used in previous methods. The authors claim this enables application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces, resulting in computational efficiency and competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1. A method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive expected values (E(R) > 0), enabling PPMI weighting that was previously impossible with standard random projection methods
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive results on the MEN relatedness test (0.75 average score)
3. The computational efficiency claim, suggesting models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days

Strengths:
1. The core idea of enabling PPMI application through positive random projections is potentially valuable. The authors correctly identify that standard random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) cannot use PPMI due to their zero-mean distributions, which is a genuine limitation of those approaches. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as PPMI weighting is known to significantly improve performance in count-based models.

2. The computational efficiency claim is potentially significant. The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive results without neural network training, which could be valuable for resource-constrained settings. The authors provide evidence that the method can build models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days, which would be a substantial advantage if accurate.

3. The empirical results on MEN are promising, showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving 0.75 average score, comparable to state-of-the-art methods. The authors demonstrate that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models, and the PPMI transformation significantly boosts performance.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks clarity and fails to adequately distinguish PoP from existing random projection methods. The authors claim PoP is "similar to RI" but fail to clearly explain how it differs in implementation, theoretical basis, or advantages beyond the E(R) > 0 aspect. The introduction and methodology sections are confusing, with poor transitions ("in addition," "since," "thus" used incorrectly) that make the rationale hard to follow.

2. The evaluation is insufficiently broad. The authors only report results on the MEN relatedness test, failing to include other standard benchmarks like WordSim-353, SimLex-999, or RG-65. This is a major limitation, as performance on a single dataset does not constitute strong evidence for the method's general applicability.

3. The paper contains numerous English language errors that significantly hurt readability (e.g., "To a large extend" [Line 86], "such PPMI" [Line 142]). These errors, combined with poor organization, make the paper difficult to follow for non-experts.

4. The paper is not self-contained for readers unfamiliar with random projections. The authors assume significant prior knowledge without providing necessary context or explanations, making it hard for readers to evaluate the novelty and validity of the claims.

5. The authors' claim that "PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" lacks proper contextualization. They don't provide a clear comparison to specific state-of-the-art methods or their reported scores, making this claim difficult to verify.

Questions to Authors:
1. How exactly does PoP differ in implementation from RI (which also uses sparse random projections)? The paper states PoP is "similar to RI" but doesn't clarify the key differences.

2. Why was the MEN test the only benchmark used? What evidence is there that PoP would perform similarly on other standard semantic similarity datasets?

3. The paper claims "models can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" - what hardware was used for these timing measurements, and how does this compare to the timing of state-of-the-art neural methods?

4. The authors state "PoP-constructed models display a better performance than RI on tasks of semantic similarity assessments" without providing quantitative evidence. Can they provide specific numbers showing this improvement?

5. The paper mentions "a very low dimensionality" for PoP vectors but doesn't specify what dimensionality was used for the main results. What is the dimensionality of the final vectors used in the MEN test?
