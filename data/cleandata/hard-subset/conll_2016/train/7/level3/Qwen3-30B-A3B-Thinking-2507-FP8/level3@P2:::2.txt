

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper proposes "Positive-only Projection" (PoP), a method for constructing word embeddings using random projections with positive expected values, enabling the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) weighting to the resulting spaces. The authors claim computational efficiency and competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks compared to neural embedding methods. The core idea is that by using projection matrices with E(R) > 0 (unlike previous methods with E(R) = 0), their approach allows PPMI application without the need for high-dimensional count-based models.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel random projection technique (PoP) that enables PPMI weighting on the resulting embeddings, addressing a limitation of previous random projection methods.
2. A demonstration that PoP-constructed models combined with PPMI achieve competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test (average 0.75).
3. A computational efficiency claim showing models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days.

## Strengths
1. The core insight that positive random projections enable PPMI application is potentially valuable. This addresses a genuine limitation of existing random projection methods (like RI) that cannot use PPMI due to their E(R)=0 property.
2. The computational efficiency claim, if validated, would be significant for practical applications where training neural embeddings is prohibitively expensive.
3. The use of Kendall's τb for similarity measurement in non-Gaussian spaces is a thoughtful adaptation to the method's properties, recognizing that traditional cosine similarity may not be optimal.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clarity and poor writing**: The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous grammatical errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86), unclear explanations, and poor sentence structure (e.g., "in addition," "since," "thus" starting sentences on lines 114, 137, 154). The paper fails to be self-contained for readers unfamiliar with random projections, making it inaccessible to many readers.
   
2. **Insufficient evaluation**: The paper evaluates only on the MEN dataset without comparison to standard benchmarks like WordSim353, SimLex-999, or WordAnalogy. The authors fail to justify why MEN alone is sufficient to claim competitive performance, especially since their results (0.75) are lower than state-of-the-art neural methods (which often exceed 0.80 on MEN).

3. **Unclear differentiation from prior work**: It's not clear how PoP fundamentally differs from existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) beyond the positive expectation. The paper doesn't adequately explain why E(R) > 0 is the critical distinction that enables PPMI application, nor does it provide a clear mathematical justification for this.

4. **Unsupported claims**: The paper makes strong claims about competitive performance without sufficient comparison to relevant baselines. For instance, it states that "PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms," but provides no direct comparison to specific state-of-the-art methods.

5. **Poor presentation of results**: The paper's presentation of results (e.g., Figure 1) is confusing, and the explanation of why PoP outperforms RI is not convincing. The claim that PoP "converges faster" without clear evidence of the speed advantage is unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide a clear mathematical distinction between PoP and existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) that explains why PoP can use PPMI while others cannot? Specifically, how does E(R) > 0 fundamentally enable PPMI application?

2. Why was only the MEN dataset used for evaluation? What evidence supports the claim that MEN results generalize to other semantic similarity tasks, especially given that most state-of-the-art methods report results across multiple benchmarks?

3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with other count-based methods that use PPMI (e.g., Levy and Goldberg 2014) to demonstrate the specific advantage of PoP+PPMI over these methods?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper addresses a potentially interesting problem but is currently unsuitable for publication due to insufficient clarity, inadequate evaluation, and lack of proper comparison with relevant prior work. The core idea has potential, but the paper fails to communicate it effectively or provide convincing evidence for its claims. The authors should substantially improve the presentation, clarify the method's distinction from prior work, and broaden the evaluation to standard benchmarks before resubmission.
