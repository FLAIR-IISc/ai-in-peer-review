

This paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection technique for word embeddings that uses a projection matrix with positive expected value (E(R) > 0), enabling subsequent application of PPMI weighting. The authors claim this approach achieves computational efficiency while maintaining competitive performance in semantic similarity tasks.

The primary contribution appears to be the PoP method itself, which they claim enables PPMI application on random projection spaces (unlike previous methods with E(R)=0). A secondary contribution is the demonstration that PoP+PPMI achieves MEN scores comparable to neural methods (0.75 average). However, the paper fails to clearly articulate how PoP fundamentally differs from existing random projection techniques like RI or RMI, making the claimed novelty difficult to assess.

Strengths include the empirical demonstration of PoP's performance on the MEN dataset and the logical connection between positive projection matrices and PPMI applicability. The authors also provide some parameter analysis showing how dimensionality affects performance.

The weaknesses are substantial. The paper lacks clarity throughout, with arguments hard to follow from the introduction onward. Key statements are poorly framed (e.g., "in addition," "since," "thus" at the start of lines 114, 137, 154). The paper is not self-contained for non-experts, requiring prior knowledge of random projection methods to follow the rationale. Evaluation is insufficiently broad, testing only on MEN without justification for excluding standard benchmarks like WordSim or SimLex. Numerous English language errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142) further hinder readability.

The authors' claim of computational efficiency lacks concrete evidence beyond vague assertions, and the paper fails to demonstrate how PoP outperforms existing methods in a way that justifies its novelty. Without addressing these clarity issues and expanding the evaluation, the paper's contribution remains unclear and unconvincing. The work requires substantial revision to clarify the method's novelty, improve presentation, and provide broader empirical validation before reconsideration.
