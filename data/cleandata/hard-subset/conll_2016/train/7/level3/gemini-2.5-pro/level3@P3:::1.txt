### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel random projection technique for creating low-dimensional word embeddings. The key innovation is the use of a projection matrix with only non-negative entries (expected value > 0). Unlike standard Random Indexing (RI), where the resulting vectors have an expected value of 0 and are unsuitable for certain transformations, PoP vectors preserve distributional properties of the original count space. This allows for the subsequent application of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly on the low-dimensional vectors. The authors demonstrate that this approach is computationally efficient and that PoP vectors, when weighted with PPMI, achieve competitive performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables post-hoc weighting.** The core idea of using a positive-only projection to create low-dimensional vectors that are still amenable to PPMI transformation is a clever and valuable contribution. It effectively bridges the efficiency of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of a highly efficient pipeline for creating effective word embeddings.** The paper shows that the PoP+PPMI+Pearson pipeline achieves strong results (Spearman's ρ = 0.75 on MEN) with significantly lower computational cost than methods requiring SVD on high-dimensional matrices or extensive neural network training.

### Strengths

1.  **Computational Efficiency and Scalability:** The primary strength of the proposed method is its efficiency. As an incremental random projection method, it avoids the high computational costs associated with SVD or training neural networks. The ability to apply PPMI on the already-reduced dimension vectors is a significant practical advantage, making the construction of high-quality embeddings feasible in seconds or minutes, as claimed by the authors. This makes the method highly attractive for resource-constrained scenarios or applications requiring rapid model building.
2.  **Novel and Sound Methodology:** The idea of designing a random projection to specifically preserve properties that allow for PPMI weighting is novel. Standard random projections aim to preserve L2 distances, which washes out the distributional information needed for PPMI. By using positive-only projections, the authors show (Figure 3) that the long-tailed distribution of the original count space is largely maintained, which is the key mechanism enabling the subsequent successful PPMI transformation.
3.  **Strong Performance on the Chosen Task:** The PoP+PPMI+Pearson model achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.75 on the MEN dataset, which is a strong and competitive result. The authors also report reaching 0.78 with further tuning. This demonstrates that the proposed lightweight method does not make a significant trade-off in quality, at least on this specific task, and validates the overall approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** This is a major weakness. The paper claims its results are "comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" and neural embeddings, yet it provides no direct comparisons. The only baselines are a simple count-based model and Random Indexing (RI). To substantiate its claims, the paper must include comparisons to standard, strong baselines like word2vec (SGNS), GloVe, and SVD on a PPMI matrix, all trained and evaluated on the same UKWaC corpus under a controlled experimental setup. Without these comparisons, the claimed competitiveness of the method remains unverified.
2.  **Unclear and Inconsistent Methodological Choices:** The paper's choices regarding similarity metrics are not well-justified.
    *   The definition provided for Kendall's τb (Section 2.2) is non-standard. It seems to be defined over pairs of observations *within* the vectors, i.e., `(x_j, y_j)` and `(x_{j+1}, y_{j+1})`, which is unusual and unexplained. The standard definition considers all pairs of indices `(i, j)`. This non-standard definition requires clarification and justification.
    *   The paper initially motivates the use of Kendall's τb for non-Gaussian PoP vectors but then reports its best results using Pearson correlation on PPMI-transformed vectors. Given that Spearman's ρ is the evaluation metric and is also a rank-based correlation measure robust to non-Gaussian distributions, it is unclear why it wasn't used for similarity calculation throughout. The inconsistency in using different correlation measures for internal similarity and external evaluation is not justified.
3.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The entire evaluation rests on a single semantic relatedness dataset (MEN). While MEN is a respectable benchmark, robust evaluation of word embeddings typically involves a suite of tasks, including semantic similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and analogy tasks (e.g., Google Analogy). Relying on one dataset makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the embeddings produced by PoP and to understand their properties (e.g., do they capture more relatedness than similarity?).
4.  **Presentation and Clarity Issues:**
    *   In Figure 1, the legend indicates three methods (Count-based, RI, PoP), but only two lines are clearly visible. The text states that the performance of RI is "almost identical to the performance of the count-based model," suggesting the lines are overlapping. This should be made explicit in the caption to avoid confusion.
    *   The central motivation for why PPMI-transformability is a critical property could be strengthened. While the results show its effectiveness, the paper could better articulate why enabling this specific transformation is a more promising research direction than, for example, designing a random projection that directly produces vectors with better geometric properties for similarity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide direct, controlled comparisons of your PoP+PPMI+Pearson model against standard baselines like word2vec (SGNS) and GloVe, trained on the same UKWaC corpus and evaluated on the MEN dataset?
2.  Could you please clarify the definition of Kendall's τb used in Section 2.2? The pairwise definition seems non-standard and its derivation is unclear. A citation or a more detailed explanation of how pairs of observations are formed would be helpful.
3.  Given that Spearman's ρ is the evaluation metric for the task and is a robust rank-correlation measure, what was the rationale for not using it as the similarity function for the PoP and PoP+PPMI vectors, instead of Kendall's τ and Pearson's r?
4.  In Figure 1, do the lines for the count-based model and RI perfectly overlap? If so, could you please clarify this in the caption?
