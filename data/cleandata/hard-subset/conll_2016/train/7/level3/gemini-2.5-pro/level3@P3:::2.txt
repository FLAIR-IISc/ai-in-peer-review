### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection method for creating word embeddings. The core idea is to use a projection matrix whose elements have a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), in contrast to standard random indexing methods where E(R) = 0. The authors claim this property makes the resulting low-dimensional space amenable to subsequent weighting with Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a technique typically applied to high-dimensional count matrices. This approach aims to combine the computational efficiency and incrementality of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-weighted models. The method is evaluated on the MEN semantic relatedness task, where it reportedly achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art systems.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  The proposal of a random projection technique with a positive expected value (PoP), specifically designed to enable the application of PPMI weighting on the resulting low-dimensional vectors. This is a conceptually interesting attempt to merge two different paradigms of distributional semantics.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this PoP+PPMI combination can achieve strong performance on the MEN relatedness benchmark, suggesting the method holds promise.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Core Concept:** The central idea of designing a random projection specifically to preserve properties that allow for subsequent PPMI weighting is novel and well-motivated. It addresses a known limitation of standard random projection methods, which often do not perform as well as count-based models with sophisticated weighting like PPMI. The attempt to combine the efficiency of the former with the effectiveness of the latter is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Potential for High Computational Efficiency:** The paper makes a strong case for the method's efficiency. By first projecting to a low-dimensional space and *then* applying PPMI, the most computationally expensive step (PPMI calculation) is performed on small vectors. This, combined with the incremental nature of random projections, could offer significant speedups over traditional PPMI+SVD pipelines or neural network training, making it highly attractive for large-scale or resource-constrained scenarios.

3.  **Promising Preliminary Results:** Despite a narrow evaluation, the reported performance on the MEN dataset (ρ ≈ 0.75) is competitive and comparable to many state-of-the-art systems from that period. This result suggests that the proposed method has practical merit and is worthy of more thorough investigation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Severe Lack of Clarity:** The paper's greatest weakness is its poor presentation, which makes the work exceptionally difficult to understand. The argumentation is often confusing, and key claims are not sufficiently explained or justified.
    *   The central motivation rests on the claim that previous random projection methods "cannot be adjusted and transformed using weighting techniques such as PPMI" (Line 114) because E(R)=0. This crucial point is stated without immediate justification. The explanation that column/row sums become zero appears much later (Line 370-373) and is itself not clearly proven. It is not obvious why the sums in a finite RI-built matrix would be exactly zero, rather than just having an expectation of zero.
    *   The paper is not self-contained. It assumes a deep familiarity with random projection theory, making it inaccessible to readers who are not experts in this specific subfield. The rationale for many design choices (e.g., the specific distribution in Eq. 2, the use of Kendall's τb) is not developed from first principles.
    *   Many arguments are framed as unsupported assertions. For instance, "Since the method is based on random projections, models can be built incrementally and efficiently" (Line 137) is a leap in logic that merges two distinct properties without explanation. Similarly, the claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" instead of "hours, days or months" (Line 154) is a dramatic statement that requires more direct empirical evidence (e.g., a table of run times).

2.  **Insufficient and Narrow Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is not broad enough to support the paper's strong claims of competitiveness.
    *   Performance is only demonstrated on a single dataset, MEN, which measures semantic relatedness. It is well-known that model performance can vary drastically between tasks measuring relatedness versus those measuring similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) or other semantic phenomena (e.g., analogies).
    *   Without evaluation on a wider suite of standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, TOEFL synonyms), it is impossible to assess the general effectiveness of the method or to properly contextualize its performance relative to other models. The authors should either include these benchmarks or provide a strong justification for their exclusion.

3.  **Numerous Language and Formatting Errors:** The paper is marred by frequent English language errors that significantly impede readability. Examples include "To a large extend" (Line 86), "competitive results concerning accuracy" (Line 140), and "such PPMI" (Line 142). These are symptomatic of a broader need for careful proofreading. The accumulation of such errors makes the already dense technical arguments even harder to parse and detracts from the paper's professionalism.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the critical claim that in RI-constructed models, "the sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0" (Line 370-373)? Is this strictly true for any finite corpus, or an asymptotic property? Why does E(R)=0 necessarily lead to this outcome, and how does this mathematically prevent the application of Equation 3?

2.  The paper admits a lack of theoretical grounding (Section 2.3). However, could you provide more intuition or a mathematical sketch for why a projection with E(R)>0 preserves the distributional information needed for PPMI to be effective, while a projection with E(R)=0 allegedly destroys it?

3.  Given the paper's strong claims of achieving state-of-the-art performance, why was the evaluation restricted to the MEN relatedness task? How does PoP+PPMI perform on standard semantic *similarity* benchmarks like SimLex-999 or the combined WordSim-353 dataset? These would provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities.
