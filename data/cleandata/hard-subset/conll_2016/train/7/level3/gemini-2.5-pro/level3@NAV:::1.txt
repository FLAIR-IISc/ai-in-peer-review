This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel random projection method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. The core idea is to use a projection matrix with only positive values, which allows the resulting vectors to retain distributional properties of the original count space. This enables subsequent weighting with methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to be applied efficiently in the low-dimensional space, a step not possible with standard random indexing. The primary contribution is a method that combines the high computational efficiency of random projections with the performance benefits of PPMI weighting. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this hybrid approach can achieve competitive results on a semantic relatedness task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The most significant strength of the proposed method is its training speed. By applying dimensionality reduction first via an efficient random projection, the computationally expensive PPMI transformation is performed on small vectors. The authors claim this reduces model construction time from hours or days (for neural networks) to minutes or seconds, which is a substantial practical advantage for researchers with limited computational resources.

2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The paper presents a clever way to bridge the gap between random projection methods and traditional count-and-weight models. The insight that a positive-only projection matrix (E(R) > 0) preserves the applicability of weighting schemes like PPMI is novel and addresses a key limitation of standard random indexing, where E(R) = 0 makes PPMI mathematically undefined.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's most critical flaw is the lack of direct, quantitative comparison to established state-of-the-art methods. The authors claim their results are "comparable" to neural embeddings but do not provide a results table comparing their scores on the MEN dataset against standard models like word2vec, GloVe, or even a strong SVD-on-PPMI baseline. This omission makes it impossible to properly situate the work and verify its claimed competitiveness.

2.  **Methodological Justification and Consistency:** The paper's choice of similarity metrics is not well-justified. The definition provided for Kendall’s tau appears non-standard and is not explained. Furthermore, there is an inconsistency in using Spearman's rank correlation for the final evaluation while opting for Kendall's tau and Pearson's correlation internally. The authors should justify why Spearman, a standard non-parametric measure for this type of evaluation, was not also used for similarity calculations within their model.

3.  **Limited Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single dataset (MEN). To make a convincing case for the method's general utility, it should be benchmarked on a broader suite of standard tasks, including semantic similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) and analogy tasks, to provide a more comprehensive performance profile.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a table that directly compares your best PoP+PPMI results on the MEN dataset with scores from widely-used models like word2vec (CBOW/Skip-gram), GloVe, and a PPMI+SVD baseline, citing the papers from which these scores are taken?

2.  Can you please clarify the motivation for using your specific implementation of Kendall’s tau? Why was this non-standard approach preferred over Spearman's correlation, especially given that Spearman's is used as the final evaluation metric?
