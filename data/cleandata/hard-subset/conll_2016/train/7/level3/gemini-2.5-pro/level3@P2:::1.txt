### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings based on random projections. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix where the expected value of its elements is positive (E(R) > 0), in contrast to standard random indexing (RI) techniques where E(R) = 0. The main advantage of this approach is that the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain distributional properties that allow for subsequent weighting with techniques like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is not possible with standard RI. The authors show that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors, and then using Pearson correlation for similarity, achieves strong performance on the MEN semantic relatedness benchmark, comparable to state-of-the-art methods but with significantly lower computational cost.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables post-hoc weighting.** The key insight is the use of a positive-only projection matrix, which creates low-dimensional embeddings that, unlike those from traditional RI, are compatible with count-based weighting schemes like PPMI. This successfully bridges the efficiency of random projections with the effectiveness of PPMI.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of a highly efficient and effective embedding pipeline.** The paper shows that the PoP+PPMI combination is extremely fast—building models in seconds or minutes—while achieving competitive results on a standard relatedness task. This presents a compelling practical alternative to computationally intensive neural network training or SVD on massive matrices.

### Strengths

1.  **High Efficiency and Scalability:** The paper's strongest argument is the method's computational efficiency. By performing dimensionality reduction first via an incremental random projection and then applying the more expensive PPMI weighting on the resulting small vectors (e.g., dimension 1000-5000), the method avoids the major bottlenecks of traditional count-based models (SVD/PPMI on matrices with millions of columns) and predictive models (lengthy neural network training). The claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" is a significant practical contribution.
2.  **Novel Methodological Approach:** The idea of designing a random projection scheme specifically to preserve the applicability of subsequent weighting functions is clever and well-motivated. Standard random indexing focuses on preserving L2 distances, which unfortunately nullifies the sums required for PPMI calculation. The PoP method's use of a matrix with E(R) > 0 is a simple but effective modification that directly addresses this limitation, representing a valuable contribution to the family of random projection algorithms for NLP.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on the Chosen Benchmark:** The reported performance of 0.75 Spearman's ρ on the MEN dataset is a strong result and is indeed competitive with many published models. The paper includes a thorough ablation study, investigating the effects of dimensionality (Figure 4) and the number of non-zero elements (Figure 5), which lends credibility to the results and provides useful guidance for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art Baselines:** This is a major weakness. The paper claims its results are "comparable to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" but provides no direct quantitative comparisons. The primary baselines in 2016 for this task would be PPMI+SVD, SGNS (word2vec), and GloVe. To be convincing, the authors must compare their method against these models trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus. Without these baseline scores, the claim of competitiveness is unsubstantiated, and it is impossible to gauge the method's true standing.
2.  **Unconventional and Poorly Justified Similarity Metric:** The choice of Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure between vectors (Section 2.2) is highly non-standard and its implementation is unclear. Vector similarity is typically computed with measures like cosine or Euclidean distance. The paper's justification for τb is that the projected space is non-Gaussian, but the description of its calculation—involving pairs of observations `(xj, yj)` and `(xj+1, yj+1)`—is ambiguous for comparing two vectors. This raises questions about what is actually being measured. This lack of clarity undermines the results of the unweighted PoP model.
3.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The entire empirical evaluation rests on a single dataset, MEN. While MEN is a standard benchmark for relatedness, robust evaluation of word embeddings requires testing on a wider suite of tasks that measure different aspects of semantic representation. This includes word similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset). Relying on one dataset makes it difficult to assess the general quality and utility of the produced embeddings.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** In Figure 1, the text states that the performance of RI and the count-based model are "almost identical," but the figure is hard to interpret as it appears to show only two lines instead of the three methods discussed. A clearer legend or explicit note in the caption would improve readability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The use of Kendall’s τb as a vector similarity metric is unconventional. Could you please clarify precisely how the pairs of observations for calculating concordant/discordant pairs are formed from two input vectors, `~x` and `~y`? Why was this chosen over more standard non-parametric similarity measures or other rank correlations like Spearman's ρ?
2.  Your claim of achieving competitive performance is central to the paper, but you do not report results for standard baselines like PPMI+SVD, word2vec (SGNS), or GloVe trained on the same UKWaC corpus. Can you provide these results to properly contextualize your method's performance?
3.  You use Spearman's ρ for the final evaluation, which is standard for the task. Given this, have you experimented with using Spearman's ρ instead of Kendall's τb as the internal similarity function for the base PoP models? If so, how did it perform?
