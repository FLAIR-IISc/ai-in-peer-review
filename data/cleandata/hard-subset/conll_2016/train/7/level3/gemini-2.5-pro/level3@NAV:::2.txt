**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing word embeddings. The method's key feature is the use of a projection matrix with a positive expected value, unlike previous methods like Random Indexing where the expectation is zero. The authors claim this property allows for the application of weighting schemes such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly on the low-dimensional embeddings, which is mathematically impossible in zero-centered projected spaces. The paper presents this as a computationally efficient approach that achieves competitive performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel concept of using a random projection matrix with a positive expectation. This design choice is intended to make the resulting low-dimensional space compatible with established and effective weighting schemes like PPMI, bridging a gap between efficient projection methods and count-based models.
2.  The paper provides a preliminary empirical result showing that the PoP method, when combined with PPMI, can achieve strong performance on a standard semantic task.

**Strengths**
1.  The central idea of creating a PPMI-compatible random projection is conceptually novel and interesting. It addresses a well-known limitation of traditional random projection methods and offers a potentially powerful way to combine their scalability with the empirical strengths of PPMI weighting.
2.  If the authors' claims are validated, the method promises significant computational efficiency. Performing PPMI calculations on low-dimensional vectors is vastly faster than on the massive, sparse co-occurrence matrices typically used in distributional semantics, offering a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Severe Lack of Clarity:** The paper’s greatest weakness is its poor presentation. The manuscript is exceptionally difficult to follow, with unclear arguments from the introduction onward. It fails to adequately explain how PoP differs from related methods or provide a clear, self-contained rationale for its design choices, making the work inaccessible to non-experts in random projections.
2.  **Unsupported Assertions:** The text contains numerous claims that are either poorly framed or lack sufficient justification. For example, the logical flow is weak at several key points (e.g., Line 114 "in addition," Line 137 "Since," Line 154 "thus"), giving the impression of unsubstantiated leaps in reasoning.
3.  **Insufficient Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is too narrow. The method's performance is demonstrated on only the MEN dataset. A convincing validation requires benchmarking against a wider suite of standard tasks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) or a strong justification for the single-dataset focus.
4.  **Numerous Language Errors:** The paper is marred by frequent grammatical and stylistic errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on Line 86). These issues significantly impede readability and detract from the paper's professional quality, making the already-unclear technical details even harder to comprehend.

The paper, in its current state, is not ready for publication. It requires a substantial revision to fundamentally improve its clarity, strengthen its arguments, and broaden its empirical evaluation.
