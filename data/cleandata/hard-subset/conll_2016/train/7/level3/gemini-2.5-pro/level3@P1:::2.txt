This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing word embeddings. The main idea is to use a random projection matrix R where the expected value is positive (E(R) > 0), unlike traditional methods like Random Indexing (RI) where E(R) = 0. The authors claim that this property allows for the application of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) *after* the dimensionality reduction step. This results in a method that is computationally efficient, as it operates on low-dimensional vectors, but also achieves high performance on semantic similarity tasks, which is characteristic of PPMI-based methods. The method is evaluated on the MEN relatedness dataset, where it is shown to achieve competitive results compared to state-of-the-art models.

### Contributions

1.  **A Random Projection Method Amenable to PPMI Weighting:** The primary contribution is the proposal of Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random projection method that uses a non-zero-mean projection matrix. This design choice enables the post-hoc application of weighting functions like PPMI to the low-dimensional embeddings, which is not possible with standard random indexing methods whose vector components sum to zero. This potentially bridges the gap between efficient but less discriminative projection methods and effective but computationally expensive count-and-weight methods.

2.  **Empirical Demonstration of an Efficient and Effective Combination:** A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors, combined with an appropriate similarity measure (Pearson's r), can achieve strong performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task. The paper shows this combination is competitive with more resource-intensive methods, while retaining the efficiency benefits of an incremental, projection-based approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Intriguing Core Idea:** The central concept of designing a random projection scheme that is compatible with subsequent PPMI weighting is clever. Traditional random indexing methods preserve distances but lose the raw count-like structure needed for PPMI. The proposed PoP method offers a plausible solution to this problem, combining the scalability and efficiency of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI for creating discriminative semantic spaces.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** A significant strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. By performing dimensionality reduction first and then applying PPMI on the much smaller vectors (e.g., dimension 1000-5000), the approach avoids the computationally expensive and memory-intensive step of calculating PPMI on a very high-dimensional, sparse co-occurrence matrix. This could make it a practical and scalable choice for building word embeddings from extremely large corpora.

3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The performance on the MEN dataset is promising. Achieving a Spearman's ρ of around 0.75-0.78 is a strong and competitive result. This demonstrates that the proposed pipeline (PoP projection, followed by PPMI weighting and Pearson correlation) can indeed produce high-quality semantic representations, at least for the task of semantic relatedness.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Poor Presentation:** The paper's greatest weakness is its overall lack of clarity. The core arguments are difficult to follow, making it hard to rigorously assess the novelty and correctness of the proposed method. For instance, it is not made sufficiently clear how PoP fundamentally differs from or improves upon the family of random projection methods beyond the E(R) > 0 constraint. The logical flow is often weak, with key claims being introduced by connecting phrases that are not well-supported by the preceding text. For example:
    *   Line 114: "In addition, since these methods use... their resulting vectors cannot be adjusted and transformed using weighting techniques such as PPMI." This conclusion is presented as a direct consequence of E(R)=0 without sufficient, clear justification for a reader not already an expert in the area.
    *   Line 137: "Since the method is based on random projections, models can be built incrementally and efficiently." This is a property of all random projection methods, not a unique consequence of the PoP design, making the "Since" connector misleading.
    *   Line 154: "Thus, instead of hours, days or months, models can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes." This comes across as an overstatement rather than a carefully benchmarked claim.
    The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with random projections, making it not self-contained and inaccessible to a broader NLP audience.

2.  **Insufficiently Broad Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is too narrow to convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. Performance is assessed on a single semantic relatedness task (MEN). State-of-the-art evaluation of word embeddings requires testing on a wider range of standard benchmarks, which typically include both relatedness (e.g., WordSim-353) and semantic similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) tasks, as well as downstream tasks like analogy questions. Relying on a single dataset makes it impossible to know if the model's performance is robust and generalizable or specific to the characteristics of the MEN test set. The authors should either include more benchmarks or provide a strong justification for their choice of a single dataset.

3.  **Numerous Language Errors:** The manuscript suffers from frequent grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that significantly impede readability and detract from the paper's professionalism. Examples include "To a large extend" (line 86), "such PPMI" (line 142), and generally unconventional sentence structures throughout. The paper would benefit immensely from a thorough round of proofreading and editing by a native English speaker.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the key theoretical difference between PoP and other sparse random projection methods like RI? Beyond the fact that E(R) > 0, what specific properties of the projection allow it to preserve the necessary information for PPMI to be effective, while standard RI does not?

2.  The justification for using Kendall's τb for the base PoP vectors and Pearson's r for the PPMI-weighted vectors is based on the presumed distribution of the vectors (non-Gaussian vs. Gaussian-like, as shown in Figure 3). Have you empirically verified these distributional assumptions across the vocabulary using formal normality tests? How sensitive are the results to the choice of similarity metric in each case?

3.  Why was the evaluation limited to the MEN dataset? Do you have any preliminary results on other standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, or the Google analogy task) that you could share during the author response period to demonstrate the generalizability of your approach?
