### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing word embeddings based on random projections. The key distinction from prior random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI) is that the projection matrix `R` in PoP has a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), whereas in RI, E(R) = 0. The authors claim this property allows for the application of weighting schemes, such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), directly to the low-dimensional projected vectors. This is not possible with traditional RI-generated vectors. The proposed pipeline involves creating low-dimensional embeddings via PoP and then applying PPMI, yielding a method that is computationally efficient while achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art models on the MEN semantic relatedness task.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables post-projection weighting.** The primary contribution is the method itself. By designing a projection matrix with only non-negative values, the authors create a low-dimensional space that preserves properties of the original count space, most notably the non-negativity required for applying PPMI. This cleverly circumvents a major limitation of traditional random projection methods.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of an efficient and high-performing embedding pipeline.** The paper shows that combining the highly efficient PoP dimensionality reduction with a subsequent PPMI transformation on the small vectors results in a model that is competitive with computationally expensive alternatives.

### Strengths

1.  **Clever and Well-Motivated Method.** The core idea of designing a random projection scheme specifically to enable subsequent weighting is novel and addresses a known limitation of methods like RI. Standard RI is efficient but does not improve the quality of the underlying count space; it merely approximates it. By enabling the application of a powerful weighting scheme like PPMI *after* dimensionality reduction, the authors propose a potentially powerful bridge between the efficiency of random projections and the high performance of weighted count models.
2.  **Significant Potential for Computational Efficiency.** The primary practical advantage of this method is its speed. Applying PPMI to a traditional high-dimensional, sparse co-occurrence matrix is computationally demanding. By performing this calculation on a dense, low-dimensional matrix (e.g., a few thousand dimensions), the proposed method could offer orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over both traditional PPMI+SVD pipelines and resource-intensive neural network training, without a significant drop in quality. This makes it an attractive option for large-scale or resource-constrained applications.
3.  **Strong Preliminary Results.** Despite the limited scope of the evaluation, the performance reported on the MEN relatedness test (Spearman's ρ of 0.75, with potential for 0.78) is highly competitive. These scores are comparable to those achieved by well-established and more complex models, suggesting that the proposed PoP+PPMI pipeline is a viable and powerful approach for capturing semantic relatedness.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Severe Lack of Clarity and Poor Presentation.** The paper's greatest weakness is that it is exceptionally difficult to read and understand. The arguments are often disjointed and the underlying logic is not clearly explained, making it inaccessible to readers not already deeply familiar with the nuances of random projection literature.
    *   The distinction from prior work is not established clearly enough. While the E(R)>0 property is stated, its full implications and how it fundamentally alters the projection are not well-explained.
    *   The writing contains logical leaps that are hard to follow. For example, the claim in the introduction (lines 114-117) that RI vectors "cannot be adjusted and transformed using weighting techniques such as PPMI" because E(R)=0 is asserted rather than explained. This crucial point is only vaguely revisited later with a brief mention of row/column sums being zero, which is insufficient.
    .
2.  **Insufficient and Narrow Empirical Evaluation.** The paper's claims of achieving "state-of-the-art" performance are based entirely on a single dataset, MEN. While MEN is a standard benchmark for semantic relatedness, robust evaluation requires a much broader suite of tests. The authors should have included performance on tasks that measure semantic similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and possibly analogy tasks to provide a more comprehensive picture of the embeddings' properties. Without this, it is impossible to know if the method's high performance is specific to the relatedness phenomenon captured by MEN or if it produces generally useful word representations.
3.  **Numerous Language and Formatting Errors.** The paper suffers from frequent grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that severely impede readability and give it an unpolished feel. Examples include "To a large extend" (line 86), "such PPMI" (line 142), and generally convoluted sentence structures. These issues, combined with the overall lack of clarity, make it a frustrating experience for the reader and obscure the paper's technical contributions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The central claim is that PPMI cannot be applied to RI-generated vectors because their column and row sums are zero. Could you provide a more detailed and intuitive explanation for why this is the case in an RI space? Is it a direct consequence of using a zero-centered projection matrix (e.g., with values {-1, 0, 1})?
2.  You motivate the use of Kendall's τb by the non-Gaussian nature of the PoP-projected space. However, the original count space is also highly non-Gaussian (long-tailed), yet cosine similarity is a standard and effective metric there. Could you further justify why the PoP projection specifically necessitates a shift to a rank-based correlation metric, rather than continuing to use a geometric measure like cosine?
3.  Figure 4 shows that for the unweighted PoP model, performance *decreases* as dimensionality increases beyond 2000. You suggest this might be due to using too few non-zero elements, but this seems counter-intuitive, as higher dimensionality should generally reduce projection errors. Could you offer a more concrete hypothesis for this performance degradation? Does it suggest an instability in the PoP method under certain parameterizations?
