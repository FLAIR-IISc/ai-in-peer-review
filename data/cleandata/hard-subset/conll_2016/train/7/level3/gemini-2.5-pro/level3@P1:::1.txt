This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection method for creating word embeddings. The core idea is to use a sparse, random projection matrix where all non-zero elements are positive. This contrasts with traditional Random Indexing (RI), which uses projection matrices with an expected value of zero. The authors claim that this "positive-only" property allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be transformed using weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a step that is not possible with standard RI vectors. The method is incremental and computationally efficient. The authors evaluate their method on the MEN semantic relatedness dataset, showing that PoP combined with PPMI and a Pearson correlation similarity measure achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art systems, but with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, a novel random projection technique that uses a positive-only projection matrix. This design choice uniquely allows for the application of count-based weighting schemes like PPMI *after* dimensionality reduction, effectively combining the speed of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this efficient, incremental method can achieve competitive performance on a semantic relatedness task without requiring the extensive training time of neural network models or the heavy computational cost of SVD on large, dense matrices.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The most significant strength of the proposed method is its efficiency. By leveraging random projections, the model avoids the time-consuming training required for neural embedding models and the expensive matrix factorization (e.g., SVD) of high-dimensional co-occurrence matrices. The claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" is a substantial practical advantage, making the method highly scalable and accessible.
2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The central idea of designing a random projection to be compatible with subsequent PPMI weighting is novel and clever. Traditional random indexing aims to preserve Euclidean distance in a lower-dimensional space, but in doing so, it produces vectors with zero-centered distributions, rendering PPMI inapplicable. PoP circumvents this limitation by preserving the non-negative, count-like nature of the data in the projected space, thereby bridging the gap between efficient dimensionality reduction and powerful statistical weighting.
3.  **Thorough Internal Analysis:** The paper provides a good analysis of the model's parameters and behavior. Figures 4 and 5 systematically explore the effects of vector dimensionality (m) and the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors. This investigation offers valuable insights into the trade-offs within the model and provides guidance for practitioners on how to tune these parameters for different use cases (e.g., with or without a subsequent PPMI transformation).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** This is a major weakness. The paper claims its performance is "comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" but fails to provide direct, controlled comparisons. The baseline "PMI-Dense+Cos" is presented, but it is not clear if this is a strong, SVD-tuned baseline. For the claims to be substantiated, the paper must include comparisons to standard, widely-used models like SGNS (word2vec) and GloVe, as well as the canonical PPMI+SVD baseline, all trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus with identical preprocessing. Without these direct comparisons, the performance claims remain largely anecdotal.
2.  **A Seemingly Non-Standard Definition of Kendall's Tau:** The definition provided for computing Kendall's τb in Section 2.2 appears highly unconventional. The paper defines concordant/discordant pairs by comparing adjacent elements `(xj, yj)` and `(xj+1, yj+1)`. Standard implementations of Kendall's tau compare all possible pairs of observations `(xi, yi)` and `(xj, yj)` where `i != j`. The described method would measure a form of local, sequential correlation rather than the global rank correlation Kendall's tau is intended to capture. This is a critical methodological issue that could invalidate the results reported using τb and undermines the justification for its use.
3.  **Limited and Unclear Motivation/Evaluation:** The motivation for why PPMI-compatibility is a crucial feature is not fully developed. While it enables a powerful weighting scheme, the paper does not argue why this is superior to other fast embedding methods that have their own implicit weighting (e.g., the negative sampling in SGNS). Furthermore, the evaluation is confined to a single dataset, MEN. To make a convincing case for a general-purpose word embedding method, evaluation should be conducted on a suite of standard benchmarks that test for different aspects of semantic representation, such as semantic similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) and analogy tasks, in addition to relatedness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the definition of Kendall’s τb used in Section 2.2? The comparison of adjacent pairs `(xj, yj)` and `(xj+1, yj+1)` is non-standard. Is this a typo in the description, or is it an intentional methodological choice? If it is intentional, please provide justification for this novel variant of the measure.
2.  To substantiate the performance claims, could you provide results comparing PoP+PPMI directly against strong, standard baselines (specifically SGNS, GloVe, and PPMI+SVD) trained on the same corpus and with the same vocabulary and preprocessing?
3.  The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN relatedness task. Do you have results on other standard semantic evaluation tasks, such as SimLex-999 (similarity) or the Google Analogy dataset, which would provide a more comprehensive picture of the model's capabilities?
4.  In Figure 1, the text states that the performance of the RI and count-based models are "almost identical," but only two lines are clearly visible. Are the lines for these two models perfectly overlapping? If so, it would improve clarity to state this in the caption or use different markers.
