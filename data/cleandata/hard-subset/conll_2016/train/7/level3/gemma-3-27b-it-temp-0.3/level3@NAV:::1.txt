## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections with positive values. The core idea – using a projection matrix with a non-zero expected value – allows for the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* projection, a feature absent in traditional random projection methods. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving scores comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, and highlight the method’s speed and efficiency.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the exploration of a different random projection approach that *enables* post-projection weighting. While random projections are well-established, the specific constraint of positivity to facilitate subsequent PPMI application is a novel angle. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this approach can achieve competitive results with significantly reduced computational cost compared to neural methods. Finally, the detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity (dimensionality, sparsity) provides valuable insight into the method’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

*   **Efficiency:** The reported speed of model construction is a significant advantage, particularly when compared to the training time of neural networks. This is a compelling argument for the method's practicality.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** The use of a positive-only projection matrix to enable PPMI weighting is a unique and potentially valuable contribution to the field of semantic space construction.
*   **Detailed Parameter Analysis:** The thorough investigation of the impact of dimensionality and sparsity on performance demonstrates a good understanding of the method's behavior and provides practical guidance for implementation.
*   **Clear Motivation (eventually):** While initially unclear, the paper builds a reasonable case for why enabling PPMI weighting after projection is beneficial, particularly in the context of computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Comparison to Related Work:** A major concern is the absence of a direct comparison to existing dimensionality reduction techniques *combined* with PPMI. The authors compare to RI, but not to, for example, SVD or PCA followed by PPMI weighting. This makes it difficult to assess the true benefit of PoP.
*   **Non-Standard Kendall's Tau:** The use of a non-standard definition of Kendall's tau is unexplained and potentially confusing. The authors need to clearly justify their choice and explain how it differs from the standard definition.
*   **Inconsistency in Correlation Measures:** The authors acknowledge the standard use of Spearman correlation in semantic tasks but don’t adequately justify why it wasn’t used consistently, especially given its use during evaluation.
*   **Evaluation Dataset Choice:** The reliance on non-standard datasets is problematic. Inclusion of standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) is crucial for establishing the method's generalizability.
*   **Figure 1 Issue:** The missing line in Figure 1 hinders understanding of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI to other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., SVD, PCA) *followed* by PPMI weighting, in terms of both performance and computational cost?
2.  Please clarify the specific definition of Kendall’s tau used and justify its choice over the standard definition.
3.  Why was Spearman correlation not used consistently throughout the evaluation, despite its common use in semantic tasks?
4.  Will you include standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (WordSim353, SimLex-999) in a revised version of the paper?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The typo "large extend" should be corrected to "large extent."
*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused introduction, clearly stating the problem and the proposed solution.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution. However, addressing the weaknesses, particularly the lack of comparison to related work and the issues with evaluation and methodology, is crucial for strengthening the paper and increasing its impact.
