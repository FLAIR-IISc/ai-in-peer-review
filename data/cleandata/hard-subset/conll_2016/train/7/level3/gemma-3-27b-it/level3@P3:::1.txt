## Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:** This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings based on random projections with positive values. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix with a non-zero expected value, allowing for the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* projection, a capability lacking in standard random projection methods. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, but with significantly improved computational efficiency.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling post-projection weighting:** While random projections are not new, the modification to enforce positive values in the projection matrix to allow for subsequent weighting with methods like PPMI is a potentially useful contribution. This addresses a limitation of existing methods.
2. **Empirical demonstration of PPMI effectiveness on PoP-constructed spaces:** The paper shows, through experimentation, that PPMI weighting demonstrably improves the performance of PoP embeddings. This supports the core hypothesis of the method.
3. **Initial exploration of parameter space and impact of dimensionality:** The work includes a preliminary, though reasonably thorough, investigation into the influence of parameters like vector dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements in the projection matrix.

**Strengths:**

1. **Computational Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is the potential for significant computational savings. The authors convincingly argue that PoP, due to its incremental nature and low-dimensional projections, can be substantially faster than training neural networks or performing PPMI weighting directly on large count matrices. This is a valuable benefit, especially for resource-constrained environments or rapid prototyping.
2. **Preservation of Distributional Properties:**  The paper correctly identifies the issue of heavy-tailed distributions in count-based models and positions PoP as a potential solution. The ability to apply PPMI *after* projection suggests a way to address the non-discriminative nature of raw counts without the computational burden of full matrix factorization.
3. **Clear Motivation (Despite Concerns – see below):** The authors clearly articulate the problem they are trying to solve – the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy in semantic space construction – and position PoP as a potential compromise.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation (within the scope of the paper):** The authors present a fairly thorough evaluation using several parameter settings and variations of their method, demonstrating the impact of dimensionality and PPMI weighting. The inclusion of error bars in their results adds credibility.
5. **Detailed Methodological Description:** The method is described with sufficient detail that it could be reproduced, including the specific distribution used for generating the projection matrix.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to Related Work:** This is the most significant weakness. The paper fails to adequately position PoP within the broader landscape of dimensionality reduction and semantic space construction techniques.  There’s minimal discussion of how PoP compares to other methods like Locally Linear Embedding (LLE), t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE), or even more recent work in non-negative matrix factorization. The claim of "comparable to state-of-the-art" feels unsupported without a direct comparison on standard benchmarks.
2. **Unclear Motivation for PPMI Transformability:** The emphasis on the *ability* to apply PPMI, while technically correct, feels somewhat circular. The authors don’t sufficiently explain *why* this transformability is inherently valuable beyond the observed performance gains. Is it a fundamental property, or just a convenient side effect of the chosen projection?
3. **Non-Standard Kendall’s Tau Definition:** The paper mentions a non-standard definition of Kendall's tau (τb) without sufficient explanation.  Readers unfamiliar with this specific variation will be left wondering about its impact on the results and whether it introduces biases.
4. **Inconsistent Correlation Choice:**  The authors acknowledge using Spearman’s correlation for evaluation but don’t justify why they didn’t use it consistently throughout, particularly given its common usage in semantic relatedness tasks and their reported use of Pearson’s correlation *after* the PPMI transformation. The rationale for choosing Kendall’s τb initially is also weak.
5. **Limited Dataset Choice:** The reliance on non-standard datasets is a concern. While the authors justify their choices, including standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) would strengthen the evaluation and allow for more direct comparison with existing methods.
6. **Figure 1 Issue:** The missing line in Figure 1 is a minor but noticeable issue. It hinders complete interpretation of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of PoP to other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., LLE, t-SNE, NMF), focusing on computational cost and performance?
2.  What is the rationale for using the specific, non-standard definition of Kendall’s τb? How does it differ from the standard definition, and how might this affect the results?
3.  Why was Spearman's correlation used for initial evaluation but not maintained throughout the analysis?
4.  Why was the choice made to not use standard semantic relatedness benchmark datasets (e.g. WordSim353, SimLex-999)?
5.  Can you elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of PoP? Are there any theoretical bounds on the error introduced by the random projection?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The typo "large extend" should be corrected to "large extent."
*   The formatting of Equation 3 could be improved for readability.
*   The supplemental material statement is appreciated. Sharing code and embeddings is crucial for reproducibility.
