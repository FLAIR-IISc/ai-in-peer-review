This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings through random projections. The core idea is to utilize a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, differing from traditional methods employing matrices with an expected value of zero. This allows for the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) post-projection, enabling efficient transfer to spaces discriminative for semantic similarity. The authors claim competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, approaching state-of-the-art results while offering significant computational efficiency gains.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the exploration of non-standard random projections – specifically, those with a positive expected value – and demonstrating their compatibility with post-hoc weighting techniques like PPMI. While random projections are well-established, the ability to subsequently apply weighting methods is a novel aspect, potentially opening avenues for faster and more scalable semantic space construction.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of PoP’s performance on the MEN dataset, showing comparability to more computationally intensive methods.  Finally, the paper’s consideration of the interplay between dimensionality, sparsity, and weighting techniques provides a useful investigation into the parameter space of this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly argues for the efficiency of PoP, stemming from its reliance on random projections and the reduced dimensionality of the resulting vectors. This is a significant advantage, particularly in scenarios where training complex neural models is prohibitive. The potential to achieve competitive results in a fraction of the time represents a valuable contribution.
2. **Novelty in Random Projection Application:** The core idea of using random projections with a positive expected value to enable post-hoc weighting is a notable distinction from existing methods like RI, RRI, and ISA.  This opens up a new design space for random projection-based embedding methods.
3. **Exploration of Parameter Space:** The paper delves into the impact of parameters like dimensionality (m) and the proportion of non-zero elements (s) on performance.  This exploration, along with the discussion of the trade-offs involved, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to method development and provides useful insights for practitioners.
4. **Consideration of Distributional Properties:** The authors correctly identify the problem of heavy-tailed distributions in count-based models and the need for transformations to improve discriminative power.  The connection made between PPMI, Gaussian distributions, and the choice of similarity measure (Pearson’s r vs. Kendall’s τb) demonstrates a solid understanding of the underlying principles.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Comparison:** A major weakness is the limited comparison to established baselines. While the paper mentions neural embeddings and SVD-based approaches, it lacks a direct, head-to-head comparison against widely used pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe, fastText) on standard semantic similarity benchmarks. Demonstrating superiority or highlighting specific trade-offs relative to these benchmarks is crucial for establishing the method’s value.
2. **Unclear Motivation for PPMI Compatibility:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes the importance of PPMI compatibility but doesn't sufficiently justify *why* this is a key advantage. The authors need to better articulate the specific benefits that PPMI weighting provides in this context and why it cannot be achieved with alternative approaches. Is PPMI absolutely essential, or simply a convenient option?
3. **Non-Standard Kendall’s τb Implementation & Justification:** The use of a specific implementation of Kendall’s τb (Knight, 1966) is presented without sufficient justification. The paper needs to explain why this particular implementation was chosen and acknowledge any potential differences in results compared to other implementations or standard correlation measures like Spearman’s rho. The authors also introduce Spearman’s rho at evaluation time, contradicting their earlier reasoning.
4. **Evaluation Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on the MEN dataset is insufficient. While a valid test case, it’s not a standard benchmark for evaluating semantic similarity. Inclusion of widely used benchmarks like SimLex-999, WordSim-353, or RG-65 would significantly strengthen the evaluation and allow for fairer comparison with other methods.
5. **Figure 1 Clarity:** Figure 1 is difficult to interpret. Only two lines are clearly visible, and the third line appears truncated or obscured, making it impossible to assess the performance of all three methods across the different context window sizes.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI with established pre-trained word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, fastText) on standard semantic similarity benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RG-65)?
2.  Please elaborate on the specific advantages of PPMI weighting in the context of PoP. Why is PPMI essential, and what limitations would exist if alternative weighting schemes were used?
3.  Why was the Knight (1966) implementation of Kendall’s τb chosen? How does its performance compare to other implementations or Spearman's rank correlation, especially considering you eventually used Spearman’s for evaluation?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of PoP to the choice of α (currently fixed at 0.5)? Have you experimented with different values of α, and if so, what were the results?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical basis for the observed performance improvements with increasing dimensionality (m)?
