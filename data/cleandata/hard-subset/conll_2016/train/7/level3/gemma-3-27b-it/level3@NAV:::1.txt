## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections with positive values. The core idea is to enable the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* projection, something precluded by traditional random projection methods that rely on zero-mean matrices. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive results on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, but with significantly faster training times.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in identifying the constraint imposed by zero-mean random projections and proposing a simple yet effective modification – using positive-only projections – to overcome it. This unlocks the ability to apply post-projection weighting, a potentially valuable addition to the toolkit of dimensionality reduction techniques for NLP. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration that this approach yields results competitive with existing methods while offering a significant speed advantage.

**Strengths:**

*   **Computational Efficiency:** The method’s reported speed is a significant advantage, particularly given the computational cost associated with training neural embeddings. This makes it a potentially attractive option for resource-constrained settings or rapid prototyping.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** Addressing the limitation of traditional random projections regarding post-projection weighting is a clever insight. The simplicity of the proposed solution is appealing.
*   **Preservation of Distributional Properties:** The claim that the method preserves distributional properties – allowing for the application of weighting techniques – is a core strength. This distinguishes it from methods that simply reduce dimensionality without consideration for these properties.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation is Unclear:** The paper doesn’t convincingly argue *why* the ability to apply PPMI after projection is inherently valuable. While it leads to good results, the benefit isn’t clearly articulated beyond enabling a specific combination of techniques.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Related Work:** A major oversight is the absence of direct comparison to other fast, non-neural methods for semantic space construction. The authors mention RI and related techniques but don’t offer a head-to-head performance comparison beyond a single figure, leaving the reader unsure of PoP's relative advantages.
*   **Non-Standard Kendall’s Tau:** The use of a non-standard definition of Kendall’s tau is problematic without clear justification. The authors need to explicitly state which variant they are using and why.
*   **Inconsistent Correlation Measure:** The authors use Spearman correlation for evaluation but don’t explain why they didn't use Pearson's correlation, especially after applying PPMI, which aims to produce a Gaussian-like distribution where Pearson’s correlation is more appropriate.
*   **Dataset Choice:** The reliance on non-standard datasets weakens the evaluation. Including standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) would greatly enhance the paper’s impact.
* **Figure 1 Issue:** The missing third line in Figure 1 is a minor but noticeable issue.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more compelling justification for *why* enabling PPMI weighting after projection is a desirable goal beyond simply achieving better results?
2.  Please provide a direct, quantitative comparison of PoP against other fast, non-neural methods for semantic space construction (e.g., variations of RI, SVD-based approaches).
3.  Please clarify the specific definition of Kendall’s tau used and justify its choice over other variants.
4.  Why was Spearman correlation used for evaluation instead of Pearson correlation, given the eventual application of PPMI?
