## Review of “Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction”

This paper proposes Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a random projection method intended to enable the application of weighting schemes like PPMI to otherwise unsuitable projected spaces, claiming computational efficiency. While the core idea has potential, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues and lacks convincing justification for its novelty and advantages. Substantial revision is required before resubmission.

**Contributions (as seen by this reviewer):**

1. **Identification of a limitation in standard random projection:** The paper correctly points out that standard random projection matrices with zero expected value preclude direct application of weighting methods like PPMI. This observation, though likely known to experts, is a reasonable starting point.
2. **Proposal of a simple modification to random projection:** The idea of using a projection matrix with a positive expected value is a straightforward modification to existing techniques. However, the novelty of this contribution is questionable (see Weaknesses).
3. **Empirical demonstration on a single dataset:** The paper presents results on the MEN dataset, showing PoP combined with PPMI achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods. The value of this contribution is limited by the insufficient evaluation (see Weaknesses).

**Strengths:**

1. **Potential for computational efficiency:** If the claims regarding computational speed are accurate, particularly concerning the application of PPMI to low-dimensional PoP vectors, this could be a significant benefit. The authors highlight a key bottleneck in traditional methods—the computational cost of weighting large, sparse count matrices—and suggest a viable solution.
2. **Clear articulation of the problem:** The paper clearly defines the problem of applying weighting techniques to spaces created by standard random projections. The connection between the expected value of the projection matrix and the applicability of PPMI is logically presented.
3. **Focus on incremental construction:** The emphasis on incremental model building is potentially valuable for large-scale data scenarios where full matrix computations are impractical. This is a practical consideration that strengthens the potential appeal of the method.
4. **Discussion of theoretical limitations:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a thorough mathematical justification for PoP and appropriately situate the work within the context of other randomized algorithms with similar limitations. This honesty is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of clarity and organization:** The paper is remarkably difficult to follow. Arguments are poorly structured, and the introduction lacks a clear roadmap. The flow of ideas is frequently disrupted, making it hard to grasp the core contribution and its significance.
2. **Unclear novelty:** The paper fails to adequately differentiate PoP from existing random projection methods. It doesn’t provide a convincing argument that simply enforcing a positive expected value offers a substantial improvement over previous techniques, or why this hasn’t been explored before. A thorough literature review addressing this is crucial.
3. **Unsupported and poorly framed statements:** Several statements lack sufficient justification. For example, Line 114 ("in addition") introduces a point without proper connection to the preceding context. Similarly, Line 137 (“Since”) begins a statement that lacks clear logical grounding. Line 154 (“thus”) presents a conclusion not readily supported by the preceding discussion. These instances highlight a broader issue with the paper's argumentative structure.
4. **Insufficient background and self-containment:** The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with random projections and related concepts. Readers unfamiliar with the field will struggle to understand the motivation and technical details. More detailed explanations and references are needed.
5. **Numerous English language errors:** The paper is marred by grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., Line 86 "To a large extend," Line 142 "such PPMI"). These errors hinder readability and detract from the paper’s credibility.
6. **Limited evaluation:** Evaluating the method solely on the MEN dataset is insufficient. The authors should include results on other standard semantic similarity benchmarks (e.g., WordSim, SimLex) to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach. If a single dataset was intentionally chosen, a strong justification for this choice is required.
7. **Overstated claims:** While the results on MEN are promising, the authors’ claims about achieving “comparable” performance to state-of-the-art neural methods are potentially overstated without a more comprehensive evaluation against a wider range of baselines.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific aspects of PoP differentiate it from existing random projection methods, beyond simply enforcing a positive expected value? Please provide a detailed comparison and contrast.
2. Can you provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis or justification for the choice of α = 0.5 in Equation 2? What is the sensitivity of the method to variations in this parameter?
3. Why was the MEN dataset selected as the sole evaluation benchmark? What are the limitations of using only this dataset, and how might the results differ on other datasets?
4. Could you provide more detailed computational timings comparing PoP+PPMI to other methods (e.g., SVD on weighted count matrices, training neural embeddings) on the same dataset and hardware?
5. What is the impact of the ‘s’ parameter on performance, and how does it relate to the sparsity of the input data?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a clearer visual representation of the PoP process, perhaps a diagram illustrating the random projection and PPMI weighting steps.
* The discussion of Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s correlation is insightful but could be further elaborated, particularly regarding the conditions under which each measure is most appropriate.
* Consider providing code and data to facilitate reproducibility.
