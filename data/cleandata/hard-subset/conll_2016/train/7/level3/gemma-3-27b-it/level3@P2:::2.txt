## Review for CONLL 2016 – Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing word embeddings. The central claim is that by utilizing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, PoP enables the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting embeddings, achieving competitive performance with improved computational efficiency. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the *attempt* to bridge the gap between the efficiency of random projection methods and the discriminative power gained through weighting techniques, specifically PPMI, which are typically incompatible with traditional random projections. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of reasonable performance on the MEN relatedness test.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potential for Efficiency:** The core idea of PoP – constructing embeddings quickly via random projection while retaining the ability to apply PPMI weighting – is potentially valuable. If the claims of computational efficiency hold true (and are convincingly demonstrated), this method could offer a faster alternative to training neural embeddings or applying PPMI directly to high-dimensional count-based models.
2. **Addressing a Known Limitation:** The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of standard random projection methods: their incompatibility with weighting schemes like PPMI due to the zero-mean nature of the projection matrices. Addressing this limitation is a relevant contribution.
3. **Empirical Validation (albeit limited):** The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves a respectable score (0.75) on the MEN relatedness test, placing it in the range of state-of-the-art methods. While not groundbreaking, this suggests the approach has merit.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity and Motivation:** The paper suffers from a significant lack of clarity. The introduction is dense and difficult to follow, making it challenging to understand the precise motivation for PoP and how it differs conceptually from existing random projection techniques (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI). The authors repeatedly *state* that PoP overcomes limitations, but do not adequately *demonstrate* *why* standard random projections suffer from these limitations in a way that a reader unfamiliar with the field can easily grasp.
2. **Insufficient Technical Detail & Justification:** The rationale behind using a projection matrix with a positive expected value is not sufficiently explained. The paper states that this allows for PPMI application, but doesn’t provide a rigorous justification. The choice of α = 0.5 is arbitrary and lacks explanation. Numerous statements feel unsubstantiated, for example: “in addition…” (Line 114), “Since…” (Line 137), “Thus…” (Line 154). These phrases introduce claims without sufficient supporting evidence.
3. **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined solely to the MEN dataset. This is insufficient to assess the generalizability of the proposed method. To strengthen the paper, the authors should include results on other standard semantic similarity benchmarks such as WordSim-353 or SimLex-999. If the authors argue for the suitability of MEN specifically, a clear justification for this choice is needed.
4. **Presentation and Language Quality:** The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, hindering readability. Examples include “To a large extend” (Line 86) and “such PPMI” (Line 142). A thorough proofread is essential. The overall organization is also weak; arguments are presented in a fragmented and difficult-to-follow manner.
5. **Missing Comparison to Baselines:** While the paper compares to RI, a more comprehensive comparison to recent and relevant baselines, including simpler alternatives or recent improvements to existing random projection methods, is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* traditional random projections with E(R) = 0 are incompatible with PPMI weighting, beyond simply stating that the sum of vector elements is zero? A mathematical clarification would be greatly appreciated.
2.  What is the justification for choosing α = 0.5? How sensitive is the performance of PoP to variations in this parameter?
3.  How does the computational cost of PoP compare to other random projection methods and to PPMI weighting applied directly to count-based models, with concrete numbers or time measurements?
4.  Could you elaborate on the "derandomization techniques" mentioned in the discussion and how they might be integrated with PoP?



**Additional Comments:**

The supplemental material statement is appreciated, but the paper should stand on its own merits. The code should be considered a bonus, not a requirement for understanding the core contribution. The numerous references to other work (e.g., Baroni et al., 2014) feel somewhat disconnected; integrating those arguments more directly into the paper’s narrative would improve clarity.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject with major revisions. The core idea behind PoP is interesting, but the paper’s current state is significantly hampered by a lack of clarity, insufficient justification, limited evaluation, and poor presentation. Substantial improvements to the presentation, a more rigorous justification of the method, a broader evaluation, and careful editing are required before resubmission.
