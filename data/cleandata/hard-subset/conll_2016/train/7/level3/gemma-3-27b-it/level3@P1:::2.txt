## Review for CONLL_2016: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing word embeddings. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, purportedly enabling the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) *after* projection, thereby offering computational efficiency and competitive performance. While the potential benefits of such an approach are intriguing, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues and lacks thorough justification for its claims.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the *attempt* to bridge the gap between the efficiency of random projection methods and the improved performance achievable through weighting techniques like PPMI. Previous random projection methods, relying on matrices with an expected value of zero, preclude the direct application of these weighting schemes. PoP proposes a solution by altering the projection matrix’s properties, theoretically allowing post-projection weighting. A secondary, though less significant, contribution is the demonstration of competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test when PoP is combined with PPMI. The empirical evaluation, while limited, provides some initial support for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potential for Efficiency:** The promise of a computationally efficient method for constructing word embeddings is a valuable one. Random projections are inherently scalable, and the claim that PoP maintains this efficiency while allowing for subsequent weighting is a strong argument in its favor.  If verified, this could offer a compelling alternative to resource-intensive neural embedding techniques.
2. **Novelty in Projection Matrix Design:** The core idea of utilizing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value to facilitate post-projection weighting represents a novel approach within the realm of random projection methods. This distinction, if clearly established and supported, differentiates PoP from existing techniques like RI, RRI, and ISA.
3. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving a score of 0.75 on the MEN relatedness test is a reasonable result, placing PoP in the vicinity of state-of-the-art methods. While not a groundbreaking improvement, it demonstrates the viability of the approach and warrants further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is difficult to follow. The introduction meanders through a discussion of distributional semantics without clearly establishing the *specific* problem PoP aims to solve or adequately motivating the proposed solution. The rationale behind using a positive expected value projection matrix is not sufficiently explained. Why does E(R) > 0 enable PPMI? This crucial connection requires more detailed explanation.
2. **Insufficient Differentiation from Existing Work:** The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how PoP differs from existing random projection methods beyond the positive expected value of the projection matrix. The authors assert that previous methods cannot be adjusted with weighting techniques, but this claim needs a more rigorous justification. What specific limitations of RI, RRI, and ISA prevent PPMI application, and how does PoP fundamentally overcome these limitations? Several sentences start abruptly, creating logical gaps (e.g., Line 114 "In addition…", Line 137 "Since…", Line 154 "Thus…").
3. **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely based on the MEN relatedness test. This is insufficient to establish the generalizability of the proposed method.  The authors should include results on other standard semantic similarity benchmarks, such as WordSim353 or SimLex-999, or provide a strong justification for focusing solely on MEN. Without broader evaluation, it is difficult to assess the true effectiveness of PoP.
4. **Poor Writing Quality & Numerous Errors:**  The manuscript contains numerous grammatical and stylistic errors which hinder readability and detract from the overall presentation. Examples include "To a large extend" (Line 86) and "such PPMI" (Line 142). These errors, while potentially minor individually, collectively impede understanding.
5. **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper assumes a level of familiarity with random projections that may not be shared by all readers. It lacks a clear and concise explanation of the underlying principles of random projections and their limitations. This makes it difficult for non-experts to fully appreciate the novelty and potential benefits of PoP.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* using a random projection matrix with E(R) > 0 enables the application of PPMI weighting, while methods with E(R) = 0 do not? A formal justification would be helpful.
2.  How does the choice of 's' (the probability of non-zero elements in the projection matrix) impact the performance of PoP? What guidelines can you provide for selecting an appropriate value for 's' based on the characteristics of the input data?
3.  What is the computational complexity of PoP compared to other random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA) and neural embedding techniques (Word2Vec, GloVe)? Provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for each step of the process.
4.  Could you expand on the theoretical justification for using Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure in the PoP space? Is there a mathematical link between the distribution of values in PoP-constructed vectors and the appropriateness of τb?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplemental material offering code and embeddings would be valuable if the paper is accepted, assuming the code is well-documented and reproducible. The discussion section is quite verbose and could be streamlined to focus on the key takeaways and limitations of the work. Consider adding a table summarizing the differences between PoP and other relevant random projection methods.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea with potential, but it requires substantial revision. The authors need to significantly improve the clarity of their presentation, provide a more thorough justification for their claims, and conduct a more comprehensive evaluation of their method. With significant rewriting and additional experiments, this paper could become a valuable contribution to the field. Currently, I recommend rejection, but encourage resubmission after addressing the concerns outlined above.
