## Review for CONLL 2016: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, allowing for subsequent application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) – a feature reportedly unavailable with standard random projection approaches. The authors demonstrate the method’s performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in the exploration of non-standard random projection matrices (those with E(R) > 0) as a means of retaining the ability to apply count-based weighting schemes *after* dimensionality reduction. While random projection itself is well-established, this specific approach and its consequence—compatibility with PPMI—represent a novel angle. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that even without PPMI weighting, PoP outperforms standard Random Indexing (RI). Finally, the paper offers a detailed exploration of the method’s parameters and their impact on performance, providing valuable insights for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Computational Efficiency:** The core promise of PoP – efficient semantic space construction – is compelling. The authors convincingly argue that the method offers a viable alternative to computationally expensive neural embedding techniques, particularly for resource-constrained scenarios.  The claim of building models in “seconds or minutes” compared to the “hours, days, or months” required for neural methods is a significant potential benefit.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The specific innovation of using a random projection matrix with a positive expected value to enable post-projection weighting is genuinely novel. Existing random projection methods typically preclude this step due to their Gaussian-distributed projections.  Exploring this space of projection matrices is a worthwhile endeavor.
3. **Detailed Parameter Exploration:** The paper goes beyond simply proposing a method and delves into the impact of key parameters like dimensionality (m) and the number of non-zero elements (s) on performance.  The discussion of the trade-offs involved and the resulting insights into the method’s behavior are valuable contributions.
4. **Emphasis on Theoretical Underpinnings:** The authors attempt to contextualize PoP within the broader landscape of randomized algorithms and dimensionality reduction, acknowledging the need for theoretical justification and drawing parallels to related work like Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma. While a complete theoretical treatment is not provided, the attempt to ground the method in established principles is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the limited comparison to established baselines. While the authors compare to RI, a critical omission is a direct comparison to simple, well-tuned PPMI + SVD baselines.  It is unclear how much of the observed performance gain is due to the projection itself versus the application of PPMI. A more rigorous evaluation against a wider range of methods, including simpler count-based approaches with dimensionality reduction, is essential.
2. **Unclear Motivation for PPMI:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the importance of being able to apply PPMI *after* projection. However, the rationale for this is not fully convincing.  The benefits of PPMI are well-known, but the argument for needing to apply it *specifically* after a random projection, rather than directly to the original count data, remains underdeveloped. Why is this transformation step advantageous in this context?
3. **Non-Standard Kendall’s τb Usage & Inconsistency:** The use of Kendall’s τb for similarity measurement is presented as a key aspect of the method. However, the explanation of why this is preferred over Pearson correlation, particularly given its non-standard application in this field, is insufficient.  Furthermore, the evaluation utilizes Spearman’s rank correlation – a metric closely related to Pearson correlation – making the choice of Kendall’s τb in the method’s core seem arbitrary. The provided justification relating to non-Gaussian distributions feels post-hoc.
4. **Evaluation Dataset & Reporting:** The exclusive reliance on the MEN dataset is limiting. While MEN is a valid benchmark, inclusion of standard semantic relatedness datasets like WordSim353 or SimLex-999 would significantly strengthen the evaluation and allow for broader generalization. Additionally, Figure 1 has visibility issues; a crucial line appears missing, hindering interpretation.
5. **Minor Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the paper’s polish. For instance, the phrase "large extend" should be "large extent." The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from redundancy.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI to a standard PPMI+SVD baseline, using identical data and evaluation procedures? This is crucial for establishing the value added by the projection step.
2.  Please clarify the specific rationale for choosing Kendall’s τb over Pearson correlation, particularly given its atypical usage in semantic similarity tasks.
3.  What theoretical guarantees can you provide regarding the error introduced by the random projection in PoP? Can you offer a more formal analysis of the trade-off between dimensionality reduction and information loss?
4.  Would the performance of PoP+PPMI degrade if Pearson correlation were used instead of Kendall’s τb after the PPMI transformation?



**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material should contain all code used in the experiments and a detailed description of the experimental setup. Further investigation of the impact of different PPMI normalization schemes is also recommended.
