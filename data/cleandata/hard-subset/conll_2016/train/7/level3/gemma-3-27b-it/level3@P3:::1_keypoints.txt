- Summary: Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a random-projection method with positive-valued matrices enabling post-projection PPMI and competitive, efficient embeddings.

- Contribution 1: Proposes PoP, a random projection variant with non-zero expected values to allow post-projection weighting like PPMI.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows PPMI applied to PoP spaces improves performance on the MEN relatedness test.

- Contribution 3: Provides an initial exploration of parameter effects, including dimensionality and sparsity of projection matrices.

- Strength  1: Claims significant computational efficiency over neural training and full PPMI on large count matrices.

- Strength  2: Preserves distributional properties by enabling PPMI after projection, addressing heavy-tailed count issues.

- Strength  3: Clear motivation framing the trade-off between computational cost and semantic accuracy.

- Strength  4: Comprehensive empirical evaluation within the paper’s scope, including parameter sweeps and error bars.

- Strength  5: Method described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, including projection matrix distribution.

- Weakness 1: Fails to compare PoP against related dimensionality-reduction and semantic-space methods (e.g., LLE, t-SNE, NMF).

- Weakness 2: Motivation for prioritizing PPMI transformability is underexplained and may be circular.

- Weakness 3: Uses a non-standard Kendall’s tau (τb) definition without adequate explanation of its impact.

- Weakness 4: Inconsistent choice of correlation metrics (Spearman vs. Pearson vs. Kendall) lacks justification.

- Weakness 5: Evaluation uses non-standard datasets; omitting benchmarks like WordSim353 or SimLex-999 weakens comparisons.

- Weakness 6: Figure 1 has a missing line, impairing result interpretation.

- Question 1: Please compare PoP to other dimensionality-reduction methods focusing on computational cost and performance.

- Question 2: Why was a non-standard Kendall’s τb definition used and how does it differ from the standard?

- Question 3: Why was Spearman’s correlation used initially but not consistently throughout the analysis?

- Question 4: Why were standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) not used?

- Question 5: Can you provide theoretical analysis or error bounds for PoP’s random projection approximation?

- Additional comment 1: Correct typo "large extend" → "large extent."

- Additional comment 2: Improve formatting of Equation 3 for readability.

- Additional comment 3: Positive note on supplemental material and request to share code/embeddings for reproducibility.
