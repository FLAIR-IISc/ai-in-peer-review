Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection method that uses sparse, nonnegative random index vectors (E[R] > 0) to build low-dimensional semantic spaces incrementally. Because the projected space remains nonnegative and non-Gaussian, the authors argue it supports post-hoc weighting with PPMI, and they advocate Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure in the unweighted PoP space, switching to Pearson after PPMI. Empirically, they report MEN relatedness scores around 0.75 with PoP+PPMI+Pearson and emphasize substantial speed advantages over neural methods and dense count-based pipelines.

Contributions as I see them
1) An engineering technique: a simple positive-only random projection scheme that enables applying PPMI after projection, allowing fast, incremental construction of distributional spaces at low dimensionality.
2) Empirical observations about similarity and distributional shape: τb works better in raw PoP space, while Pearson improves after PPMI due to approximate normality.
3) A practical parameter study on m and sparsity (s), illustrating variance and performance behaviors for randomized projections.

Strengths
- Efficiency and scalability: The approach avoids expensive training and works incrementally, requiring only random vectors and simple additions. This is a strong practical contribution, especially for rapid iteration or resource-constrained settings.
- Post-hoc weighting at low dimensionality: Being able to apply PPMI after projection is appealing in practice; the paper shows that PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches MEN scores (~0.75) comparable to widely used baselines while keeping computation light. This highlights a potentially favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-off.
- Sensible link between distributional shape and similarity choice: The observation that raw PoP vectors are non-Gaussian (favoring a rank-based measure) and become more Gaussian after PPMI (favoring Pearson) is well-motivated and empirically supported.
- Parameter exploration and variance reporting: The analysis over dimensionality m and number of nonzeros offers useful guidance (e.g., variance decreases with larger m; dense index vectors can harm performance via non-orthogonality), which enhances the utility of the method.

Weaknesses
- No direct, controlled comparisons to strong baselines: The paper does not run head-to-head experiments against SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, fastText, or standard PPMI+SVD on the same corpus, with matched preprocessing and context definitions. Claims of being “comparable to state-of-the-art” rely on numbers from other papers rather than controlled experiments, making it difficult to assess the true trade-offs.
- Unclear motivation and justification for “PPMI transformability”: The paper asserts that RI-style methods cannot be weighted with PPMI post-projection (arguing sums are zero), but the core issue is that PMI is defined on nonnegative co-occurrence probabilities, not on arbitrary signed projections. It remains unclear why enabling PPMI after projection is a decisive advantage versus the conventional and strong pipeline “counts → PPMI → (optional) SVD,” or “counts → PPMI → (sparse) random projection.” A direct empirical comparison of these pipelines is missing.
- Methodological issue with Kendall’s τb: The definition given considers adjacent pairs (xj, yj) and (xj+1, yj+1), which is nonstandard; τb is defined over all pairs. This needs correction and a precise description of the implementation. Moreover, the choice of Kendall as vector similarity is not justified against Spearman correlation or cosine; yet the evaluation metric to human judgments is Spearman ρ. This inconsistency requires clarification and empirical comparison.
- Limited evaluation: The study focuses on MEN (and UKWaC as the source corpus). A convincing evaluation should include multiple standard semantic relatedness/similarity benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW, MTurk-771/287, RG-65, MC-30), possibly also analogy or categorization tasks, and assess robustness across corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, Common Crawl).
- Presentation issues: Figure 1 appears to show only two curves; the third line referenced in the text is not visible. There are minor language issues (e.g., “large extend” → “extent”) and some figure/footnote formatting problems that hinder readability.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Can you provide controlled, same-corpus comparisons (accuracy, wall-clock time, memory) to: (a) SGNS/word2vec/fastText, (b) GloVe, (c) PPMI+SVD, and (d) counts→PPMI→random projection (to the same dimensionality as PoP)? Please include preprocessing and hyperparameters for fairness.
- Please correct and fully specify the Kendall’s τb computation. Did you also try Spearman correlation and cosine as vector similarities for PoP (before/after PPMI)? Report the results.
- Clarify the argument that RI cannot be combined with PPMI post-projection. Isn’t the main problem that PMI requires nonnegative co-occurrence probabilities? How does PoP compare to applying PPMI before projection (followed by a sparse Achlioptas-style projection)?
- Provide comprehensive timing: end-to-end runtime (including corpus scanning, projection, weighting), memory usage, and throughput vs. the baselines above.
- Can you extend the evaluation to WS-353, SimLex-999, RW, MTurk, RG-65, MC-30, and possibly to other corpora?

Additional comments
- Figure 1: only two lines are visible; please fix the plotting or legend so all methods are shown.
- Typo: “large extend” → “extent.”
- Consider releasing code/embeddings and specifying random seeds to facilitate replication and variance analysis.

Overall, the paper presents a neat and potentially useful engineering idea with promising efficiency, but the empirical evidence and methodological clarity are not yet sufficient for acceptance without stronger, controlled comparisons and corrections to the τb definition and similarity choices.
