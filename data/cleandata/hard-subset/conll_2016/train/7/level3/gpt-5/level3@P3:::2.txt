Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing word embeddings. Unlike traditional random indexing (RI) and related projections with zero-mean entries, PoP uses a sparse nonnegative projection matrix (E[R] > 0). The authors argue that (a) the resulting low-dimensional representations can be efficiently built incrementally from streaming context counts, (b) the nonnegativity allows application of PPMI weighting post-projection, and (c) Kendall’s τb is a better similarity measure in the pre-weighting non-Gaussian space, while Pearson’s r works well after PPMI. Experiments on MEN report up to ρ≈0.75 for PoP+PPMI, comparable to some neural and PPMI+SVD baselines, and the paper explores sensitivity to dimensionality and sparsity.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple sparse, nonnegative random projection for word co-occurrence data that purportedly enables post-hoc PPMI weighting in the projected space.
2) An empirical study of PoP parameterization (dimension, sparsity, similarity metric) and preliminary comparison to RI and simple count-based models.
3) An efficiency claim: PoP allows fast, incremental construction and cheaper weighting compared to full co-occurrence PPMI.

Strengths
- Practical, incremental construction: The two-step scheme (assign random index vectors to contexts; accumulate into word vectors on the fly) supports streaming and avoids storing full co-occurrence matrices. This property is valuable for large corpora and real-time updates.
- Clear empirical knobs and diagnostics: The paper reports how performance varies with dimensionality m and number of nonzero entries, and discusses the trade-off between sparsity and distortion (including the risk of non-orthogonal index vectors). The histograms illustrating distributional changes before/after PPMI are helpful.
- Sensible choice of similarity metrics: The rationale for using rank-based Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and Pearson’s r post-PPMI is reasonable given distributional assumptions and is supported by results.
- Competitive MEN scores with a simple pipeline: Achieving ρ≈0.75 on MEN via PoP+PPMI without heavy training is encouraging and could be useful when compute budgets are tight.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning unclear: The difference between PoP and prior art is not clearly delineated. Sparse nonnegative projections resemble known techniques such as feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch applied to co-occurrence matrices, as well as nonnegative random projections explored in the dimensionality-reduction literature. The paper should explicitly compare/relate to RI with nonnegative entries, hashing trick/CMS baselines, and nonnegative JL variants, and clarify what is fundamentally new.
- PPMI on projected dimensions needs justification: PPMI is defined on co-occurrence counts over interpretable contexts; after projection, dimensions are mixtures of contexts. The paper argues E[R]>0 makes PPMI “applicable,” but it does not explain whether PPMI retains its information-theoretic meaning, what bias/collision effects arise, or why it should outperform doing PPMI on the original matrix followed by projection/factorization. An ablation comparing: (i) PPMI on full matrix then project, (ii) project then PPMI (PoP), and (iii) PPMI+SVD, is needed.
- Insufficient evaluation breadth and baselines: Results are only on MEN. Standard practice is to include WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, MC-30, Rare Words, SimVerb-3500, and ideally one extrinsic task (e.g., similarity/relatedness in downstream classification). Comparisons should include SGNS/CBOW, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with tuning, RI/RRI/ISA/RMI, feature hashing/CMS, and report multiple runs with variance and significance tests.
- Efficiency claims lack measurements: The paper repeatedly claims orders-of-magnitude efficiency but provides no wall-clock time, memory usage, or throughput comparisons against baselines (e.g., PPMI computation on full matrices, SGNS training on the same corpus, RI). Without concrete profiling, the computational advantage remains speculative.
- Clarity and organization issues: The argumentation is hard to follow from the introduction onward. Key definitions and assumptions (e.g., distribution of T, role of s, α, expected collisions) are scattered and sometimes imprecise. Specific statements are unsupported or poorly framed, e.g., line 114 “In addition …” lacks a clear antecedent and evidence; line 137 “Since …” posits a consequence without a stated premise; line 154 “thus …” overclaims based on limited data. The paper is not self-contained for readers unfamiliar with random projections; essential background (e.g., JL lemma intuition, hashing/collision effects, known zero-mean vs nonzero-mean implications) is missing.
- Language quality harms readability: Numerous grammar/spelling issues (e.g., line 86 “To a large extend” → “extent”; line 142 “such PPMI”) and awkward phrasing impede comprehension. Careful proofreading is required.

Questions to the authors
- How does PoP differ from feature hashing or Count-Min Sketch applied to the word–context matrix (with nonnegative updates)? Please compare theoretically and empirically.
- Why is it preferable to apply PPMI after projection rather than before? Can you quantify the bias introduced by collisions in PoP+PPMI? Provide an analysis or simulation on synthetic data with known co-occurrence structure.
- Can you provide wall-clock time, memory, and throughput comparisons for: (a) constructing the model; (b) computing PPMI; (c) similarity computation—against RI, PPMI+SVD, SGNS/GloVe? Please include variance across runs.
- How sensitive are results to corpus size and preprocessing (e.g., sub-sampling, stopword removal, context selection)? Can you report on additional datasets (e.g., Wikipedia) and multiple benchmarks?
- Is there a theoretical guarantee (even a bound on expected inner product distortion) for nonzero-mean sparse projections used here? If not, can you at least characterize empirically how PoP preserves rank order vs counts?

Additional comments
- Provide a concise algorithm box or pseudocode defining PoP (inputs, parameters m and s, update rules).
- Define all notation once and stick to it; ensure equations are legible and variables are introduced before use.
- Report multiple seeds and confidence intervals; include significance testing for leaderboard-style comparisons.
- Improve the introduction: clearly state contributions, scope, and hypotheses; move background on similarity metrics and distributional assumptions to a related work/background section.

Recommendation
I recommend rejection in the current form. The idea has potential, but the paper needs substantial rewriting for clarity, stronger positioning and novelty, broader and more rigorous evaluation (including efficiency measurements), and a clearer justification for applying PPMI post-projection. I encourage the authors to address these points and resubmit.
