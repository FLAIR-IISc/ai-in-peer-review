Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces with nonnegative entries (E[R] > 0). Because PoP preserves positivity, the authors argue that post-hoc weighting schemes such as PPMI can be applied directly to the projected space, avoiding heavyweight computation on high-dimensional count matrices. They propose using Kendall’s τb to compute similarities in the non-Gaussian space before weighting and Pearson’s r after PPMI, and report MEN relatedness scores around 0.75 with PoP+PPMI, with claims of substantial speed and memory advantages over neural methods.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, incremental random projection scheme using positive-only sparse index vectors that enables post-projection weighting such as PPMI at low dimensionality. This is the core contribution, with an attractive efficiency–accuracy trade-off.
2) An empirical observation about similarity metrics: Kendall’s τb performs better on raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian), whereas Pearson’s r is better after PPMI (Gaussianized), along with a brief parameter study (m and s) and runtime advantages.
3) A practical recipe for fast semantic space construction that approaches competitive performance on MEN without neural training or SVD.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency and simplicity: PoP requires only generating sparse positive index vectors and summing, which is compatible with streaming/incremental construction. Applying PPMI on already low-dimensional vectors is efficient compared to classic count-based+PPMI+SVD pipelines. This makes the approach attractive for rapid prototyping and large-scale updates.
- Competitive performance with lightweight computation: On MEN, PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75, competitive with several non-neural baselines, and approaching neural methods, while avoiding training time and GPU/CPU costs.
- Clear algorithmic description and ablations: The two-step construction is easy to reproduce. The paper reports multiple runs with averages/min/max, explores dimensionality m and sparsity s, and examines effects on variance, providing a useful guide to hyperparameters.
- Insightful link between distribution shapes and similarity metrics: The rationale for τb in non-Gaussian spaces and Pearson after PPMI (variance stabilization/Gaussianization) is a sensible, data-driven choice that could generalize beyond this specific method.
- Incremental/online applicability: The method’s compositionality and need for only index vectors suit incremental corpora or dynamic vocabularies where frequent retraining is impractical.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines and direct comparisons: The evaluation lacks side-by-side comparisons on the same corpus and preprocessing with established baselines such as SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, fastText, or count-based PPMI+SVD/SPPMI+SVD. The claim of being comparable to “state-of-the-art” is weakened without replicated baselines and significance testing. A timing/memory table comparing PoP, PPMI+SVD, and SGNS would strengthen the efficiency claim.
- Motivation for post-projection PPMI is underjustified: The key argument is that E[R] > 0 allows PPMI after projection, while standard RI (E[R]=0) does not. However, a natural baseline is to compute PPMI (or SPPMI) in the original space and then randomly project (including with nonnegative R) or use dimensionality reduction (e.g., SVD or randomized SVD). The paper does not compare PoP+PPMI to PPMI→projection pipelines, leaving unclear whether post-hoc weighting is necessary or superior.
- Similarity metric definitions and consistency issues: The definition of Kendall’s τb appears nonstandard (it describes concordant/discordant “adjacent” pairs rather than all pairs), while the implementation mentions Knight’s O(n log n) algorithm. This needs correction and a precise definition. Moreover, the paper alternates among τb, cosine, and Pearson without a clear, principled protocol or comprehensive comparison across settings; it would help to report cosine baselines consistently and justify metric choices per distributional regime.
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only on MEN with lemmatized UKWaC. MEN is standard but not sufficient. The paper would be stronger with additional benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RareWord, SimVerb-3500) and at least one downstream or analogy evaluation, plus OOV handling and robustness analyses. Statistical significance across runs is not reported.
- Theoretical grounding is thin: While empirical performance is the focus, the paper makes distributional claims (e.g., positivity enabling PPMI, variance ∝ 1/m) and analogies to RI without formal bounds or even informal approximations tailored to PoP’s distribution. Some theoretical clarification (even partial bounds or concentration arguments) would increase confidence.
- Presentation issues: Figure 1 is described as having three curves, but only two appear visible; please ensure all curves and legends are readable. Minor language issues (e.g., “to a large extend” → “to a large extent”), and occasional notational slippage (e.g., switching T dimensions) should be cleaned up.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines: Can you provide controlled comparisons on the same data/preprocessing with (a) SGNS/word2vec (skip-gram with negative sampling), (b) GloVe, (c) PPMI/SPPMI+SVD, and (d) randomized SVD? Please include training time, memory footprint, and variance across runs.
- PPMI timing: What are end-to-end runtimes and memory usage for PoP+PPMI vs count-based+PPMI+SVD at comparable performance? A small ablation table would be valuable.
- Post- vs pre-projection weighting: How does PoP+PPMI compare to PPMI-before-projection (with a nonnegative or standard random matrix) and to PPMI+randomized SVD? If PoP’s advantage is primarily computational, demonstrating that would strengthen the case.
- τb definition and usage: Please correct/clarify the definition (all pairs vs adjacent pairs) and report head-to-head results for τb, cosine, and Pearson both before and after PPMI across several m and s.
- Hyperparameter selection: How were m and s chosen—tuned on a dev split of MEN or fixed a priori? How sensitive are results to s when m is large? Any rule-of-thumb guidance?
- Generality: Can PoP be used for contextualized features (e.g., subword, dependency-based contexts)? Does positivity constrain expressivity or induce bias compared to centered projections?
