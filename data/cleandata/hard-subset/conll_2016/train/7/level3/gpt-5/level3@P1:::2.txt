Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection approach to construct low-dimensional word embeddings with strictly non-negative, sparse projection vectors (E[R] > 0). The key claim is that PoP preserves enough of the long-tailed structure of count features to allow post-hoc weighting with PPMI at low dimensionality, yielding competitive relatedness scores on MEN while being highly efficient and incremental. The paper further advocates Kendall’s τb in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian) and Pearson after PPMI (Gaussianized), and explores sensitivity to dimensionality and sparsity.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Practical observation that positive-only, sparse random projections permit efficient post-projection PPMI weighting, achieving competitive MEN performance (≈0.75 with PoP+PPMI+Pearson) with much lower computational cost than neural or high-dimensional count-based pipelines.
2) An incremental, simple construction procedure for semantic spaces that couples: positive-only random index vectors, rank-based similarity (Kendall’s τb) pre-weighting, and Pearson correlation post-PPMI.
3) An empirical parameter study (m, number of non-zero entries s) and a qualitative analysis of orthogonality vs distortion, informing usable defaults.

Strengths
- Computational efficiency and practicality: The method avoids expensive training and large dense matrices, while enabling fast PPMI weighting in a low-dimensional space. The incremental construction is attractive for large-scale or streaming scenarios.
- Clear intuition for similarity choices: The τb vs Pearson rationale is coherent with distributional assumptions (nonparametric in non-Gaussian raw PoP vs Pearson after log/PPMI Gaussianization), and the paper shows this matters empirically.
- Useful parameter exploration: The analyses over dimensionality (m) and sparsity (s) give concrete guidance, including the trade-off between orthogonality and distortion, and the finding that 1–4 non-zero entries often suffice.
- Competitive MEN result with simple ingredients: Achieving ≈0.75 Spearman on MEN with UKWaC and straightforward contexts, and noting further gains with tuning, suggests the idea is viable for relatedness.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and organization: The exposition is difficult to follow from the introduction onward, with multiple logic jumps and insufficient background. Readers not already versed in random projections will struggle. Numerous language errors hurt readability (e.g., “To a large extend” (Line 86), “such PPMI” (Line 142)). Several paragraphs begin with weak connectors (“in addition” Line 114; “Since” Line 137; “thus” Line 154) and make unsupported or poorly framed claims. The paper needs substantial rewriting for clarity and flow.
- Novelty relative to prior random projection methods is underdeveloped: How PoP differs in principle and practice from Random Indexing, reflective RI, ISA, RMI, feature hashing, or non-negative/binary random projections (e.g., Achlioptas-style) is not articulated rigorously. The claim that E[R] > 0 uniquely enables PPMI is plausible but not demonstrated theoretically; the effect of projection on joint/marginal statistics for PPMI is not analyzed, and it is unclear whether this approximates PMI of the original counts in any bounded sense.
- Evaluation is too narrow: Results are only on MEN. A robust demonstration should include multiple standard benchmarks (WordSim353, SimLex-999, RG-65, RareWords), analogy tests, and possibly a downstream task. There is no head-to-head comparison with strong baselines on the same corpus and contexts, such as: count+PPMI(+SVD), SGNS/CBOW/GloVe, or RI+postprocessing. Statistical significance and variance across seeds should be reported more systematically.
- Missing ablations and controls: It is unclear how much of the gain comes from PPMI vs the positive-only projection vs the choice of similarity. A direct comparison of PPMI on PoP vs PPMI on original counts at matched compute budgets would clarify the specific benefit of projecting first. Likewise, cosine vs τb vs Pearson comparisons should be shown systematically.
- Limited theoretical grounding and guarantees: The paper explicitly states that a mathematical account for error bounds δ is not provided. While acceptable for an initial empirical report, stronger justification or at least a clearer connection to known results on non-negative/sparse projections would strengthen the case.

Questions to Authors (to clarify in rebuttal)
- Novelty and positioning: How does PoP differ from prior non-negative or binary random projection schemes and feature hashing? Please situate the method against Achlioptas-type sparse projections, RI with {−1,0,+1}, and non-negative variants.
- PPMI after projection: What quantities does Equation (3) estimate when computed on the projected matrix? Do you expect PPMI(PoP) to approximate PPMI(counts) in any sense? Can you provide empirical evidence (e.g., correlation of PMI weights before vs after projection on a manageable subset)?
- Guarantees for positivity: You argue E[R] > 0 ensures non-zero row/column sums needed for PPMI. Can you specify conditions on s and m that guarantee no zero marginals in practice, and discuss the effect of collisions?
- Baselines and scope: Can you add results on WordSim353, SimLex-999, and at least one analogy set, and compare to count+PPMI(+SVD) and SGNS trained on the same corpus/settings? Please report means, standard deviations, and significance.
- Efficiency claims: Provide wall-clock and memory comparisons versus count+PPMI(+SVD) and SGNS on UKWaC, including preprocessing time and PPMI computation time at different m.

Additional comments
- Improve writing, structure, and grammar; define notation once and adhere to it; avoid non sequiturs and ensure claims are supported or marked as conjecture.
- Release code and embeddings regardless of acceptance to facilitate reproducibility.
- Consider reporting cosine alongside τb/Pearson for completeness, and include sensitivity to context window choices and stopword handling.

Overall recommendation
Reject for now. The idea is promising and pragmatically useful, but the paper requires substantial improvements in clarity, positioning w.r.t. prior work, and breadth of evaluation before it can be considered.
