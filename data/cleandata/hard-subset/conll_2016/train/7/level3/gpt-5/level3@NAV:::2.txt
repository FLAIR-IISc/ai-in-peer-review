Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random-projection approach where the projection matrix has strictly nonnegative entries and positive expectation. The authors argue this enables post-hoc application of PPMI weighting on low-dimensional embeddings, yielding computational savings while reaching competitive MEN relatedness scores (~0.75), especially when combined with Pearson correlation after PPMI. They also motivate Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure for the non-Gaussian pre-PPMI space and provide some sensitivity analyses over dimensionality and sparsity.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding that combining a sparse nonnegative random projection with PPMI and Pearson achieves competitive MEN performance at low computational cost.
- Introduction of PoP, a random projection variant with positive expectation intended to preserve nonnegativity to permit PPMI after projection.
- Exploration of Kendall’s τb as a similarity metric for non-Gaussian projected spaces before PPMI.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency emphasis: Building low-dimensional embeddings first, then applying PPMI, is a plausible route to reduce the cost associated with weighting/factorization; several plots examine m and sparsity.
- Reasonable empirical performance on MEN (ρ ≈ 0.75) with simple pipelines (PoP+PPMI+Pearson), suggesting a good cost–performance trade-off.
- Clear articulation of an incremental construction procedure akin to RI makes the method easy to implement and potentially useful for streaming scenarios.
- Some attention to parameter sensitivity (dimensionality m, number of nonzeros), offering initial guidance on settings and stability.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and framing: The paper is difficult to follow from the introduction onward; key arguments (e.g., why E(R)>0 is essential for PPMI) are asserted rather than demonstrated. Several sentences are dangling/unsupported (e.g., Line 114 “in addition…”, Line 137 “Since…”, Line 154 “thus…”).
- Novelty relative to prior random projections (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) is unclear: how PoP differs theoretically and practically, and whether claimed advantages over these methods (and over count-based PPMI+SVD) hold, is insufficiently substantiated.
- Evaluation is too narrow: only MEN is used. Standard benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words, SimVerb) or a principled justification for a single dataset are needed, plus comparisons to strong baselines (SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD) with time/memory metrics.
- Theoretical grounding is thin: no guarantees or bounds on distortion, no complexity analysis beyond informal statements, and mixed claims about cosine vs correlation that may be misleading to non-experts.
- Not self-contained for readers unfamiliar with random projections; background and rationale are sparse, and several statements are imprecise (e.g., about why RI precludes PPMI).
- Numerous English errors hurt readability (e.g., Line 86 “To a large extend”, Line 142 “such PPMI”), compounding the clarity issues.

Questions to Authors
- Please formalize the distribution of T, its expectation/variance, and provide a clear argument why E(R)>0 is necessary/sufficient for valid PPMI post-projection. Could RI with nonnegative projections or rectification yield similar outcomes?
- Provide wall-clock time and memory vs. baselines (count+PPMI, SGNS, GloVe, RI) on the same hardware.
- Report results on additional benchmarks and include ablations: PoP without PPMI, PoP+cosine, RI+PPMI (if feasible with nonnegative variants), PoP+SVD.
- Clarify the role and benefit of Kendall’s τb vs Pearson/cosine with supporting statistics on distributional assumptions.

Overall recommendation: substantially improve presentation, clarity, grounding, and evaluation breadth before resubmission.
