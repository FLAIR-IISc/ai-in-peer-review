Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random-projection approach for constructing word embeddings with a projection matrix whose entries are nonnegative and E(R) > 0. The claimed advantages are: (i) incremental, low-cost construction akin to random indexing; (ii) use of Kendall’s τb in the non-Gaussian projected space; and, crucially, (iii) the ability to apply PPMI after projection (because sums are positive), followed by Pearson correlation, yielding competitive performance on MEN with much lower computational cost than dense count-based PPMI or neural embeddings.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A practical recipe for building low-dimensional embeddings via positive-only sparse projections that allow post-hoc PPMI weighting, with empirical guidance on similarity metrics (Kendall pre-weighting, Pearson post-weighting) and parameters (m, s).
2) An empirical result that PoP+PPMI attains MEN relatedness scores around 0.75 while being computationally light and incrementally constructible.
3) A parameter study suggesting how dimensionality and sparsity affect performance and stability.

Strengths
- Efficiency and incrementality: The method inherits the incremental, streaming-friendly construction of random indexing and avoids expensive training or large-scale SVD. Computing PPMI on low-dimensional projected matrices is significantly cheaper than on high-dimensional count matrices, which is practically valuable for large corpora or frequent model updates.
- Empirical competitiveness on MEN: The reported PoP+PPMI+Pearson performance (~0.75; up to ~0.78 with tuning) is on par with strong baselines, while outperforming RI/count-only baselines, especially for small context windows. The paper also sensibly switches from Kendall’s τb to Pearson’s r after PPMI, which is consistent with the Gaussianization induced by log/PPMI.
- Parameter exploration: The paper provides informative experiments varying dimensionality m and sparsity s (number of nonzeros), and discusses orthogonality/collision effects. This guidance is useful for practitioners and helps demystify the behavior of sparse random projections.
- Clarity about lack of theory and pragmatic focus: The authors are transparent that formal guarantees are not yet available and position their contribution as an empirical engineering advance, which may appeal to applied audiences if the empirical evidence is broadened.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and organization: The paper is difficult to follow from the introduction onward, and is not self-contained for readers unfamiliar with random projections. Key rationale (why E(R) > 0 meaningfully enables PPMI; how projected counts retain interpretable co-occurrence structure) is not explained at a level accessible to non-experts. Numerous prose issues hamper comprehension (e.g., Line 86 “To a large extend”; Line 142 “such PPMI”), and several sentences start with connectors that render arguments ungrammatical or unsupported (e.g., Line 114 “in addition”; Line 137 “Since”; Line 154 “thus”).
- Novelty and positioning w.r.t. prior RP methods: It remains unclear what is fundamentally novel beyond choosing a nonnegative sparse projection and then applying PPMI. RI/ISA/RMI comparisons are primarily qualitative; the paper should articulate more precisely what property is gained by E(R) > 0 and whether this is new in the literature. The claim that RI’s E(R)=0 “prevents PPMI” conflates algebraic feasibility (sums equal zero) with statistical validity (are projected “counts” meaningful probabilities?). A careful discussion of whether PMI computed on projected mixtures preserves or approximates PMI on the original counts is missing.
- Limited evaluation: Results are reported only on MEN using UKWaC. This is insufficient to establish generality. Standard similarity benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words), and at least one downstream task (e.g., text classification, analogy, or lexical substitution) should be included. No statistical significance, robustness across corpora, or domain variation is shown. There is no ablation comparing PoP+PPMI to strong dense PPMI(+SVD) under equal compute constraints.
- Missing baselines and measurements: The paper asserts large efficiency gains, but provides no wall-clock time, memory, or throughput measurements versus (i) dense PPMI, (ii) PPMI+SVD, and (iii) neural baselines (SGNS, GloVe). Without these, the efficiency claim is not quantifiable. Similarly, no comparison to hashing-based dimensionality reduction or to nonnegative random projections commonly used in streaming/sketching is provided.
- Methodological gaps: Key implementation choices (e.g., the distribution floor(1/U^α), choice of α=0.5, normalization, context selection such as stopword removal, window weighting) are insufficiently specified for exact reproduction. The rationale for Kendall vs cosine vs Pearson is asserted but not rigorously evaluated across measures. The discussion of non-orthogonality/collisions is informative but lacks a concrete analysis of the induced bias in PMI or similarity rankings.

Questions to authors
- Statistical validity of PPMI after projection: What is the formal or empirical justification that PMI computed on PoP-projected matrices correlates with PMI on the original count matrix? Can you provide a synthetic study where true PMI is known to show bias/variance trade-offs as a function of m and s?
- Relation to prior work: Are there prior uses of nonnegative random projections in distributional semantics or feature hashing that enable post-hoc weighting? Please clarify what is novel relative to RI/RRI/ISA/RMI and to Johnson–Lindenstrauss-style nonnegative projections.
- Similarity measures: Can you report a comprehensive comparison of Kendall, Pearson, and cosine on raw PoP, PoP+PPMI, and dense PPMI across multiple datasets, with significance tests?
- Efficiency evidence: Please include wall-clock time and memory profiles for PoP+PPMI versus dense PPMI and PPMI+SVD on the same corpus, as well as build-time comparisons to SGNS/GloVe.
- Evaluation breadth: Can you add results on WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words, and at least one downstream task? If constrained to MEN, please justify and discuss generalization.
- Parameter guidance: Provide principled guidance on choosing s and m (beyond empirical sweeps), and clarify the impact of α. How sensitive are results to corpus size, vocabulary thresholds, and context selection?

Additional comments
- Please substantially revise for clarity: tighten the introduction, provide a concise background on random projections and their properties, and explicitly state assumptions and limitations. A small running example illustrating how projected “counts” support PPMI would help.
- Language and style: Numerous grammatical issues (e.g., “To a large extend” → “extent”; “such PPMI” → “such as PPMI”) and awkward connectors should be corrected.
- Consider releasing code and scripts to ensure reproducibility.

Overall: promising practical idea, but the paper requires significantly improved exposition, clearer novelty and justification, broader evaluation, and quantitative efficiency evidence before it can be considered for acceptance.
