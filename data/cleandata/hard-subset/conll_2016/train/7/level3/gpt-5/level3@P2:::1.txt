Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing semantic spaces using sparse, non-negative random index vectors (E[R] > 0). The authors argue that the non-negativity preserves long-tailed distributions after projection, enabling the application of count-based weighting schemes such as PPMI post hoc on low-dimensional vectors. They compute similarity in the projected space using Kendall’s τb (and Pearson’s r after PPMI), and report results primarily on MEN, with some analysis of dimensionality and sparsity parameters. The central empirical claim is that PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ≈0.75 Spearman ρ on MEN while being much faster than training neural embeddings or computing PPMI on full count matrices.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A projection scheme with non-negative, sparse random matrices that preserves sufficient structure to make downstream PPMI weighting feasible and efficient at low dimensionality.
- An empirical study showing competitive MEN relatedness performance (≈0.75 ρ) with simple, fast construction, and analysis of PoP hyperparameters (dimension m, sparsity s) and similarity choices (Kendall vs Pearson).
- A practical framing of incremental, streaming construction of semantic spaces without heavy training, highlighting potential engineering benefits.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency and simplicity: The method avoids expensive training (neural) or dense PPMI over very high-dimensional count matrices. The incremental, streaming construction is attractive for large corpora and frequent updates. This is a valuable engineering contribution.
- Clear empirical gains over RI and raw count-based baselines: On MEN, PoP outperforms RI and unweighted counts, and PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves scores competitive with stronger baselines, at least on this single benchmark.
- Insightful parameter exploration: The paper examines the effects of m and s, discusses orthogonality vs distortion trade-offs, and provides qualitative distributional analyses (histograms) that help motivate when Kendall vs Pearson might be appropriate.
- Conceptual link between E[R] > 0 and post-projection weighting: The observation that zero-mean projections preclude standard count-based weighting (PPMI) and that positive-only projections restore this option is interesting and may inspire further hybrids between count-based weighting and random projections.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient comparison to strong baselines: The paper lacks direct, controlled comparisons to SGNS/CBOW, GloVe, and PPMI+SVD (with reasonable truncation) trained on the same corpus with matched preprocessing. Claims of “comparable to state of the art” need to be substantiated on multiple datasets with standard baselines and significance testing.
- Methodological ambiguity around similarity measures: The definition of Kendall’s τb appears nonstandard (only adjacent pairs (j, j+1) are considered), and the motivation for choosing τb over cosine/Pearson is not empirically demonstrated via ablations. The paper also switches to Pearson after PPMI but does not show systematic comparisons (τb vs cosine vs Pearson) for all configurations.
- Evaluation scope is too narrow: Results are reported mainly on MEN. Standard benchmarks such as WordSim-353 (similarity and relatedness splits), SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words, SimVerb-3500, and perhaps analogy tests are missing. Relying on a single dataset weakens the generality of the claims.
- Theoretical justification is minimal: While the paper acknowledges the absence of theory, there is no formal analysis of how PoP affects co-occurrence statistics, how PPMI computed on projected counts should be interpreted, or how distortions depend on m and s beyond qualitative observations. Without bounds or guarantees (even approximate), it is hard to assess robustness.
- PPMI semantics after projection are unclear: After random aggregation, “counts” in PoP space are mixtures of many contexts; the marginals used in the PPMI formula no longer correspond to true co-occurrence probabilities. The paper should justify why PPMI remains meaningful and when it may fail, or at least empirically validate against PPMI on full counts with SVD.
- Missing diagnostics and reproducibility details: There are no runtime/memory comparisons versus count+PPMI and SGNS/GloVe; no significance tests; and some figure/reporting issues (e.g., Figure 1 appears to show only two curves though three are described). Hyperparameters, preprocessing choices, and seeds are not fully specified.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Please provide a precise, standard definition of Kendall’s τb used, including how ties are handled and whether all O(m^2) pairs are considered (as per τb) or only adjacent pairs (as suggested by the text). Include an ablation comparing τb vs cosine vs Pearson for PoP (unweighted) and PoP+PPMI across multiple m and s.
- Can you add controlled comparisons on the same UKWaC setup to: (a) SGNS and GloVe with tuned hyperparameters; (b) PPMI+SVD with typical truncations (e.g., 200–500 dims), and (c) PPMI without SVD but with proper smoothing/scaling? Please report Spearman ρ on at least MEN, WordSim-353 (Sim/Rel), and SimLex-999.
- What are the actual runtime and memory measurements for building PoP, computing PPMI on PoP vectors, and, as baselines, building full counts+PPMI and training SGNS/GloVe? Please include wall-clock times and peak memory.
- How should we interpret PPMI computed on PoP-projected counts? Are there circumstances where using PPMI on the projected space provably approximates PPMI on the original counts? Could you compare PoP+PPMI to true PPMI+SVD on a smaller corpus where the latter is tractable?
- Is E[R] > 0 essential? How does PoP compare to biased R drawn from {−1, +1} with a positive bias, or to adding a constant offset to zero-mean projections? Please include an ablation.
- Can you clarify the preprocessing (windowing, POS tagging, stopwords, frequency thresholds), choice of m and s for the reported best score, and random seeds? Please provide code and embeddings as promised.

Additional Comments
- Figure 1: The text describes three curves (counts, RI, PoP), but only two appear visible. Please fix the figure and legend so all curves are distinguishable.
- Typo: “to a large extend” → “to a large extent.” There are several minor grammatical issues throughout; a careful proofreading would help.
- Complexity discussion: Even with O(m log m) per τb computation, pairwise similarity over a large vocabulary can be costly; practical retrieval scenarios (nearest neighbors) may need approximate indexing or pre-pruning. Some guidance would be useful.
- Clarity: It would help to explicitly state how PPMI marginals are computed over PoP matrices and whether any smoothing is applied.
