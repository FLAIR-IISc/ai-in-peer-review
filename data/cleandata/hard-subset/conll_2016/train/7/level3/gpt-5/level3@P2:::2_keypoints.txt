- Proposal: Introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse nonnegative random-projection scheme enabling post-hoc PPMI and low-cost incremental word embeddings.
- Main contribution 1: Practical recipe for low-dimensional PoP embeddings with empirical guidance on similarity metrics (Kendall pre-weighting, Pearson post-weighting) and parameters (m, s).
- Main contribution 2: Empirical claim that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive MEN scores (~0.75–0.78) while being computationally light and incrementally constructible.
- Main contribution 3: Parameter study on how dimensionality (m) and sparsity (s) affect performance and stability, with discussion of orthogonality/collision effects.
- Strength — efficiency/incrementality: Inherits streaming-friendly random indexing properties and allows much cheaper PPMI computation on low-dimensional projections.
- Strength — empirical competitiveness: PoP+PPMI+Pearson competes with strong baselines on MEN and outperforms RI/count-only baselines, especially for small windows.
- Strength — parameter exploration: Provides useful experiments varying m and s, offering practical guidance for practitioners.
- Strength — transparency: Authors are explicit about lack of formal guarantees and frame the work as an empirical engineering advance.
- Weakness — clarity/organization: Paper is hard to follow, not self-contained for non-experts, and contains many prose and grammar issues.
- Weakness — novelty/positioning: Unclear what is fundamentally new beyond nonnegative projections and PPMI; needs clearer relation to RI/ISA/RMI and literature on nonnegative projections.
- Weakness — limited evaluation: Results are only on MEN/UKWaC; lacks other similarity datasets, downstream tasks, corpus robustness, and statistical significance tests.
- Weakness — missing baselines/measurements: No wall-clock, memory, or throughput comparisons to dense PPMI, PPMI+SVD, SGNS/GloVe, or hashing/nonnegative projection baselines.
- Weakness — methodological gaps: Key implementation choices (distribution parameters, normalization, context handling) are under-specified and some methodological claims aren’t rigorously analyzed.
- Question — PMI validity: What formal or empirical evidence shows PMI on PoP-projected matrices approximates PMI on original counts (bias/variance study as a function of m,s)?
- Question — relation to prior work: Are there prior uses of nonnegative random projections for post-hoc weighting, and how exactly does this differ from RI/RRI/ISA/RMI and JL-style methods?
- Question — similarity measures: Can authors provide comprehensive comparisons (Kendall, Pearson, cosine) across raw PoP, PoP+PPMI, and dense PPMI with significance testing?
- Question — efficiency evidence: Please supply wall-clock time and memory profiles for PoP+PPMI vs dense PPMI, PPMI+SVD, and neural baselines on the same corpus.
- Question — evaluation breadth and parameters: Add results on standard benchmarks (WordSim, SimLex, RG-65, Rare Words), at least one downstream task, and principled guidance on choosing s, m, and α; report sensitivity to corpus/vocab/context choices.
- Additional comment — revise exposition: Tighten introduction, add background on random projections, and include a small running example showing how projected counts support PPMI.
- Additional comment — fix language/style: Correct numerous grammatical errors and awkward connectors to improve readability.
- Additional comment — reproducibility: Consider releasing code and scripts to ensure reproducibility.
- Overall: Promising practical idea but needs much clearer exposition, stronger novelty/positioning, broader evaluation, and quantitative efficiency evidence before acceptance.
