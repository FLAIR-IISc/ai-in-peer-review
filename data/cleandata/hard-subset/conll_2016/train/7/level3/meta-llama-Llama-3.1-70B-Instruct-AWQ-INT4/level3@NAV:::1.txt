**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a novel technique called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. PoP is based on random projections and is designed to preserve some distributional properties in dimensionality reduction. The method uses a random projection matrix with a non-standard distribution, which allows for the application of weighting techniques such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting vectors. The authors evaluate the performance of PoP on the MEN relatedness test set and show that it achieves competitive results compared to neural embedding techniques, but with much faster training times.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel random projection method**: The paper introduces a new random projection method, PoP, which preserves some distributional properties in dimensionality reduction.
2. **Efficient dimensionality reduction**: PoP achieves efficient dimensionality reduction without changing the distribution of the original data to a Gaussian shape with E = 0.
3. **Applicability of weighting techniques**: PoP-constructed models can be weighted using techniques such as PPMI, which is not possible with previous random projection-based methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Faster training times**: PoP achieves competitive results compared to neural embedding techniques, but with much faster training times.
2. **Preservation of distributional properties**: PoP preserves some distributional properties in dimensionality reduction, which is important for semantic similarity assessments.
3. **Efficient dimensionality reduction**: PoP achieves efficient dimensionality reduction without requiring expensive computations.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of direct comparison to related work**: The paper does not provide a direct comparison to related work, which makes it difficult to evaluate the performance of PoP in the context of existing methods.
2. **Non-standard Kendall's tau definition**: The paper uses a non-standard definition of Kendall's tau, which is not explained in detail.
3. **Inconsistency in using Spearman correlation**: The authors use Spearman correlation for evaluation, but do not justify why they do not use it for computing similarities in the PoP-constructed models.
4. **Non-standard evaluation dataset**: The paper uses non-standard datasets for evaluation, which may not be representative of the performance of PoP in other tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a direct comparison to related work, such as neural embedding techniques and other random projection-based methods?
2. Can you explain the non-standard definition of Kendall's tau used in the paper?
3. Why do you use Spearman correlation for evaluation, but not for computing similarities in the PoP-constructed models?
4. Can you provide additional experiments using standard semantic relatedness benchmarks to evaluate the performance of PoP?
