**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel technique called Positive-only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The PoP method is based on random projections and uses a transformation matrix with a positive expected value, allowing for the application of weighting techniques such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors claim that their method is highly scalable and computationally efficient, making it a suitable alternative to neural network-based approaches.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1. The introduction of the PoP method, which enables the construction of semantic spaces that can be weighted using PPMI.
2. The demonstration of the method's efficiency and competitiveness in semantic similarity assessment tasks, achieving an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test.
3. The proposal of a new similarity measure, Kendall's τb, for computing similarities in non-Gaussian spaces.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficiency**: The PoP method is reportedly much faster than neural network-based approaches, making it a promising solution for large-scale natural language processing tasks.
2. **Preservation of distributional properties**: The method preserves some distributional properties of the original data, which is essential for semantic similarity assessment tasks.
3. **Competitive performance**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance in semantic similarity assessment tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art neural network-based approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of direct comparison to related work**: The authors do not provide a direct comparison to other related work in the field, making it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of their method.
2. **Methodological issues**: The authors use a non-standard definition of Kendall's τb, which is not clearly explained. Additionally, they do not justify the use of Kendall's τb over other correlation measures, such as Spearman's correlation.
3. **Inconsistent evaluation metrics**: The authors use Spearman's correlation for evaluation but do not provide a clear justification for not using it as a similarity measure in their method.
4. **Non-standard evaluation datasets**: The authors use non-standard datasets for evaluation, which may not be representative of the broader semantic relatedness benchmark.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind using PPMI weighting in your method?
2. How do you plan to address the methodological issues raised, such as the non-standard definition of Kendall's τb and the inconsistent use of evaluation metrics?
3. Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison to other related work in the field, including neural network-based approaches and other dimensionality reduction techniques?
4. How do you plan to extend your method to address the limitations mentioned in the paper, such as the need for theoretical accounts and the development of new methods combining PoP with other weighting techniques?
