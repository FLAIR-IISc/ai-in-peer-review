## Review for CONLL 2016: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper introduces a novel method for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings called Positive-Only Projection (PoP). The core idea is to use random projection matrices with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), unlike traditional random projection methods where E(R) = 0. This allows for the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) after the projection step, effectively transferring the embeddings to a more discriminative space. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, while being computationally efficient.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling post-projection weighting:** This is the primary contribution. The use of a positive-valued random projection matrix to allow subsequent application of PPMI weighting is a clever idea that addresses a limitation of standard random projection methods.
2. **Empirical demonstration of PoP + PPMI’s effectiveness:** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation on the MEN relatedness test, showing that the proposed method achieves competitive results compared to existing techniques.
3. **Detailed analysis of parameters affecting PoP's performance:** The extensive experimentation with parameters like dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors provides valuable insights into the method’s behavior and offers practical guidance for its use.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightfulness:** The core idea of using a positive-valued random projection matrix to enable post-projection weighting is novel and insightful. It effectively bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of weighting schemes.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with existing techniques and a detailed analysis of parameter settings. The use of multiple experiments and the reporting of average and range of performance scores strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Clear Explanation of Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the PoP method, including the mathematical formulation and the underlying rationale. The discussion of the differences between PoP and other random projection techniques (RI, RRI, etc.) is also helpful.
4. **Practical Implications:**  The method’s computational efficiency and competitive performance make it a practical alternative to more expensive methods like neural embeddings. The paper clearly highlights this benefit.
5. **Detailed Parameter Exploration:** The experiments exploring the impact of dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements provide a valuable resource for users of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the MEN relatedness test is a standard benchmark, the evaluation could be strengthened by including more diverse tasks and datasets. Evaluating on tasks beyond semantic relatedness (e.g., word similarity, analogy) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s capabilities.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error bounds (δ) associated with the PoP method. While they provide some discussion related to existing theoretical work, a more rigorous analysis would be beneficial. The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma could be made more explicit.
3. **Sensitivity to Parameter Settings:** The results indicate a sensitivity to parameter settings (e.g., the number of non-zero elements), and finding optimal settings might require significant experimentation. More guidance on parameter selection based on dataset characteristics would be helpful.
4. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are made, it would be beneficial to include results from more recent and state-of-the-art baselines, including newer neural embedding techniques or more advanced count-based methods.  The field has rapidly evolved since some of the cited baselines.
5. **Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s r:** The justification for using Kendall’s τb initially, and then switching to Pearson’s r after PPMI transformation, feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more thorough explanation of the statistical properties of each measure in relation to the projected spaces would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Analysis:** Do you have any plans to develop a more formal theoretical analysis of the PoP method, including error bounds and convergence properties?
2. **Parameter Selection:** Could you provide more specific guidance on how to select the optimal parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) for different types of datasets and tasks?
3. **Computational Cost Breakdown:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of PoP compared to other methods, such as neural embeddings and SVD-based techniques?
4. **Dataset Dependency:**  Do you anticipate the optimal parameter settings to be significantly different across various domains and corpora? If so, how might one adapt the method accordingly?
5. **Generalization to other weighting schemes:** Have you experimented with other weighting schemes besides PPMI? If so, how did they perform?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* The inclusion of a supplemental material section is a good practice.
* Consider addressing minor grammatical issues for improved clarity. (e.g. “concern-ing” should be “concerning”).
* It would be beneficial to provide example code or a link to a publicly available implementation of the PoP method.
