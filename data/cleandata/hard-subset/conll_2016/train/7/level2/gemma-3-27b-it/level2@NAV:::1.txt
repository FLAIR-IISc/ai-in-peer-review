## Review of “Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction”

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – utilizing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value – allows for the application of weighting methods like PPMI *after* projection, something previously unavailable with standard random projection methods. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, while highlighting the computational efficiency of their approach.

The primary contribution of this work is the PoP method itself, enabling the combination of the scalability of random projections with the discriminative power of weighting techniques. A secondary, yet significant, contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of PoP's effectiveness, supported by a well-defined experimental setup and comparative results against RI and count-based methods. Finally, the detailed exploration of parameter settings (dimensionality and sparsity) and their impact on performance provides valuable insights into the method’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of using a random projection matrix with E(R) > 0 is a clear and innovative departure from existing random projection techniques.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation using a standard benchmark (MEN) and demonstrates competitive results. The inclusion of ablation studies varying dimensionality and sparsity is commendable.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  The authors convincingly argue for and demonstrate the efficiency of PoP, particularly concerning the application of PPMI to low-dimensional spaces. This is a substantial practical benefit.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The exploration of parameter sensitivity and the discussion of potential theoretical underpinnings (though not fully developed) are positive aspects.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The method is explained relatively clearly, with a helpful mathematical formulation and justification for design choices.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Grounding:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of a complete theoretical justification, this remains a significant limitation. Further investigation into the properties of the proposed projection and its relationship to existing random projection theory would strengthen the work.
*   **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single benchmark (MEN).  Exploring performance on other semantic similarity tasks and potentially different datasets would enhance the generality of the findings.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper reveals that performance is sensitive to parameter choices (s, dimensionality). Providing more guidance or a principled approach for selecting these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Kendall’s τb Justification:** The initial justification for using Kendall’s τb feels somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from a more robust explanation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential theoretical connections between PoP and existing random projection theory, specifically regarding the properties of the distribution resulting from E(R) > 0?
2.  Do you have preliminary results on other semantic similarity benchmarks beyond MEN?
3.  What strategies would you recommend for automatically tuning the 's' parameter in PoP, given its sensitivity and the trade-offs discussed in Section 3.3?

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to semantic space construction. The core idea is novel and well-supported by empirical results. While the lack of a complete theoretical foundation and the limited scope of evaluation are weaknesses, the potential benefits of PoP – particularly its efficiency and the ability to leverage PPMI – make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the identified weaknesses and responding to the questions above. Encouraging the authors to pursue the theoretical underpinnings and broader evaluation would likely lead to a stronger and more impactful follow-up work.
