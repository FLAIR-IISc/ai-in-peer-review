## Review for CONLL 2016: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The core idea is to utilize random projection matrices with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), enabling the application of weighting methods like PPMI to the resulting spaces. The work claims to achieve competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art methods, while being significantly more efficient.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution lies in the novel design of the random projection method, specifically the use of matrices with E(R) > 0. This allows for the subsequent application of weighting techniques, a capability lacking in traditional random projection methods like RI and RRI. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration that PoP, when combined with PPMI, achieves results comparable to strong baselines on the MEN relatedness test. Finally, the work provides a detailed analysis of the method's parameters and behaviour, including the impact of dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements in the projection vectors. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of using random projections with E(R) > 0 to facilitate weighting is a genuinely novel contribution. It addresses a significant limitation of previous random projection techniques, bridging the gap between efficiency and discriminative power. This overcomes a known issue with standard random projections that prevent the application of weighting schemes.
2. **Efficiency and Scalability:** The paper convincingly argues that PoP is computationally efficient due to its reliance on random projections and its ability to operate on lower-dimensional spaces. The claim of building models in seconds or minutes, versus hours or days for neural embeddings, is a strong selling point. This is especially important for resource-constrained environments.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the MEN relatedness test demonstrate that PoP, when coupled with PPMI, achieves competitive performance. The inclusion of comparisons to established methods (though limited) lends credibility to the claims. The detailed analysis of different parameter settings (dimensionality, non-zero elements) provides valuable insights into the method’s behaviour.
4. **Clear Explanation and Motivation:** The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the underlying principles and motivations behind PoP. The connection to the broader context of distributional semantics, the limitations of count-based models, and the benefits of weighting are clearly articulated. The discussion of the theoretical underpinnings, even if incomplete, is commendable.
5. **Detailed Analysis of Parameters:** The exploration of the parameters (dimensionality and number of non-zero elements) provides valuable guidance for practical application and sheds light on the trade-offs involved. The observed relationship between sparsity, orthogonality, and performance is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is largely confined to the MEN relatedness test. While this provides a baseline, it is insufficient to fully assess the generalizability of the method. Evaluating PoP on a broader range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word analogy, semantic text similarity) and potentially on downstream NLP tasks (e.g., text classification, named entity recognition) would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to other methods is somewhat limited. While PPMI-based methods are mentioned, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art random projection methods *with* weighting schemes (if they exist) would be beneficial.  Specifically, it’s unclear whether the gains are solely due to the E(R)>0 property, or a combination of factors.
3. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a detailed mathematical account for the observed performance. While theoretical justifications for random projections exist, a more rigorous analysis of the specific properties of PoP, and how they relate to its effectiveness, would be desirable. The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and the discussion of δ are promising but require further development.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the parameter analysis is detailed, it also highlights the sensitivity of the method to parameters like *s* (number of non-zero elements).  More guidance on how to select optimal parameters for different datasets or tasks would be helpful. The discussion about the relationship between sparsity and distortion could be expanded.
5. **Presentation of Results:** The figures (especially Figure 1 and Figure 4) could be improved for clarity. Clearer labelling of axes, more descriptive captions, and consistent use of visual elements would enhance readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you explored alternative similarity measures beyond Kendall’s τb and Pearson’s r, particularly those designed for non-Gaussian distributions?
2. Could you elaborate on the potential for applying derandomization techniques to further improve the performance of PoP, and provide any preliminary results in this direction?
3. Were any attempts made to optimize the choice of *α* (currently fixed at 0.5)? What is the sensitivity of performance to this parameter?
4. How does the performance of PoP degrade with very large vocabularies? Are there any scalability limitations that emerge as the vocabulary size increases?
5. What is the computational cost associated with building the PPMI weighting matrix for PoP-constructed models compared to building it for traditional count-based models? A more quantitative comparison would be beneficial.

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to Jurafsky and Martin (2015) as a source for PMI explanation seems unusual given its focus.
* The phrase "P6⊥" in Figure 6's caption is unclear and should be clarified.



Overall, this paper presents a promising new approach to semantic space construction. The novelty of the idea, combined with the empirical results and detailed analysis, makes a strong case for its potential. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the evaluation scope and providing a more rigorous theoretical justification, would significantly strengthen the contribution.  The paper is currently of good quality and worthy of acceptance, contingent on addressing the questions and suggestions provided.
