Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme for building distributional semantic spaces with two key twists: (i) the projection vectors are non-negative (E[R] > 0), which allows standard weighting schemes such as PPMI to be applied after projection; and (ii) Kendall’s τb is used for similarity in the non-Gaussian projected space, while Pearson’s r is used after PPMI when distributions become approximately Gaussian. The method is incremental and computationally lightweight. On MEN, PoP+PPMI+Pearson reportedly reaches ≈0.75 Spearman, approaching strong baselines while being faster to build.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) Empirical demonstration that post-projection PPMI on a positive-only random projection yields competitive relatedness performance at much lower computational cost than dense count-based PPMI or neural embeddings.
2) A careful empirical study of parameterization (projection dimensionality m, sparsity s/#nonzeros) and similarity choices (Kendall pre-PPMI, Pearson post-PPMI), with variance across random seeds.
3) A practical, incremental construction scheme akin to random indexing that favors efficiency and memory footprint, with evidence of superiority over standard RI on MEN.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency and simplicity: The approach is easy to implement, requires no training beyond generating sparse random index vectors, and supports streaming/incremental updates. Applying PPMI on low-dimensional projected matrices is much cheaper than on full co-occurrence matrices.
- Solid empirical gains over RI and count-based baselines: Across window sizes, PoP outperforms RI and raw counts; with PPMI, it attains ρ≈0.75 on MEN, comparable to popular neural baselines reported in prior work. The paper also reports stability across 10 runs and examines variance, which is valuable for randomized methods.
- Sensible choice of similarity metrics: The rationale for using Kendall’s τb in the non-Gaussian projected space, and Pearson’s r after PPMI (when the distribution becomes more Gaussian-like), is clear and supported by histograms and results.
- Parameter exploration: The paper examines how m and the number of nonzero positions affect performance (including the risk of non-orthogonal collisions), providing guidance (e.g., small k for PPMI, slightly larger k for unweighted PoP).
- Clarity of motivation: The link from heavy-tailed counts to the need for weighting, and the desire to keep the benefits of random projection while regaining the ability to apply PPMI, is well articulated.

Weaknesses
- Limited and narrow evaluation: Results are reported only on MEN using UKWaC windows. There is no evaluation on other standard benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353 similarity/relatedness splits, Rare Words) or downstream tasks. Comparisons to strong baselines (tuned SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with best practices) on the same corpus/settings are missing. The claim of competitiveness would be more convincing with a broader, controlled evaluation and statistical significance testing.
- Novelty relative to existing sketching/hashing methods: PoP’s construction—summing small positive random “index” vectors per context—is very close to feature hashing and the Count-Min Sketch (CMS) for co-occurrence matrices (multiple hash functions, additive positive updates). Those approaches also yield E[R]>0 and support post-sketch weighting on approximate counts, with known error bounds. The paper does not discuss or compare to hashing/CMS (e.g., Weinberger et al., Cormode & Muthukrishnan). Without situating PoP in that literature, the novelty of “PPMI-enabled projections” is unclear.
- Theoretical underpinnings are thin: The paper acknowledges the lack of analysis and bounds. Given close ties to CMS/feature hashing, existing guarantees could likely be leveraged to characterize error/bias in marginals and PMI after projection. As is, claims rest on empirical results from a single benchmark.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility details: Equation (2) and the precise distribution/discretization of tij (e.g., “⌊U^α⌋”) need clearer specification; in practice this seems to yield mostly 1’s—how often do larger integers occur and do they matter? How is s chosen as n and m change to ensure at least one nonzero per row? The computational complexity and wall-clock costs for τb vs cosine at scale (e.g., nearest neighbor search) are not quantified. PPMI on projected features changes the interpretation of marginals; the potential bias from collisions is not analyzed. Finally, results such as the “0.78” are mentioned without full experimental detail.
- Fairness and tuning: Many choices (window sizes, filtering, scaling of PMI, m, k) appear tuned on MEN without a separate development set, which risks overfitting. Confidence intervals or significance tests for differences between methods are not provided.

Questions to authors
- Relationship to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch: In what ways does PoP differ from hashing/CMS with multiple hash functions and positive updates? Could you add baselines where co-occurrence matrices are built via CMS/feature hashing and then PPMI (and SVD) is computed on the sketch? This would clarify novelty and might enable theoretical error bounds.
- Projection distribution details: Please specify precisely how tij are sampled and discretized (range of possible integer values, expected number of nonzeros k, role of α). Did you experiment with α or alternative magnitude distributions, and what was the impact?
- PPMI on projected spaces: How do you interpret the marginals computed over m pseudo-features? Can you estimate the bias introduced by collisions (e.g., via controlled simulations) and its effect on PMI/PPMI?
- Efficiency measurements: Can you report wall-clock times and memory for building models and computing PPMI (and, if applicable, SVD) compared to dense PPMI and SGNS on the same hardware/corpus, across vocab sizes and m?
- Scalability of τb: For large vocabularies (e.g., 100k+), how do you compute similarities efficiently? Did you try cosine or Pearson prior to PPMI as a speed-quality trade-off, or approximate NN methods?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a simple and promising practical approach that delivers competitive relatedness scores with much lower computational overhead, and it offers useful empirical insights. However, the evaluation is too narrow, theoretical grounding is limited, and the relationship to established sketching/hashing methods is not discussed. Addressing these points—especially by adding broader comparisons and clarifying ties to CMS/feature hashing—would substantially strengthen the submission.
