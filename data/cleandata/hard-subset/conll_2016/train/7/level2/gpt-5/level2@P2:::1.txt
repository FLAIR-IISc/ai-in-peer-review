Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method to construct low-dimensional word vectors using a sparse projection matrix with strictly non-negative entries (E[R] > 0). Because the projected space remains non-negative, the authors argue that association-weighting schemes such as PPMI can be applied after projection, thus combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI.
- The paper advocates Kendall’s τb for similarity in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and Pearson correlation (or cosine) after PPMI (Gaussian-like) transformation.
- Empirically, on MEN, PoP alone outperforms RI and a simple count baseline, and PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75 Spearman’s ρ, claimed to be comparable to several published methods, at a fraction of the computational cost. The paper also explores sensitivity to dimensionality m and sparsity s.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency and incremental construction: PoP keeps the appealing properties of random indexing (streamable, simple, and fast), and the argument that PPMI can be applied after projection (because the space is nonnegative) is practically compelling. The paper identifies a real pain point in PPMI computation (dense, high-dim matrices) and offers a reasonable workaround by applying PPMI in a compressed space.
- Empirical signal with ablations: The experiments, while limited in scope, are careful about randomization effects (10 runs), and explore key hyperparameters (m, number of non-zeros) and similarity choices (Kendall vs Pearson post-PPMI). The observed monotonic improvement with m for PoP+PPMI and the variance shrinkage are informative.
- Clear intuition about distributions: The discussion linking the distributional shape of representations (long-tailed counts, Gaussianization by log/PPMI) to choice of similarity (Kendall vs Pearson) is well argued and borne out by the reported results.
- Simplicity: The projection rule is straightforward to implement, requires no training, and is potentially attractive as a pre-processing “sketch” for more costly pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines: Results are only on MEN. There is no direct in-paper comparison against strong, widely used baselines trained on the same corpus (e.g., SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD, HPCA/Hellinger PCA) nor against other sketching baselines. Claims of being “comparable to SOTA” rely on external reported numbers. This weakens the significance claim.
- Missing comparison to established sketching/hashing methods: The approach is conceptually close to feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch for co-occurrence matrices (positive-only, incremental, collision-prone) followed by PPMI. Prior NLP work has used CM-sketch to compress co-occurrence counts and then apply weighting. Without a head-to-head comparison and citation of that line of work, novelty is unclear.
- Semantics of PPMI after projection: PMI is a function of joint and marginal probabilities of word-context events. After random aggregation of many contexts into m “pseudo-contexts,” the theoretical meaning of PPMI becomes murky. The paper argues empirical usefulness but does not analyze bias/variance introduced by collisions, nor whether particular choices (α, s) systematically distort PMI estimates. This limits the methodological justification.
- Similarity computation practicality: Using Kendall’s τb for similarities in the raw PoP space is O(m log m) per pair. For large-vocabulary retrieval or nearest neighbor search this is likely impractical, and the paper does not provide timing comparisons vs cosine or Pearson nor an ANN strategy. The efficiency narrative would be stronger with runtime/memory benchmarks end-to-end (construction + weighting + similarity).
- Incomplete theoretical grounding and some questionable statements: The paper acknowledges the lack of theoretical guarantees. Additionally, the claim that RI yields row/column sums of exactly zero (hence PPMI undefined) is not strictly correct; the more accurate issue is that projected entries can be negative and thus not interpretable as probabilities. The choice of tij distribution (floor(1/U^α) with α=0.5) lacks justification beyond “works in practice.”

Questions to authors
- Prior art: How does PoP compare empirically and conceptually to Count-Min Sketch or feature hashing applied to word-context matrices, followed by PPMI (and optionally SVD)? Please add citations and a direct comparison. What (if anything) does the specific random weight distribution buy over binary hashing?
- Semantics of PPMI post-projection: Can you formalize the bias induced in PMI estimates by random aggregation, as a function of collision rate and distributional sparsity? Is α=0.5 motivated by variance stabilization or collision-robustness? Have you tried α=0 (binary) or constant weights?
- Efficiency: Please report wall-clock times and memory footprints for (a) count+PPMI, (b) RI, (c) PoP, and (d) PoP+PPMI on the same hardware and corpus. Include the cost of similarity computation for retrieval over a large vocabulary.
- Generalization across tasks: How does PoP perform on other standard benchmarks (WS353, SimLex-999, RW, SimVerb-3500) and on an analogy or downstream task? Does the advantage persist beyond MEN?
- Sensitivity to preprocessing: You report improved scores with stopword removal and frequency cutoffs. How sensitive are results to these choices? Can the method benefit from syntactic contexts?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include a stronger set of baselines on the same corpus (SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD, HPCA) and report significance tests on multiple runs. This will substantiate the “comparable to SOTA” claim.
- Clarify the mathematical properties of the projection matrix: its expected value, variance, and how s and m jointly control collision probability; relate to Johnson–Lindenstrauss-type bounds where possible. Even partial bounds or concentration arguments would help.
- Provide an ablation of PPMI-after-projection vs PPMI-before-projection-then-projection (if feasible) to separate the effects of PPMI and PoP.
- Report cosine vs Pearson in PoP+PPMI for completeness, and consider using standard ANN methods for scalable similarity search.
- Release code and exact settings (m, s, α, context definitions) to improve reproducibility.

Overall, the idea of enabling post-projection PPMI via positive-only random projections is practical and shows promise. However, the empirical scope and the positioning relative to existing sketching/hashing approaches need to be significantly strengthened, and the theoretical/semantic underpinnings of “PPMI on random pseudo-contexts” should be clarified.
