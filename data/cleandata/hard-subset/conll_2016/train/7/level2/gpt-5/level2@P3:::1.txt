Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing word embeddings that uses nonnegative, sparse projection vectors (E[R] > 0), enabling post-hoc weighting with PPMI on the projected space. Similarity is computed with Kendall’s τb in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and with Pearson’s r after PPMI, arguing that the PPMI transformation produces approximately Gaussian coordinates. The work evaluates PoP on MEN using UKWaC, reports comparisons to count-based vectors and Random Indexing (RI), and analyzes the effect of projection dimensionality m and sparsity s.

As seen by this reviewer, the main contributions are:
1) Methodological: introducing positive-only random projections that preserve nonnegativity and allow efficient, post-hoc PPMI weighting directly in the low-dimensional space.
2) Empirical: showing that PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains MEN ρ ≈ 0.75 (up to 0.78 with tuning) while being incremental and much faster than training neural embeddings or computing PPMI on full count spaces; and providing a careful parameter study (m, s, nonzeros).
3) Similarity-choice rationale: connecting distributional assumptions to similarity measures (Kendall’s τb in non-Gaussian PoP, Pearson’s r after PPMI).

Strengths
- Elegant idea with practical impact: Using E[R] > 0 resolves a central limitation of RI-like methods (incompatibility with PMI/PPMI on the projected space). This enables a powerful combination: randomized dimensionality reduction plus discriminative weighting, at very low cost. The formulation is simple (Eq. 2), incremental, and easily implemented.
- Solid empirical improvements over RI and count-based on MEN: Across window sizes, PoP consistently outperforms RI and raw counts (Figure 1), with PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaching ρ ≈ 0.75 (Figure 2) and up to 0.787 with tuning. The paper reports averages over 10 runs and explores variance (Figures 4–5), which is good practice for randomized methods.
- Parameter analysis and actionable guidance: The study of m and the number of nonzero entries sheds light on the trade-offs, including the non-orthogonality issue (Figure 6) and practical defaults (e.g., 1–2 nonzeros for PPMI, larger m reduces variance).
- Coherent statistical perspective: The argument that PPMI acts as a power/variance-stabilizing transform yielding approximately Gaussian coordinates, motivating Pearson’s r post-PPMI, is well-articulated and empirically supported (Figure 2 and the histograms in Figure 3).
- Efficiency and practicality: The approach is incremental, requires no training over parameters, and computes PPMI on small m (100–5000) instead of the full context space, making it attractive for large-scale or streaming scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and comparability: Results are only on MEN relatedness with UKWaC. There is no evaluation on other standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words) or downstream tasks, and no controlled comparison against strong baselines on the same corpus (e.g., SGNS/CBOW, GloVe, PPMI+SVD). The claim of being comparable to state-of-the-art heavily relies on cross-paper numbers with differing training data and preprocessing.
- Theoretical underpinnings are thin: The paper explicitly lacks a bound or characterization for the error δ of PoP projections or conditions under which PPMI on projected features approximates PPMI on counts. While the pragmatic stance is reasonable, even a partial argument (e.g., moments, concentration, or connections to CountSketch/feature hashing) would strengthen the contribution.
- Questionable statement about RI and PPMI: The argument that RI yields row/column sums that are “always 0” and thus PPMI is undefined appears too strong; in finite samples, sums need not be exactly zero. A more precise derivation or empirical demonstration is needed to substantiate that PPMI is unusable on RI or to clarify under which RI variants this holds.
- Similarity computation cost and scalability details: Kendall’s τb is O(m log m) per pair. With large vocabularies and nearest-neighbor queries, practical latency vs. cosine (O(m)) is unclear. The paper asserts efficiency but provides no wall-clock time or memory benchmarks versus RI, count+PPMI, or SGNS/GloVe.
- PPMI semantics after projection: Computing PPMI on projected dimensions mixes original context features. The paper acknowledges PPMI’s role as a variance-stabilizing transform, but a discussion of how projection affects the probabilistic interpretation of PPMI (and whether some information is irretrievably entangled) would be valuable.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the response)
- Clarify Eq. 2: tij = floor(1/U^α)? What is the exact distribution and why α = 0.5? How sensitive are results to α? Are the integer magnitudes essential or would fixed positive weights suffice?
- Provide controlled baselines on the same corpus and preprocessing: SGNS (skip-gram with negative sampling), CBOW, and GloVe; also PPMI+SVD on counts. How do scores and resource usage compare when training on UKWaC with identical windows and vocabulary?
- Runtime and memory: Report wall-clock times and peak memory for building models and computing similarities (PoP, PoP+PPMI, RI, count+PPMI, SGNS), for representative vocabulary sizes and m.
- RI and PPMI: Please justify or refine the claim that row/column sums in RI are “always 0,” making PPMI undefined. Under common RI settings (e.g., ±1 with sparsity), are the sums provably zero, or is the issue numerical instability rather than undefined probabilities?
- Choice of similarity: For raw PoP, Kendall’s τb is used; after PPMI, Pearson’s r. How do cosine and Spearman compare in both regimes? Any accuracy/latency considerations for large-scale retrieval?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Expand evaluation to at least two additional intrinsic benchmarks and add a downstream task (e.g., word similarity beyond MEN, and a simple text classification or semantic similarity task). Include controlled comparisons to SGNS/CBOW/GloVe and count+PPMI(+SVD) on the same corpus with matched settings.
- Add efficiency benchmarks (build time, query time, memory) and scalability analysis for τb vs. cosine.
- Tighten the discussion on RI and PPMI, either via a derivation or an empirical demonstration, and clarify the interpretation of PPMI on projected features.

Optional but beneficial:
- Provide partial theoretical analysis (e.g., expected marginals after projection, variance as a function of m and s, or connections to feature hashing and Johnson–Lindenstrauss-type intuitions).
- Evaluate on an additional corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) to test robustness.
- Release code and exact preprocessing details to facilitate replication (the supplemental note is appreciated).

Overall assessment
The method is simple, novel, and practically appealing, with clear gains over RI and strong efficiency advantages. The empirical section is promising but too narrow to substantiate comparability to state-of-the-art. With broader evaluation, controlled baselines, and clarified claims about RI/PPMI and similarity costs, this work could make a valuable contribution.
