

Summary: The paper introduces "positive-only projection" (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 (unlike previous methods that use E(R) = 0). This allows for application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces. The authors claim PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural network-based methods.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel random projection method that enables application of weighting techniques like PPMI to random projection-based semantic spaces.
2. Demonstrated computational efficiency (seconds/minutes for model construction vs. hours/days for neural methods) while achieving competitive performance.
3. Theoretical justification for using Kendall's τb as the similarity measure in non-Gaussian spaces.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods (inability to apply PPMI due to E(R) = 0) and provides a simple yet effective solution. The core idea is well-motivated and addresses a practical problem in semantic space construction.
2. The computational efficiency claim is compelling and well-supported by the paper's discussion of the method's incremental construction and low dimensionality. The demonstration that models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days is a significant practical advantage.
3. The empirical results are well-presented, with clear comparisons between PoP, RI, and count-based methods. The paper demonstrates that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models across various context window sizes, with PoP+PPMI achieving 0.75 on MEN.
4. The theoretical justification for using Kendall's τb as the similarity measure in non-Gaussian spaces is sound and well-explained, addressing a key concern about the appropriateness of similarity measures.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim requires stronger justification. While the paper states that previous methods use E(R) = 0, it doesn't thoroughly compare with existing random projection methods to demonstrate that PoP is truly novel in this aspect. A more comprehensive literature review would strengthen this claim.
2. The performance comparison to state-of-the-art methods is insufficient. The paper claims "competitive results" but doesn't provide a direct comparison to other leading methods (e.g., GloVe, fastText, or other count-based methods with PPMI+SVD) on MEN or other benchmarks. Without this, it's difficult to assess the true significance of the 0.75 score.
3. The computational efficiency claim lacks concrete measurements. The paper states that PoP is "incredibly faster" but doesn't provide actual runtime measurements or memory usage comparisons to other methods, making it difficult to evaluate this claim objectively.
4. The paper doesn't explain how PPMI is applied to PoP-constructed models in sufficient detail. The claim that PPMI is "much faster" on PoP models due to lower dimensionality is important but lacks quantitative support.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of PoP with existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) beyond just RI? Specifically, how does PoP differ from methods that might already use E(R) > 0?
2. Can you provide actual runtime measurements for PoP compared to other methods (e.g., GloVe, fastText, or PPMI+SVD) on the same hardware? This would help validate the computational efficiency claim.
3. Please provide a comparison table showing PoP+PPMI performance on MEN alongside state-of-the-art methods from recent literature (e.g., Chen & de Melo 2015, Tsvetkov et al. 2015). This would clarify how competitive PoP is.
4. Could you elaborate on the implementation of PPMI on PoP-constructed vectors? Specifically, how does the low dimensionality of PoP vectors affect the PPMI calculation compared to standard count-based models?

Overall, this is an interesting paper that presents a potentially useful technique for semantic space construction. The core idea is sound and the computational efficiency claim is compelling, but the paper would benefit from stronger novelty justification, more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and concrete evidence to support the computational efficiency claim. With these improvements, PoP could make a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics.
