

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 rather than the standard E(R) = 0. The main contributions are: (1) PoP enables efficient construction of semantic spaces that can be weighted using PPMI, unlike previous random projection methods; (2) PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN) without neural network training costs; and (3) the paper provides systematic parameter analysis for optimal implementation.

Strengths: The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to semantic space construction that addresses a clear limitation in existing random projection methods. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that PoP+PPMI outperforms RI and count-based models, with the authors showing how PoP-constructed spaces can achieve competitive results with dramatically reduced computational requirements (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural methods). The systematic parameter analysis (dimensionality, non-zero elements) provides valuable practical guidance for implementation.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim of "comparable to state-of-the-art" is problematic as it only compares against methods from 2014-2015 (e.g., Chen & de Melo 2015), missing recent advances like contextualized embeddings (BERT, ELMo) that have set new benchmarks. The theoretical justification for using E(R) > 0 is insufficiently developed, with the authors acknowledging they cannot provide detailed mathematical bounds for the error δ. Additionally, the paper lacks comparison with more recent efficient methods like fastText, which would strengthen the novelty claim.

The paper makes a compelling case for PoP's computational efficiency and practical utility, but the claims about performance relative to current state-of-the-art require significant strengthening through updated comparisons. The method's potential for resource-constrained applications is promising, but the authors should clarify how PoP differs from approaches that might achieve similar benefits through other means.

The paper would benefit from: (1) updated comparisons with recent top-performing methods, (2) deeper theoretical analysis of why E(R) > 0 enables PPMI application, and (3) discussion of how PoP might integrate with modern NLP pipelines. The core idea has merit, but the presentation needs to better position it within current research trends.
