

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive expectation (E(R) > 0), enabling application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces. The main contributions are: (1) a random projection technique that maintains positive vector sums, allowing PPMI application; (2) empirical demonstration that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) with significantly lower computational cost; (3) analysis of parameter effects on PoP's performance.

Strengths: The paper identifies a clear limitation in existing random projection methods (inability to apply PPMI due to E(R)=0) and proposes a simple but effective solution. The computational efficiency claim is well-supported with evidence that models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing PoP with RI and count-based models across multiple parameter settings, with consistent performance improvements. The explanation for using Kendall's τb instead of cosine is well-motivated given the non-Gaussian nature of PoP-constructed spaces. The demonstration that PoP-constructed spaces can be transformed using PPMI to achieve performance comparable to neural methods is a significant practical contribution.

Weaknesses: The theoretical justification for why E(R) > 0 enables PPMI application is insufficiently developed. The paper states that vectors have positive sums but does not explain how this mathematical property specifically enables the PPMI calculation as defined in Equation 3. The comparison with state-of-the-art neural methods is limited; the authors claim "comparable performance" but don't provide direct comparisons with recent neural methods that might have achieved higher scores on MEN. The counterintuitive performance decrease observed when dimensionality exceeds 2000 for unweighted PoP models needs more thorough analysis and explanation. The paper lacks sufficient detail on reproducibility aspects, particularly regarding the random nature of PoP - the authors report average performance over 10 runs but don't provide enough information about result variance. The claim that PoP achieves "comparable performance to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" is unsubstantiated without specific scores from recent methods.

Questions to Authors: Could the authors provide more detailed comparisons with recent neural methods (e.g., those from 2015-2016) that might have surpassed the 0.75 score reported on MEN? The paper mentions achieving 0.78 with fine-tuning but doesn't detail the specific procedures. Could the authors provide these details to help assess the practical benefits of their approach? The paper states that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on MEN, but doesn't compare this score to recent neural methods that might have achieved higher scores (e.g., 0.80+). Could the authors clarify how their method compares to the current state-of-the-art in the field?
