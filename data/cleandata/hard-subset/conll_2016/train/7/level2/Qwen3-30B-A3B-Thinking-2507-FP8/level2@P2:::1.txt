

Summary: The paper proposes "positive-only projection" (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 instead of the typical E(R) = 0. The authors claim PoP enables the application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces, which previous random projection methods couldn't support. They report that PoP+PPMI achieves 0.75 average score on MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art methods but with much lower computational cost.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel random projection technique (PoP) that preserves the ability to apply weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting semantic spaces.
2. An efficient approach to semantic space construction that achieves competitive performance without neural network training.
3. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI can match state-of-the-art performance while being computationally much more efficient.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a genuine technical limitation in existing random projection methods (E(R)=0 preventing PPMI application) and proposes a clear solution (E(R)>0). This addresses a real problem in the field, as the authors correctly note that previous methods like RI cannot use PPMI weighting.
2. The computational efficiency claim is well-supported with evidence showing models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days, which is a significant practical advantage for many applications.
3. The paper provides thorough empirical validation across different parameter settings (dimensionality, non-zero elements) and demonstrates consistent improvement over RI and count-based models, particularly when combined with PPMI.
4. The method is well-motivated by the need to overcome limitations of heavy-tailed distributions in language data, which is a well-established challenge in distributional semantics.

Weaknesses:
1. The claimed novelty is overstated. The key innovation (using E(R)>0) is a simple technical modification that doesn't seem to represent a significant conceptual advance over existing work. The paper fails to properly contextualize how this differs from prior work that could have used positive random projections (e.g., sparse random projections with positive entries). The authors cite RI, RRI, ISA, and RMI but don't explain why they couldn't have used positive random projections in those methods.
2. The performance claims are not sufficiently contextualized against the state-of-the-art for 2016. The paper compares to RI and count-based models but doesn't directly compare to neural network methods that were state-of-the-art at the time (e.g., GloVe, word2vec). The claimed 0.75 MEN score is presented as comparable to "state-of-the-art," but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this score is actually competitive with contemporary neural methods.
3. The theoretical justification is weak. The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the error bounds (δ), which is a significant drawback for a method based on random projections. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and other theoretical foundations for random projections are well-established, and the absence of such justification for PoP weakens the paper's contribution.
4. The choice of similarity measure (Kendall's τb vs. Pearson) is not well-justified. The authors claim τb is appropriate because PoP spaces have non-Gaussian distributions, but then they use Pearson for PPMI-transformed spaces without adequately explaining why the transformation to Gaussian-like distributions would make Pearson superior. The paper doesn't provide empirical evidence comparing different similarity measures for PoP spaces.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the performance of PoP+PPMI compare to contemporary neural methods like GloVe (2014) or word2vec (2013) on MEN and other standard benchmarks?
2. Can you provide the exact MEN scores for the state-of-the-art methods you claim to be comparable to, and the specific parameter settings used for those comparisons?
3. What is the theoretical basis for the claim that PoP preserves the distribution of the original space (as claimed in Figure 3d), and how does this differ from other random projection methods?
