**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a novel technique called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. PoP is a randomized algorithm that uses random projections to reduce the dimensionality of vectors, similar to existing methods like Random Indexing (RI). However, PoP uses a different type of random projection matrix that allows for the application of weighting techniques like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting vectors. The authors evaluate the performance of PoP on the MEN relatedness test and demonstrate that it achieves competitive results with neural embedding techniques without the need for time-consuming training.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Introduction of the PoP technique**: The paper introduces a new method for constructing semantic spaces that combines the efficiency of random projections with the ability to apply weighting techniques like PPMI.
2. **Evaluation of PoP on the MEN relatedness test**: The authors demonstrate that PoP achieves competitive results with neural embedding techniques on the MEN relatedness test, with an average score of 0.75.
3. **Analysis of the effects of PoP parameters on performance**: The paper investigates the impact of various parameters, such as the dimensionality of the index vectors and the number of non-zero elements, on the performance of PoP.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficient and scalable**: PoP is a randomized algorithm that can be computed efficiently and scaled to large datasets.
2. **Competitive performance**: PoP achieves competitive results with neural embedding techniques on the MEN relatedness test.
3. **Flexibility**: PoP allows for the application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting vectors.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper does not provide a detailed mathematical account of the PoP technique, which may limit its understanding and adoption.
2. **Sensitivity to parameters**: The performance of PoP is sensitive to various parameters, such as the dimensionality of the index vectors and the number of non-zero elements, which may require careful tuning.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates PoP on the MEN relatedness test, which may not be representative of other tasks or datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed mathematical account of the PoP technique and its underlying assumptions?
2. How do you plan to address the sensitivity of PoP to various parameters, and what are the implications for its practical application?
3. Can you evaluate PoP on other tasks or datasets to demonstrate its generalizability and robustness?
