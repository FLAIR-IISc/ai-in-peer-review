## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The core idea – using a random projection matrix with a positive expected value – allows for the application of weighting methods like PPMI *after* projection, a key limitation of previous random projection methods. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving scores comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, while highlighting the computational efficiency of their approach.

The primary contribution of this work is the PoP method itself, enabling PPMI weighting on spaces created via random projection. This is a significant step forward as it bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of weighted models. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation, demonstrating that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves strong results on a standard semantic relatedness benchmark. Finally, the detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity (dimensionality and sparsity) provides valuable insights into the method's behavior.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed method is clearly explained and motivated, addressing a known limitation of existing techniques. The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing a standard dataset and comparing against relevant baselines. The thorough analysis of the impact of different parameters (dimensionality and sparsity) is particularly commendable, offering practical guidance for implementation. The authors also acknowledge the randomized nature of the algorithm and discuss potential limitations.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the performance is competitive, the improvement over existing methods isn't dramatically presented. A more detailed comparison, perhaps including runtime comparisons, would strengthen the claims of efficiency. The theoretical justification for PoP remains limited, relying heavily on empirical observation. While acknowledging this, a more in-depth discussion of the underlying mathematical principles would be beneficial. The discussion of the relationship between PPMI and Gaussian distributions feels somewhat disconnected and could be more tightly integrated with the core contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed runtime comparison between PoP+PPMI and other competitive methods (e.g., SVD-based approaches, neural embeddings) on the MEN dataset?
2.  The paper mentions potential theoretical work to justify PoP. Could you elaborate on the specific areas of theoretical investigation you plan to pursue?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of PoP to the choice of the α parameter in Equation 2? Was any experimentation done to optimize this value?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clear. The inclusion of a supplemental material section is appreciated. The figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts and results. Overall, this is a promising contribution with the potential to significantly impact the field of semantic space construction. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
