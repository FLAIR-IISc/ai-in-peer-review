**Summary of the Paper**
This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel and efficient method for constructing semantic vector spaces. The method is based on random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), ensuring scalability and incremental processing. The core innovation lies in using a projection matrix with only non-negative entries, resulting in a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), which contrasts with traditional random projection methods where E(R) = 0. This critical property allows for the application of effective weighting schemes, most notably Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), to the vectors *after* they have been projected into a low-dimensional space. This two-stage process—efficient dimensionality reduction followed by a fast weighting step on the compact vectors—yields a computationally inexpensive pipeline. The work demonstrates empirically that PoP-constructed models, when combined with PPMI, achieve performance on the MEN word relatedness benchmark that is competitive with state-of-the-art systems, but with significantly lower computational overhead.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A novel random projection method that enables post-projection weighting.** The primary contribution is the design of a random projection scheme that produces low-dimensional vectors amenable to subsequent transformations like PPMI. Previous methods based on zero-mean projections make such weighting mathematically undefined. This work provides a key technical solution to combine the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI.
2.  **An efficient and scalable pipeline for creating high-quality word embeddings.** The work presents a complete methodology (PoP+PPMI) that serves as a practical and compelling alternative to computationally intensive approaches. It avoids both the expensive factorization of large, dense PPMI matrices (e.g., via SVD) and the lengthy training times required for neural network-based embeddings, while achieving comparable results.
3.  **An empirical analysis of similarity measures for different projected spaces.** The paper provides a well-reasoned motivation and empirical validation for using specific similarity metrics tailored to the statistical properties of the vector spaces. It advocates for Kendall's τb in the raw non-Gaussian PoP space and shows the superiority of Pearson's correlation coefficient after the PPMI transformation induces a more Gaussian-like distribution.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Computational Efficiency and Simplicity.** The proposed method is conceptually simple, building on the established framework of incremental random indexing. Its main practical advantage is its efficiency. By enabling PPMI to be applied to a low-dimensional matrix (e.g., dimensionality of 1000s) instead of a massive vocabulary-by-context matrix (dimensionality of 100,000s or millions), it drastically reduces both time and memory requirements. The claim of building models in "seconds or minutes" makes this a highly attractive method for practical applications.
2.  **Strong and Well-Presented Empirical Results.** The evaluation is thorough and effectively validates the paper's central claims. The experiments clearly show the stepwise improvement from baseline count/RI models to the final PoP+PPMI system on a standard benchmark task (MEN). The performance (ρ ≈ 0.75) is convincingly shown to be in the range of top-performing systems.
3.  **Clear Exposition and Insightful Analysis.** The paper is very well-written, with a clear motivation and explanation of the method's mechanics. The analysis in Section 3.2, particularly the use of histograms in Figure 3 to visualize the distributional properties of vectors from different methods, provides an excellent and intuitive explanation for *why* PoP enables effective PPMI weighting while RI does not.
4.  **Novelty and Practical Significance.** The work successfully addresses a key limitation of prior random projection techniques, bridging a gap between efficient but less discriminative methods (like RI) and powerful but expensive methods (like SVD on PPMI or neural embeddings). This provides the community with a valuable and practical tool in the word embedding landscape.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The paper candidly acknowledges the absence of a formal mathematical analysis of the PoP projection (Section 2.3). While the empirical results are strong, a foundational paper introducing a new random projection technique would be significantly strengthened by theoretical support, such as an analysis of its distortion properties, analogous to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections. This remains a notable omission.
2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope.** The empirical evaluation is focused on a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on MEN) and a single corpus. To make a more robust claim of being "comparable to state-of-the-art," the resulting embeddings should be evaluated on a broader suite of tasks, including word analogy (e.g., Google analogy dataset) or synonym detection (e.g., TOEFL), and potentially on downstream NLP applications like text classification or named entity recognition. This would provide a more comprehensive picture of the embeddings' quality and generalizability.
3.  **Ambiguity in Parameter Selection.** The investigation into the model's hyperparameters (dimensionality `m` and sparsity `s`) in Section 3.3 reveals a complex interaction, where the optimal setting for `s` depends on whether a subsequent PPMI transformation is used. While the paper provides recommendations, it would benefit from more concrete guidance or heuristics for setting these parameters, potentially by linking them to properties of the input data (e.g., vocabulary size). The current analysis suggests that careful tuning may be required to achieve optimal performance.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The computational efficiency is a primary motivation for the work. Could the paper provide concrete measurements of wall-clock time and memory usage for building the UKWaC models using (a) the proposed PoP+PPMI pipeline and (b) a widely-used baseline like SVD on a dense PPMI matrix or a popular neural implementation like word2vec? Such a comparison would quantify the claimed efficiency gains.
2.  The justification for using Pearson's correlation relies on the PPMI-transformed space being "Gaussian-like." Could this property be measured quantitatively (e.g., using a normality test like Shapiro-Wilk on a sample of vectors)? This could provide stronger evidence for the choice of similarity metric and further illuminate the effect of the PPMI transformation.
3.  The paper suggests that PoP-constructed models could serve as a low-dimensional input for neural embedding methods. Have any preliminary experiments been conducted in this direction? This appears to be a very promising application that could leverage PoP's efficiency to potentially accelerate the training or improve the final performance of neural models.
