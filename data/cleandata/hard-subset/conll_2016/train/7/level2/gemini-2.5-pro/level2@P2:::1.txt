### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings based on random projections. The key insight is to use a random projection matrix `R` where all non-zero elements are positive integers, ensuring that `E(R) > 0`. This contrasts with standard Random Indexing (RI), where `E(R) = 0`.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables post-hoc weighting.** By using positive-only projections, the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain the non-negative characteristic of the original count vectors. This property makes it possible to apply standard and powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly to the low-dimensional space, a procedure that is mathematically undefined for traditional RI-based embeddings.
2.  **A highly efficient pipeline for creating high-quality embeddings.** The proposed method combines the computational efficiency and incrementality of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI weighting. The authors demonstrate that this combination achieves performance on a word relatedness task that is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, while drastically reducing the computational cost from potentially days or months to minutes.
3.  **An empirical analysis of non-standard similarity metrics for projected spaces.** The paper explores the use of Kendall's τb as a similarity measure for the non-Gaussian spaces produced by PoP, and subsequently shows that Pearson's correlation is more effective after the PPMI transformation induces a more Gaussian-like distribution. This provides useful insights into the geometry of the resulting embedding spaces.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The core idea of PoP is simple, elegant, and addresses a clear limitation of existing random projection methods. It cleverly bridges the gap between efficient but less discriminative methods like RI and effective but computationally intensive methods like PPMI+SVD on high-dimensional count matrices. The ability to apply PPMI *after* a massive, efficient dimensionality reduction is a significant and genuinely novel contribution.
2.  **Significant Practical Utility:** The primary motivation for this work—computational efficiency—is extremely well-supported. Applying PPMI requires calculating row and column sums, which is intractable for traditional, very high-dimensional sparse count matrices. By first projecting to a low-dimensional dense space (e.g., m=3000), these calculations become trivial. This makes the power of PPMI accessible for very large corpora or in resource-constrained environments, a substantial practical advantage.
3.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates strong performance on the MEN relatedness benchmark, achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75, which is highly competitive. The authors systematically compare PoP against raw counts and RI, clearly showing its superiority. Furthermore, the analysis of hyperparameters (dimensionality `m`, sparsity `s`) in Section 3.3 is thorough and provides valuable insights into the method's behavior. The visualization of vector component distributions in Figure 3 is particularly effective at illustrating the key differences between count-based, RI, PoP, and PPMI-transformed vectors.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors candidly state in Section 2.3 that they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account" for the properties of their projection. While they draw parallels to RI's early development, the absence of theory is a notable weakness. Unlike Johnson-Lindenstrauss-style results for Gaussian projections, there are no guarantees about how distances or other properties are preserved or transformed by PoP. This leaves the method feeling like a successful and well-motivated heuristic rather than a theoretically principled technique.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The entire empirical evaluation rests on a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, strong claims about creating competitive word embeddings require a broader evaluation. It is unclear how PoP would perform on word similarity tasks (which are distinct from relatedness, e.g., SimLex-999), analogy tasks, or as input features for downstream NLP applications. The lack of this broader comparison makes it difficult to assess the general applicability of the embeddings.
3.  **Indirect Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper claims comparability with neural embedding techniques but does not provide a direct, controlled comparison. The results are compared to numbers reported in other papers, which may use different corpora, preprocessing, or hyperparameters. A stronger evaluation would include results from standard models like word2vec (Skip-gram) or GloVe trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus and vocabulary, providing a direct point of reference for both performance and training time.
4.  **Unclear Guidance on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The analysis in Section 3.3 reveals a complex relationship between hyperparameters. For instance, increasing dimensionality improves performance for PoP+PPMI but degrades it for unweighted PoP (Figure 4). Similarly, increasing the number of non-zero elements helps unweighted PoP but hurts PoP+PPMI (Figure 5). While explanations are offered, the paper lacks clear, principled guidance on how to set these crucial parameters a priori, suggesting that significant tuning may be required.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In footnote 11, you state that SVD truncation on your models results in negligible improvements. This seems to run counter to a large body of work (e.g., Levy et al., 2015) showing that PPMI+SVD is a very strong baseline, often outperforming neural methods. Could you clarify the setup for this experiment? Was SVD applied to the full high-dimensional PPMI matrix or to the low-dimensional PoP+PPMI matrix? If the latter, could this explain the small improvement, as PoP has already performed a form of dimensionality reduction?
2.  Regarding the theoretical properties, while a full proof is a high bar, could you provide more intuition or connections to existing work on non-Gaussian random projections? For example, how does PoP relate to projections based on other stable distributions, and are there any theoretical insights that might explain why it is particularly well-suited to preserving the information needed for a subsequent PPMI transformation?
