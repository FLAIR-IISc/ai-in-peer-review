### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel and efficient method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. The method is based on random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but uses a projection matrix with only non-negative entries. The authors' central claim is that because the projection matrix has a positive expectation, the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain properties of the original count space (e.g., non-negative components, positive sums) that allow for the application of subsequent weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). This is a key advantage over standard RI, where the zero-mean projection makes PPMI undefined. The paper demonstrates empirically that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors results in embeddings that achieve competitive performance on the MEN word relatedness task, while being significantly more computationally efficient than traditional PPMI-based models or neural network approaches.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that is compatible with post-projection weighting.** The core innovation is the use of a positive-only random matrix, which enables the application of powerful weighting functions like PPMI to already low-dimensional vectors. This elegantly bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projection methods and the high performance of count-based weighting schemes.
2.  **An efficient and scalable pipeline for creating high-quality embeddings.** The paper demonstrates a complete pipeline: incremental construction of low-dimensional PoP vectors, followed by a fast PPMI transformation on these vectors. This process avoids the computational bottlenecks of traditional methods, such as SVD on massive co-occurrence matrices or lengthy training of neural networks.
3.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness.** The proposed PoP+PPMI approach is shown to achieve a Spearman correlation of 0.75 on the MEN dataset, a result that is competitive with many state-of-the-art systems. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of appropriate similarity measures for the different vector spaces produced (Kendall’s τb for raw PoP, Pearson’s r for PPMI-transformed PoP).

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Novelty:** The core idea is both simple and highly effective. Modifying the random projection matrix to be positive-only is a small change with a significant and well-motivated consequence: it makes the resulting space amenable to PPMI weighting. This is a novel contribution that directly addresses a known limitation of methods like Random Indexing and provides a clear practical benefit.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper makes a very strong case for the method's efficiency. By design, PoP is incremental and can be constructed in a single pass over a corpus. Crucially, the expensive PPMI calculation is deferred until after the dimensionality has been drastically reduced. This makes the method scalable to very large corpora and vocabularies, which is a significant advantage over both traditional PPMI+SVD models and computationally demanding neural approaches. The claim that models can be built in "seconds or minutes" instead of "hours, days or months" is a compelling practical argument.
3.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are convincing. The method is shown to substantially outperform relevant baselines like raw count vectors and Random Indexing. The final performance on the MEN dataset is very strong and competitive with published results for more complex models. The analysis in Section 3.3, which explores the effects of key hyperparameters like dimensionality and sparsity, adds valuable insight into the method's behavior. Furthermore, the justification for switching from Kendall’s τb to Pearson’s r after the PPMI transformation, supported by the histograms in Figure 3, is a nice piece of analysis that strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors are upfront in acknowledging this (Section 2.3), but it remains a notable weakness. Unlike Gaussian random projections, which are supported by the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, the properties of the PoP transformation are not formally analyzed. It is not clear what geometric or distributional properties are preserved from the original high-dimensional space. While the empirical success is evident, a theoretical foundation would help explain *why* the method works so well and would provide guarantees on its behavior, potentially guiding future improvements.
2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a single task (word relatedness) using the MEN dataset. While MEN is a standard benchmark, the performance of word embeddings is typically assessed across a broader suite of tasks that measure different aspects of semantic representation, such as word similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and analogical reasoning. Without this broader evaluation, it is difficult to assess the general utility of PoP embeddings and how they compare to other methods on tasks that require capturing more fine-grained similarity rather than topical relatedness.
3.  **Absence of Direct Comparison to Neural Embeddings:** The paper repeatedly motivates the work by contrasting its efficiency with neural embedding methods like word2vec. However, the results table does not include a direct comparison to a popular neural model (e.g., SGNS) trained on the same UKWaC corpus. While the authors cite other papers to claim their performance is "comparable," a direct, controlled comparison would be much more powerful. Given that well-tuned PPMI and SGNS models often perform similarly on relatedness tasks, including SGNS as a baseline would help to more precisely position PoP+PPMI within the current landscape of word embedding techniques.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance of `PoP+PPMI` (Figure 5) surprisingly degrades as the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors increases. The paper suggests this may be due to uncontrolled "distortions." Could you elaborate on this hypothesis? Is it related to the loss of orthogonality between the random index vectors, and if so, how does that interact with the subsequent PPMI weighting?
2.  The distribution for the non-zero elements of the projection matrix is given as `b 1Uα c` with α=0.5. Was this specific form chosen based on empirical tuning, or is there a more theoretical motivation? Have you experimented with other distributions for the positive values (e.g., a constant value of 1, or sampling from a different distribution)?
3.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation for what the PoP projection is accomplishing? Figure 3 suggests it largely preserves the original long-tailed distribution. Does this mean it can be viewed as a form of randomized feature sampling, and if so, how does this view reconcile with the use of Kendall's τb as a similarity measure?
