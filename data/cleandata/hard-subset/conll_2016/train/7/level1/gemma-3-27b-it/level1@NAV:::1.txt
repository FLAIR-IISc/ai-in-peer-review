## Review of “Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction”

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – utilizing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value – is designed to allow for subsequent application of weighting methods like PPMI, which are incompatible with traditional random projection methods relying on zero-mean matrices. The authors demonstrate that combining PoP with PPMI achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms on the MEN relatedness test.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that constructing random projections with a positive expected value enables the application of weighting schemes, effectively bridging the gap between efficient random projection techniques and the performance gains offered by weighting and dimensionality reduction methods. This is a significant insight, as it unlocks the potential for improving existing random projection methods without the computational cost of neural embeddings. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this technique, showcasing competitive performance on a standard semantic similarity benchmark. The detailed exploration of parameter sensitivities (dimensionality, non-zero element count) and their impact on performance is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  The core idea of utilizing a positive-mean random projection matrix to enable PPMI weighting is a genuinely novel approach. While random projections are well-established, this specific modification and its implications haven’t been explored before.
2. **Practical Efficiency:** The method promises significant computational efficiency. The authors highlight the speed of model construction – seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural embeddings – which is a compelling advantage.
3. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art on the MEN test demonstrates the viability and effectiveness of the approach. This is not just a theoretically interesting idea; it delivers results.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The thorough investigation of different parameters (dimensionality, sparsity) and their effect on performance, supported by visualizations (Figure 4, 5, 6), strengthens the credibility of the findings.
5. **Clear Explanation & Motivation:** The paper provides a well-structured and clear explanation of the motivation behind PoP, its mathematical foundation, and its relation to existing work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely based on the MEN relatedness test. While this is a standard benchmark, performance on other semantic similarity tasks, or even different NLP tasks leveraging these embeddings, would be beneficial to demonstrate broader applicability.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis of the method. While acknowledging this is honest, a more detailed discussion of potential theoretical limitations and avenues for future work would be helpful. The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma feels somewhat superficial.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity & Heuristics:** The optimal parameter settings (especially 's') seem somewhat heuristic, relying on empirical observation.  A more systematic approach to parameter tuning or a method for automatically determining optimal parameters would enhance the usability of the technique.
4. **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to RI is helpful, a more thorough comparison to simpler, non-randomized dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., truncated SVD directly on count matrices) would provide a clearer understanding of the benefits offered by PoP.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you explored the performance of PoP on datasets beyond the MEN test, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., different types of semantic relations)?
2.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of the current theoretical understanding of PoP, and what specific aspects require further investigation?
3.   Could you provide a more detailed explanation or justification for the chosen value of α (0.5)? Was this value explored through experimentation, or is it based on theoretical considerations?
4.  What is the computational cost of computing the PPMI weights in PoP compared to applying PPMI directly to the original count matrix? A quantitative comparison would be valuable.
