## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:** This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing word embeddings based on random projections. Unlike traditional random projection methods with zero-mean matrices, PoP utilizes matrices with positive expected values, enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* projection. The authors claim PoP offers a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings, achieving comparable performance on semantic relatedness tasks. They present experimental results on the MEN dataset, demonstrating competitive performance when combined with PPMI and Kendall’s τb or Pearson correlation.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **The Positive-Only Projection Technique:** The core idea of using random projections with positive expected values to allow post-projection weighting is the most significant contribution. This circumvents a limitation of existing random projection methods and opens up new possibilities for combining efficient dimensionality reduction with discriminative weighting schemes.
2. **Demonstration of PPMI Applicability:**  Successfully applying PPMI weighting to PoP-constructed spaces is a notable contribution. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this combination yields competitive results compared to established methods.
3. **Empirical Evaluation & Parameter Exploration:** The thorough experimentation with different parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and similarity measures provides valuable insights into the behavior of the PoP method and offers practical guidelines for its application.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of PoP is genuinely novel within the context of random projection methods.  By addressing the limitation of existing techniques regarding post-projection weighting, it opens up a potentially impactful avenue for building efficient and effective semantic spaces. The promise of comparable performance to neural methods *without* the associated computational cost is compelling.
2. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper presents the PoP method with remarkable clarity. The mathematical formulation is well-explained, and the connection to existing techniques (RI, PPMI) is clearly established. The step-by-step explanation of the method’s construction and the rationale behind using Kendall's τb is particularly helpful.
3. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing a standard benchmark dataset (MEN).  The ablation study exploring different parameter settings (dimensionality, sparsity) and similarity measures adds significant value, demonstrating the sensitivity and robustness of the method. The inclusion of figures illustrating the distribution of weights is insightful.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The speed advantages of PoP over neural embedding methods, as highlighted in the introduction, are very appealing for resource-constrained scenarios or applications requiring rapid model building. The ability to achieve competitive results with significantly less computational overhead is a strong selling point.
5. **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the need for further theoretical analysis and openly discuss the impact of parameter choices on performance, demonstrating a critical awareness of the method’s limitations.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the comparison to RI and count-based models is useful, the paper lacks a direct comparison to more recent and competitive *non-neural* word embedding methods (e.g., fastText with pre-trained vectors, word2vec trained on very large corpora). Demonstrating superiority or at least comparability to these methods would significantly strengthen the paper.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN dataset and a single corpus (UKWaC).  Expanding the evaluation to other semantic relatedness datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) and potentially different corpora would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s generalizability.
3. **Theoretical Justification:** The authors correctly identify the lack of a rigorous theoretical foundation for PoP. While acknowledging the parallel to other randomized algorithms, a more in-depth discussion of the expected error bounds and the impact of the positive-only projection on the resulting semantic space would be beneficial. The discussion about the parameter ‘s’ feels somewhat hand-wavy.
4. **Overemphasis on Implementation Details:** The detailed discussion of PPMI transformation and the exploration of different similarity measures, while insightful, sometimes overshadows the core contribution of the PoP projection itself. The paper could benefit from a more focused presentation of the main idea and its implications.
5. **Figure Quality:** Some of the figures (e.g., Figure 3) are difficult to interpret due to small font sizes and lack of clear labeling.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparison:**  Have you compared PoP+PPMI to fastText or other strong non-neural baselines trained on large corpora? If not, can you provide a justification for why these baselines were not included in the evaluation?
2. **Generalizability:** Have you evaluated PoP on datasets beyond MEN?  If so, what were the results? If not, do you anticipate any significant performance differences on other semantic relatedness tasks?
3. **Theoretical Analysis:** What are the ongoing efforts to provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for PoP?  Specifically, what are the key challenges in deriving error bounds and characterizing the properties of the resulting semantic space?
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:**  Can you provide more guidelines for selecting the optimal value of ‘s’ based on the characteristics of the input corpus (e.g., sparsity, vocabulary size)?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written, but some minor editing for clarity and conciseness would be helpful.
* The "Supplemental Material" section is a bit vague. It would be helpful to specify exactly what will be included (e.g., code, pre-trained embeddings).
* The connection between the choice of Kendall’s τb and the non-Gaussian distribution of the projected space is well-explained, but could be further elaborated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this paper presents a novel and promising approach to constructing word embeddings. The core idea of PoP is innovative, the method is well-explained, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The potential for significant computational savings compared to neural methods makes this work particularly valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, especially a stronger comparison to relevant baselines and a more detailed discussion of the theoretical aspects of the method.
