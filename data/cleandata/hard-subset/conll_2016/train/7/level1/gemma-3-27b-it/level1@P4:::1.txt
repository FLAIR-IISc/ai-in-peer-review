## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing word embeddings based on random projections with a non-zero expected value. The core idea is to enable the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* the projection step, something precluded by traditional random projection methods with E(R) = 0. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, while highlighting the method’s computational efficiency.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novelty in Random Projection:** The key contribution lies in shifting the focus of random projections from preserving distances (as in RI, RRI, etc.) to enabling post-projection weighting techniques like PPMI.  This is a conceptually distinct approach that overcomes a limitation of existing random projection methods.
2. **Practical Performance & Efficiency:** The paper demonstrates that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on a standard semantic similarity benchmark (MEN). The reported efficiency gains – building models in seconds or minutes versus hours or days – are significant.
3. **Empirical Analysis of Parameter Space:** The detailed exploration of parameters like dimensionality (m) and the number of non-zero elements (s) in the projection matrix, and the analysis of their impact on performance, constitutes a valuable contribution. This provides practical guidance for applying the method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Clear Limitation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem with existing random projection methods – their incompatibility with post-projection weighting. PoP directly addresses this limitation, opening up new possibilities for leveraging these techniques.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of PoP, comparing it to related methods (RI, count-based models) and conducting ablation studies by varying key parameters. The use of multiple metrics (Spearman’s rank correlation) and visualizations (histograms) strengthens the analysis.
3. **Detailed Technical Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically grounded description of the PoP method, including the rationale behind using a non-zero expected value for the projection matrix. The connection to PPMI weighting and the discussion of Kendall's τb distance are well-explained.
4. **Demonstrated Efficiency:** The claimed computational efficiency is a significant strength. While not quantified with precise timings, the contrast with training neural embeddings is compelling. This makes PoP a potentially valuable tool for resource-constrained scenarios.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Randomness:** The authors acknowledge the inherent randomness of the method and discuss potential sources of error. The exploration of how parameters influence variance and the connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma concepts adds depth to the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on the MEN relatedness test. While this is a standard benchmark, a more diverse set of tasks (e.g., word analogy, semantic similarity with different datasets) would provide a more robust assessment of the method’s generalizability.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The presented results lack statistical significance testing. While the authors report average performance and ranges, formal tests (e.g., t-tests) would be necessary to definitively demonstrate the superiority of PoP over baseline methods.
3. **Overly Detailed Discussion in Section 2.3:** The section on “Some Delineation of the PoP Method” (2.3) is somewhat tangential to the core contribution of the paper. While the discussion of randomized algorithms is relevant, it feels overly lengthy and detailed, potentially distracting from the main technical novelty. The mathematical justifications for δ are not fully developed and are presented as future work.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The optimal parameter settings (s and m) appear to be quite sensitive to the dataset and task. A more systematic analysis of how these parameters should be tuned across different scenarios would be beneficial. The somewhat contradictory recommendations (0.002 < s for direct use vs. 1 or 2 for PPMI weighting) could be clarified.
5. **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods:** The comparison focuses on established methods (RI, count-based models). A comparison to more recent, high-performing embedding techniques (e.g., fastText, GloVe) would provide a more contemporary benchmark.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:** Beyond the MEN test, what is the performance of PoP on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353)?
2. **Statistical Significance:** Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claim that PoP outperforms the baseline methods?
3. **Parameter Tuning:**  Can you provide more guidance on how to choose the optimal values for *s* and *m* given a new dataset or task?  Is there a principled way to determine these values, or is it primarily empirical?
4. **Computational Complexity:** While you mention the efficiency of PoP, can you provide more concrete estimates of the computational cost (e.g., training time) for different parameter settings and dataset sizes?
5. **Comparison to fastText/GloVe:**  How does PoP compare to more recent embedding methods like fastText and GloVe in terms of performance and computational cost?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of a Supplemental Material section is a positive step.
*   Consider shortening Section 2.3 to focus on the key takeaways relevant to PoP.
*   The discussion of Kendall’s τb in relation to Gaussian projections could be more concise.
