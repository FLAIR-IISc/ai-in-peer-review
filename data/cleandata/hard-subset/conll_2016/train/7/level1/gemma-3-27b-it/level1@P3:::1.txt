## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections with a positive bias. The core idea – using a random projection matrix with a positive expected value to enable subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI – is intriguing and addresses a limitation of standard random projection techniques. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, showcasing the potential of this approach.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **The PoP Projection Method:** The introduction of a random projection scheme that intentionally avoids a zero-centered distribution, opening the door for applying weighting techniques normally incompatible with standard random projections (RI, RRI, etc.). This is the most significant contribution.
2. **Demonstration of PPMI Compatibility:**  Successfully applying PPMI weighting to spaces constructed with PoP and showing that this combination achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods.  This empirically validates the core premise of the method.
3. **Detailed Exploration of Parameter Sensitivity:** The extensive experiments and analysis surrounding the impact of dimensionality and the 's' parameter (controlling the proportion of positive elements in the projection matrix) provide valuable insights into the behavior of PoP and practical guidance for its use. This goes beyond simply presenting results and dives into *why* the method works (or doesn't).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Insight:** The core idea of using a non-zero-centered random projection is a genuinely novel approach.  It directly addresses a known limitation of existing random projection methods, which are excellent for dimensionality reduction but struggle with subsequent weighting steps. This makes a significant contribution to the landscape of efficient semantic space construction.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The results on the MEN dataset are convincing. Achieving comparable performance to more computationally expensive methods (neural embeddings, SVD-weighted count models) while being significantly faster is a compelling argument for the method’s practical utility.  The inclusion of error margins in the performance plots (Figures 4 & 5) is excellent practice and strengthens the claims.
3. **Thorough Analysis and Parameter Tuning:** The paper doesn’t simply present results; it meticulously analyzes the impact of different parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and provides clear recommendations for practical application.  The discussion around the trade-offs between sparsity and performance is particularly insightful.  The exploration of orthogonality of index vectors and its impact on performance is a strong point.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is explained in detail, the experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The mathematical notation is appropriate and the connection to established concepts like PPMI and Kendall's τb is well-explained.
5. **Acknowledgement of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a formal theoretical analysis for the method’s error bounds and the need for further investigation. This honesty is commendable and demonstrates a responsible approach to research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, evaluating on only one task limits the generalizability of the findings. Semantic similarity is just one application of word embeddings. Demonstrating the effectiveness of PoP on other downstream tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would significantly strengthen the claims.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:**  The comparison primarily focuses on RI and more complex methods. It would be valuable to include a comparison to simpler baselines like simple count-based embeddings with dimensionality reduction via PCA. This would provide a clearer understanding of the specific benefits of PoP over more straightforward approaches.
3. **Overly Detailed Justification & Repetition:** While the detailed exploration of the method is generally a strength, certain sections (particularly the discussion of PPMI and the motivation behind using Kendall's τb) become somewhat repetitive and verbose. Streamlining these sections would improve readability. The level of detail regarding historical context (e.g., citing Karp 1991 in the context of randomized algorithms) is occasionally excessive.
4. **The Role of 's' Requires Further Clarification:** The explanation of how the 's' parameter affects the distribution and performance is somewhat complex and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. Connecting it more explicitly to the trade-off between sparsity and information retention would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalization to Other Tasks:**  Have you experimented with PoP on other downstream NLP tasks beyond semantic similarity? If so, what were the results? If not, do you have plans to do so?
2.  **Impact of Corpus Size:** How does the performance of PoP scale with the size of the training corpus? Do you observe diminishing returns as the corpus grows larger?
3.  **Alternative Similarity Measures:** You mention exploring other similarity measures. Have you investigated the performance of other metrics besides Pearson's correlation and Kendall’s τb after PPMI weighting? Which performed best?
4.  **Computational Complexity Analysis:** Although you mention efficiency gains, a more formal analysis of the computational complexity of PoP compared to other methods (RI, SVD, neural embeddings) would be valuable.




**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 3 is very helpful for visualizing the effect of the PPMI transformation.
*   The mention of a publicly available implementation is excellent and should be emphasized.
*   The phrasing around "worst and best performances" in relation to Figure 4 could be clarified. The shaded area represents variance, not necessarily the absolute worst/best results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and innovative paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of semantic space construction. The core idea of PoP is well-motivated, thoroughly investigated, and empirically validated. While some improvements could be made through broader evaluation and streamlined presentation, the paper represents a valuable contribution and is highly recommended for acceptance. I recommend a strong accept, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above during the author response phase.
