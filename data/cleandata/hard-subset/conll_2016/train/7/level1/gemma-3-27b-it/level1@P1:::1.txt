## Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. PoP leverages random projections, but crucially differs from existing methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) by employing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0). This allows for the application of weighting techniques like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) after projection, which is not possible with traditional random projection methods where E(R) = 0.  The authors demonstrate that PoP, when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms, but with significantly improved computational efficiency.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling post-projection weighting:** The core idea of using a random projection matrix with E(R) > 0 to unlock the possibility of applying weighting schemes like PPMI *after* dimensionality reduction is the most significant contribution. This bridges a gap between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of weighting methods.
2. **Demonstration of competitive performance with substantial efficiency gains:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that PoP + PPMI achieves results on par with more computationally expensive methods while being orders of magnitude faster. This is a practical and valuable contribution, particularly for resource-constrained settings or large-scale datasets.
3. **Empirical analysis of PoP parameters:** The thorough experimentation exploring the impact of dimensionality (m) and sparsity (s) on performance provides valuable insights into the behavior of the method and offers practical guidance for its application.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Idea:** The fundamental idea of modifying the random projection matrix to enable post-projection weighting is genuinely novel and provides a fresh perspective on combining random projection techniques with count-based methods.  The rationale behind this approach – overcoming the limitations of traditional random projections regarding weighting – is well-articulated.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a clear and well-structured evaluation using the MEN relatedness test. The results consistently show that PoP + PPMI performs competitively with established methods, and the ablation studies (varying dimensionality and sparsity) strengthen the validity of the findings.  The inclusion of performance comparisons with RI is particularly insightful.
3. **Computational Efficiency:** The emphasis on computational efficiency is a major strength. The authors effectively convey the potential of PoP to significantly reduce processing time and resource requirements compared to neural embedding methods or SVD-based approaches. This is a critical advantage for many NLP tasks.
4. **Detailed Parameter Analysis:** The analysis of parameters *m* and *s*, including the discussion of trade-offs and the impact on performance, demonstrates a deep understanding of the method's behavior and provides valuable practical guidance to potential users. The visualization of the distribution of weights (Figure 3) is helpful for understanding the underlying mechanism.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background information and explain the technical details of the method clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, the paper acknowledges the lack of a formal theoretical analysis of PoP’s properties and the bounds on its error (δ).  This is a significant weakness.  Without theoretical grounding, it's difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the method. While acknowledging prior work lacking theoretical justification, this does not negate the need for such analysis here.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on the MEN relatedness test. While this is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see the method evaluated on a broader range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy completion) and potentially on downstream NLP applications.
3. **Over-Reliance on PPMI:**  The paper heavily emphasizes the combination of PoP with PPMI. While this combination yields good results, it would be valuable to explore the performance of PoP with other weighting schemes or even without any weighting at all, to better understand the intrinsic capabilities of the projection itself. A more systematic exploration of different weighting options could reveal further advantages of PoP.
4. **Discussion of Non-Orthogonality:** While the discussion of non-orthogonal index vectors is insightful, it feels somewhat disconnected from the main narrative. A clearer explanation of how non-orthogonality impacts performance and how the choice of *s* can be used to manage this effect would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Analysis:** What are the authors' plans for providing a theoretical justification for PoP? Specifically, can they offer any insights into the expected error bounds or the conditions under which PoP is likely to perform well?
2. **Generalizability:** Have the authors investigated the performance of PoP on other semantic similarity datasets or downstream NLP tasks? If so, could they share those results?
3. **Alternative Weighting Schemes:** Have the authors experimented with weighting schemes other than PPMI? If so, what were the results? If not, are they planning to explore this in future work?
4. **Impact of Corpus Size:** How does the size of the training corpus affect the optimal parameters (m and s) for PoP?  Is there a relationship between corpus size and the dimensionality required to achieve good performance?
5. **Orthogonality Control:**  Could the authors elaborate on potential methods for explicitly controlling the orthogonality of the index vectors during the PoP process, beyond simply adjusting the parameter *s*?



**Additional Comments:**

* The supplemental material commitment is appreciated.
* The notation is generally clear, but the use of symbols like “P6⊥” could be explained more explicitly.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, alongside the conclusions.
