**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel technique called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The PoP method is based on random projections and uses a transformation matrix with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), which allows for weighting transformations such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to be applied to the constructed spaces. The authors evaluate the performance of PoP-constructed models in the MEN relatedness test and show that they achieve competitive results compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Random Projection Technique**: The authors propose a new random projection technique, PoP, which uses a transformation matrix with a positive expected value, allowing for weighting transformations to be applied to the constructed spaces.
2. **Efficient Construction of Semantic Spaces**: The PoP method is shown to be highly scalable and computationally efficient, making it suitable for large-scale natural language processing tasks.
3. **Competitive Performance in Semantic Similarity Assessment**: The authors demonstrate that PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve competitive results in the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficient Construction of Semantic Spaces**: The PoP method is shown to be highly scalable and computationally efficient, making it suitable for large-scale natural language processing tasks.
2. **Flexibility in Weighting Transformations**: The authors demonstrate that PoP-constructed models can be weighted using various techniques, including PPMI, which enhances their performance in semantic similarity assessment tasks.
3. **Competitive Performance in Semantic Similarity Assessment**: The authors demonstrate that PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve competitive results in the MEN relatedness test, comparable to state-of-the-art methods.
4. **Low-Dimensional Vector Representations**: The PoP method produces low-dimensional vector representations, which can be beneficial for various natural language processing tasks.
5. **Easy to Implement and Extend**: The authors provide a clear and concise description of the PoP method, making it easy to implement and extend.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The authors acknowledge that they are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error caused by replacing deterministic decisions with random ones (δ) in the PoP method.
2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters**: The authors demonstrate that the performance of the PoP method is sensitive to hyperparameters such as the dimensionality of the projected index vectors and the number of non-zero elements in index vectors.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the performance of the PoP method in the MEN relatedness test, which may not be representative of other semantic similarity assessment tasks.
4. **No Comparison to Other Random Projection Techniques**: The authors do not compare the performance of the PoP method to other random projection techniques, such as Random Indexing (RI) or Reflective Random Indexing (RRI).
5. **No Analysis of the Impact of Sparsity**: The authors do not analyze the impact of sparsity on the performance of the PoP method, which may be an important factor in certain natural language processing tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of sparsity on the performance of the PoP method?
2. How does the PoP method compare to other random projection techniques, such as RI or RRI, in terms of performance and efficiency?
3. Can you provide a theoretical justification for the PoP method, including a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ?
4. How does the performance of the PoP method change when using different weighting transformations, such as TF-IDF or word embeddings?
5. Can you provide a more detailed evaluation of the PoP method in other semantic similarity assessment tasks, such as the WordSim353 or the SimLex-999 datasets?
