## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The core idea – using a random projection matrix with a positive expected value – is presented as a way to enable the application of weighting methods like PPMI *after* projection, something precluded by traditional random projection methods with zero-mean matrices. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, achieving scores comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the insightful observation that a positive-biased random projection allows for post-projection weighting techniques like PPMI to be effectively applied. This is a non-trivial point, as it bridges a gap between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of weighted models. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of this approach, showing that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves strong results on a standard semantic similarity benchmark. The detailed analysis of parameter settings (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and their impact on performance is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The concept of using a positive-biased random projection to facilitate post-projection weighting is a genuinely novel contribution. It addresses a limitation of existing random projection techniques and opens up new possibilities for combining their benefits with those of weighting schemes.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a solid empirical evaluation of PoP on the MEN dataset. The results demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance, comparable to more complex approaches. The inclusion of ablation studies (varying parameters) strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Parameter Tuning:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results; it delves into the impact of different parameter settings (dimensionality, sparsity) on performance. This provides valuable insights into the behavior of the method and guides practical application.
4. **Clear Explanation & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind PoP, explaining the limitations of existing methods and how PoP addresses them. The connection to the broader context of distributional semantics and the trade-offs between efficiency and accuracy are well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, the evaluation is somewhat limited. Assessing performance on a wider range of semantic similarity tasks and potentially other NLP tasks would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis of PoP. While empirical results are encouraging, a more formal understanding of the method's properties (e.g., error bounds, convergence behavior) would be highly desirable.
3. **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive. Including simpler baselines (e.g., SVD on raw counts) alongside more sophisticated techniques would provide a clearer picture of PoP's relative advantages.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost difference between applying PPMI to PoP-constructed vectors versus applying it to traditional count-based models, with specific numbers?
2.  You mention the potential for derandomization techniques. Could you provide some concrete examples of how these might be applied to PoP?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the method to the choice of the α parameter in Equation 2? Did you explore different values and their impact on performance?
