### Paper Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings based on random projections. The key innovation is the use of a projection matrix containing only non-negative values (E(R) > 0), in contrast to standard Random Indexing (RI) where the projection matrix has a zero mean (E(R) = 0). This seemingly small modification has a crucial consequence: the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain a count-like, non-Gaussian distribution. This property allows for the application of powerful count-based weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly on the low-dimensional space, a step that is mathematically undefined for vectors produced by standard RI. The authors demonstrate that this PoP+PPMI approach is extremely efficient and achieves performance on the MEN word relatedness benchmark that is competitive with computationally expensive state-of-the-art methods.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and elegant method for enabling post-weighting in random projection models.** The primary contribution is the simple but powerful insight of using a positive-only random projection to bridge the gap between efficient, incremental random projection methods and effective, but computationally demanding, weighting schemes like PPMI. This allows a model to benefit from the speed and scalability of the former and the discriminative power of the latter.
2.  **The development of an efficient yet high-performing word embedding algorithm.** The PoP+PPMI pipeline is a practical and valuable contribution. The authors show it can be constructed in seconds or minutes, a significant advantage over methods requiring days of training or massive matrix factorizations, while still achieving a highly competitive Spearman's ρ of 0.75 on the MEN dataset.
3.  **An insightful empirical analysis of similarity metrics for different vector space distributions.** The paper's investigation into using Kendall’s τb for the native non-Gaussian PoP space and Pearson's r for the PPMI-transformed (and more Gaussian-like) space provides a useful demonstration of how the choice of similarity metric should be adapted to the underlying data distribution.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Simplicity.** The core idea is both simple and highly novel. Modifying the random projection matrix to be non-negative is a small change that unlocks the ability to apply a whole class of well-established weighting techniques (like PPMI) that were previously incompatible with random projection methods. This provides an elegant and effective solution to a known limitation of methods like Random Indexing, combining the best aspects of two distinct families of word space models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results Combined with High Efficiency.** The paper presents a compelling case based on performance and efficiency. Achieving a score of 0.75 on the MEN benchmark is a strong result, placing the method among competitive models of its time. The main strength, however, is that this performance is achieved with incredible efficiency. By applying the expensive PPMI calculation to a matrix of low dimensionality (e.g., 751 words x 1000 dimensions) instead of the full vocabulary x context matrix, the authors present a method that is orders of magnitude faster than its competitors. This trade-off is extremely attractive for practical applications.
3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Analysis.** The evaluation is well-designed and supports the paper's claims effectively. The direct comparison against count-based and RI baselines in Figure 1 clearly establishes the baseline advantage of the PoP projection itself. The subsequent analysis of applying PPMI (Figure 2) demonstrates the central claim of the paper convincingly. Furthermore, the visualization of vector component distributions in Figure 3 provides a clear and intuitive explanation for *why* the method works, showing how PoP preserves the long-tail distribution necessary for PPMI, which then transforms it into a more Gaussian-like shape. The parameter sensitivity analysis in Section 3.3 adds to the paper's reproducibility and practical value.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The authors candidly acknowledge this weakness in Section 2.3, stating they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ and ε for the results obtained by the PoP method". While empirical success is valuable, the absence of any theoretical justification (e.g., a connection to concentration of measure phenomena like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma) leaves the reader wondering what geometric or statistical properties are being preserved by the projection. This makes the method feel more like a well-engineered and successful heuristic rather than a principled technique, and it is a notable weakness for a paper introducing a new projection method.
2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope.** The paper's empirical claims rest entirely on a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While MEN is a standard benchmark, contemporary work on word embeddings typically evaluated on a wider suite of tasks, including synonymy (e.g., WordSim-353, TOEFL) and analogy (e.g., Google analogy dataset). Without results on these other tasks, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the method. The model might be uniquely suited to capturing the topical relatedness prominent in MEN, but it is unclear how it would perform on tasks requiring more fine-grained semantic or syntactic understanding. This limitation weakens the claim of being "comparable to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" in a general sense.
3.  **Indirect Comparison to Competing Models.** The paper claims its results are comparable to neural methods but only cites their reported scores from other papers, without performing a direct, controlled comparison. A more rigorous evaluation would have involved training a benchmark model like word2vec (skip-gram with negative sampling) on the exact same preprocessed UKWaC corpus. Furthermore, a direct comparison to PPMI+SVD, which Levy et al. (2015) showed to be a very strong baseline, would have been particularly informative, as PoP+PPMI can be seen as a highly efficient approximation to it. The current comparison makes it difficult to precisely gauge the performance-cost trade-off against these key competitors.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The distribution for the non-zero elements in the projection matrix is defined as `b1/U^αc`. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the model to this specific choice? Have you experimented with a simpler scheme, such as setting all non-zero elements to a constant value of 1? This would seem to be the simplest way to achieve a positive-only projection.
2.  Your analysis in Figure 5 suggests that for the PoP+PPMI model, sparser index vectors (i.e., fewer non-zero elements) lead to better performance. This is an interesting and somewhat counter-intuitive finding. Do you have a hypothesis that explains this phenomenon?
3.  The core benefit of your method is applying PPMI to a low-dimensional matrix. Could you discuss how PoP+PPMI compares to an alternative pipeline: building the full high-dimensional sparse count matrix, applying PPMI, and *then* applying a standard (zero-mean) random projection to reduce the dimensionality of the resulting dense PPMI matrix? This would also result in a low-dimensional space and might serve as a useful point of comparison.
