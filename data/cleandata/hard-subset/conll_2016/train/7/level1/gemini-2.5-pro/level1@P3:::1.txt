**Reviewer for CONLL_2016**

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel and computationally efficient method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. The method is based on random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but critically uses a projection matrix with only non-negative entries. The key insight is that this property allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be post-processed with standard count-based weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). This is a significant advantage over standard RI, where the zero-mean projection matrix makes PPMI arithmetically undefined. The authors demonstrate that combining PoP with PPMI (PoP+PPMI) achieves performance on the MEN word relatedness benchmark that is competitive with computationally intensive state-of-the-art methods like SVD and neural embeddings, but at a fraction of the cost.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel method for combining random projections with PPMI weighting.** The core contribution is the elegant and effective idea that a positive-only random projection enables the application of powerful weighting schemes like PPMI directly on the low-dimensional representation. This cleverly bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projection methods and the high performance of PPMI-weighted count models.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of an efficient and effective embedding model.** The paper provides compelling evidence that the PoP+PPMI method is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool. It achieves strong results on a standard benchmark while being incremental and significantly faster than competing high-performance methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea.** The paper’s central idea is simple, intuitive, and highly effective. The limitation of RI and similar methods—that the resulting vectors cannot be meaningfully re-weighted with association measures like PPMI—is a well-known bottleneck. The proposed solution of using a positive-only projection is a direct and clever fix to this problem. It effectively synthesizes the strengths of two major families of distributional models: the scalability and incrementality of random projections and the discriminative power of PPMI weighting. This represents a significant and valuable conceptual contribution to the field of distributional semantics.
2.  **Impressive Performance-Efficiency Trade-off.** The authors convincingly show that PoP+PPMI achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.75 on the MEN dataset, which is highly competitive with top-performing systems from the period (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, SVD-on-PPMI). The crucial advantage is that this performance is achieved with dramatically lower computational cost. The method is incremental, does not require expensive matrix factorization (like SVD) or lengthy iterative training (like neural networks), and performs the expensive PPMI calculation on a matrix of much lower dimensionality (e.g., p x 3000 instead of p x |V|). This makes it a very attractive option for resource-constrained settings or applications requiring rapid model construction.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Analysis.** The evaluation is well-structured and provides strong support for the authors' claims. The paper systematically compares PoP to appropriate baselines (raw counts, RI), demonstrating its superiority even without PPMI. The analysis of vector component distributions in Figure 3 offers a clear and insightful justification for the choice of similarity metrics (Kendall's τb for raw PoP, Pearson's r for PoP+PPMI). Furthermore, the hyperparameter studies on dimensionality and sparsity (Figures 4 and 5) provide valuable practical guidance and build confidence in the method's robustness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The authors candidly acknowledge this weakness, but it is a significant one. While RI has a theoretical foundation in the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma (preserving L2 distances), the properties of the PoP projection are not mathematically characterized. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties of the original high-dimensional space are being preserved, and why this preservation is conducive to PPMI weighting. The justification for using Kendall’s τb is largely intuitive rather than formal. Without a stronger theoretical basis, the method feels somewhat like a successful heuristic, and it is difficult to understand its fundamental limitations or predict its behavior beyond the experiments shown.
2.  **Evaluation is Limited to a Single Task.** The entire empirical validation of the method rests on the MEN word relatedness task. While this is a standard benchmark, it primarily evaluates broad topical/thematic similarity. The paper would be substantially stronger if it demonstrated the method's effectiveness on a more diverse set of intrinsic evaluations. For example, performance on word analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) would test for the linear algebraic regularities that are a hallmark of popular neural embeddings. Similarly, performance on synonym selection tasks (e.g., TOEFL) would assess a different facet of semantic similarity. Without such results, it is difficult to assess the generality of PoP+PPMI as a word representation technique.
3.  **Insufficient Contextualization with Respect to Non-negative Methods.** The paper positions PoP against RI, SVD, and neural networks. However, it misses an opportunity to discuss the relationship to a relevant class of methods: non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) and other sparse coding techniques. NMF also produces non-negative, low-dimensional representations from count data and is often interpreted as learning additive, "parts-based" features. A discussion comparing and contrasting PoP with NMF, both conceptually (e.g., interpretability of dimensions) and empirically (performance vs. efficiency), would provide a more complete picture of where PoP fits within the broader landscape of representation learning.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The PPMI calculation requires marginal counts (row and column sums). In your PoP+PPMI method, are these marginals computed from the low-dimensional PoP matrix, or are the true marginals from the original high-dimensional co-occurrence matrix used? If the former, this is an approximation. Could you clarify this detail and comment on the effect of this approximation on the PPMI values?
2.  The results in Figures 4 and 5 show an interesting interaction between dimensionality (m) and sparsity (number of non-zero elements). For PPMI-weighted models, performance degrades as index vectors become denser, while the opposite is true for unweighted models. Could you provide more intuition on why the PPMI transformation benefits from extremely sparse projections (e.g., 2 non-zero elements)?
3.  Given that PoP+PPMI performs so well on a relatedness task, have you conducted any preliminary experiments on analogy tasks (e.g., `king - man + woman`)? The ability to capture such relational structures is a key selling point for neural embeddings, and it would be very interesting to see if your computationally cheaper method also exhibits this property.
