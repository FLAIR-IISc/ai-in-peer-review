### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings incrementally and efficiently. Unlike standard Random Indexing (RI), where the projection matrix has an expected value of zero, PoP uses a sparse random matrix containing only positive integers, resulting in an expected value greater than zero. The primary advantage of this approach is that it allows for the application of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly on the low-dimensional vectors. This is not possible with traditional RI-generated vectors. The authors propose using Kendall’s τb correlation as a similarity metric for the resulting non-Gaussian space, but show that after PPMI transformation, which makes the vector distributions more Gaussian-like, Pearson's correlation is more effective. The experimental evaluation, conducted on the MEN word relatedness task, demonstrates that PoP+PPMI achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods while being significantly more computationally efficient.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables post-projection weighting.** The core contribution is the design of a positive-only random projection matrix. This simple but effective modification to existing random projection techniques allows for the application of powerful association measures like PPMI in the reduced dimensional space, overcoming a key limitation of methods like Random Indexing.
2.  **An efficient and scalable methodology for constructing high-quality word embeddings.** By integrating the computational efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI weighting, the paper presents a practical framework (PoP+PPMI) for building semantic spaces. This approach serves as a compelling alternative to computationally intensive neural network training or large-scale matrix factorization (SVD), as it applies the expensive weighting step only after dimensionality has been drastically reduced.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical analysis of the proposed method.** The paper provides a solid empirical investigation into the method's behavior, including its performance relative to baselines (count-based, RI), the impact of key parameters like vector dimensionality and sparsity, and the choice of similarity metric. The analysis effectively demonstrates the method's viability and offers practical insights for its application.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Practical Significance.** The idea of modifying the random projection matrix to be positive-only in order to enable subsequent PPMI weighting is both novel and insightful. It elegantly combines the benefits of two distinct families of methods: the scalability of random projections and the proven performance of PPMI-based weighting. This presents a significant contribution, offering a highly efficient and scalable tool for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale distributional semantics.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis.** The experimental results are convincing. The paper clearly shows that PoP outperforms its closest baseline (RI) and that the full PoP+PPMI model achieves a score (0.75 on MEN) competitive with top-performing systems. The ablation studies presented in Figures 4 and 5 provide a clear picture of how performance is affected by dimensionality and the sparsity of the projection matrix. Furthermore, the visualization of weight distributions in Figure 3 provides an excellent, intuitive explanation for why PPMI is applicable to PoP vectors and how it reshapes the vector space.
3.  **Clarity and Methodological Soundness.** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation for the work is well-established, and the proposed method is described with sufficient detail for replication. The authors are transparent about the current limitations of the method's theoretical grounding (Section 2.3) but provide a solid empirical foundation for their claims. The connection drawn between the distribution of the projected vectors and the appropriate choice of correlation measure (Kendall's τb for non-Gaussian, Pearson's r for post-PPMI Gaussian-like) is methodologically sound.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding.** The authors acknowledge that a detailed mathematical account for the properties of PoP is currently lacking. While randomized algorithms often precede formal proofs, the absence of theoretical analysis regarding which properties of the original space are preserved (or distorted) by this non-standard projection is a notable weakness. Unlike Johnson-Lindenstrauss-based projections which offer guarantees on preserving pairwise distances, it is unclear what PoP formally accomplishes, making it difficult to reason about its expected behavior beyond the provided empirical results.
2.  **Evaluation is Confined to a Single Task.** The paper's entire empirical validation rests on the MEN word relatedness benchmark. While this is a standard task, robust evaluation of word embeddings typically requires testing across a diverse suite of tasks, including word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353) and syntactic/semantic analogies. Performance on a single relatedness task does not guarantee general utility, as the embeddings might not capture other important linguistic properties. This limited scope of evaluation makes it difficult to assess the general applicability of the proposed method.
3.  **Parameter Sensitivity and Lack of Principled Selection.** The performance of the method appears sensitive to its hyperparameters, particularly the number of non-zero elements in the projection vectors. Figure 5 shows that the optimal setting for this parameter is different for the unweighted PoP and the PPMI-weighted PoP+PPMI variants. The justification provided for this behavior is heuristic. The paper lacks a principled method or strong intuition for setting key parameters like `s` and `α`, which may hinder the method's adoption and reproducibility on new datasets or tasks.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The optimal number of non-zero elements in the index vectors appears to be low (e.g., 2-4) for the PoP+PPMI model. Does this suggest that the primary function of the PoP projection in this pipeline is not to preserve geometric structure (as in RI), but rather to act as a low-dimensional "sketch" or feature hash, whose components can then be effectively re-weighted by PPMI? Could you elaborate on this?
2.  The evaluation is focused exclusively on the MEN relatedness task. Have you conducted preliminary experiments on other tasks, particularly those that measure similarity rather than relatedness (e.g., SimLex-999)? The strong performance of PPMI is often tied to its ability to capture broad topical relatedness, and it would be valuable to know if the PoP+PPMI embeddings generalize to tasks requiring a more fine-grained understanding of semantics.
3.  Could you provide more intuition regarding the choice of Kendall's τb for the unweighted PoP space? While it is justified as a non-parametric correlation measure, standard RI uses cosine similarity, which is computationally cheaper. How does cosine similarity perform on the raw PoP vectors compared to Kendall's τb in your experiments?
