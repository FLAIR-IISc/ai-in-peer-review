- Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP): a random projection with non-negative entries that yields low-dimensional, count-like word vectors.
- Key insight: non-negative projection preserves non-Gaussian, count-like distributions so PPMI weighting can be applied in low-dimensional space.
- PoP+PPMI is extremely efficient and attains competitive performance (Spearman’s ρ ≈ 0.75 on MEN) compared to much costlier methods.
- Contribution: enables post-weighting in random projection models, bridging fast incremental methods and powerful weighting schemes like PPMI.
- Contribution: provides a practical, fast word-embedding pipeline constructible in seconds/minutes while remaining high-performing.
- Contribution: empirical analysis showing appropriate similarity metrics depend on vector distribution (e.g., Kendall’s τb for PoP, Pearson’s r after PPMI).
- Strength: high novelty and simplicity—small change (non-negative projection) unlocks established weighting techniques for RI-style methods.
- Strength: strong empirical results combined with orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains by applying PPMI to a low-dimensional matrix.
- Strength: thorough, illuminating analysis including direct baselines, distribution visualizations, and parameter sensitivity studies.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical grounding or formal guarantees about what geometric/statistical properties the projection preserves.
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope—results reported only on the MEN relatedness task, leaving generality to other tasks unclear.
- Weakness: comparisons to neural and other strong baselines are indirect; no controlled experiments (e.g., word2vec or PPMI+SVD on same corpus).
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the chosen distribution for non-zero projection elements; would constants (e.g., all ones) work?
- Question: why do sparser index vectors improve PoP+PPMI performance—what hypothesis explains this counterintuitive effect?
- Question: how does PoP+PPMI compare to applying PPMI to the full count matrix followed by a standard zero-mean random projection as an alternative pipeline?
