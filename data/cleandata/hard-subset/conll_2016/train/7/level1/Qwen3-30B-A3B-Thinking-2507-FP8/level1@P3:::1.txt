

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 (as opposed to traditional random projection methods with E(R) = 0). The authors claim PoP enables efficient application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting vector spaces, which was previously impossible with methods like Random Indexing (RI). The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. The key insight that random projection matrices with positive expected values (E(R) > 0) enable PPMI weighting, which is not feasible with standard random projection methods.
2. A practical implementation of PoP that achieves competitive semantic similarity performance (0.75 average on MEN test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural network approaches.
3. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI outperforms RI and count-based models while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Technical Insight**: The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods (the inability to use PPMI due to E(R)=0) and provides a simple, effective solution (E(R)>0). This insight has clear practical implications for semantic space construction, as PPMI weighting is known to improve performance in semantic similarity tasks. The authors demonstrate this insight through a well-designed empirical evaluation showing PoP+PPMI's advantages over RI.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates the computational advantages of PoP. The claim that models can be built "in a matter of seconds or minutes" instead of "hours, days or months" is supported by the paper's description of the method's incremental construction process and low dimensionality. For resource-constrained applications, this efficiency could be highly valuable.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The evaluation on the MEN relatedness test is well-executed and appropriately contextualized. The paper shows PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving 0.75 average score, which is comparable to state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies (Figures 1-5) clearly demonstrate PoP's advantages over RI and count-based models, and the parameter analysis (Figures 4-6) provides useful guidance for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The paper claims novelty for using E(R)>0, but this appears to be a rather minor technical adjustment rather than a fundamentally new concept. The authors don't adequately establish that this approach has not been explored before. In fact, the paper cites Kanerva et al. (2000) who proposed RI without mathematical justification, suggesting similar approaches might have been considered previously. The paper needs to better establish why this specific modification to random projection matrices constitutes a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. **Inadequate Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI achieves "comparable results" to "state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" with 0.75 on MEN. However, the best results on MEN are typically around 0.80-0.85 (e.g., from GloVe or fastText). The paper fails to provide a clear comparison to these methods, making it difficult to assess the true competitiveness of the approach. The paper should explicitly compare to current SOTA methods.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper acknowledges it cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of PoP, which is a significant weakness for a method based on random projections. While the authors cite previous work on random projections (Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma), they don't establish theoretical bounds for their specific approach. The paper relies entirely on empirical results without addressing why E(R)>0 enables PPMI weighting from a mathematical perspective.

4. **Overstated Performance Claims**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI "achieves an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms." However, the paper doesn't provide sufficient context for this score. The best results on MEN are typically higher, and the paper doesn't indicate how PoP+PPMI compares to the most recent SOTA methods on this benchmark.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does PoP+PPMI compare to the best published results on MEN (which are typically 0.80-0.85)? The paper's claim that 0.75 is "comparable to state-of-the-art" seems questionable given current benchmarks.

2. The paper states that "for index vector dimensions m ≥ 3000, PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75" but doesn't provide a comparison to other methods at similar dimensionality (e.g., GloVe with 300 dimensions). How does this compare to standard approaches?

3. The paper claims PoP is "highly scalable and computationally efficient" but doesn't provide concrete timing comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. How long does it take to construct a PoP model compared to, say, GloVe or fastText on the same corpus?

4. The paper states that "PoP-constructed models, in contrast to previously suggested random projection-based models, can be still weighted using PPMI." However, it doesn't cite any prior work that attempted this with E(R)=0. Can you clarify why this approach was not previously considered?
