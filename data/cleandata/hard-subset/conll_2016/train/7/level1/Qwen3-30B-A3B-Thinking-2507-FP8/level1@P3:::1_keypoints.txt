- PoP proposes using random projection matrices with positive expected value (E(R)>0) to enable PPMI weighting.

- Implemented PoP achieves competitive semantic similarity (0.75 on MEN) with much lower computational cost than neural approaches.

- Empirically, PoP+PPMI outperforms Random Indexing and count-based models while remaining computationally efficient.

- Novel technical insight: E(R)>0 overcomes the inability of standard random projections to support PPMI.

- Computational efficiency: PoP can build models “in seconds or minutes,” suitable for resource-constrained settings.

- Strong empirical validation: results on MEN, ablation studies, and parameter analyses support the method’s benefits.

- Questionable novelty: E(R)>0 may be a minor tweak and the paper doesn't convincingly show this is a new contribution.

- Inadequate SOTA comparison: the 0.75 MEN score is not compared to top results (typically ~0.80–0.85 from GloVe/fastText).

- Lack of theoretical justification: no error bounds or formal analysis explaining why E(R)>0 enables PPMI.

- Overstated performance claims: claiming parity with state-of-the-art is not sufficiently contextualized or supported.

- How does PoP+PPMI compare directly to best published MEN results (~0.80–0.85)?

- How does PoP performance at m ≥ 3000 compare to standard methods at comparable dimensionalities (e.g., GloVe 300d)?

- Can you provide concrete timing comparisons to GloVe/fastText on the same corpus to substantiate scalability claims?

- Why was PPMI not previously combined with E(R)=0 random projections, and what prior attempts or literature address this?
