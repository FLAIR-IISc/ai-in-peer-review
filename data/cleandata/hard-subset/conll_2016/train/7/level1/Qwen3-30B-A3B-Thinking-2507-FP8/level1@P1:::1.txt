

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces "positive-only projection" (PoP), a technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive expected values (E(R) > 0) rather than the conventional E(R) = 0. The key contribution is demonstrating that this approach enables the application of PPMI weighting to the projected spaces, which is not possible with traditional random projection methods. The authors claim that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 average score on MEN relatedness test) while being computationally efficient. 

The actual contributions of this work are:
1. The PoP method itself, which enables PPMI weighting of projected spaces by using random projection matrices with E(R) > 0.
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks without the computational burden of neural network training.
3. Clear explanation of why traditional random projection methods cannot be used with PPMI (due to E(R) = 0 causing undefined PPMI weights).

## Strengths

1. **Practical computational advantage**: The paper provides compelling evidence that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive results (0.75 on MEN) while being orders of magnitude faster than neural network approaches. The authors correctly note that training neural embeddings can take "hours, days or months," whereas PoP models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes." This addresses a significant pain point in NLP research.

2. **Clear technical insight**: The paper makes a valuable observation about why traditional random projection methods (like RI) cannot be combined with PPMI - because their random matrices have E(R) = 0, making PPMI weights undefined. This is a well-articulated technical distinction that has practical implications for method selection in semantic space construction.

3. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive comparisons across multiple configurations (context window sizes, dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and clearly demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms both unweighted PoP and traditional RI. The visualization in Figure 2 effectively shows how PPMI transformation significantly boosts performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper acknowledges it cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of the PoP method, which is a significant weakness for a computational linguistics conference. Randomized algorithms typically require theoretical guarantees about error bounds, and the absence of this analysis makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the method.

2. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art**: The authors claim results are "comparable to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" but only provide comparisons with RI, count-based models, and PMI-transformed models. They fail to directly compare with modern neural embedding approaches (like GloVe, FastText, or recent transformer-based models) that would provide a more meaningful benchmark.

3. **Questionable similarity measure justification**: The paper claims Kendall's τb is "a reasonable candidate for measuring similarities" because PoP-constructed spaces have "non-Gaussian distribution," but provides only minimal justification. The transition from "non-Gaussian" to "τb is better than cosine" is not well-supported by evidence beyond the authors' own experiments. The paper should provide a more rigorous explanation of why τb is superior to other non-parametric measures.

4. **Insufficient scalability analysis**: While the paper claims high computational efficiency, it doesn't provide concrete runtime measurements or scalability analysis across different corpus sizes. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed efficiency holds for larger, more realistic corpora.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't systematically investigate how different components contribute to the final performance (e.g., how much of the improvement comes from PoP versus PPMI versus the specific similarity measure).

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide concrete runtime measurements comparing PoP+PPMI with neural network training (e.g., GloVe) on the same hardware for the same corpus size? This would help quantify the claimed computational efficiency.

2. How does your method perform on more challenging semantic tasks beyond MEN relatedness (e.g., word analogy, semantic textual similarity)? The current evaluation is limited to a single relatedness test.

3. The paper states "the PPMI transformation is much faster, compared to the one performed on count-based models, due to the low dimensionality of vectors in the PoP-constructed model." Could you provide a computational complexity analysis showing how the time complexity differs between applying PPMI to count-based models versus PoP-constructed models?

4. The paper claims "PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms." Please provide specific comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods (post-2015) to substantiate this claim.

5. The paper states "for index vector dimensions m ≥ 3000, PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75." How does this compare to the performance of standard PPMI+SVD approaches (e.g., the GloVe baseline) when using similar dimensionality (m = 3000)?
