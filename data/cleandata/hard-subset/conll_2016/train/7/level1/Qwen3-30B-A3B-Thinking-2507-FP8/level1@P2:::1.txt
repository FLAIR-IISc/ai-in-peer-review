

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections. The key innovation is using random projection matrices with E(R) > 0 instead of the standard E(R) = 0 approach, enabling subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI. The authors claim PoP-constructed spaces combined with PPMI achieve competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN test) with significantly faster computation than neural methods.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables application of weighting techniques like PPMI to projected spaces, addressing a key limitation of existing methods
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive semantic relatedness performance with dramatically lower computational requirements
3. Analysis of the distributional properties of PoP-constructed spaces and their compatibility with PPMI weighting

## Strengths

1. **Novel solution to a genuine limitation**: The paper identifies a significant gap in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) that cannot apply weighting techniques like PPMI due to their E(R)=0 property. PoP's E(R)>0 approach directly addresses this limitation, which is a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors provide clear evidence that PoP-constructed spaces can undergo PPMI transformation while other methods cannot (Section 2.3), which is a substantial technical insight.

2. **Strong empirical results with practical significance**: The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 average on MEN test (comparable to state-of-the-art), but with computation in "seconds or minutes" instead of "hours, days or months." This is a compelling practical advantage, especially for resource-constrained applications. The results in Figures 1-2 provide convincing evidence that PoP outperforms both count-based models and RI, and that PPMI transformation significantly boosts performance.

3. **Clear explanation of the method's theoretical rationale**: The paper effectively explains why PoP enables PPMI application (Section 2.3) and why Kendall's τb is appropriate for PoP-constructed spaces (Section 2.2). The comparison of vector distributions in Figure 3 is particularly valuable in illustrating how PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution necessary for PPMI application, unlike RI which produces Gaussian distributions with E=0.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper acknowledges they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for error bounds (δ) or theoretical justification for PoP's performance. While they note this is common in similar work (e.g., RI), the absence of theoretical grounding is a significant weakness for a computational linguistics paper. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and similar theoretical results are standard for random projection methods, and their omission makes it difficult to assess the method's fundamental properties.

2. **Insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper claims results are "comparable to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" but only provides vague references to Chen & de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015). For a 2016 conference, the paper should directly compare to methods published in 2015-2016, such as fastText, GloVe, or other recent neural methods. The absence of such a comparison weakens the claim of competitiveness.

3. **Inadequate explanation of similarity measure choice**: The paper uses Kendall's τb for PoP spaces but doesn't sufficiently justify why this is better than other measures like Pearson or cosine. While they explain why it's appropriate for non-Gaussian spaces, they don't provide evidence that τb outperforms other measures in this context. The switch to Pearson's correlation in PPMI-transformed spaces (Figure 2) is noted but not properly contextualized.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" but acknowledges similar methods (RI) later received theoretical justification. What are the authors' plans for theoretically justifying PoP, and how might they bound the error (δ) in projected spaces?

2. The paper claims "competitive results" but doesn't directly compare to recent state-of-the-art methods from 2015-2016. Could the authors provide a direct comparison with methods like fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2016) or GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) to strengthen their competitiveness claim?

3. The paper uses Kendall's τb for PoP spaces but Pearson's correlation for PPMI-transformed spaces. Can the authors provide evidence that τb is indeed superior to other measures for untransformed PoP spaces, or is this choice somewhat arbitrary?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a novel and practical approach to semantic space construction with clear advantages in computational efficiency. The core insight about enabling PPMI application through E(R)>0 is valuable and well-justified. However, the lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods are significant weaknesses that need addressing. With stronger theoretical justification and more direct comparisons, this work could be an excellent contribution to the field. As is, it's a solid but not exceptional submission. I recommend rejection, but would consider acceptance with major revisions addressing the theoretical and comparative weaknesses.
