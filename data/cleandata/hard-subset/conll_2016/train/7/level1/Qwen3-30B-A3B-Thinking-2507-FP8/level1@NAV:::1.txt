

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive elements (E(R) > 0) instead of the typical zero-centered distributions. The key contribution is enabling PPMI weighting on projected spaces, which previous random projection methods couldn't support due to E(R)=0. The authors demonstrate that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN) comparable to neural methods but with significantly lower computational requirements. The second contribution is the empirical analysis of PoP's parameters, showing how dimensionality and sparsity affect performance. The third contribution is the practical demonstration that PoP-constructed models can be transformed using PPMI to achieve Gaussian-like distributions suitable for Pearson correlation.

Strengths: First, the computational efficiency claim is compelling - the authors convincingly argue that PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural embeddings, which is a major practical advantage. Second, the theoretical justification for why PoP enables PPMI weighting (unlike previous methods) is clear and well-supported, addressing a genuine limitation in the field. Third, the empirical results demonstrate that PoP+PPMI outperforms both RI and count-based models while achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art methods.

Weaknesses: First, the paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for PoP's performance, with the authors acknowledging they cannot provide detailed error bounds (δ) as would be expected for a random projection method. Second, the claimed 0.75 MEN score is not compared against recent SOTA methods (e.g., contextual embeddings from transformer models), making it difficult to assess true competitiveness. Third, the paper doesn't compare against other efficient methods like SGNS or GloVe, which are both fast and high-performing, weakening the argument for PoP's unique value proposition.

Questions to Authors: How does PoP+PPMI compare to recent fast methods like FastText or SGNS on standard benchmarks? Can you provide the exact parameters used for the 0.75 MEN score to allow reproducibility? What is the computational time comparison between PoP+PPMI and state-of-the-art neural methods for the same dataset size?
