Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse, nonnegative random projection scheme for constructing low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces. PoP differs from prior RI-like methods by using index vectors with positive entries (E[R] > 0), enabling post-hoc application of PPMI weighting on the projected space. The authors also argue for Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure in the non-Gaussian pre-weighting space and report MEN relatedness results showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson competitive with count-based SVD and some neural baselines.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A practical and simple randomized pipeline that combines a nonnegative sparse random projection with PPMI weighting computed in the reduced space, yielding competitive MEN performance at low computational cost.
2) An empirical characterization of design choices (dimension m, sparsity s/number of nonzeros, similarity measure) and their effect on performance and variance.
3) A qualitative argument (and histograms) about distributional effects: PoP preserves heavy-tailed behavior pre-weighting and moves toward Gaussian-like distributions after PPMI, motivating Pearson correlation post-weighting.

Strengths
- Simplicity and efficiency: The approach reduces co-occurrence matrices first and only then applies weighting. This order-of-operations is compelling for scalability; PPMI on m≈3k is much cheaper than on a full n-context matrix. The incremental construction and independence from iterative training are attractive, especially for streaming or resource-constrained settings.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with minimal machinery: Achieving ~0.75 Spearman on MEN with UKWaC and simple symmetric windows is on par with strong count-based baselines and some neural results given similar data. The parameter studies (varying m, nonzero counts) and the switch from Kendall to Pearson after PPMI are sensible and empirically supported.
- Clear exposition of the pipeline and parameters: The paper provides a concrete definition of the projection distribution, an implementation detail for Kendall’s τb, and ablation figures showing effects of m and sparsity. The discussion of variance across runs is useful for understanding randomized behavior.
- Potentially useful bridge between hashing/sketching and PMI weighting: While not framed this way, PoP is essentially a sketch of the co-occurrence matrix that remains amenable to information-theoretic-style weighting. This could catalyze further work combining sketching with sophisticated weighting/normalization.

Weaknesses
- Lack of theoretical justification and unclear statistical interpretation of PPMI post-projection: Computing PPMI on the projected matrix P = C·T is not estimating PMI over true events but over random mixtures of contexts (pseudo-contexts). The mutual-information interpretation breaks, and bias/variance of PMI estimates under the sketch are not analyzed. Without bounds or an approximation guarantee (e.g., in the spirit of Count-Min Sketch error bounds), it is unclear what is being optimized beyond a heuristic variance-stabilizing transform.
- Limited evaluation and incomplete baselines: Results are only on MEN with a single corpus and bag-of-words windows. There is no evaluation on other intrinsic sets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RG-65, Rare Words) or extrinsic tasks (text classification, analogy, lexical substitution). Missing strong baselines include SPPMI+SVD (Levy & Goldberg), GloVe, well-tuned SGNS on the same corpus, and more pertinent to PoP, sketching/hash baselines: feature hashing (“hashing trick”), Count-Min Sketch of co-occurrences followed by (S)PPMI and SVD. Without these, the comparative claim is weak.
- Efficiency claims lack quantitative evidence: No wall-clock time, memory footprint, or throughput measurements are provided, nor asymptotic and constant-factor comparisons to alternative pipelines (e.g., SPPMI on pruned contexts, randomized SVD on sparse PMI). The added O(m log m) for Kendall’s τb is argued to be negligible but not measured. Claims like “seconds/minutes” need reproducible numbers.
- Questionable claims about RI and PPMI infeasibility: The argument that RI-preprojected matrices make PPMI “undefined” because sums are zero conflates negative entries with zero marginals. While standard PMI requires nonnegative counts, one can maintain separate positive/negative sketches or signed hashing to estimate counts; moreover, column/row sums are not identically zero in RI accumulations. The right critique is interpretability and negativity, not undefined marginals per se.
- Methodological choices are ad hoc and under-motivated: The heavy-tailed index weights (tij = floor(1/U^α), α=0.5) lack justification beyond “doesn’t matter much.” The selection of τb pre-weighting is plausible but presented without ablation against robust rank-based cosines or Spearman-on-ranks. Hyperparameters (stopwording, frequency thresholds) are acknowledged to improve results but mostly omitted in the main evaluation.

Questions to authors
- What is the formal objective of PPMI on the projected space? Can you provide an analysis showing that PPMI(P) approximates (in any sense) a function of PPMI(C), or at least quantify the bias introduced by projection for PMI estimation? Any connection to known sketching error bounds?
- How does PoP compare to Count-Min Sketch or feature hashing applied to the co-occurrence matrix, followed by (S)PPMI and (optionally) randomized SVD? Please include empirical comparisons and discuss memory/time trade-offs.
- Please report runtime and memory usage for PoP, PPMI-on-PoP, and count-based PPMI (with/without SVD), on the same hardware and corpus, across m. Include build time, similarity computation time, and peak memory.
- Clarify the RI/PPMI claim: are you asserting that PMI is undefined because of negative entries or because row/column sums are zero? Could shifted-PMI or separating positive/negative parts be used with RI, and how would that compare empirically?
- What is the motivation for the particular weight distribution in T (floor(1/U^α), α=0.5)? Did you try signed or uniform positive weights, or multiple independent hashes? How sensitive are results?
- Can you provide additional benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words) and an extrinsic task to demonstrate generality? Also include statistical significance tests across runs.

Additional comments
- The notation in Section 2.1 mixes x and m for target dimensionality; please harmonize. Provide exact hyperparameters for best results (m, s, nonzeros).
- Consider discussing derandomization or data-dependent projections, and whether learning s/nonzeros per feature helps.
- Release code and exact preprocessing to ensure reproducibility.
