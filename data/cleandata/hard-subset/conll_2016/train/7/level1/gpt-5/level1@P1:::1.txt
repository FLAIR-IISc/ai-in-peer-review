Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based technique for constructing word semantic spaces. Unlike prior random indexing (RI) approaches that use zero-mean projection matrices and cosine similarity, PoP uses sparse, nonnegative index vectors (E[R] > 0), enabling post-hoc application of weighting schemes such as PPMI on the projected space. The authors also advocate Kendall’s τb for similarity in the raw PoP space and Pearson correlation after PPMI, arguing distributional considerations. Experiments on MEN (using UKWaC) suggest PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75, comparable to strong baselines, while being fast and incremental. Parameter studies examine dimensionality m and sparsity (s).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, incremental random projection scheme with nonnegative, sparse index vectors that allows post-hoc PPMI weighting on the low-dimensional projected space. The claimed benefit is substantial efficiency vs. dense count matrices while retaining access to PPMI-like transformations.
2) Empirical evidence and analysis that, in the raw PoP space, Kendall’s τb outperforms cosine, and that after PPMI transformation, Pearson correlation is preferable—framed by distributional arguments.
3) Practical parameter study of PoP (m, s/number of nonzeros) and qualitative discussion of behavior, variance, and sensitivity.

Strengths
- Practical simplicity and incremental construction: PoP preserves the operational advantages of RI (online, lightweight, no matrix factorization, no neural training), and the index-vector construction is easy to implement. The ability to build representations on-the-fly is compelling for streaming or resource-limited settings.
- Enabling post-hoc weighting on compressed representations: Applying PPMI after projection is the most interesting idea. If valid, it provides a way to obtain the known benefits of PPMI without ever materializing the huge dense co-occurrence matrix. This can be a useful engineering contribution.
- Competitive MEN performance with small vectors: The reported ρ ≈ 0.75 for PoP+PPMI+Pearson at m ≥ 3000 is in the ballpark of popular baselines, with additional evidence that unweighted PoP already beats RI. The parameter ablations (dimensionality and sparsity) are thorough for MEN and illuminate behavior (variance shrinks with m; sensitivity to non-orthogonality as s increases).
- Clear discussion of distributional choices and similarity measures: The paper thoughtfully motivates Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson/cosine depending on the (non-)Gaussianity of the induced spaces and shows histograms that align with the narrative.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to existing hashing/sketching methods: The core mechanism—adding counts into a small number of randomly chosen nonnegative bins per context—is very close to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch (CMS). CMS is a well-known, nonnegative, sparse randomized mapping for approximate counting, widely used in NLP, and supports post-hoc weighting on the sketch. The paper does not cite or compare to feature hashing/CMS, and the “E[R]>0 enables PPMI” argument applies equally to CMS-like sketches. As such, the conceptual novelty over existing nonnegative hashing approaches appears modest.
- Questionable probabilistic validity of PPMI on the projected space: PMI/PPMI is defined on true word-context co-occurrence events; after random projection, columns are synthetic bins without semantic identity, and their marginals are not marginals of any real context distribution. The paper offers no theoretical or empirical justification that PPMI on the projected matrix approximates PPMI on the original counts or improves discrimination for principled reasons (beyond empirical gains on a single task). This is a key methodological gap.
- Narrow and potentially non-representative evaluation: Results are confined to MEN on UKWaC, with no tests on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RareWords, analogy tasks, or downstream evaluations (e.g., text classification, similarity in context). The comparison set is minimal (count-based, RI, dense (P)PMI) and omits strong baselines trained on the same corpus (e.g., SGNS, GloVe, SVD-PPMI) under controlled settings. Claims of “comparable to state-of-the-art” are not convincingly supported.
- Missing scalability evidence and experimental details: The core claim is efficiency, yet there are no runtime, memory, or throughput measurements versus count-based PPMI, SVD-PPMI, SGNS, or CMS/feature hashing at realistic vocabulary sizes. It appears the model is built only for the 751 MEN lemmas, which trivializes compute and invalidates speed claims for realistic use cases. Without large-vocabulary experiments (~100k–1M types), the efficiency advantage remains speculative.
- Lack of theoretical underpinning for the proposed distribution and choices: The index-vector value distribution (⌊U^−α⌋, α=0.5) and s selection are ad hoc; there is no analysis of how these choices impact bias/variance, collision behavior, or approximation quality. The paper acknowledges no current theoretical analysis of PoP’s error, but given the centrality of the “PPMI-after-projection” claim, some bounding or at least connections to known sketching theory are needed.

Questions to authors
- Scope of the constructed vocabulary: Did you construct PoP vectors for only the 751 MEN lemmas, or for the full corpus vocabulary? If only for MEN targets, please rerun with a realistic vocabulary and report compute/memory and accuracy to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Relationship to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing: How does PoP differ theoretically and empirically from CMS/feature hashing (e.g., multiple hash functions with nonnegative updates), which also allows post-hoc weighting on compressed counts? Please compare directly.
- Validity of PPMI after projection: Can you provide theoretical arguments or empirical tests (e.g., recovering true PMI on a subset, or showing rank preservation of associations) that PPMI computed on the projected bins approximates PPMI on original contexts?
- Baselines: Can you report SGNS and SVD-PPMI baselines trained on the same UKWaC data, along with wall-clock and memory comparisons? Also include GloVe (count-based) to strengthen the positioning.
- Similarity measure choice: Beyond MEN, does Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson/cosine show consistent advantages across datasets? How sensitive are results to τb on raw PoP vs. cosine?
- Parameterization: Please justify α=0.5 and the heavy-tailed index weights. Do simple {0,1} updates (as in CMS) or fixed small integers perform similarly or better?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from improved presentation (typos, footnote placement, clearer algorithm description) and a public code release regardless of acceptance.
- Reporting confidence intervals across runs and seeds would improve reproducibility.
- If the main contribution is “efficient PPMI on compressed representations,” a broader comparison to other compression schemes (sketches, subsampling, pruning) is warranted.

Overall, the idea is practical and promising, but its novelty versus established sketching methods, the lack of validation of PPMI-on-projection, and the limited evaluation substantially weaken the submission.
