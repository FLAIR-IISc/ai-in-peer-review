Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random projection method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces with nonnegative entries and positive mean. Unlike Random Indexing (RI) and related projections with zero-mean entries, PoP is designed so that standard weighting schemes for count-based models, notably PPMI, can be applied post-projection at low dimensionality. The authors argue that similarities in the resulting non-Gaussian spaces are better captured by Kendall’s τb; after log/PPMI transformations (which induce more Gaussian-like distributions), Pearson correlation is used. On MEN relatedness with UKWaC, PoP alone outperforms RI and raw count models; PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves average ρ≈0.75 (up to 0.78 with tuning), reportedly comparable to top systems, while being efficient and incremental.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, incremental random projection scheme with positive-only entries that enables applying PPMI after projection, thereby combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI at low dimensionality.
2) An empirical study showing that PoP outperforms RI and raw counts on MEN, and that PoP+PPMI+Pearson approaches strong PMI/PPMI baselines while being more computationally tractable; includes parameter sensitivity analyses over dimensionality and sparsity.
3) A practical choice of similarity measures matched to the induced distributions: Kendall’s τb for unweighted PoP (non-Gaussian) and Pearson’s r after PPMI (approximately Gaussian).

Strengths
1) Clear and practical methodological idea: Using a positive-mean sparse projection is a minimal change to RI that unlocks post-hoc PPMI weighting, a capability not available for zero-mean projections. This is well-motivated and directly addresses a known limitation of RI-type methods.
2) Efficiency and incrementality: The method preserves key advantages of random projections (streaming construction, small m, no expensive factorization). Although not quantified, the complexity arguments (τb at O(m log m) with small m; PPMI computed on low-dimensional matrices) make the scalability claims plausible.
3) Empirical effectiveness over RI and counts: Across multiple window sizes, PoP > RI ≈ counts; PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ρ≈0.75 on MEN, and the paper provides parameter studies (varying m and number of nonzeros) that illuminate stability and variance trends. The observed reduction in variability with larger m increases confidence in robustness.
4) Thoughtful treatment of similarity measures: The rationale for τb in non-Gaussian projected spaces and switching to Pearson post-PPMI is coherent and supported by experiments, improving clarity about when cosine is suboptimal.
5) Use of public resources and some reproducibility signals: UKWaC and MEN are standard; the paper outlines algorithmic steps and promises code release, aiding replication.

Weaknesses
1) Limited theoretical grounding: The paper provides no derivations or bounds for distortion/error under PoP, and Equation (2) (distribution of nonzero values) is insufficiently specified. The admission of lacking theory weakens technical soundness, especially given the centrality of post-projection PPMI where probabilistic interpretation is unclear.
2) Narrow evaluation and baselines: Results are reported only on MEN with UKWaC. There are no direct comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., SGNS, GloVe, SPPMI+SVD) trained on the same corpus with matched preprocessing. The claim of “comparable to state-of-the-art” is therefore weakly supported. Statistical significance testing is absent.
3) Missing efficiency evidence: Despite emphasizing scalability, the paper provides no runtime, memory, or throughput measurements, nor cost comparisons against count-based+PPMI or neural embeddings. Without empirical efficiency data, the central efficiency claim remains anecdotal.
4) Questionable argument about RI and PPMI: The paper states RI-induced matrices have row/column sums “always 0,” making PPMI undefined. In practice, RI has zero-mean entries but not identically zero sums; the issue is the presence of negative/zero values, not strictly zero totals. This overstatement undermines the clarity of the motivation and invites alternatives (e.g., shifting/rectifying RI outputs) that are not addressed.
5) Clarity and specification issues: Notation and typesetting errors (e.g., Eq. (2) for tij, ambiguity about whether nonzero values are floor(1/U^α), choice of α and s), and some informal statements (e.g., “2 or 3 non-zero elements are sufficient”) hinder reproducibility. The probabilistic meaning of PPMI after projection (on non-count values) is not established.

Questions to Authors
1) Distribution of nonzero entries: Please precisely specify Eq. (2): Is tij = floor(1/U^α)? What are the expected value and variance of tij, and how are α and s chosen in practice to ensure at least one nonzero per row?
2) Efficiency: Provide wall-clock time and memory usage for constructing PoP, computing τb/PPMI, and querying similarities, compared to (a) count-based+PPMI(+SVD) and (b) SGNS/GloVe on UKWaC with matched vocabulary and window sizes.
3) Baselines: Report MEN (and, if possible, SimLex-999 and WordSim-353) results for SGNS, GloVe, and SPPMI+SVD with matched preprocessing, plus significance tests versus PoP+PPMI.
4) PPMI after projection: What is the probabilistic interpretation of PPMI on PoP matrices? Can you relate PPMI(PoP(C)) to PPMI(C) under any approximation or bound? Does PoP preserve co-occurrence ratios relevant to PMI?
5) Alternative fixes for RI: How does PoP compare to simple modifications of RI (e.g., rectifying or shifting to nonnegative values before PPMI)? Are there empirical results to rule out such baselines?
6) Similarity measures: Beyond distributional arguments, do you have empirical comparisons of τb vs cosine vs Pearson in unweighted PoP across multiple datasets?
