Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method to build low-dimensional word co-occurrence spaces using index vectors with strictly nonnegative entries (E[R] > 0). Because the projected counts remain nonnegative and sums are nonzero, PoP allows applying PPMI weighting after projection. The authors recommend Kendall’s τb for similarity in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and Pearson after PPMI (more Gaussian-like).
- They demonstrate on MEN relatedness that PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines without weighting, and that PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75 ρ (up to ~0.78 with tuning), competitive with neural embeddings, while being much more efficient and incremental.
- They provide an empirical parameter study (dimension m, sparsity s, nonzero count) and discuss the behavior (variance across runs, non-orthogonality effects) of the randomized construction.

Main contributions (ordered by strength)
1) Practical contribution: a simple, incremental, highly efficient random projection scheme that permits post-hoc PPMI weighting in the compressed space, yielding competitive performance on MEN with far lower computational cost than training neural embeddings or computing PPMI in the original high-dimensional space.
2) Empirical analysis: ablations comparing count-based, RI, PoP with/without PPMI, and similarity measures (Kendall vs Pearson), plus parameter sweeps over m and s that show variance reduction with larger m and trade-offs with sparsity.
3) Methodological observation: using Kendall’s τb as a correlation-based similarity for non-Gaussian projected spaces and switching to Pearson after PPMI transforms the distribution.

Strengths
- Clear practical efficiency story: The method avoids high-dimensional counting and expensive model training; PoP builds low-dimensional spaces incrementally and then applies PPMI cheaply. This directly addresses the “count models are sparse/high-dim and costly” problem and is well-justified by the design (R with E>0) and the post-projection weighting step. The argument is concrete and addresses a common bottleneck.
- Solid, multifaceted empirical comparison on the chosen task: The paper contrasts raw counts, RI, PoP, and PoP+PPMI; switches between Kendall/pearson/cosine; and averages over multiple random runs. The results consistently show PoP > RI/counts in the unweighted setting and substantial gains with PPMI. The parameter study (m, number of nonzeros) is informative and supports the claims about variance and capacity.
- Competitive accuracy with simple machinery: Achieving ~0.75 on MEN (and ~0.78 after tuning) without neural training is impressive for a simple randomized pipeline. The finding that PPMI after compression performs comparably to dense PPMI or neural embeddings is practically valuable.
- Incremental and scalable by construction: The mapping from contexts to random index vectors and additive updates supports streaming/incremental processing. This is a concrete advantage over batch matrix factorization or long neural training cycles.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Only MEN is reported. No results on other standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words), analogy tasks, or downstream evaluations. Neural baselines (SGNS/CBOW, GloVe) trained on the same corpus and settings are absent; comparisons rely on external papers, which can be apples-to-oranges. The SVD-PPMI baseline is only lightly discussed; tuned SPPMI/SVD and modern hyperparameterizations are not systematically compared.
- Weak theoretical grounding where it matters: The paper acknowledges lacking theory for the error δ under the proposed positive-only projection. Importantly, applying PPMI after random projection mixes co-occurrence events, so the PMI quantities no longer have their usual probabilistic semantics; positivity ensures nonzero sums but does not guarantee that projected counts are unbiased estimators of original co-occurrences. The paper motivates with distributional arguments (Gaussianization) and histograms but does not justify when/why PPMI on compressed counts preserves the ranking signal.
- Overclaiming and missing quantitative efficiency evidence: While the paper argues “seconds/minutes” and “dramatic” savings, no concrete timing/memory numbers, CPU/GPU specs, or complexity-vs-baselines measurements are reported. Without wall-clock/memory comparisons against (a) dense PPMI, (b) SVD-PPMI, (c) SGNS/GloVe on the same corpus, the efficiency claim remains qualitative.
- Ad-hoc choices and incomplete ablations: The distribution of nonzero values (floor(1/U^α), α=0.5), the choice of Kendall’s τb in raw PoP, and the recommendation of 1–2 nonzeros after PPMI feel heuristic. The analysis of non-orthogonality is helpful but not tied to downstream accuracy via controlled experiments (e.g., fixing collision rates). Comparisons to alternative compressions (feature hashing, count-min sketch + PPMI) are missing; those are natural and strong baselines for compressed co-occurrence modeling.
- Reproducibility gaps: Code and embeddings are promised “if accepted.” Important details (vocabulary/context sizes, window weighting, subsampling, stop-word policies for the main runs, exact corpus statistics, random seeds, confidence intervals or significance tests) are incomplete, making it harder to reproduce or verify robustness.

Questions to authors
- Can you report exact time and memory usage for each method/configuration (counts, RI, PoP, PoP+PPMI, SVD-PPMI), on the same hardware and corpus, including build time and similarity computation time?
- Please clarify the statistical meaning of PPMI on projected counts: are the projected counts unbiased (or at least monotone-preserving in expectation) for the true co-occurrence counts? Can you derive or empirically estimate how projection distorts PMI and how that affects rank correlation?
- Could you compare against feature hashing or count-min sketch with PPMI computed in the hashed space, and against SPPMI+SVD and SGNS/GloVe trained on exactly the same UKWaC setup?
- What is the vocabulary and context dimensionality before projection? What frequency cutoffs and stop-word handling are used in the main reported numbers (not just in the “tuned” results)?
- How sensitive are results to window weighting (position/decay), subsampling frequent contexts, or using dependency-based contexts?
- Can you report additional benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW) and provide variance across 10 runs (stddev/confidence intervals) and significance tests?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a simple and clever twist—positive-only random projections enabling post-hoc PPMI—that yields a highly practical pipeline with competitive MEN performance at low computational cost. The idea is novel in its specific combination and is well-supported empirically on MEN with ablations and parameter studies. However, the evaluation breadth and theoretical justification are insufficient for strong claims, and key baselines and efficiency numbers are missing. With expanded experiments (stronger baselines on the same data, multiple datasets, timings) and clearer analysis of PPMI-after-projection, this would be a solid and impactful contribution. As is, it meets the bar on practicality and insight, but falls short on rigor and breadth.
