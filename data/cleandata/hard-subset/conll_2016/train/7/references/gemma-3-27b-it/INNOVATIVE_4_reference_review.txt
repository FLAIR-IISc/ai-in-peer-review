This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – using random projections with strictly positive values to enable weighting transformations like PPMI – is genuinely interesting and represents a departure from traditional random indexing techniques. The authors correctly identify a limitation of existing methods (incompatibility with weighting) and propose a simple yet effective solution. The reported results, achieving competitive performance with significantly reduced computational cost, are promising.

**Main Contributions:**

1.  **Novel Projection Method:** The PoP technique itself is the primary contribution. It offers a genuinely new approach to random projection, cleverly circumventing the limitations of existing methods regarding weighting.
2.  **Efficiency & Scalability:** The demonstrated speed advantage—building models in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural embeddings—is substantial and addresses a real bottleneck in semantic space construction.
3.  **PPMI Compatibility:** Re-enabling the application of PPMI weighting to random projection outputs is a significant technical achievement, combining the benefits of both approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold Idea & Clear Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-defined problem—the computational cost of traditional semantic space construction—with a bold and well-motivated solution. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and position PoP as a direct response.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** While the evaluation is limited to a single test set (MEN), the results are compelling. Achieving competitive performance with significantly reduced computational expense is a major strength. The inclusion of comparisons with RI, count-based models, and a nod towards neural embeddings strengthens the claims.
3.  **Insightful Discussion of Theoretical Implications:** The authors thoughtfully acknowledge the lack of rigorous theoretical grounding for PoP and draw parallels to the historical development of other random projection methods (RI). The discussion of the trade-off between accuracy and computational complexity, and the attempt to relate parameters (s, m) to performance, demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying principles.
4.  **Practical Impact:** The speed and efficiency gains offered by PoP have the potential to unlock semantic space construction for resource-constrained environments and large-scale datasets where neural methods are prohibitive.
5. **Awareness of Limitations & Future Directions:** The authors openly acknowledge the need for further theoretical analysis and outline several promising avenues for future research (derandomization, combined methods). This self-awareness is a positive sign.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation relies solely on the MEN dataset. While a standard benchmark, it’s insufficient to fully assess the generalizability of PoP. Evaluation on a broader range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word analogy, rare word similarity) is crucial.
2.  **Parameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The paper acknowledges the importance of parameters (s, m) but provides limited guidance on how to tune them effectively. The discussion of the interplay between these parameters and the resulting vector distributions feels somewhat tentative. A more systematic parameter study would strengthen the findings.
3.  **Lack of Ablation Study:**  It would be helpful to see an ablation study showing the individual contributions of PoP (positive-only projection) versus PPMI weighting.  Is the performance gain primarily due to the projection method itself, or is it largely driven by the re-introduction of PPMI weighting?
4.  **Statistical Significance:** The results presented in Figures 1, 2, 4, and 5 show variance in performance across multiple runs. While averages are reported, a formal statistical significance test (e.g., t-test) to demonstrate the robustness of the improvements would be beneficial.
5. **Figure Quality:** Figure 3’s labels are somewhat difficult to read.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of α = 0.5 in Equation 2?  Was this value empirically optimized, or is there a theoretical justification for it?
2.  You mention that "increasing m over 2000 decreases the performance" for the count-based PoP method. Can you provide a more detailed explanation for this counterintuitive behavior? Is it related to the sparsity of the vectors or other factors?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of PoP to the choice of similarity measure (Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s r)? Would using a different similarity measure consistently yield better results, and if so, why?
4.  Can you provide more details on the computational resources (CPU, memory) required to build PoP models with different dimensionality settings? A more precise quantification of the speedup compared to neural embedding training would be valuable.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are a bit convoluted. A careful proofread would improve clarity.
*   The reference to "dense PPMI-weighted models" in Figure 2 is a bit vague. Clarify what these models entail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the evaluation is limited, the results are encouraging, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying principles. The novelty and potential for efficiency gains outweigh the current limitations. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, pending satisfactory responses to the questions above.** The paper’s strengths – particularly the bold idea and potential practical impact – are compelling enough to warrant acceptance despite the incomplete validation.
