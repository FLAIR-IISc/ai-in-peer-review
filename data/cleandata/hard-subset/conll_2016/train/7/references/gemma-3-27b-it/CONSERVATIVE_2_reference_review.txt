This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces via random projections, with the key claim being that it allows for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI – something traditional random indexing methods struggle with – while maintaining computational efficiency comparable to RI. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a technically sound exploration of how to modify random projection techniques to *enable* weighting schemes traditionally applied to count-based models. While the core idea isn’t revolutionary – combining the speed of random projections with the discriminative power of weighting – its successful implementation and the demonstration that PPMI can indeed be applied is a noteworthy refinement. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation, showing PoP achieves competitive results against established methods like neural embeddings, albeit with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is clearly explained and logically motivated. The mathematical formulation of PoP is presented concisely and the rationale for using positive-only projections to facilitate PPMI weighting is well-articulated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard random indexing methods concerning PPMI application.
2. **Efficient Implementation:** The emphasis on computational efficiency is a significant strength. The paper convincingly argues that PoP offers a compelling trade-off between accuracy and speed, potentially making it suitable for resource-constrained environments or applications requiring rapid model building.
3. **Comparative Evaluation:** The authors appropriately compare PoP against established baselines – count-based models, RI, and (implicitly) neural embeddings – using a standard benchmark (MEN). The results demonstrate that PoP is a viable alternative, particularly when combined with PPMI.
4. **Thorough Investigation of Parameters:** The detailed analysis of the impact of dimensionality (m) and the sparsity parameter (s) demonstrates a good understanding of the method’s behavior. The visualizations (Figures 4, 5, and 6) are helpful in illustrating these effects.
5. **Clear Discussion of Limitations:** The authors are honest about the limitations of PoP, acknowledging its randomized nature and the need for further theoretical analysis. They also correctly point out that performance is sensitive to parameter settings and context element selection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Concept:** While the *implementation* is clever, the overarching concept of combining random projections with weighting is not entirely novel. Existing literature explores dimensionality reduction techniques followed by weighting, and the core contribution lies in making PPMI applicable to a specific random projection scheme, rather than a groundbreaking new approach to semantic space construction.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper claims competitive performance with neural embeddings but lacks a direct, head-to-head comparison with commonly used, pre-trained embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset. Simply stating that neural nets are “computationally costly” isn’t a sufficient justification for not including a proper benchmark. A clear demonstration that PoP achieves comparable performance *with significantly less computational effort* is crucial.
3. **Over-reliance on a Single Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the MEN relatedness test. While this is a standard benchmark, it doesn’t provide a comprehensive assessment of PoP’s performance across different tasks or datasets (e.g., word analogy, text classification). Generalizability needs to be demonstrated.
4. **Theoretical Justification is Weak:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a rigorous theoretical foundation for PoP. While they provide some intuition based on existing work on random projections, a more detailed mathematical analysis of the method’s properties (e.g., error bounds, convergence rates) would significantly strengthen the paper. The discussion about δ feels incomplete.
5. **Kendall’s τb vs Pearson’s r:** The justification for switching similarity measures *after* the PPMI transformation feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled explanation for why Pearson’s correlation is more appropriate for Gaussian-like distributions would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI+Pearson against established pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset, including training time and computational resources used for each approach?
2. Have you experimented with other weighting schemes besides PPMI? If so, how does PoP perform with different weighting functions?
3. What steps have you taken to ensure the reproducibility of your results? Will you be releasing the code and data used in your experiments?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of α = 0.5 in Equation 2? Was this parameter optimized, or is it a heuristic choice? What impact does changing α have on performance?
5. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the performance of the count-based PoP model *decreases* with increasing dimensionality beyond a certain point, despite the expectation that higher dimensionality should generally improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, the frequent use of parenthetical remarks can make the text somewhat dense. Consider restructuring some sentences for improved readability. The supplemental material offer is welcome.
