## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces a novel method, Positive-Only Projection (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – using random projections with strictly positive values to enable subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI – is intriguing and addresses a limitation of traditional random projection techniques. The authors position PoP as a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, and demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The PoP Method:** The central contribution is the proposed PoP method itself. The design choice of using positive-only random projections to retain the ability to apply weighting techniques (specifically PPMI) to the resulting vectors is a clever workaround to a known limitation of standard random projection methods like RI and RRI.
2. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of PoP, comparing it against RI and demonstrating the benefit of combining PoP with PPMI weighting. The detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of dimensionality (m) and the sparsity parameter (s) are valuable and contribute to a deeper understanding of the method's behavior.
3. **Bridging the Gap:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projections and the accuracy gains from weighting schemes like PPMI, offering a potential sweet spot for resource-constrained applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of using positive-only random projections to enable weighting transformations is genuinely novel and addresses a practical issue in distributional semantics. It offers a new perspective on random projection techniques.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a well-designed empirical evaluation, utilizing a standard benchmark (MEN) and conducting ablation studies to dissect the impact of key parameters.  The inclusion of performance variances (min/max values) for different runs adds robustness to the findings.
3. **Detailed Explanation and Motivation:** The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the underlying principles and rationale behind PoP, drawing connections to existing work in distributional semantics and random projections. The discussion surrounding the limitations of traditional random projections and the advantages of PoP is well-articulated.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The claim of computational efficiency is compelling. The ability to build models in “seconds or minutes” compared to hours/days for neural embeddings is a significant advantage for many NLP applications.
5. **Thorough Analysis of Parameters:** The detailed exploration of the 's' parameter and its impact on performance, combined with the discussion of orthogonality and distribution considerations, demonstrates a strong understanding of the method's intricacies.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While PoP is compared to RI, the comparison to more recent and powerful count-based methods leveraging efficient dimensionality reduction (e.g., fastText with pre-trained vectors) is missing. It is unclear how PoP compares to these readily available alternatives. Including such comparisons would significantly strengthen the claim of competitiveness.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN relatedness test. Expanding the evaluation to other semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy tasks) and potentially tasks requiring distributional representations (e.g., word sense disambiguation) would enhance the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis of PoP's behavior. While this is reasonable, even a preliminary attempt to provide theoretical bounds on the approximation error (δ) or to relate PoP to existing theoretical frameworks for random projections would be valuable. The discussion relies heavily on empirical observations.
4. **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the ablation studies explore 'm' and 's', the sensitivity of PoP's performance to other potential hyperparameters (e.g., the choice of context window size) isn't thoroughly investigated. 
5. **Figure Clarity:** Some figures (e.g. Figure 3) are difficult to interpret due to the density of information and potentially small font sizes. Clearer labeling and simplification would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparisons:** How does PoP compare to fastText with pre-trained vectors in terms of both accuracy and computational cost (training time, memory usage)?
2. **Generalizability:** Have you evaluated PoP on datasets other than MEN? If so, what were the results? If not, do you have plans to do so?
3. **Theoretical Analysis:** Could you elaborate on your plans for a theoretical analysis of PoP? Are you considering exploring connections to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma or other relevant theoretical results?
4. **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Did you perform any systematic hyperparameter optimization beyond the ablation studies presented in the paper? If so, what were the results, and how did you choose the parameters used in your final experiments?
5. **Practical Considerations:**  For real-world applications, how sensitive is PoP to noisy or incomplete data? Are there any strategies for mitigating the impact of data quality issues?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, although some sections could benefit from tighter editing for conciseness.
* The supplemental material offer is appreciated.
* The historical context provided regarding the evolution of distributional semantics and random projection techniques is helpful.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to constructing semantic spaces. The PoP method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates competitive performance. However, strengthening the baseline comparisons and expanding the evaluation scope would significantly enhance the paper's impact.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  The potential of PoP as a computationally efficient and accurate alternative to existing methods warrants further investigation.
