## Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. It presents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics. While the writing is somewhat dense and could be clearer, the core idea—PoP—is well-motivated, appears technically sound, and demonstrably improves upon existing random projection methods. The empirical results, while limited to a single evaluation dataset, are promising, and the analysis of the method’s behavior is insightful.

**Summary & Main Contributions:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a new method for constructing word embeddings. Unlike traditional random projection techniques (RI, RRI, etc.), PoP uses a random projection matrix comprised of only positive values. This key difference enables the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* the projection step, which is impossible with standard random projections due to their zero-mean nature. The authors demonstrate that PoP, when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance with neural embeddings and other sophisticated methods while being significantly faster and less computationally demanding. The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, enabling weighting of projected vectors. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of its effectiveness and efficiency. A further contribution is the detailed analysis of the method's parameters and behavior, providing useful insights into its strengths and limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Technical Soundness:** The core idea of using positive-only random projections to enable post-projection weighting is genuinely novel. The justification for why traditional random projections preclude this step is clear and convincing. The mathematical formulation is presented adequately, and the explanation of why PPMI can be applied to PoP-generated vectors is well-reasoned.
2. **Practical Efficiency:** The authors clearly emphasize and demonstrate the computational efficiency of PoP. The ability to build models in seconds or minutes, compared to hours or days for neural embeddings or SVD-based methods, is a significant advantage, especially for resource-constrained environments or rapid prototyping.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, using the MEN dataset, that PoP+PPMI outperforms both RI and traditional count-based models. While the evaluation is limited, it provides a solid starting point. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing PoP with and without PPMI, different dimensionality settings) enhances the credibility of the results.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Parameter Exploration:**  The authors go beyond simply presenting results and delve into a detailed analysis of PoP's behavior. The investigation of the impact of dimensionality (m) and the number of non-zero elements (s) on performance, along with the discussion of orthogonality and sparsity, is particularly valuable. This level of analysis demonstrates a strong understanding of the method and its limitations.
5. **Clear Motivation & Connection to Existing Work:** The paper provides a well-structured introduction that effectively contextualizes PoP within the broader landscape of distributional semantics, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and motivating the need for a new approach.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, it doesn’t provide a comprehensive assessment of PoP's generalizability. Evaluation on more diverse tasks (e.g., word analogy, sentence similarity) and datasets would strengthen the paper considerably.
2. **Writing Clarity & Density:** The writing is often dense and difficult to follow. The authors use complex sentence structures and technical jargon without sufficient explanation.  Several sections could benefit from more concrete examples and a more intuitive presentation of the concepts. The frequent parenthetical remarks disrupt the flow.
3. **Missing Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the comparison to RI and neural embeddings is valuable, the authors should also compare PoP to simpler baselines, such as simple count-based models with dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA). This would provide a clearer understanding of the specific benefits of PoP beyond simply reducing dimensionality.
4. **Theoretical Justification Gap:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error bound (δ) of PoP.  While they appropriately point to existing work on randomized algorithms, a more thorough discussion of the theoretical properties of PoP, even if incomplete, would be beneficial.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Evaluation Expansion:**  What are the authors’ plans for evaluating PoP on a broader range of tasks and datasets? Specifically, will they include word analogy tasks, sentence similarity tasks, or other standard NLP benchmarks?
2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The analysis of parameters ‘m’ and ‘s’ is insightful.  Are there other parameters (e.g., the value of α in Equation 2) that significantly influence PoP’s performance?  If so, can you provide an analysis of their impact?
3. **Computational Cost Breakdown:** Could the authors provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of PoP, comparing it to the cost of training neural embeddings and performing PPMI weighting on count-based models for different corpus sizes?
4. **Orthogonality Concerns:**  You mention that increasing 's' can lead to less orthogonal index vectors.  How does this relate to the performance drop observed in some scenarios? Are there techniques to mitigate this issue beyond simply reducing 's'?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 3 is excellent for illustrating the distributional differences, but the axes labels are very small and difficult to read.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters and their recommended ranges for optimal performance.
*   The discussion of Kendall's τb and Pearson's r is a bit convoluted. A more concise and intuitive explanation would be helpful.




In conclusion, this is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly the limited evaluation and writing clarity, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
