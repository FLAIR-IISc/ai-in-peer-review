- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — novel, well-motivated method with strong empirical results but limited theoretical grounding.
- Core contribution: Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP) to enable weighting schemes like PPMI in random projection frameworks.
- Empirical claim: PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance on the MEN semantic similarity benchmark versus heavier methods (neural embeddings, SVD).
- Parameter analysis: Thorough exploration of dimensionality and sparsity ('s') effects provides practical insights.
- Strength: Addresses trade-off between computational efficiency and accuracy by enabling weighting in random projections.
- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation with appropriate notation and explanations.
- Strength: Strong empirical validation including baselines, ablation studies, established datasets, and relevant metrics.
- Strength: Authors acknowledge limitations and outline future theoretical and derandomization work.
- Strength: PoP is fast and efficient, suitable for resource-constrained or rapid model construction scenarios.
- Weakness: Lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis explaining why PoP works; JL discussion is insufficiently developed.
- Weakness: Evaluation scope is limited to a single task (MEN); generalizability to other NLP tasks is untested.
- Weakness: Method shows sensitivity to the sparsity parameter 's', potentially requiring careful tuning per dataset/task.
- Weakness: Comparisons omit more recent embeddings (e.g., fastText, GloVe), limiting contextual relevance.
- Question: Are there ongoing efforts to develop a theoretical analysis or connections to random projection theory?
- Question: Can the authors provide practical heuristics or data-dependent guidance for selecting 's'?
- Question: Are there preliminary results on PoP’s performance for other NLP tasks beyond semantic similarity?
- Question: What are PoP’s scalability limits for extremely large corpora and vocabularies (e.g., memory usage)?
- Additional notes: Figures are clear, supplemental material aids reproducibility, and the Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson discussion is insightful.
