## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:**

This paper presents Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP leverages random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but crucially generates positive-only random vectors. This allows subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI, which are incompatible with standard RI due to its zero-mean projections. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks (MEN test) compared to neural embeddings and SVD-based approaches, while being significantly faster and less computationally expensive.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel approach to random projection enabling weighting:** The core contribution lies in the design of PoP, which addresses a limitation of existing random projection methods by enabling the application of weighting transformations like PPMI. This opens up avenues for combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of weighting schemes.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:** The paper provides a robust empirical evaluation on a standard benchmark (MEN), demonstrating that PoP+PPMI achieves performance comparable to more complex methods, while requiring significantly less computational effort.
3. **Insightful discussion of theoretical considerations & parameter tuning:** The authors don't shy away from discussing the theoretical underpinnings (or lack thereof) and provide a valuable analysis of parameter sensitivity (dimensionality, sparsity), offering practical guidelines for using the method.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a clear limitation of existing methods:** The incompatibility of PPMI weighting with standard RI is a significant problem, and PoP directly addresses this, offering a practical solution. The authors clearly articulate this problem and position their work as a solution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The MEN test results are convincing, demonstrating that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance. The comparison to RI is particularly important, highlighting the benefit of enabling weighting.  The inclusion of ablation studies, showing the impact of dimensionality and sparsity, further strengthens the empirical validation.
3. **Computational Efficiency:**  The paper consistently emphasizes the computational advantages of PoP. This is a key selling point, especially in an era where training large neural models is becoming increasingly resource-intensive. The claim of building models in "seconds or minutes" is significant.
4. **Thoughtful Discussion of Theoretical Aspects:** The authors acknowledge the lack of formal theoretical guarantees and discuss the connections to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and related work.  This honesty and awareness of limitations are commendable.  Their discussion of the connection between distribution shape and appropriate similarity measures (Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s correlation) is particularly insightful.
5. **Detailed Analysis & Parameter Tuning Guidance:** The inclusion of figures (3, 4, 5, 6) and the associated discussions around dimensionality and sparsity offer practical guidance to potential users of the method. This is a significant contribution beyond just presenting a new algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN dataset and the UKWaC corpus. While MEN is a standard benchmark, evaluating on a wider range of tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would significantly strengthen the claims of generalizability. 
2. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent/Strong Baselines:** The comparison is primarily to RI and count-based models.  While this is relevant, it would be more compelling to compare to more recent and competitive baselines, such as fastText or even lightweight neural embeddings (e.g., those trained with negative sampling).
3. **The "Why" of Positive-Only Projections isn't fully explored:** While the authors explain *that* positive-only projections enable weighting, they don't deeply explore *why* this is the case.  A more detailed explanation of the mathematical properties that allow PPMI to be applied to PoP-generated vectors would be beneficial.  It feels somewhat hand-wavy as it stands.
4. **Potential Sensitivity to Random Initialization:** As a randomized algorithm, the performance of PoP is likely sensitive to the random seed.  The authors report ranges in the figures, but a more formal analysis of this sensitivity (e.g., reporting standard deviations) would be valuable.
5. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Search:** The paper mentions tuning of parameters (s, dimensionality), but doesn't provide details on the search strategy used. A brief description of the hyperparameter optimization process would improve reproducibility.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Evaluation Expansion:** What is the computational cost of evaluating PoP on other tasks (e.g. word analogy, text classification) and do you have plans to extend the evaluation to these tasks?
2. **Baseline Comparison:** Could you include a comparison to fastText or lightweight neural embeddings as baselines?  Even a brief comparison would be helpful.
3. **Mathematical Explanation:** Could you provide a more detailed mathematical explanation of why the positive-only projection allows PPMI to be applied, specifically highlighting the property that prevents PPMI from working with standard RI?
4. **Random Seed Sensitivity:** What is the standard deviation of the performance metrics (e.g., Spearman’s rho) across multiple runs with different random seeds?
5. **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter optimization process?  What search strategy was employed to find optimal values for 's' and the dimensionality 'm'?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The paper is well-motivated and addresses a relevant problem in NLP.
* The inclusion of supplemental materials (code, embeddings) would be highly valuable.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics. The PoP method offers a compelling combination of efficiency and performance, and the authors have provided a thorough empirical evaluation and thoughtful discussion of the method’s properties. While there are some weaknesses regarding the scope of evaluation and baseline comparisons, I believe these can be addressed in a revised version. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
