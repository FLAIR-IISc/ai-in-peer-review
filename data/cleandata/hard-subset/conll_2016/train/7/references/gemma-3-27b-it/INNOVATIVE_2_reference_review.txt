This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces through random projections, designed to overcome limitations of existing techniques like RI and address the computational cost of neural embeddings. The core idea – using a random projection matrix with only positive values to allow for weighting transformations like PPMI – is genuinely interesting and potentially impactful. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive results on semantic similarity tasks, while being significantly faster than both neural approaches and traditional count-based methods with SVD.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A Novel Random Projection Technique:** The PoP method itself represents a significant departure from existing random projection techniques. The constraint of positive values, enabling PPMI weighting, is a clever and non-obvious solution to a long-standing problem.
2. **Demonstrated Effectiveness:** The empirical results, while limited to the MEN dataset, show PoP+PPMI achieving competitive accuracy alongside substantial computational savings. This is a compelling practical advantage.
3. **Bridging the Gap:** The paper effectively positions PoP as a middle ground between the computational expense of neural embeddings and the limitations of simpler, count-based methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The core idea behind PoP is highly novel. The authors identify a key limitation of standard random projections (incompatibility with weighting) and propose a surprisingly simple yet effective solution. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and a willingness to explore unconventional approaches.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly argues for and demonstrates the computational benefits of PoP. The ability to build models in seconds/minutes, compared to hours/days for neural methods, is a substantial advantage for resource-constrained environments or rapid prototyping.
3. **Strong Theoretical Justification (attempted):** While acknowledging the lack of formal mathematical guarantees, the authors attempt to provide a theoretical grounding for PoP, drawing parallels to other randomized algorithms and discussing the implications of the positive-only constraint. This demonstrates intellectual honesty and a desire for rigorous understanding.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the PoP method and its advantages. The mathematical notation is appropriate and the experimental setup is described adequately.
5. **Addressing a Core Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in distributional semantics: balancing model accuracy with computational cost. PoP offers a promising approach to this trade-off.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (MEN) and a limited set of parameters. While the results are promising, they need to be validated on a wider range of datasets and tasks to establish generalizability.
2. **Lack of Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The authors rightly acknowledge the need for a more thorough theoretical analysis of PoP, particularly regarding error bounds (δ).  Without such analysis, it’s difficult to assess the robustness and reliability of the method. The analogies drawn to other randomized algorithms are helpful but don't constitute a formal proof of performance.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of parameter sensitivity (specifically ‘s’ – the probability of a non-zero element) suggests the method is somewhat fragile and requires careful tuning. More guidance on parameter selection would be beneficial.
4. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the paper compares PoP to RI and count-based methods, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art, lightweight embeddings (e.g., fastText) would be valuable.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The claims of “competitive results” need to be qualified. While PoP performs well, it isn’t clear if it consistently *outperforms* other methods, or simply achieves comparable accuracy with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of PoP compared to other methods (e.g., neural embeddings, SVD)? A table showing runtimes for different dataset sizes would be helpful.
2.  What is the sensitivity of the method to the choice of PPMI parameters (e.g., smoothing constant)? Do different PPMI settings significantly impact performance?
3.  Have you explored the use of different similarity measures beyond Kendall’s τb and Pearson’s r? Would other measures (e.g., cosine similarity after different normalization techniques) be appropriate for PoP-constructed spaces?
4. Can you elaborate on the "derandomization techniques" mentioned in the discussion? What specific approaches do you envision for incorporating linguistic knowledge into the PoP framework?
5.  What are the limitations of PoP? Are there specific types of semantic relationships or tasks where it is likely to perform poorly?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The inclusion of a supplemental material section is appreciated. Please ensure that code and embeddings are publicly available upon acceptance.
*   Figure 3 is very helpful for visualizing the distribution of weights.
*   The discussion of the trade-off between sparsity and performance is insightful.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I recommend **accepting** this paper. The novelty of the PoP method, its potential for computational efficiency, and the authors’ thoughtful exploration of the underlying principles are compelling. While more rigorous evaluation and theoretical analysis are needed, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field and opens up exciting avenues for future research. I value bold ideas and this paper delivers on that front, outweighing the methodological shortcomings.
