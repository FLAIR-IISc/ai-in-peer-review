## Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP aims to overcome limitations of traditional count-based models and random projection techniques by generating random vectors with only positive values, enabling the application of weighting methods like PPMI – a crucial step often missing in existing random projection approaches. The authors demonstrate that PoP, when combined with PPMI weighting, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks compared to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, but with significantly lower computational cost.  The paper details the method, provides empirical evaluation on the MEN dataset, and discusses its properties and potential extensions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Novel Random Projection Technique:** The core contribution is the PoP method itself, specifically the use of positive-only random vectors. This seemingly simple change unlocks the ability to combine the efficiency of random projections with the benefits of weighting schemes like PPMI, a significant improvement over existing techniques.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Effectiveness:** The paper provides convincing evidence, through the MEN dataset evaluation, that PoP + PPMI achieves comparable performance to more computationally expensive methods, and outperforms basic RI.  The ablation studies (varying dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) are helpful.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:**  The paper goes beyond just presenting a method; it delves into the theoretical underpinnings, discusses the algorithm's randomness, the influence of parameters, and potential directions for future work.  This thoughtfulness increases the value of the contribution.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real Problem:** The paper tackles a significant bottleneck in semantic space construction: the trade-off between computational cost and model accuracy.  Existing methods either require expensive training (neural networks) or sacrifice discriminative power (basic random projections). PoP offers a compelling alternative.
2. **Clear and Well-Explained Method:** The PoP method is described clearly and mathematically, making it easy to understand and potentially reproduce. The connection to existing random projection techniques is well-established, and the rationale behind the positive-only constraint is well-motivated.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results on the MEN dataset demonstrate that PoP + PPMI is a viable approach, achieving competitive performance.  The comparison to RI and the ablation studies add weight to the findings.  The inclusion of performance variations (min/max) is a good practice.
4. **Thorough Discussion of Theoretical Implications:** The paper doesn’t shy away from discussing the theoretical limitations and potential extensions of the method. The exploration of the trade-off between sparsity, dimensionality, and orthogonality is particularly insightful. The justification for using Kendall’s τb initially and then Pearson’s correlation after PPMI transformation demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying statistical principles.
5. **Practical Significance:** The low computational cost of PoP makes it potentially valuable for resource-constrained environments or for rapid prototyping and exploration of semantic models.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, its limited size and focus on word relatedness might not fully capture the generalizability of the method.  Evaluation on more diverse tasks (e.g., analogy completion, word similarity, document classification) would strengthen the claims.
2. **Parameter Sensitivity & Guidance:** The paper acknowledges the sensitivity of PoP to parameters like 's' (the probability of a non-zero element) and dimensionality. However, the guidelines for selecting optimal values are somewhat vague.  A more systematic exploration of parameter space and clearer recommendations would be beneficial.  The discussion of the interplay between sparsity and dimensionality is good, but feels incomplete.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Efficient Alternatives:**  The paper focuses on comparing PoP to neural embeddings and SVD, which are known to be computationally expensive.  It would be valuable to compare PoP to other efficient methods for dimensionality reduction and semantic space construction, such as Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH) or FastText, especially concerning both performance *and* computational cost.
4. **Justification for Kendall's Tau:** The initial justification for using Kendall's Tau feels a little convoluted. While the point about non-Gaussian distributions is valid, the explanation could be streamlined. A more direct link to the properties of the PoP-generated distributions would be helpful.
5. **Limited Discussion of Negative Results:** The paper mentions that increasing dimensionality beyond a certain point *decreases* performance without PPMI.  This observation is interesting but deserves more in-depth investigation. What causes this degradation? Is it purely a consequence of the randomness of PoP, or are there other factors at play?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:**  Have you experimented with PoP on datasets beyond MEN? If so, what were the results? If not, what are your plans for evaluating the method's performance on more diverse tasks?
2. **Parameter Tuning:** Can you provide a more concrete guideline for selecting the optimal value of 's' based on the characteristics of the corpus (e.g., corpus size, vocabulary size, sparsity)?
3. **Comparison to FastText/LSH:** Have you compared PoP to other efficient methods for semantic space construction, such as FastText or LSH, in terms of both performance and computational cost? If so, what were the results?
4. **Dimensionality Degradation:** Can you elaborate on the reason why increasing dimensionality beyond a certain point degrades performance *without* PPMI weighting?  Is there a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon?
5. **Implementation Details:**  Will the code and trained models be made publicly available?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The inclusion of the Supplemental Material promise is a good sign.
* The discussion of the randomized nature of the algorithm and the attempt to provide a bound for the error (δ) are commendable, even if incomplete.
* The visualisations (Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6) are helpful for understanding the method and its performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel and potentially valuable contribution. The PoP method addresses a real problem in semantic space construction and offers a compelling trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. While the paper has some weaknesses, they are not severe and can be addressed through further experimentation and analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  The strong strengths, particularly the novelty of the method and the encouraging empirical results, outweigh the weaknesses.
