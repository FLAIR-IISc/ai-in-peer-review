## Review of “Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction”

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. This paper presents a novel and promising approach to building semantic spaces, offering a compelling trade-off between computational efficiency and performance. While not groundbreaking in theoretical novelty, the practical benefits and clear demonstration of competitive results, coupled with a thorough empirical evaluation, make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**1. Summary & Main Contributions:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing word embeddings using random projections that, unlike existing methods, allows for subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI. The core idea is to use random vectors with only positive values, enabling weighting while maintaining computational efficiency due to the low dimensionality of the resulting spaces. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves performance comparable to more computationally expensive methods like neural embeddings and SVD on a semantic similarity task.

I identify the following key contributions:

1. **The PoP method itself:** The core idea of using positive-only random projections to allow for weighting transformations is a valuable addition to the toolkit for building semantic spaces.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The thorough evaluation on the MEN dataset, comparing PoP to various baseline methods (RI, count-based models, and implicitly neural embeddings) provides strong evidence of its effectiveness.
3. **Practical Efficiency:**  The demonstrated ability to build models much faster than traditional methods like neural embeddings is a significant practical advantage.

**2. Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The paper tackles the well-known trade-off between the performance of predictive models (like neural embeddings) and the computational cost of training them. PoP offers a viable alternative for resource-constrained scenarios or rapid prototyping.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The MEN dataset results are compelling.  The consistently competitive performance of PoP+PPMI, particularly given its efficiency, is a major strength. The ablation studies (comparing with/without PPMI, and varying dimensionality) are well-executed and informative.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The mathematical notation is appropriate, and the connection to existing work (RI, PPMI, etc.) is clearly established.  The discussion of the method's limitations and potential future work is also appreciated.
4. **Detailed Parameter Exploration:** The extensive analysis of parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) shows a deep understanding of the method and provides valuable insights for practical application. Figure 4 and 5 are very helpful in this regard.
5. **Thoughtful Discussion of Theoretical Underpinnings:** While acknowledging the lack of a complete theoretical justification, the authors engage with the existing literature on random projections and discuss potential avenues for future theoretical analysis. They provide reasonable justifications for design choices.



**3. Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset (MEN) and a single task (semantic similarity). While the results are encouraging, it’s unclear how well PoP generalizes to other datasets, tasks, or languages.  This is a common limitation for papers in this area, but it should be acknowledged.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Although the authors mention neural embeddings as a benchmark, they don’t include direct performance comparisons against commonly used pre-trained embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset. Including these would provide a more robust assessment of PoP’s competitiveness.
3. **Theoretical Novelty is Incremental:** The underlying random projection technique isn't radically new. The core contribution lies in the clever modification (positive-only values) to enable weighting. While valuable, this is an incremental rather than a breakthrough theoretical advance. The paper adequately acknowledges this.
4. **Discussion of Non-Orthogonality feels incomplete:** While identified as a potential issue, the discussion around the impact of non-orthogonality and the role of 's' feels somewhat underdeveloped. A more detailed exploration of this parameter and its effect on performance would be beneficial.



**4. Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalization:** Could you comment on the expected performance of PoP on datasets with different characteristics than MEN (e.g., different sizes, domains, or languages)? Do you anticipate needing to adjust the parameters (e.g., *s*, dimensionality) for different datasets?
2. **Baseline Comparison:** Could you add a comparison against pre-trained word embeddings like word2vec or GloVe on the MEN dataset? This would help to contextualize your results and demonstrate PoP's performance relative to widely used baselines.
3. **Non-Orthogonality:** Could you elaborate on the relationship between the parameter *s* and the degree of non-orthogonality in the projection matrix?  Is there a theoretical or empirical threshold for *s* beyond which performance degrades significantly due to non-orthogonality?  Could you explore methods to mitigate the effects of non-orthogonality?
4. **Scalability:** While you highlight the efficiency of PoP, could you provide more concrete details on the computational resources (CPU, memory, time) required to build models with different dimensionalities and using different dataset sizes? This would be helpful for readers assessing the practical applicability of the method.




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s correlation is helpful for understanding the rationale behind the choice of similarity measures.
*   The supplemental material offering to share code and embeddings is a positive aspect.




In conclusion, this is a well-executed and promising piece of work. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly through expanded evaluation and a more in-depth discussion of non-orthogonality, would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I believe it warrants acceptance.
