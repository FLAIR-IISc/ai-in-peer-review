## Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:** This paper introduces a new method called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces. PoP utilizes random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but crucially allows for subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI, which is not possible with traditional RI due to its Gaussian distribution. The authors claim to achieve competitive performance in semantic similarity assessment compared to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, while offering significant computational efficiency.  They present experiments on the MEN dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of PoP, particularly when combined with PPMI weighting.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling weighting transformations:** This is the core contribution.  The design of PoP to allow for PPMI weighting, circumventing the limitations of standard RI, is a meaningful advancement.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance with significant efficiency gains:** The paper provides experimental results on a standard benchmark (MEN) demonstrating that PoP, when combined with PPMI, achieves accuracy comparable to more computationally expensive methods, while being significantly faster.
3. **Detailed analysis of PoP’s parameters and behavior:** The exploration of the impact of dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements in the projection vectors provides valuable insights into the method’s performance and trade-offs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a real problem:** The computational cost of training neural embeddings and the limitations of traditional random projection methods are well-recognized issues in NLP. PoP offers a compelling alternative that addresses these challenges.
2. **Clear and logical presentation:** The paper is well-structured and explains the PoP method in a clear and understandable manner. The connection to existing techniques (RI, PPMI) is well established, and the mathematical formulation is appropriate.
3. **Thorough empirical evaluation:** The experiments on the MEN dataset, with varying parameters and comparisons to baseline methods, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of PoP. The inclusion of ablation studies (PoP with/without PPMI, different similarity measures) is valuable.
4. **Detailed analysis of parameters:** The investigation of dimensionality and sparsity parameters (number of non-zero elements) provides practical guidance for applying the method and highlights important considerations for optimal performance. The discussion about the impact of these parameters on vector orthogonality is insightful.
5. **Practical implications:** The claimed speed improvements are substantial and could make semantic space construction more accessible for resource-constrained environments or large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, it may not fully represent the diversity of NLP tasks where semantic spaces are used. Evaluation on other semantic similarity datasets or downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, word analogy) would strengthen the claims.
2. **Lack of theoretical justification for parameter settings:** While the authors acknowledge the need for theoretical analysis, the choice of parameters (e.g., α=0.5, s=0.01) appears somewhat arbitrary. Providing a more principled justification for these choices would increase confidence in the method’s robustness.
3. **Potential sensitivity to data characteristics:** The authors note that performance can be affected by data sparsity and the choice of context windows.  A more detailed discussion of how the method performs under different data conditions would be beneficial.
4. **Insufficient comparison to recent, efficient embedding methods:**  The paper compares against established techniques, but doesn't directly compare to more recent work focusing on fast embedding generation (e.g., fastText, or very recent sparse embedding methods).  Positioning PoP relative to these methods is important.
5. **Minor clarity issue regarding Kendall's τb:** The justification for using Kendall's τb initially, and then switching to Pearson's correlation after PPMI transformation, could be clearer. A more detailed explanation of why PPMI necessitates the change in correlation measure would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical considerations that guided the choice of α = 0.5 and s = 0.01? Is there a relationship between these parameters and the desired properties of the projected space?
2.  How does PoP perform on datasets with varying levels of data sparsity? Are there specific pre-processing steps or parameter adjustments that are recommended for sparse datasets?
3.  Have you considered evaluating PoP on downstream NLP tasks, such as text classification or word analogy, to demonstrate its utility beyond semantic similarity assessment?
4.  Could you provide a comparison of PoP’s performance and computational cost to more recent efficient embedding methods like fastText or other sparse embedding approaches?
5.  The discussion of PPMI leading to a Gaussian-like distribution and justifying the switch to Pearson’s correlation is somewhat brief. Can you elaborate on the mathematical reasoning behind this choice?

**Additional Comments:**

* The supplemental material is a welcome addition.
* The figures are generally clear and informative.
* The discussion of the limitations of the method is appreciated.
