## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces a novel method, Positive-Only Projections (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – utilizing random projections with exclusively positive values to enable weighting transformations like PPMI – is interesting and addresses a limitation of traditional random projection techniques. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of PoP in terms of computational efficiency and competitive performance compared to neural embeddings and count-based methods combined with SVD.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The PoP Method:** The introduction of a novel random projection technique (PoP) that allows for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI, overcoming a key limitation of existing methods like RI. This is the primary contribution, and the most significant aspect of the work.
2. **Empirical Validation with PPMI:** Demonstrating that PoP, when combined with PPMI weighting, achieves competitive results on a semantic similarity task (MEN), rivaling more computationally expensive methods. The comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies and parameter tuning, provides strong evidence for the method’s effectiveness.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Parameters:** A thorough investigation of the method's sensitivity to parameters (dimensionality 'm' and the sparsity parameter 's'), providing practical guidance for implementation and optimization. This detailed analysis offers valuable insights into the behavior of PoP and informs future research directions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance:** The proposed PoP method addresses a clear gap in the existing literature regarding random projections and their compatibility with weighting schemes.  The problem of efficiently constructing predictive semantic spaces from count-based data is a long-standing challenge in NLP, and PoP offers a compelling solution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a robust empirical evaluation on the MEN dataset, demonstrating that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance compared to established methods. The comparisons to RI and count-based models are particularly convincing, highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Parameter Tuning:** The comprehensive analysis of the influence of dimensionality and sparsity (through 's') is a major strength. This level of detail is crucial for practical application and reproducibility. The inclusion of figures illustrating performance variations is highly valuable.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible. The motivation for the work is well-articulated, and the connection to existing literature is effectively established.
5. **Computational Efficiency:** The emphasis on computational efficiency is a significant benefit. The ability to build models in seconds or minutes, compared to hours or days for neural embeddings, is a compelling advantage, especially for resource-constrained environments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset (MEN) and a single task (semantic similarity). While the results are promising, it’s unclear how well PoP would generalize to other datasets, tasks (e.g., analogy, word categorization), or languages.  A broader evaluation would strengthen the claims about the method's generalizability.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of a complete theoretical understanding of PoP, this remains a significant weakness. Providing some theoretical bounds on the approximation error or offering insights into the properties of the resulting semantic space would significantly enhance the paper. The comparison to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma is a good start, but further exploration is needed.
3. **Choice of Kendall's τb:** The justification for initially using Kendall’s τb as the similarity measure is somewhat convoluted. While the explanation relating to non-Gaussian distributions is reasonable, the subsequent switch to Pearson’s correlation after PPMI transformation feels ad-hoc. A more principled justification for the choice of similarity measures would be beneficial.  The connection to the underlying distributional properties could be more clearly explained.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The detailed analysis reveals a sensitivity to the 's' parameter. This could make the method more challenging to deploy in practice, as finding the optimal 's' may require significant tuning. Further investigation into methods for automatically selecting 's' would be valuable.
5. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to RI and count-based methods is thorough, the paper doesn’t directly compare PoP to more recent and sophisticated embedding techniques (e.g., fastText, GloVe) on the MEN dataset.  This would provide a more comprehensive benchmark for evaluating PoP’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:** What is the performance of PoP on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353)? How does it perform on tasks beyond semantic similarity, such as word analogy or word categorization?
2. **Theoretical Bounds:** Are there any preliminary efforts towards establishing theoretical bounds on the approximation error introduced by the PoP projection?
3. **Automatic Parameter Tuning:** Have you explored any methods for automatically selecting the optimal value of the sparsity parameter 's'? Could techniques like cross-validation be used to optimize this parameter?
4. **Scalability:** What is the computational complexity of PoP when scaling to very large vocabularies and corpora? How does it compare to the scalability of other methods like fastText or GloVe?
5. **Impact of Context Window:**  How sensitive is PoP’s performance to the size of the context window, and does this sensitivity interact with the choice of the ‘s’ parameter?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation could be slightly improved for clarity. For example, consistently using subscripts for variables would be helpful.
* The discussion of the relationship between PPMI and Gaussianization could be expanded.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters and their impact on performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and promising paper that introduces a novel and potentially valuable method for constructing semantic spaces. The empirical results are compelling, and the detailed analysis provides valuable insights. While the limited scope of evaluation and lack of complete theoretical justification are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the significant contributions of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  Addressing the questions posed to the authors will further strengthen the paper and its impact.
