This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces via random projections, with the key advantage of allowing subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI – something traditional random indexing methods cannot do. The authors claim that PoP offers a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, achieving competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel constraint on random projection matrices:** The core idea of enforcing positive values in the projection matrix to enable weighting transformations is interesting and potentially valuable.
2. **Demonstration of PPMI application to random projections:** Showing that PPMI can be effectively applied to spaces generated by PoP, overcoming a limitation of existing random projection techniques, is a concrete contribution.
3. **Empirical comparison to baselines:** The paper presents a comparison to RI and suggests competitive performance with more computationally expensive methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a known limitation of Random Indexing:** The inability to apply weighting schemes like PPMI to standard random projections is a valid and well-recognized issue. PoP directly addresses this, offering a potential pathway to combine the efficiency of random projections with the effectiveness of weighting.
2. **Computational Efficiency Claim:** The authors consistently highlight the speed advantage of PoP, which is a strong selling point, particularly for resource-constrained settings or large datasets. The reported training times (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural nets) are compelling *if substantiated by thorough benchmarking*.
3. **Clear Methodological Description:** The paper provides a relatively clear and detailed description of the PoP method, including the mathematical formulation and implementation details. The step-by-step explanation of the process is helpful.
4. **Use of Established Evaluation Metric:** Utilizing the MEN dataset and Spearman’s rank correlation provides a standard and recognizable benchmark for evaluating semantic similarity models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to RI is present, but feels somewhat superficial. The authors claim competitiveness with neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, but provide *no direct comparison* to these approaches. Simply stating the performance is “competitive” is insufficient; the authors *must* include results from established baselines (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, fastText, SVD on PPMI-weighted matrices) to support this claim. Without these, the performance gains of PoP are difficult to assess. The current comparison heavily relies on stating that RI and count-based methods are inadequate, without demonstrating a clear advantage *over readily available alternatives*.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Parameter Choices:** The choice of α = 0.5 and the value of ‘s’ are somewhat arbitrary. The authors offer some rationale in the later sections, but a more systematic exploration of these parameters and their impact on performance is needed. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the method to the number of non-zero elements is noted, but not thoroughly investigated.
3. **Overstated Novelty:** While the positive-only constraint is interesting, the paper often presents PoP as a highly innovative breakthrough. However, the underlying concept of random projections is well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the constraint itself, but the overall approach builds heavily on existing work. The authors need to temper the claims of innovation.
4. **Theoretical Justification is Weak:** The discussion of theoretical underpinnings is largely aspirational. The acknowledgement that they cannot provide bounds on the error (δ) is concerning. While referencing relevant work on randomized algorithms, the paper lacks a concrete theoretical analysis of PoP’s properties. The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma feels tenuous and unsupported by formal analysis.
5. **Kendall's τb Choice is Not Convincing:** The justification for using Kendall’s τb over cosine similarity is weakly supported. While the argument about non-Gaussian distributions is valid, it is not clear why τb is the *best* alternative. Further investigation into the sensitivity of results to the choice of similarity measure is warranted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparison:** Please provide a direct comparison of PoP (with and without PPMI weighting) to established word embedding methods (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, fastText) and SVD applied to PPMI-weighted matrices on the MEN dataset. Include performance metrics (Spearman's correlation) and computational time for each method.
2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** Present a more detailed analysis of the impact of the ‘s’ parameter and dimensionality (m) on performance. How does the optimal value of ‘s’ change with different corpus sizes or data characteristics?
3. **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the performance differences between PoP and RI are statistically significant, and not due to random chance.
4. **Theoretical Analysis:** Can you elaborate on efforts to derive theoretical bounds for the error (δ) in PoP? If a full analysis is not possible, can you provide any preliminary insights or potential avenues for future research?
5. **Choice of Similarity Metric:** Justify the selection of Kendall’s τb more rigorously. Have you experimented with other similarity measures (e.g., cosine, Pearson correlation) on PPMI-transformed spaces, and what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting and writing style are generally good, but could be more concise.
* The supplemental material section is unnecessary at this stage.
* The references are comprehensive.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting idea with potential, but the lack of thorough evaluation and theoretical justification prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including a comprehensive comparison to established baselines, is crucial for demonstrating the value of PoP. I lean towards rejection at this time, but would be open to reconsideration if the authors can adequately address the concerns raised.
