This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections, with the key innovation being the use of positive-only random vectors enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI. The authors claim that PoP offers a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings and traditional count-based methods with dimensionality reduction, achieving competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks. The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, and a secondary contribution is a demonstration of its effectiveness on the MEN relatedness test.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potential for Efficiency:** The core idea of using positive-only projections to allow weighting transformations on randomly projected spaces is potentially valuable. If the reported speedups are accurate, this could be a significant advantage over training neural embeddings or performing SVD on large count matrices. The claim of building models in seconds or minutes is compelling, assuming this holds across different datasets and parameter settings.
2. **Sound Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of count-based models (sparsity, high dimensionality) and the computational cost of neural methods. The motivation for seeking a more efficient and discriminative alternative is well-established. The connection to existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) is also helpful for contextualizing the proposed approach.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a reasonably thorough evaluation using the MEN dataset, a standard benchmark for semantic similarity. The comparison to RI is particularly relevant, demonstrating PoP’s advantage in enabling weighting. The exploration of PPMI weighting and the comparison to dense PPMI methods is also a strength.
4. **Detailed Methodological Description:** The paper provides a clear and relatively detailed description of the PoP method, including the mathematical formulation and the step-by-step construction process. The discussion of the parameter 's' and its influence on performance demonstrates some awareness of the method’s nuances.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Robustness & Limited Evaluation:** The primary weakness is the limited scope of the evaluation. Performance is reported on *only one* dataset (MEN) and using *only one* similarity measure (Kendall’s τb initially, then Pearson’s r after PPMI weighting). While the authors mention other potential similarity measures, there's no exploration of performance across diverse tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) or datasets. A single dataset result does not provide sufficient evidence of generalizability. The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., ‘s’) is also cursory; more systematic experimentation is needed.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to baselines is weak. While RI is included, there's no comparison to simpler, widely-used baselines like Skip-gram or GloVe (even pre-trained embeddings). The authors dismiss neural embeddings due to their computational cost, but don’t demonstrate whether PoP actually *outperforms* them given sufficient resources. Simply stating they are "competitive" without providing direct comparisons is insufficient. The authors also do not explicitly report training/computational time for the baselines they *do* compare against.
3. **Theoretical Justification is Limited:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a strong theoretical foundation for PoP, relying instead on empirical results. While acknowledging previous work on randomized algorithms, the authors don’t provide a rigorous analysis of the error bounds or the properties of the proposed random projection. The explanation relating to Gaussian distributions and the choice of Kendall’s τb feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a deeper theoretical grounding. The claims about the dimensionality 'm' representing capacity are intuitive but unsubstantiated.
4. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Nuance:** The paper sometimes makes strong claims (e.g., “dramatically enhance the performance”) that are not fully supported by the results. The discussion of PPMI weighting feels overly optimistic, and the limited exploration of hyperparameters (like 's') suggests that the method's performance may be sensitive to parameter tuning. The text could benefit from more nuanced language and a more cautious tone.
5. **Presentation & Redundancy:** The paper contains some redundancy, particularly in the introduction. The connection to established methods (Harris, distributional hypothesis, etc.) feels somewhat perfunctory and doesn't add significant value. The discussion of related work could be more concise.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational time required to train PoP models with different dimensionality settings compared to training Skip-gram or GloVe embeddings on the same corpus?
2.  Have you experimented with different values of 's' in a more systematic way, and can you provide a clear plot showing the relationship between 's' and performance on the MEN dataset?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the PoP method to the choice of context window size?
4.  Can you evaluate the PoP method on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353) or downstream tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification)?
5.  Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the theoretical properties of the PoP method, including an attempt to derive error bounds or characterize the distribution of the projected vectors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is generally clear, but could be more consistent.
*   The supplemental material statement is good.
*   The discussion of potential future work is reasonable.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting idea, but its limited evaluation and lack of theoretical grounding prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The potential benefits of PoP (efficiency, ease of use) are intriguing, but require more compelling evidence. I recommend rejection in its current form, but would encourage resubmission after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly with a more comprehensive evaluation and a stronger theoretical foundation.
