## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. It presents a novel and potentially valuable technique (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces that addresses limitations of existing methods. While the theoretical grounding could be stronger, the empirical results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance with significantly reduced computational cost. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the method and experimental setup.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for dimensionality reduction and semantic space construction. Unlike traditional random projection techniques (RI, RRI), PoP uses random vectors with only positive values, enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* projection – something previously impossible. The primary contribution is this ability to combine the efficiency of random projections with the effectiveness of PPMI weighting. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that PoP, even without weighting, outperforms RI, and achieves comparable results to more complex methods (neural embeddings, SVD-weighted counts) when combined with PPMI, *at a fraction of the computational cost*. A tertiary contribution is the detailed analysis of the method’s parameters and performance characteristics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Addressing a Gap:**  The core idea of creating a random projection method compatible with post-projection weighting is genuinely novel. Existing random projection methods are fundamentally limited in this respect due to their Gaussian or Cauchy distributions. PoP cleverly circumvents this limitation with its positive-only vector construction, opening up new possibilities for efficient semantic space building.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the speed advantage of PoP.  The authors clearly articulate the computational bottleneck of applying weighting/factorization techniques to high-dimensional count data and show how PoP mitigates this by performing the projection *first* onto a lower-dimensional space. The claim of building models in “seconds or minutes” vs. “hours, days, or months” is compelling.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results on the MEN dataset are solid. Achieving comparable performance to neural embeddings and SVD-weighted counts *with significantly less computational effort* is a substantial achievement. The inclusion of RI as a baseline strengthens this argument.
4. **Thorough Empirical Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results. The authors investigate the influence of key parameters (dimensionality 'm', number of non-zero elements 's') and provide insightful observations about their impact on performance, including the trade-off between sparsity and orthogonality.  The discussion surrounding the choice of similarity measure (Kendall's τb vs. Pearson's r) after PPMI transformation is particularly well-reasoned.
5. **Clear Explanation & Well-Structured:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is explained clearly, even for someone not deeply familiar with random projections. The use of equations and diagrams aids in understanding the process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Foundation:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis of PoP's properties.  While the authors rightly point to the difficulty of deriving bounds on the error (δ), a more in-depth discussion of the underlying assumptions and potential limitations of the method would be beneficial.  The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, while mentioned, feels superficial.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is solely based on the MEN dataset. While widely used, this dataset represents only one type of semantic relatedness task. Evaluating PoP on a more diverse range of tasks (e.g., word analogy, topic modeling) would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity analysis reveals that PoP's performance is influenced by the choice of parameters (m and s). The guidelines for selecting these parameters appear somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic approach to parameter tuning, perhaps using a validation set, would be valuable.
4. **Limited Comparison to Simpler Baselines:**  The comparison focuses heavily on sophisticated methods like neural embeddings and SVD. Comparing PoP to simpler, readily available baselines like simple PPMI weighting without dimensionality reduction could provide a clearer picture of the added value of PoP.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Justification:** Can you elaborate on the potential avenues for a more rigorous theoretical analysis of PoP? Are there existing results in random matrix theory that could be leveraged to provide bounds on the error (δ)?
2. **Generalizability:** Do you have any preliminary results on the performance of PoP on datasets beyond MEN?  If not, are you planning to conduct such experiments?
3. **Parameter Tuning:** Could you provide more details about the process used to select the parameters 'm' and 's' for the experiments reported in the paper?  Was a separate validation set used?
4. **Ablation Study:**  Could you present results comparing PoP to a simple PPMI weighting without dimensionality reduction to better quantify the benefit of PoP?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Figure 3 could be improved for clarity.
*   The references are well-cited, but a quick check for completeness would be useful.




In conclusion, this is a strong submission with a promising new technique. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the theoretical justification and expanding the evaluation, would further enhance the quality of the paper and increase its impact.
