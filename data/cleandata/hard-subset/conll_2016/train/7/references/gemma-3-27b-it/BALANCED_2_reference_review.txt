## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections, designed to overcome limitations of existing techniques like RI and address the computational cost of neural embeddings. The core idea – using positive-only random vectors to enable weighting transformations like PPMI – is interesting and potentially valuable. The paper provides a detailed method description, empirical evaluation on the MEN relatedness test, and a thorough discussion of the technique's properties.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The PoP Method:** The primary contribution is the introduction of the PoP method itself, a novel approach to random projections that allows for subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI, something not possible with traditional random projection methods. This addresses a key limitation of earlier work.
2. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The paper provides a robust empirical evaluation of PoP, comparing it against RI and demonstrating its effectiveness, particularly when combined with PPMI weighting. The detailed ablation studies exploring the influence of parameters like dimensionality and non-zero element count are valuable.
3. **Detailed Discussion & Theoretical Grounding (Attempt):** The authors provide a detailed discussion of the method’s properties, its relationship to existing techniques, and attempt to ground it within theoretical frameworks of randomized algorithms and distributional semantics.  While incomplete, this effort demonstrates a strong understanding of the broader research landscape.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  The core idea of using positive-only random projections to enable PPMI weighting is genuinely novel. This is a clever solution to a known limitation of standard random projection techniques.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is well-designed. The use of the MEN dataset, comparison with relevant baselines (RI), and thorough parameter exploration provide strong evidence supporting the method's effectiveness.  The inclusion of ablation studies is particularly commendable.
3. **Detailed Method Description:**  The paper provides a clear and mathematically precise description of the PoP method, making it easy to understand and potentially reproduce. The equations and explanations are well-presented.
4. **Thorough Discussion:** The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of their work, relating it to concepts like randomized algorithms, distributional semantics, and information theory.  The discussion of the trade-offs and limitations is also appreciated.
5. **Practical Implications:** The emphasis on computational efficiency and the ability to achieve competitive results without extensive training is a significant practical advantage. This makes PoP an attractive option for resource-constrained environments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:**  While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  Performance on a wider range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word analogy, metaphor detection) and potentially different languages would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
2. **Theoretical Gaps:** The attempt to theoretically ground PoP is a strength, but the discussion remains somewhat incomplete. A more rigorous analysis of the error bounds (δ) and the distribution of the projected spaces would be valuable. The comparisons to existing random projection theory could be more explicit and detailed.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of PoP appears to be sensitive to parameter choices (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements). While the paper explores this, a more systematic approach to parameter optimization and a clearer set of guidelines for choosing appropriate values would be helpful.
4. **Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s r:**  The justification for initially using Kendall’s τb is reasonable given the non-Gaussian distribution of the initial projections. However, the switch to Pearson’s r after PPMI transformation feels slightly ad-hoc. A more thorough investigation of the impact of similarity measure on performance, perhaps with a theoretical justification, would be beneficial.
5. **Presentation and Redundancy:** The paper is quite lengthy and contains some redundancy.  The detailed discussion, while valuable, could be more concisely presented.  Some of the justifications for design choices feel repetitive.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of PoP compared to other methods (RI, neural embeddings, SVD-based approaches) for large-scale datasets?  Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with corpus size and vocabulary size?
2.  You mention the potential for "derandomization" techniques to improve PoP. Could you elaborate on specific approaches you envision and how they might be integrated into the method?
3.  What is the impact of different PPMI smoothing parameters on PoP’s performance? Did you explore the sensitivity of the method to these parameters?
4.  The paper shows an improvement when PPMI is applied. Have you explored other weighting schemes beyond PPMI, and do they offer comparable improvements?
5.  In Figure 4, the performance of PoP+PPMI+Pearson plateaus after a certain dimension.  Is there a diminishing return to increasing the dimensionality further, and if so, what factors contribute to this?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of some equations is slightly inconsistent.
*   The figures are generally clear, but could benefit from more informative captions.
*   Consider providing code and data alongside the paper to facilitate reproducibility.
* The phrase "P6⊥" is not defined in the text.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and promising method for constructing semantic spaces. The PoP technique addresses a significant limitation of existing random projection methods and demonstrates competitive performance with a considerable efficiency advantage. While some theoretical gaps and limitations in the evaluation remain, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions above. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics, and I believe this work has the potential to be widely adopted.
