This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces via random projections. The core idea – using a random projection matrix with only positive values – is surprisingly simple yet potentially impactful, as it allows for the application of weighting techniques like PPMI *after* the projection, something standard random indexing methods cannot do. The primary contribution is this enabling of PPMI weighting within a random projection framework, leading to competitive results with significantly reduced computational cost. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of PoP's performance, showing it outperforms standard RI and approaches the accuracy of more complex methods like neural embeddings, all while being substantially faster.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The central idea of using positive-only projections is genuinely novel. While random projections are well-established, the constraint to positive values to allow subsequent weighting transforms is a clever and insightful twist. It bypasses a key limitation of existing methods and opens up a new design space. This is the strongest aspect of the paper; it isn’t incremental, it's a reframing.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly argues and demonstrates the computational benefits of PoP. The speed advantage over neural methods and even SVD-based weighting is significant. In a landscape increasingly concerned with sustainability and accessibility, methods that reduce computational burden are highly valuable.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a solid empirical evaluation using the MEN dataset, demonstrating PoP’s competitive performance, particularly when combined with PPMI weighting. The comparison to RI and the acknowledgement of neural net performance provides appropriate context. The ablation studies (figures 4 & 5) are particularly useful for understanding the impact of various parameters.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind PoP, grounding it in the broader context of distributional semantics and the challenges of count-based models. The connection to the limitations of traditional random indexing is well articulated.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Theoretical Implications:** The authors don’t shy away from acknowledging the lack of a complete theoretical justification for PoP, but they provide insightful discussion and draw parallels to related work (e.g., Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma). The discussion of the relationship between sparsity, orthogonality, and performance is thoughtful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, it’s relatively limited in scope. Evaluation on a wider range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word analogy, relatedness to word embeddings) would strengthen the claims of PoP’s generalizability.
2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of PoP appears sensitive to parameters like ‘s’ (the probability of a non-zero element) and the dimensionality ‘m.’ A more systematic exploration of the parameter space and guidelines for setting these parameters would be helpful. The discussion feels slightly *post hoc* rather than driven by principled parameter selection.
3. **Theoretical Gaps:** The lack of a formal theoretical analysis of PoP is a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, providing even preliminary theoretical insights (e.g., bounds on the error introduced by the positive-only constraint) would be valuable. While I value novelty over perfection, a bit more effort here would be reassuring.
4. **Kendall’s τb Justification:** The justification for using Kendall’s τb initially feels a little convoluted. The leap from Gaussian projections to non-Gaussian and then to τb needs more explicit explanation, even if it's a heuristic argument. The later justification relating to PPMI transformation makes more sense, but the initial reasoning is weaker.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you explored other similarity measures beyond Kendall's τb and Pearson's r? If so, what were the results?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of PoP compared to other methods, specifically breaking down the cost of each step (projection, weighting, similarity calculation)?
3.  You mention the possibility of "derandomization" techniques. Can you elaborate on specific derandomization strategies you envision applying to PoP?
4.  In the context of large-scale corpora, what strategies would you recommend for efficiently handling the memory requirements of the projection matrix, even with its relatively low dimensionality?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplemental material should *definitely* include the code for reproducibility.
*   Figure 3 is excellent for illustrating the effect of PPMI weighting.
*   The consistent emphasis on computational efficiency is commendable.



Overall, this is a promising and innovative paper. The core idea is strong, the empirical results are encouraging, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature. While some theoretical gaps and limitations in evaluation exist, the novelty of the approach and potential for impact outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on addressing the questions raised and a commitment to providing open-source code for reproducibility. I’m willing to overlook the less-than-complete theoretical justification because the paper introduces a genuinely new and potentially valuable technique.
