## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections. The core idea – using positive-only random vectors to allow for weighting transformations like PPMI – is interesting and addresses a known limitation of traditional random projection techniques. The paper provides a thorough description of the method, along with empirical evaluation on the MEN dataset, demonstrating competitive performance compared to neural embeddings and other count-based methods, with a significant speed advantage.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling weighting transformations:** This is the primary contribution. The clever constraint of using only positive values in the projection matrix allows for the application of PPMI and other weighting schemes that are incompatible with standard random projections (RI, RRI, etc.). This is a non-trivial modification with significant practical implications.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:**  The paper provides solid empirical evidence showing that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves results comparable to more computationally expensive methods on a standard semantic similarity benchmark (MEN). The speed advantage is a strong selling point.
3. **Detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity:** The extensive experimentation with dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements provides valuable insight into the behavior of the method and practical guidance for its application. The discussion of the trade-offs between sparsity, orthogonality, and performance is particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and comprehensive explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and evaluation. The mathematical notation is used effectively, and the connection to existing work (RI, PPMI, etc.) is well-established.
2. **Strong motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing methods (count-based models, standard random projections, neural embeddings) and positions PoP as a viable alternative that addresses these limitations. The discussion of the heavy-tailed distributions and the need for dimensionality reduction is compelling.
3. **Thorough experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, varying key parameters and comparing PoP to relevant baselines. The ablation studies (e.g., with and without PPMI) are particularly valuable.
4. **Practical relevance:** The reported speed improvements over neural embeddings are significant and make PoP a potentially attractive option for resource-constrained environments or large-scale applications.
5. **Detailed analysis and discussion:**  The paper goes beyond simply reporting results and provides a nuanced discussion of the method’s strengths, weaknesses, and potential avenues for future research.  The investigation of the impact of `s` (number of non-zero elements) and its connection to vector orthogonality is a particularly strong point.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited dataset:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MEN). While MEN is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see the method’s performance on a more diverse set of tasks and datasets to assess its generalizability. This is a common issue, but worth noting.
2. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a theoretical foundation for PoP. While empirical results are promising, a theoretical analysis of the method’s properties (e.g., convergence, error bounds) would significantly strengthen the paper.  The analogies drawn to other randomized algorithms are helpful but not a substitute for rigorous mathematical analysis.
3. **Kendall's τb vs. Pearson’s r:** The justification for using Kendall's τb initially feels a bit convoluted. While the explanation regarding non-Gaussian distributions is valid, the switch to Pearson's r *after* PPMI transformation feels inconsistent. A more principled argument for the choice of similarity measure in each case would be helpful.  It could be clarified why PPMI transforms the data to a distribution where Pearson's is appropriate.
4. **Figure 3 clarity:** The histograms in Figure 3 are a little difficult to interpret.  Adding labels to the x-axes (indicating the value ranges of the weights) would improve clarity. Also, a more direct comparison of the shapes (e.g., showing overlaid distributions) might be more informative.
5. **Incremental learning limitations:** Although the paper mentions PoP's incremental nature, the evaluation focuses on offline construction. Exploring the performance of truly incremental updates (e.g., adding new data points over time) would be valuable and highlight a key advantage of the method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:**  Have you experimented with PoP on other semantic similarity datasets or downstream NLP tasks (e.g., text classification, topic modeling)?  If so, what were the results?
2. **Theoretical Analysis:** Are there any ongoing efforts to develop a theoretical understanding of PoP’s behavior, particularly concerning the choice of `s` and its impact on the quality of the projected space?
3. **Computational Complexity:**  While you mention the speed advantage of PoP, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the method, compared to other approaches, would be helpful.  Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with the size of the corpus and the dimensionality of the projected space?
4. **Incremental Updates:** Can you provide more details on how PoP handles incremental updates (i.e., adding new data points to the model after it has been initially constructed)?  Are there any challenges or considerations specific to incremental learning with PoP?
5. **Choice of α:**  Why was α = 0.5 chosen? Was any experimentation done with different values?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "dense PMI-weighted models" in Figure 2 could be clarified. What specific implementation of dense PMI weighting was used for comparison?
*   The mention of "serialization of various weighting techniques" in the Discussion section is intriguing. Could you provide a concrete example of how this might be implemented?



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to semantic space construction. The empirical results are compelling, and the detailed analysis provides valuable insights. While the lack of theoretical justification and the limited dataset are weaknesses, they are not critical.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above. The potential impact of PoP, particularly its speed and efficiency, makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
