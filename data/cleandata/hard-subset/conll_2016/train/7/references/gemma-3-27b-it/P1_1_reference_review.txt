## Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP utilizes random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but crucially generates projections with strictly positive values. This allows for the application of weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which are incompatible with traditional RI due to its centering around zero. The authors demonstrate that PoP, particularly when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks compared to neural embeddings and traditional count-based methods with SVD, while being significantly faster and less computationally demanding.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) that overcomes a key limitation of existing methods:** The ability to apply weighting transformations like PPMI to random projection-based models, unlocking a pathway to more discriminative and accurate semantic spaces.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:** Showing that PoP + PPMI achieves results comparable to much more computationally expensive methods on a standard semantic similarity benchmark (MEN).
3. **Highlighting the efficiency benefits:**  The paper clearly emphasizes the speed advantage of PoP, enabling rapid construction of models that would be impractical with traditional approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant limitation in the field:** Existing random projection methods suffer from incompatibility with crucial weighting techniques. PoP directly tackles this limitation in a conceptually simple and elegant way.  The argument that standard RI's zero-centering prevents PPMI application is well-articulated.
2. **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a clear and well-structured experimental setup, comparing PoP to relevant baselines (count-based, RI) and demonstrating its effectiveness. The use of the MEN test set is appropriate and common in this area.  The inclusion of ablation studies (varying dimensionality, PPMI application) strengthens the claims.
3. **Clear and concise writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation, method, and results.  The inclusion of the matrix notation and equation for PPMI enhances clarity.
4. **Emphasis on efficiency:** The speed benefits of PoP are a major advantage, particularly for resource-constrained settings or applications requiring rapid model updates. This aspect is repeatedly and effectively highlighted.
5. **Insightful discussion on the method's behavior and parameters:** The exploration of the effect of dimensionality (`m`) and the number of non-zero elements (`s`) is valuable and demonstrates a good understanding of the method’s nuances. The discussion about orthogonality is a particularly interesting observation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core idea:** While the *combination* of positive-only projections and PPMI is novel, the underlying random projection concept itself is not.  The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how PoP relates to existing random projection techniques beyond simply stating the difference in centering.  A more detailed analysis of *why* positive-only projections enable PPMI would be beneficial.
2. **Lack of theoretical grounding:** The authors acknowledge the lack of a formal theoretical analysis of PoP’s properties (e.g., error bounds). While a full derivation may be beyond the scope of the paper, a more in-depth discussion of the potential theoretical implications and future research directions in this area would be valuable.  The analogy to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma feels superficial without further elaboration.
3. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The evaluation focuses solely on the MEN dataset.  While this is a standard benchmark, performance on additional semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) or even more complex NLP tasks (e.g., text classification, analogy completion) would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
4. **Kendall's τb justification is not entirely convincing:** The justification for using Kendall’s τb initially feels somewhat contrived. While the explanation regarding non-Gaussian distributions is reasonable, it's not fully clear why τb is *superior* to cosine in the initial PoP setup (before PPMI). This point could be clarified.
5. **The connection between 's' and orthogonality, and its impact on performance, needs more explanation:** While interesting, the observation about the effect of `s` on orthogonality and performance requires a more detailed explanation. The paper states that increasing `s` can *hurt* performance, but doesn't fully explain the mechanism behind this.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Analysis:** What are the primary challenges in developing a formal theoretical analysis of PoP, specifically concerning error bounds or properties of the resulting semantic space? Are there existing theoretical frameworks that could be adapted to analyze PoP?
2. **Alternative Similarity Measures:** You mention that Pearson’s correlation works better with Gaussian distributions. Could you provide more details on the experiments that led you to choose Kendall’s τb *before* the PPMI transformation?  Were other non-parametric correlation measures considered?
3. **Generalizability:** Have you experimented with PoP on datasets beyond MEN, or applied it to downstream NLP tasks? If so, could you share those results? If not, what are your plans for exploring the generalizability of PoP?
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** You mention that the optimal value of `s` depends on the sparsity of the original data. Can you provide any guidance or heuristics for choosing an appropriate value of `s` based on the characteristics of a given corpus?
5. **Orthogonality and Distortion:** Could you elaborate on the connection between the orthogonality of index vectors, the value of 's', and the potential for distortion in similarity measurements?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplemental material offering code and embeddings would be a significant addition.
*   The paper could benefit from a table summarizing the key parameters and their impact on performance.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in random projection techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and promising paper with a novel contribution.  While the core idea isn’t groundbreaking, the practical benefits of enabling PPMI weighting in random projections and the demonstrated performance gains are significant.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing more theoretical justification and expanding the evaluation – would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
