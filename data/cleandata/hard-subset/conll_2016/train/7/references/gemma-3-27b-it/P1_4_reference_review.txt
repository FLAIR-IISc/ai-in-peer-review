## Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel method, Positive-Only Projection (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces. PoP leverages random projections, similar to existing techniques like RI, but crucially allows for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI due to the properties of its projection matrix. The authors claim PoP provides a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings and traditional count-based methods with matrix factorization, achieving competitive results on semantic similarity tasks while significantly reducing training time. The paper details the method, presents experimental results on the MEN relatedness test, and discusses the method’s behavior and potential extensions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling weighting transformations:** The core innovation lies in designing a random projection method that avoids the limitations of previous methods (RI, RRI, etc.) which were incompatible with weighting techniques like PPMI. This opens up possibilities for combining the efficiency of random projections with the representational power of weighting.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:** The paper demonstrates that PoP, combined with PPMI weighting, achieves performance comparable to more computationally expensive methods on a standard semantic similarity benchmark.
3. **Detailed analysis of method parameters and behavior:**  The authors provide a thorough investigation of the method's performance as a function of dimensionality and sparsity, offering valuable insights into its practical application and limitations.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a real problem: Computational cost of semantic space construction:** The paper tackles the well-known trade-off between accuracy and computational expense in building word embeddings. Offering a fast alternative without sacrificing too much performance is a valuable contribution.
2. **Clear and well-explained method:** The mathematical formulation of PoP is clearly presented, and the intuition behind the method is easy to grasp. The authors do a good job of connecting PoP to existing techniques like RI and explaining its key differences.
3. **Comprehensive experimentation:** The authors evaluate PoP on a standard benchmark (MEN) and provide a detailed analysis of the method's behavior under different parameter settings. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing with and without PPMI, varying dimensionality) strengthens the results.
4. **Insightful discussion of theoretical implications:**  The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical foundation for PoP and discusses its connection to related work on randomized algorithms and random projections. The discussion on the relationship between PPMI, Gaussian distributions, and correlation measures is particularly strong.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The speed gains reported – building models in seconds or minutes instead of hours/days – are significant and make this method potentially very useful for rapid prototyping and resource-constrained environments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single benchmark (MEN). While MEN is a standard test, it doesn’t fully capture the breadth of semantic relationships and tasks that word embeddings are used for.  Testing on more diverse tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification, or downstream applications) would greatly strengthen the claims.
2. **Lack of comparison to strong baselines:** While comparisons to RI and count-based methods are included, there's a lack of comparison to more recent and powerful fastText or GloVe implementations.  Demonstrating superiority or comparable performance to these readily available methods is crucial.
3. **Parameter sensitivity & guidance:** While the authors explore the influence of *m* and *s*, the optimal parameter settings seem heavily dependent on the specific dataset and task.  More guidance on parameter tuning and a clearer understanding of the trade-offs between dimensionality, sparsity, and performance would be beneficial.  The statement in the conclusion regarding optimal *s* values feels somewhat heuristic.
4. **Theoretical justification still lacking:** The authors acknowledge the lack of theoretical guarantees for PoP. While this is acceptable at this stage, further investigation into the properties of the projection matrix and the resulting semantic space would be highly valuable.  The connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma could be explored further.
5. **Kendall’s Tau vs Pearson’s correlation:** The justification for switching from Kendall’s Tau to Pearson’s correlation after PPMI weighting, while explained, feels slightly ad-hoc. A more rigorous discussion of the statistical assumptions behind each measure and their appropriateness for the transformed data would be useful.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Evaluation:** Could you provide results on additional semantic similarity datasets or downstream tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) to demonstrate the generalizability of PoP?
2. **Baselines:**  How does PoP compare to fastText or GloVe in terms of performance and training time on the MEN dataset and other potential evaluation benchmarks?
3. **Parameter Tuning:** Can you provide a more concrete recipe or guideline for selecting optimal values for *m* and *s* based on dataset characteristics (e.g., corpus size, vocabulary size)?  What impact does the choice of PPMI parameters have on performance?
4. **Theoretical Analysis:** Are you planning to investigate the theoretical properties of the PoP projection matrix and its impact on the resulting semantic space?  Specifically, can you provide any bounds on the distortion introduced by the projection?
5. **Scalability:** How does PoP scale with very large vocabularies and corpora? Are there any practical limitations related to memory usage or processing time as the dataset size grows?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplemental material offer is appreciated. Providing the code and embeddings will greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   Figure 3 is helpful in visualizing the effect of PPMI on the weight distributions.
*   The discussion of derandomization techniques is an intriguing direction for future work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper presenting a novel and potentially valuable method for constructing semantic spaces. While the evaluation is currently limited, the clear explanation, thorough analysis, and competitive performance warrant acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly expanding the evaluation and providing stronger baselines) would further strengthen the paper and its impact. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.
