## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel method for constructing semantic spaces called Positive-Only Projection (PoP).  PoP is presented as an incremental random projection technique designed to overcome limitations of existing methods like RI, RRI, and ISA, specifically the inability to easily incorporate weighting schemes like PPMI. The core idea is to use random projections with only positive values, allowing for subsequent PPMI weighting. The authors demonstrate that PoP, particularly when combined with PPMI and Kendall’s τb or Pearson correlation, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity assessment (MEN test) while being significantly faster and less computationally expensive than neural embedding approaches or traditional count-based methods with matrix factorization. The paper extensively details the method, provides a mathematical formulation, and includes empirical evaluation showcasing PoP’s performance and sensitivity to its parameters.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Projection Technique (PoP):** The introduction of Positive-Only Projection, a random projection method explicitly designed to be compatible with weighting schemes like PPMI, is the primary contribution.  This addresses a limitation in existing random projection techniques.
2. **Empirical Validation and Parameter Exploration:** The thorough empirical evaluation of PoP on the MEN dataset, along with a detailed exploration of its parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements), provides valuable insights into its behavior and optimal configuration. The systematic study of these parameters is a significant strength.
3. **Demonstration of PPMI Compatibility:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that PoP enables the effective use of PPMI weighting, leading to competitive performance in semantic similarity tasks, bridging the gap between the efficiency of random projections and the accuracy of weighted count-based models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Clear Limitation:** The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods – the incompatibility with weighting techniques – and provides a clear solution with PoP. This targeted approach is commendable.
2. **Detailed Methodological Description:** The method is described thoroughly and mathematically, making it easy to understand and potentially reproduce.  Equation 1 and Equation 2 are particularly helpful in clarifying the process.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is well-designed, employing a standard benchmark (MEN) and conducting a detailed parameter study. The inclusion of multiple metrics (Kendall’s τb and Pearson correlation) and comparisons with relevant baselines (count-based, RI) strengthen the results.  Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6 present the results in a clear and informative manner.
4. **Emphasis on Efficiency:**  The paper consistently highlights the computational efficiency of PoP, which is a key benefit and a strong argument for its adoption. The comparison to the computational cost of neural embeddings is particularly effective.
5. **Discussion of Theoretical Considerations:** The paper demonstrates awareness of the theoretical underpinnings of random projections and acknowledges the need for further theoretical analysis of PoP. This self-awareness enhances the credibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:**  While the MEN dataset is a standard benchmark, evaluating the method on a wider range of tasks and datasets (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would further strengthen the claims of generalizability.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Embedding Models:** The comparison is largely to simpler methods (count-based, RI). A more direct comparison to current state-of-the-art pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText, BERT embeddings) would provide a stronger assessment of PoP’s competitive advantage, even acknowledging the computational trade-offs.
3. **Justification for Parameter Choices:** While the parameter study is extensive, the rationale behind the initial choices for parameters like α = 0.5 and s = 0.01 could be further explained. Is there a theoretical basis or empirical justification for these values?
4. **Overly Detailed Discussion:** Some sections, particularly the discussions of statistical theory surrounding random projections (e.g., Section 2.3), are quite lengthy and may detract from the core message.  A more concise presentation of these ideas could improve readability. The depth of the theoretical discussion feels somewhat disproportionate to the empirical novelty.
5. **Repetitive Emphasis on Efficiency:**  The repeated emphasis on efficiency, while valid, becomes somewhat redundant and could be streamlined.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:** Have you tested PoP on tasks beyond semantic similarity, such as word analogy or text classification? If so, what were the results?
2. **State-of-the-Art Comparison:** How does PoP compare to state-of-the-art pre-trained embeddings (GloVe, fastText, BERT) in terms of performance and computational cost?
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of α and s?  Have you explored a wider range of values for these parameters, and if so, what were the findings?
4. **Orthogonality and Performance:** The paper mentions the impact of non-orthogonal index vectors. Could you elaborate on the connection between the level of orthogonality and the performance of the model? Is there a way to proactively control the orthogonality during the index vector generation process?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 3 is excellent for illustrating the distribution changes.
*   The supplemental material offer is appreciated.
*   The writing style is generally clear, but occasionally verbose.  Tightening up the language in some sections would improve readability.
*   Consider a table summarizing the performance comparisons across different methods and parameter settings.
