This paper proposes a new method, Positive-Only Projection (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces, aiming to combine the efficiency of random projection methods with the ability to apply weighting schemes like PPMI. The primary claimed contribution is a method for efficient semantic space construction that achieves competitive accuracy compared to neural embeddings and traditional count-based methods with matrix factorization, all while requiring significantly less computational resources. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that PoP-constructed spaces *can* be effectively weighted with PPMI, a feat not possible with standard random projection techniques like RI.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** While the combination of positive-only projections and PPMI weighting is presented as novel, the underlying concept of random projections for dimensionality reduction is well-established. The core idea feels like a relatively minor modification to existing techniques rather than a groundbreaking innovation. The paper doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate *why* restricting projections to positive values unlocks capabilities beyond simply using a different random matrix. The justification relies heavily on the ability to then apply PPMI, which feels like the actual key contribution, rather than the positive-only projection itself.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical foundation for PoP, stating they are "not able to provide a detailed mathematical account." This is a significant weakness. While empirical results are presented, the absence of theoretical analysis leaves the reader with little understanding of *why* PoP works or its limitations.  The discussion of δ (the error introduced by randomization) feels superficial without concrete bounds or justification. Relying on analogies to other random projection methods (RI) doesn't establish theoretical validity.

3. **Overstated Claims & Inconsistent Reporting:** The claim of achieving “competitive accuracy compared to neural embeddings” is not fully supported by the presented results. The primary evaluation is on the MEN dataset, which, while a standard benchmark, doesn’t represent the full spectrum of NLP tasks. Moreover, the performance gains over RI, while statistically significant, are relatively modest (especially before PPMI weighting). The authors oscillate between claiming substantial speedups and acknowledging performance improvements are dependent on parameter tuning (dimensionality, 's' value).  The reporting also feels selective, focusing on scenarios where PoP performs well without thoroughly investigating scenarios where it might fall short. The claim about building models in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" needs to be contextualized with hardware specifications and dataset size.

4. **Presentation and Redundancy:** The paper suffers from verbose writing and repetitive explanations.  The introduction, in particular, could be significantly condensed.  Concepts are often re-explained multiple times, and the use of equations is not always clearly motivated. The extended discussion on Kendall's τb, while potentially relevant, feels excessive and distracts from the core methodology. The organization of sections 3.1 and 3.2 feels somewhat disjointed; the transition from comparing PoP to RI to discussing PPMI weighting isn’t seamless.

5. **Parameter Sensitivity & Limited Exploration:**  The performance of PoP appears highly sensitive to parameters like 's' (the probability of a non-zero element) and dimensionality (m).  The paper provides some discussion of this, but the exploration of parameter space feels incomplete. The optimal value of ‘s’ seems to depend on whether PPMI weighting is applied, but the rationale for this is not fully explained.



**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Efficiency:** The potential for faster model construction is a legitimate strength. PoP offers a computationally efficient alternative to training neural embeddings, particularly for resource-constrained environments.

2. **PPMI Compatibility:** Demonstrating that PoP allows for PPMI weighting, which is not feasible with standard random projection techniques, is a valuable contribution. This unlocks the benefits of PPMI's discriminative power while retaining the efficiency of random projections.

3. **Clear Explanation of Method:** Despite some verbosity, the core PoP methodology is explained relatively clearly, with the two-step incremental construction process well-defined. The use of matrix notation aids in understanding the transformation process.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of PoP compared to other methods (RI, neural embeddings, SVD-based approaches) for different dataset sizes?  Please include a breakdown of time and memory requirements.
2. What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of α in Equation 2?  Have you explored other values besides 0.5?
3. You mention that increasing the dimensionality 'm' beyond a certain point diminishes performance for the unweighted PoP model.  Can you elaborate on the factors contributing to this effect?
4. Can you provide a theoretical justification (even if incomplete) for *why* restricting the random projection matrix to positive values allows for PPMI weighting, while standard Gaussian random projections do not?
5. The paper mentions potential derandomization techniques. Can you briefly discuss potential approaches and their expected impact on performance?




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a potentially useful technique, the lack of theoretical grounding, overstated claims, and stylistic weaknesses outweigh the benefits. The contribution feels incremental rather than transformative, and the paper would significantly benefit from a more rigorous analysis, more comprehensive evaluation, and a more concise presentation. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly providing a more robust theoretical justification and a more balanced discussion of performance, could warrant reconsideration.
