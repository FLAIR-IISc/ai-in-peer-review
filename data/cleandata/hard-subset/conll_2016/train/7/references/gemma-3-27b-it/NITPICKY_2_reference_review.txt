This paper proposes Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections. The central claim is that PoP overcomes limitations of traditional random indexing methods by enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI, resulting in competitive performance with neural embeddings but with significantly lower computational cost. The authors present empirical results on the MEN relatedness test, demonstrating competitive performance when combined with PPMI weighting.

**Summary of Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **A novel random projection scheme:** The core idea of using positive-only random projections is a potentially interesting contribution, as it unlocks the ability to apply weighting schemes previously incompatible with standard random indexing.
2.  **Empirical validation of PPMI application:** Demonstrating that PPMI can be effectively applied to PoP-constructed spaces and achieves competitive results is a tangible contribution.
3.  **Efficiency gains:** The potential for faster model construction compared to neural embeddings is a practical benefit, although the paper lacks a detailed comparative runtime analysis.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a genuine problem:** The high computational cost of neural embeddings and the limitations of standard random indexing are well-recognized issues in semantic space construction. PoP attempts to bridge this gap.
2.  **Clear and well-defined method:** The mathematical formulation of PoP is relatively straightforward, and the paper provides a clear explanation of the underlying principles. Equations 1 and 2 are well-presented.
3.  **Empirical results support claims:** The experiments on the MEN dataset demonstrate that PoP, when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive results. The inclusion of comparisons to RI and count-based methods provides a baseline for evaluating the method’s performance.
4.  **Detailed parameter exploration:** The discussion regarding the impact of dimensionality (m) and the number of non-zero elements (s) demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the method’s parameters. Figures 4, 5, and 6 provide valuable insights into these relationships.
5.  **Identification of theoretical gaps:** The authors acknowledge the lack of theoretical justification for PoP and highlight future research directions. This honest assessment is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient novelty in core idea:** While the *combination* of positive-only projections and PPMI is interesting, the individual components are not novel. Positive constraints on random matrices have been explored previously (though perhaps not in this specific context), and PPMI weighting is a well-established technique. The paper doesn’t adequately position PoP within the broader landscape of related work and fails to highlight *why* positive-only projections are uniquely suited to this application beyond merely enabling PPMI.
2.  **Lack of rigorous runtime comparison:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes the computational efficiency of PoP, but provides no concrete runtime measurements. A comparison of training times with SVD, neural embeddings (even small models), and other relevant methods is essential to substantiate this claim. The claim of “seconds or minutes” is insufficient.
3.  **Overreliance on MEN dataset:** Evaluating performance solely on the MEN dataset is insufficient. While it's a commonly used benchmark, it's known to be susceptible to certain biases and may not generalize well to other semantic similarity tasks. Evaluation on additional datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) is crucial.
4.  **Inconsistent use of correlation measures:** The justification for switching between Kendall’s τb and Pearson’s r feels *ad hoc*. The authors state τb is used because PoP vectors are non-Gaussian, but then acknowledge that PPMI aims to *create* a Gaussian distribution, leading to a switch to Pearson’s r. A more principled explanation is needed. Why not use Pearson’s r *from the start* and assess the effect of PPMI on the distribution?
5.  **Formatting and minor errors:** Numerous minor errors throughout the text distract from the paper's quality. Examples include: "concern-ing" instead of "concerning", inconsistent hyphenation, and occasional grammatical awkwardness. Equation numbering is also irregular (e.g., Equation 1 is referenced before it appears). The use of “Ibid” is somewhat archaic for a modern conference paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed runtime comparison of PoP (with PPMI) against other relevant methods like SVD, small neural embeddings, and standard RI implementations? Please provide specific training times for different corpus sizes.
2.  Could you elaborate on previous work exploring positive constraints on random matrices and explain how PoP differs from those approaches?
3.  Why was Kendall's τb initially chosen, and what is the justification for switching to Pearson's r after PPMI transformation? Could you explore the performance impact of using Pearson's r consistently?
4.  What is the theoretical justification for choosing α = 0.5 in Equation 2? Is this value sensitive, and how does performance change with different values of α?
5.  Will you release the code and trained models used in your experiments to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the manuscript for grammatical errors, typos, and formatting inconsistencies.
*   The discussion of theoretical bounds (δ) feels incomplete and could be removed or significantly expanded.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the resulting vector spaces (e.g., using t-SNE) to provide a qualitative understanding of the learned representations.



I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea and demonstrates competitive performance on a limited dataset, the lack of substantial novelty, the absence of a rigorous runtime analysis, the insufficient evaluation, and the numerous minor flaws undermine its overall quality. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would significantly strengthen the paper, but as it stands, it does not meet the standards for acceptance at a competitive conference like CoNLL.
