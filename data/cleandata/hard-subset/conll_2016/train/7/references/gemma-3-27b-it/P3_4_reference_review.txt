## Review of “Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction”

**Summary:** This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP leverages random projections with positive integer values, allowing for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI that are incompatible with traditional random indexing methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI). The authors demonstrate that PoP, particularly when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive results on semantic similarity tasks (MEN test) with significantly reduced computational cost compared to neural embeddings and count-based methods followed by matrix factorization.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and efficient alternative to traditional random projections:** PoP offers a way to retain the speed and scalability of random projection methods while unlocking the benefits of weighting transformations, addressing a significant limitation of prior work.
2. **Demonstration of PPMI’s effectiveness with random projections:** The paper successfully integrates PPMI with a random projection framework, showing that this combination can yield strong performance without the extensive training required by neural methods.
3. **Empirical validation and detailed analysis:**  The authors provide thorough experimental results on the MEN test, including ablation studies and parameter sensitivity analysis, offering valuable insights into the method's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a clear limitation of existing methods:** The incompatibility of weighting transformations with standard random projection techniques is a well-recognized issue. PoP directly tackles this problem, offering a compelling solution. The authors clearly articulate this motivation in the introduction.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The results on the MEN test are encouraging, demonstrating that PoP+PPMI achieves comparable performance to established methods like neural embeddings, while being significantly faster to compute. The inclusion of comparisons to RI provides a clear baseline and demonstrates the added value of PoP.
3. **Detailed analysis and parameter exploration:** The authors don't just present results; they *investigate* the method's behavior. The exploration of different dimensionality settings (Figure 4), the effect of the 's' parameter (Figure 5 & 6), and the discussion of non-orthogonality are crucial for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. This level of analysis elevates the paper beyond a simple method presentation.
4. **Clear and accessible explanation:**  The paper is well-written and the methodology is explained clearly, even for readers unfamiliar with random projection techniques. The use of matrix notation (Equation 1) is appropriate and aids understanding.
5. **Practicality and Scalability:** The claim of building models “in a matter of seconds or minutes” is a significant advantage, particularly for resource-constrained environments or applications requiring rapid prototyping.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the MEN test is a standard benchmark, relying on a single dataset limits the generalizability claims. Evaluating PoP on a wider range of semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) and potentially different languages would strengthen the findings.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors acknowledge the absence of a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error bounds (δ). While acknowledging previous work in randomized algorithms, providing even a preliminary theoretical analysis would significantly enhance the paper's credibility. The analogy to Kaski (1998) and Hecht-Nielsen (1994) is weak without a more formal connection.
3. **The Choice of Kendall's τb:** The justification for using Kendall’s τb initially is sound, given the non-Gaussian distribution of PoP vectors. However, the switch to Pearson’s correlation *after* PPMI transformation feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed discussion of the implications of PPMI on the vector distribution and why Pearson’s correlation then becomes appropriate would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to RI are useful, the paper would be strengthened by a more direct comparison to simpler, yet strong, baselines like word2vec or GloVe with similar dimensionality and training time constraints.  Demonstrating that PoP consistently outperforms these methods would be compelling.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalization to other datasets:** Have you experimented with PoP on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353)? If so, what were the results? If not, do you anticipate similar performance gains compared to the methods you evaluated on the MEN test?
2. **Theoretical Analysis:**  Are you planning to investigate the theoretical properties of PoP, particularly concerning the error bounds (δ) and the impact of the 's' parameter on the quality of the projection?
3. **Justification for Similarity Measure Switch:** Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind switching from Kendall’s τb to Pearson’s correlation after the PPMI transformation?  What specifically changes in the vector distribution that makes Pearson’s correlation more appropriate?
4. **Baseline Comparison:**  Have you compared PoP+PPMI to word2vec or GloVe trained with similar dimensionality and time constraints? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are well-produced and informative.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The promise of sharing code and embeddings is commendable and will greatly facilitate reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The proposed PoP method is a valuable addition to the toolbox of techniques for constructing semantic spaces. While some theoretical gaps and limitations in evaluation exist, the empirical results and insightful analysis make this work worthy of acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
