This paper proposes a novel method, Positive-Only Projection (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces, aiming to combine the efficiency of random projection methods with the ability to apply weighting schemes like PPMI – a feature lacking in traditional random indexing techniques. The core contribution is a random projection matrix constructed with only positive values, enabling PPMI weighting. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test compared to both neural embedding methods and traditional count-based approaches with PPMI and SVD, achieved with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a genuine limitation of existing random projection methods:** The paper correctly identifies that standard random projection methods, due to their zero-centered projections, are incompatible with weighting schemes like PPMI. PoP offers a direct solution to this problem, allowing for the application of these established, beneficial weighting techniques to a computationally efficient random projection framework. This is a well-motivated research direction.
2. **Demonstrated empirical performance:** The results on the MEN dataset, while not state-of-the-art, are competitive, and importantly, achieved with substantially lower computational cost than neural methods. The ablation studies (comparing PoP to RI and count-based models, and with/without PPMI) effectively showcase the method’s benefits.  The empirical evaluation provides some confidence that the proposed method is not merely a theoretical exercise.
3. **Clear and logical presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanation of the method is relatively easy to follow. The authors make a reasonable effort to connect their work to existing literature and to justify their design choices.
4. **Incremental construction:** The incremental nature of PoP is a significant advantage for large-scale datasets and dynamic applications where models need to be updated frequently. This is highlighted adequately throughout the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of extensive comparison to strong baselines:** While the results are competitive, the comparison is limited. The paper focuses heavily on comparison to RI, which, as the authors acknowledge, doesn't outperform PPMI+SVD. A more rigorous evaluation would involve comparisons to optimized, publicly available implementations of word2vec, GloVe, or fastText, particularly focusing on runtime and resource usage *as well as* accuracy.  The claim of "competitive results" feels weak without demonstrating a clear trade-off between speed and accuracy against established neural methods.
2. **Limited theoretical justification and analysis:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error bounds (δ) associated with the random projection. While recognizing the precedent of RI being proposed without initial justification, this remains a significant weakness. A more thorough exploration of the theoretical properties of the proposed projection matrix (beyond the observation about positive values) would strengthen the contribution. The discussion of the connection between the distribution and appropriate similarity measures (τb vs. Pearson) feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks deeper theoretical grounding.
3. **Parameter sensitivity & limited hyperparameter exploration:**  The paper demonstrates sensitivity to parameters like 's' (controlling the proportion of positive elements). The optimal value appears dataset-dependent and requires careful tuning. The authors acknowledge this, but the exploration of this parameter space feels incomplete. A more systematic hyperparameter optimization procedure and discussion of the robustness of the method to different parameter settings would be beneficial. The influence of dimensionality (m) needs further analysis – the diminishing returns beyond a certain point are noted, but not fully explained.
4. **Overreliance on a single dataset:** The evaluation is solely based on the MEN dataset. While a standard benchmark, this limits the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating on additional semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) and potentially on a downstream task (e.g., word analogy) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s effectiveness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the runtime and resource usage (memory, CPU) of PoP+PPMI with a comparable implementation of word2vec or GloVe on the UKWaC corpus? A table showing training time, model size, and inference time would be highly valuable.
2.  You mention the potential for "derandomization techniques." Could you elaborate on what specific derandomization strategies you envision and how they might be incorporated into the PoP framework?
3.  The choice of α = 0.5 seems somewhat arbitrary. Have you explored other values of α, and if so, what impact did they have on performance? What is the justification for choosing 0.5 specifically?
4.  Could you explain why the performance of the count-based PoP model degrades after a certain dimensionality (m)? What is the underlying mechanism causing this behavior, and how does it relate to the sparsity of the projection matrix?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplemental material should include the code used for the experiments, as well as the generated embeddings for reproducibility.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of PoP beyond semantic similarity assessment, such as topic modeling or document clustering.
*   The formatting of some equations is slightly inconsistent. Please review and ensure uniformity.
