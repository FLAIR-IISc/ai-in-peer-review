This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces via random projections, aiming to combine the efficiency of random indexing with the ability to apply weighting techniques like PPMI. The primary claimed contribution is a faster alternative to neural embeddings and count-based methods with weighting and dimensionality reduction, achieving comparable performance on semantic similarity tasks. A secondary claim is the method’s inherent ability to be weighted with PPMI, unlike traditional random indexing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** The fundamental idea of using random projections for dimensionality reduction is well-established (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI are cited). While the “positive-only” constraint is a modification, its theoretical justification and demonstrable impact beyond enabling PPMI weighting are not convincingly presented. The paper states it overcomes problems with existing methods, but doesn’t deeply analyze *why* the positive constraint inherently improves performance beyond simply allowing PPMI. It feels like a technical tweak rather than a paradigm shift.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis of PoP’s error bounds (δ). While citing relevant work on randomized algorithms, it doesn't attempt to provide even a preliminary theoretical justification for the method's success. The discussion of δ based on Kaski (1998) and Hecht-Nielsen (1994) is hand-wavy and lacks specific application to the PoP method. Assertions about the relationship between dimensionality and performance are empirically supported but lack theoretical backing.

3. **Overly Detailed and Sometimes Redundant Discussion:** The paper is excessively verbose. The extended discussion of matrix notation, Gaussian distributions, and the history of random projections feels unnecessarily detailed and detracts from the core contribution. The explanation of Kendall's τb is overly lengthy and could be significantly condensed. Several points are reiterated multiple times throughout the text. This impacts readability and obscures the essential arguments.

4. **Inconsistent Evaluation & Parameter Sensitivity:** The evaluation focuses solely on the MEN dataset, limiting generalizability. While the results are competitive, the sensitivity analysis reveals a strong dependence on parameter choices (specifically 's', the probability of a non-zero element). The optimal 's' value appears highly data-dependent, and the paper doesn’t offer clear guidance on selecting this parameter for new datasets. The behavior described when increasing 'm' beyond 2000 feels counterintuitive and requires more explanation. Furthermore, the claim of "competitive" performance with neural methods feels overstated given the limited scope of the evaluation.

5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** Numerous minor issues detract from the paper's professionalism. Footnotes are overused and break the flow of the text. Equation (3) is poorly formatted. There are instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors throughout. The consistent use of "i.e." is excessive. The supplemental material mention feels premature at this stage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Advantage: PPMI Applicability:** The key strength of PoP is genuinely the ability to apply PPMI weighting, which is blocked in traditional random indexing due to the zero-centered nature of the projections. This opens up possibilities for combining the speed of random projections with the benefits of PPMI weighting.

2. **Efficiency Gains:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that PoP can construct models significantly faster than training neural embeddings or performing PPMI weighting on full count matrices. This efficiency advantage is a valuable contribution, particularly for resource-constrained environments.

3. **Clear Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is clearly described, and the code availability (promised in the supplemental material section) will be helpful for reproducibility. The comparison with RI and count-based models provides a baseline for evaluating PoP’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more rigorous justification for the choice of positive-only projections, beyond simply enabling PPMI weighting? What inherent properties of this constraint contribute to improved performance?

2.  What theoretical work has been considered, or could be applied, to provide bounds on the error (δ) introduced by the random projection in PoP?

3.  How does the optimal value of 's' vary across different datasets and corpora? Can you provide guidelines for selecting 's' in the absence of extensive experimentation?

4.  Can you elaborate on the counterintuitive performance drop observed when increasing 'm' beyond 2000? What is the underlying mechanism causing this behavior?

5.  Will the code and data be made available *before* the camera-ready deadline, allowing for independent verification of the results?



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a potentially useful contribution (PPMI applicability), the paper suffers from a lack of theoretical depth, excessive verbosity, and inconsistencies in evaluation. The perceived novelty of the core idea is limited, and the sensitivity to parameter choices raises concerns about generalizability. I lean towards **rejection** due to the substantial number of weaknesses, even acknowledging the potential practical benefits. Significant revisions addressing the theoretical grounding, streamlining the presentation, and expanding the evaluation are required before reconsideration.
