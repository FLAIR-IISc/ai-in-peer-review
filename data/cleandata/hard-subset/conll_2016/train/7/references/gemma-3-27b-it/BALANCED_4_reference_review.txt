## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections, with the key advantage of enabling the application of weighting transformations like PPMI, which is often impossible with traditional random indexing methods. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the computational cost of neural embeddings and the limitations of existing random projection techniques. They present empirical results on the MEN relatedness test, demonstrating competitive performance compared to neural approaches and count-based methods combined with matrix factorization.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The PoP Method:** The core contribution is the introduction of PoP itself.  Constructing random projection matrices with exclusively positive values allows for the application of PPMI weighting, a crucial step often missing in other random projection techniques. This addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
2. **Empirical Validation & Comparison:** The thorough experimental evaluation comparing PoP (with and without PPMI) against RI, count-based models, and implicitly, neural methods (through performance benchmarks) on the MEN dataset provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The analysis of parameter sensitivity (dimension 'm' and number of non-zero elements) is particularly valuable.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Parameter Tuning Guidance:**  Beyond simply presenting results, the paper provides a detailed analysis of the method's behavior, including the impact of different parameters (dimensionality, sparsity) and a discussion of the trade-offs involved. The resulting guidance for parameter selection is a practical contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of using positive-only projections to enable PPMI weighting is a genuinely novel approach to semantic space construction. While random projections are well-established, this specific modification addresses a fundamental limitation of the technique.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation of PoP, comparing it to several relevant baselines and exploring the impact of key parameters. The use of multiple metrics and analysis of variance in results are commendable. The inclusion of visualizations (histograms) helps to understand the effect of PPMI transformation.
3. **Strong Motivation & Clear Explanation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work, highlighting the shortcomings of existing methods and the potential benefits of PoP. The technical explanation is well-structured and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with random projection techniques. The connection to count-based models and their limitations is well established.
4. **Practical Guidance:** The detailed discussion of parameter tuning and the recommendations for selecting appropriate values for 's' (sparsity) and 'm' (dimensionality) are highly valuable for practitioners. This practical aspect strengthens the paper's contribution.
5. **Well-Written and Comprehensive:** The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise style. The inclusion of related work and a discussion of future directions enhances the overall quality. The exploration of the theoretical underpinnings, while acknowledged as incomplete, adds depth to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:**  The paper acknowledges the lack of a detailed mathematical account for the method’s behavior and a theoretical justification for its performance. While the authors discuss related work on random projections and mention potential avenues for future investigation, a more rigorous theoretical analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, deriving bounds for δ (the error caused by randomization) would be highly beneficial.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN dataset and the UKWaC corpus. While this dataset is commonly used, evaluating PoP on a wider range of semantic similarity tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of its generalizability.  Evaluation on different corpora is also needed.
3. **Over-reliance on PPMI:** The paper heavily emphasizes the benefits of combining PoP with PPMI. While this combination is demonstrably effective, it would be valuable to explore the performance of PoP *without* PPMI in more detail.  The results suggest it outperforms RI even without weighting, but the extent of this advantage isn't fully explored.
4. **Correlation Measure Choice:** The justification for switching between Kendall's τb and Pearson's r based on the Gaussianity of the distributions feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic comparison of different correlation measures and a discussion of their respective strengths and weaknesses would be helpful.
5. **Presentation of Figures:** Figure 4 and Figure 5 display error margins (shaded regions) which are helpful, but could be made clearer.  It’s difficult to quickly determine if the differences in means are statistically significant based solely on visual inspection.  Providing p-values or confidence intervals would be more informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of PoP compared to RI and standard count-based methods with PPMI? A more precise analysis of the time and space requirements would be valuable.
2.  The paper mentions that the dimensionality 'm' can be seen as the capacity of the method. How does this capacity relate to the vocabulary size and the corpus size? Is there a rule of thumb for choosing an appropriate value for 'm'?
3.  You mention that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves comparable performance to neural methods. Could you provide a more direct comparison with specific neural embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset, including details on the training data and hyperparameters used?
4.  You state that the distributions of vectors are Gaussian-like *after* PPMI weighting. Can you quantify the degree of Gaussianity (e.g., using a normality test) before and after weighting?
5.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the value of α in Equation 2? Was α = 0.5 chosen arbitrarily, or was it determined through experimentation?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplemental material commitment is good.
*   Minor: There are a few instances where the writing could be slightly more concise. For example, the introduction feels somewhat lengthy.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to semantic space construction. The technical contribution is significant, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the paper is well-written. While the lack of theoretical justification and the limited scope of evaluation are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above. The potential impact of this work is substantial, particularly for applications where computational efficiency is a major concern.
