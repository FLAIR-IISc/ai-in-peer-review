## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:** This paper introduces a new method, Positive-Only Projection (PoP), for constructing semantic spaces. PoP utilizes random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but differs by employing random vectors containing only positive integer values. This allows for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI, which are incompatible with traditional RI due to its zero-centered projections. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity assessment (specifically the MEN test) while being significantly faster and less computationally expensive than neural embedding methods or count-based models with matrix factorization.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Projection Method (PoP):** The core contribution is the introduction of PoP, a random projection technique that uniquely enables the application of weighting transformations like PPMI. This is a significant deviation from existing random projection methods like RI, which are inherently incompatible with such weighting schemes.
2. **Empirical Validation & Comparison:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of PoP against both baseline methods (count-based models, RI) and more sophisticated approaches (neural embeddings). Demonstrating competitive performance on the MEN dataset, alongside substantial speed improvements, is a valuable contribution.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Parameters:**  The authors go beyond simply presenting results and provide a detailed investigation of the impact of different parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) on PoP’s performance, offering practical guidelines for its implementation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Clear Limitation of Existing Methods:** The paper directly tackles a known limitation of traditional random projection techniques – their inability to be combined with weighting methods. PoP effectively bridges this gap, offering a practical alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance of PoP+PPMI on the MEN dataset is compelling, demonstrating that the method can achieve competitive accuracy without the substantial computational cost of neural embeddings.  The comparison to RI is particularly insightful, highlighting the benefit of enabling weighting transformations.
3. **Detailed Parameter Exploration:** The extensive parameter analysis (Figures 4-6) is a significant strength. It provides valuable insights into how to optimize PoP’s performance and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the method's behavior. This level of detail is rarely seen in papers introducing new methods.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are easy to follow. The connection between the theoretical motivations (addressing long-tailed distributions) and the practical implementation is well established.
5. **Practical Relevance:**  The claimed speed improvements are substantial and could make semantic space construction feasible in resource-constrained environments or for rapid prototyping.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The evaluation is solely based on the MEN dataset. While a standard benchmark, a broader evaluation on diverse semantic similarity tasks would strengthen the claims of generalizability.  Other datasets, or even tasks beyond semantic similarity, would be beneficial.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the paper acknowledges the lack of a formal theoretical analysis of PoP's properties (e.g., bounds on error, convergence properties), this is a significant omission.  The comparison to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and other related work is helpful, but a dedicated theoretical investigation would greatly increase the paper's impact.
3. **Sensitivity to Parameter Tuning:** The detailed parameter analysis reveals that PoP’s performance is sensitive to parameter settings, particularly the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors. This sensitivity could make the method more challenging to deploy in practice, as optimal parameters may need to be determined empirically for each dataset.
4. **Over-reliance on PPMI:** The strong performance relies heavily on the PPMI weighting transformation. While justified, the paper could benefit from exploring other weighting schemes or demonstrating the effectiveness of PoP without PPMI in certain scenarios.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalization:** Beyond the MEN dataset, have you tested PoP on other semantic similarity datasets or different NLP tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification)?  If so, what were the results?
2. **Theoretical Analysis:** Do you have any preliminary work or planned research directions for providing a theoretical justification for PoP, such as deriving bounds on the error introduced by the random projection or analyzing its convergence properties?
3. **Parameter Selection:**  Can you provide more guidance on selecting the optimal value for *s* (the probability of a non-zero element) based on the characteristics of the input corpus? Are there any heuristics or rules of thumb that can be used?
4. **Alternative Weighting Schemes:** Have you experimented with alternative weighting schemes beyond PPMI?  If so, how did they compare in terms of performance and computational cost?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of Kendall’s τb and Pearson’s correlation is excellent and demonstrates a strong understanding of statistical principles.
* The supplemental material promise is a good addition.
* The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of random projections in general, and how PoP attempts to mitigate some of those limitations.
