This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea – using random projections with strictly positive values to allow for subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI – is genuinely interesting and represents a significant departure from traditional random indexing methods. The authors correctly identify a limitation of existing approaches (inability to combine with weighting) and offer a direct solution. The demonstrated speed gains are compelling, achieving comparable performance to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods with drastically reduced computational cost.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A Novel Random Projection Technique:** The PoP method itself is the primary contribution. The constraint of positive values in the projection matrix, while seemingly simple, unlocks the ability to apply weighting schemes that were previously incompatible with standard random indexing.
2. **Efficient Semantic Space Construction:** The paper demonstrates a significant speed advantage over traditional methods like neural embeddings and SVD, offering a viable alternative for resource-constrained scenarios or rapid prototyping.
3. **Re-enabling Weighting in Random Projections:** This work bridges a gap between the efficiency of random projections and the accuracy gains achieved through weighting methods, showcasing a synergistic combination.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The core idea is both novel and well-explained. The paper clearly articulates the problem with existing random projection methods and presents PoP as a direct and logical solution. The mathematical formulation is concise and easy to follow.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The results on the MEN dataset are encouraging, showing competitive performance with established methods. The comparison to RI is particularly insightful, highlighting the benefit of enabling weighting transformations.
3. **Computational Efficiency:** The reported speed gains are substantial and represent a practical advantage. The paper effectively emphasizes the potential for rapid model building, which is a valuable contribution.
4. **Thorough Exploration of Parameters:** The analysis of the impact of dimensionality (m) and the sparsity parameter (s) demonstrates a good understanding of the method’s behavior and provides practical guidance for users. The discussion of orthogonality and its relation to performance is insightful.
5. **Clear Justification of Choices:** The rationale for using Kendall's τb initially, and switching to Pearson’s correlation after PPMI transformation, is well-explained and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset (MEN) and a single task (semantic similarity). While the results are positive, a broader evaluation on diverse datasets and NLP tasks would strengthen the claims.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis, this is a significant gap. While analogous work exists for standard random projections, a deeper understanding of the theoretical properties of PoP (e.g., distortion bounds, convergence guarantees) would be highly valuable. The discussion of δ feels somewhat hand-wavy.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity to the ‘s’ parameter (sparsity) needs further investigation. While the paper discusses it, the optimal value seems to be heavily data-dependent, and a more systematic approach to parameter tuning could improve robustness.
4. **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While comparisons to RI and neural embeddings are present, a comparison to simpler weighting techniques *without* random projections (e.g., direct PPMI weighting on raw counts) would help isolate the benefit of the PoP projection itself.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the PPMI transformation itself for different vector dimensionalities (e.g., comparing the cost for m=100 vs. m=5000)? This would further quantify the benefits of the low-dimensional projection.
2.  Have you explored alternative similarity measures beyond Kendall's τb and Pearson’s correlation? Could you comment on the robustness of the results to different similarity measures?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of α in Equation 2? Have you experimented with different values of α, and if so, what were the observed effects?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the choice of 's' relates to the sparsity of the original count matrix and the desired level of distortion in the projected space? A more principled method for choosing 's' would be extremely valuable.



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of a supplemental material section is appreciated. The discussion of related work is adequate. Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel idea and encouraging results. While the theoretical analysis and scope of evaluation could be strengthened, the core contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. I lean towards accepting, prioritizing the innovative approach and potential impact over the current limitations.
