This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a random projection method designed to address the limitations of traditional count-based word embeddings and offer a computationally cheaper alternative to neural methods. The core idea is to use random projections with strictly positive values, allowing for subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI that are incompatible with standard random projection techniques (RI, RRI, etc.) due to their zero-mean projections. The authors demonstrate that PoP, especially when combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks, with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling PPMI weighting:** This is the core technical contribution. By avoiding zero-mean projections, PoP unlocks the application of weighting schemes to random projection-based models, a previously insurmountable limitation.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:** The authors show that PoP+PPMI achieves results comparable to more computationally expensive methods on the MEN dataset.
3. **Highlighting the efficiency benefits of the approach:** The paper stresses the drastically reduced training time compared to neural embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a genuine problem:** The computational cost of training neural embeddings and the limitations of traditional random projection methods are well-established challenges in NLP. This paper directly tackles those issues.
2. **Clear and well-motivated methodology:** The paper provides a clear explanation of PoP, its theoretical grounding (connecting it to existing random projection methods and matrix transformations), and its advantages. The rationale for using positive-only projections is convincingly presented.
3. **Rigorous empirical evaluation:** The authors evaluate PoP using a standard benchmark (MEN) and compare it against relevant baselines (count-based models, RI). The inclusion of ablation studies (PoP vs. PoP+PPMI, varying dimensionality) strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Insightful discussion of limitations and future work:** The authors acknowledge the lack of theoretical bounds on the error introduced by the randomization and suggest promising avenues for future research, including derandomization techniques and integration with neural models.
5. **Detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity:** The exploration of dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements provides valuable insights into the behavior of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (MEN) and a single task (semantic similarity). While MEN is a standard benchmark, the generalizability of the results is uncertain. Evaluation on a wider range of tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) and datasets is crucial.
2. **Lack of comparison to strong, readily available baselines:** While RI is included as a baseline, there’s no comparison to pre-trained, publicly available word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe). These embeddings are often used as a standard benchmark and would provide a more direct comparison to the proposed method’s performance/efficiency trade-off. The authors claim competitiveness with neural nets, but without direct comparison, this claim is unsubstantiated.
3. **Insufficient justification for parameter choices:** The selection of α = 0.5 and s = 0.01 appears somewhat arbitrary. While the authors explore the impact of *m* and the number of non-zero elements, a more systematic parameter tuning process would strengthen the results.
4. **Theoretical discussion remains incomplete:** While the authors acknowledge the need for a theoretical analysis of the error bounds, the current discussion remains largely descriptive. A more rigorous mathematical treatment would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.
5. **Overstated novelty:** While the idea of using positive-only projections is novel, the core concept of random projections and PPMI weighting are well-established. The paper’s contribution lies primarily in the *combination* of these techniques, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a comparison of PoP+PPMI to publicly available pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset and potentially other semantic similarity benchmarks?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of PoP+PPMI compared to training a comparable neural embedding model (e.g., word2vec) on the UKWaC corpus? Please provide concrete numbers.
3.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the choice of α = 0.5? Did you explore other values, and if so, what were the results?
4.  Can you provide more detail on the limitations of the theoretical analysis and your plans for addressing them in future work? Specifically, what approaches are you considering for deriving bounds on the error introduced by the random projection?
5.  The paper suggests that PoP is more sensitive to noise. Can you elaborate on this sensitivity and discuss potential mitigation strategies beyond increasing dimensionality?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are well-presented and informative.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The supplemental material offer is appreciated.
*   The discussion of the relationship between PPMI and Gaussian distributions is insightful.



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to building efficient semantic spaces. However, the limited scope of evaluation and the lack of comparison to strong baselines raise concerns about the generalizability and practical impact of the proposed method. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper. I lean towards **reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit**, contingent on a more comprehensive evaluation and a more thorough comparison to existing methods.
