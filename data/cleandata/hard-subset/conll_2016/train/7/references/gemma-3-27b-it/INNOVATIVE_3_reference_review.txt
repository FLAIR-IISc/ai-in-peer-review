This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces through randomized projections that, crucially, allows for subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI – something traditional random indexing methods cannot do. The core contribution is the design of a projection matrix using only positive values, maintaining the ability to apply count-based weighting transformations. The paper demonstrates competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks, achieving results comparable to neural embeddings but with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The core idea of using positive-only projections to enable weighting transformations is genuinely novel. Existing random projection methods inherently preclude this due to their zero-centered nature. This represents a significant shift in thinking about how to combine the efficiency of random projections with the power of weighting schemes. This is not an incremental improvement, but a new direction.
2. **Efficiency and Scalability:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the computational benefits of PoP. Building models in seconds or minutes, as opposed to hours or days for neural embeddings, is a massive advantage, especially for resource-constrained environments or rapid prototyping. This is a practical, impactful benefit.
3. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving performance comparable to neural embeddings with a vastly simpler and faster method is a strong result. While not state-of-the-art, the gap is small enough to be incredibly promising, especially considering the method’s simplicity. The fact that PoP outperforms RI even *without* weighting is also notable.
4. **Thorough Exploration & Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting the method. It includes a detailed analysis of the method’s parameters (dimensionality, sparsity) and their impact on performance, backed up by experiments and figures. The discussion of the trade-offs and limitations is also commendable.
5. **Clear Explanation & Motivation:** The paper provides a well-reasoned motivation for the work, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches and how PoP addresses them. The connection to the broader context of distributional semantics and the evolution of word embedding techniques is well established.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN dataset and a single corpus (UKWaC). While this provides a starting point, it doesn’t fully demonstrate the generalizability of the method. Performance on other semantic similarity datasets, and potentially downstream NLP tasks, would significantly strengthen the paper.
2. **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of a complete theoretical understanding of PoP's behavior, this remains a significant gap. A more rigorous analysis of the error bounds (δ) and the properties of the resulting semantic space would be beneficial, even if preliminary. The reliance on empirical observation is currently somewhat unsatisfying.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to parameters like ‘s’ (sparsity) and dimensionality. More guidance on how to optimally tune these parameters for different datasets and tasks would be helpful. The discussion in section 3.3 feels a bit fragmented and lacks a clear prescriptive recommendation.
4. **Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson’s r:** The justification for switching to Pearson’s correlation after PPMI transformation feels a bit convoluted. While the authors’ reasoning regarding Gaussian distributions is valid, a more concise and direct explanation would be preferred.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with different PPMI normalization techniques (e.g., truncating negative values, applying a smoothing factor)? If so, how did these affect performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost difference between applying PPMI weighting to PoP-constructed spaces versus directly to count-based models? Quantify the speedup with timing experiments.
3.  What is the impact of different random number generators (e.g., different seeds, different distributions beyond uniform) on the stability and performance of PoP?
4.  Beyond semantic similarity, have you explored applying PoP to any downstream NLP tasks (e.g., text classification, topic modeling)? If so, what were the results?




**Additional Comments:**

The formatting and writing quality are generally good. Figure 3 is particularly helpful for visualizing the effect of the PPMI transformation. The reference to supplemental material is appreciated. I encourage the authors to consider releasing the code publicly to facilitate reproducibility and further research.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.** Despite the weaknesses, the novelty and potential of this work are compelling. The authors have presented a genuinely new idea with promising results. The limitations are not severe enough to warrant rejection, and I believe the authors can address them adequately during the rebuttal phase. The paper’s contribution lies in opening up a new avenue for research in efficient semantic space construction. I am willing to overlook the incomplete theoretical validation given the significant conceptual advancement and practical benefits demonstrated.
