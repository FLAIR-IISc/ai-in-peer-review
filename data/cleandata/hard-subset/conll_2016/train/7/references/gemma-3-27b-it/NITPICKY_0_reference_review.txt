This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces via random projections, aiming to combine the efficiency of random indexing with the ability to apply weighting transformations like PPMI. The primary claimed contribution is a method that achieves competitive semantic similarity results compared to neural embeddings and count-based models with factorization, but with significantly reduced computational cost. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that PPMI weighting *can* be applied to spaces created with a specific type of random projection (positive-only), something not possible with traditional random indexing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** The core idea of using random projections for dimensionality reduction is well-established (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI are all cited). While the positive-only constraint is a technical difference, its fundamental contribution feels incremental. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* positive-only projections are crucial beyond enabling PPMI weighting, and doesn't sufficiently explore why previous methods didn’t consider this constraint. Simply stating it allows PPMI isn’t enough justification for a novel method.

2. **Insufficient Empirical Justification & Parameter Sensitivity:** The experimental results, while showing PoP outperforms RI, aren’t compelling enough. The performance gains are modest, and highly dependent on parameters (dimensionality *m* and *s*, the probability of a non-zero element). Figure 4 demonstrates performance *decreasing* with increasing dimensionality for the unweighted PoP. The explanation offered, concerning the sparsity and appropriate *s* value, feels ad-hoc and lacks theoretical grounding.  The paper also fails to adequately address how the optimal parameters are determined and if they generalize beyond the specific dataset used (MEN). The range of performance variation shown in the figures (shaded areas) is also concerningly large, suggesting instability.

3. **Overstated Claims & Inconsistent Presentation:** The paper frequently makes strong claims (“dramatically enhance performance,” “competitive with neural embeddings”) that are not fully supported by the results.  The performance improvements over RI, while statistically significant perhaps, are not substantial enough to warrant such strong language. The presentation also suffers from inconsistencies. For example, the paper initially states PoP outperforms RI *even without* weighting, but Figure 1 suggests otherwise, making the reader question the reliability of presented claims.  Additionally, the frequent use of "as shown in Figure X" without clear visual emphasis on the relevant aspects of the figures hinders understanding.

4. **Missing Baselines & Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison against strong baselines. While neural embeddings are mentioned, specific models (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) are not included in the experimental evaluation. A crucial ablation study is missing: what is the performance of PoP *without* PPMI weighting?  This would isolate the contribution of the positive-only projection itself, rather than the combined effect. The claim that PoP is "much faster" requires quantitative demonstration—execution times for PoP, RI, and PPMI weighting on count-based models are not provided.

5. **Theoretical Weakness & Hand-Waving:** The discussion of the theoretical underpinnings feels incomplete. The attempt to connect the method to randomized algorithms and the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma feels superficial. The claim that δ (the error due to randomization) scales as 1/m is presented without derivation. The justification for using Kendall's τb and then switching to Pearson's correlation after PPMI weighting feels driven by convenience rather than solid theoretical reasoning. The discussion of non-orthogonality (Figure 6) is unclear and its relevance to performance is not adequately explained.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional count-based models and the computational cost of neural embeddings, providing a reasonable motivation for exploring alternative approaches.

2. **Conceptually Simple & Efficient Method:** The PoP method itself is relatively straightforward to understand and implement. The potential for incremental construction and low dimensionality is attractive from a computational perspective.

3. **Demonstration of PPMI Compatibility:** Successfully demonstrating that PPMI weighting can be applied to a space created via a random projection is a positive, albeit incremental, contribution. This opens up possibilities for combining the benefits of both approaches.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical justification for choosing a positive-only distribution for the random projection matrix?  Why isn’t a Gaussian or other distribution equally valid or potentially superior?
2.  Can you provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and execution times of PoP, RI, and the PPMI weighting step for various dataset sizes?  Quantify the speedup achieved by PoP.
3.  How sensitive is the performance of PoP to different values of *s* and *m*?  Can you provide guidelines for selecting appropriate parameters based on dataset characteristics?
4.  What is the performance of PoP *without* PPMI weighting compared to RI and other baselines?
5.  Can you include a comparison against established neural embedding models (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) in your evaluation?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, hyphenation). The writing quality could be improved by streamlining the explanations and avoiding overly strong claims. The supplemental material promise should be taken seriously if the paper proceeds, providing runnable code and complete experimental results would be essential.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea, the lack of substantial novelty, insufficient empirical validation, and overstated claims outweigh the strengths. The paper needs significant revisions, a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and a stronger theoretical foundation to be considered for acceptance.
