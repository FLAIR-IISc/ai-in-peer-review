## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP leverages random projections, similar to existing techniques like RI, but crucially uses a random matrix with only positive values. This allows for the application of weighting transformations like PPMI, which are incompatible with traditional random projection methods relying on Gaussian distributions. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive results on semantic similarity tasks compared to neural embeddings and other count-based methods, but with significantly lower computational cost and faster build times.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) enabling weighting transformations:** This is the core contribution. By using a positive-only random matrix, the paper overcomes a fundamental limitation of existing methods, opening up possibilities for combining the efficiency of random projections with the performance benefits of weighting schemes like PPMI.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance:** The paper provides evidence, through the MEN relatedness test, that PoP+PPMI achieves results comparable to more computationally expensive methods.
3. **Detailed analysis of parameter influences:** The exploration of `s` (the probability of non-zero elements) and dimensionality provides valuable insights into the behavior of the method and practical guidance for its application.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a clear limitation in existing methods:** The incompatibility of standard random projections with weighting techniques is a valid and significant problem. PoP directly addresses this, offering a practical solution. The paper clearly articulates this problem in the introduction and motivates the need for their approach.
2. **Strong empirical results:**  The results presented in Figure 1 clearly show PoP outperforming RI and the basic count-based model.  The subsequent improvement with PPMI (Figures 2 & 4) convincingly demonstrates the value of enabling this weighting transformation. The comparison to neural net-based approaches (though not directly benchmarked, but referenced) is compelling given the speed advantages.
3. **Thorough analysis and ablation studies:** The exploration of parameter settings (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and their impact on performance, as shown in Figures 4, 5, and 6, demonstrates a good understanding of the method’s behavior and provides practical guidance for users.  The discussion of the trade-offs and the importance of `s` is particularly insightful.
4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6) is helpful.
5. **Practical relevance and efficiency:** The significant reduction in computational cost and build time compared to neural methods is a major strength. This makes PoP a viable option for resource-constrained environments or applications requiring rapid model updates.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of direct comparison to strong baselines:** While the paper mentions comparisons to neural embeddings, there's no direct, head-to-head performance comparison *within the paper* on the MEN dataset.  Referring to other papers for such comparisons feels less conclusive.  A direct comparison would strengthen the claims of competitive performance.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MEN) and a single task (semantic similarity).  While this is a standard test, it would be beneficial to see performance on other datasets and tasks to assess the generalizability of the method.  Exploring other similarity datasets or even a simple word analogy task would add weight to the findings.
3. **Theoretical Justification Remains Weak:** The paper acknowledges the lack of a rigorous theoretical justification for PoP.  While the connection to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma is mentioned, a more in-depth analysis or derivation of error bounds would be valuable. The discussion of δ, while honest, feels incomplete.
4. **Overly detailed discussion of Kendall's τb:** While a justification for using τb is provided, the extended explanation feels somewhat out of place and could be streamlined.  The reader may not need to fully understand the intricacies of this correlation measure to grasp the core ideas of the paper.
5. **Discussion of non-orthogonality in Figure 6 feels disconnected:** While interesting, the explanation of the impact of non-orthogonality doesn't directly tie back to performance improvements or suggest practical steps to mitigate this issue.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Could you provide a direct performance comparison to a widely used pre-trained word embedding model (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) on the MEN dataset, including training time and computational resources used?**  This will provide a more concrete benchmark.
2. **What is the sensitivity of PoP's performance to the choice of α (currently fixed at 0.5)? Did you explore different values and what were the results?**
3. **Can you elaborate on the impact of the size of the UKWaC corpus on the results? Would a smaller or larger corpus significantly affect performance or the optimal parameter settings?**
4. **You mention the potential for "derandomization techniques." Could you provide a specific example of such a technique and how it might be applied to PoP?**
5. **The paper mentions that PPMI normalization improves the performance. What happens if a different normalization technique is applied?**



**Additional Comments:**

*  The supplemental material offer is welcome.
*  The frequent references to other papers are helpful for context but sometimes disrupt the flow of the text.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The method is well-motivated, the results are promising, and the analysis is thorough. While the lack of a direct comparison to strong baselines and the limited scope of evaluation are weaknesses, they are not fatal. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above. The work has the potential to be valuable to researchers and practitioners working in distributional semantics and related fields.
