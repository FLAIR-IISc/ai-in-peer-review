## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces. PoP is a randomized dimensionality reduction technique inspired by Random Indexing, but designed to allow for subsequent application of weighting methods like PPMI, which is not possible with traditional random projection methods due to their zero-mean distributions. The authors demonstrate that PoP, when combined with PPMI weighting, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks (MEN test) compared to neural embeddings and SVD-based methods, while being significantly faster and less computationally expensive.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) that bridges the gap between efficiency of random projections and the effectiveness of weighting/transformation methods like PPMI.** This is the core contribution, addressing a limitation of existing random projection approaches.
2. **Empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of PoP + PPMI.** The results on the MEN dataset show a compelling performance relative to established methods, especially considering the computational efficiency.
3. **A detailed analysis of the method’s parameters and behavior**, including the impact of dimensionality and sparsity on performance, providing valuable insights for practical application.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real Problem:** The paper tackles a significant issue in semantic space construction – the trade-off between computational cost and model quality.  Traditional methods often involve expensive training or dimensionality reduction steps. PoP offers a compelling alternative that avoids these bottlenecks.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The MEN dataset results are convincing. Achieving comparable performance to neural methods with significantly less computational overhead is a substantial achievement.  The ablation studies examining the impact of dimensionality and sparsity further strengthen these claims. The comparison to RI is particularly valuable as it clearly highlights the advantage of enabling PPMI weighting.
3. **Thorough Analysis and Justification:** The paper provides a clear rationale for the design of PoP, connecting it to the broader context of distributional semantics and random projections.  The discussion regarding the choice of Kendall’s τb versus cosine similarity, and the justification for PPMI weighting are well-articulated.  The explanation of the theoretical limitations regarding proving bounds for δ (error) is honest and reasonable, acknowledging the current state of research.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is used appropriately, and the explanations are clear and concise. The step-by-step breakdown of the method makes it easy to understand and potentially implement.
5. **Practical Significance:** The speed and efficiency of PoP make it particularly attractive for applications where rapid prototyping or deployment on resource-constrained devices are important.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation:** While the MEN test is a standard benchmark, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  Performance on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) and potentially on downstream NLP tasks would strengthen the conclusions. Showing performance *beyond* semantic similarity assessment is crucial.
2. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Fast Embeddings:**  The paper compares to neural embeddings generally, but doesn’t specifically compare to recent, efficient embedding methods designed for speed (e.g., fastText, or distilled versions of BERT/RoBERTa). A direct comparison to these methods would better position PoP within the current landscape.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity & Heuristic Tuning:** The discussion of parameter tuning (specifically 's') feels somewhat heuristic. While the authors provide guidelines, a more systematic exploration of the parameter space and its impact on performance (perhaps with a small grid search) would be beneficial.  More discussion on *why* certain parameter values work well would be helpful.
4. **Theoretical Gaps:**  While the authors acknowledge the lack of a formal theoretical analysis of error bounds (δ),  some attempt to connect PoP to existing theoretical frameworks for random projections (beyond the reference to Johnson-Lindenstrauss) would be valuable. Even a qualitative discussion of expected error behavior would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Beyond MEN:** Have you evaluated PoP on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999)? If so, what were the results? If not, do you plan to do so?
2. **Comparison to Fast Embeddings:** How does PoP compare to computationally efficient embedding methods like fastText or distilled versions of BERT/RoBERTa in terms of both performance and training/inference speed?  Can you provide a quantitative comparison?
3. **Parameter Optimization:**  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the recommended values for 's'?  Have you performed any systematic parameter optimization or sensitivity analysis?
4. **Scalability:** What are the limitations of PoP in terms of corpus size and vocabulary size? Have you tested its performance on very large datasets (e.g., billions of tokens)?
5. **Downstream Tasks:**  Have you explored the use of PoP embeddings in any downstream NLP tasks (e.g., text classification, sentiment analysis)? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 3 is particularly insightful and should be highlighted more prominently in the paper.
*   The discussion of PPMI and its role in transforming the distribution of vector elements is excellent.
*   The code availability is a strong plus.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While there are some weaknesses regarding the scope of evaluation and theoretical justification, the paper presents a novel and promising approach to semantic space construction. The strong empirical results and the clear articulation of the method's benefits outweigh the identified limitations.  Addressing the questions posed to the authors during the rebuttal period would further strengthen the paper. This work has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics.
