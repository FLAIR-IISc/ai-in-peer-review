## Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces based on random projections. The core idea is to use random vectors containing only positive integer values, enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI – something traditional random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) struggle with due to their zero-centered nature. The authors demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity assessment (MEN test) compared to neural embeddings and other count-based methods, but with significantly reduced computational cost.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel random projection technique (PoP) that allows for subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI.** This addresses a fundamental limitation of existing random projection methods and opens up possibilities for combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of weighting.
2. **Empirical demonstration of competitive performance on a standard semantic similarity benchmark.** The results indicate that PoP + PPMI can achieve accuracy comparable to more complex methods, providing a strong validation of the approach.
3. **A detailed analysis of the method's parameters and behavior.**  The paper provides insights into the impact of dimensionality and sparsity on performance, offering practical guidance for implementation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a real problem in semantic space construction.** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of both traditional count-based models and computationally expensive neural embeddings, and provides a compelling motivation for a more efficient alternative.
2. **Sound technical approach.** The use of positive-only random vectors is a clever solution to enable weighting transformations. The explanation of how PoP fits within the broader context of matrix factorization and random projections is well-structured and easy to follow.
3. **Thorough empirical evaluation.**  The experiments using the MEN dataset are well-designed, comparing PoP to relevant baselines (RI, count-based models) and exploring the impact of PPMI weighting. The ablation studies (varying dimensionality and sparsity) provide valuable insights.
4. **Detailed analysis and discussion.** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results, delving into the theoretical underpinnings of the method, discussing its limitations, and suggesting directions for future research. The analysis of the distribution of weights is particularly insightful.
5. **Efficiency gains are significant.** The authors highlight the substantial reduction in computation time compared to neural embeddings and even SVD-based methods, making PoP a potentially valuable tool for resource-constrained applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation.** While the MEN dataset is a good starting point, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  Performance on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) and potentially different NLP tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing.** The paper presents performance comparisons but doesn’t consistently include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests). This makes it difficult to definitively conclude whether the observed differences between PoP and the baselines are statistically meaningful.
3. **Theoretical Justification Could Be Stronger.** While the paper alludes to theoretical connections to randomized algorithms and the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, a more rigorous mathematical analysis of the error bounds and convergence properties of PoP would be beneficial. The discussion of 'δ' feels somewhat hand-wavy.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity.** The analysis of parameters (dimensionality, sparsity) is good, but the optimal settings appear somewhat task-dependent.  More exploration of how these parameters interact and how to automatically tune them would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:**  Have you experimented with PoP on datasets other than MEN? If so, can you share the results? If not, do you have plans to evaluate its performance on a broader range of benchmarks?
2. **Statistical Significance:**  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the performance differences reported in Figure 1 and Figure 2?
3. **Theoretical Analysis:**  Are you planning to pursue a more formal mathematical analysis of PoP's error bounds and convergence properties?  Specifically, can you elaborate on the potential connection to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma?
4. **Parameter Tuning:**  Do you have any recommendations for automatically tuning the parameters of PoP (dimensionality, sparsity) based on the characteristics of the corpus or the downstream task?
5. **Computational Cost Breakdown:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of PoP versus other methods (e.g., training time for neural embeddings, SVD computation time)?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clear, but some sentences are a bit verbose.  Consider streamlining the language for improved readability.
* The inclusion of code and embeddings upon acceptance, as mentioned, is highly appreciated.
* The discussion of Kendall's τb and Pearson correlation is interesting, but perhaps a bit lengthy for the main body of the paper. Consider moving some of this discussion to the appendix.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and promising approach to semantic space construction. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the contributions are significant.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the evaluation scope and statistical rigor, would further enhance the impact of this work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
