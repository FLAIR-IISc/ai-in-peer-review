## Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend this paper for acceptance, with minor revisions. It presents a novel and potentially impactful method for constructing semantic spaces that addresses a key limitation of existing random projection techniques – the inability to leverage weighting schemes like PPMI. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and includes a thorough experimental evaluation. While the theoretical grounding is currently limited, the empirical results are compelling, and the authors acknowledge this gap.

**Summary & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novelty of Positive-Only Projection (PoP):** The core contribution is the introduction of PoP, a random projection method designed to enable the application of weighting techniques (specifically PPMI) that are incompatible with traditional random projection methods relying on zero-mean distributions. This unlocks a pathway to combine the efficiency of random projection with the accuracy gains of weighting.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Effectiveness:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance on a standard semantic similarity task (MEN) compared to more computationally expensive methods like neural embeddings and SVD-based approaches.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Parameter Sensitivity:** The thorough exploration of the effects of dimensionality and sparsity (through the 's' parameter) on PoP's performance provides valuable insights into the method's behavior and practical considerations for its application.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper directly tackles the trade-off between computational efficiency and model accuracy in semantic space construction. Existing random projection methods lack the flexibility to incorporate weighting schemes, limiting their performance. PoP offers a viable solution.
2. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The mathematical notation is used appropriately and explained adequately.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The comparison to baseline methods (count-based, RI) and the demonstration of competitive performance with more sophisticated approaches (neural embeddings) are compelling. The inclusion of ablation studies (varying dimensionality and sparsity) adds robustness to the findings.  The use of established datasets (MEN, UKWaC) and metrics (Spearman's rank correlation) enhances the credibility of the evaluation.
4. **Acknowledgement of Limitations & Future Work:** The authors honestly acknowledge the lack of a rigorous theoretical justification for PoP and outline clear directions for future research, including theoretical analysis and exploration of derandomization techniques. This demonstrates a mature and responsible research approach.
5. **Practical Implications:** The speed and efficiency of PoP, combined with its competitive accuracy, make it a potentially valuable tool for applications where computational resources are limited or rapid model construction is required.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of *why* PoP works. A deeper understanding of the mathematical properties of the positive-only projection matrix and its impact on the resulting semantic space would significantly strengthen the work. The discussion of Johnson-Lindenstrauss Lemma is a good starting point but needs further elaboration in the context of PoP.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to a single semantic similarity task (MEN).  While this is a standard benchmark, demonstrating the effectiveness of PoP on a broader range of NLP tasks (e.g., word analogy, text classification) would provide further evidence of its generalizability.
3. **Sensitivity to Parameter 's':** The detailed analysis of the 's' parameter highlights its importance, but also reveals a potential fragility of the method. Finding the optimal 's' value may require careful tuning for different datasets and tasks. The paper could benefit from guidelines or heuristics for selecting an appropriate 's' value.
4. **Comparison to More Recent Embedding Methods:** The paper compares to relatively older neural embedding techniques. While the authors acknowledge these are constantly evolving, a brief comparison to more recent methods (e.g., fastText, GloVe) would provide a more up-to-date context for the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Justification:** Can you elaborate on any ongoing efforts to provide a theoretical analysis of PoP? Specifically, are you exploring any connections to existing theory on random projections or dimensionality reduction?
2. **Parameter Tuning:**  Could you provide some practical guidance for selecting the 's' parameter in different scenarios?  For example, are there any data-dependent heuristics that can be used to estimate a suitable value?
3. **Generalizability:** Do you have preliminary results on the performance of PoP on other NLP tasks beyond semantic similarity?
4. **Scalability:**  While the paper emphasizes efficiency, what are the scalability limitations of PoP for extremely large corpora and vocabularies? (e.g., memory usage).




**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are well-designed and effectively illustrate the key findings.
* The inclusion of a supplemental material section is appreciated and will be valuable for reproducibility.
*  The discussion of Kendall’s τb vs. Pearson correlation is insightful and well-justified.




In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution and compelling empirical results. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly the lack of theoretical grounding and limited scope of evaluation, would further strengthen the work and increase its impact.  I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the literature on semantic space construction.
