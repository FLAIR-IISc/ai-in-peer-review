- Introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a random projection method compatible with PPMI weighting.  
- PoP + PPMI achieves competitive semantic similarity performance on MEN while being faster and less computationally expensive than SVD/neural methods.  
- Provides analysis of PoP parameters (dimensionality, sparsity) and their impact on performance.

- Addresses trade-off between computational cost and model quality in semantic space construction.  
- Empirical results on MEN convincingly show PoP’s effectiveness and computational advantage; ablations strengthen the claim.  
- Clearly motivates PoP design, connects to distributional semantics, and justifies evaluation choices (e.g., Kendall’s τb, PPMI).  
- Paper is well-written with clear notation and an implementable step-by-step method.  
- PoP’s speed and efficiency make it practical for rapid prototyping and resource-constrained deployment.

- Evaluation is limited mainly to MEN; lacks results on other similarity datasets and downstream tasks.  
- No direct comparison to other fast embedding methods (e.g., fastText, distilled BERT/RoBERTa).  
- Parameter tuning (particularly 's') appears heuristic; needs more systematic sensitivity analysis and rationale.  
- Theoretical analysis is incomplete—no formal error bounds or deeper connection to random projection theory beyond JL reference.

- Have you evaluated PoP on other semantic similarity benchmarks (WordSim353, SimLex-999)?  
- How does PoP compare quantitatively to fast embeddings (fastText) and distilled contextual models in performance and speed?  
- What is the rationale and empirical basis for the recommended 's' values; any systematic grid search?  
- What are PoP’s scalability limits regarding corpus and vocabulary size; tested on very large corpora?  
- Have PoP embeddings been tested on downstream NLP tasks (classification, sentiment) and with what results?

- Figure 3 is particularly informative and should be emphasized more.  
- The discussion of PPMI’s role in transforming vector distributions is strong.  
- Availability of code is a major positive.

- Overall recommendation: accept—novel, promising method with strong empirical efficiency gains despite limited evaluation and theoretical gaps.
