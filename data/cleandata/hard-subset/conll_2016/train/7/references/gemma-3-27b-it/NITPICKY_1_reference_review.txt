This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections, with the key feature of enabling subsequent weighting transformations like PPMI. The authors claim PoP offers a computationally efficient alternative to neural embeddings and traditional count-based methods with dimensionality reduction, achieving competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks. The primary claimed contribution is a novel random projection technique that maintains the ability to apply weighting schemes, overcoming limitations of existing methods like RI. A secondary contribution is a demonstration of competitive performance on the MEN test, and a final, less substantial, claim is the speed of model construction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Concept:** While the authors frame PoP as a novel technique, the fundamental idea of using random projections for dimensionality reduction is well-established (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI are cited). The key “innovation” – ensuring positivity for PPMI application – feels more like a targeted engineering solution to a known problem with existing methods rather than a groundbreaking algorithmic advance. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate *why* this specific positive-only approach is superior to alternative modifications of existing random projection techniques that might achieve a similar outcome. The justification revolves around ease of PPMI application, but doesn’t rigorously explore alternative approaches.

2. **Insufficient Rigorous Evaluation & Parameter Sensitivity:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MEN) and a relatively simple experimental setup. The extensive parameter exploration (Figures 4 & 5) *reveals* significant sensitivity to parameter choices (especially 's', controlling the number of non-zero elements). The optimal parameter settings seem highly dependent on the context window size and data characteristics. The paper does not provide a robust strategy for parameter selection, leaving the user with a potentially difficult tuning problem.  The discussion of the underlying mathematics supporting parameter selection is also remarkably shallow; stating “it seems that the sparsity of vectors plays a role” is not a satisfying explanation.

3. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Comparative Depth:** The claim of “competitive performance” compared to neural embeddings is weak. The paper doesn’t directly compare PoP to modern, well-tuned neural embeddings on MEN. Instead, it favorably compares against RI, which is a relatively weak baseline.  Furthermore, the discussion focuses heavily on the *speed* of PoP, but the performance gains over RI seem marginal, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a scenario where the speed advantage outweighs the performance difference when compared to more powerful (but slower) alternatives. The framing consistently downplays the computational cost of PPMI weighting, even while emphasizing PoP’s ability to facilitate it.

4. **Presentation and Redundancy:** The paper suffers from repetitive phrasing and a somewhat verbose style. The introduction, in particular, is overly descriptive and could be significantly condensed. The extensive discussion of the mathematical background, while attempting rigor, often feels tangential and detracts from the core contribution. The continual re-statement of the limitations of existing methods becomes tiresome. The supplemental material promise is appreciated, but doesn’t alleviate the current lack of detailed experimental configurations.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The inability to apply weighting methods like PPMI to standard random projection techniques *is* a legitimate limitation. PoP offers a straightforward solution to this problem, which could be valuable in scenarios where computational resources are constrained and a lightweight, interpretable model is desired.

2. **Demonstrated Feasibility:** The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly impressive, demonstrate that PoP can indeed be used to build semantic spaces that achieve reasonable performance on a standard semantic similarity task. The positive results on the MEN test, even with the limitations of the evaluation, provide some evidence for the viability of the approach.

3. **Clear Motivation and Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind PoP, grounding it in the broader context of distributional semantics and the trade-offs between count-based models, random projections, and neural embeddings. The citations are generally relevant and demonstrate an awareness of the existing literature.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered alternative methods for ensuring the positivity required for PPMI application in random projections (e.g., applying a non-linear transformation to the projection matrix)? If so, why was the PoP approach chosen over these alternatives?
2.  What guidelines can you provide for selecting the optimal value of 's' (number of non-zero elements) based on the characteristics of the corpus and the desired trade-off between performance and computational cost?  Can you formulate a rule-of-thumb, or propose an automated parameter tuning approach?
3.  Please provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI to at least one state-of-the-art neural embedding technique (e.g., GloVe, fastText) on the MEN dataset, reporting both performance scores and training/inference times.
4. Can you provide more details about the computational resources used in your experiments (CPU, GPU, memory) to allow for a more accurate assessment of the practical benefits of PoP?
5. The discussion of the theoretical underpinnings of PoP (particularly concerning δ) feels incomplete. Can you elaborate on the assumptions and limitations of the analogies drawn to Johnson-Lindenstrauss and related lemmas?



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper identifies a genuine practical problem and proposes a feasible solution, the lack of significant novelty, the limited evaluation, the parameter sensitivity, and the overstated claims outweigh the strengths. The work feels more like a targeted engineering fix than a substantial research contribution. Addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above might strengthen the paper, but substantial revisions would be required.
