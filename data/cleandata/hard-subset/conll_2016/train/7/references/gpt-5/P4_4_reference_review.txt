Summary and claimed contributions
The paper introduces Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse, nonnegative random projection scheme for incremental construction of distributional semantic spaces. Unlike standard random indexing (RI) with zero-mean projections, PoP uses sparse vectors with positive integer entries, so the projected co-occurrence matrix remains nonnegative and can be subsequently reweighted by PPMI in the reduced space. The authors argue that this enables fast, incremental models with competitive accuracy without expensive matrix factorization or neural training. They also propose using Kendall’s τb for similarity on non-Gaussian projected vectors and switching to Pearson’s r after PPMI, and present empirical results on the MEN relatedness benchmark using UKWaC.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A simple, incremental, sparse nonnegative random projection approach that permits applying PPMI after projection, yielding competitive MEN scores with much lower dimensionality and computational cost than dense count spaces. The work includes parameter studies over projection dimension and sparsity.
2) An empirical observation and protocol for similarity computation: Kendall’s τb performs better on raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian), while Pearson’s r is better after PPMI (approximately Gaussian), with corresponding performance analyses.
3) An efficiency-oriented workflow that combines random projection and PPMI at low dimension, showing comparable accuracy to dense PPMI on MEN while being computationally lighter.

Strengths
- Efficiency and incrementality: The method constructs low-dimensional representations directly from streams via sparse additions, avoiding heavyweight training and expensive factorization. The design is practical for large corpora or online updates.
- Post-projection reweighting: Enabling PPMI after projection is the core insight; computing PPMI in low dimension is substantially cheaper than on the original high-dimensional counts, and empirical results show that PPMI substantially narrows (or closes) the gap to stronger baselines on MEN.
- Systematic parameter exploration: The paper reports performance as a function of projected dimension, number of nonzero entries, and similarity measures, including variance across multiple random runs. This helps readers understand stability and trade-offs.
- Clear empirical linking between distributional shape and similarity measures: The analysis of histograms before and after PPMI, and the resulting Pearson vs Kendall choice, is coherent and supported by results.
- Competitive accuracy on MEN: PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves around 0.75 Spearman on MEN (and up to roughly 0.78 with additional tuning), which is in the range of commonly reported non-neural and some neural baselines, suggesting the approach is viable.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only reported on MEN with a single corpus (UKWaC). There are no tests on other standard intrinsic benchmarks (e.g., SimLex, WordSim datasets) or extrinsic tasks (e.g., analogy, downstream classification, similarity-in-context). No comparisons to strong neural baselines trained on the same corpus with controlled settings. This limits claims of broad competitiveness.
- Missing or incomplete theoretical justification: The paper explicitly lacks error bounds or approximation guarantees for using PPMI after projection, i.e., whether PMI computed on projected counts approximates PMI on original counts and under what conditions. The statistical consequences of mixing counts by projection (bias/variance trade-offs) are not analyzed. The choice of τb as a similarity metric for randomly projected vectors is only heuristically motivated.
- Related work omissions and novelty concerns: The method is closely related to known sketching/feature hashing/count-min style approaches that use nonnegative sparse projections to compress co-occurrences and then apply weighting; this literature and baseline comparisons are absent. The argument that existing random projection methods cannot be followed by weighting because of zero-mean entries is not fully compelling in light of these lines of work.
- Questionable claim about RI and PPMI: The paper asserts that PPMI is undefined after RI because row/column sums are always zero due to E(R)=0. In general, the sum of projected entries is not identically zero; rather, signs can cause negative entries and cancellations, which complicates probability estimates. This point needs correction and a fair comparison (e.g., clipping/shift or absolute values) to establish that PoP enables something RI cannot, rather than simply making it more convenient.
- Lack of efficiency measurements: While the text repeatedly stresses speed and resource savings, no wall-clock time, memory profile, or throughput measurements are provided. The computational cost of Kendall’s τb at scale (e.g., for nearest neighbor retrieval over large vocabularies) is not addressed, and the practicality vs cosine is not quantified.
- Reproducibility gaps: Code and embeddings are promised only upon acceptance. Important preprocessing details (e.g., context selection, frequency thresholds, tokenization variants), hyperparameter search procedure, and random seeds are not fully specified. This hinders verification.

Questions to authors
- Can you formalize how PPMI computed after PoP relates to PPMI on the original counts? Is there an unbiasedness or bounded distortion result for PMI under your projection scheme? If not, can you provide empirical ablation where you compare PPMI-before-then-project vs project-then-PPMI?
- Please clarify and, if necessary, correct the claim that RI yields row/column sums of exactly zero and thus precludes PPMI. Can you compare against baselines that use RI or hashed/sketch-based nonnegative projections with simple nonnegativity fixes before PPMI?
- Can you add runtime and memory comparisons (count-based+PPMI vs PoP+PPMI) and report end-to-end build times and query times, including the cost of Kendall’s τb and Pearson computations?
- How does PoP perform on additional benchmarks and a downstream task? Are the trends consistent beyond MEN?
- What is the impact of collisions (non-orthogonality) theoretically or empirically on PMI estimates? Is there a principled way to set s as a function of corpus sparsity and target dimensionality?

Overall, the paper presents a simple and potentially useful practical approach with encouraging initial results, but its evaluation breadth, theoretical grounding, and positioning relative to closely related sketching/feature hashing methods need strengthening.
