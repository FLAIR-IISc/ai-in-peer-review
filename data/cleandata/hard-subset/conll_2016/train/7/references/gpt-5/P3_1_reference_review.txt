Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse randomized projection scheme with nonnegative entries that permits subsequent application of PPMI weighting in a low-dimensional space. The method incrementally builds distributional word representations by summing a small number of positive random coordinates per context (“index vectors”), then computes similarities via Kendall’s τb; after PPMI reweighting, Pearson’s r is used. On MEN with UKWaC, PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines; PoP+PPMI approaches the performance of predictive embeddings, while being computationally lightweight.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple randomized sketching approach that preserves nonnegativity, enabling direct PPMI weighting on compressed co-occurrence representations and yielding strong performance on MEN.
2) An empirical study of PoP’s parameterization (dimension m and sparsity s) and the effect of similarity metrics (Kendall vs Pearson post-PPMI).
3) The observation that rank-based similarity helps unweighted PoP, while PPMI makes the projected distributions more Gaussian-like, favoring Pearson.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: The “positive-only, very sparse” projection is easy to implement, incremental, and compatible with classic count-based weighting. This fills a niche between RI (signed projections that preclude PMI) and dense factorization/neural models.
- Empirical gains over RI and counts: The paper shows consistent improvements over RI and raw counts on MEN, with clear parameter sweeps (m, number of nonzeros) and discussions of sensitivity/noise.
- PPMI after projection: Demonstrates that applying PPMI in the compressed space achieves competitive relatedness scores (~0.75 average on MEN) with small vectors and no heavy training. This is intuitively attractive and could be impactful in resource-constrained settings.
- Incrementality and efficiency claims: The design is amenable to streaming updates and avoids expensive SVD/neural training. If substantiated, this is a practical advantage.

Weaknesses
- Missing key baselines from sketching/feature hashing: The proposed mechanism is very close in spirit to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing (Weinberger et al., 2009; Cormode & Muthukrishnan, 2005) which also maintain nonnegative approximate counts in compressed spaces. PMI/PPMI can be computed on sketches too. A comparison to CMS (with k hashes, unit weights) and Count-Sketch variants is essential to establish novelty and effectiveness. The paper does not cite or compare to this literature.
- Limited and potentially biased evaluation: Results are only on MEN relatedness with a single corpus. No tests on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RareWords, or downstream tasks; no comparison on analogy. The main comparison uses Kendall for PoP but cosine for RI/counts; Kendall is never applied to RI/counts, leaving open whether some observed gains stem from the similarity metric rather than the representation. Likewise, the tuning and preprocessing (e.g., stopword removal) are improved for PoP in some reports; comparable tuning for the baselines is not shown.
- Incomplete neural and SPPMI baselines: Modern SGNS/fastText trained on UKWaC can be trained quickly and would provide a fairer, contemporary baseline than Baroni et al. (2014) training-time anecdotes. Likewise, strong SPPMI(+SVD) setups with context distribution smoothing, subsampling, and symmetric windows (per Levy & Goldberg, 2014) are missing; the claim that SVD adds “negligible” improvements is surprising without details on hyperparameters and context selection.
- Theoretical justification and design choices are ad hoc: The choice tij = floor(1/U^α) with α=0.5 and small s is not justified. There is no analysis of bias/variance introduced by positive-only collisions, nor guarantees analogous to JL/Count-Min. The discussion claims RI “cannot be PPMI-weighted because sums are zero”; more precisely, RI yields signed entries (not valid as counts), not that row/column sums are identically zero. PMI on projected “buckets” lacks a probabilistic interpretation; the paper should discuss why PMI over bucketed features is still meaningful and under what collision regimes it helps.
- Efficiency claims lack measurements: No wall-clock, memory, or asymptotic comparisons versus (i) sparse count matrices + PPMI with top-K contexts, (ii) CMS-based approximations, (iii) SGNS. Given modern optimized implementations, it is important to quantify speed/space trade-offs.

Questions to authors
1) How does PoP compare to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing baselines for building compressed co-occurrence matrices and then applying PPMI? Please add these baselines (varying sketch width/depth), and discuss theoretical connections/differences.
2) Can you report Kendall’s τb and Pearson/cosine for all methods (RI, counts, PoP), to decouple representation effects from similarity-metric effects? Conversely, try Kendall on RI/counts and cosine on PoP.
3) Please include additional benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW) and at least one downstream task. Also report SGNS/fastText trained on UKWaC under reasonable settings and robust SPPMI(+SVD) per Levy & Goldberg with context distribution smoothing and subsampling, all tuned comparably.
4) Provide runtime and memory benchmarks (construction time, PPMI time, similarity time) vs baselines, across m and vocabulary sizes. How does PoP scale in practice?
5) Clarify and justify the projection distribution: Why 1/U^0.5 and the flooring? What is the expected L1/L2 norm of index vectors; how does it affect bias? Provide guidance for choosing s to guarantee (with high probability) at least one nonzero per row and to control collision rates (orthogonality).
6) What is the statistical meaning of PPMI computed over random “bucket” dimensions? Under what collision regimes do you expect it to approximate PPMI over true contexts? Can you provide an analysis or at least controlled experiments varying collision rates?
7) In your “improved” setup (0.78 on MEN), were identical preprocessing and hyperparameter tuning applied to all baselines? Please provide fair, side-by-side comparisons.

Additional comments
- The claim that RI “retains distances” yet “cannot be improved with weighting” should be nuanced; RI can be combined with other weightings suited for signed features, though PMI specifically is not applicable to negative entries.
- Please release code and seeds to aid reproducibility; include error bars on all plots.
- A small ablation on α and on using unit weights versus heavy-tailed positive weights would help isolate what drives gains.

Overall, the idea of enabling PPMI in a compressed, incremental space is interesting and potentially useful. To reach the bar for a venue like CoNLL, the paper needs stronger baselines (especially sketching/feature hashing and modern SGNS/SPPMI), broader evaluation, principled analysis of the projection scheme, and concrete efficiency measurements.
