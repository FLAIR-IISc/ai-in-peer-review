Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse, nonnegative random projection scheme for incremental construction of distributional semantic spaces. Unlike traditional Random Indexing (RI) with zero-mean projections, PoP preserves nonnegativity, which then allows applying PPMI (and related weighting) efficiently in the reduced space.
- It presents an empirical study of PoP’s behavior: choice of dimensionality m, sparsity s (number of nonzeros per index vector), and similarity measures (Kendall’s τb pre-weighting; Pearson after PPMI). Results on MEN suggest PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75 Spearman ρ (up to 0.78 with tuning), competitive with some neural embeddings, at much lower computational cost.
- It argues for the practicality of performing weighting in low dimensions (after projection) rather than on the full co-occurrence matrix, with resultant speed/memory benefits, and offers qualitative insights on distributional effects before/after PPMI.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: PoP is conceptually simple (random index vectors with positive entries, accumulate, then weight) and supports incremental updates with low memory. This is an appealing engineering contribution for practitioners who cannot afford training large neural models or computing SVD on huge PPMI matrices.
- Unlocking PPMI after projection: The core idea—use nonnegative projections so that projected features can be reweighted with PPMI—addresses a real limitation of RI and sign-preserving random projections. The empirical finding that PPMI on PoP-projected features matches PPMI-on-counts (with far less compute) is interesting.
- Parameter study: The paper provides a reasonably thorough exploration of m and s, including variance across runs and the effect of non-orthogonality as s increases. The observation that PoP+PPMI prefers very sparse index vectors while unweighted PoP benefits from more nonzeros is useful guidance.
- Speed potential: While hard numbers are missing (see weaknesses), the methodology is clearly cheaper than dense PMI/SVD or training SGNS/GloVe, and the argument that weighting over m≈10^3 projected dimensions is dramatically faster than over the original feature space is sound.
- Clarity about limitations: The authors candidly state the lack of theoretical guarantees and position the work as empirically motivated, which helps set expectations.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and relation to prior art: The method is very close in spirit to feature hashing / the hashing trick (Weinberger et al., 2009) and Count-Min Sketch (Cormode & Muthukrishnan, 2005) applied to co-occurrence counts, where nonnegative compressed counts permit downstream weighting. The paper does not cite or compare to these widely used techniques, nor to nonnegative sparse projections (e.g., Achlioptas variants). The contribution risks being viewed as an instantiation of known sketching ideas in a distributional semantics setting.
- Ambiguity in the projection distribution (Eq. 2): The definition of tij is unclear (the notation “1Uα c” is ambiguous). If it is ceil(U^α), then it always equals 1; if it is floor(1/U^α), it induces a heavy-tailed integer distribution. This must be clarified, as it impacts both reproducibility and interpretation. The claim that the exact positive-integer distribution matters little contradicts the motivation for choosing α=0.5.
- Limited and uneven evaluation: Only the MEN relatedness dataset is used. No results on other standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW, SimVerb-3500) or downstream tasks. Baseline comparisons are incomplete: PPMI+SVD results are dismissed as “negligible improvements” without numbers; no comparison to SGNS/GloVe with tuned hyperparameters on the same corpus; no use of recent strong count-based baselines (e.g., shifted PPMI with context distribution smoothing). Statistical significance is not assessed.
- Mixed similarity metrics and apples-to-apples concerns: PoP is evaluated with Kendall’s τb (pre-weighting) and then Pearson’s r (post-PPMI), while baselines often use cosine. This complicates fairness. A consistent metric across methods (e.g., cosine everywhere, or r everywhere once distributions are standardized) would be preferable, or at least report all metrics for all methods.
- PMI semantics after projection: Applying PMI/PPMI to projected features (linear combinations of hashed contexts) changes the probabilistic interpretation of PMI. It is not shown that PPMI-on-projections approximates PPMI-before-projection, nor are bounds provided on the distortion of association statistics. This is a central conceptual gap, even if empirics are favorable.
- Claims about efficiency lack concrete evidence: No wall-clock timings, memory usage, or asymptotic/constant-factor comparisons against count-based PPMI+SVD or SGNS. The “seconds/minutes” claim needs substantiation.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Several details are under-specified: exact preprocessing; window weighting; stopword handling in main experiments; how s is set as a function of m; whether index vectors are re-used or re-sampled per context type; seed control. The code is “to be shared if accepted,” which limits reproducibility during review.

Questions to authors
- Projection definition: Please precisely define Eq. (2). Is tij sampled as 0 with prob 1−s, otherwise as Z ~ floor(1/U^α) for U~Uniform(0,1]? If not, what is the exact distribution, including whether you use ceil/floor? What values did you actually instantiate in experiments?
- Relation to hashing/CMS: How does PoP differ from feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch for compressing co-occurrence matrices (multiple hash functions per context, nonnegative counts), followed by PPMI on the sketch? Can you compare empirically and discuss theoretical differences?
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to SGNS/GloVe trained on UKWaC with standard hyperparameters, and to PPMI+SVD (with numbers, e.g., 300–500 dims)? Also report cosine and Pearson for all methods to ensure apples-to-apples.
- PMI after projection: Can you provide justification (empirical or theoretical) that PPMI computed on projected counts is a good approximation to PPMI on the original co-occurrence matrix? For example, show correlation between PPMI scores per word pair across the two pipelines or provide a bound on expected distortion under collisions.
- Efficiency: Please provide runtime and memory benchmarks (end-to-end) for building PoP, computing PPMI, and querying similarities, compared to dense PPMI and SGNS, on UKWaC. Also quantify build time variance over runs.
- Sensitivity and robustness: How robust are results across datasets and corpora? Can you include at least one more similarity dataset and one downstream task? Do improvements hold when using narrow syntactic contexts?

Additional comments
- The claim that RI “sums are always 0” needs care: while expectations are zero, actual row/column sums are not zero but entries can be negative; this is sufficient to invalidate PMI but the phrasing should be corrected.
- Using Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure is unusual in distributional semantics; please justify more thoroughly and include cosine/ρ results for PoP to rule out metric-specific gains.
- The discussion of SVD providing negligible gains contradicts much prior work; please verify your SVD setup (target rank, weighting/scaling, smoothing) and report details.
