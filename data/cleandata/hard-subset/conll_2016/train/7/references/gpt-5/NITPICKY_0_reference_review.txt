Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly positive integer entries, enabling downstream weighting (notably PPMI) on the projected word-by-index space. The method supports incremental construction akin to RI but aims to maintain nonnegativity to allow information-theoretic weighting.
- It advocates Kendall’s τb for similarity in raw PoP space (non-Gaussian coordinates) and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussianized coordinates).
- Empirically, on MEN with UKWaC, PoP outperforms RI and count-based models; PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ~0.75 Spearman, claimed comparable to neural embeddings but with drastically lower computational cost.

Strengths
- Practical idea: Using a nonnegative, sparse random projection to enable PPMI on low-dimensional representations is simple and potentially useful in resource-constrained settings. The incremental construction is attractive.
- Empirical observations on parameter sensitivity (m, s, number of non-zeros) and variance across runs are helpful. Reporting min/avg/max across 10 runs is good practice for randomized algorithms.
- The insight to use Pearson after PPMI (variance-stabilizing/log transform) and Kendall on raw non-Gaussian coordinates is reasonable and aligns with standard statistical guidance.
- The focus on computational efficiency is timely; pushing weighting steps into low-dimensional spaces could be beneficial in pipelines where SVD/dense PPMI is prohibitive.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical correctness and clarity: Several mathematical statements are inaccurate or misleading.
  - The Kendall τb definition is wrong: it compares adjacent pairs (j, j+1) rather than all pairs (j < k). This is a serious flaw in the methodological exposition and undermines the similarity computation section (even if code used Knight’s algorithm).
  - The claim that in RI “the sum of values of row and column vectors are always 0” is incorrect. For P = C R with R having zero-mean entries, sums are zero only in expectation, not identically. This “always 0” justification for why PPMI is undefined on RI is not sound; the real issue is negative entries/log domain and interpretation, not column/row sums being exactly zero.
  - Notation is inconsistent and sometimes self-contradictory: T vs R for the projection; E(R) sometimes written as “R of E(R)” or “E = (0)”; “η-normed” vs “α-normed”; “x 6= n and x n” (missing relation symbol); using both Tn×x and Tn×m. These impede reproducibility and understanding.
  - The distribution in Eq. (2) lacks justification, bounds, or analysis. Using ⌊1/U^α⌋ with α=0.5 yields a heavy tail; no discussion of scaling, expected norm, or numerical stability (rare large integers) is provided. Guarantees like “each row has at least one non-zero” are asserted but not ensured; the probability (1−s)^m should be addressed or a resampling scheme specified.
- Empirical evaluation is too narrow and contains confounds:
  - Only MEN is used. No tests on SimLex-999, WordSim353 (Sim/Rel), Rare Words, or downstream tasks; no analogy or categorization tasks; no statistical significance testing. Claims of competitiveness with neural embeddings are weak without controlled comparisons on identical corpora/training protocols.
  - Comparisons are not apples-to-apples: PoP (raw) uses Kendall; RI uses cosine; dense PPMI uses cosine; PoP+PPMI uses Pearson. While the paper argues for choice of metric, fairness demands reporting baselines under multiple similarity measures (e.g., PPMI+Pearson for dense, RI+Kendall where appropriate).
  - Hyperparameters appear tuned on the test set (e.g., choice of window, s, m); no dev split is reported. The “fine-tuned” 0.78 figure is post-hoc with additional preprocessing (stopwords/cutoffs) and scaling, without details.
- Conceptual validity of PPMI on projected dimensions is under-argued: After projection, columns are not interpretable contexts. PPMI is applied to word-by-projection-dimension matrices, not co-occurrence counts. The paper acknowledges discarding the information-theoretic interpretation, but then uses the PPMI formula unchanged. This is a different objective (variance stabilization), but then comparisons to “true” PPMI on word-context matrices are not conceptually aligned.
- Claims about complexity and speed are vague: The paper asserts “seconds or minutes” without concrete runtime/memory metrics, hardware specs, or corpus sizes processed. It also conflates RI as a “replacement” for eigenvector computations (SVD), and cites odd complexities (e.g., O(n^2 log m) for eigenvectors), which are not standard. This diminishes credibility.
- Writing and formatting quality are below venue standards:
  - Numerous grammatical errors (“To a large extend”), typos, and broken LaTeX (“E =(0)”, “x n”).
  - Figures are referenced but not properly integrated; axes/units are sometimes unclear. Footnotes appear inlined, some references incomplete (“δ and in Gaussian random projections”).
  - Inconsistent capitalization (PoP vs POP). Ambiguous symbols (η vs α). Missing details for corpus preprocessing and vocabulary statistics.

Questions to Authors
- Please correct and clarify the formal definition and computation of Kendall’s τb used in your experiments. Did you actually use all pairs as in Knight (1966) or adjacent pairs as defined in the paper?
- Can you provide a precise and correct argument for why PPMI is “undefined” for RI outputs, beyond negative entries/log domain concerns? Would rectification or offsetting be a viable baseline? Please add RI+rectified-PPMI or alternative weighting baselines.
- How do you ensure each row of T has at least one non-zero? Do you resample until non-zero? What s and m combinations guarantee a high-probability bound on zero rows/columns? Please provide the probability calculations and implementation details.
- Please report controlled comparisons on additional benchmarks (SimLex-999, WS353-Sim/Rel, RW) and, if possible, on the same corpus/training budget as SGNS/GloVe baselines. Include significance tests and use a dev/test split for hyperparameter tuning.
- Provide runtime and memory measurements (indexing, projection, PPMI, similarity computation) with hardware specs and corpus statistics (tokens, vocab, windowing). How does cost scale with m and s?
- Can you bound the tail of ⌊1/U^α⌋ or propose a clipped/normalized variant? What is E[tij], Var[tij] for α=0.5, and how do rare large entries affect stability?
- Please standardize notation (R vs T, η vs α), fix equation typos, and provide a reproducibility checklist (code, seeds, corpus preprocessing, hyperparameters).

Recommendation: Reject. The core idea is interesting but the paper currently suffers from substantial methodological inaccuracies, limited and confounded evaluation, and significant writing/formatting issues. A thoroughly revised version with corrected theory, stronger and fairer experiments, and polished presentation could be promising.
