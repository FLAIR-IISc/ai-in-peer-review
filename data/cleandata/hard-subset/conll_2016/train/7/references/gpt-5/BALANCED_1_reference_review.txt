Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with nonnegative integer entries that enables incremental construction of low-dimensional distributional word representations. Unlike classic Random Indexing (RI) with zero-mean projections, PoP’s nonnegative projections allow subsequent application of PMI/PPMI weighting in the projected space. The authors argue for Kendall’s τb as a similarity metric in the non-Gaussian pre-weighted space, and Pearson’s r after PPMI. Empirically, on MEN using UKWaC with window-based co-occurrence, PoP outperforms RI and raw counts, and PoP+PPMI+Pearson approaches the performance of dense PPMI models while being substantially more efficient.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, streaming-compatible, nonnegative sparse projection matrix that permits PMI/PPMI weighting in the compressed space, enabling efficient construction of predictive distributional spaces without large dense matrices or neural training.
2) An empirical study connecting distributional shape to similarity measures (Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI, Pearson post-PPMI), and analysis of PoP’s parameters (dimension m, sparsity s) on MEN.

Strengths
- Practicality and efficiency: The method is simple, incremental, and eliminates expensive dense count matrices. Allowing PPMI weighting after projection is a useful engineering contribution that addresses a real bottleneck in count-based pipelines. The parameter studies (varying m and s) and the discussion of variance across runs are informative for practitioners.

- Empirical improvements over RI and raw counts: The paper convincingly shows that PoP performs better than RI and unweighted count-based models on MEN, especially for small windows, and that PPMI in PoP-space closes much of the gap to strong count-based baselines while remaining efficient.

- Insightful treatment of similarity metrics: The rationale for Kendall’s τb in non-Gaussian spaces and Pearson’s r after PPMI is thoughtful and supported by the observed performance shifts, with useful distributional visualizations illustrating why a parametric correlation is preferable after PPMI.

- Incrementality and streaming suitability: The two-step construction (assign sparse index vectors to contexts, then accumulate into targets) is well-suited to large-scale, on-the-fly updates, which is valuable in practice and is not matched by many neural approaches.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to known sketching/hashing methods is unclear: The proposed PoP is very close in spirit to feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch/Count-Sketch applied to word–context counts (sparse positive hash projections and bucketed accumulation). Prior work in streaming approximate count matrices and their use in NLP should be cited and contrasted. The contribution may be better framed as “PPMI in a sketch space” with careful analysis, rather than as a new projection family. Lack of discussion of this literature weakens the novelty claim.

- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Results are reported only on MEN, with window-based co-occurrence from a single corpus. There are no comparisons to strong predictive baselines trained on the same data (SGNS, GloVe, fastText) nor to PPMI+SVD at comparable target dimensionalities. Claims of being “comparable to neural embeddings” lean on numbers from other papers and different training setups. No extrinsic or additional intrinsic datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words) are included.

- Inconsistent similarity metrics across methods: RI and count-based models are evaluated with cosine, while PoP uses Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and Pearson post-PPMI. This makes cross-method comparisons less clean. The argument that Pearson/Kendall are too costly for high-dimensional count models is not an adequate reason to avoid a fair comparison (e.g., on a subset or with efficient implementations), especially when advocating for metric choice as a core part of the contribution.

- Insufficient quantitative efficiency evidence: While the paper emphasizes speed and memory savings, there are no runtime/memory measurements or complexity comparisons against baselines (dense PPMI, PPMI+SVD, SGNS/GloVe). Concrete numbers (time, peak memory, throughput) would substantiate the main practical claim.

- Theoretical gaps and some questionable statements: The paper explicitly lacks theoretical analysis (even high-level bounds) of distortion/error under PoP. Some claims (e.g., RI “sum of rows/columns is always 0” precluding PPMI) are inaccurate as stated; in expectation, zero-mean projections can yield negative entries, making log undefined, but sums are not identically zero. Alternatives (e.g., shifting, nonnegative sketches) and their trade-offs should be discussed.

Questions to authors
- Relation to sketching/hashing: How does PoP differ from Count-Min Sketch, Count-Sketch, and feature hashing applied to word–context matrices? Can you position PoP’s distribution (Equation 2) and update rule relative to these, including collision behavior and bias? Why not simply use standard CMSketch with multiple hash functions and then compute PPMI on the sketch?

- PMI/PPMI on projected counts: PPMI requires consistent marginals. When computing PPMI in PoP-space, what exactly are the row/column sums representing, and how do collisions bias PMI estimates? Can you characterize when PPMI computed on compressed counts approximates the true PPMI ordering? Any empirical ablation measuring distortion of PMI under projection?

- Fair baselines and metrics: Can you report results where all methods use their best-performing similarity metric, and also a setting where all use the same metric for strict comparability? For count-based baselines, Pearson/Kendall can be computed on a reduced vocabulary or with efficient partial sorting; have you tried that?

- Broader evaluation and efficiency: Please add results on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, and at least one extrinsic task. Include wall-clock time and peak memory for building each model, as well as for applying PPMI and (optionally) SVD, under matched hardware.

- Hyperparameterization: How should one choose m and s as a function of vocabulary size and context sparsity? Can you provide a rule-of-thumb or a small held-out tuning procedure?

- Reproducibility: Will you release code regardless of acceptance? Please specify preprocessing details (tokenization, vocab thresholds, stopwording), random seeds, and exact PPMI variant (smoothing, shift, scaling).

Decision
Borderline reject. The idea of enabling PPMI in a compressed, streaming-friendly space is practical and could be impactful, but the novelty over established sketching/hashing techniques is not convincingly established, and the evaluation is too narrow to support the performance and efficiency claims. A revised version that situates PoP within the sketching literature, provides fair and broader comparisons (including neural and PPMI+SVD baselines), reports concrete efficiency metrics, and clarifies metric choices would be much stronger.
