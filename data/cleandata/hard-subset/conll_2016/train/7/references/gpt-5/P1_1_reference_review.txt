Summary and Claimed Contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection method that uses nonnegative, integer-valued index vectors to incrementally construct low-dimensional semantic spaces. Because the projected space preserves nonnegativity and has E(R)>0, the authors argue it becomes amenable to count-based weighting schemes such as PPMI applied post-projection, which is infeasible for standard RI (Gaussian/±1 projections).
- Empirically, PoP (with Kendall’s τb as similarity) outperforms RI and simple count-based models on MEN. With PPMI applied in the projected space and Pearson correlation for similarity, PoP attains Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75 on MEN, comparable to published neural embeddings, while being far cheaper computationally (no training; low-dimensional weighting).
- The paper includes parameter studies varying dimensionality m and sparsity s (non-zero elements per index vector), and discusses randomization effects.

Main Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, incremental, low-compute pipeline that projects counts into a low-dimensional nonnegative space and applies PPMI there, achieving competitive MEN results with minimal resources.
2) An empirical study showing PoP > RI and count-based baselines on MEN, plus analyses of m and s.
3) A conceptual argument for using correlation-based similarities (τb pre-PPMI; Pearson post-PPMI) given the induced distributions.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The method is easy to implement, incremental, and does not require expensive training or eigen-decompositions. Applying PPMI on a low-dimensional matrix is appealing and could be impactful in low-resource/online settings.
- Clear empirical gains over RI and raw co-occurrence: On MEN, PoP consistently improves over RI (which preserves distances but does not increase discriminativeness) and over unweighted counts, particularly in smaller windows.
- Sensible parameter exploration: The paper studies effects of dimensionality and sparsity, variance across random runs, and discusses non-orthogonality and collision effects—useful guidance for practitioners.
- Honest discussion of theory gaps and randomized behavior: The authors are transparent about the lack of current guarantees and position the work as empirical, with pointers to related theory.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning: The method appears closely related to known sketching/feature hashing techniques, especially Count-Min Sketch (CMS) and the hashing trick (nonnegative, additive, collision-prone projections), as well as Achlioptas-type sparse projections. Not citing or comparing to CMS/feature hashing and nonnegative random projections weakens the novelty claim and misses an important body of related theory (bias, variance, collision bounds).
- Evaluation is narrow and comparisons are not strictly fair: Results are only on MEN with a single corpus (UKWaC). No comparisons to strong baselines trained on the same data (e.g., SGNS/GloVe/PPMI-SVD), no additional datasets (e.g., WordSim, SimLex-999, Rare Words), and no significance testing. Moreover, PoP is evaluated with τb (pre-PPMI) while baselines use cosine, potentially confounding fairness. The neural comparisons are indirect (from the literature), not controlled on the same corpus.
- Lack of runtime/memory evidence: The paper makes strong efficiency claims (seconds/minutes) but provides no wall-clock timings, memory footprints, or complexity-vs-baseline plots (e.g., building dense PPMI vs PoP+PPMI). This is crucial for the claimed contribution.
- Questionable or imprecise mathematical claims: 
  - “RI row/column sums are always 0,” hence PPMI undefined, is not accurate; RI produces values centered around 0 in expectation, but not identically zero; more importantly, applying PMI to signed projected values lacks probabilistic meaning. The core issue is that random projections do not preserve counts/marginals or nonnegativity, making PMI ill-defined—not just the sum being zero.
  - The distribution in Eq. (2) is not well motivated (heavy-tailed positive weights via floor(1/U^α)); there is no ablation vs simply using 1s as in CMS or vs alternative sparse distributions; the choice of α=0.5 is unexplained.
  - The argument for τb (vs cosine/ρ) is somewhat ad hoc and not supported by broad empirical comparisons.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps: Code/data promised only upon acceptance. Preprocessing details are insufficient (vocab selection, min counts, window weighting, sub-sampling, context filtering). Figures lack precise numbers and error bars; hyperparameter settings (m, s) vary across plots without a consolidated table.

Questions to Authors
- Novelty/related work: How does PoP differ from Count-Min Sketch or feature hashing applied to co-occurrence matrices, followed by PPMI on the sketched matrix? Please provide conceptual and empirical comparisons and discuss theoretical error characteristics (bias, overestimation, collision bounds) known for CMS.
- Fairness of similarity metrics: Why evaluate PoP with τb while using cosine for count/RI? Please include results with a consistent metric across methods (cosine, Pearson), and report τb for RI and counts too. Post-PPMI, please show cosine and Pearson for all methods.
- Semantics of PPMI in the projected space: Since projection destroys additivity and true marginals, what is the justification for interpreting projected entries as pseudo-counts in Eq. (3)? Can you measure the correlation between PPMI computed in the original space and PPMI computed after PoP projection for words/contexts that fit in memory (small-n setting)?
- Efficiency evidence: Please provide wall-clock time and peak memory for (i) building counts, (ii) PoP projection, (iii) PPMI on dense counts vs PoP-projected matrices, and (iv) inference time for similarity, all on the same hardware, and compare to SGNS/GloVe on UKWaC.
- Distribution in Eq. (2): Why the heavy-tailed positive weights instead of binary 1s? Do these weights help empirically vs 1s? Please add an ablation and clarify α and s selection guidelines.
- Robustness and generality: Results on more benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW, analogy) and corpora would strengthen claims. Can you report statistical significance and variability over runs?

Overall, the paper proposes a promising, simple idea with practical potential, but requires stronger positioning relative to sketching/hashing literature, broader and fairer evaluation, and concrete efficiency evidence to warrant acceptance.
