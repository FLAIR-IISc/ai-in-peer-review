Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme in which the projection matrix has only positive integer entries. The key idea is to (a) build low-dimensional incremental “count-like” representations by summing positive random index vectors for observed contexts (as in RI), and (b) subsequently apply PPMI weighting directly in the projected space, which is argued to be infeasible for RI because RI yields zero-mean, signed entries. The authors also advocate Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and Pearson after PPMI (Gaussianized). They present experiments on MEN using UKWaC, analyze sensitivity to dimensionality m and sparsity s, and claim competitive performance (≈0.75–0.78 Spearman) with much lower compute than neural embeddings.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Method: A positive-only, sparse random projection for incremental word space construction that enables post-projection PPMI weighting.
2) Empirical observation: On MEN, PoP+PPMI+Pearson performs substantially better than RI and simple count models and approaches reported neural baselines, with parameter and distributional analyses.
3) Efficiency claim: PPMI computed on low-dimensional projected spaces is much faster than on high-dimensional count matrices (though concrete runtimes are not reported).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: PoP is a small but clever modification of random indexing that preserves nonnegativity and thus enables information-theoretic weighting (PPMI) post-projection. The incremental, one-pass construction is appealing for streaming or large-scale settings.
- Clear empirical improvement over RI and raw counts: The comparisons show that (i) RI preserves distances but does not improve relatedness, and (ii) PoP, especially with PPMI, yields meaningful gains on MEN.
- Sensible distributional reasoning: The paper connects the distributional shape of vectors (long-tailed vs. Gaussianized via log/PPMI) to the choice of correlation measures (Kendall vs. Pearson) and provides informative histograms and parameter sweeps (m, s).
- Parameter analyses and variance reporting: The multiple runs and min–max bands help convey robustness of the stochastic method, and the discussion of non-orthogonality as s increases is useful.
- Potential to bridge two worlds: If sound, the “PPMI after projection” idea could combine efficiency of sketching/RP with accuracy of well-established weighting.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and baselines: The empirical section only tests MEN on UKWaC. Stronger baselines (SGNS/Skip-gram with negative sampling, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with standard best practices such as subsampling/CDS/context weighting, modern non-neural baselines) are missing. MEN alone is insufficient; SimLex-999, WordSim-353 (Sim/Rel), Rare Words, and at least one downstream task would better establish generality. Without these, the “competitive with neural embeddings” claim is not well substantiated.
- Theoretical justification and correctness of PPMI-after-projection: The paper concedes the lack of theory. More critically, PMI is defined over joint/marginal probabilities of words and contexts in the original event space. Computing PPMI over “sketched” pseudo-context dimensions (random index coordinates) introduces collisions and distortions whose bias/variance properties are not analyzed. Under what conditions does PPMI on the sketched matrix approximate PPMI on the full co-occurrence matrix then projected? This is the core novelty and requires at least an approximate analysis or empirical ablation (e.g., compare to feature hashing buckets with/without signs; compare PPMI-before vs. PPMI-after projection when feasible on a smaller setting).
- Questionable or overstated claims: The statement that “in RI the sum of row and column vectors is always 0, hence PPMI is undefined” is not accurate as written. RI produces signed projected values that are not valid nonnegative counts; that is the core incompatibility with PMI, not that sums are identically zero. Similarly, “cosine equals Pearson” only under centering/normalization conditions; the discussion oversimplifies this relationship. The choice of Kendall’s τb as a vector similarity measure also needs stronger justification: τb operates on ranks of coordinates, which depend on arbitrary component ordering and may discard magnitude information; this is unusual in distributional semantics and merits ablation versus cosine/inner product in the same spaces.
- Missing baselines from related sketching literature: The approach is close in spirit to feature hashing (hashing trick) with or without signs, and to nonnegative random projections. A comparison to hashed co-occurrence with PPMI in the hashed space is essential. Without it, it is difficult to assess how much the heavy-tailed positive entries (floor(1/U^α)) help relative to simpler 0/1 or fixed small positive entries.
- Efficiency claims lack concrete evidence: The paper asserts seconds/minutes vs. hours/days but does not report wall-clock time, memory, or throughput for corpus scanning, model construction, and PPMI computation, nor a comparison to baselines on the same hardware. τb has O(m log m) per similarity; for large vocabularies this could still be nontrivial—some complexity and profiling would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide runtime and memory benchmarks (end-to-end) versus RI, PPMI+SVD, SGNS, and feature hashing on the same corpus and vocabulary size? Please include vocabulary sizes beyond the MEN subset to demonstrate scalability.
- How does PoP+PPMI compare to: (a) PPMI computed on the full co-occurrence matrix then projected to m via SVD (or random projection), and (b) feature hashing (with and without signs) followed by PPMI in the hashed space? Ablations isolating the effect of positive-only and of the heavy-tailed index weights would be valuable.
- Could you evaluate on additional benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353-Sim/Rel, RW) and at least one downstream task (e.g., text classification or similarity) to validate generality?
- Please clarify the similarity function choice: How sensitive are results to using cosine vs. τb in the raw PoP space? Is τb’s dependence on coordinate ordering a concern? Do you normalize vectors prior to Pearson after PPMI?
- Can you provide a small-scale setting where both “PPMI before projection” and “PPMI after PoP” are computable, to empirically assess approximation quality (correlations of similarities, nearest neighbors overlap)?
- How do you set s in practice as vocabulary and corpus scale? Any heuristic based on observed sparsity of the original co-occurrence matrix?

Additional comments
- The paper would benefit from tightening the exposition, correcting imprecise statements about cosine vs. Pearson and RI’s sums, and improving related work coverage (feature hashing, nonnegative/random projections, sketching for co-occurrence).
- Releasing code and scripts is important for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, and the empirical gains over RI are clear; however, the evaluation is too narrow, key baselines are missing (especially feature hashing + PPMI), theoretical underpinnings for PPMI-after-projection are not provided, and some claims are overstated. With a broader, stronger experimental section and clearer analysis of the sketched-PPMI rationale, this work could become a solid contribution.
