Summary and claimed contributions
- Introduces PoP (Positive-only Projections), a random projection scheme with sparse, strictly positive integer index vectors that enables applying PPMI weighting after projection, unlike RI-style zero-mean projections.
- Empirical claim: PoP and especially PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieve competitive MEN relatedness scores (~0.75) with low-dimensional vectors and without neural training, outperforming count-based and RI baselines on MEN; also explores parameter effects (dimension m, sparsity s, nonzeros).
- Argues for Kendall’s τb as a similarity metric on unweighted PoP (non-Gaussian coordinates) and Pearson after PPMI (Gaussianized coordinates).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and potential practicality: the projection can be built incrementally like RI with very low compute, and post-projection PPMI is indeed attractive for efficiency.
- Empirical ablations: some exploration of m, number of non-zeros, and similarity measures (τ vs Pearson) provides useful intuitions; the observation that PPMI roughly Gaussianizes projected coordinates is reasonable and illustrated.
- The manuscript provides a clear algorithmic recipe for building PoP spaces and computing similarities, and highlights a plausible niche (streaming/incremental construction with later global weighting).
- The insight to tailor the similarity measure to the distributional properties (nonparametric τ vs parametric Pearson) is thoughtful and could matter in practice.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is far too narrow and often unfair. Only MEN is used, no SimLex-999, WordSim353/Sim, Rare Words, or downstream tasks; no statistical significance tests. Comparisons are confounded: the authors use cosine for RI and count models but τ for PoP, then Pearson for PoP+PPMI, making like-for-like comparisons impossible. RI with τ (and especially after PPMI or other nonnegative transforms) is not evaluated; SVD-PPMI baselines are not matched on corpus and settings. Claims of parity with neural embeddings cite results from different corpora/settings.
- Core claim about PPMI being “undefined” for RI because “row/column sums are always 0” is incorrect. With zero-mean projections, entries can be negative so probabilities/logs are indeed problematic, but row/column sums are not identically zero; the correct issue is sign, not sum. Moreover, RI variants with nonnegative projections or offsets, SPPMI shifts, or absolute-value transforms could be considered as baselines. This undermines the stated exclusivity of PoP.
- PMI/PPMI semantics on projected features are not justified. After projection, “contexts” are random mixtures of original contexts; PMI on these synthetic features no longer estimates word–context association probabilities. The paper leans on a variance-stabilization argument, but then the method is no longer PMI in the information-theoretic sense. This conceptual mismatch should be acknowledged and studied (e.g., bias introduced by projection before PMI).
- Missing theory where it matters most. The paper concedes lack of bounds or JL-style guarantees for the proposed distribution, but also provides no analysis of expected biases in co-occurrence estimates, E[T], Var[T], concentration with respect to m and s, or how τ behaves under the induced distortions. Claims like σ^2_δ ≈ 1/m are asserted by analogy without derivation for the proposed heavy-tailed positive distribution 1/U^α.
- Incrementality claim is overstated. PPMI requires global row/column totals; thus the key performance comes only after a global, non-incremental pass. No discussion of online PPMI approximations or how often reweighting must be done, nor empirical runtime/memory benchmarks. The paper repeatedly claims “seconds/minutes” but reports no measured wall-clock times, memory footprints, or hardware details.
- Methodological gaps and ambiguities: Equation (2) is ambiguously typeset (is it floor(1/U^α)?); α is fixed to 0.5 without justification or sensitivity analysis; the probability that a row in T has a nonzero is hand-waved (“can be guaranteed”) without quantifying (1−s)^m; no details on context extraction (windowing, weighting, stopwords) beyond a few remarks; no hyperparameter selection protocol; no random seed control; no significance or confidence intervals beyond min/max shading.

Questions to authors
- Clarify Equation (2): confirm tij ∼ 0 w.p. 1−s and tij = floor(1/U^α) w.p. s with U∼Uniform(0,1]; what α values were tested, and how sensitive are results to α?
- How do you justify PMI/PPMI on projected, synthetic features? Can you characterize the bias between PMI on C versus PMI on P = C T as a function of T’s distribution? Any empirical study comparing PPMI on C versus P with matched ranks?
- Why not evaluate RI (and other random projections) with (i) τ for unweighted vectors, and (ii) nonnegative transforms enabling PPMI (e.g., shift/offset, absolute values, truncated Gaussians)? This would isolate the benefit of “positive only” vs “random projection per se.”
- Please report matched, controlled baselines on the same corpus: SVD-PPMI (with and without truncation), SGNS/word2vec, GloVe. Include runtime and memory numbers for building and weighting, with hardware specs.
- Provide statistical significance tests on MEN and evaluate on additional standard datasets (SimLex-999, WS353/Sim, RW). Are results stable across random seeds (distributional heavy tails can increase variance)?

Additional comments and minor issues
- Inconsistent notation: T vs R for projection; E(R) notation sometimes written “E=(0).”
- Claim that “sufficiently large s guarantees nonzero column sums” is misleading; please quantify P(column sum=0) under the stated distribution.
- Similarity metrics should be held constant across methods when possible; otherwise report both cosine and τ/Pearson for all.
- The claim that “RI retains distances” is not tied to cosine; please ensure all baselines use the same similarity unless you justify otherwise.
- Many typos/formatting: “To a large extend,” “effect the performance,” inline footnotes and “Ibid,” broken sentences (“As put by,”), figure captions with missing units/axes, “If the paper accepted,” missing references (e.g., QasemiZadeh, 2015?). Please thoroughly proofread.
- Reproducibility: code/data promised “if accepted” is insufficient; include implementation details sufficient to re-run, or provide anonymized code.

Overall recommendation: Reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the evaluation is too limited and the comparisons insufficiently controlled; key claims are either incorrect (RI/PPMI) or unsubstantiated (PMI on projected features), and the paper needs substantial theoretical, empirical, and presentation improvements.
