Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method to construct low-dimensional, incremental distributional semantic spaces. Unlike traditional Random Indexing (RI), PoP uses sparse, nonnegative integer projection vectors, which the authors argue allows subsequent weighting with PPMI. The paper motivates Kendall’s τb as a similarity metric for non-Gaussian projected spaces and shows, on the MEN relatedness benchmark with UKWaC, that PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines and, when combined with PPMI and Pearson correlation, approaches the performance of some neural embeddings while being far cheaper to compute. The paper also presents a brief parameter sensitivity analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple positive-only random projection scheme that preserves nonnegativity and enables efficient downstream application of PPMI weighting in a compressed, incremental co-occurrence space.
2) An empirical demonstration on MEN that PoP outperforms RI and count-based baselines, and that PoP+PPMI+Pearson can be competitive with some neural baselines at far lower computational cost.
3) An initial exploration of design choices (projection dimensionality, sparsity, choice of similarity function pre/post PPMI) and a qualitative discussion of the method’s randomized behavior.

Strengths
- Practical, simple idea with potential impact: Using sparse, positive-only projections to keep vectors nonnegative is an appealing way to combine the incremental, low-cost benefits of RI-like methods with the discriminative gains of PPMI. The approach is easy to implement and potentially useful for streaming or resource-constrained settings.
- Clear empirical gains over RI/count baselines: On MEN, PoP improves over both count-based and RI under the same window settings; the gains are especially visible at smaller windows, and PoP remains robust as window size grows.
- Sensible analysis of similarity metrics: The observation that Kendall’s τb works better pre-PPMI (non-Gaussian) while Pearson works better post-PPMI (Gaussianized) is plausible and supported by results; this framing is useful to practitioners.
- Initial parameter sweep: The paper explores dimensionality and sparsity of projection vectors, and discusses the role of collisions and orthogonality. These analyses help demystify the behavior of randomized methods.
- Efficiency motivation: The target of “seconds/minutes” to build models with competitive accuracy is compelling for many applications, if substantiated.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and missing strong baselines: Results are reported only on MEN with UKWaC. There is no evaluation on other standard similarity/relatedness sets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW, MTurk-771) or on downstream tasks. Crucially, comparisons to widely used baselines (SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD) trained under matched conditions are missing. The paper cites neural results from the literature but does not reproduce them on the same corpus.
- Unclear relationship to prior hashing/sketching methods: PoP appears closely related to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing (which also yield positive-only, incremental, low-dimensional approximate co-occurrence counts and have known error guarantees). These lines of work are not cited or compared, and the claimed novelty over them is unclear. A direct empirical and conceptual comparison is needed.
- Method specification is ambiguous: Equation (2) is difficult to parse (e.g., “1Uα c”; is this ceil(1/U^α)?), and the role of s is inconsistently described. In places s is “extremely small,” elsewhere it is effectively set to k/m to achieve k nonzeros per row. The claim “each row has at least one nonzero” is not guaranteed under Bernoulli sampling unless enforced by construction. More precise notation and an explicit algorithm are needed.
- Some technical claims are inaccurate or overstated: The statement that RI’s rows/columns “are always 0” (hence PPMI undefined) is not correct; E[R]=0 does not imply exact zero sums. The real issue is that RI yields negative coordinates that cannot be directly treated as probabilities for PMI. The paper should correct this and more carefully justify why PPMI on PoP’s projected counts approximates PPMI on true counts.
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: Baselines use cosine, whereas PoP uses Kendall before PPMI and Pearson after; the metric choice itself can drive differences. Assessing all methods with multiple similarity metrics (cosine, Pearson, τb) would isolate the effect of the projection. The efficiency claims lack quantitative evidence (wall-clock time, memory, hardware), and there is no streaming/incremental experiment despite that being a key motivation.
- No theoretical characterization: While a full theory is not required, the absence of even basic bias/variance analysis or collision error bounds (common in sketching literature) is a gap, especially given the emphasis on randomized projections.

Questions to Authors
- Please clarify Equation (2): the exact distribution of nonzero entries (is it ceil(1/U^α)?), the expected value, and how s is set in practice. Do you enforce at least one nonzero per row (e.g., resampling), or is it only in expectation?
- How does PoP compare empirically and conceptually to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing (with/without signs)? These methods also produce positive-only, streaming, low-dimensional co-occurrence approximations and have known accuracy guarantees. Can you provide baselines and discuss error properties?
- Can you evaluate on additional datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, MTurk-771, Rare Words) and at least one downstream task? Do gains persist beyond MEN?
- Please report compute/memory: preprocessing time, projection time, PPMI time, total runtime and peak memory for all methods on the same hardware and corpus size.
- Can you provide ablations using the same similarity metric across methods to control for metric effects? For instance, cosine and Pearson on all methods pre/post PPMI.
- Do you have evidence that PPMI on PoP approximates PPMI on the true count matrix? E.g., measure bias due to collisions vs. dimensionality/sparsity, akin to CMS error bounds.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and practically appealing, and the early results are encouraging. However, the paper needs clearer method specification, stronger and fairer empirical evaluation with appropriate baselines (including sketching/hashing methods and neural models), corrected technical claims, and quantitative efficiency evidence. With these revisions, this work could become a useful contribution.
