Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random projection technique that maps high-dimensional count-based co-occurrence matrices into low-dimensional spaces using sparse, non-negative, integer-valued index vectors. Because the projected space remains non-negative with positive sums, PoP allows subsequent weighting with PPMI (and other count-based weightings), unlike standard RI with zero-mean signed projections.
- It argues for using Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure on raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian), and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussianized), and evaluates on MEN with UKWaC, showing PoP>RI and PoP+PPMI reaching about 0.75 ρ with modest dimensionality.
- It provides some parameter studies (m, sparsity s/number of nonzeros) and qualitative distributional analyses, and claims efficiency and incrementality (no expensive training; fast weighting due to low dimensionality).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical sketching-style variant of random indexing that uses non-negative sparse projections so that PPMI can be computed in the projected space, yielding competitive accuracy at low cost.
2) An empirical study showing when Kendall vs Pearson works better, and how PoP parameters (m and number of non-zeros) affect performance and variance.
3) The claim of incremental, fast construction and weighting on low-dimensional projections, enabling use of heavy weightings like PPMI without operating on the original huge matrices.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: Using non-negative sparse projections to enable PPMI post-weighting is an appealing engineering idea. It addresses a widely felt pain point: computing PMI/PPMI on huge, sparse count spaces is expensive, while standard RI precludes PMI because of signed entries.
- Empirical gains over RI and raw counts: On MEN, PoP (with Kendall) consistently outperforms both RI and count-based models across window sizes; PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches 0.75, which is in the ballpark of classical PPMI+SVD/neural baselines on similar corpora.
- Parameter analysis and stability: The paper reports averages over multiple runs and provides variance bands, explores m and number of non-zeros, and discusses non-orthogonality as a function of sparsity—useful practical guidance.
- Sensible similarity choices: The rationale for τb on non-Gaussian distributions and Pearson after PPMI (Gaussianization) is reasonable and supported by observations.
- Incremental and efficient: Building in reduced dimension and then applying PPMI there promises substantial computational savings; the approach is naturally streaming/incremental.

Weaknesses
- Missing theoretical grounding and key related work: While lack of formal guarantees is acknowledged, the paper does not connect PoP to closely related sketching literature (feature hashing, Count-Min Sketch, Count Sketch) that also uses non-negative (or signed) sparse projections to accumulate counts. PoP’s construction appears very close to feature hashing/Count-Min on bag-of-words co-occurrences; this overlap, and differences (e.g., collisions, bias correction), should be discussed and empirically compared. Without this, novelty is unclear.
- Limited and dated evaluation: Results are only on MEN with UKWaC, primarily varying window sizes. Stronger baselines (SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with standard hyperparameters, context distribution smoothing, shifted PPMI) and additional datasets (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW, SimVerb-3500, modern STS benchmarks) are missing. Claims of competitiveness with neural methods are not supported by direct, controlled comparisons.
- PMI semantics on projected counts: Equation (3) computes PPMI on the projected space P. But the entries in P are not true joint counts; they are sums of hashed random bins. The paper reframes PPMI as a power transform rather than true MI, but then the interpretation and fairness of comparisons to “true” PPMI on original counts becomes muddled. A discussion of the bias/variance of PPMI computed on PoP sketches, and whether it approximates PPMI on the original matrix, is needed.
- Incomplete methodological details and clarity: The distribution for non-zero entries in T (ceil/floor of U^α) is unclear and likely degenerates to 1 almost always; this deserves clarification and justification. Preprocessing (vocab size, frequency cutoffs, stopword handling, context selection, weighting details like smoothing/shifting) is under-specified. Runtime and memory benchmarks, crucial to the efficiency claim, are not reported.
- Overstatements and scope: The statement that RI cannot be PPMI-weighted because row/column sums are always zero is too strong; rather, the presence of negative entries makes standard PMI ill-defined. Also, while PoP is said to be incremental and fast, there is no empirical evaluation of streaming updates or wall-clock comparisons against baselines.

Questions to authors
- Relation to feature hashing and sketching: How does PoP differ from feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch applied to word–context matrices (multiple hash functions, non-negative updates)? What are the theoretical/empirical implications for collision bias and variance? Can you compare PoP directly to hashing/CMS baselines with identical dimensionality and sparsity?
- PPMI on projected space: Do you view PPMI(P) as approximating PPMI(C) in any sense? Can you quantify the bias introduced by computing PMI on sketched counts? Have you tried variants like shifted PPMI, context distribution smoothing, or smoothing to handle zeros?
- Distribution of T: Please clarify Equation (2). With U in (0,1] and α=0.5, ceil(U^α) (or floor?) will almost always be 1. Are non-zero entries always 1? If not, what is the intended distribution and why is it beneficial?
- Efficiency and incrementality: Please provide wall-clock time and memory comparisons for building and weighting PoP vs (i) raw PMI/PPMI on counts, (ii) SGNS/GloVe training, and (iii) feature hashing/CMS. Also, show incremental updates (adding new data) and their cost.
- Broader evaluation: Can you report results on additional intrinsic benchmarks (SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, WS353, RW) and at least one downstream task (STS or text classification) with fair preprocessing and strong baselines?

Additional comments
- The Kendall τb choice is interesting but unusual for vector similarity in NLP; a brief ablation against cosine and Spearman on raw PoP vectors would help.
- Some notation and claims need tightening (e.g., “retain distances” vs inner products; RI’s means exactly zero vs approximately zero).
- Releasing code and exact preprocessing details will be essential for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is promising and practically useful, but the novelty relative to established sketching methods is unclear and the evaluation is too limited. A revised version that situates PoP within the sketching literature, adds strong baselines and broader experiments, clarifies method details, and provides efficiency measurements would be significantly stronger.
