Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly nonnegative integer entries that enables applying PPMI weighting after projection. The method incrementally builds low-dimensional semantic vectors by summing positive index vectors for observed co-occurrences, then computes similarity with Kendall’s τb on raw PoP vectors, or Pearson correlation after PPMI. On the MEN relatedness benchmark (using UKWaC), the authors report that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models without weighting, and that PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75—comparable to dense PPMI and some neural baselines—while being much more efficient. The paper also explores sensitivity to projection dimensionality m and sparsity s.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple random projection scheme with nonnegative entries that is compatible with information-theoretic weighting (PPMI) post-projection, unlike standard RI with zero-mean entries.

2) An empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson can match the accuracy of dense PPMI models on MEN at much lower dimensionality and with incremental, training-free construction.

3) Practical guidance on similarity metrics and parameters: τb suits unweighted PoP, Pearson suits PoP+PPMI; analysis of m and sparsity s.

Strengths
- Practical, simple idea with clear motivation: Using nonnegative projections to preserve the semantics of “counts” so that PPMI remains meaningful post-projection. This addresses a known limitation of RI-like methods.

- Incremental and potentially very efficient: The approach avoids expensive training and allows building spaces online. The low dimensionality makes global weighting feasible. This is appealing for large-scale or streaming settings.

- Competitive accuracy on MEN: The reported ρ ≈ 0.75 for PoP+PPMI+Pearson is on par with dense PPMI and within the range of some neural models, suggesting the approach is viable.

- Sensitivity analysis: The paper carries out multiple runs, shows variance bands, and studies both m and the number of nonzero entries, including a helpful discussion of orthogonality and distortions.

- Thoughtful discussion of similarity measures: The rationale for τb on non-Gaussian raw PoP and Pearson after PPMI is coherent and empirically supported.

Weaknesses
- Limited and confounded evaluation: Results are shown only on MEN. There are no additional similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, WS-353, RW) nor extrinsic tasks. Moreover, Figure 1 compares PoP (τb) to count/RI (cosine), conflating the method with the similarity function. For a fair comparison, each baseline should also be paired with its best-performing similarity measure, or at least the same metric across methods.

- Incomplete baselines and missing quantification of efficiency: Dense PPMI+cos is included, but key baselines like PPMI+SVD (truncated to the same output dimensionality), SGNS/GloVe with standard settings, and hashing-based or Count-Min/feature hashing compressions are absent. Claims of large efficiency gains are not supported by wall-clock/runtime, memory, or throughput measurements.

- Theoretical underpinnings are thin: The paper acknowledges the lack of formal guarantees (e.g., JL-type distortion bounds), but does not provide even simple moments (E, Var) of the projection, normalization/scaling needed to control magnitude, or a discussion of the bias introduced in PMI estimates through projection. The definition of the entry distribution is also unclear (see questions below).

- Clarity and notation issues: There are inconsistent symbols (T vs R), unclear definition of tij (floor of 1/U^α?), typographical issues, and informal statements (e.g., “guarantee each row has a nonzero” with s choice) that hinder reproducibility and understanding.

- Overstatement of claims: The main performance lift appears to come from PPMI; PoP+PPMI essentially matches dense PPMI rather than exceeding it. Without stronger baselines and significance testing, the claim of competitive performance to neural approaches remains tentative.

Questions to authors
- Distribution of projection entries: Please formalize Equation (2). Is tij = floor(1/U^α) with probability s and 0 otherwise? If so, what are E[tij] and Var[tij] as functions of s, α, and m? Do you apply any normalization to control vector norms across words with different frequencies?

- Similarity metrics: Can you report all methods under multiple similarity functions (cosine, Pearson, Kendall), selecting the best per method for fairness? In particular, does using τb (or Pearson) improve the count-based and RI baselines?

- Efficiency: Please provide wall-clock time, memory usage, and computational complexity comparisons for building (and weighting) (i) dense PPMI, (ii) PoP+PPMI, (iii) SGNS/GloVe, and (iv) RI, on the same hardware/corpus, including index construction and PPMI computation.

- Baselines: Could you include PPMI+SVD truncated to the same target dimensionality m, and feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch baselines that also enable low-dimensional, incremental updates? Also include widely used SGNS/GloVe with standard hyperparameters.

- Generalization: Please report additional datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, WS-353, RW) and one or two extrinsic tasks. Provide statistical significance tests over multiple runs.

- Parameterization and robustness: How do you set s to ensure each row has at least one nonzero with high probability? What is the probability that a context row is all-zero given s and m? How sensitive are results to α, sub-sampling, context weighting, and stopword pruning?

- Reproducibility: Can you share code and exact preprocessing details (tokenization, lemmatization, windowing, frequency thresholds, random seeds) for the camera-ready or during rebuttal?

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an appealing and practically useful idea, but the current submission falls short on experimental breadth, fairness of comparisons, theoretical clarity, and empirical validation of the claimed efficiency gains. Strengthening the evaluation (broader benchmarks, stronger baselines, runtime/memory profiles), clarifying the projection distribution and scaling, and tightening the exposition would make this a much stronger contribution.
