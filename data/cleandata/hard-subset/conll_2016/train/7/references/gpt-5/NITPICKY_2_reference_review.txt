Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly non-negative integer entries, enabling incremental low-dimensional semantic space construction. Unlike RI/ISA/RMI, PoP’s projected matrices have positive totals, so the authors claim PPMI can be applied after projection.
- The paper advocates Kendall’s τb for similarity in PoP spaces (pre-weighting), arguing non-Gaussian projections, and Pearson’s r after PPMI (post-weighting). It reports MEN relatedness results on UKWaC, claiming PoP(+PPMI) is competitive with neural baselines while being far cheaper to compute.
- Empirical explorations analyze m (dimension) and s (sparsity) and discuss stability/variance of randomized outcomes.

Strengths
1) Practical idea: random positive-only sketches that admit post hoc weighting. The high-level goal—obtaining fast, incremental, low-dimensional spaces that still allow weighting—is appealing, and positive-only projections make PMI-style weighting at least computationally feasible.
2) Efficiency focus: The emphasis on avoiding heavy training and enabling quick incremental updates is relevant to practitioners. The empirical sweep over m and s, and the observation that PoP+PPMI+Pearson improves with m and stabilizes variance, are useful.
3) Empirical signal: Despite methodological issues (see below), some consistent improvements over RI on MEN are shown, and the trend that PPMI+Pearson > PMI+Cosine on Gaussian-like distributions aligns with prior findings.

Weaknesses (major issues; likely fatal in current form)
1) Evaluation is too narrow and not apples-to-apples.
   - Only one dataset (MEN). No SimLex-999, WS-353 (sim/rel), RG-65, RW, SimVerb-3500, analogy tests, or downstream tasks. Results on a single relatedness set are insufficient for CoNLL.
   - Baselines are weak and mismatched: RI is measured with cosine while PoP uses Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and Pearson post-PPMI. This confounds method and similarity measure. RI+PPMI is declared “undefined” (see next point), so the comparison becomes structurally unfair.
   - Neural baselines are cited from other papers and not reproduced under the same corpus, preprocessing, and hyperparameters. No comparisons to modern SGNS/GloVe/fastText on UKWaC with matched windows and weighting.
   - No statistical significance tests across runs; shaded areas are mentioned but details are missing in figures provided.

2) Conceptual/mathematical issues with PPMI after projection.
   - PMI is an information-theoretic measure over word–context counts. Here, PMI is computed over word–random-dimension counts in P = C×T. Those dimensions do not correspond to interpretable contexts; the resulting “PPMI” lacks the standard probability semantics. Calling it PPMI is misleading; it is essentially log-rescaling/power-transforming random features. The paper acknowledges losing the information-theoretic interpretation but still bills this as PPMI.
   - The claim that RI cannot be PPMI-weighted because row/column sums are “always 0” is incorrect. Projected counts can be negative with mean 0, making probabilities invalid—but that’s different from sums being always 0. One could clip negatives or use alternative nonnegative reweighting. The argument as written is technically wrong and undermines a central motivation.

3) Incorrect and imprecise definitions.
   - Kendall’s τb is defined over all unordered pairs (i<j); the paper defines concordant/discordant using adjacent pairs (j, j+1). This is incorrect. Although the implementation uses Knight’s O(n log n) algorithm, the wrong definition in the text is a serious technical error.
   - Equation (2) is ambiguous/garbled: tij = {0 w.p. 1−s; floor(1/U^α) w.p. s?}. The LaTeX reads “1Uα c” and is unclear; the distribution, scaling, and moments of t are not clearly specified, yet they are central to the method.
   - Notational inconsistencies abound: Tn×x vs Tn×m, x vs m; R vs T; “E(R)” vs “E = (0)”; “η-normed” typos; “E = 0” vs “E(R) > 0” mixed messaging.

4) Lack of theoretical grounding and parameter rationale.
   - The paper admits no theory or bounds, but still makes claims about δ and σ^2_δ≈1/m by analogy. The chosen distribution (α=0.5) is unjustified; no sensitivity analysis is provided beyond m and s; no normalization to control variance or scale across dimensions is discussed. This is particularly important given heavy-tailed positive values from floor(1/U^α).
   - The claim about RI’s Gaussian assumption is historically imprecise; RI typically uses very sparse ternary codes; Achlioptas-style projections satisfy JL with non-Gaussian distributions. The discussion conflates distributional assumptions.

5) Reproducibility and reporting gaps.
   - No runtime or memory numbers, only qualitative statements (“seconds/minutes”). No implementation details (language, hardware, multi-threading), no vocabulary sizes or token counts, no pruning thresholds beyond one brief mention.
   - Figures and captions refer to trends but precise settings and error bars are unclear; only 10 runs are used for means, but no confidence intervals or statistical tests are provided.
   - Code/data “if accepted” is not sufficient for present-day standards.

Weaknesses (minor but numerous; harm polish and trust)
- Many typos/formatting errors, broken footnotes, dangling equation references, inconsistent citations, awkward phrasing, and missing diacritics (e.g., η-normed-based).
- Misuse of terms: “PMI-Dense+Cos” vs “PPMI-Dense+Cos” without exact configurations; “Gaussian-like” claims not supported by tests (e.g., normality tests).
- The statement “sum of column vectors is always non-zero by choosing s” ignores the nonzero-all-rows probability (1−(1−s)^m)^n; you need explicit resampling to guarantee non-zero rows.

Questions to Authors
1) Provide an unambiguous definition of the projection distribution in Eq. (2), including exact formula, moments, and rationale for α=0.5. How sensitive are results to α and to rescaling/normalization of T?
2) Clarify the feasibility of PPMI on projected dimensions: do you accept that this is no longer PMI over word–context probabilities? Why is this superior to simpler log or Box–Cox transforms on PoP counts?
3) Re-run all baselines with matched similarity measures: RI+Kendall, RI after any nonnegative transform (e.g., clipping), count-based+Pearson after PPMI, and PoP+Cosine. Report significance.
4) Expand evaluation: at least WS-353 (sim/rel), SimLex-999, RW, SimVerb-3500, analogy, and one downstream task, with matched preprocessing. Include SGNS/GloVe/fastText trained on UKWaC as in-paper baselines.
5) Provide concrete efficiency metrics (wall-clock, CPU/GPU, memory) vs. count+PPMI+SVD and vs. SGNS. How does Kendall’s O(m log m) per similarity scale to large vocabularies? Do you use ANN indices?
6) Can you guarantee non-zero rows/columns of T? If not, what resampling/repair do you use? How do you prevent collinearity as s increases? Please quantify non-orthogonality probabilities analytically, not just by simulation.
7) Please correct the Kendall’s τb definition and re-check all claims relying on it.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is intriguing, but the paper in its current form has: (i) a too-narrow and confounded evaluation; (ii) conceptual issues around “PPMI” after random projection; (iii) incorrect definitions and many presentation/notation problems; (iv) no theory or principled parameterization; and (v) insufficient reproducibility and efficiency evidence. With substantial corrections, broader evaluation, and clearer mathematical foundations, this line of work could be impactful.
