Summary and main contributions
- Introduces Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based method with nonnegative, sparse index vectors that allows building low-dimensional distributional spaces incrementally while still supporting PPMI weighting post-projection.
- Empirically shows that PoP (especially PoP+PPMI+Pearson) achieves competitive relatedness scores on MEN with dramatically lower compute than dense count-based+PPMI or neural embeddings, and clearly outperforms RI and unweighted count baselines on the same setup.
- Provides an analysis of similarity measures for non-Gaussian projected spaces (advocating Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and Pearson post-PPMI) and a useful sensitivity study of PoP’s dimensionality and sparsity parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with practical upside: The key idea—use a sparse, positive-only random projection so that projected features remain nonnegative “counts” amenable to PPMI—is simple, elegant, and practically important. This bridges the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI without constructing huge co-occurrence matrices. This is a fresh direction compared to standard RI/RRI/ISA which preclude PPMI because of zero-mean projections.
- Incremental and efficient: The construction is streaming/incremental (RI-like) and sidesteps heavy training. The claim that models build in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days is compelling for large-scale or low-resource settings. This is a strong practical contribution even if absolute performance is not SOTA.
- Solid empirical comparisons against RI and count-based baselines: The experiments convincingly show that PoP beats RI and count-based on MEN across window sizes, and that PPMI over PoP meaningfully boosts performance. The parameter studies (varying m and the number of nonzeros) are informative and align with intuition about sparsity, orthogonality, and variance.
- Sensible treatment of similarity metrics: The argument for Kendall’s τb in non-Gaussian projected spaces and Pearson post-PPMI (after Gaussianization via log/PPMI) is thoughtful, supported by distribution histograms, and likely transferable beyond this task.
- Potentially broad impact: The approach suggests multiple downstream uses—fast preconditioning for neural models, enabling heavier weighting transforms at low dimension, and connections to derandomization and better control of distortions—opening a line of research.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only on MEN with UKWaC and small to moderate windows. No tests on other semantic similarity datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353/Sim, Rare Words), no analogy or sense-related tasks, no downstream extrinsic evaluations. The claimed competitiveness with neural embeddings lacks apples-to-apples comparisons trained on the same corpus and settings.
- Missing quantitative efficiency evidence: While the method is designed for efficiency, the paper lacks concrete runtime, memory, and throughput metrics versus dense PPMI+SVD and standard word2vec/GloVe on the same hardware/data. Such numbers would strengthen the practical claim.
- Theoretical underpinnings are underspecified: The distribution of T (Eq. 2) is unusual (positive, heavy-tailed integers) and not well justified. There are no preservation guarantees (inner products, PMI structure, or rank correlations) analogous to JL-type bounds or hash-kernel analyses. The discussion acknowledges this gap but it remains a central weakness.
- Relationship to prior randomized feature mapping is underdeveloped: The method seems closely related to feature hashing / count-sketch with nonnegative updates, and to other nonnegative random projections. Positioning vs. these lines (e.g., hashing trick, count-min/count-sketch, nonnegative RP in clustering) is missing, as is analysis of collisions and bias on PMI estimates.
- PPMI on projected features needs more justification: Using PPMI on PoP’s compressed “pseudo-context” dimensions changes the semantics of PMI. What is the bias/variance trade-off relative to true co-occurrence PMI? How does positive-only projection affect independence assumptions? The empirical gains are promising, but the statistical interpretation deserves a deeper treatment.

Questions to authors
- How does PoP relate to feature hashing and count-sketch? If we view each nonzero as a hash bucket increment, PoP resembles multi-hash positive updates. Can you compare empirically and conceptually (e.g., with and without sign hashing)?
- Please provide concrete efficiency metrics: wall-clock time, memory footprint, and throughput for building spaces and computing PPMI, compared to (i) dense count+PPMI, (ii) PPMI+SVD, (iii) word2vec/SGNS and GloVe on UKWaC.
- Clarify Eq. 2: What exactly is the distribution of nonzero magnitudes (b1/U^αc)? In experiments, do you actually use integer magnitudes >1, or simply 1s with a given sparsity? How sensitive are results to α and to using constant 1s?
- PPMI over projected counts: Can you quantify the approximation error of PPMI computed in the compressed space versus true PPMI on the original co-occurrence matrix (e.g., correlation between scores or nearest neighbors)?
- Beyond MEN: Can you report results on at least one more intrinsic dataset (e.g., SimLex-999) and one extrinsic task? Even small-scale runs would help assess generality.
- Similarity computation at scale: τb is O(m log m) per pair. For large-vocab retrieval, is τb practical, or do you rely on Pearson/cosine post-PPMI? Any indexing tricks?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core idea—positive-only random projections that preserve nonnegativity to enable PPMI at low dimension—is novel, bold, and practically promising. Despite limited evaluation and missing theory, the paper opens a compelling direction that could impact how we build efficient distributional models. Strengthening empirical scope and providing efficiency numbers would elevate the work, but the conceptual contribution merits acceptance.
