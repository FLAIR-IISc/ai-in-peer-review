Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a randomized, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces. Unlike standard Random Indexing (RI), PoP uses sparse, nonnegative random projection vectors, which makes the resulting projected co-occurrence matrix suitable for downstream weighting with PPMI. The authors also suggest using Kendall’s τb for similarity in the unweighted PoP space and Pearson correlation after PPMI, arguing that PPMI approximately Gaussianizes the vector distributions. Empirically, on MEN (trained on UKWaC), unweighted PoP outperforms RI and basic count-based models, and PoP+PPMI achieves Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75 with much lower computational cost than dense PPMI or neural embeddings. The paper presents a parameter study varying dimensionality, sparsity, and similarity measure.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A practical random-projection scheme using sparse, nonnegative index vectors that enables applying PPMI directly in the reduced space, yielding competitive performance with strong efficiency benefits.
2) An empirical evaluation on MEN showing PoP > RI / counts, and PoP+PPMI ≈ dense PPMI, with a systematic sensitivity analysis over projection dimensionality and sparsity.
3) A proposal to use Kendall’s τb for similarities in non-Gaussian projected spaces and Pearson after PPMI transforms.

Strengths
1) Practicality and efficiency: PoP is simple, incremental, and avoids costly factorization or neural training. The method aligns well with streaming/online settings. The argument that PPMI computed in a low-dimensional space is much faster than on the original high-dimensional counts is well motivated.
2) Empirical gains over RI and counts: On MEN, unweighted PoP consistently outperforms both RI and raw count-based models across window sizes, indicating that the positive-only projection is more discriminative in practice than RI for this task.
3) Competitive accuracy with PoP+PPMI: The reported ρ ≈ 0.75 on MEN (and up to ~0.787 with tuning) is in the ballpark of strong baselines, supporting the claim that the approach achieves good accuracy with substantially lower computational overhead.
4) Parameter exploration and variance reporting: The study of dimensionality (m), sparsity (number of nonzeros), and choice of similarity measure is thorough for MEN. Reporting mean, min, and max over 10 runs helps quantify the stochastic variability and shows that variance decreases with larger m.
5) Clear rationale for similarity measures: The discussion connecting distributional assumptions to Kendall’s τb (nonparametric) and Pearson’s r (after PPMI) is coherent, and the experiments corroborate the expected behavior.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to known sketching/hashing methods: PoP is very close in spirit to Count-Min Sketch (CMS) and feature hashing with multiple hash functions: sparse, nonnegative, incremental projections of co-occurrence counts into a smaller space. The paper does not cite or compare to CMS or hashing, nor clarify what is fundamentally new beyond using occasional heavy-tailed positive weights. This omission weakens the novelty claim.
2) Limited evaluation: Results are confined to a single relatedness benchmark (MEN) and a single corpus (UKWaC), with no significance tests, no cross-task validation (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim, RW), and no controlled comparisons to SGNS/GloVe trained on the same data. Efficiency claims are not backed by wall-clock timings or memory profiles. This restricts the strength and generality of the empirical evidence.
3) Technical soundness gaps and unsubstantiated claims: 
   - The statement that PPMI is “undefined” for RI because row/column sums are always zero is inaccurate; rather, RI yields signed values that do not correspond to valid joint probabilities. Alternatives (e.g., applying PPMI pre-projection, shifting, or rectification) are not explored as baselines.
   - Computing PMI/PPMI on projected counts lacks probabilistic justification: after projection, entries are mixtures of multiple original events due to collisions, so standard PMI semantics are distorted. The paper does not analyze bias/variance introduced by this approximation.
   - The choice of the heavy-tailed weight distribution (floor(1/U^α), α=0.5) is ad hoc, and its effect vs. simpler {0,1} weighting is not evaluated. No theoretical guarantees (JL-type bounds, sketch error bounds) are provided.
4) Reproducibility and detail: Several important implementation details are missing or underspecified (e.g., vocabulary selection, frequency cutoffs, stopword handling for the main results, handling OOV pairs, exact τb computation protocol, initialization to ensure at least one nonzero per row given very small s). Code is promised only upon acceptance.
5) Clarity and presentation: The exposition has formatting issues and some confusing statements (e.g., initial definition of τb using adjacent pairs; the “sufficiently large s guarantees nonzero column sums” claim). Theoretical discussion is largely speculative, and related work coverage omits key connections to sketching.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
1) How does PoP formally differ from Count-Min Sketch or multi-hash feature hashing applied to the co-occurrence matrix? Can you position PoP relative to these methods and explain the empirical or theoretical advantages of your positive heavy-tailed coefficients?
2) Please clarify the PPMI vs RI claim. Would baselines such as (a) PPMI computed on the original counts followed by RI, (b) rectified/shifted RI enabling PMI-like weighting, or (c) PPMI before projection be feasible and fair? Can you add these comparisons?
3) Provide end-to-end runtime and memory usage for building dense PPMI, PoP, and RI models on UKWaC, including PPMI computation, and report wall-clock speedups.
4) How sensitive are results to the exact distribution in Eq. (2)? What happens if all nonzero entries are 1 (i.e., pure CMS-like projection)? Please report MEN scores for that variant.
5) Can you add evaluations on additional benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW) and include statistical significance tests? Also, compare to SGNS/GloVe trained on the same UKWaC preprocessing.

Overall
The paper presents a practical idea with promising efficiency and competitive accuracy on MEN, and the parameter study is useful. However, the novelty is unclear given close ties to established sketching methods, the empirical validation is narrow, and some claims require correction or stronger justification. Strengthening related work, adding broader and fairer evaluations with runtime/memory reporting, and clarifying the probabilistic status of PPMI on projected counts would substantially improve the submission.
