Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection scheme whose projection matrix has only non-negative integer entries. The goal is to build low-dimensional, incremental semantic spaces that (unlike RI) can be further weighted with PPMI, and to use Kendall’s τb (and after PPMI, Pearson r) to compute similarities.
- Empirically, PoP reportedly outperforms RI and raw count-based models on MEN; after applying PPMI and using Pearson r, it reaches ~0.75 Spearman on MEN (claimed comparable to neural embeddings), with substantial efficiency gains since weighting is applied in low dimension.
- The paper provides exploratory analyses of PoP hyperparameters (m, s, number of non-zeros) and argues PoP’s distributions can be made more Gaussian-like post-PPMI, motivating Pearson correlation.

Strengths
- Simple, incremental construction: The method reuses the attractive simplicity and online nature of random indexing, while constraining projections to be nonnegative. This makes implementation straightforward and potentially efficient.
- Empirical improvements over RI on MEN: The experiments show consistent gains of PoP over RI and raw count-based baselines, particularly at smaller windows. The sensitivity analyses (Figures 4–6) are useful in understanding the impact of m and sparsity.
- Clear intuition on distributions: The discussion linking non-Gaussian projected vectors, Kendall’s τb, and PPMI-induced Gaussian-like distributions is interesting and, while informal, thought-provoking.
- Practical angle: The idea of performing weighting (PPMI) in a low-dimensional space for efficiency is appealing, and the reported numbers suggest promising accuracy/efficiency trade-offs.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and insufficiently positioned. Positive-only/sparse random projections and feature hashing with {0,1} or {±1} are well-studied. Nonnegative random projections exist in the JL literature; random indexing variants with nonnegative index vectors and post-hoc weighting have been explored. The paper does not clearly differentiate PoP from prior nonnegative/sparse projections or feature hashing, nor does it cite closely related work on nonnegative JL transforms. As presented, PoP looks like binary/sparse RI with nonnegative entries and a post-projection weighting heuristic.
- Equation (2) is unclear or incorrect. The definition tij = floor(U^α) with U∈(0,1], α=0.5 yields 0 almost surely; if ceiling is intended, it is almost always 1, collapsing values to a binary scheme. The notation “1Uα c” is unreadable; please define precisely (distribution, support, expectation, variance). Also, the claim “extremely small s, e.g., s=0.01” is contradictory: 0.01 is not extremely small, and the probability of zero rows is not addressed rigorously (should either enforce at least one nonzero per row or give an explicit bound).
- PPMI after projection is conceptually problematic. PMI is defined on true joint and marginal probabilities of original events (word, context). Computing PPMI on mixed/projection-space coordinates (which are random linear combinations of contexts) loses its information-theoretic meaning; the “PPMI” is now with respect to artificial projected dimensions, not actual contexts. The manuscript does not acknowledge or analyze the bias this introduces. The comparison to count-based PPMI is therefore not apples-to-apples. If the core selling point is “PPMI in low-dim space,” a careful discussion of what this quantity estimates, and when it helps vs harms, is required.
- Claims about RI and PPMI are inaccurate. The paper states that sums of rows/columns in RI are “always 0,” hence PPMI is undefined. In RI, entries are typically ±1/0 and accumulated counts; row/column sums are not identically zero (they are centered in expectation). The real issue is the presence of negative values (and zeros) in the projected counts, not a zero sum identity. One can also apply weighting before projection with RI, which the paper overlooks when arguing efficiency advantages.
- Evaluation is too narrow and not comparable. Only MEN is used, on 751 lemmas; no analogy, SimLex, WordSim, or downstream tasks; no significance tests; and no strong baselines trained under identical conditions (e.g., SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD) on the same corpus with matched preprocessing/hyperparameters. The efficiency claims lack concrete runtime/memory numbers, corpus-scale experiments, or hardware specs. It appears the model is built only for MEN lemmas, which trivially reduces cost and does not reflect realistic vocabulary-scale scenarios.
- Theoretical gaps and unsupported statements. The paper admits the lack of a mathematical account (δ bounds, distributional properties) and leans on analogies with JL/RI. Several claims (e.g., variance ~ 1/m, and “PoP largely preserves long tails”) need derivation or empirical backing beyond a single histogram. The Kendall vs Pearson rationale is more heuristic than principled.
- Methodological details are missing. How are zero entries handled in log/PPMI (smoothing)? Precise definition of s vs number of nonzeros? How is “at least one nonzero per row” enforced? How are context windows defined and tokenization/lemmatization performed? Seeds and variance across runs? For Figures: axes units, exact hyperparameters, and confidence intervals are not clearly specified.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues. Misuse of symbols (η-norm vs ℓp; “E=(0)”), inconsistent notation (R vs T), grammar (“As put by, …”), footnote artifacts, missing operators/spaces (x n), and unclear captions impede readability. The discussion of cosine vs Pearson is imprecise (cosine equals correlation only after mean-centering or with zero-mean components and appropriate normalization).

Questions to authors
- Please precisely define the distribution in Equation (2): is tij ∈ {0,1} with Bernoulli(s)? Are weights >1 ever produced? If so, how? What does “1Uα c” mean? Provide explicit pseudocode for sampling T and for ensuring at least one nonzero per row.
- What is the statistical meaning of PPMI computed in the projected space? Can you characterize the bias with respect to true PMI? Under what conditions does post-projection PPMI improve Spearman, and why?
- Can you report comprehensive baselines on MEN trained under identical settings (same corpus, preprocessing), including SGNS/GloVe and PPMI+SVD, and add additional datasets (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words) and at least one downstream task?
- Please provide concrete efficiency results (wall-clock, memory) for building a full vocabulary model (e.g., 100k words), for count-PPMI+SVD vs PoP(+PPMI), with hardware specs.
- How do you handle zeros in Equation (3) (log 0) and negative values (if any) after accumulation? Any smoothing?
- Why is Kendall’s τb preferable to cosine on unweighted PoP empirically? Can you include a head-to-head comparison across measures?
- Can you position PoP with respect to feature hashing and existing nonnegative/sparse JL schemes, and cite relevant work? What, concretely, is new beyond using nonnegative projections and applying PPMI post-projection?

Overall recommendation
While the idea is simple and the preliminary results are encouraging, the paper in its current form suffers from unclear/mistaken technical definitions (Eq. 2), limited novelty relative to known nonnegative/sparse projections and feature hashing, a conceptually shaky “PPMI after projection” story, and a narrow, non-comparable evaluation without solid efficiency evidence. I recommend rejection and encourage a revised submission with corrected definitions, stronger positioning, rigorous experiments at scale, and a careful treatment of the probabilistic meaning of post-projection PPMI.
