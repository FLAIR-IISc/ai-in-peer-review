Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme where each context is mapped to an m-dimensional index vector with a few positive integer entries (and zeros otherwise). Word vectors are built incrementally by summing the index vectors of observed contexts. Because all projected counts are nonnegative, the resulting low-dimensional co-occurrence matrix can be weighted with PPMI, purportedly yielding predictive-quality embeddings efficiently. The paper argues for Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure for raw PoP (non-Gaussian), and Pearson’s r after PPMI (approximately Gaussian). Empirical evaluation is limited to MEN on UKWaC, with comparisons to raw counts and Random Indexing (RI).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, streaming-friendly variant of random projection for word co-occurrence that preserves nonnegativity (and has E[R]>0), enabling PPMI weighting in a compressed space.
2) An empirical study of similarity metrics (Kendall vs Pearson) and parameter effects (dimension m, sparsity s) for PoP.
3) Evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains MEN performance comparable to common embeddings while being computationally light.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The method is easy to implement, incremental, and potentially deployable in streaming settings. The positive-only sketch connects naturally to count-based weighting, and the experiments demonstrate fast construction without heavy optimization.
- Empirical improvements over RI and raw counts: On MEN, PoP (especially with PPMI) robustly outperforms RI and unweighted counts for small to moderate windows, and the parameter sweeps provide some insight into stability with m and sparsity.
- Sensible treatment of similarity measures: The observation that τb is more appropriate for non-Gaussian coordinates and that Pearson overtakes τb after PPMI is reasonable and borne out in the reported numbers.
- Parameter analysis: The exploration of m and the number of non-zeros per index vector, and their interaction with performance variance, is informative and practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning with respect to prior work: The core mechanism is extremely close to known sketching techniques (feature hashing/Count-Sketch and Count-Min Sketch) applied to co-occurrence matrices. Assigning each context a few positive bins is effectively a Count-Min-style sketch; the paper does not cite Cormode & Muthukrishnan (2005), Weinberger et al. (2009), or related hashing approaches for NLP co-occurrence compression. Similarly, nonnegative/randomized projections (e.g., Achlioptas “database-friendly” projections, very sparse projections, nonnegative variants) are not discussed. The claimed uniqueness of “positive-only projections enabling PPMI” is therefore overstated without a careful comparison to hashing-based co-occurrence compression followed by PPMI.
- Incorrect/unclear claims about RI and PPMI: The paper asserts that RI “cannot be adjusted and transformed using PPMI” because E[R]=0 so “row and column sums are always 0,” making PPMI undefined. In RI, projected entries are not identically zero; they can be negative, making PMI undefined without rectification, but sums are not mathematically guaranteed to be zero. The core obstacle is negativity, not zero sums. A fairer statement is that PPMI requires nonnegative counts (or a shift), which PoP enforces by construction, whereas RI would require ad-hoc rectification.
- Evaluation is narrow and lacks strong baselines: Results are reported only on MEN with UKWaC; there are no tests on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RareWords, analogies, or downstream tasks. There is no comparison to PPMI+SVD on full (or hashed) counts, nor to SGNS/GloVe trained on the same corpus with standardized settings, nor to hashing/sketch baselines followed by PPMI. The strongest reported number (0.78) appears post hoc with additional tuning and larger m, but there are no runtime/memory measurements or statistical tests.
- Theoretical grounding and distributional choices: The distribution tij = floor(1/U^α) for nonzeros is introduced without justification beyond α=0.5. The paper concedes the lack of theory; however, even basic bias/variance analysis of the sketch with respect to PMI estimates, collision rates, and distortion bounds (analogous to Count-Min/Count-Sketch analyses) is missing. The orthogonality argument in Fig. 6 is a proxy; a more direct collision/error analysis would be more convincing.
- Similarity definition issues: The text’s definition of Kendall’s τb appears to use adjacent pairs (j, j+1); standard τ uses all pairs (j, k), which is what Knight’s O(n log n) algorithm computes. Please correct the exposition. More broadly, using rank correlation across dimensions discards magnitude information and is unusual for distributional similarity; one should compare against cosine on PPMI and against Pearson/cosine on all methods.
- Methodological clarity/reproducibility: Important details are absent: preprocessing (stopwords, frequency cutoffs), vocabulary sizes, m choices per experiment, s calibration rules, and precise runtime/memory. Claims of “seconds or minutes” need evidence at scale (e.g., 100k–1M vocab).

Questions to Authors
- Relation to sketching baselines: How does PoP differ theoretically and empirically from Count-Min Sketch or Count-Sketch applied to the word–context matrix followed by PPMI? Please provide comparisons and discuss error guarantees known for those sketches.
- On RI and PPMI: Do you agree that the real issue is negativity rather than row/column sums being zero? Could simple rectifications (e.g., shifting, absolute value, clipping negatives) enable PPMI on RI, and how would that compare to PoP?
- Choice of nonzero distribution: Why floor(1/U^α) with α=0.5, as opposed to binary 1’s or other nonnegative distributions? Can you show an ablation demonstrating the value of heavy-tailed positive integers?
- Similarity measures: Please clarify the definition of τb you use and confirm your implementation considers all pairs. Also report cosine on PPMI spaces; many works use cosine with PPMI successfully.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: Can you provide results on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, and at least one downstream task, plus baselines with PPMI+SVD, SGNS, and GloVe trained on UKWaC under comparable settings? Include hashing/sketching+PPMI baselines.
- Efficiency claims: Please report wall-clock times, memory footprint, and scaling with vocabulary and corpus size, comparing PoP+PPMI to full PPMI and to SGNS/GloVe on the same hardware.
- Theoretical analysis: Can you connect PoP to known distortion/error bounds (e.g., via Count-Min analysis) and characterize the bias introduced in PMI estimates due to collisions in the projected space?

Overall, the idea is practical and potentially useful, but the novelty over established sketching/hashing approaches is unclear, some core claims are inaccurate, and the evaluation is too limited to support the paper’s conclusions. Strengthening the positioning, baselines, and empirical breadth would substantially improve the submission.
