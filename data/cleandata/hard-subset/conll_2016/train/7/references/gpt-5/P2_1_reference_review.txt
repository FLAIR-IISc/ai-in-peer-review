Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme where projection matrix entries are non-negative integers. This aims to: (i) enable incremental, low-dimensional construction of distributional semantic spaces; and crucially (ii) allow application of weighting schemes such as PPMI after projection (arguing previous RI-like methods with zero-mean projections cannot).
- It recommends Kendall’s τb as the similarity measure on non-Gaussian projected vectors; after PPMI (which makes distributions more Gaussian-like), Pearson correlation is preferred.
- Empirical results on MEN (UKWaC) suggest PoP outperforms RI and raw counts; PoP+PPMI+Pearson approaches strong baselines (≈0.75–0.78 Spearman) with much lower computational cost, and includes a parameter study over dimension m and sparsity s.

Strengths
- Practical idea with an appealing trade-off: Project first to very low dimensions, then apply PPMI quickly. If validated at scale, this directly addresses a real bottleneck in count-based models (PPMI on huge, sparse matrices).
- Clear empirical exploration of key parameters (dimension m, number of non-zero entries s), including variability across random seeds. The plots illustrate sensible behavior (variance shrinks with m; sensitivity to s).
- Thoughtful discussion connecting distributional properties to similarity metrics: using Kendall’s τb for non-Gaussian projected vectors, switching to Pearson after PPMI’s variance-stabilization/log transform. This is a useful perspective often glossed over.
- Incremental construction and simplicity: no training, just random vectors and summations. This could be attractive for rapid prototyping and streaming scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and missing baselines. All quantitative claims rest on a single relatedness dataset (MEN) and on a small target set (751 lemmas). No tests on harder similarity (e.g., SimLex-999), rare words, analogy, or downstream tasks; no results on broader vocabularies. Strong modern baselines (SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with best practices like CDS and subsampling) trained on the same corpus are not systematically reported. This makes it hard to assess generality and significance.
- Efficiency claims are not substantiated empirically. The paper asserts large speedups but provides no wall-clock timings, memory usage, or scaling with vocabulary/context size. Moreover, similarity via Kendall’s τb is O(m log m) per pair—acceptable for m≈10^3, but the cost for large-vocabulary retrieval (many pairwise computations) is not discussed. Without time/memory numbers vs. count+PPMI and SGNS, the “seconds/minutes” claim remains anecdotal.
- Conceptual justification of PPMI on projected dimensions is weak. PPMI is defined over word–context co-occurrences; after PoP, columns correspond to random features without interpretable contexts, so the probabilistic semantics of PPMI marginals is unclear. The manuscript motivates PPMI primarily as a variance-stabilizing transform rather than as MI estimation. That may be fine pragmatically, but it calls for ablations: compare (a) PPMI on counts then project, (b) project then PPMI (proposed), and (c) other monotone/nonlinear transforms (e.g., log, Box-Cox) on projected vectors without invoking PPMI. Without such comparisons, it is unclear whether “PPMI” is the key ingredient or simply “a log-like transform.”
- Novelty relative to prior random projection/RI variants is not rigorously positioned. There is a substantial literature on sparse random projections (Achlioptas), nonnegative random features, binary/ternary projections, and RI variants that interact with weighting. The claim that E(R)=0 prevents weighting is overstated; many pipelines weight before projection, or use shift/scale. The paper should clarify distinctions to nonnegative random projections and hashed/random feature maps, and discuss whether PoP satisfies any JL-type guarantees or preserves particular norms/inner products.
- Lack of theoretical footing. While a full analysis is ambitious, the method would benefit from at least a formalization of expected scaling and bias/variance of projected counts and their marginals, and a discussion of when the projected PPMI approximates a function of the true PPMI. Currently even basic properties (e.g., normalization, expected magnitude dispersion across contexts, collision effects) are not analyzed.
- Clarity/precision issues. The distribution in Eq. (2) (tij = floor(1/U^α) with small activation prob s) is unusual; the rationale for heavy-tailed magnitudes and the floor operation is not justified, nor is any normalization discussed. Some notation conflates T/R, footnotes and references are occasionally tangled, and details necessary for reproduction (random seeds, exact preprocessing, vocabulary sizes) are sparse. Code “after acceptance” is insufficient for reproducibility at review time.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide wall-clock time and peak memory comparisons for constructing models and computing similarities (end-to-end), against: (i) count+PPMI(+SVD), and (ii) SGNS/GloVe, on the same corpus and comparable vocabulary sizes?
- What is the probabilistic interpretation of PPMI computed on projected columns? Do you view it as MI on a different sample space, or simply as a power/log transform? Can you add ablations with: log-only on PoP, and PPMI-before-projection (project PPMI-weighted counts to m dims)?
- How does PoP perform on at least two additional standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words) and one downstream task? Please include tuned SGNS/GloVe and PPMI+SVD baselines trained on UKWaC.
- How are index vectors normalized (if at all)? Why use heavy-tailed magnitudes instead of binary/ternary nonnegative entries? What breaks if tij ∈ {0,1} only?
- Can you release code and exact preprocessing details now to enable verification?

Overall assessment
The idea is simple and potentially useful: project first with a nonnegative sparse map to enable fast post-hoc weighting, yielding competitive relatedness performance with negligible training. However, the current evidence is too limited to support strong claims of significance. The evaluation is narrow (one dataset, small target set), efficiency is not measured, conceptual grounding of “PPMI-after-projection” is underdeveloped, and positioning w.r.t. prior random projection literature is incomplete. I recommend rejection in its current form, with a path to a strong resubmission if the authors (i) broaden and strengthen experiments with rigorous baselines and efficiency metrics, (ii) clarify the role/interpretation of PPMI on projected features with ablations, and (iii) tighten novelty claims and method specification.
