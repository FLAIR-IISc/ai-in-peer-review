Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random projection method whose entries are nonnegative integers. Unlike standard random indexing (RI) with zero-mean projections, PoP preserves nonnegativity and strictly positive marginals, which the authors argue enables post-hoc weighting such as PPMI on the compressed co-occurrence matrix.
- It suggests using Kendall’s τb for similarity in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and Pearson/cosine after PPMI (approximately Gaussianized).
- Empirical evaluation on MEN (UKWaC corpus) shows: (i) PoP outperforms RI and raw count-based models; (ii) PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75 Spearman, comparable to strong count-based/NN baselines reported elsewhere, with claimed large efficiency gains; (iii) ablations on dimensionality m and sparsity s.

My view of the main contributions (in order)
1) A practical sketching/compression scheme for co-occurrence matrices based on nonnegative sparse random projections that supports downstream PPMI weighting and incremental construction.
2) Empirical evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson is competitive on MEN while being simple and fast, along with a basic parameter study (m, s) and similarity-metric choices.
3) A qualitative discussion connecting distributions before/after PPMI and choice of correlation measures.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: PoP is a very simple sketch of the co-occurrence matrix that can be constructed online and in small memory. If it truly permits PPMI computation in compressed space with good accuracy, this is useful for low-resource or streaming settings.
- Clear empirical advantage over RI: The paper convincingly shows that “vanilla” RI does not improve semantic relatedness vs count-based baselines, whereas PoP does, largely because it supports post-projection weighting.
- Parameter exploration: The analysis of m and s, the impact on variance across runs, and the interplay with Kendall vs Pearson is helpful and shows the authors understand their method’s behavior, including non-orthogonality and collision effects when s grows.
- Sensible treatment of similarity: The switch from a rank-based measure (Kendall) in a non-Gaussian regime to a parametric measure (Pearson) after variance-stabilizing transforms is thoughtful and justified by observed distributions.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning: The proposed projection is extremely close in spirit to known sketching methods such as the hashing trick/feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch (CMS), which also use nonnegative updates and collisions to compress high-dimensional co-occurrence matrices and support downstream weighting. The paper neither connects to nor differentiates from this literature (e.g., Weinberger et al., 2009; Cormode & Muthukrishnan, 2005; Li et al. on sparse/nonnegative projections). Without this comparison, the conceptual novelty is unclear.
- Theoretical grounding: The paper explicitly lacks any theoretical analysis (distortion guarantees, bias/variance of marginals, or connection to JL-type results). For a new projection family that is intended to be used with information-theoretic weighting, at least bounds or references to closely related analyses (e.g., CMS error bounds for marginals) are essential. The claim σ²_δ ≈ 1/m is asserted but not derived for this T.
- Questionable PMI semantics post-projection: PMI is defined over true joint/marginal probabilities of words and interpretable contexts. Applying PPMI on random projected dimensions treats random linear combinations of contexts as if they were contexts. This can work as an approximation, but the paper neither justifies that PoP yields unbiased or low-bias estimates of the required marginals nor compares PoP+PPMI weights to true PPMI weights (e.g., correlation between dense PPMI and PoP+PPMI vectors). The argument that RI cannot be PPMI-weighted because E(R)=0 is not the core issue; the main issue is loss of interpretability and sign. A comparison with simple rectified RI (e.g., absolute values or offsets) or CMS would strengthen the claim that PoP is uniquely enabling.
- Limited evaluation: Only MEN is reported. Modern practice evaluates on multiple lexical benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words), and ideally downstream tasks. Moreover, no statistical significance tests are provided, and comparisons to strong neural baselines are indirect (citations rather than a controlled re-run). Claims of computational efficiency are not backed by actual runtime/memory measurements vs dense PPMI, SVD-truncated PPMI, SGNS/GloVe for the same corpus and hardware.
- Clarity and rigor: There are notational inconsistencies (R vs T, “E(R)” without explicit expectation definition), missing algorithmic details (sampling α=0.5 motivation; exact distribution of nonzero entries; handling ties in τb under many zeros), and several editorial issues (footnotes in text, figure references). Reproducibility is hindered by “if accepted, we will release code” and absent hyperparameter specifics or preprocessing details (e.g., vocabulary thresholds, window weighting), especially since later “fine-tuning” contradicts the earlier “no preprocessing” stance.

Questions to authors
- Relationship to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch: In what ways does PoP differ theoretically and practically from CMS or hashing-based compressed co-occurrence matrices? Why not use CMS with conservative updates and then compute PPMI on the sketch? Please cite and compare empirically (accuracy vs space).
- PMI after projection: What is the bias of PoP estimates of joint and marginal counts relative to the true counts? Can you report the correlation between true dense PPMI vectors and PoP+PPMI vectors for a shared subset of words? Any bounds or empirical error distributions?
- Efficiency claims: Please provide wall-clock time and peak memory for building (i) dense counts + PPMI; (ii) dense + PPMI + SVD; (iii) RI; (iv) PoP; (v) SGNS and GloVe (same corpus, vocab, hardware). Include the effect of m and s and report throughput (tokens/sec).
- RI+nonlinearity: If the core obstacle is negative/zero-mean projections, did you try a simple nonlinearity or offset on RI (e.g., abs or shift so entries > 0) before PPMI? How does that compare to PoP?
- Robustness: Please add evaluations on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW, and an analogy task or a downstream task to demonstrate generality. Also, include significance testing across 10 runs.

Assessment against common reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderately low as written; conceptually overlaps with established sketching/hashing methods. Needs clearer differentiation and citations.
- Soundness/technical quality: Mixed. The algorithm is plausible and works empirically, but lacks theoretical justification for key claims (especially PMI post-projection). Some statements about RI and PPMI are oversimplified.
- Clarity: Fair but needs substantial polishing, clearer notation, algorithm description, and better figure integration.
- Significance: Potentially useful in practice due to simplicity and speed, but impact is limited by narrow evaluation and missing comparisons.
- Reproducibility: Currently weak. Public datasets help, but code/data and full implementation details are needed; report actual compute metrics.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is appealing and seems to work on MEN, but the paper needs (i) stronger positioning vs hashing/CMS, (ii) theoretical or at least empirical validation that PPMI on PoP approximates the intended quantities, (iii) broader and more rigorous evaluation with compute measurements, and (iv) improved clarity and reproducibility. With these addressed, the contribution could be solid and practical.
