Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a randomized, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces. PoP replaces the usual ±1/0 random index vectors with sparse, strictly nonnegative integer index vectors so that the projected matrix (P = C×T) remains amenable to weighting schemes such as PPMI. The authors argue that: (i) PoP by itself outperforms classical Random Indexing (RI) on MEN; (ii) PoP followed by PPMI (“PoP+PPMI”) allows fast weighting in low dimensions and achieves performance comparable to some neural baselines, without expensive training; and (iii) after PPMI, Pearson correlation is superior to Kendall’s τb, while τb is better in the pre-weighted PoP space. They provide ablations over projection dimension m and sparsity s.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, incremental, positive-only random projection scheme that enables PPMI-style weighting in the projected space, with empirical gains over RI on MEN.
2) An empirical study of similarity measures in PoP spaces (Kendall vs Pearson/cosine), and of key projection parameters (m, s).
3) A practical claim of efficiency: competitive MEN performance at low dimensionality and without neural training.

Strengths
- Practical simplicity and incremental construction: The method is easy to implement (C×T with sparse T) and naturally supports streaming updates, like RI. The ability to perform weighting in the small projected space is attractive for practitioners with limited compute.
- Empirical improvements over RI: The paper shows consistent gains of PoP over RI and raw count vectors on MEN across window sizes, and provides useful parameter sweeps (m, s) and stability analyses across random seeds.
- Compatibility with PPMI and speed argument: Moving the PPMI computation to the projected space is a good idea in principle, as it dramatically reduces the cost of computing and storing PMI/PPMI on large vocabularies and windows. The intuition that nonnegativity facilitates information-theoretic weighting is sound.
- Sensible discussion of similarity measures: The observation that Kendall’s τb can help in non-Gaussian pre-weighted spaces and that Pearson improves after PPMI (variance-stabilizing/log effect) is reasonable and supported empirically.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior “sketching” is unclear: PoP is very close to feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch (CMS) applied to word-context co-occurrence matrices, followed by weighting in the sketch space. The paper does not discuss or compare to hashing/CMS literature (e.g., Weinberger et al., feature hashing; Cormode & Muthukrishnan, Count-Min; Count/CountSketch for approximate matrix multiplication; recent sketching surveys). The core idea—compress counts via nonnegative randomized aggregation, then compute PMI/PPMI on the sketch—is known in large-scale NLP systems. The distinction from RI is clear, but the broader novelty claim is underspecified.
- Theoretical/formal issues and a critical typo: Equation (2) appears incorrect/ambiguous: with U∈(0,1], α=0.5, floor(U^α) is 0 almost surely, yielding degenerate vectors. If the intent was, e.g., ceil(U^{-α}) or a Zipf-like heavy-tailed positive distribution, it must be fixed and justified. More broadly, the paper concedes lacking theoretical properties (distortion bounds, bias introduced by collisions) and provides only heuristics; however, without clarifying the actual sampling distribution, even empirical reproducibility is at risk.
- Claims about RI and PPMI are inaccurate: The stated reason RI “cannot be PPMI-weighted” because row/column sums are zero confuses expectation of R with realized projected counts. The real issue is that RI produces signed projections that are not valid co-occurrence counts (negative entries), making PMI ill-defined. Please correct this argument.
- Limited and outdated evaluation: MEN only is insufficient in 2025. At minimum, include SimLex-999, WordSim-353, SimVerb-3500, Rare-Word, SCWS, and analogies (BATS/Google), with statistical significance across seeds. Crucially, compare against strong baselines on the same corpus and preprocessing: SGNS/fastText, GloVe, and high-quality PPMI+SVD with best practices (context weighting, subsampling, smoothing; Levy et al., 2015). The claim that neural training takes months is outdated for UKWaC-scale corpora. No timing/memory numbers are reported to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Ambiguity about PPMI on “pseudo-contexts”: Computing PMI against projected bins (columns of P = C×T) is not the same as PMI on original contexts; it is PMI with respect to hashed/sketched features. The paper should analyze the bias/variance introduced by collisions, whether denominators approximate the true marginals, and how this relates to CMS analyses. The current justification is purely empirical.
- Similarity choice: Using Kendall’s τb over vector coordinates is unusual in distributional semantics and may discard magnitude information important for similarity. The motivation linking cosine/Pearson via zero-mean arguments is somewhat loose (cosine equals Pearson on centered vectors), and could be clarified.

Questions to the authors
- Please fix and clarify Eq. (2). What exactly is the sampling distribution of nonzero entries (floor vs ceil, power vs reciprocal)? How sensitive are results to α?
- How does PoP differ from feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch applied to C? Is PoP+PPMI equivalent to computing PPMI on a CMS approximation to C? Please situate the work in the sketching literature and include direct comparisons (both performance and error bounds where possible).
- Can you report wall-clock time and memory for building RI, count-based+PPMI(+SVD), SGNS, and PoP(+PPMI) on UKWaC (same hardware), as well as index-vector construction cost?
- How do you maintain PPMI in an incremental setting? Do you recompute global normalizers as the corpus grows? Is there an online approximation?
- Could you provide results beyond MEN (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare-Word, SimVerb, SCWS, analogies) and comparisons to SGNS/GloVe/PPMI+SVD with strong hyperparameters on the same corpus? Please include multiple seeds and significance tests.
- Your statement about RI’s inability to use PPMI seems incorrect; the problem is negativity, not zero sums. Please revise and, if desired, compare against a nonnegative sketch baseline.
- What is the effect of using cosine in PoP(+PPMI) vs Pearson/τb? A small ablation would help.
- Can you provide an analysis of collision rates vs s,m matched to observed performance, and guidance on choosing s given the sparsity of C?
