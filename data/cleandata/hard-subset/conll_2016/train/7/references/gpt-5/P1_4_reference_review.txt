Summary and main contributions
- This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly non-negative integer entries that enables incremental construction of low-dimensional word-context representations. Because projected entries are non-negative, the authors apply PPMI weighting directly in the reduced space, arguing this combines the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI.
- The paper explores similarity measures suited to PoP: Kendall’s τb for unweighted PoP (non-Gaussian coordinates) and Pearson’s r after PPMI (postulated Gaussianization). It reports empirical results on the MEN relatedness benchmark using UKWaC showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson performance around ρ≈0.75, comparable to some reported neural embeddings, with faster construction.
- The paper provides a set of empirical studies on PoP’s parameters (dimension m, sparsity s/number of nonzeros), qualitative distributional analyses, and claims about incremental construction and practical efficiency.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: PoP is conceptually simple (random sparse non-negative index vectors plus additive updates) and incremental. This is attractive for streaming/online settings or constrained environments where building full count matrices or training neural models is impractical. The method directly constructs reduced representations, potentially saving memory and time.
- Compatibility with PPMI at low dimension: The key practical insight is to allow association weighting after projection. Computing PPMI on an m-dimensional projected matrix is vastly cheaper than on high-dimensional count matrices. Empirically, PoP+PPMI+Pearson is competitive with strong count-based baselines while avoiding SVD.
- Empirical improvements over RI and raw counts: Across window sizes, PoP (with Kendall) outperforms RI and unweighted counts, especially with small windows, consistent with the claim that PoP is more discriminative than standard RI at the same dimensionality.
- Sensible discussion of similarity measures: The paper articulates when Kendall’s τb or Pearson’s r are appropriate (pre- vs post-PPMI), supported by histogram visualizations suggesting Gaussianization after log/PPMI transforms.
- Parameter exploration: The study of how m and the number of nonzeros affect performance and orthogonality (collision probability) is useful to practitioners and offers guidance (e.g., 1–2 nonzeros if followed by PPMI; more nonzeros risk non-orthogonality and distortions).

Weaknesses
- Limited and unbalanced evaluation: Results are reported on a single intrinsic benchmark (MEN) and a single corpus (UKWaC), with no downstream tasks, no statistical significance testing, and no apples-to-apples comparisons with strong baselines trained on the same data (e.g., SGNS/Skip-gram, GloVe, or PPMI+SVD with tuned hyperparameters). The neural comparisons are indirect (citing scores from other papers). Also, there are no runtime/memory benchmarks to substantiate the key efficiency claims (seconds/minutes vs hours/days).
- Questionable claims and missing baselines around “PPMI on projected features”: While PoP enables non-negative projected “pseudo-counts,” the semantics of PMI/PPMI on randomly aggregated contexts are not discussed theoretically. In contrast, there is prior art on feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch for co-occurrence matrices; these also produce non-negative, low-dimensional aggregates that can in principle be reweighted. The paper does not cite or compare to CMS/feature hashing baselines, which threatens novelty and undermines the core claim that previous random projections cannot be PPMI-weighted. Moreover, the statement that RI’s row/column sums “are always 0,” making PPMI undefined, is inaccurate in finite samples; the deeper issue is that RI produces negative entries (not interpretable as counts). A fair comparison would include hashed non-negative projections.
- Lack of theory: The paper explicitly lacks any distortion or concentration bounds (JL-type or otherwise), even heuristic ones, for the proposed positive-only projection, nor an analysis of bias introduced in PMI estimates after projection. Given the method’s randomized nature and its reliance on post-hoc PPMI on projected counts, some justification is needed (e.g., recovery guarantees, error bounds as a function of m and s, or connections to known sketching results).
- Fairness of similarity choices: Baselines are evaluated mostly with cosine, while PoP is allowed to pick τb or Pearson, which can advantage PoP. It is not shown whether baselines (e.g., PPMI-dense) also improve with Pearson or Kendall, nor whether count-based vectors with appropriate transforms benefit similarly. Reporting numbers where all methods use their best similarity is good, but then it should be done consistently for all.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Key implementation details are missing or ad hoc (e.g., exact distribution t_ij = ceil(U^α), choice α=0.5; how s is set to ensure non-zero columns; preprocessing; stopping criteria). Figures lack precise numbers; no tables; no standard deviations; code is promised only upon acceptance. The exposition intermixes footnotes and long asides that impede readability.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide direct, controlled comparisons on UKWaC with (i) SGNS/Skip-gram and GloVe at similar dimensionalities and training budgets, (ii) PPMI+SVD baselines with typical scaling (e.g., shifted PPMI), and (iii) non-negative sketching/feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch baselines, both with and without PPMI?
- Please report wall-clock time and peak memory usage for all methods (count-based+PPMI, PoP, PoP+PPMI, RI, PPMI+SVD) for the MEN vocabulary on UKWaC, including construction and weighting phases, and sensitivity to m and number of nonzeros.
- What is the formal relationship between PoP and known sketching schemes (feature hashing, Count-Min Sketch)? Is PoP essentially a CMS with a particular bucket-weight distribution? Can you provide any bias/variance analysis or recovery bounds for PMI after projection?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of α in ceil(U^α) and to the exact distribution of non-zero weights? Why α=0.5? Would binary {0,1} indices perform similarly?
- Please quantify the benefit of Pearson vs cosine after PPMI across all baselines, and of Kendall vs cosine in unweighted settings, to ensure fairness.
- Beyond MEN, how does PoP perform on other similarity/relatedness datasets (WordSim353, SimLex-999), analogy tasks, or downstream tasks (e.g., text classification), and under standard preprocessing (stopword removal, frequency cutoffs)?

Overall evaluation
The idea of leveraging non-negative sparse projections to allow PPMI in low dimensions is practical and potentially useful. However, the current submission lacks rigorous evaluation against the most relevant baselines (especially non-negative sketching) and provides no theoretical guarantees, while some claims about RI/PPMI are shaky. Strengthening the empirical comparisons, adding runtime/memory evidence, clarifying connections to existing sketching literature, and tightening the exposition would substantially improve the paper.
