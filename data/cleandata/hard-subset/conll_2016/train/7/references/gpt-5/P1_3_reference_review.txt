Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme whose projection matrix has only nonnegative entries. Unlike Random Indexing (RI) and related zero-mean projections, PoP’s projected representations can be further reweighted using association measures such as PPMI. The authors argue that this enables building low-dimensional, incremental semantic spaces that are both fast to construct and amenable to effective weighting. They use Kendall’s τb for similarity in unweighted PoP (to accommodate non-Gaussian distributions) and switch to Pearson after log/PPMI transforms (which induce more Gaussian-like distributions). Experiments on MEN (UKWaC corpus) show PoP>RI and count-based; with PPMI, PoP reaches ~0.75 Spearman, comparable to some neural baselines, with far less compute. The paper also includes parameter sensitivity analyses over projection dimension and sparsity.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical random projection variant with nonnegative, sparse “index” vectors that enables PPMI (and similar weighting) to be computed in the projected, low-dimensional space.
2) Empirical evidence that PoP+PPMI yields competitive relatedness scores (MEN) at a fraction of the computational cost of dense count-based PPMI or neural embeddings, plus analyses of similarity metrics pre-/post-weighting.
3) Parameter studies showing how projection dimension and sparsity affect variance and performance, and a rationale for using τb vs Pearson depending on the distribution.

Strengths
- Simple, incremental, and compute-efficient: The method requires only generating sparse nonnegative index vectors and streaming updates. The claim that PPMI can be computed much faster at low dimensionality is compelling; the approach is easy to implement and deploy in resource-constrained scenarios.
- Clear, thoughtful treatment of similarity measures: The discussion connecting cosine/Pearson to zero-mean Gaussian projections and motivating τb for non-Gaussian projected spaces is insightful, and borne out by experiments (τb pre-PPMI, Pearson post-PPMI).
- Empirical gains over RI and raw counts: On MEN, PoP consistently outperforms RI and count-based models, especially for smaller context windows, supporting the central claim that positivity enables effective weighting.
- Sensitivity analyses: Systematic exploration of dimension m and sparsity s (number of nonzeros) helps users set parameters and understand trade-offs (variance reduction with larger m; risk of non-orthogonality as s grows).
- Honest discussion of theory gaps: The paper is transparent about lacking formal guarantees and positions PoP in the landscape of randomized methods, which is appropriate for a practically motivated contribution.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity and potential mistakes in the projection distribution (Equation 2): The definition tij ∈ {0, ⌈U^α⌉} with α=0.5 implies ⌈U^α⌉=1 almost surely, i.e., purely 0/1 index entries. If this is intended, please say so clearly; if not, the current form seems incorrect (and floor(·) would make matters worse). More generally, the distribution is under-specified (e.g., how to ensure at least one non-zero per row with very small s?). This needs to be clarified and aligned with the implementation used in experiments.
- PMI/PPMI semantics on projected dimensions: PPMI is defined over co-occurrence counts between targets and interpretable contexts. In PoP, P is p×m with m random feature dimensions; computing PPMI over these projected “contexts” may not approximate PPMI over the original contexts. The paper does not justify why Equation 3 applied to P yields a meaningful association measure, beyond empirical results. A derivation, bias analysis, or comparison to PPMI computed on the original counts (with/without sketching) is needed.
- Limited evaluation: Only MEN on a single corpus (UKWaC), with no tests on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words, analogies, or downstream tasks. No comparisons to strong baselines on the same data/settings (e.g., SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD), and no significance testing. The claim that SVD adds “negligible” gains (0.01–0.001) needs stronger empirical support and fair baselines at comparable dimensions.
- Missing runtime/memory evidence: The central efficiency claim would be much stronger with wall-clock time, peak memory, and throughput comparisons against (i) count+PPMI, (ii) count+PPMI+SVD, and (iii) SGNS, all on the same hardware and vocabulary. Incremental/streaming performance (updates per second, scalability with vocabulary/context size) would be valuable.
- Related work gaps: The method is closely related to sketching and feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch (CMS) with nonnegative projections, which are widely used to aggregate high-dimensional counts into low-dimensional spaces. Those connections (and differences) are not discussed or cited. Likewise, prior work on Achlioptas/sparse JL transforms, nonnegative random projections, and the SGNS≈PPMI+shift equivalence deserves more explicit positioning.

Questions to the authors
- Projection distribution: Please clarify Equation 2 precisely. Are index entries Bernoulli(s) with value 1? If not, what is the exact distribution, and how is “at least one nonzero per row” guaranteed in practice? Which version was used in all experiments?
- PPMI validity: Can you formalize or empirically validate that PPMI computed on the projected matrix P approximates PPMI on the original counts? For example, compare PoP+PPMI vs (counts→PPMI→then project) and report approximation error and ranking consistency.
- Baselines and efficiency: Please include comparisons to SGNS and GloVe trained on UKWaC, and to PPMI+SVD at matched dimensions; report training time, inference time, memory, and Spearman with confidence intervals. Are your observed gains statistically significant over RI/count baselines?
- Robustness and generality: How does PoP perform on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words, and analogy tasks? Does performance reproduce across different corpora (e.g., Wikipedia)? How sensitive are results to preprocessing, vocabulary size, and context selection?
- Practical settings: What m and s do you recommend for typical corpora? For the reported 0.78, what exact configuration (m, s, window, scaling) was used?

Overall, the idea of nonnegative random projections to enable fast weighting is practically interesting, and the initial results are promising. Strengthening the projection definition, clarifying the semantics of PPMI in the projected space, and expanding evaluation and baselines would substantially improve the paper’s rigor and impact.
