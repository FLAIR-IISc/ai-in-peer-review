Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces using randomly generated index vectors with strictly positive entries. Unlike Random Indexing (RI), the resulting projected co-occurrence matrices can be post-processed with weighting schemes that require nonnegative “count-like” entries, notably PPMI, at low dimensionality. The authors argue (and empirically show on MEN) that PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains relatedness scores comparable to dense PPMI and some neural embeddings, while being much faster and simpler to build. They also discuss choice of similarity measure (Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI; Pearson post-PPMI) and explore effects of projection dimension m and sparsity parameter s.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple variant of random indexing that uses positive-only sparse random index vectors to permit efficient PPMI weighting in the projected space (enabling dense weighting on low-dimensional representations).
2) Empirical evidence on MEN that PoP+PPMI+Pearson matches dense PPMI and approaches neural embeddings, at substantially reduced computational cost; and analysis of how m and s affect accuracy and variance.
3) A pragmatic guidance on similarity measures pre/post transformation (Kendall vs Pearson), justified by distributional considerations.

Strengths
- Practical and simple idea with clear motivation: Using positive-only sparse projections to make post-hoc weighting feasible is a neat engineering tweak that preserves the incremental/streaming and memory benefits of RI while unlocking the effectiveness of PPMI weighting. This fills a gap between fast projection methods and accurate weighted count-based models.
- Empirical gains with minimal machinery: On MEN, PoP outperforms count-based and RI without weighting and matches dense PPMI after PPMI, consistent with the claim that PPMI is the main accuracy driver and PoP delivers it cheaply. The parameter sweeps (m, s) show sensible behavior (larger m improves accuracy and reduces variance; s trades off orthogonality/collisions).
- Efficiency argument is compelling in principle: Doing PPMI on projected m-dimensional matrices instead of huge dense co-occurrence matrices is an obvious speed/memory win. Although runtime numbers are not reported, the complexity arguments and dimensionalities suggest substantial savings that are relevant for practitioners with resource constraints.
- Discussion of distributional rationale for similarity: The choice of Kendall’s τb for non-Gaussian pre-PPMI vectors and Pearson’s r for near-Gaussian post-PPMI vectors is thoughtful and grounded in standard statistical reasoning.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and baselines: Results are only on MEN with a single corpus (UKWaC). No comparisons to strong, standardized baselines trained on the same data (e.g., SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, SPPMI+SVD) nor to recent count-based improvements (e.g., context selection, subsampling, weighting variants). Claims of competitiveness with neural methods rely on numbers from other papers, not controlled experiments. This limits the strength and generality of conclusions.
- Lack of runtime and memory benchmarks: The efficiency claim is central, yet there are no empirical measurements (wall-clock time, peak memory) for dense PPMI vs PoP+PPMI across m, p, n. Without concrete numbers, it is hard to assess the practical impact.
- Theoretical framing is underdeveloped: The paper explicitly provides no theoretical guarantees or distortion bounds and only loosely connects to JL-type results. While not fatal for an applied contribution, the δ discussion remains speculative; the paper would benefit from at least a more precise characterization of the projection’s bias/variance properties and collision probabilities (beyond the helpful qualitative plots).
- Some methodological clarity issues: 
  - The presentation of Kendall’s τb uses adjacent pairs in the definition text while later using n0 = m(m−1)/2, which suggests all pairs; this needs clarification.
  - The argument that RI vectors’ row/column sums are “always 0” is inaccurate; they are zero in expectation, not in finite samples. The core point (negative entries make PMI undefined) still holds, but the explanation should be corrected.
  - Details needed for reproducibility are missing: exact preprocessing (tokenization, POS tagging choices), vocabulary and context selection thresholds, stopword handling, final m and s for best runs, and seeds. Code is only promised upon acceptance.

- Novelty is incremental: The idea of changing the random projection distribution to nonnegative entries is straightforward; connections to prior art (e.g., feature hashing with unsigned vs signed hashing, nonnegative random projections, very sparse transforms) are not fully discussed. The main novelty is the practical observation that PPMI becomes feasible/effective post-projection.

Questions to authors
1) Please report runtime and memory comparisons for (i) dense PPMI+cosine, (ii) RI (with cosine), and (iii) PoP (+PPMI) for comparable vocabulary/context sizes and m. How does time scale with m and corpus size?
2) Clarify the exact computation of Kendall’s τb: are you using all pairs (j<k) or adjacent pairs? Please provide the precise implementation details used in experiments.
3) Provide controlled baselines on UKWaC: SGNS (skip-gram with negative sampling) and/or GloVe with standard hyperparameters, trained on the same data, plus SPPMI(+SVD). How does PoP+PPMI compare under identical conditions?
4) Reproducibility: please specify preprocessing, vocabulary/context selection (frequency thresholds, stopwords), window weighting (uniform/decay), final hyperparameters (m, s, non-zeros), and random seeds for reported best scores.
5) Can RI be combined with PMI-like weighting by shifting or rectifying (e.g., using absolute values or splitting positive/negative parts)? If attempted, how does that compare to PoP+PPMI?
6) Any evidence beyond MEN (e.g., SimLex, WordSim, downstream tasks) and significance testing across runs?

Overall assessment
This is a practical, well-motivated engineering contribution with promising empirical behavior on a standard similarity benchmark and a plausible efficiency story. However, the limited evaluation, absence of runtime evidence, and some clarity/reproducibility issues reduce confidence in the breadth of the claims. With stronger empirical validation and clearer methodology, this could be a useful addition to the toolbox for building fast distributional models. At present, I view it as borderline, leaning toward rejection primarily due to evaluation scope and missing quantitative efficiency evidence.
