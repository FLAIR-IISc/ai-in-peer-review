Summary and contributions
- Proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP): a sparse, nonnegative random projection scheme that maps high-dimensional co-occurrence counts into a low-dimensional space while preserving nonnegativity, enabling post-hoc weighting (e.g., PPMI) directly in the projected space. Construction is incremental and streaming-friendly, akin to Random Indexing (RI).
- Empirical finding on MEN relatedness with UKWaC: PoP with Kendall’s τb outperforms count-based and RI baselines; PoP followed by PPMI (and Pearson) approaches the performance of dense PPMI (+/- SVD) at much lower computational cost.
- Parameter study of projection dimensionality m and sparsity s (number of nonzero entries), and discussion on similarity measures (Kendall vs Pearson) pre-/post-PPMI.

Strengths
- Practical simplicity and efficiency: The method is easy to implement, requires no training, and is incremental/streamable. This is attractive for resource-constrained settings and online updates, and a clear advantage over heavy dense PPMI or neural models in runtime and memory footprint (though the paper should quantify this; see weaknesses).
- Clear motivation for nonnegative projections: Enabling PPMI weighting after projection is an interesting design choice that distinguishes PoP from conventional RI with zero-mean random projections, and the paper explains why PPMI is undefined for signed RI projections done post hoc.
- Some empirical evidence and analysis: The paper provides (i) comparison against raw counts and RI on MEN; (ii) ablations on m and s; (iii) rationale for Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and Pearson post-PPMI, supported by distributional histograms.
- Incremental construction at low dimensions: Doing weighting at low m is practically useful; if robustly validated, this could be a valuable systems contribution for fast baseline models.

Weaknesses
- Limited and outdated evaluation: Results are only on MEN with a single corpus (UKWaC). No evaluation on stronger and more diverse benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW, SimVerb-3500, analogy, or downstream tasks). Claims of competitiveness with neural embeddings are weak without comparisons to established baselines (SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, fastText; and more recent strong baselines) under matched training data and settings. No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals.
- Missing baselines that are conceptually close: The method is very close to feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch–style projections (nonnegative, sparse collisions) followed by weighting. The paper does not cite or compare to hashing trick (Weinberger et al., 2009) or Count-Min Sketch variants, which already support incremental, nonnegative, low-dimensional count accumulation and could also be followed by PPMI or other weightings. Without this, the novelty claim is unclear.
- Theoretical underpinnings are absent: The paper explicitly states it cannot provide error bounds or guarantees for the proposed projection, nor characterize the distribution induced by the tij construction. Given the long history of JL-type results and sparse projections (Achlioptas, Li et al., Kane–Nelson, etc.), introducing a new projection distribution without analysis makes it hard to assess soundness. Heuristic observations and analogies are insufficient for a new projection scheme.
- Ambiguity in projection distribution: Equation (2) is unclear: tij = floor(1/U^α)? Why that heavy-tailed choice, why α = 0.5, and what is the expected value/variance per row/column? The paper suggests choosing s “extremely small” yet “such that each row has at least one nonzero,” but provides no formula for the probability of all-zero rows, expected nonzeros per row, or collision rates. This undermines reproducibility and principled parameter selection.
- Overstated efficiency claims without measurements: The paper asserts dramatic speedups but provides no timing, memory, or wall-clock statistics, nor complexity comparisons versus dense PPMI, PPMI+SVD, RI, or SGNS/GloVe. The Knight τb algorithm comments are not a substitute for end-to-end system metrics. Also, comments about months-long NN training are outdated and not reflective of modern implementations.
- Methodological concerns: 
  - Using τb for vector similarity is unconventional in distributional semantics; metric selection appears tuned to favor PoP. Post-PPMI, the switch to Pearson further raises concerns about metric-hacking; a fixed, standard metric (cosine) baseline should be included.
  - Applying PPMI to lossy projected counts with collisions changes the probabilistic interpretation of PMI. The paper should analyze the bias/variance introduced by projection before PMI and whether it distorts co-occurrence probabilities.
  - The comparison against dense PPMI is underspecified (context selection, cutoffs, smoothing, SVD rank, etc.). Without detailed, matched setups, the fairness of comparisons is unclear.

Questions to authors
- Novelty vs hashing/Count-Min: How does PoP differ theoretically and empirically from feature hashing (hash kernels) or Count-Min Sketch on co-occurrence counts followed by PPMI or other weightings? Please provide direct comparisons and discuss prior art.
- Projection distribution: Please specify tij precisely (is it ceil(1/U^α) or floor?), provide its expectation/variance, and justify α = 0.5. Give a formula for the probability that a row is all zeros and guidance for selecting s as a function of m and desired collision rate/orthogonality.
- Theory: Can you provide even partial guarantees (e.g., bounds on inner-product distortion, approximate preservation of PMI ordering under collisions), or at least link PoP to existing sparse JL or nonnegative projection results?
- Experimental breadth: Can you add evaluations on standard similarity/relatedness sets beyond MEN (e.g., WordSim, SimLex, RW), analogy tasks, and at least one downstream task? Include comparisons to SGNS, GloVe, fastText, and PPMI+SVD with standard hyperparameters.
- Metrics and fairness: Report results with cosine alongside τb and Pearson to control for metric effects. Provide statistical significance and variance across runs.
- Efficiency: Provide wall-clock time, peak memory, and corpus pass counts for PoP (+PPMI), dense PPMI (+/- SVD), RI, SGNS, and GloVe on the same hardware.
- Reproducibility: Clarify preprocessing (tokenization, lemmatization, POS tags, vocabulary thresholds, window weighting, stopword handling), corpus size, and all hyperparameters. Release code and scripts as promised.

Overall, while the idea of enabling post-projection PPMI via nonnegative sparse projections is intriguing and practically appealing, the current submission lacks theoretical grounding, comprehensive and fair empirical validation, and critical baselines. I cannot recommend acceptance without substantial additional evidence and clarification.
