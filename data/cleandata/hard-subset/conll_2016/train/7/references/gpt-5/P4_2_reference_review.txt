Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a randomized, incremental method to construct low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces. Unlike prior random indexing (RI) approaches that use zero-mean random projections, PoP uses sparse, non-negative integer projection matrices so that the projected co-occurrence matrix remains non-negative and can be post-processed with weighting schemes such as PPMI. The authors evaluate PoP on the MEN relatedness benchmark using UKWaC, compare against count-based models and RI, and study parameter effects (dimension m and sparsity s). They report that PoP without weighting outperforms RI/counts for small windows, and that PoP+PPMI reaches performance comparable to dense PPMI models and some neural baselines while being much faster to compute.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple randomized projection scheme with non-negative entries enabling PPMI computation after projection, thereby allowing efficient, incremental construction of compressed co-occurrence spaces that can still exploit PMI-based weighting.
2) An empirical study showing that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive MEN relatedness scores with small vector dimensionalities and without costly neural training; analyses of performance as a function of projection dimension and sparsity.
3) A discussion on choice of similarity metrics for non-Gaussian (Kendall’s τb) vs. PPMI-transformed near-Gaussian (Pearson’s r) spaces, with empirical support.

Strengths
1) Practical efficiency and incremental construction. The method preserves two desirable properties simultaneously: (i) low-dimensional, streaming construction via sparse random projections; and (ii) compatibility with PPMI weighting, which typically requires non-negative counts. This makes PoP attractive for resource-constrained or online settings where dense count matrices or neural training are costly.

2) Empirical parameter study and transparency. The paper systematically varies m and the number of non-zeros per index vector, reports averages over multiple random runs, and provides interpretable guidance (e.g., very sparse index vectors for PPMI, different s when using Kendall directly). This helps practitioners understand trade-offs and expected variability.

3) Competitive performance relative to strong count-based baselines. On MEN, PoP without weighting beats RI and raw count-based models across window sizes; PoP+PPMI+Pearson matches dense PPMI results while operating on much smaller vectors, illustrating that the approach can retain accuracy without heavy computation.

4) Clear motivation grounded in distributional statistics. The paper properly frames the heavy-tailed nature of co-occurrence data and convincingly argues why weighting (PMI/PPMI) and dimensionality reduction are needed; it also connects the distributional shape to the choice of correlation measure (Kendall vs. Pearson), supported by histogram analyses.

Weaknesses
1) Limited evaluation scope. Results are only on MEN with one corpus (UKWaC). No comparison to standard neural baselines (e.g., SGNS/word2vec, GloVe) trained on the same corpus with tuned hyperparameters, and no tests on other intrinsic (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RareWords) or extrinsic tasks. Claims of competitiveness with neural methods are indirect. Statistical significance testing is not reported.

2) Missing theory and unclear projection distribution. The paper explicitly lacks theoretical guarantees or error bounds for PoP beyond intuitive analogies to sparse projections. Equation (2) is underspecified/unclear (t_ij = floor(1/U^α)?), and the impact of this heavy-tailed positive weighting vs. simple 0/1 indexing is not theoretically justified. Without bounds (e.g., JL-type statements or concentration results), it is difficult to assess robustness.

3) Questionable or imprecise claims regarding RI and PMI. The paper states that RI-projected matrices cannot be PPMI-weighted because row/column sums are always zero due to E(R)=0. In expectation, yes, but realized sums are not exactly zero; the stronger objection is that RI projections produce negative entries that do not correspond to valid joint counts, making PMI ill-defined. The argument should be corrected and the statistical meaning of applying PMI to post-projection PoP matrices (which mix many original contexts through collisions) should be discussed more rigorously.

4) Fairness and completeness of baselines and similarity choices. Dense PPMI is evaluated with cosine, whereas PoP+PPMI uses Pearson—this difference can affect reported gaps and should be controlled (e.g., report Pearson on dense PPMI as well). Similarly, pre/post-normalization choices are not consistently described. Reporting end-to-end wall-clock time/memory would substantiate efficiency claims.

5) Clarity/presentation issues. Notation and formatting are occasionally confusing (switching T/R, footnotes in running text, Equation (2) typographic ambiguities, “E(R)” without explicit mean value, some figure references without visible plots). These issues hinder reproducibility and precision.

Questions to authors
- Please precisely define the sampling distribution in Equation (2), including the rationale for the heavy-tailed positive weights (vs. simple 0/1). Did you ablate different entry distributions (e.g., 0/1, geometric) and observe consistent trends?
- Can you report results on additional benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RareWords) and at least one extrinsic task (e.g., nearest-neighbor classification or semantic textual similarity), using the same corpus and tokenization?
- Provide controlled baselines: dense PPMI with Pearson and Kendall; SGNS and/or GloVe trained on UKWaC with standard hyperparameters. Include multiple random seeds and significance tests.
- Report wall-clock time, memory, and CPU/GPU usage for building: (i) dense counts+PPMI; (ii) RI; (iii) PoP; and (iv) PoP+PPMI, for the same vocabulary/context filtering.
- Please clarify the claim about RI and PPMI. Do you mean that RI-projected values are not valid joint counts (due to negative entries), making PMI undefined rather than marginals being exactly zero?
- How sensitive is PoP+PPMI to collisions? Can you quantify how collision rate (non-orthogonality proxy) affects performance, and whether simple derandomization (e.g., multiple independent hash projections with concatenation) improves robustness?
- Have you tried computing PMI before projection on a pruned count matrix, followed by projection, as an alternative baseline?

Overall assessment
The paper introduces a simple and potentially useful variant of random indexing that preserves non-negativity to enable efficient PMI-style weighting post-projection. The idea is practical and empirically promising on MEN, and the parameter study is informative. However, the evaluation is narrow, the theoretical underpinnings are missing, and some claims (especially regarding RI and PMI) need correction or clearer justification. Strengthening empirical comparisons, providing runtime evidence, clarifying the projection distribution, and tightening the presentation would substantially improve the submission.
