Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with nonnegative integer entries that allows building low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces incrementally (as in Random Indexing, RI) while still enabling post-hoc application of weighting transformations such as PPMI. The authors argue that because RI uses zero-mean signed projections, marginals vanish and PMI/PPMI cannot be applied meaningfully to the projected space. PoP preserves positive marginals, making PPMI feasible at low dimension. Similarity is computed with Kendall’s τb on raw PoP vectors (to handle non-Gaussian distributions) and with Pearson r after PPMI (assuming approximately Gaussianized distributions). On the MEN relatedness benchmark with UKWaC, PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines and, when combined with PPMI, approaches ≈0.75 Spearman, reportedly comparable to some neural embeddings while being much faster and incremental. The paper also reports parameter studies over dimension m and sparsity s.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple random projection scheme with positive-only sparse indices that supports applying PPMI after projection, enabling efficient weighting on low-dimensional representations.
2) Empirical evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves MEN performance comparable to dense PPMI models and better than RI/count-based baselines, with parameter analyses of m and sparsity.
3) A practical recipe (τb pre-weighting, Pearson post-weighting) motivated by distributional considerations.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: PoP is easy to implement, incremental, and potentially very fast. The idea of deferring PPMI to a low-dimensional space is attractive when dense PMI at full dimensionality is infeasible.
- Empirical gains over RI and raw count spaces: The paper shows consistent improvements on MEN, especially for smaller windows, supporting the claim that PoP is more than mere distance preservation.
- Parameter exploration: The paper investigates the effects of m and the number of nonzero entries, and discusses the trade-off between sparsity, orthogonality, and distortion; this is useful guidance for practitioners.
- Sensible similarity choice: The distributional rationale for using τb on non-Gaussian projections and Pearson after PPMI (variance stabilization) is reasonable and supported by empirical trends.
- Clear positioning vs RI and PPMI: The paper identifies an actionable limitation of RI (signed projections) for PMI-style weighting and proposes a concrete workaround.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation: Results are only on a single dataset (MEN) and single corpus (UKWaC). No tests on other similarity sets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim, RG-65, Rare Words), no downstream tasks, and no statistical significance tests. Neural baselines are not reproduced on the same data; “comparability” is asserted via numbers from other papers, which weakens the claim.
- Theoretical and methodological clarity: The distribution of the projection entries is underspecified/ambiguous (the notation for 1⌈U^α⌉ or ⌊U^α⌋ is unclear; with α=0.5, the floor would be 0 almost surely). The constraint “at least one nonzero per row” is stated but not operationalized. No formal discussion of how PPMI on the projected matrix relates to PMI of the original co-occurrence matrix (it is not PMI of any well-defined event space post-projection). The paper acknowledges the lack of bounds but gives no surrogate analysis (e.g., bias/variance of projected counts).
- Baseline and related-work coverage: The paper does not compare to the feature hashing trick (Weinberger et al., 2009) and its signed/unsigned variants, which are closely related to random aggregation of counts; nor to other nonnegative random mappings or to modern shifted-PMI+SVD/SGNS trained on the same corpus. Claims about SVD gains being “negligible” lack detailed setup and may contradict known results depending on preprocessing.
- Efficiency claims lack evidence: The paper asserts “seconds/minutes” and “dramatic” savings but provides no wall-clock runtimes, memory comparisons, or hardware specs. Kendall’s τb per-pair is O(m log m) and could be costly for large vocabularies; this practical aspect isn’t quantified.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Several presentation issues (footnote spillovers, missing precise hyperparameters, unclear notation for the projection distribution) impede reproducibility. Code is promised only upon acceptance. Details such as exact s values, nonzero counts, window weighting, tokenization/lemmatization filters, and how the “at least one nonzero per row” constraint is enforced are critical to replicate results.

Questions to authors
- Projection distribution: Please disambiguate Equation (2). Is tij = 0 w.p. 1−s and tij = ceil(U^α) or floor(U^α) otherwise? With α=0.5, how do you avoid tij=0 when selected? What integer range results? How do you enforce at least one nonzero per row—resampling or deterministic placement?
- PMI after projection: Can you provide a justification or analysis for computing PPMI on the projected matrix? What do the marginals represent post-projection, and under what assumptions does PPMI preserve or approximate associations from the original co-occurrence matrix?
- Comparisons: How does PoP compare to (a) feature hashing with and without signed hashing, and (b) SGNS/GloVe and PPMI+SVD trained on UKWaC under matched preprocessing? Could you add these baselines?
- Efficiency: Please report memory usage and wall-clock times for building PoP, dense PMI/PPMI, RI, and a neural baseline on the same machine, including the cost of τb vs cosine/pearson at query time.
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to corpus size, vocabulary pruning, stopword removal, window weighting (e.g., distance decay), and to alternative similarity measures (cosine on mean-centered vectors)?
- Scope: Have you tested on other benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999) where topical co-occurrence is less predictive? Does PoP+PPMI retain an advantage?

Overall assessment w.r.t. CoNLL criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The specific “positive-only random projections to enable PPMI post-projection” is a neat twist on RI/feature hashing, but closely related ideas exist; stronger positioning to prior art is needed.
- Soundness/correctness: Promising but incomplete. The algorithm is plausible and empirically effective on MEN, yet theoretical underpinnings and the semantics of PPMI after projection are not established.
- Significance: Potentially useful for efficient large-scale semantic spaces; current evidence is limited by narrow evaluation and missing baselines.
- Clarity: Generally understandable, but notation and methodological specifics must be clarified for reproducibility.
- Reproducibility: Currently limited. Release of code and fuller experimental details would help substantially.

In its current form, the work shows an interesting practical idea with encouraging initial results, but it needs broader and stronger empirical validation, clearer methodology, and tighter connections to related techniques.
