Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly non-negative integer entries that enables constructing low-dimensional, incremental semantic spaces and, crucially, allows application of count-based weighting schemes like PPMI on the projected space.
- It argues for using Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure pre-weighting (non-Gaussian projected distributions) and Pearson’s r post-PPMI (Gaussian-like), and provides an empirical study on MEN with parameter sweeps over dimensionality m and sparsity s.
- It claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves performance comparable to popular neural embeddings but with far lower computational cost, and that PoP outperforms RI on MEN, even without weighting.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and potentially impactful idea: A non-negative, sparse random projection that preserves enough “count-like” structure to apply PPMI directly at low dimensionality. This bridges random indexing–style incremental construction with the discriminative power of PPMI, which is typically impractical at full count dimensionality.
- Practical promise: The approach is incremental, memory-friendly, and training-free. If validated broadly, this is a compelling alternative for fast, on-the-fly semantic spaces and for resource-constrained settings. The observation that τb is a better fit pre-PPMI and Pearson post-PPMI is a neat, actionable heuristic.
- Empirical clarity on parameters: The paper provides informative parameter sweeps over m and the number of non-zero entries, and documents the sensitivity/noise behavior, which is valuable for practitioners.
- Competitive results on MEN: PoP+PPMI+Pearson approaching ~0.75–0.78 on MEN suggests the pipeline is not only fast but also competitive with classic PPMI+SVD and some neural baselines (at least as cited).

Weaknesses
- Positioning and novelty relative to sketching/feature hashing is missing. PoP is extremely close in spirit to Count-Min Sketch (CMS) / feature hashing: a positive, sparse, collision-prone mapping that yields approximate non-negative pseudo-counts enabling downstream statistics. The paper does not cite or compare to CMS, Count-Sketch, or hashing trick baselines (e.g., Cormode & Muthukrishnan; Weinberger et al.). This omission undermines novelty and makes it unclear whether PoP offers theoretical or empirical advantages beyond established sketches (which can also support PMI-like statistics on sketched co-occurrence matrices).
- Evaluation is too narrow. Results are only on MEN, a single intrinsic relatedness dataset, with a single corpus. No comparisons to strong baselines on the same data/hyperparameters (SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD), no extrinsic tasks (e.g., similarity/relatedness suites beyond MEN, SimLex, RareWords, or downstream tasks), no statistical tests, and no replication on other corpora. As a result, the claim of competitiveness is not convincingly substantiated.
- Efficiency claims are unquantified. The paper repeatedly emphasizes speed and resource savings but provides no concrete runtime/memory numbers, nor throughput for incremental construction, nor comparisons against count-based PPMI+SVD and SGNS. Without wall-clock measurements and memory footprints, the core selling point remains anecdotal.
- Theoretical and definitional gaps. The distribution in Eq. (2) is unclear (typesetting of b1/U^αc? support? tails?), no expectations/variance are given, and there’s no analysis of bias/variance of the sketched marginals and PMI estimates (CMS-like analyses are well-known). The claim “sums are always zero” in RI and thus PPMI is undefined is overstated: while negative entries indeed break PMI, there are straightforward workarounds (e.g., shifted/nonnegative variants) and at minimum this requires a more careful argument. More broadly, there is no guarantee that PoP’s pseudo-count marginals yield well-calibrated PMI; the histogram anecdote is insufficient.
- Reproducibility and presentation. Code is promised only upon acceptance; preprocessing and parameterization details are sparse; several typos/notation issues (R vs T, expectations “E(R)” wording, Eq. (2) rendering) and references to figures without clear quantitative tables make it hard to independently validate.

Questions to authors
- Clarify Eq. (2) precisely: how are non-zero entries sampled (support, distribution, expected value)? Provide the exact sampling algorithm and show E[tij], Var[tij], and the expected number of nonzeros per row/column. What role does α play empirically?
- How does PoP differ from Count-Min Sketch / feature hashing applied to the co-occurrence matrix, followed by PPMI on the sketched matrix? Please position against this literature and provide empirical comparisons; if different, articulate the advantages (bias, variance, collision behavior, accuracy).
- PMI semantics under collisions: Are the projected marginals unbiased estimators of the true marginals? What is the bias/variance of PPMI under PoP, and how does it compare to CMS? Can you derive bounds reminiscent of CMS analysis?
- Provide concrete efficiency numbers: wall-clock time and peak memory to build spaces (incrementally and batch), and to compute PPMI, across m and s, compared to (i) dense PPMI and (ii) SGNS/GloVe on the same corpus.
- Beyond MEN: Can you add at least SimLex-999, WS-353, and an extrinsic task (e.g., text classification or STS) to validate the generality of the gains? Also compare to PPMI+SVD (truncated) and SGNS on UKWaC with matched windows and subsampling.

Overall assessment
The paper introduces a simple and potentially useful idea—nonnegative, sparse random projections enabling PPMI at low dimensionality—and reports encouraging MEN results with informative parameter studies. However, the lack of positioning against Count-Min Sketch/feature hashing, limited evaluation, missing efficiency measurements, and theory/definition gaps make it premature. With proper positioning, stronger baselines, broader benchmarks, and quantified efficiency, this could become a noteworthy contribution. In its current form, I recommend rejection, but I encourage the authors to revise—explicitly connecting to sketching methods and strengthening empirical and theoretical support could make this an impactful, novel direction.
