Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random projection scheme for building low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces where all projection weights are positive integers. The key idea is to retain the efficiency and incrementality of Random Indexing (RI) while enabling post-hoc weighting such as PPMI, which standard (zero-mean, signed) random projections do not support as-is. The authors also explore Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure pre-weighting and argue for Pearson correlation post-weighting. Empirically, PoP + PPMI + Pearson attains MEN ρ ≈ 0.75 on UKWaC with small index vectors and very fast construction.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, positive-only, sparse random projection matrix that allows incremental construction and supports standard weighting schemes (PPMI) in the projected space.
2) An empirical study showing that PoP outperforms RI and unweighted count-based models on MEN, and that PoP + PPMI + Pearson reaches performance competitive with popular embeddings while being much cheaper to compute.
3) A useful exploration of parameter regimes (dimension m, sparsity s, number of nonzeros) and the impact of Kendall vs Pearson under different distributions.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with high practical potential: Turning random projections “positive-only” to enable PPMI weighting downstream is a neat, bold idea that directly addresses a long-standing limitation of RI-like methods. The method preserves incrementality and efficiency while unlocking the strong empirical gains of (P)PMI in a much smaller space.

- Efficiency-oriented contribution: The approach constructs compact representations on the fly (hundreds to a few thousands of dimensions), then applies PPMI there—dramatically reducing the cost of weighting relative to high-dimensional count matrices. This is well-aligned with current needs for scalable, quick-to-train representations.

- Clear empirical trend analyses: The paper systematically varies m and the number of non-zero entries, reports variance across runs, and draws sensible conclusions about capacity vs distortion and the role of τb vs Pearson after PPMI. This parameter study is genuinely useful for practitioners.

- Competitive performance with simple components: Achieving MEN ρ ≈ 0.75 using a single corpus with a very lightweight pipeline is impressive, and the low computational footprint is a meaningful advantage over heavy neural training.

- Potential to inspire further work: The framing opens doors to combining sketch-like projections with classical weighting, exploring other variance-stabilizing transforms in small spaces, and integrating PoP as a fast pre-stage for downstream neural models.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and baselines: Results are reported only on MEN/UKWaC, with no extrinsic tasks or multiple benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RG-65, Rare Words, or downstream tasks). Comparisons to strong baselines such as SGNS, GloVe, and PPMI-SVD (with proper hyperparameter tuning) are missing or only lightly discussed. The claim of “competitive with neural embeddings” needs direct apples-to-apples comparisons under the same corpora and settings.

- Theoretical claims and justifications are thin: The paper explicitly notes the lack of bounds or theory for the proposed projection. Some of the reasoning about cosine vs correlation, and about RI precluding PPMI, is oversimplified. For instance, while signed projections complicate PMI probabilities, sums are not strictly zero—practical workarounds (shifts, separate positive/negative channels, or hashing-based approximations) exist. More careful discussion and/or references (e.g., connections to Count-Min Sketch/feature hashing for co-occurrence estimation) would strengthen the contribution and situate novelty precisely.

- Ambiguity around the PoP distribution and its implications: The choice of tij ~ floor(1/U^α) with α=0.5 leads to heavy-tailed integer weights; it’s unclear why this distribution is preferable to simpler {0,1} or {0,1,…,k}, and how α impacts bias/variance and collisions. No ablation on α is provided.

- Missing runtime/memory benchmarks: The paper repeatedly emphasizes speed and low resource use but provides no quantitative wall-clock or memory comparisons versus count-based + PPMI, PPMI-SVD, and SGNS/GloVe. Given that efficiency is central to the claim, concrete numbers are necessary.

- Claims about similarity measures could be more rigorous: The argument that cosine equates Pearson under zero-mean conditions, and the rationale for using Kendall’s τb pre-weighting, are plausible but would benefit from clearer empirical evidence (e.g., reporting correlations across measures) and caveats.

Questions to Authors
1) How does PoP compare empirically to Count-Min Sketch (CMS) or feature hashing for co-occurrence estimation followed by PPMI computed in the hashed space? Conceptually, PoP seems close to CMS-style positive sketches; benchmarking against CMS would help clarify novelty and benefits.

2) Can you provide wall-clock, memory, and energy comparisons for building: (a) count+PPMI, (b) count+PPMI+SVD, (c) RI, (d) SGNS/GloVe, and (e) PoP (+PPMI), all on the same corpus and vocabulary?

3) How sensitive are results to α in the projection distribution? Would simpler positive projections (e.g., fixed small integer weights) perform similarly?

4) Could you include direct comparisons to SGNS and GloVe trained on UKWaC with matched preprocessing and window sizes, and to PPMI-SVD with standard hyperparameters (e.g., context distribution smoothing)?

5) Regarding PPMI after signed projections: could you clarify why simple shifts or splitting positive/negative channels wouldn’t suffice, and provide empirical evidence that PoP materially improves over such baselines?

Overall assessment
This is a bold, simple idea with promising practical implications and nice empirical signals. Despite gaps in theory and breadth of evaluation, I find the core concept novel enough and the potential impact high. With stronger baselines and efficiency numbers (and ideally a comparison to CMS/feature hashing), this could be a compelling contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the evaluation breadth and efficiency reporting.
