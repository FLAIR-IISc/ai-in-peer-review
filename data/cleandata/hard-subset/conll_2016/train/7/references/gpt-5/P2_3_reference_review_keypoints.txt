- Proposal: Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse nonnegative integer random projection enabling post-hoc PPMI on low-dimensional incrementally built semantic spaces.
- Motivation: RI’s zero-mean signed projections eliminate marginals, preventing PMI/PPMI weighting; PoP preserves positive marginals so PPMI is feasible at low dimension.
- Similarity strategy: Use Kendall’s τb on raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian) and Pearson after PPMI (approx. Gaussianized).
- Empirical claim: PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines on MEN (UKWaC) and PoP+PPMI approaches ≈0.75 Spearman, competitive with some neural embeddings.
- Parameter study: Authors analyze effects of projection dimension m and sparsity s on performance and trade-offs.
- Contribution 1: A simple positive-only sparse random projection scheme that supports PPMI after projection.
- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson matches dense PPMI models and beats RI/count baselines, with m and sparsity analyses.
- Contribution 3: Practical recipe recommending τb pre-weighting and Pearson post-weighting based on distributional considerations.
- Strength — practicality: PoP is simple, incremental, and potentially fast; deferring PPMI to low-dim space is attractive when full PMI is infeasible.
- Strength — empirical gains: Consistent improvements over RI and raw counts on MEN, especially with smaller windows.
- Strength — parameter exploration: Useful analysis of m and nonzero entry trade-offs (sparsity, orthogonality, distortion).
- Strength — similarity choice rationale: Reasonable and empirically supported use of τb then Pearson based on distributional properties.
- Strength — clear positioning: Identifies RI’s limitation for PMI-style weighting and offers a concrete workaround.
- Weakness — limited evaluation: Single dataset (MEN) and single corpus (UKWaC); no other similarity sets, downstream tasks, or significance tests; neural baselines not reproduced on same data.
- Weakness — unclear projection distribution: Equation (2) notation ambiguous, α exponent unclear, and “at least one nonzero per row” constraint not operationalized.
- Weakness — theoretical gaps: No formal relation or bounds connecting PPMI on projected matrix to PMI of original co-occurrence matrix; no bias/variance or surrogate analysis.
- Weakness — missing related work comparisons: No comparisons to feature hashing (signed/unsigned), other nonnegative mappings, or matched SGNS/GloVe/PPMI+SVD baselines.
- Weakness — unsubstantiated efficiency claims: “Seconds/minutes” and “dramatic” savings asserted without runtimes, memory, or hardware; Kendall’s τb cost not quantified.
- Weakness — reproducibility and clarity issues: Presentation problems, missing hyperparameters, unclear notation, and code promised only on acceptance hinder replication.
- Question — projection distribution: Clarify Equation (2), the role of α, integer range of tij, and how at least one nonzero per row is enforced.
- Question — PMI after projection: Provide justification/analysis for computing PPMI on projected counts and what marginals represent post-projection.
- Question — additional comparisons: Compare to feature hashing (signed/unsigned), SGNS/GloVe, and PPMI+SVD trained on UKWaC with matched preprocessing.
- Question — efficiency reporting: Provide wall-clock times, memory usage, and hardware for PoP, dense PMI/PPMI, RI, and neural baselines, and cost of τb vs cosine/Pearson.
- Question — robustness: Report sensitivity to corpus size, vocab pruning, stopword removal, window weighting, and alternative similarity measures.
- Question — scope: Test on other benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999) where topical co-occurrence is less predictive.
- Overall originality: Moderate — neat twist on RI/feature hashing but closely related prior ideas exist.
- Overall soundness: Promising empirically but lacking theoretical underpinning and formal semantics of PPMI post-projection.
- Overall significance: Potentially useful for efficient semantic spaces but currently limited by narrow evaluation and missing baselines.
- Overall clarity/reproducibility: Understandable but requires clearer notation, full methodological details, and released code to be reproducible.
