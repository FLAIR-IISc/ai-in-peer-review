Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Random Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection method for incremental construction of distributional semantic spaces. Unlike standard Random Indexing (RI) that uses zero-mean projections with negative and positive entries, PoP uses random index vectors with only positive integer entries. The authors argue this enables applying PPMI weighting directly to the projected, low-dimensional space, improving discriminability while keeping computation lightweight. Similarity is computed with Kendall’s τb for raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian), and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussianized). Empirical evaluation on MEN with UKWaC reports that PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75–0.78, competitive with neural embeddings, and outperforms RI and count-based baselines. Parameter studies probe the effects of dimensionality and sparsity.

Contributions as assessed:
1) A simple, incremental, sparse positive-only random projection scheme that enables efficient PPMI weighting at low dimensionality, yielding competitive performance on MEN (primary).
2) Empirical analysis of similarity metrics (Kendall vs. Pearson) matched to distributional properties before/after PPMI, with parameter sweeps over dimension and sparsity.
3) A practical observation that PPMI on projected counts can approach dense PPMI performance with substantially reduced dimensionality.

Strengths
- Practical and simple method with incremental construction: The two-step scheme mirrors RI’s streaming construction, requiring only generation of sparse index vectors and additive updates per co-occurrence. This suits large-scale or online settings and does not require iterative training.

- PPMI on low-dimensional projections: Showing that PPMI can be computed in the projected space (because entries are non-negative) is valuable in practice. The reported MEN scores for PoP+PPMI+Pearson (≈0.75) comparable to dense PPMI and neural baselines, using vectors of a few thousand dimensions, indicates a favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-off.

- Parameter sensitivity and variance reporting: The paper averages over multiple runs, reports min/max bands, and studies the influence of dimensionality m and the number of non-zeros per index vector. The observed reduction in variance with larger m and the trade-off between sparsity and performance are informative.

- Clear motivation for similarity choices: The argument that Kendall’s τb is appropriate in non-Gaussian projected spaces and Pearson’s r after PPMI’s variance-stabilizing/log transform is coherent, and the empirical results support this choice.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to feature hashing/sketching: The core mechanism—mapping contexts to a few positive indices and accumulating counts—is very close to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch for co-occurrence matrices. The paper does not acknowledge or compare to these established techniques. The positive-only choice is known to introduce overestimation bias; signed hashing is typically used to reduce bias. Without positioning PoP against hashing baselines, novelty and contribution are unclear.

- Insufficient theoretical grounding and some inaccurate claims: The paper explicitly lacks guarantees or error bounds for distortion. Additionally, the claim that in RI “the sum of values of row and column vectors is always 0” because E(R)=0 is inaccurate; zero expectation does not imply zero sums in finite projections. More broadly, applying PMI/PPMI requires probabilistic counts; using projected, collided counts changes marginals in a biased way. The effect of collisions on PMI estimates is not analyzed.

- Narrow evaluation and missing efficiency evidence: Results are reported only on MEN with UKWaC. No evaluation on other standard similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999, RW), no downstream tasks, and no statistical significance testing. Claims of dramatic efficiency (seconds or minutes) are not supported with runtime/memory benchmarks or comparisons against dense PPMI+SVD or widely used neural methods (e.g., SGNS, GloVe) under matched resources.

- Ambiguity in projection distribution and parameterization: Equation (2) defines tij with a heavy-tail via floor(U^α), but implementation details are vague: how many non-zeros per row are enforced (fixed k vs. Bernoulli with expected ks)? How is “ensure at least one non-zero per row” implemented? The role of α and its impact are not studied. The choice of s is reported in ad hoc ranges without principled selection criteria.

- Similarity computation scalability: Kendall’s τb per pair is O(m log m). While m is small, large-vocabulary retrieval requires many pairwise similarities; practical scalability and indexing considerations are not discussed, nor comparisons to cosine or Pearson at similar accuracy.

Questions to authors
- Relationship to hashing/sketching: How does PoP differ from feature hashing or Count-Min Sketch applied to the word–context matrix, both in mechanism and bias/variance? Please include baselines for signed hashing and CMS (with multiple hash functions) and analyze bias in PMI estimates under collisions.

- Efficiency evidence: Provide wall-clock time and peak memory for constructing models and computing PPMI on PoP vs. (a) dense PPMI+SVD and (b) SGNS/GloVe on the same corpus and vocabulary. Include scaling with m and with the number of non-zeros per index vector.

- Projection specification: Is the number of non-zero entries per row fixed or random? How is “at least one non-zero per row” guaranteed? What is the rationale for α=0.5 and for the specific integer value distribution? Please provide pseudocode.

- PMI on projected counts: Are PMI/PPMI weights computed solely from the projected matrix P? If so, what is the expected bias in marginals due to collisions, and how does this affect PMI? Any analytic or empirical study (e.g., ablations varying collision rate) would clarify.

- Similarity metrics: For raw PoP, how does Kendall’s τb compare to cosine and Pearson in accuracy and runtime? For PPMI-transformed PoP, is Pearson consistently superior to cosine? Please report systematic comparisons.

- Broader evaluation: Can results be provided on additional word similarity benchmarks and a downstream task (e.g., document classification or lexical substitution)? Include significance tests across random seeds.

Overall, the paper introduces a practical variant of sparse projections enabling PPMI at low dimensionality and presents encouraging MEN results and parameter studies. However, the overlap with established hashing/sketching methods, limited evaluation, and lack of theoretical and efficiency evidence weaken the case for publication without further clarification and experiments.
