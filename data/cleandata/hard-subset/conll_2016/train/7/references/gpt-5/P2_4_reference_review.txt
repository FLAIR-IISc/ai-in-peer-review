Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly nonnegative integer entries, enabling application of frequency-based weighting such as PPMI in the projected space. The core idea: project first with a positive-only random matrix, then apply PPMI and compute similarities (Kendall τb on raw PoP; Pearson r after PPMI).
- It presents empirical comparisons to count-based models and Random Indexing (RI) on MEN relatedness using UKWaC, showing PoP > RI and PoP+PPMI ≈ dense PPMI with substantially lower dimensionality and claimed efficiency.
- It explores sensitivity to dimensionality m and sparsity (number of nonzeros per index vector), and discusses the method’s randomized behavior.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The use of a strictly positive sparse random projection to preserve nonnegativity and enable PPMI in the low-dimensional space is a neat and practically motivated twist on RI. This addresses a known limitation of RI-like methods (negative entries prevent PMI-style weighting).
- Practical simplicity and incremental construction: The two-step indexing procedure is straightforward, incremental, and compatible with streaming data. This is attractive for resource-constrained settings and for quickly building spaces without training neural models.
- Empirical evidence of utility over RI: On MEN, raw PoP outperforms RI and count-based baselines, and PoP+PPMI approaches the performance of dense PPMI, with parameter sweeps that expose behavior vs. m and sparsity. The evaluation includes averaging across multiple random runs and reporting min–max, which is good practice for randomized algorithms.
- Clear qualitative rationale for similarity measures: The choice of Kendall τb for non-Gaussian projected vectors and Pearson after PPMI (variance-stabilizing/log transform) is well motivated and empirically supported.
- Discussion of randomness and sensitivity: The paper acknowledges the lack of theory and provides some intuition about variance reduction with larger m and the trade-offs in sparsity, offering useful guidance for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and baselines: Results are only on a single intrinsic benchmark (MEN) with one corpus (UKWaC). No tests on other standard similarity sets (WS353, SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, RW), no rare-word robustness, and no extrinsic tasks (e.g., downstream classification or parsing). Comparisons to strong non-neural count-based baselines (PPMI+SVD with proper hyperparameters) and to widely used predictive models (SGNS/skip-gram, GloVe, fastText) are missing under controlled settings. Claims of competitiveness are supported by literature-reported numbers rather than head-to-head comparisons on the same data and preprocessing.
- Efficiency claims are unquantified: The paper asserts large speedups (“seconds or minutes”) and memory benefits but provides no timing, memory, or complexity measurements versus dense PPMI, PPMI+SVD, or SGNS/GloVe. Without wall-clock results and hardware specs, the practical advantage remains speculative.
- Theoretical grounding is thin: The distribution of T’s entries (floor(1/U^α)) and the choice of α=0.5 are ad hoc. The paper candidly states the lack of guarantees (JL-type bounds, distortion analyses, or bias introduced by projecting before PMI). While not fatal for an empirical paper, at least a discussion of how projection-before-PPMI alters PMI estimates (and potential bias/collision effects) would strengthen the contribution.
- Ambiguity and rigor gaps in method details: The probability construction does not actually guarantee at least one nonzero per row; if this is required, it should be enforced procedurally. The role of s and its range is inconsistently described (“extremely small” vs. tuned ranges), and α is not justified. Preprocessing choices (stopwords, frequency cutoffs) are only used in late tuning claims, not the main evaluation. Figures suggest additional tuning yields ρ≈0.78, but those conditions are not specified or reproducible. No statistical significance tests are reported for differences in ρ.
- Reproducibility: Code is promised upon acceptance. For a method hinging on randomization and delicate hyperparameters (m, sparsity, α, similarity measure), releasing code, seeds, and exact preprocessing is important at submission time. As is, the work is hard to verify.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide head-to-head comparisons on the same corpus and preprocessing against (a) PPMI+SVD with standard scaling (as in Levy et al. 2015), (b) SGNS, and (c) GloVe/fastText, including runtime and memory usage? This is crucial to substantiate the efficiency/accuracy trade-off.
- What is the sensitivity to α in the index-value distribution? Why 0.5, and how do results change with α∈{0, 0.25, 1}? Would fixed positive weights (e.g., 1) suffice?
- How does projecting before PPMI affect PMI estimation? Can you analyze bias from collisions (e.g., via Count-Sketch-style arguments) or empirically compare “PPMI(counts) → projection” vs. “projection → PPMI”?
- Can you enforce and describe the procedure that guarantees at least one nonzero per row of T? What is the expected collision rate among index vectors as a function of m and sparsity for realistic n?
- Please report concrete timing and memory numbers for constructing PoP, PoP+PPMI, dense PPMI, and SGNS on UKWaC, with hardware specs.

Assessment against CoNLL criteria
- Originality: Moderate. A clear and interesting twist on RI via nonnegative sparse projections enabling downstream weighting. Prior work on nonnegative/random projections and hashing is not fully covered.
- Soundness/correctness: Partial. The method is plausible and shows empirical gains over RI, but lacks theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis of projection-before-PPMI. Some methodological details need tightening.
- Significance: Currently limited. With broader evaluation and quantified efficiency gains, significance could be high; as presented (single benchmark, limited baselines), impact is uncertain.
- Clarity: Mixed. High-level ideas are understandable and figures helpful, but the paper contains typos, notation inconsistencies, and unsubstantiated parameter choices; reproducibility details are insufficient.
- Resources/reproducibility: Weak. No code at submission; incomplete reporting of preprocessing and hyperparameters; no runtime/memory data.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and practically motivated, and the initial empirical evidence is encouraging. However, to meet CoNLL standards, the paper needs broader and stronger empirical validation with quantified efficiency, clearer and more rigorous methodological exposition, and released code to support reproducibility.
