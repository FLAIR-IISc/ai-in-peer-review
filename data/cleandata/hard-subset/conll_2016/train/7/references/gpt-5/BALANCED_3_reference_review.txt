Summary
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with nonnegative entries that enables applying weighting schemes such as PPMI after projection. The method builds low-dimensional word vectors incrementally by summing a small number of positive integer index vectors per context occurrence. The authors argue that, unlike RI (with zero-mean projections), PoP preserves positive “pseudo-counts” so that PPMI is well-defined and computationally cheap in the projected space. They recommend Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure before weighting (non-Gaussian regime) and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussian-like regime). On MEN using UKWaC, PoP outperforms count-based and RI baselines; PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ~0.75–0.78 Spearman ρ, comparable to reported neural embeddings, with substantially lower computation.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, incremental, positive-only sparse random projection for distributional semantics that allows PPMI weighting in the low-dimensional space.
2) An empirical study showing PoP (especially with PPMI) is competitive on MEN while being faster and lighter than count-based+PPMI and neural embeddings; analysis of parameter effects (dimension m, sparsity s, nonzeros).
3) A proposal to use Kendall’s τb for similarity in non-Gaussian projected spaces and Pearson after PPMI.

Strengths
- Practical efficiency and incrementality: PoP is simple to implement, requires no heavy training, and supports streaming updates. The idea of doing PPMI in the projected space is attractive and can drastically reduce memory and compute versus dense co-occurrence matrices.
- Empirical gains over RI and raw counts: The paper convincingly shows that PoP alone can outperform RI and count-based models on MEN, especially at smaller windows, and that PoP+PPMI+Pearson matches or exceeds dense PMI/PPMI baselines in their setup.
- Sensible analysis of similarity measures and distributions: The discussion linking distributional assumptions (non-Gaussian vs. Gaussian-like after log/PPMI) to Kendall vs. Pearson is thoughtful; the histograms help illustrate why correlation-based similarities can be preferable here.
- Parameter and stability analysis: The exploration of m and the number of non-zero entries, and their effect on performance variance and potential vector collisions, provides useful guidance for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing randomized sketching/feature hashing: The core mechanism—mapping high-dimensional sparse counts to a low-dimensional space by adding a few nonnegative bucketed index vectors per co-occurrence—appears very close to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch (CMS). The paper does not discuss or compare to this literature (e.g., hashing trick with/without signs; CMS; signed hashing preserving inner products). The claimed novelty over RI is emphasized, but the broader connection to well-known nonnegative sketches is a major omission and raises concerns about novelty.
- Limited and narrow evaluation: Results are reported only on MEN using a single corpus (UKWaC). There are no evaluations on other standard similarity/relatedness sets (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, RW), analogy, or downstream tasks. No comparisons to strong baselines like SGNS/GloVe or PPMI+SVD on competitive settings are provided in a controlled, reproducible way. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- Lack of theoretical grounding and some questionable claims: While the paper openly acknowledges missing theory, several claims are asserted without justification (e.g., that RI renders PPMI “undefined” due to zero-sum rows/columns; more precisely, PMI requires nonnegative counts and is not meaningful on signed projected features, but the denominator argument is not accurate). The distribution in Eq. (2) is unclear/possibly mis-specified (ceil/floor notation garbled), and the role of α is not well motivated. The rationale for Kendall’s τb as a replacement for cosine is suggestive but not rigorously backed.
- Reproducibility and experimental clarity: Important details are missing or ambiguous: preprocessing, vocabulary/context cutoffs, exact parameter settings per figure, hardware/runtime, and whether code/data will be available before acceptance. The “best” numbers (0.78) come from additional tuning not fully described. Figures are referenced but not all numeric results are tabulated; no error bars for many conditions.
- Conceptual mismatch of PPMI on projected dimensions: Applying PPMI to projected “pseudo-contexts” is pragmatic, but its relationship to PPMI on original co-occurrences is not analyzed. Collisions may systematically distort association estimates; there is no study of collision bias, signed hashing alternatives, or multiple-hash debiasing known from sketching.

Questions to authors
1) How does PoP differ from Count-Min Sketch or feature hashing (with or without sign hashing) applied to the word–context co-occurrence matrix? Please position your method within that literature and, if appropriate, compare empirically.
2) Can you clarify Eq. (2)? What exactly is “1Uα c”? Is it ceil(U^α)? If α=0.5 and U∈(0,1], does this not always yield 1 for nonzero entries? Are magnitudes beyond 1 ever used and do they matter?
3) The claim that PPMI is undefined for RI because row/column sums are zero seems off. Isn’t the issue rather that RI yields signed real-valued features, so PMI on projected dimensions is not meaningful? Please clarify and correct.
4) Provide full comparisons to strong baselines: SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD (with recommended hyperparameters and context selection), and signed hashing baselines, all trained on the same corpus. Can you include more benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WS-353, RW) and report significance?
5) What are the exact runtime/memory footprints vs. dense PPMI and SGNS for the reported settings? Please include hardware details and complexity per update.
6) How sensitive is performance to collisions? Can you report collision rates, their impact on PPMI estimates, and whether signed hashing or CMS-style multiple hashes/median-of-hashes improves robustness?
7) Beyond MEN, can you show downstream effects (e.g., STS, text classification) and whether Kendall vs. cosine vs. Pearson materially changes outcomes?

Decision
Reject. The idea is practical and potentially useful, and the empirical trends are promising. However, the contribution appears insufficiently novel given existing sketching/feature hashing methods, and the evaluation is too limited to support strong claims. Strengthening the related work, broadening the experiments, clarifying the method and theory, and providing rigorous baselines and ablations would make this a much stronger submission.
