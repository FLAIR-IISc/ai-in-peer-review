Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with non-negative integer entries, to build low-dimensional, incremental distributional word representations. Unlike standard Random Indexing (RI), PoP’s projections have positive expectation, which the authors claim enables applying PPMI weighting directly in the reduced space.
- The paper advocates using Kendall’s τb for similarity in the raw PoP space (non-Gaussian), and Pearson’s r after applying a log/PPMI transform (Gaussian-like).
- Empirical evaluation on MEN relatedness with UKWaC suggests PoP improves over RI and a raw count model; with PPMI and Pearson, PoP reaches ~0.75 Spearman, claimed to be competitive with neural embeddings, at a fraction of the computational cost.

Strengths
- Simplicity and incremental construction: The method is easy to implement, online, and potentially useful in streaming or memory-limited settings. It does not require training an explicit predictive model.
- Empirical parameter exploration: The paper reports trends for dimensionality m and sparsity s (number of non-zeros), including variability across random seeds, and discusses sensitivity to collisions/non-orthogonality. This helps practitioners understand how to set m and s.
- Practical focus on efficiency: The idea to perform weighting on a low-dimensional space is attractive and could reduce the otherwise prohibitive cost of PPMI on very high-dimensional co-occurrence matrices.
- Clarity of the high-level motivation: The paper clearly lays out the distributional hypothesis background and why RI-like schemes are appealing but limited when paired with weighting.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established hashing/sketching: PoP is essentially feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch-style aggregation with non-negative entries. The core idea—projecting high-dimensional count vectors into a low-dimensional space with sparse, non-negative collisions—is well-known in large-scale learning. The paper neither positions PoP against these methods nor establishes a clear theoretical or empirical advantage. The “PPMI-enabled” claim does not by itself imply novelty, since one can apply nonlinear transforms on hashed features as well; the question is whether the transformed statistics remain meaningful and competitive.
- Lack of theoretical grounding and unclear distributional claims: The paper explicitly provides no guarantees (JL-type or bias/variance bounds). Moreover, PMI/PPMI is defined over joint and marginal probabilities of the original events. After PoP, each dimension mixes many contexts with random, heavy-tailed weights; the “PPMI” computed in the projected space no longer estimates PMI of any well-defined underlying events. The information-theoretic interpretation is acknowledged as lost, but then the central claim reduces to “a power transform on hashed counts helps,” which requires much stronger empirical support.
- Very limited evaluation: Only MEN relatedness is used (and on the same corpus used to build embeddings). MEN is known to reward topical relatedness and co-occurrence-heavy models; this is a favorable setting. Absent are SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words, MTurk, or downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, NER, STS). There are no variance/confidence intervals across seeds for final scores, nor significance tests. It is not possible to assess robustness or generality.
- Weak baselines and incomplete comparisons: The paper does not compare to strong, established baselines constructed on the same corpus and preprocessing: (a) SGNS/CBOW/fastText; (b) GloVe; (c) PPMI+truncated SVD with randomized SVD; (d) feature hashing baselines with signed vs unsigned hashing; (e) Count-Min/CountSketch-based co-occurrence representations. The claim of being “comparable to neural embeddings” cites external numbers rather than reproducing competitive baselines under matched conditions.
- Methodological issues: 
  - Similarity choice: Using Kendall’s τb on word vectors is unconventional; a systematic comparison to cosine/Pearson in both raw PoP and PPMI-PoP is needed. Reported choices seem tuned per condition, which risks cherry-picking.
  - Hyperparameter tuning on test set risk: Window sizes, m, s and weighting choices appear tuned on MEN itself; there is no validation split or held-out tuning procedure.
  - Efficiency claims are anecdotal: No wall-clock time, memory usage, or hardware specs are reported. Claims like “seconds or minutes” are not quantified or compared fairly (e.g., against randomized SVD on PPMI, or modern word2vec implementations).

Questions to authors
- Relation to feature hashing/Count-Min/CountSketch: In what precise sense does PoP differ from these established methods? Why is unsigned positive hashing preferable to signed hashing (which is known to reduce bias)? Please include direct empirical comparisons.
- PPMI after projection: What quantity is the projected-space “PPMI” estimating? Can you formalize the bias introduced by collisions and nonzero-mean projections, or provide an ablation showing that simpler power/log transforms on hashed counts perform equivalently?
- Distribution and sampling details: The definition tij = floor(1/U^α) with probability s is unclear and appears to create heavy-tailed integer weights. Why this choice versus fixed small integer weights? How do results change with 0/1 weights? How do you ensure each row has at least one nonzero without violating independence?
- Evaluation breadth and rigor: Can you report results on multiple benchmarks (SimLex-999, WS-353, RW, MTurk) and at least one extrinsic task? Please add strong baselines trained on the same corpus and preprocessing, and report standard deviations and significance tests across seeds.
- Efficiency: Provide wall-clock times and memory for PoP, count-based+PPMI(+SVD), and word2vec/fastText on the same hardware and corpus, including construction and similarity computation costs.

Overall assessment
While the idea is simple and potentially practical, the paper lacks sufficient novelty relative to established hashing/sketching methods, provides no theoretical justification, and offers a very narrow empirical evaluation without competitive baselines or rigorous measurement. Given CoNLL’s standards and my preference for well-validated methods, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. Substantial strengthening is needed: clearer positioning vs hashing/sketching, theoretical analysis or error bounds, broader and rigorous evaluation, and transparent runtime/memory comparisons.
