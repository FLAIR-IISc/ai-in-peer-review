Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random projection scheme that uses sparse non-negative integer “index vectors” to build low-dimensional distributional representations incrementally. Unlike standard random indexing (RI) with mean-zero projections, PoP aims to permit post-hoc weighting such as PPMI in the projected space.
- The authors evaluate on MEN relatedness (UKWaC) and report that PoP > RI and count-based baselines; with PPMI and Pearson’s r (PoP+PPMI+Pearson), they claim results (~0.75–0.78) comparable to neural embeddings, with much lower computational cost.
- They provide some sensitivity analysis over projection dimensionality m and sparsity s, and argue for Kendall’s τb (pre-PPMI) vs Pearson’s r (post-PPMI).

Strengths
- Simplicity and incremental construction: The method is easy to implement, streaming-friendly, and avoids heavy training. This can be valuable for constrained settings or rapid prototyping.
- Empirical gains over RI and unweighted count-based baselines: On MEN, PoP yields higher correlations than RI and naive counts across context window sizes, with a small parameter study exploring m and s.
- Practical observation on similarity measures: The pre-/post-PPMI shift from Kendall’s τb to Pearson’s r is reasonable given distributional changes, and the paper documents this empirically.
- Speed potential: Although not quantified, applying weighting in low dimensions could be advantageous compared to computing PMI/PPMI on a high-dimensional co-occurrence matrix.

Weaknesses
- Very limited evaluation and weak baselines: Results are reported only on MEN, using a single corpus. There are no comparisons to strong, established baselines trained on the same data (e.g., SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD with tuned hyperparameters), no coverage of other standard intrinsic datasets (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words), and no extrinsic evaluations. MEN-only evidence is insufficient to support broad claims of competitiveness with neural embeddings.
- Unfounded or incorrect claims re: PPMI and RI: The paper asserts RI-induced vectors cannot be PPMI-weighted because row/column sums are “always 0.” In RI, the projection matrix has zero mean in expectation, but projected vectors do not have exactly zero sums; more importantly, PMI/PPMI has a probabilistic interpretation tied to co-occurrence counts. Applying PPMI directly to a projected space (PoP) produces quantities that generally no longer correspond to probabilities. The paper reframes PMI as a variance-stabilizing transform, but this abandons PMI’s semantics without providing theoretical justification that PPMI-on-projection is well-founded or comparable to PPMI-on-counts followed by dimensionality reduction.
- Missing related work and positioning: The method is close in spirit to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing (hashing trick) with non-negative updates; these literatures study collisions, variance, and recovery guarantees for frequency and co-occurrence estimation. The paper does not compare against or even cite this line of work, nor does it clearly differentiate PoP from known non-negative/sparse projection methods.
- No rigorous theoretical analysis: The paper explicitly lacks distortion/error guarantees (JL-type or otherwise) and offers only qualitative claims. Given the method’s novelty rests on positive-only projections combined with PPMI in projected space, stronger theoretical grounding (or at least controlled empirical validation across tasks and datasets) is needed to justify the approach.
- Insufficient empirical support for efficiency claims: There are no wall-clock benchmarks, memory footprints, or throughput comparisons against established baselines (e.g., SGNS with negative sampling on the same corpus/hardware). Claims about neural training being prohibitively slow are outdated relative to modern implementations and smaller corpora (UKWaC), undermining the core motivation.
- Methodological concerns: 
  - The use of Kendall’s τb as a vector similarity is unconventional in distributional semantics; a comparison to cosine/Spearman beyond anecdotal mention would help. 
  - Parameter studies are limited; significance testing is absent; variance over runs is only partially shown. 
  - Some statements are imprecise or inaccurate (e.g., “RI retains distances,” deterministically; expectation vs realization confusion).
- Reproducibility: Code and artifacts are promised “if accepted,” which is not satisfactory for review. Clear hyperparameters, preprocessing, and exact splits/settings are not fully specified; tuning on MEN is mentioned later, raising potential overfitting concerns.

Questions to authors
- Theory/semantics: What is the probabilistic or statistical justification for applying PPMI to the projected PoP space? Since projection mixes counts, the PPMI formula no longer reflects joint/ marginal probabilities. Why is this preferable to (i) applying PPMI in the count space followed by dimensionality reduction (e.g., SVD or random projection), or (ii) learning on the count space directly?
- Baselines: Please provide comparisons on the same corpus/hardware to SGNS (word2vec) and GloVe with standard hyperparameters; also include PPMI+SVD, and feature hashing / Count-Min Sketch-based baselines. How does PoP fare on SimLex-999, WordSim-353, and Rare-Word datasets? Any extrinsic task (e.g., similarity/relatedness in downstream tasks)?
- Efficiency: Report wall-clock training times and peak memory for PoP (+PPMI), PPMI+SVD, SGNS, and GloVe on UKWaC; include sensitivity to vocabulary size and window size.
- Relation to Count-Min/feature hashing: How does PoP differ from standard Count-Min Sketch schemes (multiple hash functions, positive updates) or unsigned feature hashing? Do your parameters map to known error bounds (collisions) from that literature? Could you leverage those bounds to justify your choice of m and s?
- Robustness: Provide statistical significance tests for gains over baselines; show variance across more seeds and multiple corpora. Can you quantify the impact of stopword removal/frequency cutoffs and report a consistent protocol?

Overall assessment
The core idea (non-negative sparse random projections enabling low-dimensional weighting) is simple and potentially useful, but the current submission lacks rigorous theoretical support and relies on a very narrow evaluation with questionable comparisons and some incorrect claims about existing methods. Without stronger baselines, broader benchmarks, efficiency measurements, and clearer connection to established sketching/feature-hashing literature (and/or a solid theoretical account), I cannot recommend acceptance at this time.
