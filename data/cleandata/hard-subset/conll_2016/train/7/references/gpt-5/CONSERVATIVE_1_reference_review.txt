Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with nonnegative integer entries. The key claim is that, unlike standard random indexing (RI) with zero-mean projections, PoP produces projected representations for which PPMI weighting is still “applicable,” enabling an incremental, low-dimensional, streaming construction of word representations.
- The authors report results on MEN where PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ρ≈0.75 (and up to 0.78 with tuning), comparable to classic PPMI+SVD/neural baselines, at substantially lower computational cost. They also present exploratory analyses of design choices (dimension m, sparsity s, similarity metric: Kendall pre-PPMI vs Pearson post-PPMI).
- The paper argues that PoP provides practical efficiency advantages over both dense count-based models and neural embeddings, while outperforming RI/count baselines.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The approach is straightforward to implement, compatible with streaming/incremental updates, and avoids heavy training/factorization. For resource-constrained settings, this is a relevant design point.
- Efficiency motivation: The paper clearly motivates the cost/latency issues of dense PPMI and neural embedding training, and proposes a method designed to be lightweight. The parameter studies (m, s) and the discussion of Kendall vs Pearson are useful for practitioners.
- Empirical signal on MEN: The reported MEN ρ scores for PoP+PPMI+Pearson are in the range of classic strong baselines, suggesting that the method is at least competitive on a standard relatedness benchmark under the chosen setup.
- Incremental construction: Being able to construct a usable semantic space online without storing gigantic co-occurrence matrices is an attractive property, and worth exploring further.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical justification is missing: The paper explicitly states it cannot provide a mathematical account of distortion/error (δ) under PoP. Beyond the analogy to randomized methods and JL-type intuitions, there is no analysis of how positive-only sparse projections affect co-occurrence geometry, distance preservation, or bias/variance. This is a core gap for a projection-based contribution intended as a replacement for established techniques.
- Questionable use of PPMI post-projection: PMI/PPMI are defined on joint and marginal probabilities derived from counts. After a random projection, entries no longer correspond to counts and can reflect collisions/interference. Applying PMI on the projected matrix requires a principled justification of what the “probabilities” mean and what bias is introduced. The claim that PPMI is “undefined” for RI because of zero-mean entries oversimplifies the issue; the more fundamental problem is interpretability of PMI on non-count quantities. A rigorous comparison to (a) applying PPMI before projection, and (b) established sketching methods for counts (e.g., Count-Min/Count-Sketch, feature hashing) that have known error bounds is missing.
- Limited and outdated evaluation: Only MEN (relatedness) on UKWaC is used. Modern standards require multiple intrinsic benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare Words) and, crucially, extrinsic tasks (e.g., analogy, similarity-in-context, or downstream tasks). No statistical significance testing across runs is reported. The comparison set omits strong and common baselines (SGNS, GloVe, fastText, PPMI+SVD with tuned hyperparameters, and feature hashing/Count-Min sketches).
- Inconsistent similarity measures and fairness of comparisons: The method uses Kendall’s τb pre-PPMI and switches to Pearson post-PPMI, while baselines (count, RI) are primarily evaluated with cosine. This confounds interpretability of gains. If the choice of similarity function is a key driver, baselines should be re-run under the same metric conditions for fair comparison.
- Efficiency claims are unquantified: The paper asserts “seconds or minutes” without reporting wall-clock times, memory footprints, hardware details, or complexity as a function of vocabulary/context size. Without concrete measurements against optimized baselines (PPMI+SVD, SGNS with typical settings), the efficiency advantage remains anecdotal.
- Novelty positioning and related work: The proposal is closely related to known sketching and feature hashing techniques that produce nonnegative sketches and support streaming updates. The paper does not compare with nor cite that body of work in any depth, nor clarify what PoP adds beyond established sketching guarantees. Similarly, the claim that RI cannot be weighted ignores the option to weight before projection.
- Reproducibility: Code and embeddings are only promised upon acceptance. Many preprocessing and training details are underspecified (lemmatization/POS pipeline, windowing, subsampling, cutoff thresholds, random seeds). Figures are referenced but precise numeric results (means/SDs, N) and significance are not systematically reported.

Questions to authors
- What is the formal justification for computing PMI/PPMI on a projected matrix whose entries are not counts? Can you derive bias/variance or consistency properties of PMI estimates under PoP? How does this compare to applying PPMI before projection, or to Count-Min/Count-Sketch with known error bounds?
- Please provide comprehensive, end-to-end efficiency measurements: wall-clock time, peak memory, and CPU/GPU specs, for constructing spaces on UKWaC and at larger scales, compared to (i) dense PPMI+SVD, (ii) RI, (iii) SGNS, and (iv) feature hashing/Count-Min sketches.
- Can you expand evaluation to multiple benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, RW) and at least one extrinsic task? Include strong neural baselines and PPMI+SVD with standard tuning. Report means, variance across ≥10 runs, and significance tests.
- How is s chosen in practice to ensure nonzero rows without introducing bias? Are index vectors sampled with replacement? What is the collision rate empirically, and how does it affect performance?
- Please release code, preprocessing scripts, and exact hyperparameters to enable reproducibility.

Overall, while the idea is practical and the initial signal on MEN is encouraging, the paper currently lacks theoretical grounding, rigorous and comprehensive evaluation, fair comparisons, and quantified efficiency claims. I cannot recommend acceptance in its present form.
