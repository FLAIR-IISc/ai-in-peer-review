Summary and main contributions
- Proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly non-negative entries that enables incremental construction of low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces while remaining compatible with PPMI and related weighting schemes.
- Provides an empirical recipe for similarity computation tailored to PoP: Kendall’s τb for raw PoP spaces (non-Gaussian) and Pearson correlation after PPMI (Gaussian-like), showing strong gains over RI and count-based baselines on MEN with far lower compute.
- Offers an exploration of key parameters (projected dimension m and sparsity s/number of nonzeros), and argues that PoP+PPMI reaches performance comparable to neural embeddings without training, with the practical advantage of streaming/incremental construction.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: Using a positive-only random projection to preserve non-negativity and make post-projection association weighting feasible is an elegant twist on RI/feature hashing. The explicit E(R)>0 choice breaks with the standard signed projections and unlocks efficient PPMI at low dimension. This is an original and potentially impactful idea for scalable distributional semantics.
- Practicality and efficiency: The method is streaming, simple to implement, and avoids the heavy cost of building full co-occurrence matrices or training neural models. The claim that PPMI becomes dramatically cheaper in m≈10^3–10^4 dimensions (versus n≫10^5 contexts) is compelling for large-scale scenarios.
- Clear empirical behaviors and actionable guidance: The paper provides systematic trends across m and number of nonzeros, along with the switch from Kendall to Pearson after PPMI. This gives practitioners tangible settings (e.g., 1–2 nonzeros with PPMI; τb pre-PPMI) and cautions about orthogonality and noise as s grows.
- Competitive accuracy with tiny cost: On MEN, PoP+PPMI+Pearson reaches ≈0.75–0.78, in the ballpark of strong PPMI+SVD and some neural embeddings, but without training and with low memory and compute footprint.
- Incrementality: The method naturally supports dynamic updates—an under-served need relative to many batch-only embedding methods.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope is too narrow: Results are only on MEN with UKWaC, and a small target vocabulary (751 lemmas). This is insufficient to establish robustness. Strong baselines (SGNS, GloVe, fastText; PPMI+SVD; dependency-based contexts) on multiple benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim, Rare Words, SimVerb, and at least one downstream task) are needed to substantiate claims of competitiveness.
- Missing comparisons to closely related randomized schemes: The approach is very close in spirit to feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch (CMS) for co-occurrence estimation (non-negative updates, collisions, streaming). CMS has known error bounds and has been used for scalable co-occurrence statistics and PMI-like estimates. A direct comparison (accuracy/memory/time), and a discussion of similarities/differences (e.g., multi-hash, overestimation bias, error guarantees) are necessary to position novelty.
- Theoretical underpinnings are absent: The paper openly lacks distortion/error bounds or concentration results for the proposed distribution (Eq. 2), and provides only qualitative analogies. Even preliminary bounds (e.g., on inner product or rank-correlation distortion as a function of m and sparsity) or a link to known sketching results would strengthen the contribution.
- Ambiguity in the projection distribution: Equation (2) and the value-generation process are unclear (the meaning of “⌊1 U^α⌋” vs “⌊U^α⌋”, expected number/variance of nonzeros per row, and the value distribution of nonzero entries). Reproducibility and interpretability hinge on specifying how positions and magnitudes are sampled.
- Probabilistic meaning of PPMI after projection: Applying PPMI on a mixed, projected space no longer corresponds to true pointwise mutual information of original events. While the paper reframes PPMI as a variance-stabilizing transform, the loss of a clean probabilistic interpretation (and the bias induced by collisions) deserves analysis. Comparisons to PPMI computed on exact counts (with/without SVD) at similar memory/time budgets would clarify trade-offs.
- Claims about RI and PPMI: The statement that RI “cannot be adjusted and transformed using PPMI” is too categorical. While it’s ill-defined to apply PPMI on signed projected values, one can apply weighting pre-projection (expensive) or use non-negative sketches. Clarifying that PoP specifically enables post-projection PPMI (and why that’s desirable) would avoid overclaiming.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify Eq. (2) precisely: how many nonzeros per row are sampled (in expectation and in practice)? How are indices chosen (with/without replacement)? What is the exact distribution of nonzero magnitudes and why α=0.5?
- How does PoP compare empirically to Count-Min Sketch and feature hashing baselines for co-occurrence estimation with PPMI (similar memory and update cost)? Can you report MEN scores, wall-clock, and memory?
- Can you provide runtime and memory numbers (and throughput for streaming updates) vs: (a) dense count matrix + PPMI(+SVD); (b) SGNS/GloVe training; (c) RI?
- How are probabilities estimated for PPMI in projected space? Do you treat projected dimensions as “pseudo-contexts” and compute marginals there? How sensitive are results to the number of target types included when forming the normalizers in Eq. (3)?
- Beyond MEN, can you add at least SimLex-999 and a downstream task (e.g., text classification or similarity search) to demonstrate robustness?

Overall assessment
- This is a fresh and promising idea with clear practical appeal. Despite the limited evaluation and missing theoretical backing, I find the conceptual novelty and efficiency benefits compelling. With clarifications and stronger baselines (especially vs CMS/feature hashing), this could be impactful.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing clarity of the projection distribution and adding at least one strong sketching baseline and broader evaluation.
