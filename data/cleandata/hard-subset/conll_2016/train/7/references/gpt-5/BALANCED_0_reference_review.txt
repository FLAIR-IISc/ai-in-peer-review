Summary
This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme that maps high-dimensional count-based co-occurrence matrices into a much lower-dimensional space using random index vectors with only positive integer entries. Because projected entries remain nonnegative and sums are positive, the authors argue that standard association weighting such as PPMI can be applied directly in the low-dimensional space, yielding accuracy competitive with neural embeddings at a fraction of the cost. The paper evaluates PoP on MEN relatedness using UKWaC, explores similarity measures (Kendall’s τb vs Pearson’s r), and reports parameter studies over dimensionality and sparsity.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical recipe for building low-dimensional, incrementally updatable semantic spaces via positive-only sparse random projections that can be directly PPMI-weighted, with compelling efficiency claims.
2) Empirical finding that PPMI applied after positive-only projection, with Pearson correlation for similarity, attains competitive performance on MEN (≈0.75–0.78) without heavy training.
3) A parameter and similarity-measure study (dimensionality m, sparsity s, Kendall vs Pearson) illuminating when the approach works best.

Strengths
- Efficiency and simplicity: The approach reduces heavy-weight PPMI computation on massive high-dimensional co-occurrence tables to PPMI on low-dimensional projected counts. The incremental construction and elimination of SVD/neural training are attractive for practitioners; the reported “seconds/minutes” build time is promising.
- Clear empirical narrative: The paper separates unweighted PoP (Kendall), RI, dense PPMI, and PoP+PPMI, making it easy to see where the gains come from. The switch from Kendall (pre-PPMI) to Pearson (post-PPMI) is justified and empirically validated.
- Parameter exploration: The studies over m and s, and discussion of non-orthogonality/variance trade-offs, provide useful practical guidance and suggest the method’s robustness.
- Competitive accuracy on MEN: PoP+PPMI+Pearson approaches strong baselines on a standard benchmark, with an ablation story (context window size, PPMI vs PMI, SVD) that aligns with prior literature.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and connections to existing sketching/hashing methods: The proposed mechanism is extremely close to known positive-only randomized summarization methods (feature hashing/the “hashing trick,” Count-Min Sketch), which map contexts to a small number of bins and increment counts, after which one can compute PPMI over hashed “pseudo-contexts.” The paper does not cite or compare to these baselines. The main claimed novelty—“PPMI-enabled random projections”—appears to be a rediscovery/variant of hashing-based co-occurrence compression rather than a fundamentally new projection family.
- Limited evaluation: Results are only on MEN with a single corpus (UKWaC). No comparisons to strong neural baselines trained on the same data (e.g., SGNS, GloVe) with matched preprocessing; no additional word similarity/relatedness sets (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare-Word), no analogy or downstream tasks, no OOV/low-frequency analysis. This leaves generality and competitiveness unclear.
- Incomplete efficiency evidence: While the method should be fast, the paper lacks wall-clock time, memory, and throughput measurements versus dense PPMI+SVD, RI, hashing/CMS, and SGNS (with and without negative sampling). The proposed Kendall-based similarity is O(m log m) vs cosine O(m) and may be costly at scale; empirical costs are not reported.
- Theoretical justification is thin: The paper explicitly provides no analysis of how PoP affects PMI estimates, marginal preservation, or distortion bounds (δ). Given that PMI is applied to projected “pseudo-contexts,” a discussion of bias/variance relative to true PMI and comparisons with known theory for sketching/counting would strengthen the claims.
- Questionable claims about RI and PPMI: The argument that RI cannot be PPMI-weighted because E(R)=0 and row/column sums are zero is not convincing. What blocks PPMI is the presence of negative projected entries and loss of nonnegativity/interpretability, not that row/column sums are exactly zero. In contrast, positive hashing/sketching trivially yields nonnegative pseudo-counts. Clarifying this and comparing to simple baselines (e.g., hash contexts into m bins, count, then PPMI) is essential.
- Reproducibility: Best-score settings involve additional preprocessing (stopwords, frequency cutoffs, scaling as per Levy et al.), but the paper lacks full hyperparameter and preprocessing details, and code is promised only upon acceptance.

Questions to authors
- How does PoP differ formally and empirically from feature hashing and Count-Min Sketch with k hash functions mapping contexts into m bins (increment-only), followed by PPMI on the hashed co-occurrence matrix? Please add citations and a direct comparison.
- Can you report wall-clock time, memory, and construction throughput for PoP vs: (1) dense PPMI, (2) dense PPMI+SVD, (3) RI, (4) CMS/hash+PPMI, (5) SGNS/GloVe, all on UKWaC with matched preprocessing?
- How stable are results across datasets (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW), corpora sizes, and subword/OOV handling?
- What is the interpretability of “pseudo-contexts” after projection? Do you have evidence that marginals or PMI ranks are approximately preserved under PoP?
- For Kendall’s τb vs cosine vs Pearson: can you provide a systematic comparison across unweighted and PPMI-weighted PoP for multiple m and s, and include computational costs for large-scale nearest-neighbor queries?

Recommendation
Weak Reject. The practical idea is appealing and results are promising, but the paper underplays close prior art in hashing/sketching, lacks rigorous or broad evaluation and efficiency measurements, and makes some debatable claims regarding RI/PPMI. Strengthening novelty positioning and substantially expanding empirical comparisons would make this a strong submission.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reposition PoP in the context of feature hashing/Count-Min Sketch and compare directly (accuracy and efficiency).
- Add broader evaluations and runtime/memory measurements; include SGNS/GloVe trained on the same data.
- Provide more precise algorithmic details and release code; include a development set to avoid tuning on MEN test.
- If possible, offer simple theoretical insight into bias/variance of PMI after projection, or empirical calibration plots comparing true PMI ranks vs PoP+PPMI ranks.
