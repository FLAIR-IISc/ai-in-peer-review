Summary and main contributions
- Introduces Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse random projection scheme with strictly nonnegative integer entries that enables incremental construction of low-dimensional semantic spaces which remain compatible with count-based weighting schemes like PPMI.
- Argues for using Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure before weighting (non-Gaussian projected spaces) and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussianized weights), providing an interpretable link between the distributional assumptions of the vectors and the similarity metric.
- Empirically shows on MEN (UKWaC) that PoP outperforms RI and raw count spaces; with PPMI and Pearson correlation, PoP reaches ρ≈0.75 at m≥3000, approaching reported neural embeddings, while being far cheaper and incremental. Provides parameter studies over dimensionality and sparsity.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with practical implications: Using positive-only sparse projections so that post-projection PPMI remains well-defined is a simple but bold idea that could unlock fast, streaming-compatible, low-memory semantic spaces with competitive quality. This is a clear, distinctive contribution beyond classic RI/feature hashing.
- Efficient, incremental construction: The method builds the target space on the fly without expensive training or high-dimensional intermediate counts, fitting scenarios where resources or latency matter. The incremental property is valuable and differentiates PoP from many embedding pipelines.
- Distribution-aware similarity choice: The move from Kendall’s τb (nonparametric, pre-weighting) to Pearson (post-PPMI) is thoughtful and supported by histograms, providing a clean rationale for metric selection tied to vector distributions. This is a nice, underexplored angle that improves interpretability.
- Empirical gains over RI and counts, with parameter analysis: Clear, repeated wins over RI and counts on MEN; sensible studies on m and sparsity s, including discussion of non-orthogonality and its effect on distortions, show understanding of the method’s behavior.
- Simplicity and extensibility: The approach is easy to implement and likely to combine well with other weighting/normalization steps, and even to serve as a fast preprocessor for downstream models.

Weaknesses
- Limited and narrow evaluation: Only MEN on a single corpus (UKWaC). No cross-corpus tests, no diverse similarity/relatedness sets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353, RareWords), no analogy or downstream tasks, no robustness to domain shift. This makes it hard to assess generality.
- Missing strong baselines and fairness concerns: The comparison omits SGNS/GloVe/fastText trained on the same data and preprocessing, and a compressed-count baseline such as Count-Min Sketch + PPMI or other nonnegative sketches. The claim that RI cannot be PPMI-weighted is framed narrowly; while directly applying PPMI to signed RI outputs is ill-posed, a fair comparison would consider (i) PMI before projection plus projection, (ii) shifting/rectifying, or (iii) nonnegative hashing/sketching compatible with PPMI.
- Theoretical underpinnings are thin: No concentration or distortion guarantees, no analysis of bias introduced by computing PPMI on compressed counts. The assertion σ²δ≈1/m is imported from related work without proof for this specific distribution (heavy-tailed positive entries), and the choice α=0.5 is not justified. Given the paper’s emphasis on a new projection family, even partial bounds or bias/variance analysis for marginals and PMI would strengthen the case.
- Similarity computation scalability: Kendall’s τb for nearest-neighbor search is atypical and potentially expensive in large vocabularies. While m is small, O(m log m) per pair and tie handling could still be a bottleneck; no indexing/search strategy or runtime profiling is provided. After PPMI, Pearson is more standard, but the story mixes metrics and might complicate deployment.
- Reproducibility and reporting details: Some preprocessing choices are under-specified (e.g., tokenization/lemmatization details, vocabulary thresholds, context weighting), and there are no concrete runtime/memory numbers. The promise to release code “if accepted” reduces reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines and fairness: Can you add comparisons to (a) SGNS/GloVe/fastText trained on UKWaC with matched preprocessing and report quality, runtime, and memory; and (b) compressed-count baselines like Count-Min Sketch + PPMI, feature hashing with nonnegative entries + PPMI, and “PPMI then projection” variants?
- Theory and bias: What is the expected bias of PPMI computed on PoP-compressed counts relative to true PPMI? Can you provide at least a sketch of concentration results for marginals and pairwise counts under your positive-only projection? Any empirical calibration (e.g., correlation between true and projected PMI) would help.
- Distribution and α: What is the empirical benefit of the heavy-tailed integer draws (⌊1/U^α⌋) over using constant 1 for nonzeros? Please ablate α, and report whether results materially change with α∈{0,0.25,0.5,0.75,1} or with fixed weights.
- Similarity and scalability: How do build/query times scale for τb versus cosine/pearson in k-NN search over full vocabularies (e.g., 100k–1M types)? Can you index PoP vectors to enable fast retrieval, and what are the measured throughputs?
- Robustness and scope: How does PoP perform on additional benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim353, RW, analogy) and corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, Common Crawl slices)? How stable are results across random seeds and parameter ranges?
- Clarification on RI + PPMI claim: Could you precisely state the limitation and provide a controlled experiment where RI (or a nonnegative variant) is combined with PPMI in the most favorable way you can devise, to isolate PoP’s advantage?

Overall assessment: This is a bold, novel direction with clear practical promise; despite the limited evaluation and theoretical gaps, I am inclined to be positive if the authors can shore up baselines and provide more evidence on robustness and scalability.
