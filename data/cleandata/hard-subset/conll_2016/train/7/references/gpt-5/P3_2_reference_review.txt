Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a sparse, nonnegative random projection scheme for incremental construction of distributional semantic spaces. Each context type is assigned an m-dimensional index vector with a few positive integer entries; word vectors are formed by summing the indices of their observed contexts. Because the projection is nonnegative, the resulting compressed co-occurrence matrix can be post-weighted with PPMI, which the authors argue is infeasible for standard Random Indexing (RI) due to signed projections. For similarity, Kendall’s τb is proposed for raw PoP vectors (non-Gaussian), and Pearson’s r after PPMI (Gaussian-like). On MEN-3000 with UKWaC, PoP outperforms RI and count baselines; PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves Spearman ρ ≈ 0.75 with small models and no neural training.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple nonnegative sparse random projection scheme that enables PPMI (and other weightings) to be applied after sketching, with incremental updates.
2) An empirical study of similarity measures and PoP hyperparameters (m, sparsity), showing competitive MEN scores with low computational cost.
3) A practical argument for efficiency: computing PPMI on low-dimensional sketches rather than on large sparse co-occurrence matrices.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The method is easy to implement (two-line streaming update), requires no training, and supports incremental construction. This is attractive for rapid prototyping and for low-resource settings where training SGNS/GloVe is costly.
- Empirical improvements over RI and raw counts: Across window sizes, PoP outperforms RI and count-based models; with PPMI+Pearson it reaches ρ ≈ 0.75 on MEN, competitive with some neural baselines reported in prior work.
- Enabling weighting after compression: Allowing PPMI at low dimensionality is a useful idea; the paper demonstrates that post-weighting the sketch substantially boosts accuracy and that Pearson’s r is preferable after PPMI.
- Hyperparameter exploration and insights: The effect of m and sparsity s (number of nonzeros) is thoughtfully explored, including the trade-off between sparsity, collisions, and performance. The recommendation of very sparse signatures (1–2 nonzeros) for PPMI is actionable.
- Efficiency potential: Although not quantified, the approach plausibly reduces both time and memory (constant m, streaming updates), and τb’s O(m log m) is small for m≈10^3.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to known sketching methods: The core mechanism is very close to Count-Min Sketch/feature hashing applied to word-context counts (nonnegative, collision-based accumulation). Prior work has sketched co-occurrence matrices and then applied weighting or dimensionality reduction. The paper neither positions PoP against Count-Min/Count-Sketch/feature hashing baselines nor analyzes collision bias. As framed, the contribution feels incremental.
- Insufficient evaluation: Only MEN-3000 is reported. No results on complementary datasets (WS-353, RG-65, SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, Rare Words), no intrinsic/extrinsic tasks (e.g., analogy, similarity-in-context), and no controlled comparisons to strong baselines trained on the same corpus (SGNS, GloVe, PPMI+SVD). The claim of competitiveness with neural methods relies on numbers from other papers.
- Methodological and theoretical gaps: 
  • Equation (2) is underspecified (the exact distribution, expected number of nonzeros per row, and role of α are unclear). 
  • The assertion that RI rows/columns sums are “always 0” is incorrect; expectation is zero but sums are not deterministically zero. The more precise point is that negative values make PMI ill-defined, but one could consider shifts or alternative weighting on signed sketches. 
  • The cosine–Pearson equivalence argument is not rigorous; cosine equals correlation only after centering and scaling.
  • No analysis is provided for the bias and variance of PMI estimating on sketched buckets; this is important given collisions.
- Scalability and metric choice concerns: Kendall’s τb is computationally heavier than cosine for large-scale nearest neighbor search. While m is small, using τb for millions of pairwise similarities is still nontrivial. The paper evaluates only on 3k pairs, not on large neighborhood retrieval.
- Claims about “intractability” of PPMI on dense counts and negligible SVD gains are overstated: Modern sparse implementations compute PPMI and truncated SVD efficiently on vocabularies of 10^5–10^6 with cutoffs. The paper should provide a fair runtime/memory comparison and concrete wall-clock numbers.
- Reproducibility: Code and embeddings are promised “if accepted.” Hyperparameters used for best scores (m, s, window, cutoffs) are not fully specified; no seeds, hardware, or timing numbers are reported.

Questions to authors
1) Relation to Count-Min/feature hashing: How does PoP differ theoretically and empirically from Count-Min Sketch or unsigned feature hashing of word-context co-occurrences followed by PPMI? Please include this baseline and discuss collision bias and variance.
2) Equation (2) details: Please formalize the distribution. What is tij’s exact pmf, how do you ensure exactly k nonzeros per row (is s a target probability or fixed count), and what motivates α=0.5? Do the integer magnitudes matter empirically vs all-ones signatures?
3) PMI on sketched buckets: What is the theoretical relationship between PMI computed on compressed buckets and PMI in the original space? Under what assumptions on collisions does PoP+PPMI approximate PPMI on the full co-occurrence matrix?
4) Efficiency claims: Please report memory footprint, indexing/build time, and PPMI computation time for PoP vs (a) sparse count matrix + PPMI, and (b) SGNS/GloVe trained on UKWaC, all on the same hardware. Also report similarity computation costs (τb and Pearson) and the cost of nearest-neighbor search.
5) Baselines and datasets: Can you add results on standard similarity/relatedness datasets (WS-353, SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500, RW) and an apples-to-apples comparison to PPMI+SVD, SGNS, and GloVe trained on UKWaC with matching preprocessing?
6) Metric choice: τb is unusual for distributional semantics. Have you compared cosine and Spearman rank cosine on PoP and PoP+PPMI? For large-scale retrieval, how do you plan to make τb tractable?
7) Fairness of the RI comparison: RI is typically evaluated with tuning of signature length and sparsity and often combined with weighting or normalization. Did you try unsigned RI (nonnegative) or RI followed by suitable weighting?

Overall, the idea is practical and yields promising numbers on MEN, but the paper’s novelty claims are weakened by missing connections to established sketching methods and the limited evaluation. With stronger baselines, more datasets, clarity on the projection distribution, and a principled treatment of PMI-on-sketches (or at least a comparison to Count-Min+PPMI), the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
