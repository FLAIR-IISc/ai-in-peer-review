Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing low-dimensional distributional semantic spaces using index vectors with strictly positive integer entries. Unlike prior random indexing variants that rely on zero-mean projections, PoP preserves strictly positive entries in the projected co-occurrence matrix, enabling downstream weighting with PPMI at low dimensionality. The paper evaluates PoP on the MEN relatedness benchmark using UKWaC, compares against count-based models and Random Indexing (RI), and studies choices of similarity metrics (Kendall’s τb vs Pearson’s r) and parameters (dimensionality m, sparsity s).

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Methodological: Introduction of a random projection family with positive-only entries that allows PPMI weighting post-projection, enabling incremental, memory- and compute-efficient construction of predictive spaces.
2) Empirical: Evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains competitive MEN correlations (≈0.75) with substantially reduced computation, and that unweighted PoP outperforms RI on MEN; analysis of sensitivity to m, s, and choice of similarity measure.
3) Practical recipe: A clear, simple pipeline (PoP → PPMI → Pearson) and guidance on parameter ranges for building efficient word spaces.

Strengths (ordered by importance)
1) Enabling PPMI after projection with a simple modification. The core novelty—using positive-only sparse random projections—addresses a known limitation of RI-like methods (zero-mean projections preclude standard PMI/PPMI). This opens a practical path to apply strong weighting schemes on compact representations, with plausible impact for scalable semantic space construction.

2) Empirical ablations and parameter studies. The paper systematically examines dimensionality (m), sparsity (s), and similarity metrics, and reports stability across multiple runs. The observed improvements from PoP over RI (for unweighted spaces), and the transition to Pearson’s r after PPMI when distributions become Gaussian-like, are consistent and well-motivated.

3) Efficiency motivation and incremental construction. The proposed two-step construction (assign random index vectors to contexts; accumulate into word vectors online) is incremental and avoids costly SVD or NN training. Even without wall-clock tables, the method’s operations are lightweight, and PPMI on m ≪ n is inherently more tractable.

4) Clarity of exposition for the method. The transformation is expressed cleanly in matrix form; the distribution of projection entries, the rationale for τb in non-Gaussian spaces, and the behavior with s and m are explained with intuition and references.

Weaknesses (ordered by importance)
1) Limited and potentially non-comparable evaluation. Results are reported only on MEN and a single corpus (UKWaC). Comparisons to RI and count-based models use cosine for baselines versus τb for PoP, and PoP+PPMI+Pearson versus dense PPMI+cosine, which may confound gains attributable to the similarity function. Stronger baselines (e.g., SGNS/GloVe with tuned hyperparameters, PPMI+SVD at comparable dimension) and additional benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare-Word) are missing.

2) Lack of theoretical analysis. The paper explicitly lacks guarantees or bounds analogous to Johnson–Lindenstrauss-type results or bias/variance characterizations of PMI computed on projected features. The distribution tij := ⌊U^(-α)⌋ with α=0.5 has no justification or sensitivity study beyond α fixed; the impact on concentration or distance preservation is unclear.

3) Questionable validity of PMI on projected “pseudo-contexts.” PPMI is defined on word-context co-occurrence counts; after projection, columns aggregate many contexts stochastically. While empirically helpful, it is not established what PMI on these mixed features represents, nor whether it systematically approximates PMI on the original space. This raises interpretability and soundness concerns.

4) Missing efficiency measurements. Claims that PoP builds models in “seconds/minutes” are not supported with wall-clock, memory, or asymptotic cost comparisons versus count+PPMI, PPMI+SVD, SGNS, or GloVe. Scalability with vocabulary and corpus size, and the cost of τb at retrieval time, are not quantified.

5) Reproducibility gaps. Code is promised post-acceptance. Critical settings (e.g., α choice, s schedules, handling of stopwords, vocabulary thresholds, tokenization/lemmatization specifics, hardware, exact τb implementation) are insufficiently detailed. Statistical significance testing and full variance reporting (beyond min/max shading) are absent.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results on additional benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353, Rare-Word) and at least one extrinsic task (e.g., analogy, downstream text classification) to substantiate generality?
- Can you re-run baselines with matched similarity functions: τb for RI and count-based, and Pearson for all PPMI-weighted models, to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons?
- Please provide wall-clock runtime and peak memory for building models and computing similarities, versus dense PPMI (+/- SVD) and SGNS/GloVe, across a range of vocabularies and m.
- What is the rationale for α=0.5 in the projection value distribution? How sensitive are results to α, and to using fixed small integers (e.g., 1s) instead of heavy-tailed positive entries?
- How do you interpret PPMI on projected features? Does PoP+PPMI approximate PPMI on the original co-occurrence matrix in any sense (e.g., in expectation)? Any empirical study comparing PMI matrices before/after projection?
- Could you explore signed-shifted projections (e.g., zero-mean with positive shift) to balance distance preservation with PMI applicability?
- Can you release code and preprocessing details for reproducibility during the rebuttal, or at least include precise pseudocode and all hyperparameters?

Overall, the idea is simple and potentially useful, with encouraging preliminary results and practical advantages. However, the current submission would benefit from broader, fairer evaluations, basic runtime evidence, and at least partial theoretical or empirical justification of PPMI on projected features.
