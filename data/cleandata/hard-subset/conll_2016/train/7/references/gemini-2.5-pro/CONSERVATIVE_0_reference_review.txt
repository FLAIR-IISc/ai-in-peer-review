**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projections" (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix populated with sparse, positive integer values, in contrast to established methods like Random Indexing (RI) which use zero-centered distributions.

The primary contribution is the development of a random projection technique that produces vectors amenable to subsequent weighting transformations, most notably Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Standard RI vectors cannot be used with PPMI because their component sums are zero. PoP overcomes this limitation, enabling the application of a powerful and well-established weighting scheme to a low-dimensional space. This effectively combines the computational efficiency of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-weighted models, bypassing the need to compute PPMI on a high-dimensional, sparse co-occurrence matrix.

A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this PoP+PPMI combination achieves competitive results on a word relatedness task, performing on par with dense PPMI models while being significantly faster to compute.

**Strengths**

1.  **Practical Efficiency:** The main strength of the proposed method lies in its computational efficiency. By performing dimensionality reduction *before* applying PPMI, the authors address a major bottleneck in traditional count-based distributional semantics. The ability to build models incrementally and then apply a powerful weighting scheme to a small (e.g., m=1000) matrix is a significant practical advantage over methods that require SVD or PPMI calculation on matrices with tens or hundreds of thousands of dimensions.

2.  **Novel Combination of Established Concepts:** The paper presents a clever synthesis of two well-known lines of work: random projections (like RI) and count-based weighting (like PPMI). The insight that the choice of the random projection distribution is key to enabling subsequent transformations is novel and conceptually sound. It offers a bridge between these two families of models, inheriting desirable properties from both.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance (within its scope):** The results presented on the MEN relatedness task are compelling. The PoP+PPMI model significantly outperforms both the raw count-based baseline and the standard RI baseline. Achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75-0.78 demonstrates that the method is not just a theoretical curiosity but can produce high-quality semantic representations that are competitive with established techniques from the literature it cites.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient and Outdated Evaluation:** The paper's primary weakness is the narrowness of its empirical validation. The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on the MEN dataset). For a method proposed as a general technique for semantic space construction, this is insufficient. There is no evaluation on downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, text classification, chunking) where the utility of word embeddings is most critically assessed. Furthermore, the baselines are weak by current standards. While comparison to RI and dense PPMI is necessary, the lack of a direct, rigorously controlled comparison against standard neural baselines like word2vec (CBOW/Skip-gram) or GloVe, trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus, is a major omission. The authors claim comparability by citing scores from other papers, but this is not a substitute for a direct comparison under identical conditions.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors candidly admit that they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account" for their method. This is a significant concern. Established random projection methods like RI are backed by theoretical results such as the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which provides guarantees about distance preservation. PoP lacks any such justification. The entire method, including the choice of similarity metric (switching from Kendall's τb to Pearson's r), is motivated by empirical observation and statistical analogy rather than formal proof. The claim that PPMI transforms the PoP vector distributions into a "Gaussian-like" shape is supported only by a single histogram for one word, which is anecdotal evidence at best. Without a stronger theoretical foundation, it is difficult to understand the method's properties and limitations.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity and Ad-hoc Justifications:** The method introduces several hyperparameters (dimensionality `m`, sparsity `s`, number of non-zero elements, choice of similarity measure) whose interactions appear complex and not fully understood. The results in Section 3.3 are particularly concerning. For instance, the unweighted PoP model's performance decreases as dimensionality increases beyond m=2000, which is counter-intuitive and contradicts common wisdom in random projections. The authors' explanation regarding "distortions" is speculative. Similarly, the finding that PPMI-weighted models prefer very sparse index vectors (1-2 non-zero elements) while unweighted models prefer denser ones is an interesting empirical result, but the justification remains post-hoc. This sensitivity makes the method appear less robust and harder to deploy than established techniques.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The evaluation is limited to a single intrinsic task. Could you comment on why no downstream task evaluations were performed? Given that the goal is to create general-purpose semantic spaces, demonstrating their utility in tasks like named entity recognition or sentiment analysis would be critical for validating the method's effectiveness.

2.  Why were direct comparisons to standard neural embedding baselines like word2vec or GloVe, trained on the same UKWaC corpus with comparable hyperparameters, not included in the evaluation? Citing results from other papers is not a controlled comparison and weakens the claim of achieving competitive performance.

3.  The theoretical justification for the method is acknowledged as an open question. However, the choice to switch from Kendall's τb to Pearson's correlation hinges on the claim that PPMI induces a "Gaussian-like" distribution. Is there more systematic evidence for this distributional shift beyond the single-word histogram provided in Figure 3? For instance, have you performed normality tests (e.g., Shapiro-Wilk) across a large sample of word vectors before and after PPMI transformation? Without this, the choice of similarity metric seems arbitrary.
