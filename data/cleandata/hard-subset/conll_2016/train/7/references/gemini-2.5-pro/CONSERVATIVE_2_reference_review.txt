### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel incremental method for constructing semantic vector spaces, termed Positive-Only Projections (PoP). The central idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix populated with positive integers, in contrast to established random indexing (RI) methods that use zero-centered distributions. The primary claimed advantage of this approach is that the resulting low-dimensional vectors, unlike those from RI, can be subjected to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which are known to significantly improve the performance of count-based models.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of the Positive-Only Projection (PoP) method, a modification of random projection techniques that preserves the positive-valued nature of count vectors, thereby enabling the application of count-based weighting schemes like PPMI in a low-dimensional space.
2.  An empirical demonstration that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors yields strong performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task, achieving results comparable to more computationally expensive methods while retaining the efficiency of an incremental, projection-based approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Limitation of an Established Method:** The paper correctly identifies a significant drawback of standard Random Indexing (RI) and related techniques: their inability to be combined with powerful weighting schemes like PPMI due to their zero-centered nature. The proposed PoP method offers a simple and seemingly effective solution to this problem, which is a commendable and well-motivated goal.

2.  **Emphasis on Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for random projection methods is their computational efficiency and incremental nature. This work successfully combines this efficiency with the high performance typically associated with PPMI, which usually requires operations on very large, dense matrices. The ability to compute PPMI on vectors with a dimensionality of a few thousand rather than tens or hundreds of thousands is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance on the Chosen Task:** The results presented in Figure 2 and Section 3.3 are compelling for the single task evaluated. The PoP+PPMI+Pearson combination achieves a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset, which is a strong result and demonstrates the potential of the proposed pipeline. The ablation studies exploring the effects of dimensionality and sparsity (Figures 4 and 5) provide useful insights into the method's behavior.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The most critical weakness of this paper is the absence of a theoretical foundation for the proposed projection. The authors themselves state: "At this moment, unfortunately, we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ and for the results obtained by the PoP method". This is a major omission. Established random projection methods like RI are grounded in the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which provides guarantees about the preservation of distances. Without a similar theoretical underpinning, PoP appears to be more of an engineering heuristic than a principled method. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties this projection preserves, or how distortion is controlled. Citing the fact that RI also initially lacked justification is not a convincing defense in the current state of the field.

2.  **Limited and Insufficiently Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single semantic relatedness task (MEN). While performance on this benchmark is strong, it is insufficient to validate a new vector space construction method. The community standard for evaluating new embedding methods involves a wider suite of intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity, synonymy, analogy) and often extrinsic, downstream tasks. Furthermore, the comparison to baselines is not as rigorous as it should be. The performance of neural embeddings is cited from other papers rather than being produced from a controlled experiment on the same corpus and preprocessing pipeline. Crucially, the paper dismisses the well-established PPMI+SVD baseline by stating that SVD offers "negligible improvements." This contradicts significant findings in the literature (e.g., Levy and Goldberg, 2014) where SVD is shown to be a critical component for achieving state-of-the-art performance with count-based models. This baseline must be properly implemented and reported.

3.  **Ad-hoc Methodological Choices:** Several methodological decisions seem arbitrary and lack strong motivation. The paper first introduces Kendall's τb as a suitable non-parametric similarity measure for the non-Gaussian PoP vectors. However, it then switches to Pearson's r after PPMI transformation, based on a qualitative observation that the distribution becomes "Gaussian-like" (Figure 3). This switch feels post-hoc and is not rigorously justified. A more principled approach would either defend a single measure or demonstrate that the choice of metric is robust. The selection of hyperparameters, particularly the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors, also appears to be guided purely by empirical performance on the dev set, with conflicting recommendations depending on whether PPMI is used or not.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide any theoretical justification, even if preliminary, for the PoP transformation? Is there any connection to existing work on non-centered or positive random projections in the statistics or computer science literature? Without understanding what properties this projection preserves, it is difficult to assess its reliability and generalizability.

2.  Why was the evaluation limited to a single relatedness task? To strengthen the claims of the paper, results on other standard intrinsic benchmarks, such as the Google analogy dataset or the WordSim-353 similarity/relatedness split, are necessary. A direct, controlled comparison against standard word2vec (CBOW/SG) trained on the same UKWaC corpus would be essential for contextualizing the performance of your method.

3.  Could you provide more details on your implementation of the PPMI+SVD baseline? Specifically, what were the hyperparameters used (e.g., SVD dimensions), and what was the exact performance score? The claim that SVD provides "negligible" improvement is a strong one that runs counter to established findings and requires more substantial evidence.
