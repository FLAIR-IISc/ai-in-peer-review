### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integers, in contrast to standard Random Indexing (RI) which uses a zero-mean projection. The primary contribution is that this positive-only property allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be subsequently weighted using techniques like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a step which is mathematically undefined for standard RI-projected vectors. The authors demonstrate empirically that this combination (PoP+PPMI) achieves performance on a semantic relatedness task that is competitive with computationally expensive dense PPMI+SVD models, while retaining the efficiency and incremental nature of random projection methods. A secondary contribution is the principled analysis of similarity metrics, justifying the use of the non-parametric Kendall's Tau-b for raw PoP vectors and Pearson's correlation for the Gaussian-like vectors resulting from PPMI transformation.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Method:** The central idea of the paper is both simple and highly effective. The problem that standard random projection methods like RI cannot be combined with PPMI weighting is a known limitation. The proposed solution—using a projection matrix with an expected value greater than zero—is an elegant fix that directly addresses the mathematical constraint (avoiding division by zero) while preserving the core benefits of the random projection framework (speed, incrementality, low memory usage). This constitutes a strong conceptual contribution to the family of count-based distributional models.

2.  **Compelling Empirical Results and Efficiency:** The paper provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The PoP+PPMI+Pearson model achieves a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset, which is a very competitive score. This performance is achieved without the time-consuming training of neural networks or the expensive SVD computation on a massive co-occurrence matrix. The key value proposition—achieving high accuracy with high efficiency—is well-supported by the experiments comparing PoP to RI and dense PMI models.

3.  **Thorough Analysis and Justification:** The authors do an excellent job of explaining the "why" behind their choices. The justification for why PPMI works on PoP but not on RI is clear and convincing (Section 3.2). The investigation into similarity measures (Section 2.2) and the switch from Kendall's Tau-b to Pearson's r post-PPMI is well-motivated by the distributional shift, which is compellingly illustrated in Figure 3. Furthermore, the ablation studies in Section 3.3, which explore the impact of dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements, provide valuable insights and practical guidance for using the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the properties of their projection, unlike the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma which underpins Gaussian random projections. While the empirical results are strong, this theoretical gap is a significant weakness. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties are being preserved (or systematically distorted) by the PoP transformation. This makes the method feel more like a well-engineered and successful heuristic rather than a theoretically principled technique.

2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The entire empirical evaluation rests on a single intrinsic task (semantic relatedness on the MEN dataset) in English. While MEN is a standard benchmark, the utility of word embeddings is often demonstrated across a wider suite of tasks, including synonymy (e.g., TOEFL), analogy (e.g., Google analogies), and downstream applications (e.g., sentiment analysis, NER). Without broader evaluation, it is difficult to assess whether PoP vectors capture different facets of meaning compared to other models or if their strong performance is specific to relatedness.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The paper effectively positions PoP against other count-based and random projection methods (RI, dense PPMI, SVD). However, while neural embeddings are mentioned as a key motivator, there is no direct quantitative comparison to standard, efficient neural baselines like word2vec (specifically Skip-Gram with Negative Sampling) or GloVe. The claim of being "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" is supported by citing scores from other papers, but a direct comparison using the same corpus and a comparable training time would be much more powerful and is standard practice. It is unclear how PoP+PPMI would fare against a well-tuned word2vec model trained on the same UKWaC data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The computational efficiency argument is central to the paper's contribution. Could you provide concrete wall-clock timings for building the models for your experiments? Specifically, what was the time taken to build: a) the dense PPMI matrix, b) the PoP model, c) apply PPMI to the PoP model, and d) train a comparable word2vec model on the same UKWaC data?

2.  The analysis of the number of non-zero elements in Figure 5 is interesting. It shows that for PPMI-weighted PoP, performance degrades as the index vectors become denser (more non-zero elements). This is somewhat counter-intuitive. Do you have a hypothesis for why extreme sparsity in the projection matrix is beneficial when PPMI is subsequently applied?

3.  You note that applying SVD to the PoP+PPMI vectors yields negligible improvements. Does this suggest that the PoP projection, combined with PPMI, is already capturing the most salient "principal components" of the data, obviating the need for SVD?
