### Summary and Contributions of the Paper

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core problem addressed is that traditional random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI) are fast and incremental but produce vectors that are incompatible with effective weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Conversely, PPMI is highly effective but computationally expensive as it operates on high-dimensional, sparse count matrices.

The proposed PoP method uses a random projection matrix populated with only non-negative integers. This simple but critical design choice ensures that the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain properties of count vectors (e.g., non-negative sums), making them amenable to subsequent PPMI weighting. The authors demonstrate that this PoP+PPMI combination achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods on a word relatedness task, while being significantly more computationally efficient.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **The PoP method:** A novel random projection technique that, unlike standard methods, generates non-negative embeddings. This is a key technical contribution that elegantly bridges the gap between random projection and count-based weighting paradigms.
2.  **An efficient and high-performing embedding pipeline:** The paper demonstrates a practical pipeline (PoP followed by PPMI) for creating high-quality word embeddings that is fast, incremental, and requires substantially fewer computational resources than training neural models or performing SVD on large matrices.
3.  **A solid empirical analysis:** The authors provide a detailed evaluation on the MEN dataset, systematically comparing PoP to count-based and RI baselines. They also analyze the effects of key hyperparameters (dimensionality, sparsity) and justify the choice of similarity measures (Kendall’s Tau, Pearson’s r) based on the distributional properties of the resulting vectors.

### Strengths

1.  **Clever and Well-Motivated Method:** The central idea is simple yet highly effective. The paper clearly identifies a major limitation of standard random projection methods—their incompatibility with PPMI—and proposes a straightforward and elegant solution. By ensuring the projection matrix has a positive expectation (E(R) > 0), the method preserves the necessary conditions for PPMI calculation while still reaping the efficiency benefits of dimensionality reduction via random projection. This provides a compelling "best of both worlds" approach.
2.  **Significant Practical Value:** The primary appeal of this work lies in its practicality. The authors claim that models can be built in "seconds or minutes" instead of hours or days. In an era where state-of-the-art models often require massive computational resources (GPUs, TPUs) and extensive training time, a method that offers competitive performance with high efficiency is a major contribution. This makes high-quality distributional semantics more accessible to researchers and practitioners with limited resources.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Investigation:** The evaluation is well-structured and convincing within its scope. The comparisons against count-based models and RI clearly demonstrate the baseline advantages of PoP. The subsequent application of PPMI and the resulting performance boost (achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75-0.78 on MEN) successfully validates the core claim of the paper. The analysis of how performance varies with dimensionality and the number of non-zero elements (Figures 4 and 5) adds valuable insight for practitioners looking to apply the method. The visualization of vector distributions (Figure 3) provides strong intuitive support for the authors' claims about why PPMI works on PoP vectors.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, the method is explained with sufficient detail, and the experiments directly support the claims made.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors explicitly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ and ε for the results." While RI has been theoretically justified by its connection to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma (preserving L2 distances), no such foundation is provided for PoP. It is unclear what geometric or distributional properties of the original high-dimensional space are being preserved, and why the specific random distribution chosen (Equation 2) is optimal. Without a theoretical basis, the method appears more as a well-engineered and successful heuristic than a principled technique.
2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The empirical evidence, while strong, is confined to a single task (word relatedness on MEN) in a single language (English). Modern embedding evaluation relies on a broader suite of benchmarks, including word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999), analogical reasoning (e.g., Google analogy dataset), and performance on downstream tasks (e.g., text classification, NER). It is not clear whether the strong performance on relatedness, which rewards topical association, would translate to tasks requiring a finer-grained understanding of semantics like synonymy.
3.  **Missing Key Baselines:** The paper compares its final performance to scores reported in other works but lacks a direct, controlled comparison to strong contemporary baselines. A crucial missing baseline is a standard neural embedding model like word2vec (specifically Skip-Gram with Negative Sampling, SGNS) or GloVe, trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus with comparable hyperparameters. Given that Levy and Goldberg (2014) showed that SGNS implicitly factorizes a shifted PPMI matrix, a direct comparison is essential to properly contextualize the performance and efficiency claims of the PoP+PPMI model.
4.  **Unclear Generalizability of Hyperparameter Settings:** The paper finds that for PPMI-weighted spaces, performance deteriorates as the number of non-zero elements increases (Figure 5), suggesting very sparse index vectors (1-2 non-zero elements) are optimal. This is an interesting finding, but the justification provided is brief. It remains unclear how robust this finding is and how a practitioner should choose the optimal sparsity (`s`) for a new corpus or task without extensive tuning.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a direct comparison against a word2vec (SGNS) model trained on the same UKWaC corpus? This baseline is critical for contextualizing your results, given the known connections between SGNS and PPMI factorization.
2.  The evaluation is focused on the MEN relatedness task. Do you have results or intuitions on how PoP+PPMI would perform on a word similarity task like SimLex-999, which penalizes topical relatedness in favor of true similarity? This would help clarify the qualitative nature of the learned embeddings.
3.  While a full theoretical proof is not expected for a conference paper, could you provide more intuition on *why* this specific positive-only projection works so well? For instance, how does it preserve the co-occurrence structure of the original count space in a way that is particularly beneficial for the subsequent PPMI transformation, compared to other possible non-negative projections?
