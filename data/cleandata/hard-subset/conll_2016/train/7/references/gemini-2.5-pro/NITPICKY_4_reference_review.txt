### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for creating low-dimensional semantic spaces. The key innovation is the design of a random projection matrix populated with sparse, positive integer values. Unlike traditional Random Indexing (RI), which produces zero-centered vectors, PoP generates vectors with positive components. This crucial property allows for the subsequent application of weighting schemes like PPMI, which is mathematically undefined for zero-centered RI-style vectors. The authors demonstrate empirically that the combination of PoP with PPMI achieves results on the MEN semantic relatedness task that are competitive with more computationally intensive methods, while being significantly faster to construct.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The PoP method itself: a simple and efficient random projection technique that uniquely generates vectors amenable to standard count-based weighting schemes (e.g., PPMI) in the low-dimensional space.
2.  An empirical validation showing that this PoP+PPMI combination is highly effective, outperforming RI and count-based baselines and achieving performance comparable to established, more complex models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The central idea is both novel and elegant. It identifies a key limitation of existing random projection methods (inability to apply PPMI) and proposes a simple, direct solution. By bridging the gap between fast projection methods and effective weighting schemes, the work opens a promising new direction for efficient semantic space construction.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly argues for the method's efficiency. By building vectors incrementally in a low-dimensional space and then applying PPMI to this much smaller matrix, the approach sidesteps the computational bottlenecks of both neural network training and PPMI calculation on high-dimensional, sparse co-occurrence matrices. This represents a significant practical contribution.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The reported results are strong. Achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset with the PoP+PPMI+Pearson model demonstrates that the method is not merely a theoretical curiosity but a practically viable technique that competes with state-of-the-art models.

### Weaknesses

My recommendation is ultimately for rejection, as the paper's promising ideas are undermined by a significant number of methodological gaps, presentational flaws, and a general lack of scientific rigor.

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Foundation:** The method's most glaring weakness is its near-total absence of theoretical justification. The authors concede they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account" for PoP. The specific choice of the projection distribution in Equation 2, using `floor(1/U^alpha)` with an unsubstantiated `alpha = 0.5`, appears entirely arbitrary. While the paper gestures towards the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections, it offers no parallel guarantees or formal analysis for PoP, relying instead on a vague citation and hand-wavy arguments about variance. Without a more principled foundation, the method appears to be a collection of successful but poorly understood heuristics.
2.  **Insufficient and Poorly Reported Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough to support the paper's strong claims.
    *   **Baselines:** The comparison to neural models is indirect, relying on scores from other papers. This is not a substitute for a controlled experiment. To validate claims of competitive performance and superior speed, the authors must run at least one standard baseline (e.g., word2vec SGNS) on their exact corpus and preprocessing setup and report both performance and wall-clock time.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The process for selecting key hyperparameters is opaque. The final, highest score of 0.787 is mentioned as the result of "fine tuning" at the end of Section 3.3, but this process is not detailed, leaving the reader to suspect post-hoc cherry-picking.
3.  **Careless and Confusing Data Visualization:** The figures, which are critical to conveying the empirical results, are exceptionally poor and undermine the credibility of the analysis.
    *   Figure 3 is incomprehensible. The axis labels are inconsistent, unconventional, and likely erroneous. For a histogram, the y-axis should represent counts, but it is variously labeled "Frequency" and "log(Frequency)". The x-axis labels are similarly chaotic. It is impossible to interpret these plots or trust the conclusions drawn from them.
    *   In Figure 2, the "PMI-Dense+Cos" performance is shown as a perfectly flat line across different window sizes, which is highly improbable and is not adequately explained in the caption.
    *   Figure 6 contains a clear typo in the y-axis label ("P 6⊥"). This level of carelessness is unacceptable.
4.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Flaws:** The paper is marred by a distracting number of minor errors that collectively signal a lack of polish.
    *   **Notation:** The dimensionality of the projected space is inconsistently denoted as `x` in Equation 1 and `m` everywhere else.
    *   **Writing Style:** The text contains grammatical mistakes ("count-baseds model"), awkward phrasing ("results concerning accuracy"), and an over-reliance on footnotes for substantive points, which disrupts the reading flow.
    *   **Formatting:** Citations are used inconsistently, and the overall manuscript would benefit from thorough proofreading.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide a more rigorous motivation for the specific distribution chosen in Equation 2? Have you analyzed or experimented with other positive-valued distributions (e.g., Poisson, Geometric), and how do they perform in comparison? What is the basis for setting `alpha = 0.5`?
2.  To substantiate your claims of efficiency and performance, can you provide results from a word2vec (SGNS) model trained on your preprocessed UKWaC data? A direct comparison of Spearman's ρ and training time on identical hardware is essential.
3.  Please clarify and correct the plots in Figure 3. The axes must be labeled in a standard and unambiguous way for the reader to understand what is being presented. What, precisely, do the x and y axes in each sub-figure represent?
