### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The central problem it addresses is the incompatibility of standard random projection methods (like Random Indexing) with powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Standard random projections are zero-centered, which makes the necessary sums for PPMI calculation undefined. The key innovation of PoP is to use a random projection matrix populated only with positive integers and zeros. This simple but critical change ensures the resulting low-dimensional vectors remain suitable for PPMI weighting. The authors demonstrate empirically that the combination of PoP with PPMI (and a Pearson correlation similarity measure) is extremely fast to compute and achieves performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task that is competitive with computationally intensive neural embedding methods.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel random projection scheme (PoP) that, for the first time, enables the application of PPMI weighting to the resulting low-dimensional vectors.** This is the primary contribution. It elegantly solves a fundamental limitation of an entire class of dimensionality reduction techniques, effectively creating a bridge between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of PPMI.

2.  **An empirical demonstration that a computationally inexpensive, incremental model (PoP+PPMI) can achieve performance competitive with state-of-the-art neural embedding methods on a standard semantic relatedness task.** This provides a compelling practical argument for the method, showing it is not just a theoretical curiosity but a viable, high-performance alternative for building word embeddings, especially in resource-constrained or time-sensitive scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The core idea of this paper is refreshingly novel and clever. Instead of designing a new complex neural architecture or optimization function, the authors identified a fundamental roadblock in an existing paradigm (random projections) and proposed a simple, elegant solution. The insight that using a positive-only projection matrix would preserve the necessary properties for PPMI is a significant conceptual leap. This kind of bold, foundational thinking is exactly what the field needs.

2.  **Significant Practical Implications for Efficiency:** The paper’s strongest practical argument is its staggering efficiency. The ability to generate high-quality embeddings in "seconds or minutes" rather than the hours or days required for training large neural models is a massive advantage. This democratizes the creation of custom embeddings on new corpora and opens up possibilities for dynamic, on-the-fly model updates, which are intractable with standard training-based approaches.

3.  **Successful Synthesis of Methodological Paradigms:** The work successfully merges the strengths of two distinct and previously incompatible approaches. It retains the desirable properties of random projections—namely, incrementality and low computational overhead—while successfully incorporating the proven performance-boosting power of PPMI weighting from the count-based model literature. This synthesis creates a new class of models that is more than the sum of its parts.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Claim:** While the evaluation is not exhaustive, the results on the MEN dataset are convincing. Achieving a Spearman correlation of ~0.75-0.78 places the method firmly in the same performance bracket as many well-regarded neural embeddings. This provides strong evidence that the proposed combination is not just theoretically possible but practically effective, delivering top-tier performance without the associated computational cost.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors are commendably transparent about the current lack of a formal mathematical account for PoP. Unlike standard random projections, which are justified by results like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, PoP currently relies on empirical validation and analogy. While my personal preference leans towards valuing the novel idea over formal proofs at this stage, the absence of theory regarding what properties are being preserved (or distorted) by this new projection is a notable limitation and a crucial avenue for future work.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are confined to a single semantic relatedness task (MEN) on one corpus. To fully establish the method's utility, it would be beneficial to see its performance on other standard benchmarks, particularly word analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) and various downstream applications (e.g., text classification, NER). It is unclear if the method is equally effective for capturing syntactic/relational similarities as it is for topical relatedness.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Justification:** The paper shows that performance is quite sensitive to the dimensionality (`m`) and especially the sparsity (`s`) of the index vectors. The justification for optimal settings (e.g., "we propose using only 1 or 2 non-zero elements" for PPMI-weighted models) is entirely empirical. A more in-depth analysis or intuition about why extreme sparsity works best with PPMI would strengthen the paper's methodological contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice to use a projection matrix with elements drawn from `floor(1/U^alpha)` is specific. Could you provide more intuition on this choice versus, for example, a simple sparse binary matrix (containing only 0s and 1s)? Does the distribution of the non-zero integer values play a critical role, or is the core mechanism simply their non-negativity?

2.  You convincingly argue that PPMI transforms the PoP vectors' distribution towards a Gaussian-like shape, justifying the switch to Pearson correlation. Have you considered applying other variance-stabilizing transformations (e.g., Box-Cox) directly to the PoP vectors? Given the method's efficiency, it seems uniquely suited to exploring such computationally intensive transformations that would be infeasible on high-dimensional count spaces.
