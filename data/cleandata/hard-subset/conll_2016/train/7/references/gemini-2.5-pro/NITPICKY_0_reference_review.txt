**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces Random Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix populated with sparse, positive integers. Unlike traditional Random Indexing (RI), which uses a zero-centered projection matrix, PoP yields vectors that retain a count-like, non-negative distribution. The authors argue this key property makes the resulting low-dimensional vectors amenable to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is mathematically undefined for standard RI vectors. The paper's primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Novel PPMI-Compatible Random Projection Method:** The PoP technique is a genuinely new proposal that elegantly combines the computational efficiency of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting, addressing a known limitation of methods like RI.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of a Favorable Speed-Accuracy Trade-off:** The experiments show that applying PPMI to low-dimensional PoP vectors (PoP+PPMI) achieves performance on the MEN relatedness task that is competitive with computationally expensive dense PPMI models, while being orders of magnitude faster to compute.
3.  **A Principled Approach to Similarity Measurement:** The authors provide a sound statistical motivation for their choice of similarity metrics, arguing for the non-parametric Kendall’s τb on the raw, non-Gaussian PoP vectors and switching to the parametric Pearson’s r after PPMI creates more Gaussian-like distributions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Practical Idea:** The central concept of designing a random projection specifically to enable PPMI weighting is clever and addresses a clear gap in the literature. It offers a compelling, practical alternative to the standard high-dimensional count -> PPMI -> SVD pipeline, which is often a computational bottleneck.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself against the two dominant paradigms: it correctly identifies the performance limitations of RI (inability to be weighted) and the computational cost of neural embeddings, carving out a well-motivated niche for the proposed method.
3.  **Insightful Distributional Analysis:** The discussion in Section 3.2, supported by the histograms in Figure 3, which links the PPMI transformation to variance stabilization and motivates the switch from Kendall’s to Pearson’s correlation, is a highlight. It demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the underlying statistical properties of the vector spaces.
4.  **Systematic Hyperparameter Evaluation:** The study in Section 3.3, which investigates the impact of dimensionality and sparsity on performance and its variance, is valuable. Reporting the min/max performance across multiple runs provides a transparent view of the model's stability as a randomized algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

My arguments against acceptance stem from a significant number of minor-to-moderate flaws in methodology, theoretical justification, and presentation that collectively undermine the paper's rigor.

1.  **Absent Theoretical Foundation:** The method is presented without any formal justification. Section 2.3 explicitly states, "we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account," which is a critical omission for a paper proposing a new random projection technique. Assertions such as the error variance scaling with `1/m` are made by analogy rather than proof. While early work on RI also lacked theory, the field's standards are higher now, and some form of theoretical analysis (e.g., a Johnson-Lindenstrauss-style guarantee for this projection type) is expected.
2.  **Methodological Inconsistencies and Omissions:**
    *   **Sparsity Parameterization:** The method is defined in Equation 2 with a probability `s`, but the experiments in Section 3.3 are framed around a fixed number of non-zero elements (`k`). The relationship between `s` and `k` (presumably `s ≈ k/m`) is never clarified, making the method and experiments difficult to reconcile and reproduce.
    *   **Contradictory Experimental Setup:** Footnote 8 claims a simple setup with "no frequency cut-off," but the final paragraph of Section 3.3 reveals that the best score (0.78) was achieved using precisely such cut-offs, plus stopword removal. This makes the main results, which are significantly lower, appear to be based on a deliberately handicapped configuration. The most critical results should not be relegated to a concluding sentence.
    *   **Hidden Hyperparameters:** The same sentence mentions "scaling PMI weights as in Levy et al. (2015)" to achieve the best result. This is a crucial methodological detail that belongs in the Method section, as it constitutes a hyperparameter of the PPMI transformation itself.
3.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The paper claims its performance is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," but no results from standard baselines like Word2Vec or GloVe (trained on the same data) are presented in the tables or figures. This makes the central claim of competitiveness impossible to verify. The only baselines are count models and RI, which are known to be weaker.
4.  **Minor but Numerous Presentation Flaws:**
    *   The notation for vector dimensionality is inconsistent (using `x` in Eq. 1 and `m` elsewhere).
    *   The specific choice for the non-zero value distribution `tij = ⌈U⁻⁰.⁵⌉` is presented without any motivation or ablation study. Would a simpler `tij=1` work as well?
    *   Several footnotes are superfluous and detract from the paper's focus (e.g., Footnote 5 on the distributive property of matrices, Footnote 7 on NP-complete problems).
    *   The caption for Figure 6 contains an unclear symbol, `P 6⊥`, which appears to be a typo.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact parameterization of sparsity used in your experiments? Is the primary hyperparameter the probability `s` or the number of non-zero elements `k`?
2.  The paper reports a score of 0.78 using preprocessing and PMI scaling but presents main results without them. Can you justify this choice and provide results for a full, optimized PoP model directly alongside the other models in Figure 2?
3.  Have you experimented with simpler distributions for the non-zero projection values, for instance, by setting them all to a constant value of 1? The choice of `⌈U⁻⁰.⁵⌉` seems overly complex without explicit motivation.
4.  The degradation in performance for unweighted PoP as dimensionality `m` increases past 2000 (Figure 4) is highly counter-intuitive. Your explanation related to `s` is brief. Could you provide a more detailed hypothesis for this behavior? Is it possible that Kendall's τb is adversely affected by extreme sparsity?
