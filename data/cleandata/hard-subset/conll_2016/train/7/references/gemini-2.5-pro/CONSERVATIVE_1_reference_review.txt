### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integers. The authors argue that this approach, unlike standard Random Indexing (RI), produces low-dimensional vectors that are amenable to subsequent weighting with Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a technique well-established for improving the performance of high-dimensional count-based models.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, a random projection technique that enables the application of PPMI weighting directly in the reduced-dimensional space. This circumvents a known limitation of traditional random projection methods.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that the combination of PoP with PPMI weighting and Pearson correlation achieves strong performance on the MEN word relatedness task, competitive with dense PPMI models, while being significantly more computationally efficient.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The most compelling argument in favor of this work is its efficiency. By combining the speed of random projections with the effectiveness of PPMI, the method avoids the computationally expensive steps of SVD on a massive co-occurrence matrix or the lengthy training required for neural network models. The ability to generate competitive embeddings in minutes rather than hours or days is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Novel Methodological Combination:** The paper presents an elegant solution to a clear problem. It is well-known that RI-style projections, which result in vectors with an expected value of zero, cannot be used with PPMI. The proposed positive-only projection is a simple and clever modification that bridges the gap between efficient dimensionality reduction and powerful statistical weighting schemes. This allows the benefits of both paradigms to be leveraged in a single pipeline.

3.  **Clear Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited in scope, are well-designed and clearly illustrate the method's effectiveness on the chosen task. The comparison against standard count-based models and Random Indexing in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates the superiority of the basic PoP method. Furthermore, Figure 2 shows that the PoP+PPMI combination closes the gap with computationally expensive dense PPMI models, supporting the central claim of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's most significant weakness is the absence of a theoretical foundation for the proposed projection. The authors candidly admit they cannot provide a mathematical account for the method's error bounds or properties, likening their situation to the initial proposal of RI. However, in a mature field, a new projection method should be accompanied by at least some analysis of what properties (e.g., distances, norms, inner products) it aims to preserve and under what conditions. Without this, PoP appears to be a heuristic that works well empirically but lacks the principled underpinnings of established methods like Johnson-Lindenstrauss projections or SVD.

2.  **Limited and Narrow Evaluation:** The entire empirical validation rests on a single intrinsic evaluation task (MEN word relatedness) on a single corpus. While MEN is a standard benchmark, the utility of a word embedding method cannot be judged solely on this basis. Established methods have been extensively evaluated on a wide range of intrinsic and extrinsic (downstream) tasks, such as word analogy, named entity recognition, and sentiment analysis. The claim of being "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" is not substantiated without showing that these embeddings are genuinely useful in downstream applications. The current evaluation is insufficient to support such a broad claim.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Key Baselines:** The paper's comparison to the state-of-the-art for count-based models is incomplete. The most direct and powerful established competitor is a PPMI matrix followed by Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The authors mention SVD only in passing, stating it gives "negligible improvements," but do not provide a direct, rigorous comparison in their tables or figures. To be convincing, the PoP+PPMI method must be benchmarked directly against a well-tuned PPMI+SVD model on the same data. Similarly, claims of competitiveness with neural models should be supported by training a standard model like word2vec or GloVe on the same corpus, rather than citing scores from other papers which may use different data or preprocessing.

4.  **Arbitrary Choice of Similarity Metric:** The proposal to use Kendall’s τb for the raw PoP vectors is not well-motivated. The justification relies on a loose analogy to the relationship between cosine and Pearson correlation in Gaussian-projected spaces. This feels ad-hoc. The standard similarity measure in vector spaces is cosine similarity. A deviation from this standard requires a much stronger theoretical or empirical justification than provided. It raises the suspicion that this metric was chosen simply because it produced better results, rather than for principled reasons.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide a more formal or intuitive explanation of what geometric or statistical properties the PoP transformation is intended to preserve? For instance, while it does not preserve L2 distances like Gaussian projections, does it approximate another distance metric or statistical relationship from the original high-dimensional space?

2.  The claims of general utility and competitiveness with neural embeddings are quite strong but are based on a single word relatedness task. Have you performed any experiments on extrinsic, downstream tasks to validate that PoP-generated embeddings are useful beyond this specific evaluation?

3.  Could you please provide the performance of a standard, strong baseline of PPMI followed by SVD (with hyperparameter tuning for dimensionality) on your exact experimental setup? This is the most critical comparison for a method that purports to be an efficient alternative to matrix factorization techniques.
