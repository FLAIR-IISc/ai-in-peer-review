### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core problem the paper addresses is the trade-off between the computational efficiency of random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI) and the high accuracy of predictive models, which often rely on computationally expensive training (neural embeddings) or transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) applied to high-dimensional count matrices.

The key insight is that existing random projection methods use matrices with an expected value of zero, rendering the resulting vectors incompatible with PPMI. The proposed PoP method uses a sparse, random projection matrix containing only positive integers. This allows for the efficient, incremental construction of a low-dimensional space, whose vectors can *then* be weighted using PPMI.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **The PoP Method:** The primary contribution is the novel random projection technique itself. By designing a projection matrix with E(R) > 0, the authors create a bridge between efficient dimensionality reduction and powerful weighting schemes like PPMI, which was not possible with previous RI-style methods.
2.  **Empirical Validation of Efficiency and Performance:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that the combination of PoP with PPMI weighting (and Pearson correlation for similarity) achieves performance on the MEN relatedness task that is competitive with established, more computationally intensive methods. It successfully shows that this high performance can be achieved with a fraction of the computational cost.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Solution:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying a clear gap in existing methods. The inability to apply PPMI to vectors created by standard random projections is a well-defined limitation. The proposed solution—designing a projection matrix that overcomes this specific limitation—is simple, elegant, and directly addresses the problem. The motivation is compelling and easy to follow.
2.  **Significant Practical Implications for Efficiency:** The most significant strength is the method's efficiency. By combining the low computational overhead of random projections with the effectiveness of PPMI, PoP offers a practical and scalable alternative for building high-quality word embeddings. The claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" instead of hours or days is a major advantage, especially for researchers with limited computational resources or those needing to frequently retrain models on new data.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Analysis:** The evaluation is methodical and convincing. The authors systematically compare PoP to its direct predecessors (count-based models and RI), clearly demonstrating its superiority (Figure 1). The subsequent application of PPMI and the analysis of different similarity measures (Kendall's vs. Pearson's) are well-justified and supported by distributional analysis (Figure 3). The experiments exploring the effects of key hyperparameters like dimensionality (m) and sparsity (s) provide valuable insights into the method's behavior and offer practical guidance for its use.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The method is described clearly, from the matrix formulation (Equation 1) to the incremental construction steps. The figures, particularly the performance graphs and the distributional histograms, are highly effective at illustrating the paper's core claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors are upfront about this in Section 2.3, but it remains a significant weakness. While methods like RI were also initially introduced without full theoretical backing, the field has advanced since then. The paper lacks any formal analysis of what properties the PoP projection preserves or distorts, unlike the well-understood distance-preserving properties of Gaussian projections (via the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma). This makes the method feel more like a successful empirical finding than a theoretically grounded technique. Without this, it's hard to reason about its expected behavior on different types of data or tasks.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While the results are strong, the paper's claims of being "competitive" with neural net-based approaches would be much more robust if demonstrated across a wider range of intrinsic evaluations. For example, performance on word analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) or synonym identification (e.g., TOEFL) would provide a more complete picture of the semantic properties captured by the vectors. Without this, it's unclear if PoP's strength is specific to capturing topical relatedness, which MEN heavily relies on.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper compares PoP+PPMI to count-based, RI, and dense PPMI models. While these are the correct conceptual baselines, a direct comparison to standard, publicly available neural embeddings like Word2Vec (SGNS) or GloVe, trained on the same UKWaC corpus, is missing. The authors mention that their score of 0.75 is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" by citing other papers, but a controlled, head-to-head comparison is necessary for a rigorous assessment. This would have more definitively established where PoP+PPMI sits in the landscape of modern distributional models.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the lack of theoretical grounding: Beyond citing parallels to RI, could you provide more intuition about what properties your positive-only projection is approximately preserving? Is it related to some form of non-centered correlation or a different divergence measure, rather than the Euclidean distance preserved by Gaussian projections?
2.  Regarding the scope of evaluation: Have you performed any preliminary experiments on other common intrinsic evaluation tasks, such as word analogies? The structure of PoP vectors is quite different from that of neural embeddings, so it would be interesting to know if they support the vector offset method for solving analogies.
3.  The switch from Kendall's τb for unweighted PoP to Pearson's r for PPMI-weighted PoP is well-justified by the change in the vectors' distribution. For completeness, have you measured the performance of PoP+PPMI using the standard cosine similarity? This would help contextualize the results for readers more familiar with that metric and clarify how much of the performance gain comes from the vector transformation versus the choice of a specific correlation coefficient.
