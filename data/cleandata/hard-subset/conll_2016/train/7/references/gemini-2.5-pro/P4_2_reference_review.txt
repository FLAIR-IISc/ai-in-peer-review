### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel method for constructing word embeddings called Positive-Only Projections (PoP). The method aims to combine the computational efficiency of random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI) with the high performance of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Standard random projection methods produce low-dimensional vectors with a zero mean, which makes them incompatible with PPMI weighting. The key innovation of PoP is the use of a projection matrix containing only sparse, non-negative integer values. This ensures the resulting low-dimensional vectors maintain positive-valued components, analogous to raw count vectors. Consequently, these compact PoP vectors can be effectively refined using PPMI, a step that is computationally prohibitive on original high-dimensional count spaces. The authors evaluate their method on the MEN word relatedness task, demonstrating that PoP vectors followed by a PPMI transformation and Pearson correlation (PoP+PPMI+Pearson) achieve performance comparable to strong, dense PPMI models but with significantly reduced computational requirements.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel random projection method that is compatible with PPMI weighting.** The primary contribution is the PoP technique itself. By designing a projection matrix with only non-negative entries, the authors create a dimensionality reduction method that produces vectors amenable to subsequent weighting schemes like PPMI. This elegantly bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of established count-based weighting methods.
2.  **An efficient and scalable pipeline for creating high-performance count-based embeddings.** The paper presents a full pipeline (PoP construction followed by PPMI transformation) that serves as a practical, computationally inexpensive alternative to traditional methods. It avoids the massive memory footprint of building a full co-occurrence matrix for PPMI and the extensive training time required for neural network models, while achieving competitive results.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis of the method's properties.** The authors provide a solid empirical investigation into PoP, comparing it against RI and standard count models. They effectively demonstrate the statistical rationale for their choices (e.g., using Kendall's τb for raw PoP and Pearson's r for PPMI-transformed vectors) and analyze the impact of key hyperparameters such as dimensionality and projection sparsity, offering valuable insights for practitioners.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Practical Significance.** The proposed method is simple, novel, and addresses a clear limitation of existing random projection techniques. The inability to apply PPMI to RI vectors has long been a known issue. By solving this problem, the authors unlock the ability to combine the speed of incremental, projection-based space construction with the proven effectiveness of PPMI. This offers a highly practical tool for researchers and engineers who need to build high-quality embeddings quickly and with limited computational resources.
2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated.** The paper provides a clear and logical explanation of the method. The core motivation—that PoP uses a projection matrix `R` with `E(R) > 0` whereas RI uses one with `E(R) = 0`, thus enabling PPMI—is technically sound. The subsequent analysis of the vector distributions (Figure 3) and the corresponding choice of similarity metrics (Kendall's τb vs. Pearson's r) is insightful and demonstrates a strong grasp of the underlying statistical principles.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation and Analysis.** The experimental section is thorough. The authors systematically compare PoP to relevant baselines (count-based, RI) and demonstrate its effectiveness when combined with PPMI. The parameter sensitivity analysis in Section 3.3, which explores the effects of dimensionality (`m`) and the number of non-zero elements (`s`), is particularly strong. It not only validates the method's robustness but also provides clear guidance on hyperparameter tuning. The visualization of the distributions of vector components in Figure 3 is an excellent piece of evidence that effectively illustrates *why* the method works as described.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees.** The authors explicitly state (Section 2.3) that they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account or theoretical bounds for the error (`δ`) introduced by the PoP projection, unlike the well-established guarantees for Gaussian random projections derived from the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma. While the empirical results are strong, this absence of theoretical grounding is a notable weakness. The performance of the algorithm is demonstrated but not formally justified, leaving questions about its reliability and behavior in un-tested scenarios.
2.  **Evaluation is Limited to a Single Task.** The paper's entire empirical validation is based on the MEN word relatedness benchmark. While a standard task, performance on word similarity/relatedness does not always correlate with performance on other intrinsic evaluations (e.g., analogy) or, more critically, on downstream applications (e.g., text classification, named entity recognition). Without evaluation on a broader range of tasks, it is difficult to ascertain the general utility of PoP-based embeddings compared to alternatives.
3.  **Indirect Comparison with Neural Embeddings.** The paper claims performance is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," but this claim is substantiated only by citing results from other papers. No direct, controlled experiments are run against state-of-the-art neural models like word2vec (SGNS) or GloVe using the same corpus and preprocessing. Such indirect comparisons can be misleading due to subtle differences in experimental setups. A direct comparison would have made the claims of competitiveness much more compelling.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The PPMI transformation is typically applied to a `word x context` matrix. The PoP method projects this into a `word x m` matrix, where `m` is the reduced dimension. Equation 3 for PPMI involves sums over both rows (`p`) and columns (`n`). How are the column sums (i.e., `sum over j from 1 to n`) computed in the PoP-projected space, where the original `n` contexts are collapsed into `m` dimensions? Is there an assumption made, or is the PPMI formula adapted for the `p x m` matrix?
2.  The paper argues against the applicability of PPMI to RI because row/column sums are zero. An alternative pipeline could be to first compute the full, high-dimensional PPMI matrix and then apply RI for dimensionality reduction (Count -> PPMI -> RI). While this would still be computationally intensive in the first step, it would serve as a crucial baseline. How do you expect this alternative pipeline to compare with your PoP -> PPMI approach in terms of both performance and efficiency?
3.  Could you provide concrete figures for the computational resources required (e.g., wall-clock time and peak memory usage) for the main experimental conditions: (a) building the dense PPMI model, and (b) building the PoP+PPMI model? This would quantify the claimed efficiency benefits more directly.
