### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integers, which allows the method to be incremental and computationally efficient like Random Indexing (RI), while producing vectors that, unlike RI, can be post-processed with standard weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors demonstrate empirically that applying PPMI and using Pearson correlation as a similarity measure allows their method to achieve performance competitive with more computationally expensive approaches on the MEN semantic relatedness task.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **The PoP method:** A novel random projection technique that uses a sparse, positive-only matrix. The key innovation is that this design choice enables the application of count-based weighting schemes (like PPMI) to the resulting low-dimensional vectors, effectively bridging a gap between efficient random projection models and high-performing weighted count models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Efficiency and Effectiveness:** The paper provides a compelling empirical case that PoP, when combined with PPMI (PoP+PPMI), achieves high performance on a standard semantic relatedness task (MEN score of ~0.75-0.78). This performance is shown to be comparable to dense PPMI models and is claimed to be competitive with neural embeddings, but is achieved with the computational efficiency of an incremental, projection-based method.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of the Method's Properties:** The authors provide a helpful analysis of the method's behavior, including an effective visualization of the vector component distributions (Figure 3) which clearly illustrates why PPMI is applicable to PoP but not RI. The parameter sweeps over dimensionality and sparsity further strengthen the paper's empirical grounding.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Novelty:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important problem: the trade-off between the computational efficiency of methods like Random Indexing and the high accuracy of methods like PPMI+SVD or neural embeddings. The proposed solution is elegant and simple. The idea of designing a random projection specifically to preserve the applicability of subsequent weighting schemes like PPMI is novel and impactful.
2.  **High Computational Efficiency:** The primary advantage of PoP is its efficiency. It is an incremental, one-pass algorithm that avoids the need to construct and store a massive, sparse co-occurrence matrix. All expensive operations, particularly PPMI weighting, are performed on low-dimensional vectors (e.g., m=3000), making the process dramatically faster and less memory-intensive than applying PPMI to a vocabulary-sized count matrix. This is a significant practical contribution for researchers and practitioners working with very large corpora.
3.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Experimental Results:** The evaluation is thorough and convincing within its scope. The paper clearly demonstrates that vanilla PoP outperforms RI and that PoP+PPMI achieves a very competitive score of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset. The analysis extends beyond just reporting a final number; the authors investigate the effects of context window size, dimensionality, and projection sparsity, providing valuable insights and practical guidance for using the method. The discussion on switching from Kendall's tau to Pearson's correlation after the PPMI transformation (which "Gaussianizes" the data) is well-reasoned and supported by results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors are commendably upfront about this, but it is a major weakness. Random projection methods like RI are grounded in theoretical results like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which guarantees (with high probability) the preservation of Euclidean distances. PoP lacks any such theoretical analysis. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties are being preserved by this new projection. Without this, the method feels like a well-engineered and effective heuristic, but its behavior is not formally understood, which may limit its adoption and future extension.
2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The empirical evaluation is strong but narrow, focusing entirely on the MEN semantic relatedness task in English. While MEN is a standard benchmark, state-of-the-art models are typically evaluated on a wider range of intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity like WordSim-353, analogy tasks like the Google analogy dataset) and extrinsic, downstream tasks (e.g., NER, text classification). It remains an open question whether the strong relatedness performance translates to other tasks, particularly analogy, which relies heavily on the geometric structure of the vector space.
3.  **Insufficient Quantification of Efficiency Claims and Baselines:** The paper's central claim is achieving high accuracy with high efficiency. While the efficiency is qualitatively obvious, the paper would be much stronger if these gains were quantified. A table comparing wall-clock training times and peak memory usage for PoP+PPMI, dense PPMI+SVD, and a standard neural model (e.g., word2vec CBOW/SGNS) on the UKWaC corpus would provide concrete evidence. Similarly, the comparison to neural embeddings is made by citing scores from other papers, which can be problematic due to differences in corpora, preprocessing, and hyperparameters. A direct comparison to a standard word2vec or GloVe model trained on the same data would make the performance claims more robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide concrete numbers for the computational gains? A comparison of wall-clock time and memory usage for building the PoP+PPMI model versus a dense PPMI (+SVD) model and a standard word2vec model on the UKWaC corpus would greatly strengthen your central claim.
2.  The PPMI transformation is key to your model's success. Levy et al. (2015) found that word2vec's skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) implicitly factorizes a shifted PMI (SPMI) matrix. Have you considered applying SPMI or other PPMI variants to your PoP vectors, and do you have any intuition on how that might affect performance, particularly on similarity or analogy tasks?
3.  Your analysis of similarity metrics (Kendall's τb for raw PoP, Pearson's r for PoP+PPMI) is insightful. Did you experiment with applying Pearson's r or cosine to the raw PoP vectors? While you argue against it on theoretical grounds, the empirical result would be useful for understanding how much of the performance gain comes from the PPMI step versus the choice of similarity metric.
