### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix containing only non-negative integer values. This is in contrast to traditional random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI), which use zero-centered distributions. The key advantage of the PoP approach is that the resulting low-dimensional vectors maintain the characteristics of count vectors, making them suitable for subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors demonstrate that this combination, PoP followed by PPMI, achieves results on a word relatedness task that are competitive with more computationally intensive methods, while being significantly faster to compute.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method that enables the application of count-based weighting schemes in a low-dimensional space.** This is the primary contribution, bridging the efficiency of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting. This approach circumvents a fundamental limitation of prior random projection techniques, which produce vectors incompatible with PPMI.
2.  **The development of a highly efficient pipeline for building performant word representations.** By combining incremental construction with low-dimensional PPMI calculation, the method avoids both the high memory/computational cost of applying PPMI and SVD to large, sparse count matrices and the extensive training time required for neural embedding models.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis demonstrating the method's effectiveness and characterizing its behavior.** The paper provides a clear evaluation against relevant baselines (count-based, RI, dense PPMI) on a standard benchmark, supported by a sensitivity analysis of the method's key hyperparameters (dimensionality and sparsity).

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Soundness.** The central idea of designing a random projection matrix specifically to be compatible with PPMI is highly novel and solves a known problem in an elegant manner. Using a positive-only projection to create low-dimensional "pseudo-count" vectors is an insightful way to combine the benefits of two distinct families of methods (random projections and count-based weighting). The technical approach is sound and well-motivated.
2.  **Significant Practical Value and Efficiency.** The proposed method offers a compelling practical advantage. It presents a path to creating high-quality semantic representations with very low computational overhead, reportedly in seconds or minutes. This makes the approach highly suitable for rapid prototyping, applications on massive datasets, or use in resource-constrained environments where training large neural models or performing SVD on huge matrices is infeasible.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation and Analysis.** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The paper systematically compares PoP against appropriate baselines, clearly demonstrating its superiority over RI and its ability to match the performance of far more expensive dense PPMI models. The investigation into the distributions of the resulting vectors (Figure 3) provides a strong justification for the choice of similarity metrics (Kendall's τb for raw PoP, Pearson's r for PPMI-transformed PoP). The hyperparameter analysis in Figures 4 and 5 further strengthens the paper by providing practical insights into the model's behavior.
4.  **High Clarity and Quality of Writing.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the method is described with precision, and the results are presented with clear figures and cogent interpretations. The authors do an excellent job of explaining complex concepts and the rationale behind their methodological choices.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding.** The authors explicitly acknowledge the lack of a formal mathematical analysis of the PoP method's properties (Section 2.3). While this candor is appreciated, it remains a notable weakness. Unlike standard random projections which have theoretical guarantees based on the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, it is unclear what geometric or statistical properties of the original high-dimensional space PoP aims to preserve. A more formal treatment, even if preliminary, of the projection's error bounds or its relationship to specific norms would significantly strengthen the work.
2.  **Evaluation is Confined to a Single Task.** The paper's entire empirical validation rests on the MEN word relatedness benchmark. The performance of word embeddings is known to vary significantly across different tasks (e.g., relatedness vs. similarity, intrinsic vs. downstream). To establish the general utility of PoP-generated vectors, an evaluation on a broader suite of tasks is necessary, particularly including a word similarity task (e.g., SimLex-999) to assess how well the representations capture synonymy, and ideally a downstream task.
3.  **Comparison to Neural Baselines is Indirect.** The authors claim performance is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" by citing results from other papers. A direct, controlled comparison against a standard algorithm like word2vec (SGNS) trained on the exact same corpus would be much more compelling. Without this, the claims of competitiveness with neural methods are not fully substantiated.
4.  **Rationale for Specific Projection Distribution is Unclear.** The paper proposes a specific distribution for the non-zero elements of the projection matrix, `t_ij = floor(1/U^0.5)`, without a clear justification for this particular functional form. It is not evident why this is superior to a simpler choice, such as setting all non-zero elements to 1. An ablation study comparing different positive-valued distributions would be valuable to determine if this complexity is warranted.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide the motivation for choosing the specific distribution `floor(1/U^0.5)` for the non-zero projection values? Have you experimented with a simpler distribution, such as setting all non-zero entries to a constant (e.g., 1), and if so, how does it impact performance?
2.  The evaluation focuses on word relatedness. How do PoP+PPMI vectors perform on a word similarity benchmark like SimLex-999? Results on such a task would provide valuable insight into the qualitative nature of the learned embeddings.
3.  While a full theoretical proof is a direction for future work, could you offer more intuition on what properties the PoP projection is expected to preserve from the original count space? For instance, whereas RI approximates Euclidean (L2) distances, does PoP approximate L1 distances or some other information-theoretic quantity, which might explain its synergy with PPMI?
