### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing distributional semantic spaces. The method is inspired by Random Indexing (RI) but uses a projection matrix composed of sparse, positive integer values. The primary contribution is that this positive-only projection creates a low-dimensional space that, unlike RI-based spaces, is amenable to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). This effectively combines the efficiency and scalability of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-weighted models. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical investigation of the method's properties, including the effect of different similarity measures (Kendall's τb, Pearson's r) and the impact of key hyperparameters like dimensionality and projection sparsity.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Pragmatic Method:** The core idea of creating a random projection that preserves the non-negativity of the original count space is both simple and highly effective. It directly addresses a well-known limitation of standard random indexing methods (e.g., Gaussian or ternary projections), which produce vectors with zero mean, rendering PPMI calculations undefined. PoP offers a compelling "best of both worlds" solution: it is incremental and memory-efficient like RI, but it can leverage the proven effectiveness of PPMI, which is typically associated with expensive, batch-processed, dense co-occurrence matrices. This is a significant practical contribution for building large-scale models.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The experimental section is convincing. The authors first establish a clear baseline, showing that raw PoP (with Kendall's τb) outperforms both the original count space and standard RI (Figure 1). They then demonstrate the main claim: applying PPMI to the PoP space leads to a dramatic performance increase, achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset (Figure 2). This result is highly competitive with standard PPMI+SVD models and many neural embedding approaches, validating the method's effectiveness.

3.  **Excellent Diagnostic Visualizations and Hyperparameter Study:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting a final score. The histogram analysis in Figure 3 is particularly insightful, providing a clear visual explanation for *why* the method works: PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution of the original counts, which the logarithmic transformation within PPMI then successfully normalizes into a more Gaussian-like distribution, justifying the switch to Pearson correlation. Furthermore, the systematic study of dimensionality (`m`) and sparsity (`s`) in Figures 4 and 5 provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and practical guidance for users. The finding that sparser projections work better with PPMI is an interesting and non-obvious result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper's most significant weakness, which the authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3, is the absence of a theoretical foundation for the PoP projection. While standard RI is backed by the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which guarantees (with high probability) the preservation of Euclidean distances, it is unclear what geometric or statistical properties PoP preserves. The choice of Kendall's τb as a similarity metric for the raw PoP space feels empirically motivated rather than theoretically grounded. Without a formal understanding of the projection, the method remains a well-engineered and effective heuristic, but it lacks the principled underpinnings of other dimensionality reduction techniques.

2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a single corpus (UKWaC) and a single task (word relatedness on MEN). While MEN is a standard benchmark, the field has moved towards evaluating models on a wider suite of tasks that test for different linguistic properties. For example, it would be beneficial to see results on a similarity-focused dataset (e.g., SimLex-999) and an analogy task (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) to understand how PoP+PPMI compares to methods like SGNS or GloVe in capturing more syntactic or relational information versus topical relatedness. The claim of being "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" is strong and requires broader evidence to be fully substantiated.

3.  **Vague Claims on Computational Efficiency:** A primary motivation for the work is computational efficiency ("models can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes"). However, these claims are not quantitatively supported. The paper would be much stronger if it included a table comparing the actual wall-clock time and peak memory usage for building models with (a) PoP+PPMI, (b) a full co-occurrence matrix + PPMI + SVD, and (c) a modern, optimized neural embedding implementation like fastText on the same corpus. The reference to Collobert's two-month training time from 2014 is outdated and does not reflect the current state of neural embedding training.

### Questions to Authors

1.  While a full mathematical proof is outside the scope of this paper, could you provide more intuition about the properties preserved by the PoP projection? For instance, does it approximate some form of `L1` distance, given the use of positive integers and the connection of other non-Gaussian projections to `L1`-normed spaces? Is there a theoretical reason to prefer Kendall's τb over other rank correlations like Spearman's ρ for the raw PoP vectors?

2.  The PPMI transformation is known to bias models towards rare words. Neural methods like word2vec with negative sampling have a different implicit weighting scheme. How do you think this difference would manifest in downstream tasks? Have you considered evaluating on tasks where this difference might be more pronounced, such as parsing or NER, where the handling of function words and syntactic contexts is critical?

3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the distribution in Equation 2, specifically `b 1/U^α c` with `α = 0.5`? This seems quite specific. Have you experimented with simpler distributions, such as assigning a constant value (e.g., 1) for the non-zero elements? How sensitive is the model's performance to this particular choice of generating the random weights?
