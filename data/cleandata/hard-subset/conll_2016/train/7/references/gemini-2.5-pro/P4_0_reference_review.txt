### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic vector spaces. The method is based on random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but critically uses a projection matrix with only non-negative integer entries. This design choice ensures that the resulting low-dimensional vectors have properties (e.g., non-zero column and row sums) that make them amenable to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Standard RI vectors, derived from zero-mean projections, cannot be re-weighted with PPMI. The authors demonstrate that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors, followed by using Pearson correlation for similarity, yields performance on the MEN word relatedness task that is competitive with more computationally expensive methods, while retaining the efficiency and incremental nature of random projection techniques.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of a novel random projection method, PoP, specifically designed to produce low-dimensional vectors that are compatible with PPMI weighting, thus bridging a gap between efficient random projection methods and powerful statistical weighting schemes.
2.  An extensive empirical demonstration that the combination of PoP and PPMI is highly effective, achieving strong results on a standard word relatedness benchmark. This includes a valuable analysis of the method's sensitivity to its key hyperparameters (dimensionality and sparsity).
3.  The well-reasoned application of different similarity metrics (Kendall's τb and Pearson's r) to raw and PPMI-weighted PoP vectors, justified by an analysis of the underlying vector component distributions.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Novelty and Practical Utility:** The core idea of designing a random projection to be compatible with PPMI is both novel and addresses a well-known limitation of methods like RI. It carves out a new and useful niche in the landscape of distributional semantics, offering a method that is incremental and highly efficient (like RI) but can leverage the high performance of PPMI weighting (like traditional count-and-factorize models). This combination presents a compelling practical alternative for scenarios where computational resources or the need for incremental updates are constraints.
2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper provides a clear and logical explanation of the method. The motivation for why standard RI fails with PPMI (due to zero-mean projections) and how PoP's positive-only projection overcomes this is technically sound. The subsequent argument for shifting from a non-parametric similarity measure (Kendall's τb) on the raw, heavy-tailed PoP vectors to a parametric one (Pearson's r) on the PPMI-transformed, Gaussian-like vectors is well-supported by both statistical reasoning and empirical evidence (Figure 3).
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a systematic evaluation that convincingly supports their claims. They establish a clear performance hierarchy: PoP+PPMI > PoP > RI ≈ Count-based. The experiments carefully analyze the influence of crucial hyperparameters—dimensionality (`m`) and the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors—providing insight into the model's behavior (Figures 4 and 5). The final reported performance on the MEN dataset (Spearman's ρ ≈ 0.75-0.78) is strong and substantiates the claim of being competitive with more complex models. The use of visualizations to show the effect of transformations on the vector component distributions (Figure 3) is particularly effective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors explicitly acknowledge the absence of a "detailed mathematical account" for the proposed projection. While empirical success is valuable, this is a significant weakness. Unlike Gaussian random projections which are grounded in the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, the theoretical properties of the PoP projection are left unexplored. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties are being preserved or how the error `δ` behaves. The choice of the specific distribution for the random matrix elements (`ceil(1/U^alpha)`) feels arbitrary and lacks formal motivation, making it difficult to understand why it works or how it might be improved.
2.  **Narrow Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's empirical claims rest entirely on one specific task (word relatedness) on one dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, the performance of word embeddings can be highly task-dependent. To robustly claim competitiveness with neural embeddings, the evaluation should include a wider range of tasks, such as word analogy (e.g., Google analogy dataset), concept categorization, or performance on downstream NLP applications. The current evaluation, while well-executed, is too narrow to support the broad claims of the paper.
3.  **Ambiguity in Hyperparameter Selection:** The experiments reveal that model performance is highly sensitive to the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors, and that the optimal setting for this hyperparameter differs depending on whether PPMI is applied (Figure 5). For unweighted PoP, performance increases with more non-zero elements, whereas for PoP+PPMI, performance degrades sharply. This raises a practical concern: how should a user set this hyperparameter for a new task or dataset? The paper provides recommendations based on its findings but does not offer a principled method or general heuristic for tuning, which limits the method's ease of use.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice of the distribution for the non-zero elements in the projection matrix, `ceil(1/U^alpha)`, seems quite specific. Have you experimented with simpler positive distributions, such as a constant value (e.g., all non-zero elements are 1) or a discrete Poisson distribution? How does the choice of this distribution impact the final performance, and what is the theoretical or empirical reasoning for this specific formulation over simpler alternatives?
2.  A primary motivation for PoP is computational efficiency. Could you provide concrete quantitative evidence, such as wall-clock time and memory usage, for building the PoP+PPMI model on the UKWaC corpus? How does this compare directly to building a) a traditional PPMI matrix followed by SVD, and b) a standard neural model like word2vec (CBOW or Skip-gram) on the same data and hardware?
3.  Figure 4 shows that for the unweighted PoP model, performance unexpectedly decreases as dimensionality `m` increases beyond 2000. The paper hypothesizes this is due to the sparsity parameter `s` becoming too small. Did you run an experiment where the number of non-zero elements was held constant while `m` increased? This would decouple the effects of dimensionality and sparsity and could clarify whether this performance degradation is an artifact of the experimental setup or an inherent property of the method.
