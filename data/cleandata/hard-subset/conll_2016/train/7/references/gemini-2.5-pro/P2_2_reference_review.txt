### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word vector spaces. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix composed of sparse, positive integers. This design choice distinguishes it from traditional Random Indexing (RI), which uses zero-centered projections. The key advantage of this positive-only approach is that the resulting low-dimensional vectors retain a distribution similar to the original count vectors, which allows for the application of effective weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors demonstrate empirically that combining PoP with PPMI and a suitable correlation measure (Pearson's r) yields performance on the MEN relatedness task that is competitive with more computationally expensive methods, while being significantly faster to construct.

The main contributions of this work are, in my view:

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) that is compatible with PPMI weighting.** This is the primary contribution. It cleverly bridges the gap between the efficiency of random projection methods and the high performance of PPMI-weighted count models, addressing a well-known limitation of methods like RI.
2.  **A thorough empirical analysis demonstrating the method's effectiveness and efficiency.** The paper provides a well-structured evaluation comparing PoP to count-based and RI baselines, showing its superiority. Crucially, it demonstrates that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive scores, validating the central claim. The analysis includes a valuable study of hyperparameters and insightful visualizations (e.g., Figure 3) that explain the distributional properties of the method.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty.** The paper addresses a clear and important problem: the trade-off between the computational cost and the quality of word embeddings. While neural embeddings are powerful, their training cost is prohibitive for many scenarios. PoP offers a compelling alternative. The idea of designing a random projection specifically to enable a powerful, well-understood weighting scheme like PPMI is both simple and highly effective. It is a novel synthesis of existing ideas (random projections, PPMI) that creates a new capability.

2.  **Strong Practical Utility and Efficiency.** The primary appeal of PoP is its speed and resource efficiency. The method is incremental, does not require computationally intensive training or matrix factorization (like SVD), and performs the expensive PPMI calculation on a low-dimensional matrix. The authors convincingly argue that this allows for model creation in seconds or minutes, a dramatic improvement over hours or days for neural models. This makes the method highly attractive for rapid prototyping, large-scale data exploration, or resource-constrained environments.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation.** The experimental setup is sound. The authors use a standard dataset (MEN) and corpus (UKWaC) for easy comparison. The experiments systematically build the case for the method: first, showing PoP outperforms RI and raw counts (Figure 1); second, demonstrating that the combination of PoP and PPMI is highly effective and competitive (Figure 2); and third, providing a detailed analysis of the method's sensitivity to its key parameters (dimensionality and sparsity, Figures 4 & 5). The histogram analysis in Figure 3 is particularly effective at visually communicating *why* PPMI can be applied to PoP but not RI.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they cannot yet provide a formal mathematical justification for PoP's performance, unlike traditional random projections that are backed by principles like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma. The method is presented and justified based on empirical results and intuition. While the results are strong, the absence of theory makes the method feel more like a well-engineered heuristic. It is unclear what geometric or statistical properties are being preserved by this specific projection, which limits a deeper understanding of its behavior and potential failure modes.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation.** The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on the MEN dataset). While this is a standard benchmark, the utility of word embeddings is often demonstrated on a wider suite of intrinsic tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy) and, more importantly, on downstream extrinsic tasks (e.g., sentiment analysis, named entity recognition). It is not guaranteed that the properties that make PoP+PPMI excel at relatedness will transfer to tasks that may rely more on capturing syntactic or relational similarities. The comparison to neural models is also indirect (citing scores from other papers) rather than a direct run on the same data, which weakens the performance claims slightly.

3.  **Unclear Guidance on Parameter Tuning.** The parameter analysis in Section 3.3 is valuable but raises some questions. For instance, the result that performance for PoP+PPMI *decreases* with more non-zero elements in the index vectors (Figure 5) is counter-intuitive. The authors offer a brief explanation about uncontrolled "distortions," but this is not fully explored. This leaves practitioners with a somewhat ad-hoc recipe ("use only 1 or 2 non-zero elements") without a deep understanding of the trade-offs involved, potentially making it difficult to adapt the method to new domains or languages.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the lack of theoretical grounding: Can you elaborate on the connection you see between PoP and other related areas? For instance, does this projection bear any resemblance to techniques from non-negative matrix factorization or compressed sensing with non-negative constraints? Providing even a tentative link to an established theoretical framework could significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  The finding that sparser index vectors (fewer non-zero elements) work better for the PPMI-weighted model is intriguing. Your hypothesis is that denser vectors introduce uncontrolled distortions. An alternative hypothesis might be that very sparse index vectors better preserve the extreme sparsity of the original count matrix, which PPMI is known to work well with. Have you investigated the relationship between the sparsity of the original count matrix and the optimal sparsity `s` for the PoP projection?

3.  Given that PoP+PPMI creates vectors with Gaussian-like distributions where Pearson correlation is effective, have you performed any preliminary experiments on the classic word analogy task (e.g., "king - man + woman = queen")? This task relies on the vector space having a consistent linear structure, which would be a powerful demonstration of the quality of the embeddings beyond just topical relatedness.
