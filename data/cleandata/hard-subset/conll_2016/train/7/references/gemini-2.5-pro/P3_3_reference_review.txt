### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integers. This design choice is the central contribution, as it ensures that the resulting low-dimensional vectors remain "count-like" (i.e., non-negative components, positive sums), unlike vectors produced by standard Random Indexing (RI) which have an expected value of zero. This key property allows for the subsequent application of powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which are undefined for standard RI vectors.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **The PoP Method:** A new, simple, and efficient random projection technique that is specifically designed to be compatible with count-based weighting schemes like PPMI. This elegantly bridges the gap between the efficiency of incremental random projections and the effectiveness of established association measures.
2.  **Empirical Validation:** A strong empirical demonstration that the combination of PoP with PPMI and Pearson correlation (PoP+PPMI+Pearson) achieves performance competitive with traditional dense PPMI models and, by extension, neural embeddings, on the MEN semantic relatedness benchmark. This is achieved with a drastic reduction in computational cost and memory.
3.  **Ablation and Analysis:** The paper provides a useful analysis of the method's parameters (dimensionality `m` and sparsity `s`), shedding light on how they interact with the subsequent PPMI weighting step and offering practical guidance for their use.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Problem Motivation:** The paper addresses a clear and well-known limitation of classic random projection methods like RI: their incompatibility with PPMI. The proposed solution is simple, intuitive, and effective. By changing the distribution of the random projection matrix, the authors unlock the ability to apply a mature and powerful family of weighting techniques in a low-dimensional space. This is a clever and practically significant contribution.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The main appeal of the method is its efficiency. The paper convincingly argues that PoP allows for the construction of high-quality semantic spaces in seconds or minutes, as opposed to the hours or days required for training large-scale neural models or computing PPMI on high-dimensional sparse matrices. This makes the method highly attractive for rapid prototyping, applications on resource-constrained hardware, or systems that require frequent model updates.

3.  **Strong and Well-Presented Empirical Results:** The evaluation on the MEN dataset is thorough and convincing. The progression from comparing PoP to RI (Figure 1), to showing the power of PoP+PPMI (Figure 2), is logical and clear. The histograms in Figure 3 provide an excellent and intuitive visualization of the core mechanism: PoP preserves a long-tailed distribution which PPMI can then transform into a more Gaussian-like one. The parameter analysis in Section 3.3 is also a strong point, providing valuable insights into the model's behavior.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, the method is described precisely, and the experiments are easy to follow and reproduce. The authors are also commendably transparent about the current limitations of their work, particularly the lack of a formal theoretical foundation.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The most significant weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is the absence of a theoretical justification akin to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections. While the empirical results are strong, the paper does not provide a formal account of what geometric or distributional properties the PoP transformation preserves or to what extent it introduces distortion. The argument that RI also lacked theory initially is historically true but does not fully absolve the need for it. Without a theoretical basis, it is difficult to reason about the method's expected behavior on different datasets or its potential failure modes.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The entire empirical evaluation rests on a single intrinsic task (semantic relatedness on the MEN dataset) in English. While MEN is a standard benchmark, high performance here does not guarantee strong performance on other tasks. It would be much more convincing to see results on word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999), which tests for more specific semantic relations, and especially on word analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset), which are a canonical benchmark for evaluating the structural properties of vector spaces. Furthermore, performance on a downstream extrinsic task would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of practical utility.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Neural Baselines:** While the paper claims performance is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," this comparison is based on scores reported in other papers, which may use different corpora, preprocessing, or hyperparameters. A more direct and compelling comparison would involve training a standard neural model like word2vec (SGNS) or GloVe on the exact same preprocessed UKWaC corpus and comparing its performance and training time against PoP+PPMI. Without this direct comparison, the claims of competitiveness with neural methods remain somewhat speculative.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Beyond the empirical results, can you provide more theoretical intuition about the properties of the PoP transformation? For instance, while it doesn't preserve Euclidean distance, is there another distance metric or property (perhaps related to non-negative matrix factorization) that it is approximately preserving?

2.  The performance of vector space models on relatedness (MEN) and similarity (SimLex-999) tasks can be quite different. Given that PPMI is known to capture more functional/topical similarity, have you evaluated your method on a benchmark like SimLex-999 that focuses on genuine similarity, and how does it perform?

3.  Could you report the performance of the non-PPMI PoP model using cosine similarity? This would provide a more direct, apples-to-apples comparison with the RI baseline in Figure 1, helping to isolate the effect of the PoP projection itself from the choice of similarity metric (Kendall's τb vs. cosine).
