### Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary of the Paper**
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word vector representations. The method is a variant of random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI). Its key innovation lies in using a projection matrix composed of sparse, positive integers. The authors claim this allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be subsequently transformed using weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a step that is mathematically impossible for standard RI models whose projections are zero-centered. The paper evaluates PoP on the MEN semantic relatedness task, showing that PoP vectors weighted with PPMI achieve results competitive with established dense PPMI models, but with significantly greater computational efficiency.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, which cleverly combines the efficiency of incremental random projections with the high performance of PPMI weighting. The key insight is that a positive-only projection preserves the necessary conditions for applying PPMI, unlike standard zero-mean random projections.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this combination can achieve performance on a semantic relatedness task that is comparable to computationally expensive dense PPMI models, while being constructed in seconds or minutes.

**Strengths**
1.  **Practical Efficiency:** The main strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. By enabling PPMI to be calculated on a low-dimensional (e.g., m=1000) matrix rather than a massive, high-dimensional word-context matrix, the authors present a practical solution to a well-known bottleneck in distributional semantics. This significantly lowers the barrier to using powerful weighting schemes.
2.  **Novel Combination of Established Techniques:** The paper successfully integrates two distinct lines of work in distributional semantics: efficient random projections and effective PPMI weighting. While the constituent parts are not new, the synthesis is. The identification of the zero-mean property of RI as the barrier to applying PPMI and the proposal of a positive-only projection to circumvent this is an insightful and valuable contribution.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on the Chosen Task:** Within the scope of the MEN relatedness task, the PoP+PPMI model demonstrates strong performance, achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75. This result is competitive with established count-based methods, validating the core claim that the method can produce high-quality embeddings efficiently.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The most significant weakness of this work is the absence of a formal theoretical foundation. The authors openly state, "we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for... the results obtained by the PoP method." While they draw an analogy to the early days of RI, this does not excuse the lack of analysis. Established random projection methods are typically backed by theoretical guarantees (e.g., the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections). Without such a foundation, it is unclear what geometric or distributional properties PoP is preserving, and why it should be expected to work in general. The method is presented as an empirical observation rather than a principled technique.
2.  **Limited and Incomplete Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (MEN relatedness). While the results are good, this is insufficient evidence to establish the general utility of a new word embedding method. Crucially, there is no comparison against standard, strong neural baselines like word2vec (SGNS) or GloVe, trained on the same UKWaC corpus. The authors claim comparability by citing scores from other papers, but this is not a substitute for a direct, controlled experiment. A thorough evaluation would require testing on a wider range of tasks (e.g., synonymy tests like TOEFL, analogy tasks) and a direct comparison with state-of-the-art baselines under identical conditions.
3.  **Ad-hoc Methodological Choices:** The overall methodology appears to be a chain of heuristic decisions. The choice of Kendall's τb for the unweighted vectors is motivated by a loose analogy to Pearson's correlation for Gaussian data, which is not a rigorous justification. Subsequently, the switch to Pearson's correlation after PPMI is justified by observing that the transformation produces a "Gaussian-like" distribution. While plausible, this reliance on observation rather than formal properties makes the method feel less like a cohesive framework and more like a collection of components that have been engineered to work for one specific task.
4.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments in Section 3.3 reveal that the method's performance is highly sensitive to the choice of parameters, particularly the dimensionality `m` and the number of non-zero elements `s`. More concerningly, the optimal direction of tuning for `s` appears to reverse depending on whether PPMI is applied or not. This suggests that the method is not robust and requires careful, task-specific tuning, which undermines some of its claimed simplicity and efficiency.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Can you provide any formal analysis, even if preliminary, of the properties of the proposed positive-only projection? For instance, what is the expected distortion of inner products or other similarity measures under this transformation, and how does it relate to the parameters `s` and `α`?
2.  The evaluation is currently limited to the MEN dataset. To demonstrate the general applicability of your method, could you provide results on other standard intrinsic benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and, importantly, a direct and controlled comparison against widely used neural baselines like SGNS (word2vec) and GloVe, trained on the same corpus with comparable dimensionality?
3.  The optimal number of non-zero elements appears to be very low (e.g., 2-4) for the PPMI-weighted model. This seems counter-intuitive, as a sparser projection matrix would presumably discard more information from the original count space. Do you have an explanation for why increased sparsity leads to better performance post-PPMI?
