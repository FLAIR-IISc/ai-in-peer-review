### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core problem it addresses is the trade-off between the computational efficiency of random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI) and the high accuracy of predictive models, which typically rely on expensive weighting schemes like PPMI applied to high-dimensional count matrices or on training neural networks. Standard RI cannot be combined with PPMI because its zero-centered projection matrix results in vectors whose component sums are zero, making the PPMI formula undefined. PoP circumvents this by using a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integers. This allows for the fast, incremental construction of low-dimensional vectors that, crucially, can still be transformed using PPMI. The authors demonstrate empirically on the MEN relatedness task that PoP+PPMI achieves results competitive with dense PPMI models and, by extension, neural embeddings, but at a fraction of the computational cost.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Projection Method Enabling Post-Hoc Weighting:** The primary contribution is the PoP method itself. It presents an elegant and simple solution to a known limitation of standard random projection techniques, effectively creating a new class of random projections that preserve the necessary properties (positive counts) for applying classic and powerful weighting schemes like PPMI directly in the low-dimensional space.

2.  **Bridging the Efficiency-Accuracy Gap:** The work successfully combines the speed and incrementality of random projections with the high performance of PPMI weighting. This provides a compelling practical alternative for generating high-quality word vectors without requiring the extensive computational resources and training time of neural models or the memory and processing power for dense matrix factorization.

3.  **Insightful Analysis of Similarity Measures:** The paper provides a well-reasoned justification for its choice of similarity metrics, connecting cosine/Pearson correlation to Gaussian-distributed data and motivating the use of the non-parametric Kendall's Tau-b for the non-Gaussian PoP vectors. This demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the interaction between vector space properties and similarity computation.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Elegance of the Core Idea:** The central idea is both simple and highly effective. Identifying that the zero-centered nature of RI projections was the barrier to applying PPMI, and proposing a positive-only projection as the solution, is a clever and insightful contribution. It solves a clear problem with an economical and well-motivated approach.

2.  **Significant Computational Advantage:** The paper's strongest practical argument is its computational efficiency. By moving the expensive PPMI calculation from a massive, sparse co-occurrence matrix (p x n, where n can be huge) to a small, dense projected matrix (p x m, where m is a few thousand), the method dramatically reduces both time and memory requirements. This makes high-quality distributional models accessible for rapid prototyping, low-resource settings, or applications requiring dynamic updates.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while focused on a single task, are well-designed and convincing. The paper clearly shows the progression from a standard count model to RI (which offers no accuracy benefit) to PoP (a clear improvement) and finally to PoP+PPMI (which reaches a competitive performance level of ρ ≈ 0.75 on MEN). The parameter analyses in Figures 4 and 5, exploring the effects of dimensionality and sparsity, add valuable depth and practical guidance for using the method.

4.  **Clear Exposition and Positioning:** The paper is well-written and clearly situates the work within the existing literature on count-based, SVD-based, and random projection models. The explanation of why RI fails with PPMI and how PoP succeeds is particularly lucid. The discussion of PPMI as a variance-stabilizing transformation that pushes distributions toward a Gaussian shape (Figure 3) is also excellent and provides strong motivation for switching to Pearson correlation post-transformation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The authors are commendably transparent about this in Section 2.3, but it remains a key weakness. Unlike standard random projections, which are backed by theoretical guarantees like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, PoP is presented without a formal analysis of the properties of the projection or the distortion it introduces. The specific choice of the projection distribution (`t_ij` derived from `U^0.5`) is not justified against other possible positive distributions (e.g., a sparse Poisson or even a simple binary projection). This makes the method feel more heuristic than theoretically principled.

2.  **Narrow Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on MEN) in English. While the performance is strong, modern word embeddings are typically evaluated on a wider suite of tasks, including both intrinsic similarity (e.g., SimLex-999, where PPMI's topical bias can be a disadvantage) and extrinsic, downstream applications (e.g., text classification, NER). It is unclear how PoP+PPMI would perform on these other tasks compared to neural embeddings, which are often optimized for syntactic or functional similarity.

3.  **Unclear Parameter Justification:** The choice of `α = 0.5` is presented without motivation. How sensitive is the model to this hyperparameter? Furthermore, the results in Figure 5 regarding the number of non-zero elements are intriguing but the explanation feels somewhat post-hoc. It is claimed that for unweighted PoP, more non-zero elements help, but for PoP+PPMI, extreme sparsity (1-2 non-zero elements) is best. This suggests a complex interaction that is not fully explored or explained, making parameter tuning for new applications less straightforward.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the projection distribution in Equation 2: Could you provide more intuition or empirical results motivating the choice of `floor(1/U^α)` with `α = 0.5`? Have you experimented with simpler positive sparse projections, such as setting a few random elements to 1 (a binary projection) or drawing from a Poisson distribution? How do these alternatives affect performance?

2.  The computational efficiency is a major claim. Could you provide concrete wall-clock timings and memory usage figures for building the PoP+PPMI model on the UKWaC corpus, compared directly against a dense PPMI+SVD baseline and a popular neural implementation like `word2vec` or `GloVe` running on the same hardware? This would quantify the practical benefits more explicitly.

3.  Your results on the MEN relatedness task are strong. However, PPMI is known to excel at capturing topical relatedness over semantic similarity. Have you evaluated PoP+PPMI on a task like SimLex-999, which is designed to penalize models that conflate these two concepts? This would help to better characterize the qualitative properties of the resulting embeddings.
