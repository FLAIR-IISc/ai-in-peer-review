### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel and remarkably efficient method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix populated with sparse, positive integers. Unlike standard random indexing (RI) which generates zero-mean vectors, this approach produces vectors where components are non-negative. This seemingly simple change has a profound consequence: it allows for the application of powerful weighting schemes like PPMI directly on the low-dimensional vectors. The authors demonstrate that this PoP+PPMI pipeline is computationally trivial compared to neural methods or SVD on high-dimensional count matrices, yet achieves highly competitive results on the MEN word relatedness task.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **The introduction of a positive-only random projection as a new primitive for semantic space construction.** This is a genuinely novel concept that elegantly bridges the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of traditional weighting schemes. It fundamentally rethinks what properties a projection should preserve, moving beyond just distance to enable subsequent transformations.
2.  **The development of a complete, fast, and effective pipeline (PoP+PPMI) for generating word embeddings.** The paper provides a full recipe—from projection, to weighting in the low-dimensional space, to selecting the appropriate similarity measure (Pearson's r) post-transformation—that works exceptionally well out of the box.
3.  **An insightful analysis connecting the statistical distribution of vector components to the choice of similarity measure.** The authors provide a clear rationale and empirical evidence (Fig. 3) for why PoP vectors are best compared with a non-parametric measure like Kendall's Tau, while the PPMI-transformed vectors, which approximate a Gaussian distribution, are better served by Pearson's correlation.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness of the Core Idea.** This paper presents a truly original approach. For years, the community has treated random projections (like RI) and count-based weighting schemes (like PPMI) as largely separate families of methods with distinct trade-offs. The authors' insight that a simple change to the nature of the projection matrix—making it positive-only—could unify these two paradigms is brilliant. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a conceptually new direction for research in distributional semantics. I highly value this kind of bold, creative thinking.
2.  **Massive Potential for Practical Impact.** The method's primary advantage is its staggering efficiency. By shifting the computationally expensive PPMI calculation from a high-dimensional, sparse matrix to a low-dimensional, dense one, the authors have created a method that can produce high-quality embeddings in seconds or minutes on standard hardware. This is a game-changer for applications requiring dynamic updates, rapid prototyping, or for researchers with limited computational budgets. It democratizes access to high-performance distributional models.
3.  **Compelling Empirical Validation of the Central Thesis.** The experiments are well-designed to highlight the power of the core contribution. Figure 2 is particularly effective, showing that while raw PoP is a modest improvement over RI, the ability to apply PPMI is what unlocks a dramatic performance boost, bringing the method into the same league as much more complex approaches. The final reported scores (~0.75-0.78 on MEN) are very strong and provide convincing evidence that this lightweight method is a serious contender.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition and Intuition.** The paper does an excellent job of explaining *why* the method works. The discussion on how PPMI acts as a variance-stabilizing transformation that reshapes the vector component distribution towards a Gaussian is particularly insightful. The histograms in Figure 3 provide a superb and intuitive visualization of this process, clearly illustrating the limitations of RI and the unique properties of PoP.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The authors are commendably transparent about the absence of a formal mathematical account for PoP (Section 2.3). While they draw analogies to existing work on random projections, the properties of this specific positive, non-centered projection are not formally derived. In many contexts, this would be a significant flaw. However, given the novelty of the approach and the strength of the empirical results, I view this not as a fatal weakness, but as an acceptable omission that paves the way for exciting future theoretical work. The empirical success of such a bold idea justifies its publication, even if the theory is not yet fully mature.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation.** The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on MEN) using a single corpus. While the results are strong, it remains an open question how these vectors perform on downstream extrinsic tasks like text classification, parsing, or question answering. The utility of an embedding method is ultimately determined by its performance in such applications. I would have liked to see at least one extrinsic evaluation to demonstrate broader utility.
3.  **Unclear Guidance on Hyperparameter Tuning.** The analysis in Section 3.3 reveals that performance is sensitive to key parameters like dimensionality (`m`) and the sparsity/number of non-zero elements (`s`). The findings are somewhat complex (e.g., more non-zero elements helping raw PoP but hurting PoP+PPMI). While the authors offer plausible explanations, the paper could benefit from clearer, more prescriptive guidance on how to set these parameters for a new use case. This leaves a degree of "black art" to the method's application.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice of the `floor(1/U^0.5)` distribution for the non-zero projection elements seems quite specific. Have you explored simpler alternatives, such as using a constant value of 1 for all non-zero elements? Is the method's success primarily due to the positivity of the projection, or does the specific distribution of integer values play a critical role?
2.  Your central claim is a massive improvement in efficiency. This is very compelling, but the paper lacks a direct, quantitative comparison. Could you provide approximate wall-clock times and memory footprints for the end-to-end pipeline of (a) traditional PPMI+SVD vs. (b) your PoP+PPMI approach on the UKWaC corpus?
3.  You note that applying SVD to the final PoP+PPMI vectors offers negligible improvement but can be used for further dimensionality reduction. Does this imply that the PoP projection followed by PPMI weighting is effectively accomplishing a similar type of "denoising" or "latent feature discovery" that SVD is known for? I would be interested in your thoughts on the relationship between these two processes.
