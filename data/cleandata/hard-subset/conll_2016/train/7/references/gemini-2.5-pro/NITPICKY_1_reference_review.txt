### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing semantic vector spaces. The method is presented as an alternative to both computationally expensive neural embeddings and existing random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI). The primary contribution is the design of a random projection matrix populated with sparse, positive integers. Unlike standard RI, which projects vectors into a space with a zero mean, this positive-only projection creates low-dimensional vectors that remain amenable to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors claim that the combination of PoP for dimensionality reduction followed by PPMI weighting achieves performance on the MEN semantic relatedness task that is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, but at a fraction of the computational cost. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis motivating the use of different similarity measures (Kendall's τb vs. Pearson's r) for unweighted and PPMI-weighted PoP spaces, respectively.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Practical Method:** The core idea of developing a random projection scheme that is compatible with PPMI is both novel and highly practical. It directly addresses a known limitation of methods like RI, where the resulting zero-centered vectors make the marginal sums required for PMI/PPMI calculation undefined. This work provides an elegant and efficient bridge between the speed of random projections and the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed PoP+PPMI+Pearson approach demonstrates impressive results on the MEN relatedness benchmark, achieving a Spearman's ρ around 0.75. This performance is competitive with significantly more complex and computationally intensive methods, including several neural embedding models cited by the authors. This validates the central claim that the method offers a compelling trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Investigation of Parameters:** The authors provide a useful analysis of how the model's performance is affected by its key hyperparameters: the dimensionality `m` and the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors (related to `s`). The findings, such as the observation that sparser index vectors are better for PPMI-weighted models, provide valuable practical guidance for using the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Flaw in Similarity Measure Definition:** The definition of Kendall's τb provided in Section 2.2 is fundamentally incorrect for the task of comparing vector similarity. The paper defines concordant/discordant pairs by comparing adjacent components `(xj, yj)` and `(xj+1, yj+1)`. This implies a sequential ordering of vector dimensions, which is meaningless in a word space model where dimensions are unordered. The standard definition requires comparing all pairs of dimension indices `(i, j)`. This error is not merely a typo; it represents a serious misunderstanding of the similarity measure. It calls into question the validity of all results reported using Kendall's τb, as it is impossible for the reader to know what was actually implemented and measured.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Precision and Justification:** The description of the PoP method is insufficiently rigorous.
    *   The notation `b 1Uα c` in Equation 2 is non-standard and undefined. It is unclear if this represents a floor, ceiling, or rounding operation, which is critical for reproducibility.
    *   The hyperparameter `α` is set to 0.5 with no justification, appearing as a "magic number."
    *   The parameter `s` is vaguely described as "extremely small." While its value is tied to the number of non-zero elements, the procedure for selecting it to satisfy the constraint of having at least one non-zero element per index vector is not specified.
    *   There is a glaring notational inconsistency between Equation 1, which defines the output dimensionality as `x`, and the subsequent text, which consistently uses `m`.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Inconsistencies:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, typos, and unclear figures that detract from its readability and professionalism.
    *   Figures 1 and 2 use inconsistent and confusing x-axis labels (e.g., "+1", "4+4", "+9"). A consistent representation (e.g., total window size or symmetric window radius) should be used.
    *   Multiple figures contain typos in axis labels (e.g., "Spearman’s" is misspelled). Figure 2 has a duplicated legend entry ("PPMI-Dense+Cos").
    *   Figure 6 is particularly confusing. The y-axis label "P 6⊥" appears to be a typesetting error, and the right-hand plot's x-axis is simply labeled "n" while the caption describes `n` increasing.
    *   The final high score of 0.78 is mentioned with the caveat that it required "carefully selecting context elements" and "fine tuning," but these crucial details are omitted, hindering reproducibility.

4.  **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The authors openly concede in Section 2.3 that they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account" for the method's behavior or error bounds. While empirical results are valuable, the justification for the method's properties relies heavily on analogy to other techniques (e.g., citing Kaski (1998) for variance bounds) rather than formal derivation. Citing the historical precedent of RI (where theory followed practice) is not a substitute for providing theoretical support in a contemporary publication.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please clarify the exact definition of Kendall's τb that was used in your implementation. Does your code compare all pairs of dimension indices `(i, j)`, or does it follow the flawed sequential definition `(j, j+1)` presented in the paper? If the latter, the results must be re-evaluated and re-reported.

2.  What is the precise mathematical operation denoted by `b 1Uα c` in Equation 2? Is it floor, ceiling, or another function? And what is the motivation for setting `α = 0.5`?

3.  In Figure 4, the performance of unweighted PoP drops for `m > 2000` when the number of non-zero elements is fixed at 4. You hypothesize this is due to `s` becoming too small. Have you conducted an experiment where you fix `m` at a high value (e.g., 4000) and vary the number of non-zero elements (as in Figure 5) to confirm that performance can be recovered by increasing `s`?
