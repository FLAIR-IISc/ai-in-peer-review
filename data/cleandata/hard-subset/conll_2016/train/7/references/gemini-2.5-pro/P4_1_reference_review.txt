### Summary

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic vector spaces. The method is similar in spirit to Random Indexing (RI) in that it uses a sparse random projection matrix to build word vectors incrementally from a corpus, avoiding the need to store a full high-dimensional co-occurrence matrix. The key innovation of PoP is the design of its random projection matrix, which contains only non-negative integers. This property ensures that the resulting low-dimensional vectors also have non-negative components and a non-zero mean, unlike vectors produced by standard RI. The authors argue that this allows for the application of effective weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) directly to the low-dimensional vectors. This is a significant advantage, as PPMI is known to improve the quality of count-based models but cannot be applied to RI vectors. The paper provides an empirical evaluation on the MEN semantic relatedness task, demonstrating that the combination of PoP with a subsequent PPMI transformation achieves performance competitive with more computationally intensive methods, while retaining the efficiency of incremental, random projection-based approaches.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel random projection method (PoP) compatible with count-based weighting schemes.** The primary contribution is the PoP method itself. It bridges the gap between efficient but limited random projection techniques (like RI) and effective but computationally expensive weighting schemes (like PPMI). By designing a projection matrix with non-negative entries, the authors create a method that is both incremental and efficient, yet produces vectors that can be enhanced by well-established techniques like PPMI, which was not possible with prior random projection methods.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of an efficient and high-performing word embedding pipeline.** The paper presents and evaluates a complete pipeline (PoP+PPMI+Pearson correlation) that is computationally efficient yet achieves strong results on a standard semantic relatedness benchmark. This provides a practical and effective alternative to computationally demanding approaches like training neural networks or performing SVD on large PPMI matrices.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Practical Utility.** The core idea of designing a random projection to be compatible with PPMI is novel and solves a clear limitation of existing methods like Random Indexing. It combines the efficiency benefits of incremental random projections with the proven performance benefits of PPMI weighting. This makes the method highly practical, particularly for scenarios with limited computational resources or a need for rapid, on-the-fly model construction from large corpora, as it avoids both the memory requirements of full co-occurrence matrices and the training time of neural models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Efficiency.** The paper demonstrates that the proposed PoP+PPMI pipeline achieves a Spearman's correlation of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset, which is a strong result and competitive with many established models. The authors compellingly argue that this performance is achieved with a fraction of the computational cost of alternatives like neural embeddings or SVD on a full PPMI matrix. The speedup comes from both the incremental construction and applying the PPMI calculation on the already low-dimensional vectors.
3.  **Thorough Analysis of Method Components.** The evaluation is well-structured and provides valuable insights into the method's behavior. The authors systematically compare PoP to count-based and RI baselines, clearly showing its advantage. They justify the choice of different similarity metrics (Kendall's tau for raw PoP, Pearson's r for PPMI-transformed vectors) with sound statistical reasoning, linking the metric to the underlying distribution of the vector components (Figure 3). The ablation studies on dimensionality (`m`) and sparsity (`s`) (Figures 4 and 5) further strengthen the paper by providing guidance on parameter tuning and revealing interesting trade-offs.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Justification.** The authors candidly admit the lack of a formal mathematical account for the properties of the PoP projection. While RI is supported by principles like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, PoP is justified primarily by empirical results. The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of how the PoP projection (Equation 2) distorts or preserves properties of the original space, and why applying the PPMI formula to the projected space is a principled operation. This absence of theory makes it difficult to understand the method's fundamental limitations or guarantees.
2.  **Narrow Scope of Evaluation.** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a single dataset (MEN) for a single task (semantic relatedness). While the results are strong, state-of-the-art distributional models are typically evaluated on a broader suite of benchmarks, including word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999), analogy tasks (e.g., Google analogy dataset), and downstream applications. Without a more comprehensive evaluation, the claim of being "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" is not fully substantiated, as different methods excel on different tasks.
3.  **Ambiguity in the Application of PPMI.** The PPMI formula (Equation 3) is defined over a co-occurrence matrix where rows represent target entities and columns represent discrete context features. In the PoP+PPMI pipeline, this formula is applied to the dense, low-dimensional matrix `P`, where the columns no longer correspond to explicit contexts but are arbitrary dimensions resulting from the random projection. It is not clear what the marginal sums over these random dimensions signify or how they relate to the true marginals from the original high-dimensional space. The paper would be stronger if it provided a justification for why this application of PPMI is a meaningful approximation of standard PPMI.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The PPMI transformation is applied to the low-dimensional projected matrix `P`. Can you provide a more detailed justification for this step? How do the row and column sums of `P` (used in the denominator of the PPMI formula) relate to the corresponding sums in the original (unseen) high-dimensional count matrix `C`? Is this an approximation, and if so, can you characterize the nature or error of this approximation?
2.  The empirical evaluation is focused on the MEN relatedness task. To better situate your method's performance, have you conducted any experiments on other standard intrinsic evaluation benchmarks, such as word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) or the word analogy task? The performance on these tasks would provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities compared to other methods.
3.  Figure 4 shows that for the unweighted PoP model, performance decreases as dimensionality `m` increases beyond 2000 (when the number of non-zero elements is fixed). This is counter-intuitive for a random projection method. You suggest this may be due to `s` becoming too small. Could you elaborate on this? Does this imply a tight coupling between the optimal dimensionality and the sparsity of the projection that needs to be carefully tuned?
