### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The method is a variant of random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI). Its defining feature is the use of a random projection matrix containing only sparse, positive integer values. The authors argue that unlike standard RI, which projects vectors into a space with a zero mean, PoP preserves the positive, heavy-tailed distribution characteristic of raw count vectors. This key property allows for the subsequent application of powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is not possible with standard RI. The paper empirically demonstrates that the combination of PoP with PPMI (and Pearson correlation for similarity) achieves performance on the MEN word relatedness task that is competitive with computationally expensive methods like SVD or neural embeddings, but at a fraction of the computational cost.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection method that enables count-based weighting in a low-dimensional space.** The core innovation is the design of a positive-only projection matrix. This elegantly bridges the gap between the efficiency and incrementality of random projection methods and the high accuracy of PPMI-weighted models, which were previously considered incompatible.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness and efficiency.** The paper shows that the proposed PoP+PPMI model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard word relatedness benchmark, rivaling much more complex models while being orders of magnitude faster to compute.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Method:** The central idea of using a positive-only projection is both simple and highly effective. It solves a well-known limitation of methods like Random Indexing, namely their incompatibility with PPMI weighting. By creating a low-dimensional space that retains "count-like" properties, the authors have developed a practical tool that combines the best of both worlds: the speed and scalability of random projections and the proven performance of PPMI. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of distributional semantics.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Practicality:** The paper presents compelling results on the MEN dataset, achieving a Spearman's ρ of up to 0.78. This places the method in the same performance tier as popular but computationally intensive techniques like word2vec or SVD-based models. The claimed efficiency—building models in minutes rather than hours or days—is a massive practical advantage, making high-quality distributional models accessible for rapid prototyping, resource-constrained settings, or applications requiring frequent updates.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The evaluation goes well beyond reporting a single score. The authors provide a clear and well-supported analysis of their method's behavior. The comparison against count-based and RI baselines (Figure 1) effectively isolates the benefits of PoP. Most impressively, the visualization of vector component distributions (Figure 3) provides a clear, intuitive explanation for *why* PoP works with PPMI while RI does not. The subsequent parameter analysis (Figures 4-5) demonstrates a deep understanding of the model's properties and offers valuable practical guidance to future users.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they cannot provide a mathematical account for the properties of their projection. While empirical success is valuable, the paper would be substantially stronger with some theoretical justification. Standard random projections are motivated by principles like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which guarantees the preservation of certain geometric properties (e.g., L2 distances). It is unclear what properties PoP aims to preserve. A more formal discussion of this, even if preliminary, would help situate the method within the broader literature and provide a basis for understanding its behavior and limitations.

2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The empirical validation relies entirely on a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, different models often excel at different tasks (e.g., similarity vs. relatedness, intrinsic vs. downstream). To robustly claim that the method is "competitive" with neural embeddings, evaluation on a more diverse set of benchmarks is necessary, such as word similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), analogy tasks, or its use as features in a downstream application like NER or text classification.

3.  **Unclear Hyperparameter Tuning Protocol:** The experiments in Section 3.3 demonstrate that the model's performance is quite sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality `m` and the number of non-zero elements in the index vectors. However, the paper does not state how these parameters were chosen for the main results reported in Section 3.2. Without a clear description of a development set or cross-validation procedure, it raises concerns that the hyperparameters may have been tuned directly on the test set, potentially leading to an overestimation of performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter selection process for the main results presented in Figure 2 and the final reported score of 0.78? Was a separate development set used for tuning the dimensionality and sparsity of the PoP vectors?
2.  Your best results are achieved using PoP+PPMI+Pearson, which relies on the fact that PPMI transforms the distribution to be more Gaussian-like. This seems to reduce the importance of Kendall's τb, which was introduced as a key similarity measure for the non-Gaussian PoP vectors. Could you comment on the role of Kendall's τb in your final proposed framework? Is it primarily a tool for analyzing the unweighted PoP space, or do you see scenarios where it would be preferred over the Pearson correlation pipeline?
