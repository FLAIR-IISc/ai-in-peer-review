### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The central problem the authors address is the trade-off between computationally efficient but less accurate methods like Random Indexing (RI), and more accurate but computationally expensive methods like PPMI weighting on dense co-occurrence matrices followed by SVD, or training neural network embeddings.

The key idea is to modify the random projection matrix used in RI-like methods. Instead of drawing from a zero-centered distribution (e.g., {-1, 0, 1}), PoP uses a projection matrix with sparse, positive integer values. The authors argue that this fundamental change allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to retain a distribution similar to raw counts (i.e., non-negative and heavy-tailed). This property is crucial as it makes the vectors amenable to powerful post-processing weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is impossible with standard RI vectors whose column/row sums are expected to be zero. The authors empirically demonstrate that applying PPMI to PoP vectors (PoP+PPMI) on the MEN word relatedness task yields results competitive with dense PPMI-based models, while being significantly faster and more memory-efficient.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The PoP method itself:** A simple and elegant modification to the random projection framework that bridges the gap between incremental, projection-based models and effective statistical weighting schemes like PPMI. This is a conceptually novel and significant contribution.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness and efficiency:** The paper provides a clear and convincing evaluation showing that PoP+PPMI is a highly practical method that achieves strong performance on a standard semantic task without the high computational cost of traditional PPMI+SVD or neural models.
3.  **Insightful analysis of the resulting vector spaces:** The authors correctly identify that PPMI acts as a variance-stabilizing transformation, moving the vector component distributions towards a Gaussian shape. This justifies their principled choice of similarity metrics: the non-parametric Kendall's Tau for raw PoP vectors and the parametric Pearson correlation for the normalized PoP+PPMI vectors.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Practical Impact:** The core idea is both simple and highly effective. For years, the community has treated random projection methods (like RI) and count-based weighting methods (like PPMI) as largely separate families. This work provides an elegant way to combine their respective strengths: the incrementality and efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI. This has significant practical implications, making it possible to build high-quality distributional models on large corpora with modest computational resources.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper's primary claim of drastically reducing computation time is well-supported. By projecting to a low-dimensional space *first* and then applying PPMI, the authors circumvent the main bottleneck of traditional methods, which is computing PPMI on a massive, high-dimensional co-occurrence matrix. Building models in "seconds or minutes" instead of hours or days is a major practical advantage.

3.  **Well-Designed Experiments and Analysis:** The evaluation is methodical and convincing. The authors wisely compare their method against the most relevant baselines: raw counts, RI, and dense PMI/PPMI. The figures clearly illustrate the performance gains. The analysis in Section 3.2 and Figure 3, which visually demonstrates how PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution of counts and how PPMI normalizes it, is particularly insightful and adds significant depth to the paper.

4.  **Incrementality:** The paper highlights that PoP is an incremental method, a feature inherited from RI. This is a crucial advantage over methods requiring matrix factorization (SVD) or batch training (neural networks), as PoP models can be easily updated with new data without reprocessing the entire corpus. This strength could have been emphasized even more.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The authors are commendably upfront about the lack of a detailed mathematical account for the properties of their projection (Section 2.3). However, this remains a significant weakness. While RI is grounded in the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma (approximating Euclidean distances), the theoretical properties of PoP are left unexplored. The authors state `σ²δ ≈ 1/m` by analogy to other methods, but do not derive it. Without a stronger theoretical basis, the method feels more like a successful and well-motivated heuristic than a fully-principled technique.

2.  **Narrow Evaluation Scope:** The entire empirical evaluation rests on a single task (word relatedness on the MEN dataset). While MEN is a standard benchmark, state-of-the-art embedding evaluation relies on a wider suite of tasks, including synonym identification (e.g., TOEFL) and, crucially, analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset). PPMI-based models traditionally struggle with the linear offset properties required for analogy tasks. It is unclear if PoP+PPMI shares this limitation or overcomes it. This narrow evaluation makes it difficult to assess the general applicability of the proposed method.

3.  **Dated Comparison to Neural Models:** The paper positions itself as an efficient alternative to neural embeddings, but the comparisons are largely qualitative and cite somewhat dated experiences (e.g., a two-month training time from a 2014 paper). Modern implementations of Word2Vec or GloVe can be trained on corpora like UKWaC in hours, not months. A direct quantitative comparison of PoP+PPMI against these well-established neural baselines (e.g., SGNS with negative sampling) in terms of both performance *and* training time/memory usage would have provided a much stronger and more current context for the paper's contributions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice of the power `α = 0.5` in the projection matrix construction (Equation 2) seems arbitrary. Have you experimented with other values? This seems like an important hyperparameter that could affect the distribution of the projected vectors, and by extension, the effectiveness of the subsequent PPMI weighting.

2.  Could you elaborate on how PoP+PPMI might perform on analogy tasks (e.g., king - man + woman ≈ queen)? Standard PPMI+SVD vectors often fail at these tasks, while neural embeddings like Word2Vec excel. Does the random projection component of PoP introduce structural properties that might be more conducive to solving analogies than standard PPMI, or do you expect it to have the same limitations?

3.  To substantiate the claims of efficiency, could you provide concrete wall-clock timings and peak memory usage for your main experiment (UKWaC, 4+4 window) for the following methods: (a) PoP+PPMI, (b) dense PPMI followed by SVD, and (c) a standard Word2Vec (SGNS) implementation? This would provide a much clearer picture of the practical trade-offs involved.
