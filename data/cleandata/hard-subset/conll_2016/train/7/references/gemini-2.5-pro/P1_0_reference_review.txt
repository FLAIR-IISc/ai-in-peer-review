### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing semantic vector spaces based on random projections. The key innovation is the design of a projection matrix containing only non-negative values. This property, unlike in standard Random Indexing (RI), allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be post-processed with effective weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors argue that this combines the high efficiency and incrementality of random projection methods with the high accuracy of PPMI-weighted models, achieving results competitive with computationally expensive neural embedding techniques in a fraction of the time.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **The PoP method:** A novel random projection technique that, by using a positive-only projection matrix, creates low-dimensional vectors that are amenable to subsequent PPMI weighting. This is a clever and practical solution to a known limitation of methods like RI.
2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a convincing empirical demonstration that the combination of PoP and PPMI (PoP+PPMI) significantly outperforms baselines like count-based models and RI, and achieves performance on the MEN relatedness task (ρ ≈ 0.75) that is competitive with established, more complex methods.
3.  **Insightful statistical analysis:** The paper offers a clear explanation of *why* PoP enables PPMI and provides a well-reasoned justification for its choice of similarity metrics, correctly identifying that Kendall's τb is suitable for the non-Gaussian raw PoP vectors, while Pearson's correlation is better after the PPMI transformation makes the distribution more Gaussian-like. The histograms in Figure 3 are particularly effective in illustrating this point.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Practical Utility:** The paper addresses a clear gap between two major families of word embedding methods: fast but less accurate random projection models and accurate but slow PPMI/SVD or neural models. The proposed PoP method is an elegant and simple idea that effectively bridges this gap. It offers a highly practical tool for researchers who need to generate high-quality embeddings quickly, on large corpora, or with limited computational resources. The ability to build models incrementally is another significant practical advantage.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental section is well-structured and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The authors systematically compare PoP against relevant baselines (count-based, RI) to establish its superiority (Figure 1). More importantly, they demonstrate the massive performance boost gained by applying PPMI (Figure 2), which is the core selling point of the method. The ablation studies on the effect of dimensionality and sparsity (Figures 4 and 5) add depth to the analysis and provide useful guidance for practitioners.

3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written, with a clear motivation, a concise description of the method, and a logical flow. The use of matrix notation in Equation 1 helps frame the problem clearly, and the connection to the incremental RI construction algorithm makes the implementation straightforward to understand. The visualizations, especially the histograms in Figure 3, are excellent and provide strong intuitive support for the authors' statistical arguments.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The most significant weakness is the absence of a theoretical foundation for the proposed projection. The authors acknowledge this in Section 2.3, noting they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account." While methods like RI also began as empirical observations, the lack of a formal analysis (e.g., a variant of the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for this specific projection) makes it difficult to understand what geometric or distributional properties PoP preserves, what its error bounds are, and how it might behave on different data distributions. This currently positions PoP as a powerful and well-engineered heuristic rather than a theoretically principled method.

2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While MEN is a standard benchmark, the performance of word embedding models is known to vary significantly across different tasks. Claims of being "comparable to many neural embedding techniques" would be substantially stronger if validated on other canonical tasks, such as word analogy (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) or downstream applications like text classification or named entity recognition, where models like word2vec and GloVe have excelled.

3.  **Arbitrary Choice of Projection Distribution:** The paper defines the non-zero elements of the projection matrix based on `floor(1/U^0.5)` where `U` is a uniform random variable. However, there is no justification provided for this specific choice (`α = 0.5`). It is unclear if this distribution was selected based on theoretical principles, empirical tuning, or convenience. Exploring and motivating this choice would make the method feel less arbitrary and could provide insights into what makes a positive-only projection effective.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the lack of theoretical grounding: While a full proof is likely beyond the scope of a conference paper, could you elaborate on any preliminary theoretical work or intuitions you have about why this projection works? For example, what properties of the original space do you hypothesize are being preserved or usefully distorted by the PoP transformation?

2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific distribution `floor(1/U^0.5)` for the non-zero projection values? Have you experimented with other positive-only distributions (e.g., a discrete Poisson, or simply setting all non-zero values to 1), and if so, how did they compare?

3.  The results on the MEN relatedness task are very promising. Have you performed any preliminary experiments on other common intrinsic evaluation tasks, particularly the word analogy task? This would be very helpful for situating PoP+PPMI's performance relative to popular predictive models like word2vec and GloVe, which are known to excel at capturing linear algebraic relationships.
