**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. PoP is based on random projections, similar to Random Indexing (RI), but critically, it uses a projection matrix containing only non-negative values. This ensures that the resulting low-dimensional vectors are not zero-centered and retain distributional properties of the original high-dimensional count space. The main benefit of this approach is that it makes the low-dimensional vectors amenable to powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a transformation that is inapplicable to standard RI vectors. The authors demonstrate empirically that applying PPMI to PoP vectors results in embeddings that achieve competitive performance on the MEN word relatedness task, while being significantly more efficient to compute than both neural network models and traditional PPMI+SVD methods applied to high-dimensional matrices.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel random projection technique (PoP) that enables the application of count-based weighting schemes like PPMI directly in the low-dimensional space. By using positive-only random vectors, the method cleverly circumvents a key limitation of standard Random Indexing, bridging the gap between efficient incremental methods and high-accuracy weighting techniques.
2.  The paper presents a highly efficient and scalable pipeline (PoP+PPMI) for generating high-quality word embeddings. It offers a practical and compelling alternative to computationally expensive methods, achieving comparable accuracy to established models in a fraction of the time and with minimal computational resources.

**Strengths**
1.  **Elegant and Novel Method:** The core idea of using positive-only projections is simple, well-motivated, and effectively addresses a known shortcoming of Random Indexing (i.e., the inability to apply PPMI). The paper clearly articulates the problem with zero-centered projections and provides a straightforward and effective solution. This represents a clever and valuable contribution to the family of count-based and random projection models.
2.  **Significant Efficiency Gains:** The method's main appeal is its computational efficiency. By combining the one-pass, incremental nature of random projections with PPMI weighting in a low-dimensional space, the paper presents a method that can build high-quality models in minutes rather than hours or days. This is a substantial practical advantage, particularly for researchers with limited computational resources or applications requiring rapid model creation on very large corpora.
3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The experimental results on the MEN benchmark are convincing. The authors show that PoP consistently outperforms raw count-based and standard RI models. More importantly, the PoP+PPMI configuration achieves a Spearman correlation of ~0.75, which is highly competitive with state-of-the-art results from much more complex models. The ablation studies on key hyperparameters like dimensionality and sparsity (Figures 4 and 5) are thorough and provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's empirical validation relies entirely on a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on the MEN dataset). While MEN is a standard benchmark, strong performance on this task does not guarantee the general utility of the embeddings for downstream applications. To make a stronger case for PoP as a general-purpose embedding method, an evaluation on a broader suite of tasks, including extrinsic evaluations (e.g., text classification, named entity recognition), is necessary. Without this, the claims of competitiveness with neural embeddings remain only partially substantiated.
2.  **Absence of Theoretical Justification:** The authors candidly acknowledge the lack of a formal mathematical account for why their projection works (Section 2.3). Unlike standard random projections, which are supported by theoretical results like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, PoP is presented without a theoretical understanding of what geometric or distributional properties it preserves. While empirical success is valuable, this theoretical gap makes it difficult to reason about the method's limitations, its expected behavior on different data distributions, or its relationship to other dimensionality reduction techniques.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Key Baselines:** The primary high-performing count-based baseline is PPMI followed by Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The paper mentions SVD only in passing, claiming it gives "negligible improvements." This is surprising, as PPMI+SVD is a very strong and widely cited baseline (e.g., Levy et al., 2015). A direct and rigorous comparison of PoP+PPMI against PPMI+SVD, detailing both final performance and wall-clock time for computation, would be essential for properly contextualizing the contribution. The current "PMI-Dense" baseline is not sufficiently explained, especially regarding its computation for large context windows, which the authors state is intractable.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a more direct comparison against the PPMI+SVD baseline? Specifically, could you report performance and total computation time (matrix creation, PPMI, and SVD) for PPMI+SVD versus your PoP+PPMI method on the same data and task? This would provide a clearer picture of the trade-offs involved.
2.  The performance of unweighted PoP in Figure 4 surprisingly degrades as dimensionality increases past m=2000. Your hypothesis is that the sparsity parameter `s` becomes too small. Does this suggest a coupled relationship between the optimal dimensionality `m` and the number of non-zero elements, potentially complicating hyperparameter tuning? Could you elaborate on the practical guidance for setting these parameters?
3.  Given that a key motivation for efficient embeddings is their use in downstream tasks, have you performed any preliminary experiments on extrinsic benchmarks? Any results, even on a small scale, would greatly strengthen the paper's claim that PoP provides a compelling alternative to methods like word2vec or GloVe.
