### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic vector spaces. The method is inspired by Random Indexing (RI) but uses a projection matrix composed of non-negative integers. This key difference allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to retain a count-like, non-negative distribution, making them amenable to subsequent weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). Standard RI vectors, being zero-centered, are incompatible with PPMI. The authors demonstrate empirically that applying PPMI to PoP vectors (PoP+PPMI) leads to a computationally efficient model that achieves competitive performance on the MEN word relatedness task, rivaling traditional dense PPMI models and neural embeddings without their associated high computational costs.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel random projection method that uniquely enables post-projection weighting.** The core contribution is the design of a positive-only random projection that bridges the gap between efficient, incremental methods like Random Indexing and powerful weighting schemes like PPMI. This allows for the creation of low-dimensional vectors that can still be refined with mature, effective techniques from the count-based literature, combining the benefits of both paradigms.
2.  **An efficient and high-performing alternative for word vector construction.** The paper demonstrates that the PoP+PPMI method is not only orders of magnitude faster to compute than traditional PPMI+SVD or neural network training, but also achieves a high level of performance on a standard semantic relatedness benchmark. This presents a strong practical contribution for scenarios where computational resources or training time are constrained.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance.** The paper's central idea is both novel and insightful. Identifying the zero-mean property of standard random projections as the barrier to applying PPMI, and proposing positive-only projections as a simple and effective solution, is a clear conceptual advance. It elegantly solves a known limitation and opens up new possibilities for combining random projection methods with other distributional techniques.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Efficiency.** The method achieves a Spearman correlation of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset (and up to 0.787 with tuning), which is highly competitive with strong baselines and established neural methods. The primary advantage is that this performance is achieved with remarkable efficiency—building models in seconds or minutes. This combination of high accuracy and low computational cost is a significant strength.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis.** The paper provides a clear and well-reasoned experimental analysis. The authors justify their choice of similarity metrics (Kendall's τ for raw PoP, Pearson's r for PPMI-transformed PoP) with a thoughtful discussion of the underlying vector distributions. The visualizations in Figure 3, showing the distributional effects of different modeling choices, are particularly effective and greatly aid the reader's understanding of why the method works. The ablation studies on dimensionality and sparsity (Section 3.3) are also valuable for understanding the model's behavior.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation Scope.** The empirical validation of the method relies entirely on a single task (word relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While this is a standard benchmark, the general utility of the resulting embeddings is unknown. State-of-the-art embedding methods are typically evaluated on a wider suite of tasks, including word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999), analogy tasks, and downstream applications (e.g., text classification, NER). Without broader evaluation, it is difficult to ascertain whether PoP vectors are generally useful or are particularly tuned to the properties of the MEN relatedness task.
2.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding.** The authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that the method currently lacks a formal theoretical foundation. While many successful methods in NLP begin with empirical success, the absence of any analysis on what geometric or distributional properties PoP preserves is a significant weakness. It is unclear why the specific distribution in Equation 2 is effective, or how the error (δ) behaves. This makes the method feel more like a successful heuristic than a principled technique, leaving open questions about its stability and generalizability.
3.  **Comparisons to Neural Baselines are Indirect.** The paper claims competitiveness with neural embeddings but does not include a direct, controlled comparison. Instead, it references scores from other papers, which may use different corpora, preprocessing, or hyperparameters. A more convincing evaluation would involve training a standard neural model like word2vec (SGNS) or GloVe on the same UKWaC corpus and reporting its performance. While the reported score of 0.787 is strong, its standing relative to the state-of-the-art is not rigorously established within the paper's own experimental framework.
4.  **Unclear Guidance on Hyperparameter Tuning.** The analysis in Section 3.3 reveals that performance is sensitive to key hyperparameters like the projected dimension (`m`) and the number of non-zero elements (`s`). The results show some counter-intuitive behaviors (e.g., performance of unweighted PoP decreasing with higher dimensions) and opposing trends for weighted vs. unweighted models. While the paper provides recommendations for the MEN task, it offers little general guidance on how to set these crucial parameters for new datasets or tasks, which could limit the method's practical adoption.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You convincingly argue for the efficiency of PoP+PPMI. Could you provide a more formal comparison of its computational complexity against PPMI+SVD and a standard Skip-gram neural network model? An analysis in terms of key variables (vocabulary size V, context size C, corpus size N, projected dimension m) would substantially strengthen your claims of practical efficiency.
2.  The distribution for the random projection matrix in Equation 2 seems quite specific. Have you explored simpler positive distributions, for instance, a sparse binary projection (where non-zero elements are all 1) or a sparse Poisson distribution? How critical is the specific `floor(1/U^0.5)` formulation to the final performance?
3.  Given the effectiveness of PoP vectors for relatedness, have you conducted any preliminary experiments on other canonical embedding evaluation tasks? In particular, performance on a word similarity task like SimLex-999 (which emphasizes similarity over relatedness) or a syntactic/semantic analogy task (like the Google analogy dataset) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the properties of the vectors you produce.
