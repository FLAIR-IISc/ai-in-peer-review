### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core problem addressed is the trade-off between the efficiency of random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI) and the high performance of computationally expensive predictive models (e.g., neural embeddings or PPMI+SVD). Standard RI methods produce zero-centered vectors, which precludes the application of effective weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The key innovation of PoP is to use a random projection matrix containing only non-negative integers. This allows for the creation of low-dimensional, incrementally-built vectors that, crucially, can be post-processed with PPMI. The authors demonstrate empirically on the MEN relatedness task that PoP combined with PPMI and Pearson correlation achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods, but with a fraction of the computational cost.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **The PoP Method:** A novel, non-negative random projection technique that bridges the gap between traditional random indexing and count-based weighting schemes. This is a simple but highly effective conceptual contribution.
2.  **Empirical Validation of Efficiency and Performance:** A strong empirical demonstration that applying PPMI to the low-dimensional PoP space is not only feasible and extremely fast but also yields high-quality semantic representations, achieving a Spearman's ρ of ~0.75 on the MEN dataset.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides a valuable analysis of the interaction between vector distributions and similarity measures, motivating the switch from Kendall's τ for raw PoP vectors to Pearson's r for the "Gaussianized" PPMI-transformed vectors.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegance and Novelty of the Core Idea:** The central idea is both simple and powerful. Identifying the zero-centered nature of RI as the bottleneck for applying PPMI and proposing a non-negative projection as a direct solution is an elegant piece of problem-solving. It offers a new perspective on how to combine the benefits of two major families of distributional models (random projections and count-based weighting).

2.  **Significant Practical Implications for Efficiency:** The paper's strongest argument is its practical utility. The ability to generate high-quality embeddings in minutes rather than hours or days, without requiring GPUs, is a substantial contribution. This makes powerful distributional semantics accessible for rapid prototyping, resource-constrained environments, or applications requiring dynamic/incremental updates to the semantic space, a feature not easily supported by neural models or batch methods like SVD.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis of the Method's Properties:** The authors conduct a commendably detailed analysis of their proposed method. They systematically compare PoP to count-based and RI baselines (Figure 1), demonstrate the dramatic impact of PPMI (Figure 2), and investigate the sensitivity to key hyperparameters like dimensionality `m` and sparsity `s` (Figures 4 & 5). The visualization of vector component distributions (Figure 3) provides clear intuition for why PPMI is applicable to PoP but not RI, and why Pearson correlation becomes effective after the PPMI transformation.

4.  **Clarity and Sound Reasoning:** The paper is well-written and the arguments are easy to follow. The authors clearly delineate PoP from prior work and are intellectually honest about the current lack of a formal theoretical justification (e.g., a Johnson-Lindenstrauss-style lemma), framing the work appropriately as an empirical contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while thorough on its own terms, is confined to a single task (semantic relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). Relatedness tasks are known to correlate well with topical similarity, which count-based models excel at. It is unclear how PoP+PPMI would perform on tasks that require a more nuanced understanding of semantics, such as semantic similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) or analogy reasoning (e.g., Google Analogy dataset). Claims of being "competitive" with neural embeddings are strong and require validation across a wider and more diverse suite of standard benchmarks.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Key Baselines:** The paper compares its results to scores reported in other papers but lacks a direct, controlled comparison to canonical predictive models. The most critical missing baseline is a standard skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) model (word2vec) and/or GloVe trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus and preprocessing pipeline. While PoP+PPMI's score of 0.75-0.78 is strong, it's difficult to assess its competitiveness without knowing what a standard word2vec model would achieve under identical conditions. Furthermore, the PPMI+SVD baseline, arguably the strongest non-neural competitor, is dismissed as providing "negligible improvements," which downplays its role as a crucial benchmark. A more detailed comparison of performance, memory, and time against PPMI+SVD would be more convincing.

3.  **Lack of Motivation for the Specific Projection Distribution:** The choice of the projection distribution `t_ij = floor(1/U^0.5)` where `U` is uniform on (0, 1] feels arbitrary. This creates a power-law distribution of integer values. Why this specific distribution? Have the authors experimented with simpler alternatives, such as `t_ij` being uniformly chosen from `{0, 1}` or simply always being `1` for the non-zero elements? The performance of the method may hinge on this specific choice, but no justification is provided, making it seem like a "magic constant" and potentially hindering reproducibility or understanding of the underlying mechanism.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The evaluation is focused solely on the MEN relatedness task. How do you expect the PoP+PPMI model to perform on a semantic *similarity* benchmark like SimLex-999, where the goal is to distinguish genuine similarity from topical relatedness? This would be a crucial test of the quality of the learned representations.

2.  Could you provide benchmark scores for a standard word2vec (SGNS) model trained on your UKWaC corpus setup? This direct comparison is essential for substantiating the claim that PoP+PPMI is competitive with neural network approaches in terms of quality, not just speed.

3.  What is the theoretical or empirical motivation behind using the specific `floor(1/U^0.5)` distribution for the non-zero projection values? How does performance change if a simpler distribution is used, e.g., if all non-zero values are fixed to 1? This would clarify whether the specific heavy-tailed distribution of projection weights is a critical component of the method's success.
