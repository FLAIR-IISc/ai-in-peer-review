### Summary of the paper
This paper introduces a novel method for constructing word embeddings called Positive-Only Projections (PoP). The method aims to combine the computational efficiency of random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI) with the high accuracy of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The core problem addressed is that standard random projection methods produce vectors with an expected value of zero, making them incompatible with PPMI, while traditional count-based models that work with PPMI are sparse and high-dimensional, making computation expensive.

The proposed PoP method uses a sparse random projection matrix containing only positive integer values. This projects the high-dimensional count space into a low-dimensional space while preserving the non-negative, count-like nature of the vectors. The key advantage is that these low-dimensional PoP vectors can then be effectively transformed using PPMI, a step that is computationally infeasible on the original high-dimensional vectors and mathematically undefined for RI-style vectors. The authors evaluate their method on the MEN word relatedness task, showing that PoP vectors weighted with PPMI achieve results competitive with more computationally intensive methods, while being constructed in a fraction of the time.

### Main Contributions
1.  **The PoP method:** The primary contribution is the PoP algorithm itself, a novel random projection technique that creates low-dimensional, incremental word vectors that remain compatible with count-based weighting schemes. This elegantly bridges the gap between efficient but less flexible random indexing methods and accurate but computationally heavy PPMI-based models.

2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Efficiency and Accuracy:** The paper provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed method. It demonstrates that combining PoP with PPMI (PoP+PPMI) achieves a Spearman correlation on the MEN dataset (ρ ≈ 0.75) that is competitive with established SVD and neural network-based methods, but with drastically lower computational overhead (minutes vs. hours or days). This presents a highly practical and scalable approach for creating high-quality semantic spaces.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea is simple, elegant, and effectively addresses a clear limitation of existing methods. The motivation for why RI cannot be combined with PPMI (due to E(R)=0) is clearly explained, and the proposed solution (using E(R)>0) is a direct and clever fix. This makes the contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

2.  **Excellent Balance of Performance and Efficiency:** The paper’s strongest argument is the practicality of the PoP method. In an era where state-of-the-art models often require massive computational resources and training time, a method that achieves competitive performance on a standard benchmark with minimal resources is highly valuable. The claim of building models in "a few seconds or minutes" is a significant practical advantage over both neural embeddings and SVD on large matrices.

3.  **Thorough Internal Analysis:** The evaluation in Section 3 is well-structured and convincing. The authors not only compare PoP to baselines (count-based, RI) but also conduct a detailed analysis of its properties. The investigation of different similarity measures (Kendall's τb vs. Pearson's r) and their theoretical justification based on the data distribution (Figure 3) is insightful. Furthermore, the parameter sensitivity analysis in Figures 4 and 5 (varying dimensionality `m` and sparsity `s`) provides valuable practical guidance and adds depth to the empirical results.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The authors candidly acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for why PoP works, contrasting it with the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections. While the empirical results are strong, this absence of theoretical grounding leaves open questions about the method's properties and guarantees. The reliance on empirical evidence alone makes the work feel more like an engineering contribution than a fundamental scientific one, though it is a very effective piece of engineering.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on MEN), a single corpus (UKWaC), and a single language (English). While MEN is a standard benchmark, state-of-the-art embedding papers typically evaluate on a broader suite of tasks, including word similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) and analogy (e.g., Google analogy dataset), to demonstrate the different semantic properties captured. It is unclear if the PoP+PPMI embeddings would exhibit the same linear structure as models like word2vec, which is crucial for analogy tasks.

3.  **Indirect Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper claims competitiveness with neural embeddings but does not include a direct comparison. Instead, it refers to scores reported in other papers. This makes a direct assessment difficult, as performance is highly dependent on corpus, preprocessing, and hyperparameters. Including a baseline like word2vec (SGNS) trained on the same UKWaC corpus would have made the performance claims significantly more concrete and compelling. The reported score of 0.78 is strong, but its standing relative to a directly comparable neural model is unknown.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the lack of theoretical grounding: Can you provide more intuition on the choice of the projection distribution, specifically `tij = floor(1/U^0.5)`? Does this specific formulation have properties that, for example, a simple sparse binary projection (using only 0s and 1s) would not?

2.  Your evaluation focuses on word relatedness, where topical information is key. Have you performed any preliminary experiments on tasks that measure similarity more strictly, like SimLex-999, or on the word analogy task? The strong performance of PPMI-based models on similarity/relatedness is well-known, but their performance on analogy is often weaker than that of predictive models. It would be helpful to know where PoP+PPMI fits in this context.

3.  To strengthen the claim of competitiveness, would it be possible to provide results from a standard neural embedding model (e.g., word2vec's skip-gram with negative sampling) trained with your exact corpus and preprocessing pipeline? This would provide a direct and powerful point of comparison for both performance and training time.
