### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel incremental method for constructing low-dimensional semantic spaces. The core idea is to use a sparse random projection matrix where non-zero elements are positive integers, in contrast to traditional Random Indexing (RI) which uses a zero-centered distribution (+1, -1, 0). The authors argue this is a key advantage, as it allows for the subsequent application of powerful weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is not possible with RI-based methods whose vector components sum to zero. The method aims to combine the efficiency and incrementality of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-weighted count models, offering a fast and computationally inexpensive alternative to neural network-based embeddings.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **The PoP method itself:** This is a novel and well-motivated modification of random projection techniques. The insight that using a positive-only projection matrix makes the resulting low-dimensional vectors compatible with PPMI weighting is a clever way to bridge the gap between two major families of word embedding methods.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's effectiveness:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation showing that PoP combined with PPMI and Pearson correlation achieves results on the MEN word relatedness task that are competitive with dense PPMI models, while being significantly faster to compute.
3.  **An insightful analysis of similarity measures:** The authors make a principled choice of similarity metrics, using the non-parametric Kendall's tau for the raw, non-Gaussian PoP vectors, and switching to the more powerful Pearson correlation after the PPMI transformation induces a more Gaussian-like distribution. This is supported by clear distributional visualizations.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Value and Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is its practicality. It proposes a method that can generate high-quality embeddings at a fraction of the computational cost of popular alternatives like neural network training or SVD on a full co-occurrence matrix. The incremental nature of the algorithm is a significant advantage for working with massive or streaming datasets, where retraining models is infeasible. This work presents a compelling case for a "good enough, fast enough" approach that could be highly valuable for many practitioners.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Elegant Solution:** The paper clearly articulates a well-known limitation of RI-style methods: their incompatibility with effective weighting schemes like PPMI. The proposed PoP method is a simple and elegant solution to this specific problem. The explanation in Section 3.2, particularly the discussion around why the sums in the PPMI denominator are non-zero for PoP but zero for RI, is very clear and convincing.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors have conducted a comprehensive set of experiments. They not only show that their final model (PoP+PPMI+Pearson) works well, but they also systematically build up to it by comparing PoP against count-based and RI baselines (Fig. 1). Furthermore, the parameter sensitivity analysis in Section 3.3 (Figs. 4, 5, 6) regarding dimensionality (`m`) and sparsity (`s`) adds depth to the paper, providing useful guidance for future users of the method and revealing interesting properties of the model. The histograms in Figure 3 are an excellent visualization that supports the paper's core claims about data distributions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The authors candidly acknowledge this limitation in Section 2.3. While methods like RI are supported by principles like the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, which provides theoretical guarantees on distance preservation, PoP is presented without a similar formal foundation. The paper relies entirely on empirical results to validate the approach. While the results are strong, the absence of theory makes it difficult to understand the formal properties of the projection, the nature of the distortions it introduces, and how to formally bound the error.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The entire evaluation rests on a single intrinsic task (word relatedness on the MEN dataset) in English. While MEN is a standard benchmark, the performance of embedding models can be highly task-dependent. It remains unclear how PoP-generated vectors would perform on other intrinsic tasks like word analogy, or more importantly, on downstream extrinsic tasks such as text classification, named entity recognition, or parsing. The competitiveness of an embedding method is now typically judged across a suite of such tasks.
3.  **Comparison to Baselines Could Be Stronger:** The paper compares PoP+PPMI primarily against a dense PPMI model, demonstrating comparable performance at a much lower cost. However, the comparison to the main target of their criticism—neural embeddings—is indirect and relies on citing scores from other papers. A more compelling evaluation would include a direct comparison to a standard neural model (e.g., Skip-gram with negative sampling or GloVe) trained on the exact same UKWaC corpus. While they anecdotally mention achieving a high score of 0.78, this result should be integrated more formally into a comparative table with state-of-the-art baselines to solidify their claims of competitiveness.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the comparison to neural embeddings: Could you provide a direct performance comparison (e.g., in a table) of your best PoP+PPMI model against a standard word2vec SGNS or GloVe model trained on the same pre-processed UKWaC corpus? This would provide a much clearer picture of where your method stands relative to these widely-used techniques.
2.  The incremental nature of PoP is highlighted as a major benefit. Have you performed any experiments that specifically demonstrate this advantage? For example, by first building a model on a portion of the corpus and then updating it with the remainder, and showing how performance evolves and how much time the update takes compared to a full retrain of a batch model?
3.  In the definition of the projection matrix `T` (Equation 2), the non-zero values are `floor(1/U^alpha)` where `U ~ Uniform(0, 1]`. This can result in very large integer values when `U` is close to 0. Is there an implicit cap on this value in your implementation? How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of `alpha=0.5` and the resulting distribution of positive integer weights?
