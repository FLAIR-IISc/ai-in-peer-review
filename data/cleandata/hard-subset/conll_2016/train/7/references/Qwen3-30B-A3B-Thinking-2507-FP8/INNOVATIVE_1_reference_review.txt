

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces through random projections with positive integer values rather than the typical Gaussian/Cauchy distributions with mean 0. The core insight is that previous random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) cannot be PPMI-weighted due to their mean-0 vectors, while PoP's positive-only projections enable PPMI transformation, resulting in competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks with dramatically reduced computational cost.

## Main Contributions
1. The conceptual novelty of positive-only random projections enabling PPMI weighting - a fundamental solution to a previously unaddressed limitation of random projection-based semantic space construction.
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on MEN test (comparable to neural embeddings) with significantly lower computational requirements.
3. The efficiency claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" rather than "hours, days or months" required for neural embeddings.

## Strengths
1. **Genuine conceptual novelty**: The paper identifies a fundamental limitation of existing random projection methods (incompatibility with PPMI due to mean-0 vectors) and provides a novel solution (positive-only projections) that directly addresses this. This is not an incremental improvement but a new direction that enables previously impossible transformations. The insight is non-obvious and well-motivated by the paper's analysis of why previous methods cannot use PPMI.

2. **Strong performance claims with practical implications**: The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) without neural network training. This addresses a real pain point in the field: the computational cost of neural embeddings. The efficiency claim is particularly compelling as it could make high-quality semantic spaces accessible to researchers without significant computational resources.

3. **Clear empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation across multiple parameter settings (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and demonstrates that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models, with PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving the best results. The analysis of vector distributions (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into why the approach works.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of direct comparison with neural embeddings**: While the paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," it doesn't provide a direct comparison table with state-of-the-art neural methods on the MEN test. The reader must infer the performance comparison from vague references to other papers rather than seeing the actual numbers.

2. **Limited theoretical justification**: The authors acknowledge they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account" for the PoP method's behavior, relying solely on empirical validation. While this is common in applied ML, it's a notable gap for a paper making strong claims about a new mathematical approach. The paper cites previous random projection work that also lacked theoretical justification, but this remains a weakness.

3. **Insufficient exploration of limitations**: The paper doesn't address whether PoP works well for tasks beyond semantic similarity (e.g., analogy solving, named entity recognition). It also doesn't discuss potential limitations for very large-scale applications or how the method might interact with other NLP pipelines.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI+Pearson with state-of-the-art neural embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, fastText) on the MEN test, including exact performance numbers?

2. How does the computational efficiency of PoP scale when processing very large corpora (e.g., Wikipedia-sized datasets), and how does it compare to the full pipeline of neural embeddings (including training)?

3. What are the specific limitations of the PoP approach for tasks beyond semantic similarity assessment?

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a genuinely novel concept that addresses a fundamental limitation in the field of random projection-based semantic space construction. The core idea of positive-only random projections enabling PPMI weighting is bold, non-obvious, and potentially impactful for making high-quality semantic spaces more accessible. While the paper has some weaknesses in empirical comparison and theoretical justification, these are secondary to the significant conceptual novelty, which aligns perfectly with CONLL's preference for bold, novel approaches over incremental improvements. The paper's claims about computational efficiency are compelling and address a real need in the field.
