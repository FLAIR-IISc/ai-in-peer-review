

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper introduces "Positive-only Projection" (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive integer values. The primary claimed contributions are: (1) PoP enables PPMI weighting on projected spaces, unlike traditional random projection methods; (2) PoP is computationally efficient (building models in seconds/minutes); and (3) PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine limitation in current random projection methods (incompatibility with PPMI weighting) and proposes a potential solution. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models on the MEN dataset, and the computational efficiency claim is plausible given the low dimensionality of PoP vectors.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental misunderstanding of Random Indexing (RI)**: The paper repeatedly states that "RI's transformation matrix R has E(R) = 0" and that "sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0." This is completely incorrect. RI uses binary vectors (0 or 1), so the expectation would be 0.5, not 0. The sum of vector elements would be positive (not zero), making PPMI calculations possible for RI. This fundamental error undermines the paper's core comparison.

2. **Inconsistent and unclear mathematical formulation**: Equation 2 contains a typo: "tij = { 0 with probability 1− s b 1Uα c with probability s". The "b 1Uα c" portion is nonsensical. The paper also claims PoP uses "random vectors that contain only positive integer values," but the distribution described would produce mostly zeros with a small probability of 1s (not positive integers).

3. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper alternates between "PoP" (the method) and "POP" (e.g., "POP PMI Weights" in Figure 3e, "POP-constructed models" in Figure 4). This inconsistency appears multiple times throughout the paper and suggests poor attention to detail.

4. **Lack of proper comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "an average of 0.78 with the max of 0.787" on MEN, but fails to compare this to current state-of-the-art results (e.g., the paper cites Chen and de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015) without providing actual scores for comparison).

5. **Theoretical shortcomings**: The paper acknowledges it cannot provide "a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" (the error bound), which is a significant theoretical weakness for a method based on random projections. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma is mentioned but not properly applied to PoP.

## Additional Minor Issues

- Formatting errors: "Sp ea rm an's C or re la tio n ρ" in Figures 1, 2, and 4 should be "Spearman's Correlation ρ".
- The paper claims PoP vectors "have a non-Gaussian distribution" but later states that PPMI transformation "converts long-tailed distributions in count-based models to Gaussian-like distributions," which is confusing given the PoP vectors themselves are non-Gaussian.
- The paper states "the PoP method makes it possible to benefit both from the high efficiency of randomized techniques as well as from the high accuracy of PPMI transformation," but fails to provide concrete computational efficiency measurements to support this claim.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods, the fundamental misunderstanding of RI (a core comparison point) and the technical inconsistencies in the mathematical formulation are serious flaws that undermine the paper's credibility. The inconsistent terminology and formatting issues further indicate a lack of careful review. The paper's claims about PoP's advantages cannot be properly evaluated due to these foundational errors.

The paper's core contribution (enabling PPMI on random projections) might be valuable if properly executed, but the current presentation contains too many technical errors and inconsistencies to be considered for publication. The authors would need to completely revise their understanding of RI and clarify their mathematical formulation before the work could be taken seriously.
