

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

**Summary and Main Contributions:**
The paper proposes a novel method called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for incremental semantic space construction. PoP uses random projections with positive integer values instead of traditional Gaussian or Cauchy random projections. The primary contribution is a method that enables subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the projected space, which is not possible with existing random projection methods (e.g., RI) due to their zero-centered distributions. The second contribution is demonstrating that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive semantic similarity performance (ρ≈0.75 on MEN test) with significantly reduced computational requirements compared to neural embeddings and count-based models with SVD. The third contribution is providing empirical evidence that PoP's performance improves with higher dimensionality and can be fine-tuned through parameter selection.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a clear technical limitation in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) that prevents their use with weighting techniques like PPMI. The authors correctly note that these methods use random projection matrices with E(R)=0, making PPMI undefined, while PoP's positive-only projections with E(R)>0 enable PPMI application. This is a technically sound and important observation that addresses a genuine gap in the literature.

2. The empirical evaluation is well-executed and clearly demonstrates PoP's advantages. The paper shows that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ρ≈0.75 on MEN, comparable to neural embeddings, but with dramatically reduced computational cost (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days). The comparison to RI and count-based models is particularly convincing, showing PoP converges faster and outperforms both for smaller context windows.

3. The paper provides a thorough analysis of PoP's parameter sensitivity (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and demonstrates that performance improves with higher dimensionality for PPMI-transformed models. The figures showing how performance varies with different parameters (Figures 4-6) provide valuable practical guidance for implementing the method.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks theoretical justification for PoP's performance. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they rely solely on empirical results without providing any mathematical bounds for the error δ (difference between optimum and PoP results). The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and similar theoretical frameworks are mentioned but not applied to PoP, making the method's theoretical foundation weak.

2. The paper does not sufficiently compare PoP to other methods that might achieve similar benefits. For instance, the paper doesn't discuss whether other dimensionality reduction techniques (beyond random projections) that maintain positive values could achieve similar results. A more thorough literature review would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. The evaluation is limited to a single benchmark (MEN). While MEN is a standard benchmark, the paper doesn't report results on other commonly used semantic similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999), which would better establish the method's generalizability and robustness across different semantic tasks.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. The paper claims PoP is novel, but it's not clear how it differs from existing methods that use positive weights in random projections. Could the authors provide a more thorough comparison with the literature to establish PoP's novelty?

2. The paper states that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance compared to neural embeddings, but the evaluation is limited to MEN. Could the authors provide results on at least one additional standard benchmark to better establish the method's generalizability?

3. The paper mentions "derandomization techniques" as a future direction. Could the authors elaborate on what specific derandomization techniques they have in mind and how they might be applied to improve PoP's linguistic interpretability?
