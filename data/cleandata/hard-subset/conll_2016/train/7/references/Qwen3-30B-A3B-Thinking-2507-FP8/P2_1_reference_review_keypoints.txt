- Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a random projection method that enables PPMI weighting and claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ~0.75 on MEN while being much faster than neural embeddings.

- Key contribution: PoP enables PPMI on projected spaces, addressing a limitation of zero-mean random projections.

- Claims significant computational efficiency: models built in seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural embeddings.

- Empirical validation shows PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms RI and count-based baselines, especially with smaller context windows, and includes parameter sensitivity analyses.

- Clear explanation and visual evidence (histograms) for why PoP permits PPMI (positive sums) whereas RI vectors have zero sums.

- Lacks theoretical justification and formal error bounds (δ) expected for a randomized algorithm; JL-style guarantees are not provided.

- Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art neural embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText, BERT); claims of competitiveness are not directly supported.

- Limited evaluation scope: results reported only on MEN; additional benchmarks (WordSim353, SimLex-999, etc.) are needed for robustness.

- Insufficient ablation: unclear how much gains come from PoP vs. PPMI vs. other components.

- Question: How does the reported 0.75 MEN score compare numerically to SOTA results from Chen & de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015)?

- Question: Please provide a mathematical derivation showing why RI-based models cannot use PPMI (zero sums) and why PoP can (positive sums).

- Question: Provide details of fine-tuning that yielded 0.78/0.787 (context selection, frequency cut-offs, window size).

- Question: How does the capacity/dimensionality tradeoff of PoP compare to neural embeddings (e.g., GloVe 300d) and how does it affect performance?

- Overall: Novel and promising method with strong efficiency and empirical signs of effectiveness, but weakened by missing theory, limited datasets, and lack of SOTA comparisons.

- Recommendation: Accept with major revisions to add theoretical bounds, broader evaluation, ablations, and direct SOTA comparisons.
