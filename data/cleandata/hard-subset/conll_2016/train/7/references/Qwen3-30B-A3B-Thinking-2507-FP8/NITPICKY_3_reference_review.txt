

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper proposes "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), an incremental semantic space construction method that allows for efficient application of PPMI weighting. The authors claim PoP achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks while being computationally more efficient than neural embeddings and count-based models with PPMI weighting. The paper presents empirical results on the MEN relatedness test showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves reasonable performance.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The PoP method for incremental semantic space construction that allows for PPMI weighting
2. Empirical evidence showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance on MEN test
3. The observation that PoP can be used with low-dimensional vectors, making PPMI application faster

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a valid computational bottleneck: applying PPMI to high-dimensional count-based models is computationally expensive.
2. The empirical results do demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves reasonable performance on the MEN relatedness test (0.75-0.78).
3. The paper provides interesting insights about the distribution of weights in different models, particularly regarding how PPMI transforms the distributions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Mathematical notation errors and inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple critical mathematical notation errors that undermine its technical credibility. Equation (2) contains the unclear expression "b 1Uα c" which appears to be a formatting error. Equation (3) is poorly formatted and hard to parse: "ppmi(vxy) = max(0, log vxy× ∑p i=1 ∑n j=1 vij∑p i=1 viy× ∑n j=1 vxj )". These errors make it difficult to understand and reproduce the method. The paper also incorrectly writes "Pp×x" in Equation (1) when it should be "Pp×m" or "Pp×x" to be consistent.

2. **Inconsistent terminology and notation**: The paper inconsistently uses "PoP" and "POP" (uppercase vs. lowercase). For example, "PoP-constructed models" appears in some places while "POP-constructed models" appears elsewhere. This inconsistency is distracting and unprofessional.

3. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims PoP outperforms RI and count-based models but provides no statistical significance tests for these comparisons. Without these tests, it's impossible to determine if the observed performance differences are meaningful or just due to random variation. The paper reports performance differences but doesn't quantify the uncertainty.

4. **Inconsistent results reporting**: The paper reports conflicting performance scores for the same method. It states PoP+PPMI+Pearson "achieves an average score of 0.75" but later claims "an average of 0.78 with the max of 0.787". This inconsistency raises serious questions about the reliability of the reported results.

5. **Lack of reproducibility details**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about hyperparameters to reproduce the experiments. It mentions "s = 0.01" in one place but later suggests "0.002 < s" for direct use with τb. It also states "index vectors of dimensionality 1000 containing 4 nonzero elements" for RI experiments but doesn't provide the exact hyperparameters used for PoP experiments.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify the mathematical expression in Equation (2)? What is the intended meaning of "b 1Uα c"?
2. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences between PoP, RI, and count-based models?
3. Could you resolve the inconsistency in the reported MEN test scores (0.75 vs. 0.78)?
4. Could you provide the exact hyperparameters used for all experiments, including specific values of s, m, and number of non-zero elements?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies a valid computational bottleneck and presents some interesting empirical results, the numerous minor but critical flaws in mathematical notation, inconsistent terminology, lack of statistical significance testing, inconsistent results reporting, and insufficient reproducibility details undermine the paper's credibility and technical quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too significant to overlook, even if the core idea has potential. The paper requires substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
