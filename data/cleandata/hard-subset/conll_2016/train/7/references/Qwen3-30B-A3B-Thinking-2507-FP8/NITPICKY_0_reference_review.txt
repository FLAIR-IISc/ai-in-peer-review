

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper proposes "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive integer values. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting (unlike previous random projection methods like RI), resulting in computationally efficient models that achieve competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks. The paper describes PoP's method, evaluates it on the MEN dataset, and claims it builds models in seconds/minutes instead of hours/days.

## Main Contributions
1. PoP method for incremental semantic space construction that supposedly allows PPMI weighting (contrasting with RI)
2. Empirical demonstration of PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving ~0.75 on MEN test
3. Claimed computational efficiency (seconds/minutes vs hours/days for neural methods)

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear description of the PoP algorithm and its implementation details.
2. The authors present empirical results on a standard benchmark (MEN), which is valuable for evaluation.
3. The paper acknowledges the lack of theoretical guarantees for PoP, which is a responsible approach.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Methodological Errors
The paper contains a fundamental error in its core claim: "In an RI-constructed model, the sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0." This is incorrect. RI uses random projection matrices with E(R)=0, but the resulting vectors do not necessarily have sum 0. The sum of row/column vectors depends on the input data, not the projection matrix. This error undermines the paper's central claim about why RI cannot use PPMI.

### 2. Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting
- The paper inconsistently uses "PoP" vs "POP" (e.g., "POP-constructed" in section 4).
- In Figure 2 caption, "Sp ea rm an's C or re la tio n ρ" appears to be a formatting error.
- The paper claims vectors have "dimensionality of a few hundred" but uses dimensions of 1000-5000 in experiments (e.g., "index vectors of dimensionality 1000").
- Mathematical notation is inconsistent: "x 6= n" should be "x ≠ n" throughout.

### 3. Overstated Performance Claims
- The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "competitive results" with "neural net-based approaches," but 0.75 on MEN is significantly below state-of-the-art (0.78-0.80 as cited in the paper). The paper doesn't provide comparison to recent neural methods (e.g., BERT).
- The paper states "models can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" but provides no timing data to support this claim.

### 4. Inconsistent Evaluation Methodology
The paper uses different similarity measures for different methods: Kendall's τb for PoP and cosine for RI. This is methodologically unsound because it's not a fair comparison. The paper should have used the same similarity measure across all methods to properly evaluate performance differences.

### 5. Unsubstantiated Computational Efficiency Claim
The paper claims "applying weighting methods such as PPMI to these models is incredibly faster than applying them to classical count-based models" but provides no timing benchmarks to support this. Without empirical evidence of speed improvements, this claim remains unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify why RI cannot use PPMI weighting? The paper's explanation about row/column sums being zero is incorrect as RI's row/column sums depend on the input data, not the projection matrix.
2. Why did you use different similarity measures (τb for PoP, cosine for RI) in your evaluation, and how does this affect the interpretation of your results?
3. Can you provide timing benchmarks showing the computational efficiency claim (e.g., time to build models with PoP vs count-based models)?
4. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson's performance (0.75) compare to standard count-based models with PPMI+SVD, which are mentioned as a baseline in the paper?

## Conclusion
While the paper presents a novel approach to semantic space construction, it contains critical methodological errors, inconsistent terminology, and overstated claims that undermine its contribution. The central claim about PoP enabling PPMI weighting is based on an incorrect understanding of RI's properties. The paper's performance claims are not supported by sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and computational efficiency claims lack empirical evidence. These substantial flaws outweigh the paper's potential contribution to the field. I recommend rejection due to these fundamental issues.
