

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Random Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a method for incremental construction of semantic spaces. The authors claim that PoP overcomes a key limitation of existing random projection techniques (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) by using random projection matrices with only positive integer values, thereby enabling subsequent PPMI weighting that was previously impossible with zero-mean random matrices. The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive results on semantic relatedness tasks (MEN test) with dramatically lower computational requirements than neural embeddings.

Main contributions as I see them:
1. The PoP method itself, which enables PPMI weighting of random projection-based models (addressing a genuine limitation in prior work)
2. The empirical demonstration of PoP's computational efficiency and competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods
3. The thorough analysis of PoP's parameter sensitivity and behavior

## Strengths

1. **Novel solution to a genuine problem**: The paper identifies a real limitation in existing random projection methods (the inability to apply PPMI due to zero-mean random matrices) and proposes a simple but effective solution. This is a valuable contribution to the field, as PPMI weighting is known to significantly improve semantic space quality. The authors clearly demonstrate that PoP-constructed spaces can undergo PPMI transformation while other random projection methods cannot.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evidence supporting their claims. The comparison between PoP, RI, and count-based models across various context window sizes demonstrates clear advantages of PoP. The results showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving ~0.75 on MEN (comparable to neural methods) while requiring only seconds/minutes to build is compelling. The analysis of parameter sensitivity (Figure 4-6) is particularly strong.

3. **Clear computational efficiency claim**: The paper makes a convincing case for PoP's computational advantages over neural embeddings. The authors correctly note that training neural embeddings can take "hours, days or months" (citing Baroni et al.), while PoP models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes." This efficiency advantage is significant for practical NLP applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper admits it lacks a "detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" (error bounds) for PoP, unlike some prior work (e.g., Li et al. for RI). For a paper proposing a new random projection technique, this is a significant weakness. The authors acknowledge this, but the absence of any theoretical analysis or bounds on performance makes it difficult to assess the method's fundamental properties.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to the MEN relatedness test, which is a standard benchmark but not comprehensive. The paper doesn't demonstrate PoP's performance on other semantic tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy tasks, or downstream NLP applications). A broader evaluation would strengthen the claim that PoP is generally useful.

3. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: While the paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson "achieves competitive results" comparable to neural embeddings, it doesn't provide specific comparisons to recent neural methods (e.g., BERT, ELMo, or more recent word embeddings). The reference to Chen and de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015) is insufficient for a claim about current state-of-the-art.

4. **Unclear novelty relative to existing work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate PoP from other positive-weight random projection methods. The authors cite RI as a similar method but don't clarify how PoP differs in meaningful ways. Without a clear novelty argument, it's difficult to assess whether this is a truly new contribution or a minor variation of existing ideas.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify how PoP differs from the "positive random indexing" approach mentioned in the introduction (e.g., in the context of "positive-only projection" vs. other positive methods)? The distinction appears important but isn't clearly articulated.

2. The paper states "PoP-constructed models can undergo weighting transformations such as PPMI" but doesn't explain why this is impossible with other random projection methods. Could you provide a more detailed mathematical explanation of why zero-mean random matrices prevent PPMI application?

3. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "comparable results" to neural embeddings but doesn't provide specific performance numbers for recent neural methods on the MEN test. Could you add a comparison table with state-of-the-art results for context?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods and demonstrates a practical solution with clear computational advantages. The empirical results are strong and well-supported. However, the lack of theoretical justification and limited evaluation scope prevent me from fully endorsing the work. With stronger theoretical analysis, broader evaluation, and more detailed comparisons to state-of-the-art neural methods, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the expectation that the authors address the theoretical and evaluation limitations in the revision. The core idea of enabling PPMI weighting through positive random projections is interesting and potentially useful, but requires stronger justification to be considered a significant contribution.
