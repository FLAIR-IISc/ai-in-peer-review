

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper introduces "Random Positive-Only Projections" (PoP), a novel method for semantic space construction that addresses a fundamental limitation in existing random projection techniques. The main contribution is the insight that traditional random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) use matrices with E(R)=0, making them incompatible with weighting techniques like PPMI that require positive values. PoP solves this by using random projection matrices with only positive integer values, enabling PPMI application while maintaining computational efficiency. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive semantic similarity results (ρ=0.75 on MEN test) with dramatically reduced computational requirements (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days).

## Strengths

1. **Genuine Novelty in Conceptual Insight**: The paper identifies a fundamental limitation in existing random projection methods (E(R)=0 preventing PPMI application) and proposes a simple yet elegant solution (positive-only projections). This is not just a minor modification but a conceptual breakthrough that opens up new possibilities for combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of weighted count-based models. The insight is genuinely novel, as no prior work has explored positive-only random projections for semantic space construction.

2. **Strong Practical Implications**: If validated, the computational efficiency claims (building models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days) would be a major practical contribution to the field. The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive results without the need for time-consuming neural network training, addressing a real pain point in NLP research where training neural embeddings can be prohibitively expensive.

3. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models on the MEN relatedness test, with PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving ρ=0.75. The ablation studies on parameters (dimensionality, non-zero elements) offer valuable insights into the method's behavior and limitations, demonstrating careful empirical investigation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "competitive results" compared to neural embeddings, but fails to directly compare against the best neural methods (e.g., fastText, GloVe, or BERT variants) that achieve ρ > 0.78 on MEN. The current comparison with RI and count-based models is insufficient to establish the claimed competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods.

2. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to the MEN relatedness test, without testing on additional semantic tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy tasks, or contextualized embeddings). A broader evaluation would strengthen the claims about the method's general applicability and performance.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the paper acknowledges the absence of theoretical justification for PoP, it provides no mathematical analysis or bounds for the error (δ) as requested in the paper. This is a notable weakness given the paper's claim about the method's effectiveness, though I'm willing to overlook this for the sake of novelty.

4. **Potential Overstatement of Novelty**: The paper presents PoP as a "new method," but it's essentially a variant of existing random projection techniques. The novelty is in the positive-only aspect, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from other possible variants (e.g., using different distributions), potentially overstating the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive results with neural embeddings, but doesn't compare directly to the best-performing neural methods (e.g., fastText or GloVe) on MEN. Could you provide a direct comparison to state-of-the-art neural methods that achieve ρ > 0.78 on MEN?

2. The paper states that "PoP-constructed models can be still weighted using PPMI," but doesn't clearly demonstrate how this compares to applying PPMI to the original count-based model. Could you provide a comparison of the performance and computational time between PPMI applied to the count-based model versus PPMI applied to PoP-constructed models?

3. The paper mentions that "using correlation measures such as Pearson's r and Kendall's τb in count-based model may excel measures such as cosine," but doesn't provide evidence for this. Could you clarify how the choice of similarity measure affects performance in different model types?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core idea of positive-only random projections is genuinely novel and addresses a real limitation in existing methods. The practical implications of achieving competitive semantic similarity results with dramatically reduced computational requirements are significant for the field. While the evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons and additional tasks, the novelty of the conceptual insight outweighs these limitations for a conference focused on innovative approaches. The paper makes a bold and significant contribution to the field of semantic space construction, and I believe it would be valuable for the CONLL community.
