

# Review

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces that addresses a key limitation in current random projection techniques. The authors identify that existing methods like RI (Random Indexing) use random matrices with E(R)=0, preventing subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI (Positive Pointwise Mutual Information) due to undefined weights. PoP solves this by using positive-only random projections (E(R)>0), enabling PPMI weighting while maintaining the efficiency benefits of random projections.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1. A novel technical solution (PoP) that enables PPMI weighting of random projection-based semantic spaces, addressing a clear limitation in current methods.
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN test) with significantly reduced computational requirements compared to neural network approaches.
3. Comprehensive analysis of PoP's behavior under different parameter settings (dimensionality, sparsity), providing practical guidance for implementation.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies and addresses a genuine technical limitation in current semantic space construction methods. The key insight that random projection methods with E(R)=0 cannot be weighted with PPMI is well-justified and represents a meaningful gap in the literature. PoP's solution—using positive-only random projections—provides a clear, technically sound approach to this problem with practical benefits.

2. The empirical results demonstrate a compelling efficiency-performance tradeoff. The authors show that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN test) while requiring orders of magnitude less computational time (seconds/minutes vs hours/days for neural approaches). This is a significant practical contribution, especially for resource-constrained applications.

3. The paper provides thorough analysis of PoP's behavior under different parameter settings (dimensionality, sparsity), which is valuable for understanding the method's behavior and optimizing it for specific applications. The authors demonstrate how performance varies with these parameters and provide concrete guidelines for their selection (e.g., 0.002 < s for direct use, 1-2 non-zero elements for PPMI transformation).

## Weaknesses
1. The paper lacks theoretical justification for the PoP method. The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account of the error bound δ, which is a significant gap for a randomized algorithm. Randomized algorithms typically have theoretical bounds on performance, and the absence of this makes it difficult to understand the method's limitations and guarantees.

2. The novelty claim is not sufficiently established. While the authors state PoP is novel, they don't clearly distinguish it from existing random projection methods. The key difference (positive-only projections) is mentioned, but the paper doesn't explain why this specific approach is novel compared to other methods that might use positive projections. The authors also don't address whether other random projection methods with E(R)>0 have been tried and why they would fail.

3. The evaluation is limited to the MEN relatedness test. While MEN is a standard benchmark, the paper should have evaluated on multiple standard semantic similarity benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) to better establish the method's generality and robustness across different tasks.

4. The justification for using Kendall's τb before PPMI transformation and Pearson's correlation after is not fully convincing. The paper states that PoP vectors have non-Gaussian distributions, making τb more appropriate, while PPMI-transformed vectors have Gaussian-like distributions, making Pearson more appropriate. However, the paper doesn't provide strong evidence for these distribution claims, and the transition between the two correlation measures is not well-justified.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims PoP is novel, but doesn't clearly distinguish it from other random projection methods with E(R)>0. Can the authors provide evidence that other methods with positive projections have been tried and failed to work with PPMI?

2. The paper lacks theoretical justification for PoP's error bound δ. Are there any plans to provide this in future work, or is this considered a limitation of the method?

3. The evaluation is limited to the MEN test. Can the authors provide results on additional standard semantic similarity benchmarks to demonstrate the method's generality?
