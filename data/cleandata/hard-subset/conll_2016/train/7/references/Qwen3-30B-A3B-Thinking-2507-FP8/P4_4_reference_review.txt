

# Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive integer values. The primary contribution is enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based models, which existing methods (like RI) cannot support due to their use of random matrices with E(R)=0. The authors demonstrate that PoP-constructed models can achieve competitive semantic similarity results (0.75 on MEN test) with dramatically reduced computational costs compared to neural embeddings. A secondary contribution is showing that PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms count-based models and RI while maintaining efficiency. A tertiary contribution is the empirical analysis of PoP's parameter sensitivity.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing random projection methods (incompatibility with PPMI weighting) and provides a clean solution by using positive-only random projections. The authors clearly articulate why existing methods fail (RI's E(R)=0 makes PPMI undefined) and how PoP resolves this (E(R)>0). This is a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of semantic space construction.

2. **Demonstrated Efficiency Gains**: The paper provides compelling evidence of computational efficiency - building models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days required for neural embeddings. The authors show this through both theoretical discussion (low dimensionality enabling faster PPMI application) and empirical results (Figure 4 showing performance as dimensionality increases).

3. **Competitive Performance**: PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves MEN test scores (0.75 average, 0.787 max) comparable to neural embedding methods while avoiding training time. This demonstrates that the method is not merely efficient but also effective for semantic similarity tasks, addressing the main motivation for the work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The authors acknowledge they cannot provide detailed mathematical bounds for the error (δ) of PoP, unlike some other methods where theoretical justification was developed later. While they cite precedents (RI), this is a significant limitation for a theoretical contribution. The connection between the specific random distribution (Equation 2) and performance gains is not rigorously explained.

2. **Insufficient Comparison to Modern Baselines**: The paper primarily compares to RI and count-based models but lacks comparison to contemporary state-of-the-art methods (e.g., fastText, GloVe, or more recent neural approaches). The MEN test scores presented (0.75) appear comparable to older neural methods, but without comparison to current SOTA, the significance of the performance gain is unclear.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity Without Clear Guidance**: The paper demonstrates PoP's sensitivity to parameters (m, s, number of non-zero elements) but provides only limited practical guidance for parameter selection. Figure 5 and Figure 6 show performance varies significantly with these parameters, yet the paper doesn't offer a clear, principled method for choosing optimal values in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson compare to modern state-of-the-art methods like fastText or GloVe on standard benchmarks (e.g., word similarity, analogy tasks)?

2. The paper states that "the dimension m of random index vectors can be seen as the capacity of the method to memorize and distinguish entities." Can the authors provide a more rigorous theoretical basis for this claim, or explain how m relates to model capacity?

3. The paper mentions that "PPMI transformation is much faster, compared to the one performed on count-based models, due to the low dimensionality of vectors in the PoP-constructed model." Can the authors provide specific computational complexity comparisons (e.g., time to compute PPMI on count-based vs. PoP models)?

4. The authors state that "for the count-based PoP method without PPMI transformation... increasing m over 2000 decreases the performance." This contradicts standard intuition about dimensionality. Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation for this counterintuitive behavior?
