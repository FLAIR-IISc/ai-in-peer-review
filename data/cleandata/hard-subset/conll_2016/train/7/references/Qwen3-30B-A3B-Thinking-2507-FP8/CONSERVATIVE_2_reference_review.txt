

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a new method for constructing semantic spaces that uses random projections with positive integer values. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting (which previous random projection methods cannot support due to zero-mean distributions) and achieves competitive performance with much lower computational cost than neural embeddings.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new random projection technique using positive integer values (PoP)
2. The claim that PoP can be combined with PPMI weighting (unlike existing random projection methods)
3. Empirical results showing PoP+PPMI achieves ρ=0.75 on MEN relatedness test

## Strengths
1. The paper clearly articulates the methodological distinction between PoP and existing random projection techniques (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI), particularly regarding the inability of the latter to support PPMI weighting due to zero-mean projections.
2. The empirical evaluation is well-structured with proper comparisons to RI and count-based models, and the authors provide detailed parameter analysis showing how different settings affect performance.
3. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach and provide thoughtful discussion about the trade-offs between different parameter choices.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The authors explicitly state they cannot provide a mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of PoP, which is a fundamental requirement for random projection methods. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and related theoretical foundations are the bedrock of random projection techniques, and omitting this severely undermines the paper's credibility as a methodological contribution. The authors cite Kanerva et al. (2000) as having proposed RI without theoretical justification, but the field has since developed rigorous theoretical frameworks for these methods.

2. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims "competitive results" but only compares to RI and count-based models, not to established state-of-the-art methods. The MEN benchmark score of ρ=0.75 is significantly below current state-of-the-art (typically 0.80-0.85), and the paper fails to compare against the most relevant baselines like GloVe, FastText, or even count-based models with SVD and PPMI weighting, which are the standard against which such claims should be measured.

3. **Questionable novelty**: The method appears to be a minor variation of existing random projection techniques rather than a substantial innovation. The paper doesn't demonstrate why positive values are fundamentally better than zero-mean distributions for semantic modeling, and the claimed benefits (PPMI compatibility) could be achieved with simpler modifications to existing methods (e.g., adding a constant to RI vectors).

4. **Misleading performance claims**: The paper states "PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to many neural embedding techniques" - but this is factually incorrect. The authors cite Chen and de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015), but those papers report scores of approximately 0.80-0.85 for state-of-the-art methods, not 0.75. This misrepresentation of the current state-of-the-art undermines the paper's credibility.

5. **Inadequate validation**: The paper only evaluates on the MEN dataset without cross-validation on other standard semantic similarity benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). The lack of multi-dataset evaluation significantly weakens the generalizability claims.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why do you claim the performance is "comparable to neural embeddings" when your reported score (0.75) is substantially below the state-of-the-art (typically 0.80+)? Could you provide specific references to papers where neural embeddings achieve scores around 0.75 on MEN?

2. Could you provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for why PoP should outperform existing random projection methods, beyond the empirical results presented? Specifically, how does the positive-value constraint affect the error bounds (δ) compared to zero-mean random projections?

3. Why not compare against the standard count-based model with PPMI+truncated SVD, which is the established baseline for semantic similarity tasks? Your results seem to suggest PoP+PPMI achieves similar performance to this established approach, but you don't make this comparison explicit.

This paper presents an interesting incremental improvement to existing random projection techniques, but the claims of novelty and performance are overstated and not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The lack of theoretical grounding and comparison to relevant baselines makes it difficult to assess the method's true contribution to the field. For a conference like CoNLL, which emphasizes methodological rigor and sound empirical evaluation, the paper falls short of the expected standards.
