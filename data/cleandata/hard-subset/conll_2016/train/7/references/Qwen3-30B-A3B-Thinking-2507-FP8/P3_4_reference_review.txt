

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with strictly positive elements. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting (which is incompatible with standard random projections like RI), achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks, and requires dramatically less computational resources than neural embeddings.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. The conceptual innovation of positive-only random projections that enable PPMI weighting
2. Empirical demonstration of PoP's efficiency and competitive performance on MEN test
3. Theoretical justification for why PoP works (though this is incomplete)

## Strengths

**1. Novel technical insight:** The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) - their use of mean-zero random matrices makes PPMI weighting impossible. The PoP approach of using strictly positive random projections to preserve non-zero sums for PPMI calculation is a genuinely novel technical contribution. This addresses a real gap in the literature.

**2. Strong empirical results:** The authors demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 average correlation on MEN, which is competitive with neural embedding approaches. The efficiency gains are well-documented - building models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days - which is a significant practical advantage.

**3. Clear explanation of the "why":** The paper provides a well-reasoned explanation for why standard random projections fail with PPMI (due to zero-mean distributions) and how PoP overcomes this limitation. The distributional analysis in Figure 3 is particularly compelling.

## Weaknesses

**1. Insufficient theoretical foundation:** This is the paper's most significant weakness. The authors acknowledge they cannot provide error bounds (δ) for their method, unlike Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections. While they cite Kanerva et al. (2000) as having proposed RI without justification, this is not an acceptable excuse for modern NLP research. Without theoretical grounding, it's unclear why PoP works as well as it does, and how to optimally set parameters like s and m.

**2. Limited comparison to state-of-the-art:** The paper compares PoP only to RI and count-based models, not to contemporary methods. For instance, it doesn't compare to fastText, which achieves similar efficiency with competitive performance. The paper claims PoP's performance is "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," but doesn't provide direct comparisons to recent neural methods that achieve 0.80+ on MEN (e.g., fastText, GloVe). This weakens the performance claims.

**3. Inconsistent methodology description:** The paper states PoP vectors have "dimensionality of a few hundred" but uses m=1000-5000 in experiments. This inconsistency undermines the efficiency claims. Additionally, the paper claims PoP outperforms RI without weighting, but Figure 1 shows PoP+Kendall performs similarly to RI (both around 0.45-0.50), not better.

**4. Limited evaluation scope:** The evaluation is restricted to MEN test. The paper should demonstrate generalizability across multiple standard datasets (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353) to establish broader applicability. The absence of these evaluations limits the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you theoretically justify the error bounds (δ) of PoP, and how does this compare to Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections?

2. Can you provide direct comparisons to contemporary methods like fastText and GloVe on the MEN test, as well as other standard semantic similarity datasets?

3. Why does the paper claim PoP vectors have "dimensionality of a few hundred" when experiments use m=1000-5000, and how does this affect the claimed efficiency gains?

4. The paper states PoP "outperforms RI on tasks of semantic similarity assessments" without weighting, but Figure 1 shows similar performance. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a genuinely novel technical approach to a real problem in distributional semantics (enabling PPMI weighting on random projections). The efficiency claims are compelling, and the empirical results are promising. However, the lack of theoretical justification, limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and inconsistent methodology description significantly weaken the paper's contribution. With stronger theoretical grounding, broader evaluation, and more comprehensive comparisons, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes an interesting technical point but doesn't fully demonstrate its significance or novelty in the current landscape of semantic space construction.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject** (with strong encouragement for revision addressing the theoretical and comparative weaknesses)
