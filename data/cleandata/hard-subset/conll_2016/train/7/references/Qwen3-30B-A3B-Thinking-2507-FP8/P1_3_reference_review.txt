

# Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel method called "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces. PoP uses random projection matrices with only positive integer values (instead of zero-mean distributions like Gaussian or Cauchy), enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI to random projection-based models. The authors claim that PoP-constructed spaces can undergo PPMI weighting (unlike standard random projection methods), achieve competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks (0.75 on MEN test), and require dramatically less computational resources than neural embeddings (building models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days).

## Main Contributions
1. The PoP method itself - a novel random projection technique using positive values that enables PPMI weighting
2. The empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) with significantly lower computational cost than neural embeddings
3. The detailed analysis of PoP's behavior with different parameters (dimensionality, non-zero elements)

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Practical Approach to Random Projections**: The core contribution of PoP is significant. By using positive-only random projections, the authors solve a fundamental limitation of existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI), which cannot be weighted with PPMI due to their zero-mean distributions. This enables the combination of the computational efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI weighting - a powerful combination that wasn't previously possible. The paper clearly explains why previous methods couldn't incorporate PPMI weighting (due to zero sums in vectors), making the novelty clear.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The authors provide comprehensive experimental validation across multiple dimensions. Figure 1 demonstrates PoP outperforms both count-based models and RI for small context windows. Figure 2 shows that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on MEN, comparable to neural embeddings. Figure 4 and 5 provide valuable insights into how PoP's parameters affect performance. The paper thoroughly explores the trade-offs between dimensionality, sparsity, and performance, making a compelling case for the method's practical utility.

3. **Significant Computational Efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that PoP models can be built in seconds or minutes rather than hours or days required for neural embeddings. The authors cite Baroni et al. (2014) to show that neural embeddings can take months to train, while PoP achieves competitive results without heavy computation. This efficiency advantage is a major practical contribution, especially for resource-constrained applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation**: The paper acknowledges it lacks theoretical justification for PoP (Section 2.3), which is a significant weakness. While empirical validation is important, the absence of any mathematical analysis of why PoP works or how parameters affect performance is concerning. The authors state they rely on empirical results, but this is insufficient for a method that makes strong claims about its behavior. A brief theoretical analysis (even if preliminary) would greatly strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited State-of-the-Art Comparison**: The paper compares PoP primarily to RI, count-based models, and basic PPMI, but doesn't adequately compare it to recent state-of-the-art methods. For instance, it doesn't compare to fastText, GloVe, or more recent neural approaches that have achieved high performance with lower computational costs. The claim that PoP achieves "competitive results" is vague without specific comparisons to current SOTA methods. The paper mentions Chen and de Melo (2015) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015) but doesn't provide specific numbers for comparison.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity and Practical Guidance**: While the paper explores parameter effects, it doesn't provide clear, principled guidance for choosing optimal parameters (m, s, number of non-zero elements) for different tasks or datasets. Figure 5 shows that increasing the number of non-zero elements can degrade performance, but the paper doesn't provide a systematic way to determine the optimal sparsity. The recommendation of "0.002 < s" for direct use with τb and "1 or 2 non-zero elements" for PPMI-weighted spaces feels somewhat arbitrary without more rigorous justification.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on MEN. How does this compare to the current state-of-the-art on MEN? The paper mentions it's comparable to neural embeddings, but without specific numbers for recent methods, it's hard to assess the significance of this result.

2. The paper claims PoP is "incredibly faster" than applying PPMI to count-based models. Can you provide specific timing comparisons (e.g., seconds/minutes for count-based PPMI vs. PoP+PPMI) to quantify this advantage?

3. The paper acknowledges it lacks theoretical justification. What are your thoughts on how to develop a theoretical foundation for PoP in future work? Are there specific mathematical frameworks or lemmas you would like to explore?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and practical approach to semantic space construction that solves a significant limitation of existing random projection methods. The empirical results are compelling, and the computational efficiency advantage is substantial. However, the lack of theoretical foundation and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods are notable weaknesses that need to be addressed. With additional theoretical analysis and more comprehensive comparisons to recent methods, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to substantially address the theoretical limitations and provide more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods in the revision. The core idea is promising, but the paper as written lacks the depth required for strong acceptance.
