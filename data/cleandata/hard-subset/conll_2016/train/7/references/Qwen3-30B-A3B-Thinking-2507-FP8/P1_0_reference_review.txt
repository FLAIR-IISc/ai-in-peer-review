

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces. The main contributions are:

1. A new random projection technique that uses only positive integer values in the projection matrix, enabling subsequent application of PPMI weighting (which is not possible with existing random projection methods like RI that use zero-mean distributions).

2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural embeddings.

3. Comprehensive analysis of PoP's parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and their effect on performance.

## Strengths

1. **Solves a genuine gap in the literature**: The paper correctly identifies that existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) cannot be used with PPMI weighting due to their zero-mean random matrices. PoP's positive-only approach directly addresses this limitation, enabling the use of well-established weighting techniques on random projection-based models. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation on MEN relatedness test is thorough and well-designed. The paper demonstrates clear performance advantages over RI and count-based models, with PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving 0.75 average correlation (competitive with neural methods) while being significantly faster to compute. The parameter analysis (Figures 4-6) provides valuable practical guidance for implementation.

3. **Clear performance advantage over baselines**: The results show that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models across context window sizes, with PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving ρ > 0.70 with small context windows (4+4) compared to the need for larger windows with other methods. This demonstrates a practical advantage for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper acknowledges that it cannot provide a mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of PoP, citing similar gaps in RI's original formulation. However, this is a significant weakness for a theoretical contribution. Without theoretical grounding, the claim that PoP "preserves semantic information" remains unverified and the paper relies solely on empirical results.

2. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims competitive results with neural methods but only references older papers (Chen and de Melo, 2015; Tsvetkov et al., 2015). Current SOTA methods (e.g., BERT-based approaches) achieve ρ > 0.85 on MEN. The paper should provide direct comparisons to modern neural methods to properly contextualize its results.

3. **Vague computational efficiency claims**: The paper states models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" but provides no concrete timing comparisons. How much faster is PoP than neural methods? How does it compare to other efficient methods like word2vec? Without specific numbers, the computational advantage claim lacks substance.

4. **Inconsistent treatment of dimensionality**: The paper claims "vectors in PoP-constructed models are small (i.e., dimensionality of a few hundred)" but later uses m=1000-5000 in experiments. This inconsistency undermines the efficiency claim and creates confusion about the actual dimensionality used.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims PoP is "incremental" but doesn't explain how this differs from other incremental methods or why this property is particularly valuable for semantic space construction. Could you clarify this?

2. The paper states that "for m ≥ 3000, PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75." How does this compare to the current SOTA on MEN (which is ~0.85)? What specific neural methods does this compare to?

3. The paper mentions "using only 1 or 2 non-zero elements" for optimal performance with PPMI, but Figure 5 shows better performance with more non-zero elements. Can you clarify this apparent contradiction?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a meaningful contribution by solving a genuine problem in random projection-based semantic space construction. The empirical results are strong and the method is well-motivated. However, the lack of theoretical justification and limited comparison to current SOTA methods are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With additional theoretical analysis and more comprehensive comparisons to modern methods, this could be a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions: 1) Provide concrete computational timing comparisons, 2) Add direct comparisons to current SOTA neural methods, 3) Clarify the theoretical basis for the method, and 4) Resolve the inconsistencies in dimensionality claims. The paper has merit but requires significant revisions to fully establish its contribution.
