

# Review

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for incremental semantic space construction that uses random projections with only positive integer values. The primary contribution is a novel random projection method that enables subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI (Positive Pointwise Mutual Information) to the resulting vectors, which is not possible with existing random projection methods (e.g., RI, which uses matrices with E(R) = 0). The second contribution is empirical demonstration that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks (MEN relatedness test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural embeddings. The third contribution is the observation that PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution of count-based models, enabling PPMI transformation to produce Gaussian-like distributions.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Technical Approach**: The paper presents a clear technical innovation in using positive-only random projections to enable PPMI weighting, which addresses a fundamental limitation of existing random projection methods. The distinction between PoP and RI (which cannot use PPMI due to zero-mean distributions) is well-explained and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The authors provide comprehensive experimental results on the MEN relatedness test, comparing PoP with RI, count-based models, and PPMI-weighted models. The results demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ρ ≈ 0.75, comparable to state-of-the-art neural methods, but with dramatically reduced computational requirements (seconds/minutes versus hours/days).

3. **Practical Impact**: The paper convincingly demonstrates the computational efficiency advantage of PoP, which is a significant practical contribution for NLP practitioners. The authors show that PoP-constructed models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" compared to "hours, days or months" required for neural embeddings.

4. **Clear Explanations and Figures**: The paper effectively uses figures to illustrate key findings, particularly Figure 3 showing the distribution of weights across different model types, which clearly supports the claim that PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution necessary for PPMI application.

5. **Comprehensive Parameter Analysis**: The ablation studies on dimensionality (Figure 4), number of non-zero elements (Figure 5), and the relationship between non-orthogonality and performance (Figure 6) provide valuable insights into PoP's behavior and parameter sensitivity.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper acknowledges it cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying the error δ (difference between optimum result and PoP result), which is a significant limitation. While the authors cite historical precedents (like RI being proposed without justification), this remains a notable gap in the theoretical foundation of the method.

2. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The paper evaluates only on the MEN relatedness test, which is a standard benchmark, but does not compare to recent state-of-the-art methods beyond neural embeddings (e.g., fastText, GloVe). Additional benchmarks like SimLex-999 or WordSim-353 would strengthen the claims.

3. **Insufficient Comparison to Efficient Alternatives**: The paper does not compare to other efficient word embedding methods like fastText or GloVe, which achieve high performance with reasonable computational requirements. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess where PoP stands relative to the current state of the art.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity Without Clear Guidance**: While the paper explores parameter sensitivity, it doesn't provide a systematic method for determining optimal parameters (m, s) for different tasks or datasets. The recommendation "0.002 < s" for direct use and "1 or 2 non-zero elements" for PPMI-weighted models is somewhat vague.

5. **Limited Ablation on PPMI Contribution**: The paper shows that PoP+PPMI outperforms PoP alone, but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to quantify the specific contribution of PPMI versus PoP itself.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson compare to recent state-of-the-art methods like fastText or GloVe on the MEN benchmark? The paper mentions results comparable to neural embeddings but doesn't provide direct comparisons to efficient embedding methods.

2. The paper states that "even without weighting transformation, PoP-constructed models display a better performance than RI." How much better is the performance without weighting, and what explains this improvement?

3. The paper mentions that "applying SVD truncation to models results in negligible improvements." Why is this the case, and does it suggest PoP-constructed models are already well-suited for downstream tasks without additional dimensionality reduction?
