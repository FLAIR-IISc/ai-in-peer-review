

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes Positive-Only Projection (PoP), an incremental semantic space construction method using random projections with positive integer values. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting of resulting vectors (unlike existing random projection methods such as RI), while maintaining computational efficiency. The main contributions are:
1. A novel random projection method that preserves the ability to apply PPMI weighting, which is not possible with standard random projections due to their zero-mean nature.
2. A computationally efficient approach for semantic space construction (building models in seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural embeddings).
3. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (ρ > 0.75) on the MEN relatedness test.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Genuine Limitation**: The paper effectively identifies a significant limitation in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) - their inability to use PPMI weighting due to the zero-mean property of their random matrices. This is a well-justified problem that has practical consequences for semantic space construction.

2. **Computational Efficiency Claim**: The paper makes a strong case for the computational advantages of PoP, noting that models can be built "in a matter of seconds or minutes" compared to "hours, days or months" for neural embeddings. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where computational resources are constrained.

3. **Competitive Performance**: The empirical results demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ρ = 0.75 on the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to state-of-the-art neural embedding methods. This shows the method can deliver competitive results without the training overhead of neural networks.

4. **Clear Explanation of Why PoP Works**: The paper provides a good explanation for why PoP enables PPMI weighting (positive values in the transformation matrix ensure non-zero sums for PPMI calculations), which is well-supported by the mathematical formulation in Section 2.3.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The paper does not adequately distinguish PoP from existing methods that already use positive values. For example, RMI (Random Manhattan Indexing) uses positive random projections (as noted in the paper's citation to Zadeh and Handschuh, 2014), which should theoretically allow PPMI weighting. The paper fails to compare with RMI or explain how PoP differs from or improves upon it. This significantly undermines the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a mathematical account for error bounds (δ) or theoretical properties of the PoP method. While empirical results are valuable, a method claiming to be based on random projections should at least provide theoretical bounds for the error introduced by the projection, similar to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma for Gaussian projections.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with Relevant Methods**: The evaluation is limited to comparisons with RI and count-based models, but fails to compare with other positive-value random projection methods (like RMI) or state-of-the-art count-based methods with PPMI. The claim that PoP "outperforms RI" is not particularly impressive given that RI was designed to preserve Euclidean distances, not to improve semantic similarity performance.

4. **Incomplete Ablation Study**: The paper does not provide a clear ablation study to isolate the contribution of the "positive-only" aspect versus the random projection aspect. Without this, it's unclear whether the positive values are truly necessary or whether a simple modification to existing methods would suffice.

5. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to the MEN relatedness test. It would be valuable to see how PoP performs on other semantic tasks (e.g., word similarity, analogy tasks) to demonstrate broader applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims PoP is the first method that allows PPMI weighting on random projection-based models. However, RMI (Zadeh and Handschuh, 2014) uses positive random projections. Please clarify how PoP differs from RMI in implementation and performance.

2. The paper states that RI has E(R)=0, but doesn't explicitly address whether other positive-value methods (like RMI) have this property. How does PoP differ from RMI in terms of the statistical properties of the projection matrix?

3. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ρ=0.75, but doesn't compare with state-of-the-art count-based methods (e.g., PPMI with SVD) that are not neural embeddings. How does PoP compare to these established methods?

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the related work section to properly compare with RMI and other positive-value random projection methods. Clarify what makes PoP distinct from these approaches.

2. Provide theoretical justification for the method, even if preliminary. At minimum, derive the expected error bound for PoP's random projections.

3. Include a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with state-of-the-art count-based methods (PPMI+SVD) and additional semantic tasks beyond MEN relatedness.

4. Conduct a proper ablation study to isolate the contribution of the positive-only aspect versus the random projection aspect.

5. Address the limitations of the method more thoroughly, including cases where PoP might underperform compared to other approaches.

While the paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing methods and proposes a potentially useful solution, the novelty claims are undermined by insufficient comparison with existing positive-value projection methods, and the theoretical foundation is lacking. With the suggested improvements, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
