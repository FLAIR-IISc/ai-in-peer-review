- Main idea: Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), enabling PPMI weighting in random projection-based semantic spaces by using strictly positive integer projections.
- Key claim: PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN) with much lower computational cost than neural methods.
- Novelty: Solves a fundamental limitation of prior random projections (zero-mean distributions) allowing PPMI use — deemed a genuinely novel insight.
- Efficiency: Authors claim PoP builds semantic spaces orders of magnitude faster than neural models.
- Parameter analysis: Comprehensive study of PoP across dimensionality, sparsity, and other hyperparameters provides practical guidance.
- Empirical validation: Strong evaluation on the MEN test set and informative ablation studies (Figures 2, 4–6) showing PoP outperforms count-based models and RI.
- Practical significance: Potential to build useful models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days, important for resource-limited settings.
- Weakness — SOTA comparisons: Lacks direct comparisons to modern neural methods (fastText, GloVe, BERT), making the “competitive” claim hard to verify.
- Weakness — efficiency metrics: No concrete timing or memory usage data provided to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Weakness — theory gap: Missing a detailed mathematical justification for the error bound δ in the random projection analysis, weakening theoretical credibility.
- Author questions: Provide specific runtime/memory comparisons with neural methods on same hardware and corpus (e.g., UKWaC).
- Author questions: Directly compare PoP+PPMI+Pearson to fastText/GloVe on MEN (0.78–0.82 typical) to contextualize performance.
- Author questions: Clarify theoretical relationship between distribution of non-zero projection elements and final accuracy.
- Overall assessment: Valuable and novel contribution with promising empirical results, but needs stronger empirical SOTA comparisons, concrete efficiency metrics, and better theoretical justification.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing empirical comparisons, computational measurements, and theoretical gaps.
