

# Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

## Summary
This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel dimensionality reduction technique for building semantic spaces. PoP addresses a key limitation of existing random projection methods (like Random Indexing) by using random vectors with only positive integer values, enabling the application of weighting techniques like PPMI (Positive Pointwise Mutual Information) that are incompatible with traditional random projections (which use mean-zero distributions). The authors claim PoP achieves competitive performance with neural embeddings while being significantly faster to compute (seconds/minutes versus hours/days).

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables PPMI weighting of semantic spaces while maintaining computational efficiency.
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves high performance (0.75 on MEN relatedness test) comparable to neural embeddings, but with dramatically reduced computation time.
3. Comprehensive analysis of PoP's parameter sensitivity (dimensionality, sparsity, etc.) and its relationship to performance.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Innovation with Clear Value Proposition**: The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing methods (the incompatibility of PPMI with random projection techniques) and provides a straightforward solution. The claim that PoP enables PPMI weighting while maintaining the computational advantages of random projections is compelling. The authors provide concrete evidence that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on MEN (comparable to neural methods) while requiring orders of magnitude less computation time. This represents a significant practical contribution for NLP practitioners who need efficient semantic space construction.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The evaluation is well-designed and comprehensive. The authors compare PoP against count-based models, RI, and PPMI-transformed models across multiple dimensions (context window size, vector dimensionality, sparsity). Figure 2 clearly demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms both RI and count-based models while being computationally more efficient. The analysis of the distribution of weights (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into why PoP works well with PPMI.

3. **Thorough Parameter Analysis**: The paper goes beyond just presenting results to analyze how PoP's performance varies with key parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements, etc.). Figure 4 and 5 provide valuable guidance for practitioners on optimal parameter settings. The insight that "0.002 < s" is recommended for direct use with Kendall's τb, while "1 or 2 non-zero elements" are better for PPMI transformation, is particularly useful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: This is the paper's most significant weakness. The authors explicitly state they "are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" (the error bound for their method), despite acknowledging this is a standard expectation in theoretical work. While they reference historical precedents (like RI), the field has moved beyond this. Without theoretical grounding, it's difficult to assess the limits of PoP or guarantee performance across different datasets.

2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Methods**: The paper compares PoP primarily to RI and count-based models, but lacks detailed comparison to modern neural embedding methods. For example, it doesn't compare to FastText (which is both efficient and high-performing), or to recent SOTA methods on MEN. The claim of "competitive results" is vague without these comparisons.

3. **Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper states that "codes and resulting embeddings from experiments will be shared alongside the camera ready version" but doesn't provide sufficient detail to reproduce the results. Key hyperparameters (like optimal values for s, m, and context window size) are only partially specified in the results. The paper would be significantly stronger with a detailed appendix of hyperparameters used in all experiments.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "an average of 0.78 with the max of 0.787" after fine-tuning. How does this compare to the best results reported in recent literature (e.g., FastText, GloVe, or recent neural methods)? Please provide specific comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.

2. The paper claims PoP models can be built in "a matter of seconds or minutes" but doesn't provide actual timing data. How long does it take to build a PoP model for the UKWaC corpus (which is large) compared to RI or neural embeddings? What are the scaling characteristics as corpus size increases?

3. The paper states that "the sum of values of vector elements in the PoP-constructed models is always greater than 0." How does the authors ensure this in practice, and what happens if the sum is exactly 0 (which could theoretically occur with their random distribution)? How does this affect the PPMI transformation?

## Overall Recommendation
This is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of semantic space construction. The PoP method addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches and demonstrates strong empirical results. However, the lack of theoretical justification and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would be substantially strengthened by providing more detailed comparisons to recent neural methods, adding theoretical analysis of the method, and including more reproducibility details.

The paper makes a compelling case for PoP as a practical alternative to neural embeddings for applications where computational resources are constrained. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
