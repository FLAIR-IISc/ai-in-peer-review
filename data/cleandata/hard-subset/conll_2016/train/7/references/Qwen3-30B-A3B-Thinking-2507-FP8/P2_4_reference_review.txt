

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces through random projections with only positive integer values. The primary contribution is the insight that PoP's positive-only projections enable PPMI weighting, which traditional random projection methods (like RI) cannot support due to their zero-mean distributions. The secondary contribution is demonstrating that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (ρ≈0.75 on MEN test) with dramatically reduced computational requirements compared to neural embeddings (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days). The tertiary contribution is providing empirical evidence of PoP's efficiency and effectiveness.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Advantage:** The paper makes a compelling case for PoP's computational efficiency, which is a major practical contribution. The claim that semantic spaces can be constructed in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days is substantial, especially for applications with limited computational resources. This addresses a real pain point in the field where neural embeddings require substantial training time and resources. The authors provide clear evidence through their experiments showing PoP's speed advantage without sacrificing performance.

2. **Novel Technical Insight:** The core contribution of enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based models is genuinely novel. The authors correctly identify a limitation in existing methods (RI, RRI, etc.) where zero-mean random projections prevent PPMI application, and PoP overcomes this limitation through positive-only projections. This is a solid technical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature.

3. **Clear Empirical Validation:** The paper provides well-designed experiments comparing PoP with RI and count-based models on the MEN relatedness test. The results clearly show PoP's superiority, especially when combined with PPMI and Pearson correlation. The ablation studies (Figures 4-6) provide valuable insights into parameter sensitivity, which strengthens the empirical claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation:** The paper acknowledges that it lacks a detailed mathematical account for PoP's behavior, stating "we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ." While the authors cite precedents like RI's initial lack of justification, this remains a significant weakness for a computational linguistics paper. The absence of theoretical justification for why PoP works as it does, or why it outperforms other methods, weakens the paper's scientific contribution.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper's claim that PoP achieves "competitive performance" is undermined by the absence of comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods. The authors compare with RI and count-based models but don't compare with methods like fastText, GloVe, or contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT). The MEN test results are presented without context about how they compare to current SOTA approaches (e.g., Chen and de Melo, 2015; Tsvetkov et al., 2015). Without these comparisons, the claim of "competitive performance" is unsubstantiated.

3. **Limited Ablation and Parameter Analysis:** While the paper provides some parameter analysis (Figures 4-6), it lacks deeper ablation studies. For example, it doesn't isolate the contribution of PoP itself versus the PPMI transformation. The paper claims that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75, but it doesn't show how much of this comes from the PoP method versus the PPMI transformation applied to a count-based model. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of the PoP method.

4. **Implementation Details Inadequacy:** The paper lacks sufficient detail about implementation specifics. For instance, it doesn't explain how the optimal parameters (s, m, number of non-zero elements) were chosen or how they scale to larger corpora. The claim that "with careful selection of context elements... we achieve an average of 0.78" is vague without specific details about these "careful selections."

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance with neural embeddings. Can you provide specific comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., fastText, GloVe, BERT) on the MEN test? How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson compare to these methods in terms of both performance and computational efficiency?

2. The paper states that "the dimensionality of index vectors can be seen as the capacity of the method to memorize and distinguish entities." Can you provide more empirical evidence or theoretical justification for this claim, particularly regarding how dimensionality affects the model's ability to capture semantic relationships?

3. The paper mentions that "PoP-constructed models, in contrast to previously suggested random projection-based models, can be still weighted using PPMI." Can you explain why PPMI weighting works better with PoP than with count-based models (which also use PPMI)? What specific properties of PoP enable this?

4. The paper reports that "for index vector dimensions m ≥ 3000, PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75." How does this compare to the performance of count-based models with PPMI and SVD truncation? What is the computational cost of applying PPMI to PoP versus count-based models?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and practical method for constructing semantic spaces with significant computational advantages. The core insight about enabling PPMI weighting through positive-only projections is valuable and addresses a real limitation in the field. However, the lack of theoretical justification and insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods are serious weaknesses that prevent this paper from being a strong contribution to the field. The empirical results are promising but need to be placed in proper context with current SOTA approaches.

I would recommend this paper for acceptance with major revision, primarily to address the theoretical weaknesses and provide more comprehensive comparisons with contemporary methods. The practical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication if these gaps can be filled.
