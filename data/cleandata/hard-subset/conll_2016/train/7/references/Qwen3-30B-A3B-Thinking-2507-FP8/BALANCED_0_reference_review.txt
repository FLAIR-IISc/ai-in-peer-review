

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with only positive integer values. The authors identify a key limitation in existing random projection techniques (like RI, RRI, ISA, RMI), which use matrices with E(R)=0 and thus cannot be weighted using PPMI. PoP solves this by using random projections with positive values, enabling PPMI transformation while maintaining computational efficiency.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. The PoP method itself, which enables PPMI weighting on projected vectors while maintaining computational efficiency.
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) with significantly reduced computational requirements compared to neural embeddings.
3. Comprehensive analysis of PoP's parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements) and their impact on performance.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a genuine limitation in the field**: The authors correctly identify that existing random projection methods cannot be weighted with PPMI due to their mean-zero property, which is a significant practical limitation. PoP solves this problem elegantly, enabling a new combination of techniques that wasn't previously possible.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 Spearman correlation on the MEN test, which is competitive with neural embedding approaches while requiring orders of magnitude less computational resources. The performance remains above 0.50 even without PPMI weighting, which is notable.

3. **Thorough parameter analysis**: The authors provide a detailed examination of how PoP's performance varies with key parameters (dimensionality, number of non-zero elements), showing that performance improves with higher dimensionality and optimal sparsity settings. This is crucial for practitioners considering implementing the method.

4. **Clear computational advantage**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that PoP models can be built in seconds or minutes rather than hours or days required for neural embeddings, which is a significant practical contribution for resource-constrained applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for why PoP works as well as it does. While this is common in applied ML work, the absence of even a heuristic explanation for the observed performance limits the paper's scientific contribution. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma reference is insufficient to justify the specific behavior of PoP.

2. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares PoP to RI and count-based models but doesn't provide direct comparisons to current neural embedding approaches (e.g., FastText, GloVe, or recent contextualized embeddings) on the same benchmark. The claim of "competitive performance" is somewhat vague without these direct comparisons.

3. **Unclear performance advantage over standard PPMI**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why PoP+PPMI achieves better results than standard PPMI applied directly to count-based models. The computational advantage is clear, but the performance advantage isn't well-justified, making it difficult to assess the true value proposition of the method.

4. **Arbitrary similarity measure switching**: The paper uses Kendall's τb for unweighted PoP models but switches to Pearson's r after PPMI transformation without a strong theoretical justification. The authors mention this is because PPMI-transformed spaces have Gaussian-like distributions, but this claim isn't thoroughly supported by the data presented.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of PoP+PPMI+Pearson compare to standard PPMI-weighted count-based models (without random projection) on the MEN test? This would help clarify whether the performance gain is due to the projection or the PPMI weighting.

2. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "comparable performance" to neural embeddings, but doesn't provide direct comparisons to recent state-of-the-art methods. Could the authors provide these comparisons to strengthen their claim?

3. The paper suggests that PoP's ability to maintain the long-tailed distribution of original vectors (Figure 3d) is beneficial for PPMI transformation. However, the standard approach (PPMI on count-based models) also starts with a long-tailed distribution. Why does PoP+PPMI outperform standard PPMI on count-based models?

4. The paper mentions that PoP's random nature means "a randomized algorithm such as the RI and PoP methods give different results even for the same input." How do the authors suggest handling this variability in practice (e.g., averaging multiple runs)? The paper reports average results but doesn't discuss practical implementation considerations.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting method that solves a genuine problem in semantic space construction. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive comparisons, and clearer explanation of the performance advantages over existing approaches. With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.
