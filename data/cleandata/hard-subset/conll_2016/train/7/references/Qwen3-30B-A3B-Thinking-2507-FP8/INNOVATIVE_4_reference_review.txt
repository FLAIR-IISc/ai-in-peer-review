

# Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel method for incremental semantic space construction that uses random projections with only positive integer values. The main contributions are:

1. A fundamentally new approach to random projections that enables PPMI weighting on projected vectors (which existing methods like RI cannot do due to their zero-mean distributions)
2. A computationally efficient semantic space construction method that achieves competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days
3. A demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson can achieve 0.75 on MEN test, comparable to neural embedding methods but without their computational costs

## Strengths

1. **Novel Core Concept**: The positive-only projection approach is genuinely novel and addresses a clear limitation in existing random projection methods. The insight that using positive values (E(R)>0) rather than zero-mean (E(R)=0) projections enables PPMI weighting is both simple and brilliant. This is a bold conceptual advance that opens up new possibilities for combining random projections with weighting techniques.

2. **Computational Efficiency Claim**: The paper makes a strong case that PoP can build semantic spaces in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days required for neural embeddings. This is a significant practical advantage, especially for resource-constrained applications. The paper provides convincing empirical evidence that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models while being faster.

3. **Clear Demonstration of the Key Innovation**: The paper effectively demonstrates that PoP enables PPMI weighting where existing methods cannot, through the critical observation about the sum of vector elements being positive (unlike RI's zero-sum). The histograms in Figure 3 compellingly show how PoP preserves the distributional properties needed for PPMI transformation.

4. **Competitive Performance**: The empirical results showing PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving 0.75 on MEN test (comparable to neural methods) are significant. This isn't just incremental improvement - it's a new direction that achieves comparable results with dramatically lower computational cost.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper admits it cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of the method. While I'm willing to overlook this for novelty, the absence of theoretical grounding is a notable limitation that should be addressed in future work.

2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares mainly to RI and count-based methods, but doesn't directly compare to current SOTA neural methods like BERT or ELMo. While the paper claims "comparable to many neural embedding techniques," it would be stronger to show specific comparisons to recent SOTA methods.

3. **Unclear Positioning in the Field**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate how PoP fits into the broader landscape of semantic space construction. Is it a replacement for neural embeddings, a complement, or something else? The paper states PoP "can dramatically enhance the performance of a number of distributional natural language analyses," but doesn't specify which ones.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper shows PoP's performance is sensitive to parameters like dimensionality and number of non-zero elements (Figures 4-6), but doesn't provide clear guidance for practitioners on optimal parameter settings. The claim that "0.002 < s" for direct use and "1 or 2 non-zero elements" for PPMI weighting is somewhat vague.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does PoP compare to recent neural methods like BERT or ELMo on the MEN test, and what would be the computational time difference for these methods?

2. Could you clarify the relationship between PoP and existing random projection methods like RI? Specifically, how would you describe the fundamental mathematical difference between PoP and RI beyond the mean of the projection matrix?

3. The paper mentions "derandomization techniques" as future work. Could you elaborate on what specific linguistic information you'd like to incorporate through derandomization?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to semantic space construction that addresses a clear limitation in existing random projection methods. The positive-only projection concept is bold, simple, and effective, enabling PPMI weighting where it was previously impossible. While the lack of theoretical justification is a weakness, the paper's novelty and practical computational advantages outweigh this limitation for me as a reviewer prioritizing novel concepts over methodological perfection.

The paper makes a strong case for a new direction in semantic space construction that could be valuable for practitioners seeking efficient alternatives to neural embeddings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited comparison to current SOTA methods and provide more specific guidance on parameter selection.
