

# Review

**Summary:** The paper proposes "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), an incremental semantic space construction method using random projections with positive integer values. The authors claim PoP enables PPMI weighting (which is not possible with existing random projection methods like RI), achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN test) without neural network training, and requires minimal computational resources ("a few seconds or minutes" vs. "hours, days or months" for neural embeddings).

**Main Contributions:** The paper claims three primary contributions: 1) A novel random projection method (PoP) that enables PPMI weighting; 2) An efficient semantic space construction approach; 3) Competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks without neural network training.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper demonstrates PoP's compatibility with PPMI weighting, which is a valid observation (unlike RI, PoP's positive vectors avoid the zero-sum issue).
2. The empirical results on MEN test are generally consistent with expectations (PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms PoP and RI).
3. The paper provides useful insights about dimensionality trade-offs and the behavior of different similarity measures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of rigorous theoretical foundation:** The authors explicitly admit they cannot provide a mathematical account for error bounds (δ) of PoP, relying instead on empirical results. This is unacceptable for a method claiming to be a viable alternative to established approaches. The paper cites Kanerva et al. (2000) as having proposed RI without mathematical justification, but this is not a valid defense for a new method in 2023. Theoretical grounding is essential for new methods, especially when claiming to outperform established baselines.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper compares PoP primarily to RI and count-based models, but fails to compare with modern, established methods (e.g., GloVe, FastText, or even the PPMI+SVD baseline they cite). The claim of "competitive results" is meaningless without direct comparison to current SOTA methods. For instance, the paper states that "PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75," but doesn't specify how this compares to established methods like GloVe (which typically scores 0.75-0.78 on MEN). Without these comparisons, the claimed "competitive" performance is unsubstantiated.

3. **Unverified efficiency claims:** The paper claims PoP "can be built in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" but provides no concrete timing comparisons with established methods. Without benchmarking actual implementation times against methods like GloVe or FastText, this claim remains speculative. The paper's own Figure 4 shows that PoP's performance is highly sensitive to parameter choices (e.g., dimensionality), undermining the claim of consistent efficiency.

4. **Inconsistent results and lack of ablation studies:** The paper reports contradictory findings: Figure 4 shows that for unweighted PoP, performance decreases when dimensionality exceeds 2000, contrary to standard intuition. The paper offers no compelling explanation for this counterintuitive result. Furthermore, the paper fails to conduct proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of PoP versus PPMI weighting. Without these, it's impossible to determine whether the performance gain comes from PoP itself or simply from applying PPMI to any low-dimensional representation.

5. **Questionable novelty:** The core idea of using positive random projections isn't entirely new. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish PoP from existing methods like RMI (Zadeh and Handschuh, 2014), which also uses positive values. The claim of novelty appears overstated given the limited comparison with relevant prior work.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson specifically compare to GloVe (which also uses PPMI and achieves ~0.75 on MEN) in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency? Without this comparison, the claimed advantages remain unverified.

2. The paper states that "PoP-constructed models display better performance than RI on semantic similarity assessments" even without weighting. Can you provide specific numbers and statistical significance for this claim?

3. The paper reports that increasing dimensionality beyond 2000 decreases performance for unweighted PoP. What is the theoretical basis for this counterintuitive finding, and how does it align with standard dimensionality principles?

4. The paper mentions that "the involved processes are all done on vectors of low dimensionality" but doesn't specify the exact dimensionality used in their most competitive results. How does this dimensionality compare to standard practices in the field?

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents an interesting observation about positive random projections enabling PPMI weighting, the claims of novelty, efficiency, and competitive performance are not sufficiently supported. The lack of theoretical grounding, inadequate comparisons with established baselines, and inconsistent results undermine the paper's main contributions. The method appears to be a minor variation of existing techniques rather than a significant advancement. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence for the claimed advantages over established approaches.
