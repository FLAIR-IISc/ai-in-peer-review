

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive integer values. The core claim is that PoP enables PPMI weighting (which previous random projection methods like RI cannot support) while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to neural embeddings but with dramatically reduced computation time (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days).

## Primary Contributions
1. **The PoP method itself**, which enables PPMI weighting on random projection-based models by using positive random vectors (a valid technical insight)
2. **Empirical demonstration** that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive performance (0.75 MEN score) while being computationally efficient
3. **Theoretical justification** for why Pearson correlation outperforms Kendall's τb after PPMI transformation

## Strengths

1. **Valid identification of a genuine limitation**: The paper correctly identifies that previous random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) cannot support PPMI weighting due to their Gaussian random matrices with E(R)=0. This is a real gap in the literature that the paper addresses.

2. **Strong computational efficiency claim**: The empirical demonstration that models can be built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days is compelling and addresses a real pain point in NLP. The low dimensionality (100-5000) and incremental construction make this practically valuable.

3. **Good empirical evaluation**: The comparison to count-based models, RI, and PPMI-transformed models is well-structured, and the MEN test is a standard benchmark. The demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms simple PoP (0.75 vs. 0.60) is convincing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims PoP is "new" but fails to engage with prior work that used similar positive random projections. Baroni et al.'s 2007 ISA method used positive weights (with a similar "sparse positive" structure), and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish PoP from this prior work. This weakens the contribution claim significantly.

2. **Lack of theoretical foundation**: The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a mathematical account for error bounds (δ) or theoretical justification for PoP's performance. This is a major weakness for a method claiming to be based on random projections. The paper cites Johnson-Lindenstrauss but doesn't establish similar bounds for their non-Gaussian projections.

3. **Superficial comparison with neural methods**: The paper states PoP+PPMI+Pearson "achieves competitive performance" compared to neural embeddings but only compares to older methods (Baroni et al. 2014). It lacks direct comparison with modern state-of-the-art methods like GloVe (0.78 MEN), FastText (0.79), or BERT-based models. The claim of "competitive" performance without this comparison is unsubstantiated.

4. **Parameter sensitivity without clear guidance**: The experiments (Figures 4-6) show PoP is highly sensitive to parameters (s, number of non-zero elements), yet the paper provides only vague guidelines ("0.002 < s" for direct use, "1 or 2 non-zero elements" for PPMI). This limits practical adoption.

5. **Incomplete explanation of performance gains**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain *why* PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms count-based PPMI models. If the dimensionality reduction is the key factor, why doesn't RI+PPMI work (it can't)? The paper doesn't establish whether the positive projections specifically contribute beyond just lower dimensionality.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does PoP specifically differ from Baroni et al.'s 2007 ISA method, which also used positive sparse random projections? What improvements does PoP offer over ISA?

2. Can you provide a direct comparison of PoP+PPMI+Pearson with modern state-of-the-art methods (GloVe, FastText) on the MEN test and other standard benchmarks?

3. The paper claims "PoP-constructed models can be built incrementally" but doesn't explain how this incremental construction differs from RI's incremental approach. What specific advantages does PoP offer for incremental learning?

4. Why do the authors use Kendall's τb for PoP without PPMI but Pearson for PoP+PPMI? What theoretical justification supports this switch beyond the Gaussian distribution claim?

This paper presents a useful practical method with clear computational advantages, but the novelty is overstated, the theoretical foundation is weak, and the comparison to state-of-the-art methods is inadequate. With stronger theoretical grounding, proper engagement with prior work, and more comprehensive comparisons, this could be a valuable contribution. As it stands, the claims of novelty and performance are not fully supported.
