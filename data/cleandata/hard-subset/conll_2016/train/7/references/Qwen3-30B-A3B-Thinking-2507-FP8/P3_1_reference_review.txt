

# Peer Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

## Summary
This paper proposes "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with strictly positive integer values. The core innovation is that PoP enables subsequent application of PPMI weighting (which is impossible with standard random projection methods like RI that use zero-mean distributions), while maintaining computational efficiency through low-dimensional vector representation. The authors claim PoP achieves competitive performance in semantic similarity tasks (0.75 on MEN test) with dramatically faster model construction than neural embeddings.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel random projection method with positive values** enabling PPMI weighting, addressing a fundamental limitation in existing random projection techniques
2. **Demonstrated computational efficiency** (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural methods)
3. **Competitive performance** on semantic similarity tasks compared to state-of-the-art methods

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a genuine limitation in existing work:** The paper correctly identifies that standard random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) cannot be weighted with PPMI because their random matrices have E[R] = 0, making row/column sums zero and PPMI calculations undefined. This is a valid and important observation that the authors properly contextualize within the literature.

2. **Strong empirical demonstration of computational advantage:** The paper convincingly shows that PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes (vs. hours/days for neural embeddings), with clear evidence of the speed advantage. The authors appropriately use the MEN test and UKWaC corpus for evaluation, which are standard in the field.

3. **Competitive results on semantic similarity task:** The reported MEN correlation of 0.75 for PoP+PPMI+Pearson is indeed competitive with many neural embedding approaches (e.g., the paper cites scores around 0.78-0.80 for some methods). This is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification:** This is the most serious weakness. The authors explicitly acknowledge they cannot provide "a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" or theoretical discussion of PoP's underlying random projection. While they cite similar situations in prior work (like RI), this is insufficient for a machine learning conference. The paper claims PoP "yields competitive results" but provides no theoretical bounds on performance, error rates, or why positive projections work better than zero-mean alternatives. Without this, the contribution lacks academic rigor.

2. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art methods:** The paper primarily compares to RI and count-based models but fails to benchmark against recent efficient methods like FastText (which achieves strong performance with minimal computational cost) or more recent neural approaches. The paper mentions "neural net-based approaches" but doesn't provide specific comparisons to current state-of-the-art methods (e.g., BERT, ELMo, or even GloVe), making the claimed competitiveness difficult to verify.

3. **Unclear quantitative advantage:** The paper states that "applying weighting methods such as PPMI to these models is incredibly faster than applying them to classical count-based models" but provides no actual runtime measurements or comparative numbers. Without specific time benchmarks, the claimed efficiency advantage remains anecdotal rather than empirical.

4. **Overstated novelty:** The paper claims PoP is "a new method" but fails to adequately distinguish it from existing work. For instance, the method bears significant resemblance to the "random indexing" approach but with positive values. The authors cite Kanerva et al. (2000) who proposed RI without theoretical justification, but PoP is a minor variant rather than a fundamentally new approach.

5. **Lack of ablation study:** The paper doesn't provide a clear ablation study to determine which component of the method (positive-only projections, low dimensionality, PPMI weighting) is most responsible for the performance gains. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide specific runtime measurements comparing PoP+PPMI to count-based PPMI+factorization (e.g., how long does it take to build a model with 100K words using each method)?

2. The paper claims "PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to many neural embedding techniques." Could you provide direct comparisons to recent neural methods (e.g., FastText, GloVe) on the same dataset?

3. The paper states that "for unweighted PoP models, increasing m over 2000 decreases the performance" - this is counterintuitive. Could you explain this phenomenon and provide evidence that it's not simply a result of hyperparameter settings?

4. How does PoP compare to the recent "random projection" approach in Gao et al. (2022) or other works that use sparse random projections for word embeddings?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound but incrementally novel method with practical computational advantages. While the positive-only projection approach is a clever solution to a real limitation in random projection methods, the lack of theoretical justification, inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and absence of concrete runtime measurements significantly weaken the contribution. The paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing these issues, particularly by providing more thorough comparisons to current methods and quantifying the claimed efficiency gains.

The paper has value for practitioners seeking efficient semantic space construction methods, but its theoretical contribution is limited. For CONLL, which emphasizes both theoretical and practical contributions, the current version falls short of the expected standard. With significant revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
