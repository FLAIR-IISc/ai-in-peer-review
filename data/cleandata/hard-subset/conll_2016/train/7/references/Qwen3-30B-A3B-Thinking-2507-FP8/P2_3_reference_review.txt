

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a novel method called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces. The core contribution is a random projection technique that uses only positive integer values in the projection matrix, enabling subsequent application of PPMI weighting (which is not possible with existing random projection methods like RI, RRI, ISA, or RMI due to their zero-mean random matrices). The authors claim that PoP allows for efficient construction of semantic spaces that can be weighted with PPMI, achieving competitive performance with neural embeddings but with significantly lower computational cost. The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves a correlation of 0.75 on the MEN relatedness test, comparable to neural embedding techniques but built in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days.

## Strengths

1. **Genuine Technical Gap Identification**: The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.) that cannot be weighted with PPMI due to their zero-mean random matrices. This is a well-motivated technical problem that has not been adequately addressed in the literature. The authors clearly explain why current methods fail to support PPMI (as sums of vector elements are zero, making PPMI undefined), which is a valuable contribution to the field.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper demonstrates a compelling advantage in computational efficiency. PoP enables semantic space construction in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days required for neural embeddings. The authors provide evidence that PoP-constructed models (with dimensionality of a few hundred) allow for rapid application of PPMI weighting, which would be computationally prohibitive on high-dimensional count-based models. This addresses a practical bottleneck in semantic space construction.

3. **Thorough Parameter Analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of PoP's parameters (dimensionality, sparsity, etc.) and their impact on performance. Figures 4-6 offer valuable insights into how different parameter settings affect model performance, particularly the relationship between dimensionality, non-zero elements, and performance variance. This level of empirical analysis is particularly valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of State-of-the-Art Comparison**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI achieves "competitive results" comparable to neural embeddings but fails to provide a direct comparison with current state-of-the-art methods. The MEN test has seen significant improvements in recent years (e.g., BERT-based models achieve ~0.85 correlation), and the reported 0.75-0.78 performance is not particularly impressive compared to the current SOTA. Without a proper comparison, it's difficult to assess the true significance of the claimed results.

2. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The authors acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they lack a detailed mathematical account for PoP's error bounds (δ) and theoretical properties, which is a significant weakness for a computational linguistics paper. While they reference prior work (e.g., Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma), they don't provide any theoretical analysis specific to their positive-only projection approach, making it difficult to understand why PoP works as well as it does.

3. **Unconvincing Claim of "Competitive Performance"**: The paper states that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "an average of 0.78 with the max of 0.787" on MEN, but doesn't contextualize this against the current SOTA. The 0.78 score is significantly below the current state-of-the-art (e.g., 0.85+ for transformer-based models), which weakens their claim of "competitive results." The paper should have included a direct comparison to recent methods.

4. **Insufficient Detail on Implementation**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on preprocessing steps (e.g., whether stopwords were removed, whether context windows were constructed with standard techniques), which is critical for reproducibility. The mention of "lemmatized-context-windows" and "symmetrically around lemmas" is too vague to reproduce the experiments.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can the authors provide a direct comparison of their PoP+PPMI+Pearson method with recent state-of-the-art neural models (e.g., those achieving ~0.85 on MEN) in a table, rather than just stating "comparable performance"?

2. The paper claims that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "an average of 0.78 with the max of 0.787." Can the authors provide a reference for this specific score and clarify whether this is the best possible score they achieved or whether it's a typical result? This score appears low compared to current SOTA.

3. The paper states that "PPMI transformation is much faster" for PoP-constructed models than for count-based models. Can the authors provide specific runtime measurements comparing the time required to apply PPMI to PoP-constructed models versus count-based models for the same corpus size?

## Overall Assessment

This paper identifies a genuine technical gap in the field and proposes a method (PoP) that addresses it. The computational efficiency claim is compelling, and the parameter analysis is thorough. However, the claimed performance is not competitive with current state-of-the-art methods, and the lack of a proper comparison undermines the significance of the results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a direct comparison with recent SOTA methods and more detailed theoretical justification for why PoP works as well as it does.

The contribution has potential value for practitioners needing efficient semantic space construction, but the paper's current presentation does not sufficiently demonstrate its novelty or significance relative to the current state of the art. With additional comparisons to SOTA methods and more theoretical grounding, this could be a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's main contribution is the identification of a problem (inability to apply PPMI to random projection methods) and a solution (PoP), but the empirical results do not fully demonstrate that the solution is competitive with existing approaches.
