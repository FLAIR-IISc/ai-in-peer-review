- Novelty: Introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP) to enable PPMI weighting on random-projection semantic spaces, addressing a key limitation of RI/RRI.
- Empirical claim: PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ~0.75 Spearman on the MEN relatedness test, reported as competitive with neural methods.
- Parameter study: Provides comprehensive analysis of dimensionality, sparsity, and weighting effects on PoP performance.
- Strength — conceptual insight: Correctly identifies zero-mean projection as the reason standard random projections cannot use PPMI.
- Strength — efficiency: Demonstrates PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural embeddings.
- Strength — empirical rigor: Strong ablation studies and visualizations (Figures 5–6) showing sparsity/performance trade-offs.
- Weakness — theory: Lacks theoretical justification or mathematical account for key PoP parameters (e.g., δ), making it appear heuristic.
- Weakness — comparisons: Fails to compare against recent strong baselines (fastText, GloVe, contextual embeddings), undermining SOTA claims.
- Weakness — overstated results: Performance claims are misleading (marginal RI gains; PoP best reported 0.75 vs typical neural >0.78); numerical inconsistencies noted (0.78/0.787 vs Figure 2’s 0.75).
- Question — PPMI on counts: Clarify why standard positive-sum count-based models cannot use PPMI in the same way PoP can.
- Question — comparison to fastText: Provide direct comparisons to fastText and other modern embeddings to substantiate "competitive" claims.
- Question — result discrepancies/statistics: Resolve reported score inconsistencies (0.78/0.787 vs 0.75) and provide statistical significance tests versus RI and count models.
- Question — PPMI cost: Explain and quantify how PoP reduces the computational cost of applying PPMI on large matrices given PPMI’s own expense.
- Overall recommendation: Interesting and potentially impactful technical observation, but needs stronger theoretical grounding and more comprehensive, up-to-date empirical comparisons to be a substantial contribution.
