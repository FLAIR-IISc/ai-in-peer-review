

# Review of "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces that enables the application of PPMI weighting to random projection-based models. The core contribution is the insight that using random projection matrices with only positive values (instead of symmetric distributions) allows for PPMI transformation, which was previously impossible with methods like RI (Random Indexing). The authors demonstrate that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks (0.75 on MEN test) with dramatically reduced computational requirements compared to neural embeddings and count-based methods with SVD. The paper's main contributions in order of strength are:

1. The novel PoP method that enables PPMI weighting on random projection-based semantic spaces through positive-only random projections
2. The empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance with significantly reduced computational costs
3. The thorough empirical validation of PoP across various parameters and context window sizes

## Strengths

1. **Genuinely Novel Core Insight**: The paper identifies a critical limitation in existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) that use symmetric random matrices (E(R)=0), making PPMI weighting impossible. The simple but elegant solution of using positive-only random projections is genuinely novel and solves a real problem in the field. This is exactly the kind of bold, simple idea I prioritize as a reviewer who values novelty over methodological perfection.

2. **Compelling Efficiency Claims**: The paper demonstrates that PoP can build semantic spaces in seconds/minutes instead of hours/days, which is a major practical contribution. The empirical evidence supporting this claim is strong, with the authors showing consistent performance improvements with smaller context windows and lower computational overhead. This efficiency could make semantic space construction accessible to many more researchers and practitioners.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The authors provide thorough empirical evaluation across multiple parameter settings, showing consistent improvements over RI and count-based models. The comparison with RI is particularly insightful, as it demonstrates the limitations of existing random projection methods. The paper also shows that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves a correlation of 0.75 on MEN, which is competitive with neural embeddings while being orders of magnitude faster.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the paper acknowledges the lack of theoretical justification for PoP (similar to RI's initial proposal), this remains a notable weakness. The paper doesn't provide any theoretical intuition for why positive-only projections work well, nor does it establish error bounds (δ) for the method. This is particularly important given that the authors claim PoP outperforms RI, which has known theoretical properties.

2. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares PoP to RI and count-based models but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with the latest neural embedding techniques (e.g., ELMo, BERT). The claim that PoP+PPMI "achieves competitive performance" is somewhat vague without direct comparison to the best-performing methods on the MEN test.

3. **Inconsistent Similarity Measure Usage**: The paper uses Kendall's τb for PoP and Pearson's correlation for PoP+PPMI, but doesn't clearly justify why Pearson is better after PPMI transformation. The paper mentions that "PPMI transformation is much faster" but doesn't provide specific timing comparisons to make the efficiency claim more concrete.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that PoP enables PPMI weighting, but doesn't specify which other weighting techniques (beyond PPMI) could be applied to PoP-constructed models. Could you elaborate on this?

2. The paper mentions achieving 0.787 on MEN with fine-tuning, but doesn't provide a direct comparison to the best-reported scores for neural embeddings on MEN. Could you add a comparison table showing PoP+PPMI's performance relative to state-of-the-art methods?

3. The paper claims models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" compared to "hours, days or months" for neural embeddings. Could you provide specific timing comparisons (e.g., exact times on standardized hardware) to substantiate this claim?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to semantic space construction with significant practical implications. The core insight of positive-only random projections is simple yet powerful, and the empirical results strongly support the claims of efficiency and competitive performance. While the lack of theoretical justification is a weakness, it's acceptable for a paper with such strong novelty, especially since the authors acknowledge this limitation and state they'll address it in future work.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper compelling. The PoP method opens up new possibilities for semantic space construction that were previously impossible, and the efficiency gains could have a significant impact on the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the camera-ready version.
