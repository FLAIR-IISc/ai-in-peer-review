

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a new method for constructing semantic spaces that purportedly enables PPMI weighting (which is not possible with other random projection methods like RI). The authors claim PoP-constructed models can be built quickly (seconds/minutes) and achieve competitive performance (ρ≈0.75 on MEN test) without the computational burden of neural embeddings. The key innovation is that PoP uses random projection matrices with only positive elements, allowing subsequent PPMI weighting.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new random projection method (PoP) that uses positive-only elements, enabling PPMI weighting
2. Empirical demonstration that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves ρ≈0.75 on MEN test

## Strengths
- The paper provides a clear technical description of the PoP method and its implementation
- The authors demonstrate that PoP+PPMI+Pearson outperforms count-based models and RI on MEN
- The paper acknowledges limitations of the method and provides a thoughtful discussion of theoretical gaps

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper admits "we are not able to provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ" (error bounds) for PoP, unlike established methods like RI that later received theoretical justification (Li et al., 2006). The authors cite Kanerva et al. (2000) as precedent for proposing RI without justification, but this is not a valid defense for PoP. Without theoretical grounding, it's impossible to understand the method's behavior or limitations, making it difficult to trust the empirical results.

2. **Insufficient comparison with established baselines**: The paper only compares PoP with RI and count-based models, omitting comparisons with standard modern baselines like GloVe, fastText, or even SVD-based dimensionality reduction applied to PPMI-weighted models. The claim that "PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves competitive results" is unsubstantiated without direct comparison to state-of-the-art methods. For instance, the MEN test scores for current SOTA methods exceed 0.80, while PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves only 0.75 (Figure 2), which is not competitive with current standards.

3. **Unsubstantiated efficiency claims**: The paper claims models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes" but provides no actual timing measurements to support this. The paper doesn't compare build times with count-based models + SVD + PPMI, which would be the natural baseline for efficiency claims. Without timing data, the efficiency claim remains a vague assertion.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide evidence that PoP itself is necessary for the PPMI+Pearson approach. The key question is: Why use PoP instead of simply applying SVD to the count-based model before PPMI? The paper doesn't show that PoP provides any advantage over standard dimensionality reduction techniques when combined with PPMI. This undermines the core claim of the paper.

5. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to the MEN relatedness test, without testing on other standard semantic similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) or downstream tasks. This severely limits the generalizability of the claims.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson compare to standard approaches that first apply SVD to count-based models before PPMI (which is the standard practice in the field)? Without this comparison, it's impossible to claim PoP is advantageous.

2. Can you provide actual timing measurements for building PoP models compared to standard count-based models with SVD + PPMI? Without this data, the efficiency claim is unsubstantiated.

3. The paper claims PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves "competitive results" comparable to neural embeddings. What are the specific state-of-the-art scores on MEN that you're comparing against, and how do your results stack up against them?

4. The paper states "the PoP method makes it possible to benefit both from the high efficiency of randomized techniques as well as from the high accuracy of PPMI transformation." What specific benefits does PoP provide beyond standard SVD dimensionality reduction followed by PPMI?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting technical idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The lack of theoretical justification, inadequate comparison with established methods, unsubstantiated efficiency claims, and limited evaluation make it difficult to accept the paper as a significant contribution to the field. The authors have identified a technical detail (using positive-only projections) that enables PPMI weighting, but this alone is not sufficient to justify a new method without evidence of superiority over existing approaches. The paper would need substantial additional work to address these weaknesses before it could be considered for publication.
