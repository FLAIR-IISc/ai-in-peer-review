

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method called "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces. The core idea is to use random projection matrices with strictly positive integer values (unlike previous random projection methods that use zero-mean distributions), enabling subsequent application of weighting techniques like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting vectors. The authors claim that this approach combines the computational efficiency of random projection methods with the accuracy of PPMI-weighted count-based models, achieving competitive performance on semantic similarity tasks without the training time required by neural embeddings.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **The PoP method itself**, which enables PPMI weighting on random projection-based models by using strictly positive random projections rather than zero-mean distributions. This addresses a key limitation of existing random projection methods (like RI, RRI, ISA, and RMI) that cannot be weighted with PPMI.

2. **Empirical demonstration** that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 on MEN relatedness test) compared to neural embeddings, while being orders of magnitude faster to construct (seconds/minutes vs. hours/days).

3. **Insight into the distributional properties** of the resulting vectors, explaining why PPMI transformation works with PoP but not with previous random projection methods, and why Pearson correlation outperforms Kendall's tau after PPMI transformation.

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a genuine limitation**: The paper correctly identifies a key limitation in existing random projection methods (incompatibility with PPMI due to zero-mean distributions) and provides a simple, effective solution. This is a meaningful contribution to the field of distributional semantics, as PPMI is widely used in state-of-the-art count-based models.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments comparing PoP against count-based models, RI, and other baselines across multiple dimensions (context window sizes, vector dimensions, number of non-zero elements). The results consistently show PoP+PPMI outperforming alternatives, with clear explanations for the observed patterns.

3. **Strong computational efficiency claim**: The paper provides compelling evidence for the method's efficiency (constructing models in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days), which is a significant practical advantage over neural embeddings. The analysis of vector distributions (Figure 3) helps explain why PoP enables efficient PPMI application.

4. **Clear explanation of distributional properties**: The paper provides an insightful analysis of how different methods transform vector distributions (from long-tailed to Gaussian-like), explaining why Pearson's correlation outperforms Kendall's tau after PPMI transformation. This adds theoretical depth to the empirical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty claim**: The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish PoP from existing methods like Random Indexing (RI). The authors claim RI cannot use PPMI due to "E(R) = 0," but RI typically uses positive random projections with E(R) > 0 (e.g., RI uses uniform positive random values). The paper doesn't clarify how PoP's positive-only projections differ from RI's standard implementation. This undermines the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper admits it cannot provide a mathematical account for the error bounds (δ) of PoP, which is a significant limitation for a method claiming to be a random projection technique. The authors cite Kanerva et al. (2000) as having proposed RI without mathematical justification, but this is not an acceptable justification for PoP's lack of theoretical foundation.

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper only evaluates on the MEN relatedness test. To demonstrate broader applicability, the authors should include additional standard benchmarks (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim353, or RG-65). The lack of comparison with state-of-the-art neural embedding methods (e.g., FastText, GloVe) on MEN makes the "competitive performance" claim less compelling.

4. **Inadequate comparison with PPMI-weighted count-based models**: The paper claims PoP+PPMI achieves "competitive results" compared to neural embeddings, but doesn't directly compare with the current standard for this task: PPMI-weighted count-based models (which are the baseline for many recent works). The paper should show that PoP+PPMI outperforms or matches these models while being faster to compute.

5. **Unclear computational efficiency claims**: While the paper states models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes," it doesn't provide actual timing measurements for comparison with neural embeddings or PPMI-weighted count-based models. Without concrete timing data, this claim lacks supporting evidence.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims RI has "E(R) = 0" but RI typically uses positive random projections (e.g., 0 or 1 with E(R) > 0). How does PoP specifically differ from RI in terms of the random projection distribution, and why does this enable PPMI application when RI cannot?

2. The paper states "in RI-constructed model, the sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0." This contradicts standard RI implementations (which use positive random projections). Please clarify this claim and provide evidence for it.

3. The paper claims PoP+PPMI achieves "competitive results" compared to neural embeddings, but doesn't compare with state-of-the-art neural methods on the MEN test (e.g., what's the best reported score for MEN?). Please provide this comparison to strengthen your claim.

4. How does PoP+PPMI compare to the standard PPMI-weighted count-based model (which is the current baseline for this task)? Does it outperform, match, or underperform this baseline, while being faster to compute?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting method with practical advantages for building semantic spaces efficiently. While the core idea is conceptually simple and the empirical results are promising, the lack of clear distinction from existing methods (particularly RI), insufficient theoretical justification, and limited evaluation scope significantly undermine the paper's contribution. With stronger novelty justification, additional evaluation benchmarks, and clearer comparisons with the current state of the art, this work could be a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's claims are overstated relative to the evidence provided.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject** (with the understanding that with major revisions addressing the weaknesses above, this paper could become a strong contribution).
