

# Peer Review: "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Positive-only Projection" (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with strictly positive integer values. The key innovation is that PoP enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) weighting to projected vectors, which previous random projection methods (like RI, RRI, and RMI) cannot support due to their zero-mean random matrices. The authors claim PoP+PPMI achieves competitive semantic similarity performance with neural embeddings but with dramatically reduced computational costs.

## Main Contributions
1. PoP method enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based models
2. Empirical demonstration of PoP's advantages over RI and count-based models
3. Analysis of weight distribution properties explaining PoP's effectiveness

## Strengths

1. **Identifies a genuine limitation in existing methods**: The paper correctly identifies that RI and similar methods cannot apply PPMI weighting due to their zero-mean random projections. This is a real, practical limitation that has gone unaddressed in the literature. The authors' solution—using positive-only random projections—is elegant and straightforward, resolving this specific limitation.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experimental results clearly demonstrate PoP's advantages over RI and count-based models across multiple context window sizes. The performance improvement (ρ > 0.50 vs. ρ < 0.50 for count-based models) is significant and well-documented. The comparison with PPMI-transformed models (Figure 2) effectively shows PoP+PPMI achieves performance comparable to more computationally expensive methods.

3. **Insightful distribution analysis**: The weight distribution analysis (Figure 3) provides valuable theoretical justification for why PoP works. The paper correctly identifies that PoP preserves the long-tailed distribution of count-based models, which can then be transformed into Gaussian-like distributions through PPMI, enabling the use of Pearson's correlation (which outperforms Kendall's τb for these transformed spaces).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: The core insight—using positive random projections to enable PPMI weighting—is relatively straightforward and not sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The paper fails to adequately explain why previous researchers didn't consider this approach. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a compelling rationale for why it was overlooked.

2. **Insufficient state-of-the-art comparison**: The paper claims "competitive results" with neural embeddings but provides minimal context about how these results compare to current state-of-the-art methods. The MEN test results (0.75) are good but not impressive by today's standards—recent BERT-based models achieve >0.80 on this benchmark. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed method.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper acknowledges it cannot provide a mathematical account for the error bound δ, relying solely on empirical results. For a method based on random projections, this is a significant weakness. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and related theoretical foundations provide crucial context for random projection methods, which the authors fail to engage with.

4. **Insufficient differentiation from RI**: The paper claims PoP is "similar to RI" but doesn't clearly explain why the sign of the random projections matters for PPMI application. The relationship between PoP and RI needs deeper theoretical and methodological clarification to establish PoP's distinct contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does PoP+PPMI compare to recent neural network-based methods (e.g., BERT, ELMo) on standard semantic similarity benchmarks? The MEN test results are promising but need context relative to current SOTA.

2. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the error bound δ for PoP, particularly regarding how it relates to the dimensionality m and sparsity parameter s?

3. Why didn't previous researchers consider using positive random projections for enabling PPMI weighting? Is there a technical limitation that the authors have overcome?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a simple but effective solution to a real limitation in existing random projection methods. The PoP approach is clever and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. However, the novelty is limited, the state-of-the-art comparisons are insufficient, and the theoretical foundation is weak. The empirical results are strong, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish why this method represents a significant advance beyond existing techniques.

The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive comparisons with current state-of-the-art methods, and a clearer articulation of how PoP fundamentally differs from existing approaches. Without these improvements, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

While the core idea is sound and the empirical results are convincing, the paper doesn't fully demonstrate why this method should replace existing approaches or how it advances the field beyond what could be achieved with simple modifications to existing techniques. The computational efficiency claims are compelling, but without more context about the performance gap with current methods, it's difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.
