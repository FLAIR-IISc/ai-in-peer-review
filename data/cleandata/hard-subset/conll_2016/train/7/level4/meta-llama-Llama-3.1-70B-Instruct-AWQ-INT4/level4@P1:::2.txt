The paper presents a positive-only projection (PoP) word embedding method, which is a random projection technique with a random projection matrix whose expected value is positive. The authors argue that this enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is not possible with an expected value of 0, and that being a random projection method, their computation is efficient.

The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:

1. The authors propose a novel technique called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings, which is based on random projections with a positive expected value.
2. The PoP method is highly scalable and computationally efficient, making it suitable for large-scale natural language processing tasks.
3. The authors demonstrate that PoP-constructed models can be weighted using PPMI, which is not possible with previous random projection-based models, and that this combination achieves competitive results in semantic similarity assessments.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a novel and efficient technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method using the MEN relatedness test set and the UKWaC corpus, demonstrating its effectiveness in semantic similarity assessments.
3. The paper highlights the advantages of using random projections with a positive expected value, which enables the application of PPMI and achieves competitive results.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper's clarity and presentation could be improved, particularly in explaining the core differences between the proposed method and previous random projection methods.
2. Some arguments in the paper are not well-supported, and the text could benefit from more explicit connections between the claims and the evidence.
3. The paper's evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MEN), and it would be beneficial to include additional standard benchmarks to demonstrate the method's generalizability.
4. The paper contains several English mistakes, which detract from its overall quality.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the core differences between the proposed PoP method and previous random projection methods, particularly in terms of their expected values and the implications for PPMI weighting?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of non-orthogonal index vectors in the PoP method, and what are the implications for the method's performance?
3. Could you provide additional evaluation results using other standard benchmarks, such as WordSim, SimLex, or others, to demonstrate the method's generalizability?
4. How do you plan to improve the presentation and clarity of the paper to make it more accessible to readers with some experience in the field?
