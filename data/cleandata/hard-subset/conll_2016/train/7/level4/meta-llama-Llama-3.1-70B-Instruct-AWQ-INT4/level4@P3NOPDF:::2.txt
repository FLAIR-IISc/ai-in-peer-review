The paper presents a positive-only projection (PoP) word embedding method, which is a random projection approach with a random projection matrix whose expected value is positive. The authors argue that this enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is not possible with an expected value of 0. Additionally, they claim that their computation is efficient due to the random projection nature of their method.

However, I have several major reservations about this paper, primarily related to its clarity and presentation. Firstly, I struggled to understand the core difference between the proposed method and previous random projection methods. As a result, I found it challenging to grasp how (and whether) the advantages argued by the authors hold.

Furthermore, I found the paper's arguments, starting from the introduction, difficult to follow. The text could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive connections between ideas. Some arguments are not adequately supported, such as those in lines 114, 137, and 154, which start with transitional phrases ("in addition," "Since," and "thus") that do not provide sufficient justification for the claims made.

While I have experience with vector space modeling, I am not an expert in random projections, and I have not used them in my research. Unfortunately, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of the logic behind this research avenue, making it hard for non-experts to follow. I believe that a paper should be self-contained and accessible to readers with some experience in the field.

Additionally, the paper contains several English errors (e.g., "To a large extend" in line 86 and "such PPMI" in line 142), which detract from its overall clarity.

Another concern is the evaluation of the proposed method, which is only performed on the MEN dataset. While MEN is a standard benchmark, it is insufficient to evaluate a new method on only one dataset without providing a good justification. There are other widely-used benchmarks, such as WordSim, SimLex, and others, that could be used to demonstrate the method's effectiveness more comprehensively.

In light of these concerns, I recommend that the authors substantially improve the presentation in the paper, address the issues mentioned above, and resubmit to another conference.
