**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel technique called Positive-Only Projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. PoP is based on random projections and is highly scalable and computationally efficient. Unlike previous methods that use random projection matrices with an expected value of 0, PoP uses matrices with an expected value greater than 0, allowing for the application of weighting methods such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors evaluate their method using the MEN relatedness test set and the UKWaC corpus, achieving competitive results compared to state-of-the-art algorithms.

**Main Contributions**

1. The introduction of the PoP technique, which enables the construction of semantic spaces that can be transformed using weighting methods such as PPMI.
2. The demonstration of the effectiveness of PoP in semantic similarity assessment tasks, achieving competitive results compared to state-of-the-art algorithms.
3. The provision of a fast and efficient method for constructing semantic spaces, which can be beneficial for large-scale natural language processing tasks.

**Strengths**

1. The PoP technique is highly scalable and computationally efficient, making it suitable for large-scale natural language processing tasks.
2. The method allows for the application of weighting methods such as PPMI, which can enhance the performance of semantic similarity assessment tasks.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method using a standard dataset and corpus, demonstrating its effectiveness in semantic similarity assessment tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks a direct comparison to related work, making it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of the proposed method.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why it is important for vectors to be transformable with PPMI, which may limit the understanding of the method's significance.
3. The use of Kendall's tau as a similarity measure is not well-justified, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of why they chose this measure over other standard measures such as Spearman correlation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear explanation of why it is important for vectors to be transformable with PPMI, and how this relates to the goals of semantic similarity assessment tasks?
2. How does the PoP technique compare to other related work in the field, and what are the key differences and advantages of the proposed method?
3. Can you provide additional justification for the use of Kendall's tau as a similarity measure, and how it relates to the goals of semantic similarity assessment tasks?
