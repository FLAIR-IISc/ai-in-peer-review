The paper presents a novel technique called positive-only projection (PoP) for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. This method is based on random projections and is highly scalable and computationally efficient. The authors claim that PoP-constructed models, combined with positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI), achieve competitive results in semantic similarity assessments, comparable to state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The introduction of the PoP method, which enables the application of PPMI to random projection-based models, improving their performance in semantic similarity assessments.
2. The demonstration of the efficiency and effectiveness of the PoP method in constructing semantic spaces, which can be used for various natural language processing tasks.
3. The evaluation of the PoP method on the MEN relatedness test set, showing its competitiveness with state-of-the-art algorithms.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a novel and efficient method for constructing semantic spaces, which can be useful for various natural language processing tasks.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the PoP method, demonstrating its competitiveness with state-of-the-art algorithms.
3. The paper highlights the importance of considering the distribution of data in semantic space construction, which is often overlooked in existing methods.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper lacks clarity, particularly in explaining the core difference between the proposed method and previous random projection methods.
2. The introduction and some arguments in the paper are not well-supported and lack clear explanations.
3. The paper contains several English mistakes, which detract from its overall quality.
4. The evaluation of the PoP method is limited to a single dataset (MEN), which may not be sufficient to demonstrate its generalizability.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a clearer explanation of the core difference between the proposed PoP method and previous random projection methods?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the PoP method, such as its sensitivity to noise and the need for careful selection of context elements?
3. Can you provide additional evaluations of the PoP method on other standard benchmarks, such as WordSim, SimLex, and others?
4. How do you plan to improve the presentation and clarity of the paper to make it more accessible to readers with some experience in the field?
