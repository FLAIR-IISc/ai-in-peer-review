The paper presents a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings, called positive-only projection (PoP). This method is based on random projections, making it highly scalable and computationally efficient. The authors argue that the key contribution of PoP lies in its ability to use a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, enabling the application of weighting methods such as positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI).

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1. The introduction of a new random projection method, PoP, which allows for the application of PPMI weighting.
2. The demonstration of the efficiency and effectiveness of PoP in constructing semantic spaces, achieving competitive results in semantic similarity assessments without the need for heavy computations.
3. The provision of a new perspective on the use of random projections in natural language processing, highlighting the potential benefits of non-Gaussian distributions in semantic space construction.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a clear and well-structured evaluation of the proposed method, using the MEN relatedness test set and the UKWaC corpus.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the performance of PoP, including its sensitivity to parameters and its comparison to other methods.
3. The use of Kendall's τb correlation coefficient as a similarity measure is a reasonable choice, given the non-Gaussian distribution of the projected spaces.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the core differences between PoP and previous random projection methods, as well as a more detailed discussion of the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method.
2. Some of the arguments presented in the paper are not well-supported, and the text could be improved by providing more explicit connections between the claims made and the evidence presented.
3. The evaluation of the method is limited to a single dataset, and it would be beneficial to include additional benchmarks to demonstrate the generalizability of the results.
4. The paper contains several English mistakes, which detract from the overall clarity and professionalism of the presentation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical differences between PoP and previous random projection methods, and how these differences impact the performance of the method?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current evaluation, and what additional benchmarks or datasets do you intend to use in future work?
3. Can you provide more information on the potential applications of PoP in natural language processing, and how it might be used in conjunction with other methods or techniques?
