**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings efficiently. The core innovation lies in using a random projection matrix where all elements are non-negative, resulting in an expected value greater than zero (E(R) > 0). This contrasts with traditional random projection techniques like Random Indexing (RI), which use matrices with an expected value of zero. The authors claim that this "positive-only" property is crucial because it allows for the application of weighting schemes, most notably Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), to the already dimensionality-reduced vectors. This two-stage process—efficient projection followed by fast weighting in the low-dimensional space—aims to combine the scalability of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-based models. The method is evaluated on the MEN semantic relatedness task, where it achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Computationally Efficient Method for PPMI-based Embeddings:** The primary contribution is a novel pipeline that enables the application of PPMI weighting on a low-dimensional matrix created via random projection. By first reducing dimensionality with the proposed PoP method, the authors circumvent the significant computational cost of calculating PPMI on a full, high-dimensional, and sparse co-occurrence matrix, making the overall process highly efficient.

2.  **A Novel Random Projection Scheme:** The paper introduces a new type of random projection matrix composed of only non-negative values. The authors argue that this specific design (E(R) > 0) is what makes the subsequent PPMI step mathematically feasible, addressing a key limitation of standard random projection methods where such weighting is not possible.

**Strengths**

1.  **Efficiency and Scalability:** The proposed method offers a compelling practical advantage. The standard approach of applying PPMI to a vocabulary-sized co-occurrence matrix is a well-known computational bottleneck. By performing this calculation in a low-dimensional space (e.g., a few thousand dimensions), the PoP method drastically reduces both time and memory requirements. This makes it possible to generate high-quality, PPMI-style embeddings much faster than traditional methods, which is a significant strength.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on the MEN dataset are strong. Achieving a Spearman's correlation of 0.75 is competitive with top-performing models at the time of submission, including more computationally intensive neural network-based approaches. This provides solid evidence that the method is not just efficient but also effective for capturing semantic relatedness.

3.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The paper identifies a limitation in existing random projection methods (inapplicability of PPMI) and proposes a principled solution. The insight to connect the expected value of the projection matrix to the feasibility of applying PPMI weighting is both clever and novel, offering a new perspective on how to integrate the benefits of different classes of word embedding techniques.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Self-Containment:** The paper's exposition is a significant weakness. The central argument—that E(R) = 0 in methods like RI precludes the use of PPMI while E(R) > 0 in PoP enables it—is not explained with sufficient clarity for a non-expert in random projections. The reader is expected to understand why the column and row sums of an RI-projected matrix would be zero without a clear derivation or intuitive explanation. This lack of self-containment makes it difficult to fully grasp the core mechanism and verify the authors' foundational claims. The argumentation is further weakened by unsupported logical connectors (e.g., "thus" in line 154, "Since" in line 137), which undermine the paper's logical flow.

2.  **Limited and Narrow Evaluation:** The empirical validation relies solely on a single dataset, the MEN relatedness test. While the results are strong, performance on word embedding tasks is known to be highly dependent on the evaluation benchmark. To make a convincing case for a general-purpose embedding method, evaluation on a wider suite of tasks is necessary. This should include benchmarks that measure semantic similarity more strictly (e.g., SimLex-999, WordSim-353) and perhaps analogy tasks, as these test different properties of the vector space. Without this, the generalizability of the method's effectiveness remains an open question.

3.  **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The authors acknowledge in Section 2.3 that they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account or theoretical justification for the PoP method. While they correctly note that empirical validation can sometimes precede full theoretical understanding (as with RI), the absence of any formal analysis of PoP's properties (e.g., error bounds, what geometric properties are preserved) is a notable weakness. The paper relies entirely on empirical performance, which, given the limited evaluation, makes the contribution less robust than it could be.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a clearer, more self-contained explanation of why standard random projection methods with E(R)=0 render PPMI computation infeasible? Specifically, can you demonstrate why the column sums in the projected matrix (∑_i v_iy in Equation 3) become zero under such a projection? A small, concrete numerical example would be highly beneficial for reader comprehension.

2.  The evaluation is focused exclusively on the MEN relatedness task. Could you comment on why other standard benchmarks assessing semantic similarity (like SimLex-999) or analogical reasoning were not included? How do you anticipate your method would perform on these different tasks, and would the optimal parameter settings (e.g., for `s` or `m`) change?

3.  In Section 3.3, you observe that for unweighted PoP models, increasing the dimensionality `m` beyond 2000 can decrease performance. This is counter-intuitive, as higher dimensionality is typically associated with lower projection error. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for why this occurs? Does this suggest a fundamental trade-off or instability in the PoP method that is not present in other random projections?
