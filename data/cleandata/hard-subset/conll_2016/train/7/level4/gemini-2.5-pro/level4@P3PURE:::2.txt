This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random projection technique for generating word embeddings. The method utilizes a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, which the authors argue enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI)—a step they claim is not possible with standard zero-mean random projections. Furthermore, they contend that the method retains the computational efficiency characteristic of random projection approaches.

While the proposed direction may be promising, my main reservation concerns the paper's clarity, which significantly hinders a proper assessment of its contributions.

My specific concerns are as follows:

1.  **Lack of Clarity on Novelty:** The paper fails to clearly articulate the core technical difference between the proposed PoP method and previous random projection techniques. Consequently, it is difficult to understand how the claimed advantages are achieved or to verify if they genuinely hold. The connection between a positive-expected-value projection matrix and the ability to apply PPMI is not sufficiently explained.

2.  **Inaccessible Presentation:** The paper's arguments are difficult to follow, beginning with the introduction. The work assumes a high level of specialized knowledge in random projections, making it inaccessible to many researchers in the broader field of vector space modeling. A manuscript should be reasonably self-contained and its core logic should be understandable by readers with general expertise in the area.

3.  **Unsupported Arguments:** Several claims in the paper are not adequately supported by the preceding text. For example, sentences beginning with logical connectives like "in addition" (line 114), "Since" (line 137), and "thus" (line 154) appear without the necessary justification to support the stated conclusions.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to the MEN dataset. When proposing a new word embedding method, it is standard practice to evaluate on a suite of common benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.) to demonstrate robustness and generalizability. Presenting results on a single dataset without a strong justification is insufficient to validate the method's effectiveness.

5.  **Grammar and Language:** The paper contains numerous English mistakes and awkward phrasings (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86; "such PPMI" on line 142) that detract from the paper's readability and professionalism.

Due to these significant issues with presentation and evaluation, I must recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to substantially revise the paper to improve its clarity, motivation, and experimental rigor for resubmission to another conference.
