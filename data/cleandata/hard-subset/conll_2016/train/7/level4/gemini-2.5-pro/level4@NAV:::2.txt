This paper introduces a word embedding method named Positive-only Projection (PoP), which is based on a random projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors claim that this core property uniquely enables the use of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a technique not applicable to projections with a zero-mean expectation, while also preserving the computational efficiency of random projection methods.

However, the primary weakness of this manuscript is its lack of clarity, which significantly hinders the assessment of its contributions. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  The manuscript fails to clearly articulate the fundamental distinction between the proposed PoP method and existing random projection techniques. Consequently, it is difficult for the reader to ascertain the novelty of the contribution and to verify whether the claimed advantages—namely, the compatibility with PPMI and computational efficiency—are valid consequences of this distinction.

2.  The logical flow of the paper is difficult to follow, beginning with the introduction. The argumentation throughout the manuscript is not presented in a clear, cohesive manner, making it challenging to grasp the authors' reasoning.

3.  Several key arguments appear to be unsubstantiated. The paper makes logical leaps that are not adequately justified by the preceding text. For instance:
    *   Line 114: The use of "in addition" suggests a supplementary point, but its connection to the previous statement is unclear.
    *   Line 137: The argument beginning with "Since" presents a premise that is not established earlier in the text.
    *   Line 154: The conclusion introduced by "thus" does not logically follow from the information provided before it.

4.  The paper lacks the necessary background and motivation to be accessible to a broader audience within the field. While readers may have experience with vector space models, the manuscript assumes a deep, specialized knowledge of random projections without providing sufficient context. A strong scientific paper should be largely self-contained, enabling researchers in related areas to understand its core contributions and rationale.

5.  The manuscript also suffers from numerous grammatical and stylistic errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142), which detract from its readability and professionalism.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is insufficient. The authors evaluate their method solely on the MEN dataset. Standard practice in this area involves benchmarking on a suite of established datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.) to provide a comprehensive assessment of a new method's performance. The paper offers no justification for this limited evaluation.

In its current state, the paper is not ready for publication. I recommend rejection. The authors are encouraged to undertake a substantial revision to significantly improve the clarity, argumentation, and experimental rigor of the manuscript before resubmitting to a future venue.
