**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projections (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings based on random projections. The key innovation lies in using a random projection matrix R where the expected value E(R) is greater than zero. This contrasts with previous random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI), where E(R) = 0. According to the authors, this property of PoP allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be subsequently transformed using weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a step which is not possible with RI-based vectors. The paper argues that this combination offers the computational efficiency and scalability of random projections while achieving the high performance of PPMI-weighted models on semantic tasks. The method's effectiveness is evaluated on the MEN relatedness dataset, where it is shown to achieve results comparable to state-of-the-art models.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Random Projection Method for PPMI Weighting:** The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, which creates low-dimensional vectors that, unlike those from traditional random indexing, retain a distribution amenable to post-projection weighting. The ability to apply PPMI to these dense vectors is the central technical advance, aiming to bridge the gap between efficient projection methods and high-accuracy weighting schemes.
2.  **Efficient and Scalable Model Construction:** The proposed method is incremental and computationally efficient, avoiding the significant training time required for neural network-based embeddings. By operating directly in a low-dimensional space, it makes the application of transformations like PPMI far more tractable than on high-dimensional, sparse count-based matrices.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The most compelling argument for this work is its efficiency. By sidestepping the computationally expensive training procedures of neural models or the eigenvector computations of SVD, the PoP method offers a highly scalable and fast approach to creating word embeddings. This is a significant practical advantage, especially for very large corpora or resource-constrained environments.
2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The paper presents a clever synthesis of two well-established paradigms: the efficiency of random projections for dimensionality reduction and the discriminative power of PPMI for weighting. The insight that a positive-only projection matrix (E(R) > 0) is the key to enabling this combination is novel and technically sound.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results (within its own evaluation):** The results presented, particularly for the PoP+PPMI+Pearson configuration, are strong on the MEN dataset (ρ ≈ 0.75). This demonstrates that the proposed pipeline can indeed produce high-quality semantic representations, validating the core hypothesis that combining PoP with PPMI is an effective strategy.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison with State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper's most significant weakness is the absence of a direct empirical comparison to established and top-performing methods. While the authors claim their results are "comparable" to state-of-the-art algorithms, no results from these other models (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, or SVD on a PPMI matrix) are presented on the same corpus and evaluation setup. To substantiate its claims, the paper must include these critical baselines in its result tables.
2.  **Insufficient Motivation for the Importance of PPMI Compatibility:** The central motivation—that enabling PPMI transformation on projected vectors is a crucial goal—is not sufficiently justified in the context of the current landscape of word embeddings. State-of-the-art predictive models like word2vec and GloVe achieve excellent performance without relying on a post-hoc PPMI step. The paper needs to better articulate why enabling this specific capability is a more valuable contribution than simply using existing, high-performing methods.
3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single semantic relatedness task (MEN). A more robust evaluation would include a diverse set of standard benchmarks, such as semantic similarity (WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and analogy tasks (e.g., the Google analogy dataset). Relying on one dataset makes it difficult to assess the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The definition of Kendall's τb provided on page 2 (Section 2.2) does not appear to be the standard formulation. Could you please clarify the source of this formula or correct it if there is a typo?
2.  What is the justification for choosing Kendall's τb as the primary similarity measure for the unweighted PoP vectors, especially when Spearman's ρ is used for the final evaluation? Given that both are non-parametric rank correlation measures, using Spearman's ρ for internal similarity would seem more consistent. Have you experimented with this?
3.  In Figure 1, the caption describes three models being plotted (count-based, RI, and PoP). However, the graph appears to contain only two distinct lines. Could you please clarify which line corresponds to which model?
