### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection method for creating low-dimensional word embeddings. The key innovation lies in using a projection matrix where all elements are non-negative (and the expected value is positive), in contrast to standard random indexing (RI) techniques that use matrices with an expected value of zero. The authors claim that this property allows for the application of weighting schemes, such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), directly to the low-dimensional vectors *after* the projection has been performed. This is not possible with traditional RI, where the resulting vectors have a zero mean. The method is presented as a computationally efficient, incremental alternative to neural network-based embeddings and SVD-based methods, while achieving comparable performance on a semantic relatedness task.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Random Projection Method (PoP):** The core contribution is the PoP technique itself. The insight to use a positive-only random matrix to create low-dimensional vectors that retain properties amenable to subsequent weighting (like PPMI) is novel and interesting. This elegantly addresses a known limitation of standard random indexing methods.
2.  **Efficient Combination with PPMI:** The paper demonstrates an effective and highly efficient pipeline for creating high-quality embeddings. By applying the computationally expensive PPMI weighting *after* dimensionality reduction, the authors present a practical method that combines the speed of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI, achieving strong results without the extensive training time required by neural models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Computational Efficiency and Scalability:** The strongest argument for this work is its efficiency. The proposed method is incremental and significantly faster than both SVD-based approaches (which require the full co-occurrence matrix) and neural network training. The authors' claim that models can be built in minutes rather than hours or days is a significant practical advantage, making the method highly attractive for large-scale applications or resource-constrained environments.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The central idea of using a positive-only projection is simple yet clever. It provides a clear technical solution to the problem of applying traditional count-based weighting schemes in a reduced-dimension space. The paper effectively explains why this is not possible with standard RI and how PoP overcomes this limitation.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on the Chosen Task:** The combination of PoP with PPMI and a Pearson correlation metric achieves a very competitive score (average ρ of 0.75, max of 0.787) on the MEN semantic relatedness benchmark. This demonstrates that the proposed lightweight method can indeed produce high-quality embeddings that are competitive with more complex, state-of-the-art systems for this type of task.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to Key Baselines:** The most significant weakness is the absence of direct, controlled comparisons with contemporary state-of-the-art methods. While the paper claims performance is "comparable," it only cites scores from other papers, which may use different corpora, preprocessing, or hyperparameters. A convincing evaluation would require running strong baselines on the exact same data. Specifically, two comparisons are essential:
    *   **PPMI+SVD:** The standard and highly competitive baseline for count-based models is to apply PPMI to the full co-occurrence matrix and then use SVD for dimensionality reduction. A direct comparison between PoP+PPMI and PPMI+SVD is crucial to validate whether the proposed method offers a performance advantage, or simply a speed-performance trade-off.
    *   **Neural Embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe):** The paper motivates itself as an alternative to slow neural methods. Therefore, a comparison with models like Skip-gram or GloVe, trained on the same UKWaC corpus, is necessary to substantiate claims of comparable performance.

2.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single task (semantic relatedness) on one primary dataset (MEN). The general utility and robustness of the resulting embeddings are therefore unclear. To demonstrate broader applicability, the evaluation should include other standard intrinsic tasks, such as word analogy (e.g., Google analogy dataset) and semantic similarity (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). Different tasks measure different properties of word vectors, and strong performance on a wider suite of benchmarks is needed to make a compelling case for a new embedding method.

3.  **Unclear Justification for Similarity Measure:** The paper proposes using Kendall’s τb for similarity in the base PoP space, arguing that it is suitable for non-Gaussian distributions. However, this choice feels somewhat ad-hoc. The motivation for not using Spearman's rank correlation, which is the final evaluation metric for the task, is not provided. Furthermore, the use of Pearson's correlation after PPMI transformation is justified by the claim that the distribution becomes "Gaussian-like," but this transformation is primarily intended to adjust co-occurrence weights, not necessarily to normalize the entire distribution of vector components. This chain of reasoning could be strengthened with more rigorous justification or analysis.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The central motivation for PoP is the ability to apply PPMI *after* dimensionality reduction. How does the performance and efficiency of PoP+PPMI compare directly to the widely-used baseline of applying PPMI to the full count matrix *before* dimensionality reduction with SVD? A head-to-head comparison on the same data would be very insightful for understanding the trade-offs of your approach.
2.  Could you clarify the choice of Kendall's τb as the internal similarity metric for PoP vectors? Given that Spearman's ρ is used for the final evaluation, what is the advantage of Kendall's τb over using Spearman's ρ consistently, or over other measures like cosine similarity?
3.  The evaluation is currently focused on the MEN relatedness task. Have you performed any experiments on other standard benchmarks, such as word similarity (WordSim-353, SimLex-999) or analogy tasks, to assess the general quality of the PoP-generated embeddings?
