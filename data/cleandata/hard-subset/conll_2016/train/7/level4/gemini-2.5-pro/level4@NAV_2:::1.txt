### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. The method is designed to be highly scalable and computationally efficient. The key innovation is the use of a random projection matrix where the expected value of its elements is positive, in contrast to standard random projection methods like Random Indexing (RI), where the expected value is zero. This property allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to be subsequently transformed by weighting schemes such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The authors demonstrate that combining PoP with PPMI achieves results on the MEN relatedness task that are comparable to state-of-the-art methods, but with significantly lower computational cost.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of the Positive-Only Projection (PoP) method, an incremental and efficient technique for generating word embeddings. Its primary novelty lies in producing vectors that are amenable to post-projection weighting (e.g., PPMI), which is a limitation of existing random projection methods.
2.  An empirical evaluation showing that PoP, when combined with PPMI and a Pearson correlation similarity metric, yields strong performance on a semantic relatedness task. This demonstrates the method's ability to balance computational efficiency with high accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Computational Efficiency and Scalability.** The primary strength of the proposed method is its efficiency. By leveraging random projections, it avoids the time-consuming training required by neural network models. Furthermore, by enabling PPMI weighting to be applied to the low-dimensional vectors rather than the original high-dimensional count matrix, it offers a significant speed-up over traditional count-and-factorize models. This makes the approach highly practical for large-scale applications.
2.  **Novel Approach to Random Projections.** The core idea of using a projection matrix with a positive expected value (`E(R) > 0`) to preserve certain distributional properties is novel and effectively addresses a key limitation of methods like RI. Standard RI produces zero-centered vectors, rendering subsequent PPMI calculations impossible. PoP provides an elegant solution that combines the benefits of dimensionality reduction with the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The experimental results on the MEN dataset are compelling. The PoP+PPMI model achieves an average Spearman's ρ of 0.75, which is a strong result and demonstrates that the method is competitive with more complex, computationally intensive approaches. The paper provides a thorough analysis of how performance is affected by various parameters, such as dimensionality and sparsity, which strengthens the empirical claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to State-of-the-Art.** The paper's most significant weakness is the absence of a direct, quantitative comparison to related work. The authors claim their performance is "comparable" to top-performing algorithms but do not provide a comparison table with published scores from other models (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, SVD on a PPMI matrix) on the same dataset (MEN) and corpus (UKWaC). Without this context, it is difficult to properly assess the method's standing and the trade-off between its performance and efficiency.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation and Motivation.** The evaluation is confined to a single semantic relatedness task (MEN). While this is a suitable test set, claims of state-of-the-art performance would be more convincing if validated across a broader suite of standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.). Additionally, the motivation for why applying PPMI *after* projection is a crucial capability could be strengthened with a more detailed discussion of the limitations of alternative pipelines.
3.  **Clarity on Methodological Choices and Presentation.** Certain methodological choices lack clear justification. For example, the paper uses Kendall's τb as the primary similarity measure for unweighted PoP vectors but does not explain why this was chosen over the more common Spearman's ρ. Furthermore, there are minor but potentially confusing presentation issues. For example, Figure 1 is described as showing three curves (count-based, RI, and PoP), but only two are clearly visible, which makes it difficult to fully verify the experimental comparison presented.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper's central claim is that the proposed method is competitive with state-of-the-art models. Could you please provide a table that directly compares the performance of your best PoP+PPMI model on the MEN dataset with published results from other widely-used models (such as CBOW, Skip-gram, GloVe, and SVD-based methods) that were also trained on the UKWaC corpus or a corpus of comparable size?
2.  Your choice of Kendall's τb as a similarity measure for the raw PoP vectors is non-standard. Could you please justify this choice over other rank correlation metrics like Spearman's ρ, which you use for the final evaluation?
3.  On page 3, you provide a formula for Kendall's τb. Could you please clarify the source for this specific formulation to ensure it is interpreted correctly? While it appears to be the standard formula that accounts for ties, a direct citation would be helpful.
