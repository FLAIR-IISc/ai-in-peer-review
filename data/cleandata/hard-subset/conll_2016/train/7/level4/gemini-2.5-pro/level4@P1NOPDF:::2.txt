This paper introduces a word embedding method called Positive-only Projection (PoP). PoP is a random projection method that utilizes a projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors claim two main advantages for this approach: first, that the positive expected value enables the use of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which they state is not applicable to standard random projections with a zero expected value; and second, that the method retains the computational efficiency characteristic of random projection techniques.

While the idea is potentially interesting, my primary reservation is the paper's lack of clarity, which significantly hinders the reader's ability to understand and evaluate the proposed method.

Specifically, the paper has the following weaknesses in its presentation:

1.  **Unclear Contribution:** The paper fails to clearly articulate the core distinction between the proposed PoP method and existing random projection techniques. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the central claims of the paper, namely how the positive-only property uniquely enables the use of PPMI and whether the purported advantages are truly novel.

2.  **Difficult Exposition:** The exposition is difficult to follow throughout the manuscript, beginning with the introduction. The central motivation and the logical flow of the arguments are not presented in a clear and compelling manner, making it a struggle to follow the paper’s narrative.

3.  **Unsupported Arguments:** Several key arguments appear to be unsubstantiated. For instance, the claims introduced by phrases such as "in addition" (line 114), "Since" (line 137), and "thus" (line 154) are not adequately supported by the preceding text. These represent logical leaps that leave the reader unconvinced of the validity of the claims.

4.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper is not self-contained, assuming a level of specialized knowledge that may not be common even among researchers in vector space modeling. While the reviewer has a background in this area, the lack of sufficient explanation of the random projection methods being discussed made it challenging to grasp the core contribution. A successful paper should be accessible to a reasonably knowledgeable audience in the field without requiring deep expertise in the specific sub-topic.

5.  **Language and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142), which detract from its readability and professionalism.

Beyond the issues of clarity, the experimental evaluation is another significant concern. The authors evaluate their method on a single word similarity dataset (MEN). For a new word embedding method, evaluation on a single benchmark is insufficient. Standard practice requires comparison across a suite of established datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.) to provide a comprehensive and robust assessment of the model's performance. The paper offers no justification for this limited evaluation scope.

Due to these significant issues with clarity and experimental rigor, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors are encouraged to undertake a substantial revision to improve the presentation, clarify the core technical contributions, and broaden the experimental evaluation before resubmitting their work to a future venue.
