This paper introduces a word embedding method called Positive-only Projection (PoP). The technique is based on random projection, utilizing a projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors claim this property enables the application of PPMI, which they state is not possible with standard random projections where the expected value is zero. A second claimed advantage is the computational efficiency inherent to random projection methods.

My primary reservation about this paper concerns its lack of clarity, which manifests in several ways:

1.  The paper fails to clearly articulate the core technical difference between the proposed PoP method and existing random projection techniques. Consequently, it is difficult for the reader to understand or verify the claimed advantages, such as the unique ability to apply PPMI.

2.  The paper's line of argumentation is difficult to follow, beginning with the introduction and continuing throughout the manuscript.

3.  Several claims appear to be unsupported by the preceding text. For instance, the use of logical connectives such as "in addition" (Line 114), "Since" (Line 137), and "thus" (Line 154) seems unjustified, as the necessary logical links have not been established in the writing.

4.  The paper seems to assume a high degree of specialized knowledge in random projections. For a reader familiar with vector space models but not an expert in this specific sub-field, the fundamental motivation and logic of the proposed approach are difficult to grasp from the text alone. A strong paper should be largely self-contained and accessible to readers with a general background in the relevant area.

5.  The manuscript contains numerous grammatical and stylistic errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142), which detract from its overall readability.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The paper relies on a single dataset (MEN) to validate the proposed method. For a novel word embedding technique, evaluation on a single benchmark is insufficient. Standard practice requires comparison across a suite of established datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.) to provide a comprehensive assessment of the method's performance. The paper offers no justification for this limited evaluation.

Given these issues, I recommend that the authors substantially improve the paper's presentation and experimental methodology before resubmitting their work to another venue.
