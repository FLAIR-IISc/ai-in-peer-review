This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a word embedding method based on random projections. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors argue that this property enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which they claim is not possible with standard random projections that have a zero expected value. They also posit that their method retains the computational efficiency of other random projection techniques.

While the direction of combining the efficiency of random projections with the effectiveness of PPMI-based methods is potentially interesting, my primary reservation about this paper is its profound lack of clarity. This issue pervades the entire manuscript, making it difficult to assess the validity and significance of the proposed contribution.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1.  **Unclear Contribution and Motivation:** The paper fails to clearly articulate the fundamental difference between the proposed PoP method and existing random projection techniques. The mechanism by which a positive expected value in the projection matrix enables the claimed benefits is not sufficiently explained. Consequently, it is difficult to understand how—and indeed, whether—the proposed method successfully integrates PPMI in a meaningful way that was previously not possible. The manuscript lacks the necessary background and intuition to be self-contained. For a reader experienced in vector space models but not a specialist in random projections, the motivation behind this line of research and the logic of the approach remain opaque.

2.  **Weak and Unsubstantiated Argumentation:** The arguments throughout the paper are difficult to follow, beginning with the introduction. The logical flow is often disconnected, and key claims are presented without sufficient justification. For instance, several statements are presented as logical conclusions without adequate support from the preceding text. The claims introduced by phrases such as "in addition" (Line 114), "Since" (Line 137), and "thus" (Line 154) appear to be unsubstantiated assertions rather than well-reasoned deductions.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The authors evaluate their method solely on the MEN dataset. For a paper proposing a novel word embedding method, relying on a single benchmark is insufficient to demonstrate its general effectiveness. Standard practice in the field requires evaluation across a suite of established datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, etc.) that measure different facets of semantic representation. Without a broader evaluation or a compelling justification for the exclusive use of MEN, it is impossible to gauge the robustness of the PoP method or compare it meaningfully against existing approaches.

**Minor Weaknesses:**

The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that impede readability. Examples include "To a large extend" (Line 86) and "such PPMI" (Line 142). These errors detract from the paper's professionalism.

**Recommendation:**

In its current form, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance. The core issues of clarity in its contribution, argumentation, and evaluation are too significant. I strongly recommend that the authors perform a substantial revision to fundamentally improve the presentation of their work. A future submission should clearly explain the technical novelty, provide rigorous support for its claims, and present a comprehensive empirical evaluation. With these critical improvements, the paper might be suitable for resubmission to another venue.
