This paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel word embedding method based on random projections. The core idea is to use a random projection matrix with a non-negative expected value. The authors claim that this property, unlike standard zero-mean projections, enables the valid application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting vectors. They also argue that the method retains the computational efficiency inherent in random projection techniques.

While the central idea of designing a projection scheme compatible with PPMI is interesting, the paper in its current form suffers from significant issues in clarity, argumentation, and evaluation. These issues prevent a thorough assessment of the method's novelty and effectiveness.

**Major Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Motivation:** My primary concern is the overall lack of clarity.
    *   **Novelty is Unclear:** The paper fails to clearly articulate the fundamental difference between the proposed PoP method and existing random projection techniques. While the non-negative expectation of the projection matrix is stated as the key distinction, its theoretical and practical implications are not sufficiently explained. Consequently, it is difficult for the reader to understand *why* this property enables the valid application of PPMI and how it overcomes the limitations of standard methods.
    *   **Difficult to Follow:** The argumentation is hard to follow throughout the manuscript, starting from the introduction. The motivation for this specific line of research is not well-established, making it difficult to grasp the context and importance of the problem being solved.
    *   **Not Self-Contained:** The paper presumes a high degree of specialized knowledge in random projections, making it inaccessible to a broader audience within the vector space modeling community. A manuscript should be reasonably self-contained. Key concepts and the rationale behind the proposed approach need to be explained more thoroughly so that readers with general expertise in the field can follow the paper's logic.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is not comprehensive enough to support the paper's claims.
    *   The authors evaluate their method solely on the MEN dataset for word similarity. The field has established a suite of standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, SimVerb-3500) that test different aspects of semantic representation. Evaluating a new word embedding method on a single dataset provides a very limited view of its performance and generalizability. The paper offers no justification for this narrow choice of evaluation. A convincing study requires comparison against relevant baselines and state-of-the-art models across several of these standard datasets.

3.  **Unsupported Arguments and Logical Gaps:** Several arguments in the paper appear to be unsubstantiated or based on flawed reasoning. The logical connections implied by transition words are often not supported by the preceding text. For example:
    *   The use of "in addition" (Line 114) suggests a new, separate point, but the connection to the previous statement is unclear.
    *   The claim following "Since" (Line 137) does not logically follow from a clear premise.
    *   The conclusion introduced by "thus" (Line 154) is not a direct consequence of the preceding argument.
    These instances point to underlying gaps in the logical structure of the paper that need to be addressed.

4.  **Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript is marred by numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86; "such PPMI" on line 142). These errors detract from the paper's readability and professionalism and should be corrected by a thorough round of proofreading.

**Recommendation**

Due to the significant issues with clarity, argumentation, and experimental evaluation, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance at CONLL in its current form.

The core idea may have merit, but the paper requires a substantial revision to make its contributions comprehensible and its claims verifiable. I recommend that the authors focus on fundamentally improving the presentation, clarifying the novelty and mechanics of their method, providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for their claims, and conducting a comprehensive evaluation against standard benchmarks. After such a major revision, this work could be a stronger candidate for submission to a future conference.
