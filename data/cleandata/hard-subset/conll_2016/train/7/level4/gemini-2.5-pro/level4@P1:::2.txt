### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing word embeddings based on random projections. The defining feature of PoP is its use of a random projection matrix with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), in contrast to traditional methods where the expected value is zero.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  The proposal of the Positive-Only Projection (PoP) method itself, which is an incremental and computationally efficient technique for creating low-dimensional word vectors.
2.  The central claim that because PoP-constructed vectors have non-zero sums, they can be subsequently transformed using weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). This is presented as a key advantage over other random projection methods, aiming to combine their efficiency with the high performance of PPMI-weighted models.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the important and practical challenge of creating high-quality semantic representations efficiently. The goal of combining the speed of random projection methods with the discriminative power of PPMI weighting is well-motivated and potentially impactful.

2.  **Conceptually Interesting Idea:** The core idea of designing a random projection scheme specifically to be compatible with subsequent PPMI weighting is intriguing. If proven effective, it could offer a valuable alternative to computationally expensive methods like SVD or neural network training for creating powerful word embeddings.

### Weaknesses

My arguments against acceptance are primarily centered on the paper's lack of clarity, which makes it difficult to fairly assess the method's novelty, correctness, and empirical performance.

1.  **Lack of Clarity on the Core Method and its Novelty:** The paper's most significant weakness is that it fails to clearly explain its core technical contribution. I could not understand the fundamental difference between PoP and previous random projection methods beyond the assertion that E(R) > 0. The mechanism by which this property enables PPMI is not sufficiently explained or justified. For instance, it is not clear why methods with E(R) = 0 make PPMI application impossible, as the PPMI formula depends on sums of vector components, not necessarily the expected value of the projection matrix itself. This lack of a clear, self-contained explanation makes it impossible to verify the authors' central claims. The paper assumes a deep familiarity with the random projection literature, making it inaccessible to many researchers in the broader NLP community.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (MEN). To convincingly demonstrate the utility of a new word representation method, it is standard practice to evaluate on a diverse suite of benchmarks that measure different facets of semantic representation (e.g., WordSim-353 for similarity/relatedness, SimLex-999 for pure similarity, and possibly downstream tasks). Evaluating on a single relatedness task is insufficient to support the broad claims made about the method's performance and utility.

3.  **Unsupported Arguments and Logical Leaps:** The paper contains several assertions that are not adequately supported by evidence or detailed reasoning. For example:
    *   The statement "since these methods use... R with E(R) = 0, their resulting vectors cannot be adjusted and transformed using weighting techniques such as PPMI" (lines 114-117) is presented as fact without a rigorous proof or detailed explanation.
    *   The claim "Since the method is based on random projections, models can be built incrementally and efficiently" (lines 137-138) is a generalization; while often true, its specific advantages over other incremental methods are not detailed.
    *   The claim that models can be built in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes" (line 154) is a strong assertion that lacks direct empirical evidence (e.g., tables with run times) to substantiate it.

4.  **Poor Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript is hindered by numerous grammatical errors and unnatural phrasings (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142). While minor individually, their high frequency throughout the paper significantly degrades readability and professionalism, compounding the clarity issues mentioned above.

Given these substantial issues, particularly the lack of clarity surrounding the core contribution and the insufficient evaluation, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance. I encourage the authors to substantially revise the paper to improve its presentation and empirical rigor for submission to a future venue.
