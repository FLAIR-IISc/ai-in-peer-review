**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection method for constructing word embeddings. The core idea is to use a projection matrix R with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), in contrast to prior methods like Random Indexing where E(R) = 0. The authors argue that this fundamental difference allows for the subsequent application of weighting schemes such as Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the resulting low-dimensional vectors. This approach aims to combine the computational efficiency and incremental nature of random projections with the high performance of PPMI-weighted models on semantic tasks.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The primary contribution is the PoP method itself, a new type of random projection that, by design, produces vectors compatible with PPMI weighting. This overcomes a key limitation of traditional random projection techniques, which cannot be combined with PPMI, thus providing a bridge between these two distinct approaches to distributional semantics.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that the PoP method, when combined with PPMI and Pearson correlation, achieves strong performance on the MEN semantic relatedness benchmark. The results are shown to be competitive with state-of-the-art methods while requiring significantly less computational time and resources.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The central concept of modifying random projections to enable post-hoc PPMI weighting is both novel and insightful. It directly addresses a well-known limitation of methods like Random Indexing (RI), which are efficient but whose output vectors are incompatible with PPMI. This work presents a clever way to retain the efficiency of random projections while incorporating the discriminative power of one of the most effective weighting schemes.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper makes a compelling case for the efficiency of the PoP method. The ability to construct competitive word embeddings in seconds or minutes, as opposed to the hours or days often required to train large neural network models, represents a significant practical advantage. This makes the method particularly attractive for resource-constrained research or applications that require rapid or incremental model updates.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** While the evaluation is limited in scope, the reported results on the MEN dataset are impressive. Achieving a Spearman correlation of 0.75, and up to 0.78 with fine-tuning, is highly competitive and demonstrates the viability and potential of the proposed PoP+PPMI approach for capturing semantic relatedness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's main weakness is its lack of clarity, which significantly hinders the reader's ability to understand the method and its justification. The core technical distinction between PoP and previous random projection methods is not explained with sufficient detail for a non-expert in this specific sub-field. The argumentation throughout the manuscript is often difficult to follow, with several claims seeming to lack sufficient support (e.g., assertions prefaced with "in addition," "since," or "thus" on lines 114, 137, and 154 do not follow clearly from the preceding text). The paper needs to be more self-contained and provide a clearer, step-by-step motivation and explanation of the proposed technique.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is based solely on the MEN semantic relatedness dataset. While the performance on this task is strong, it is standard practice to evaluate a new word embedding method on a wider suite of benchmarks. This should include other intrinsic tasks measuring similarity (e.g., SimLex-999) and relatedness (e.g., WordSim-353), as well as extrinsic, downstream tasks. Without a broader evaluation, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the embeddings and the overall robustness of the method.
3.  **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The authors explicitly state in Section 2.3 that they cannot yet provide a detailed mathematical account or error bounds for the PoP method. While they correctly note that empirical validation has often preceded theoretical justification for similar methods (like RI), this lack of theory is a notable weakness. Without a more formal understanding of why this particular form of projection works and how it distorts the original space, the method appears more as an effective heuristic than a theoretically principled technique.
4.  **Quality of Writing:** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and instances of awkward phrasing (e.g., "To a large extend," "such PPMI"). These issues detract from the paper's overall quality and compound the clarity problems. The paper would benefit from thorough proofreading.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more formal explanation for why PPMI is undefined for RI-generated vectors? Specifically, how does E(R)=0 in RI lead to the row and column sums in Equation 3 becoming zero, and under what conditions does this hold true?
2.  The paper suggests an advantage of PoP is that it preserves the original data's long-tail distribution (Fig 3d), which is then transformed by PPMI, whereas RI directly projects to a Gaussian distribution (Fig 3c). What is the specific benefit of this two-step process? What critical information is preserved by the PoP projection that would be lost in a direct-to-Gaussian RI projection?
3.  Could you comment on the decision to evaluate only on the MEN dataset? Given that PPMI is known to capture relatedness well, how would you expect the PoP+PPMI model to perform on a task that strictly measures similarity, such as SimLex-999?
