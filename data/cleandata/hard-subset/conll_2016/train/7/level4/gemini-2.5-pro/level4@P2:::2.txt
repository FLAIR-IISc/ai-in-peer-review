### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces "Positive-Only Projection" (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings. The method is based on random projections, similar to techniques like Random Indexing (RI). The key difference is that PoP employs a random projection matrix `R` with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), whereas previous methods use matrices where E(R) = 0. The authors claim this property is crucial because it allows for the subsequent application of weighting schemes like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) to the low-dimensional vectors. They argue this is not possible with standard random projection methods. The paper demonstrates that applying PPMI to PoP-generated vectors results in a computationally efficient model that achieves performance on the MEN relatedness task comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel random projection scheme for word embeddings.** The core idea is the use of a non-zero-mean, positive-only random matrix. This is presented as a way to create low-dimensional vectors that, unlike those from traditional random projection methods, are amenable to further transformations like PPMI weighting.

2.  **An efficient method for creating high-performing embeddings.** The paper provides empirical evidence that the combination of PoP and PPMI is highly effective. It offers a practical, computationally inexpensive alternative to training neural models or performing SVD on high-dimensional co-occurrence matrices, while achieving competitive results on a standard relatedness benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Computational Efficiency.** The primary strength of the proposed method is its efficiency. By building vectors directly in a low-dimensional space, it bypasses the need to construct and store massive, sparse co-occurrence matrices. The subsequent PPMI weighting is also performed on these small vectors (e.g., 1000-5000 dimensions), making it significantly faster than applying PPMI to a full vocabulary-sized matrix. This makes the approach highly scalable and practical.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results.** The paper reports strong empirical results on the MEN relatedness task. Achieving a Spearman's ρ of 0.75 (and up to 0.78 with fine-tuning) is competitive with many more complex and computationally intensive methods from that period, including neural embeddings and SVD-based approaches. This demonstrates the potential of the approach to compete with state-of-the-art models without their associated computational overhead.

3.  **Novel Combination of Techniques.** The paper cleverly combines the efficiency of random projections with the proven effectiveness of PPMI weighting. The insight that a positive-only projection matrix makes this combination possible is the central and most interesting aspect of the work. This opens a potential avenue for research into other transformations that can be efficiently applied to these low-dimensional spaces.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity on the Core Technical Contribution.** The paper's central claim—that PPMI cannot be applied to vectors from standard random projection methods like RI—is not explained with sufficient clarity or rigor. The paper states that in an RI-constructed model, "the sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0," which would make the PPMI denominator zero. However, this assertion is not proven or adequately justified, making it difficult for a reader not deeply familiar with RI to verify this critical premise. A more formal and direct comparison with RI, explicitly demonstrating why PPMI is inapplicable there but applicable here, is needed to firmly establish the novelty and necessity of the PoP method.

2.  **Limited and Narrow Evaluation.** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single dataset, the MEN relatedness test. While performance on MEN is strong, word embedding methods are typically evaluated on a suite of standard benchmarks that test different facets of semantic representation (e.g., similarity vs. relatedness, intrinsic vs. extrinsic tasks). To provide a comprehensive picture of the method's capabilities, evaluation on other widely-used datasets like WordSim-353, SimLex-999, or an analogy task (e.g., the Google analogy dataset) is essential. Without this, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the model and its performance on tasks that require capturing more nuanced semantic similarity rather than just topical relatedness.

3.  **Presentation and Unsupported Arguments.** The manuscript would benefit significantly from improvements in presentation and clarity. The line of reasoning can be difficult to follow, and some arguments are not fully developed or supported. For example, several sentences begin with logical connectors like "Since," "thus," or "in addition" where the logical link to the preceding text is unclear. Furthermore, the paper contains several grammatical errors (e.g., "To a large extend," "such PPMI") that detract from readability. A thorough round of proofreading is needed to polish the writing and ensure the arguments are presented in a clear, self-contained, and convincing manner.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more formal explanation or proof for the claim that in RI-constructed models, "the sum of values of row and column vectors of the model are always 0" (Section 3.2)? This is a crucial point for justifying the novelty of PoP, as it is the primary reason PPMI is claimed to be inapplicable to RI but applicable to PoP.

2.  Can you provide a justification for evaluating solely on the MEN dataset? Given the variety of standard evaluation benchmarks, results on a single relatedness task may not be fully representative of the model's overall performance. Are there results on other tasks (e.g., semantic similarity like SimLex-999) that could be included or discussed in the author response?

3.  The motivation for using Kendall’s τb is that the raw PoP-projected space is non-Gaussian. However, the best results are achieved with PPMI followed by Pearson's correlation, which is more suited to Gaussian-like distributions. Does this suggest that the primary contribution is not the PoP projection itself, but rather its ability to enable a PPMI transformation that effectively normalizes the data distribution, after which standard correlation measures can be applied?
