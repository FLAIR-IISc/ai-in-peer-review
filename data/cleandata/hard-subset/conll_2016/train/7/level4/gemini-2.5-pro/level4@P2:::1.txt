### Summary of the paper
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel, incremental method for constructing low-dimensional word embeddings based on random projections. Unlike standard random indexing (RI) techniques that use projection matrices with an expected value of zero, PoP employs a matrix with a positive expected value. The authors claim this key difference allows the resulting low-dimensional vectors to retain distributional properties of the original count space, making them amenable to subsequent weighting transformations like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The method's efficiency stems from its incremental nature and the avoidance of computationally expensive training or matrix factorization. The authors evaluate their approach on the MEN semantic relatedness task, demonstrating that PoP combined with PPMI achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art models, but with significantly reduced computational cost.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel and highly efficient method for constructing word embeddings (PoP).** The primary contribution is the PoP algorithm itself, which cleverly combines the speed of random projections with the ability to apply post-hoc weighting schemes. By using a positive-only projection matrix, the method circumvents a key limitation of traditional random indexing, where the resulting zero-centered vectors are not suitable for transformations like PPMI. This offers a practical and scalable alternative to computationally intensive neural or SVD-based methods.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of bridging the gap between efficient count-based models and high-performing predictive models.** The paper shows that a simple, fast projection method, when combined with a standard weighting technique (PPMI), can achieve competitive performance on a semantic relatedness task. This provides strong evidence that the full computational machinery of neural network training or large-scale SVD is not always necessary to produce high-quality embeddings.

### Strengths
1.  **Computational Efficiency and Scalability:** The most significant advantage of the proposed PoP method is its speed. By building directly in a low-dimensional space and avoiding iterative training (as in neural networks) or expensive matrix factorization (like SVD on a full co-occurrence matrix), the method can generate embeddings in "a matter of a few seconds or minutes." This is a compelling practical benefit, especially for applications involving very large corpora or resource-constrained environments. The incremental nature of the algorithm further enhances its scalability.
2.  **Novel Combination of Methodologies:** The core idea of designing a random projection specifically to enable subsequent PPMI weighting is novel and well-motivated. It effectively merges two powerful lines of work in distributional semantics: the efficiency of random indexing and the discriminative power of PPMI weighting. The paper clearly explains why traditional RI fails in this regard (due to zero-centered projections) and how PoP's positive-only design solves this problem.
3.  **Thorough Internal Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed empirical investigation of their method's parameters in Section 3.3. The analysis of how performance changes with vector dimensionality (Figure 4) and the number of non-zero elements in index vectors (Figure 5) adds credibility to their approach and provides useful guidance for practitioners. This level of ablation study demonstrates a solid understanding of the model's behavior.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison with State-of-the-Art Baselines:** This is the most critical weakness of the submission. The paper claims its performance is "comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art top-performing algorithms" and neural embeddings, but it provides no direct comparisons. A convincing evaluation requires a table that reports the performance of established models (e.g., word2vec SGNS, GloVe, SVD on a PPMI matrix) trained on the *exact same* UKWaC corpus and evaluated on the MEN test set. Without these baseline numbers, the central claim of competitiveness is unsubstantiated. The reader is left to guess how PoP actually stacks up against the methods it aims to be a faster alternative to.
2.  **Evaluation is Limited and Non-Standard:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single task (semantic relatedness) on a single dataset (MEN). While MEN is a valid test set, standard practice in the field is to evaluate on a broader suite of benchmarks to assess different facets of semantic representation. For instance, including results on WordSim-353 (which contains both similarity and relatedness pairs) and SimLex-999 (which focuses strictly on similarity) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the embeddings' properties. The absence of these standard benchmarks makes it difficult to fully assess the quality and generalizability of the proposed method.
3.  **Unclear Justification for Certain Methodological Choices:**
    *   The paper introduces Kendall’s τb as a similarity measure for the non-Gaussian PoP space, but the formula presented in Section 2.2 is non-standard. The standard formula is simpler, and the one provided here is not immediately recognizable or cited, which could confuse readers.
    *   The rationale for choosing Kendall’s τb for internal similarity calculations, while using Spearman’s ρ for the final evaluation, is not explained. If Spearman's ρ is the ultimate figure of merit for the task, why not use a similarity measure more directly related to it, or provide a justification for the switch?
    *   Figure 1 is described as showing three lines (count-based, RI, and PoP), but the plot appears to only contain two distinct lines, making the results difficult to interpret.

### Questions to Authors
1.  To substantiate your claims of competitive performance, could you please add a comparison table showing the performance of your best PoP+PPMI model alongside established baselines (e.g., word2vec, GloVe, and PPMI+SVD) trained on the same UKWaC corpus and evaluated on the MEN dataset?
2.  The formula provided for Kendall’s τb in Section 2.2 appears non-standard. Could you please clarify its source or correct it? Additionally, could you elaborate on the motivation for using Kendall's τb to measure vector similarity internally, while using Spearman's ρ as the final evaluation metric?
3.  To demonstrate the general utility of your embeddings, would it be possible to provide evaluation results on other standard semantic benchmarks, such as WordSim-353 and SimLex-999?
4.  The established pipeline for high-performing count-based models is typically `Count Matrix -> PPMI transformation -> SVD`. Your work proposes an alternative order: `Count Matrix -> PoP projection -> PPMI transformation`. Could you more explicitly discuss the advantages of applying PPMI *after* the dimensionality reduction step, beyond the clear computational benefits?
