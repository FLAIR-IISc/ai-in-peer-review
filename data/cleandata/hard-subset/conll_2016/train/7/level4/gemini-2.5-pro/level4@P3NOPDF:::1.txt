This paper proposes a novel method for dimensionality reduction that is significantly faster to train than neural network-based approaches. The method is motivated by the goal of preserving certain distributional properties of the original high-dimensional space. While the computational efficiency is a commendable strength, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses, including a lack of clear motivation for a key theoretical constraint and, most importantly, the complete absence of a direct, quantitative comparison to related work.

**Strengths**

The primary strengths of this work are its computational efficiency and theoretical grounding. The proposed method offers a much faster training alternative to popular neural network models for creating word embeddings, which is a valuable contribution in an era of increasingly large datasets and complex models. Furthermore, the explicit goal of preserving specific properties of the original PMI-based distribution during the dimensionality reduction process is a well-founded objective.

**Major Weaknesses**

1.  **No Direct Comparison to Related Work:** The most significant weakness of the paper is the lack of empirical comparison to existing methods. The authors state that their method has certain advantages, but they do not provide any experimental results to substantiate these claims against established baselines. Without quantitative comparisons to other dimensionality reduction techniques or word embedding models on the same evaluation tasks, it is impossible for the reader to assess the practical utility or relative performance of the proposed technique. The paper would be substantially improved by including results from at least one or two standard baseline models.

2.  **Unclear Motivation for PPMI Transformability:** The motivation for requiring vectors to be transformable with PPMI is not sufficiently justified. While the authors present this as a core aspect of their method, it is not clear to the reader why this specific property is so important or what tangible benefits it provides for downstream semantic tasks. The paper would be strengthened if the authors could provide a clearer explanation of why this constraint is crucial and what theoretical or practical advantages it confers over other approaches.

3.  **Methodological and Evaluation Concerns:**
    *   The choice of evaluation datasets is unconventional. While exploring novel datasets can be insightful, the paper omits results on standard semantic relatedness and similarity benchmarks that are widely used in the NLP community (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). Including results on these standard datasets is essential for situating the work within the broader literature and allowing for a fair comparison.
    *   There are questions regarding the choice of correlation metrics. The paper relies on a non-standard definition of Kendall's tau for its optimization but uses the standard Spearman correlation for evaluation. This inconsistency needs justification.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **p.3, Kendall's Tau:** The definition of Kendall's tau provided in the paper does not seem to be the standard formula. Could the authors please clarify the origin of this definition, provide a citation, or explain the reason for the modification? Using a non-standard metric without clear justification makes the results difficult to interpret.
*   **p.3, Choice of Correlation Metric:** Given that Spearman's rank correlation is a standard metric for semantic evaluation tasks (and is indeed used by the authors for their final evaluation), why was it not used during the optimization process itself? Please provide a rationale for using the modified Kendall's tau instead.
*   **Figure 1:** The caption for Figure 1 indicates that three methods are being compared, but only two lines are visible in the plot. Please correct or clarify the figure.
