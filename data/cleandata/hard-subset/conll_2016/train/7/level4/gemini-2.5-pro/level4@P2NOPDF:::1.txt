This paper introduces a method for dimensionality reduction that is significantly faster to train than neural network-based approaches and is designed to preserve certain distributional properties of the original vector space.

While the motivation for computational efficiency is compelling, the paper has several major weaknesses that limit its contribution. The central justification for why word vectors should be transformable with PPMI is not clearly articulated. Most importantly, the paper lacks any direct empirical comparison to related work, making it impossible to assess the relative performance of the proposed method. The evaluation is also conducted on non-standard datasets, further hindering comparison with existing literature.

**Strengths:**
*   The proposed method offers a clear advantage in training efficiency over more computationally expensive neural network models.
*   The approach is motivated by the goal of preserving key properties from the original high-dimensional space, which is a desirable characteristic for dimensionality reduction.

**Major Weaknesses:**
*   **Lack of Comparison to Related Work:** This is the most critical issue. The paper makes claims about performance but provides no direct comparison to other dimensionality reduction or embedding techniques on the same tasks. Without benchmarks against established methods, the contribution and practical utility of the proposed approach cannot be verified.
*   **Unclear Motivation for PPMI Transformability:** The paper's core premise rests on the importance of vectors being "transformable with PPMI." However, the reason why this property is crucial for producing high-quality low-dimensional embeddings is not sufficiently explained or justified.
*   **Non-Standard Evaluation Datasets:** The datasets chosen for evaluating semantic relatedness are not the common benchmarks used by the NLP community (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). While exploring new datasets is valuable, the absence of results on standard sets prevents the work from being situated within the broader context of prior research.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
*   **p.3, Kendall's Tau:** The definition of Kendall's tau used in the paper appears to be non-standard and does not match the original formulation. Please provide a citation or clarify the origin of this formula.
*   **p.3, Choice of Correlation Metric:** Given that Spearman's rank correlation is a standard metric for semantic similarity tasks and is used later in the evaluation, what is the justification for using this unconventional variant of Kendall's tau during the optimization process?
*   **Figure 1:** The figure is described as showing three lines, but only two are visible in the plot. Please clarify this discrepancy or correct the figure.
