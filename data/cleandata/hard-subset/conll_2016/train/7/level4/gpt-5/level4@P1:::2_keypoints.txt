- Proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP): random-projection with strictly non-negative entries and E(R) > 0 to enable post-hoc PPMI in low-dimensional space.
- Claims a practical recipe for low-dimensional, incrementally constructed word vectors and an empirical study comparing PoP to count-based models and RI with parameter sweeps.
- Strength: Simple, incremental, and computationally light; moves heavy PPMI weighting out of high-dimensional count space.
- Strength: Clear empirical focus with comparisons and exploration of hyperparameters (m, sparsity) and similarity metrics.
- Strength: Competitive results on MEN suggest PoP could be a fast alternative to neural embeddings in some settings.
- Weakness: Unclear novelty and insufficient explanation of how PoP concretely differs from prior random projection methods.
- Weakness: Exposition is hard to follow; key ideas are not presented in a self-contained way for general readers.
- Weakness: Several key claims lack adequate justification or supporting references.
- Weakness: Numerous English errors and awkward phrasings that hinder comprehension.
- Weakness: Evaluation is too narrow (only MEN); missing standard benchmarks and stronger baselines (e.g., PPMI+SVD, SGNS/CBOW/GloVe trained on same corpus).
- Question: Provide a precise, step-by-step comparison between PoP and existing RP methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI).
- Question: Formally explain why E(R) > 0 makes PPMI computable on the projected space while E(R) = 0 would not, and whether RI row/column sums are zero in practice.
- Question: Give a concise, self-contained intuition of PoP for non-experts, including choice of distribution, role of s, number of non-zeros, and interaction with m.
- Question: Justify using only MEN or add results on standard benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999) and comparisons to strong baselines trained on the same corpus.
- Recommendation: Major revision—improve clarity, writing, formal support for claims, and broaden empirical evaluation before resubmission.
