- Paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random projection with E(R) > 0 producing low-dimensional, non-Gaussian word vectors and enabling post-hoc PPMI weighting.
- Authors claim Kendall’s τb is best for similarity in raw PoP, while Pearson is preferable after PPMI; report competitive MEN scores (~0.75–0.78) with far lower training cost than neural embeddings.
- Method is incremental and scalable, requiring only random vector generation and addition; paper explores parameters m (dimension) and s (sparsity).

Primary contributions:
- Simple random projection scheme with strictly non-negative entries that permits efficient PPMI weighting in low-dimensional spaces.
- Empirical observation that τb works better in raw PoP spaces while Pearson is better after PPMI.
- Practical efficiency: PoP+PPMI achieves MEN performance comparable to stronger baselines with much lower computation.

Strengths:
- Efficient and simple to implement, incremental, and fast, suitable for resource-limited or streaming settings.
- Empirical improvements over RI and raw counts on MEN, especially with smaller windows.
- Clear practical motivation addressing costly weighting/training in high-dimensional spaces.
- Parameter study gives useful empirical insight into behavior and stability.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks direct comparisons to strong contemporary baselines (SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, PPMI+SVD) with matched preprocessing and metrics.
- Definition and use of Kendall’s τb are non-standard/unclear and inconsistent with evaluation using Spearman’s ρ.
- Evaluation is narrow (only MEN); needs additional benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words).
- Limited evidence quantifying how much performance comes from PPMI versus the projection itself; novelty may be mainly computational.
- Insufficient theoretical grounding (no bounds/analysis on sparsity, collision rates, concentration).
- Presentation issues (Figure 1 mismatch, overloaded notation, missing citations/clarifications).

Questions for authors:
- Provide the exact formula/citation for the version of Kendall’s τb used, including tie handling.
- Why prefer τb to Spearman’s ρ for raw PoP similarities, and have you tried ρ internally? Report results with cosine, ρ, τb, and Pearson before/after PPMI.
- Add controlled comparisons to (a) SGNS/word2vec and GloVe, (b) PPMI+SVD, and (c) RI/RRI/ISA with recommended similarities, including training time and memory.
- Report results on additional benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, RW) to test generality.
- How sensitive are results to s and m beyond shown ranges, and is there a principled way to set s relative to vocabulary size to avoid non-orthogonality?
- For PPMI on PoP: do you guarantee non-zero marginals for all dimensions and how are zero marginals handled?
- For reproducibility: share code, random seeds, and full preprocessing details (windowing, tagging, cut-offs).

Additional minor comments:
- Typo: “large extend” → “large extent.”
- Clarify Figure 1 to show all referenced curves (count-based, RI, PoP) and label them.
- Keep notation consistent (R vs T) and specify distribution of tij and rationale for α and s choices.

Overall recommendation:
- PoP+PPMI is an interesting, practically motivated idea with promising preliminary results, but needs stronger empirical comparisons, clarification of τb usage, broader evaluation, and some theoretical or analytical support to be convincing.
