Summary and claimed contributions:
This paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing word embeddings using a projection matrix with strictly non-negative entries and positive expected value (E(R) > 0). The authors argue that, unlike prior random projection methods (e.g., RI, RRI, ISA, RMI) that use mean-zero matrices, PoP permits post-hoc application of PPMI to the low-dimensional projected space. They measure similarity using Kendall’s τb in the unweighted PoP space, and Pearson’s correlation after PPMI, and report competitive performance on MEN with substantially reduced computational cost.

Contributions as I see them:
- A practical recipe for building low-dimensional, incrementally constructed word vectors via positive-only random projections, enabling efficient post-hoc PPMI weighting.
- An empirical study comparing PoP to count-based models and RI, plus a parameter sweep (m, number of non-zeros) and similarity choices (Kendall vs Pearson) demonstrating where PoP helps.

Strengths:
- Practical efficiency: The approach is simple, incremental, and computationally light. If the core claim holds, being able to apply PPMI after projection is attractive because it moves heavy weighting out of the high-dimensional count space.
- Clear empirical focus: The paper includes comparisons to count-based and RI baselines and explores parameter choices (dimension m and sparsity s), which is useful for practitioners.
- Potential utility: Competitive MEN results without neural training suggest the method could be a fast alternative for applications where speed and simplicity are prioritized.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity and novelty are unclear. I could not understand the core difference between PoP and previous random projection methods well enough to assess its novelty. Specifically, why E(R) > 0 is essential in practice (beyond expectations) and how this concretely enables PPMI where E(R) = 0 would not, is not explained with sufficient precision. Because of this, it is hard to evaluate whether the claimed advantages actually hold.
- Exposition is hard to follow. From the introduction onward, the narrative is difficult to track, and key ideas are not presented in a self-contained way for readers with general experience in vector space modeling but without prior work on random projections. I found it hard to follow the logic of the approach from the paper alone.
- Unsupported statements. Several key claims are asserted without adequate justification or references (e.g., line 114 starting with “in addition”; line 137 starting with “Since”; line 154 starting with “thus”). These appear to underlie central arguments and need evidence or formal support.
- Writing quality issues. Numerous English errors and awkward phrasings hinder comprehension (e.g., line 86: “To a large extend”; line 142: “such PPMI”). This further obscures the technical content.
- Narrow evaluation. The method is evaluated only on MEN. For a new embedding method, a single benchmark is insufficient unless there is a strong justification. Standard benchmarks such as WordSim-353, SimLex-999, and others should be included, with comparable training conditions. Stronger baselines (e.g., PPMI+SVD and neural embeddings trained on the same corpus) would make the empirical case more convincing.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal):
- What is the precise, practical distinction between PoP and existing random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI)? Please provide a clear, step-by-step comparison that explains how using a positive-only projection matrix changes the properties of the projected space, beyond the expectation argument.
- Why exactly does E(R) > 0 make PPMI computable on the projected space, whereas E(R) = 0 does not? Are the row/column sums truly zero in RI in practice, or only in expectation? Please provide a formal argument or counterexample(s) to clarify this point.
- Could you provide a concise, self-contained explanation of the intuition behind PoP for readers unfamiliar with random projections, including the choice of distribution in Equation (2), the role of s and the number of non-zeros, and how these interact with m?
- Why evaluate only on MEN? Please add results on standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), and include comparisons to strong baselines trained on the same corpus (e.g., PPMI+SVD, SGNS/CBOW/GloVe). If there is a reason MEN suffices here, please justify it explicitly.

Recommendation:
While the idea is potentially useful and the efficiency angle is appealing, the paper in its current form is too difficult to follow, key claims are insufficiently supported, and the evaluation is too narrow to substantiate the contribution. I recommend substantial improvements to clarity, writing quality, and empirical coverage, followed by resubmission to another venue.
