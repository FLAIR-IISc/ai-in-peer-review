Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing word embeddings using a sparse projection matrix with strictly nonnegative entries and E(R) > 0. The key claim is that this positive bias allows applying PPMI (and similar weighting) after projection, unlike prior random projection methods with zero-mean matrices, and that PoP supports efficient, incremental construction. The authors use Kendall’s τb for similarity in the unweighted space and report that PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains competitive performance on MEN with substantially lower computational cost.

As I see it, the main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1) An empirical observation that positive-only random projections enable effective post hoc PPMI weighting at low dimensionality, yielding MEN performance (~0.75) comparable to stronger baselines while being much more efficient.
2) A practical recipe for fast, incremental embedding construction with a parameter study over m and sparsity s.
3) The proposal to use Kendall’s τb in the non-Gaussian projected space prior to PPMI.

Strengths
- Efficiency and simplicity: The method is easy to implement, requires no expensive training, and appears to run in seconds to minutes. If the performance holds more broadly, this is practically useful for large-scale or incremental settings.
- Post-projection weighting: Showing that PPMI can be applied after projection (thanks to E(R) > 0) is an interesting design choice that bridges random indexing and count-based weighting, potentially reducing the cost of computing PPMI on huge co-occurrence matrices.
- Parameter exploration: The paper investigates how m and s affect performance and variance, which is valuable for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and novelty relative to prior work: The core distinction between PoP and prior random projection methods (RI, RRI, ISA, RMI, feature hashing) is not explained with enough precision. Why exactly does E(R) > 0 lead to well-defined PPMI where E(R)=0 does not, and is this simply a matter of adding a positive bias or using counts-only updates? Without a clear, formal contrast, it is hard to assess novelty and to understand when PoP’s advantages truly hold.
- Exposition is hard to follow: The introduction and method sections are dense and sometimes meander. Key ideas (what is new, why it matters, and how it works) should be stated early and concretely. As a reader with experience in distributional semantics but not a specialist in random projections, I found the paper insufficiently self-contained to follow the logic end-to-end.
- Unsupported or unclear claims: Several statements are asserted without evidence or references (e.g., line 114 “in addition…”, line 137 “since…”, line 154 “thus…”). These need either derivations, citations, or empirical support.
- Evaluation is too narrow: Results are reported only on MEN. For a new embedding method, evaluation should include standard relatedness and similarity datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, RW, MTurk, and possibly analogy tests), ideally on the same corpus and preprocessing. Strong baselines should include count+PPMI(+SVD), SGNS/CBOW, and GloVe with matched training data and context definitions. Statistical significance and variance across runs should be reported.
- Similarity measure rationale: The argument for Kendall’s τb in the unweighted space and Pearson after PPMI is plausible, but the ablation is incomplete. More systematic comparisons (cosine vs Pearson vs Kendall before/after PPMI) and significance tests are needed.
- Sensitivity and stability: The method’s performance varies with m and s, and some counterintuitive behavior is noted (e.g., performance drop with larger m when unweighted). Clearer guidance, theoretical bounds, or heuristics for selecting s and m would help.
- Language quality: There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that impede comprehension (e.g., “To a large extend” at line 86; “such PPMI” at line 142). The paper needs careful copyediting.

Questions to authors
- Please give a precise, formal contrast to RI/RRI/ISA/RMI and feature hashing. Can you prove that, under RI-style zero-mean projections, row/column sums relevant for PPMI become zero (or undefined) in expectation, and that PoP guarantees strictly positive denominators in the PPMI formula? What distributional assumptions are necessary?
- Is PoP equivalent to aggregating counts over a randomly selected positive basis (i.e., positive-only feature hashing)? If not, what is the essential difference?
- Provide broader evaluation: multiple benchmarks (MEN, WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RW, RG-65, MTurk, SimVerb), with matched preprocessing and corpora, plus comparisons to SGNS/CBOW, GloVe, and count+PPMI(+SVD). Include runtime and memory comparisons.
- Add ablations: PoP without PPMI using cosine vs Kendall; dense count+PPMI at matched effective dimensionality (e.g., PPMI+SVD) versus PoP+PPMI; PMI vs PPMI; Kendall vs Pearson vs cosine after PPMI; effects of context window and vocabulary pruning. Report variance and significance.
- Theoretical justification: Can you provide error bounds or concentration results analogous to JL-style analyses for your positive-only projections? Even partial results (e.g., dependence on m and s) would strengthen the work.
- Reproducibility: Please release code, full preprocessing details (stop words, POS, cut-offs), hyperparameters, and seeds for review, not only upon acceptance.
- Writing: Please substantially revise for clarity, reorganizing the introduction to foreground the contribution and its implications, and correct grammatical errors.

Overall recommendation
Promising idea and practical efficiency, but the current submission lacks clarity, rigorous comparison to prior work, and a sufficiently broad evaluation. I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. With a thorough rewrite, stronger theoretical grounding, and comprehensive benchmarking, this work could be competitive in a future venue.
