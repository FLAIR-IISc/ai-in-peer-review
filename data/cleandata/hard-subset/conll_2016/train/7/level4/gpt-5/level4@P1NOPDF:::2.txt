This paper proposes a positive-only projection (PoP) method for word embeddings: a random-projection approach whose projection matrix has a positive expected value. The authors argue that this enables the use of PPMI (which they claim is not feasible with zero-mean projections) while preserving the computational efficiency of random projections.

My main concern is clarity and exposition:

1) I cannot clearly identify the core differences between PoP and prior random-projection methods. Consequently, it is unclear whether—and how—the claimed advantages actually follow.

2) The argumentation is difficult to follow from the introduction onward. The paper should make definitions, assumptions, and the logical flow of the argument explicit.

3) Several claims appear unsupported or asserted without sufficient justification. For example:
- Line 114: a claim introduced with “In addition ...” lacks evidence or citation.
- Line 137: a statement beginning “Since ...” omits the necessary derivation or reference.
- Line 154: a conclusion starting “Thus ...” is not substantiated by preceding results.

4) Although I have worked on vector-space modeling (as many have), I am not a specialist in random projections and have not used them in my research. The paper does not make the motivation and logic of this approach sufficiently clear for readers with general NLP experience. A conference paper should be self-contained and accessible to such readers.

5) The manuscript contains numerous English errors (e.g., line 86: “To a large extend” → “extent”; line 142: “such PPMI”), which further hinder comprehension.

Evaluation: I do not see why the experiments are restricted to MEN. New embedding methods are typically evaluated on multiple standard benchmarks (e.g., MEN, WordSim, SimLex, and others). Evaluating on a single dataset is insufficient unless there is a strong justification.

Recommendation: Substantially improve the presentation, clarify the novel contribution relative to existing random projections, support all claims with derivations or citations, broaden the empirical evaluation, and carefully edit the English. I recommend rejection at this venue and encourage resubmission after major revision.
