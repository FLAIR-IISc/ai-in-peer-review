Summary
The paper proposes a fast, non-neural approach to learning word vectors that aims to preserve key distributional properties under dimensionality reduction and to remain compatible with PPMI-based transformations. The empirical evaluation targets semantic relatedness, and the authors emphasize training efficiency relative to neural models.

Strengths
- Clear motivation on training speed: the method appears substantially faster to train than standard neural embeddings.
- The focus on preserving distributional structure during dimensionality reduction is interesting and potentially useful.

Major Concerns
- Limited justification for PPMI transformability: The paper argues that vectors should be transformable with PPMI, but it is not clear why this property is practically important or necessary. Please articulate concrete downstream scenarios where PPMI-compatibility yields measurable benefits, and demonstrate them empirically if possible. Without this, the design goal feels under-motivated.
- No direct comparison to related work: The paper lacks side-by-side comparisons with strong baselines. At a minimum, please include comparisons to:
  - Count-based baselines such as SVD on PPMI matrices.
  - Popular predictive models such as SGNS (word2vec) and GloVe.
  - If relevant, recent non-neural dimensionality reduction methods in distributional semantics.
  Report both accuracy and training time to substantiate the speed/quality trade-off.
- Non-standard evaluation datasets: The datasets chosen for semantic relatedness are not the community standards. While exploring new datasets is welcome, please also report results on widely used benchmarks to facilitate comparison, e.g., WordSim-353, MEN, SimLex-999, RG-65, or Rare-Word. This will make the results easier to interpret and position relative to prior work.
- Inconsistency and ambiguity in correlation metrics:
  - Page 3: The definition of Kendall’s tau presented is non-standard. It does not match the canonical formula. Please provide a citation for the variant used, explain why it is appropriate here, and, if it is a custom measure, rename it to avoid confusion with Kendall’s tau.
  - Given that evaluation elsewhere uses Spearman’s rho (standard in semantic similarity/relatedness), consider using Spearman consistently throughout. If you keep Kendall’s tau, report both and discuss any differences.
- Figure 1 issue: Only two lines are visible; the third line mentioned in the caption/legend is missing. Please fix the plot and ensure the legend, colors, and line styles are distinguishable in print.
- Limited quantification of “preserved properties”: The claim that the method preserves distributional properties during dimensionality reduction would benefit from clearer operationalization and quantitative evidence. Which properties are preserved (e.g., neighborhood structure, rank correlations of co-occurrence statistics)? How are they measured? Providing targeted diagnostics would strengthen the contribution.

Requests for Clarification
- What specific applications depend on PPMI transformability? If the benefit is primarily theoretical or for interoperability with legacy count-based pipelines, please state this explicitly.
- How sensitive is the method to hyperparameters (e.g., dimensionality, smoothing/weighting choices)? A brief ablation or sensitivity analysis would be helpful.
- What are the training-time hardware settings and corpus sizes used for the speed claims? Please include wall-clock times and memory usage to make the comparison fair and reproducible.

Minor Comments
- Page 3: Use standard definitions for Kendall’s tau or provide a clear reference and rationale for deviations. Given community practice, Spearman’s rho may be more appropriate for rank-based evaluations of semantic similarity.
- Figure 1: Ensure the third line appears; verify legends and labels are readable in grayscale.
- Typos: “large extend” → “large extent.” A careful proofreading pass would catch several minor issues.

Overall Assessment
The paper has an appealing emphasis on training efficiency and an interesting angle on preserving distributional structure under dimensionality reduction. However, the current version falls short on empirical rigor and positioning: the lack of direct, quantitative comparison to established baselines, the use of non-standard evaluation datasets without the standard ones, and the unclear motivation for PPMI transformability make it difficult to assess the practical impact. Addressing these concerns—especially by adding strong baselines on standard benchmarks, clarifying the correlation metrics, fixing the figure, and substantiating the PPMI motivation—would significantly strengthen the submission.
