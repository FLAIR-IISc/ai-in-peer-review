Summary
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random projection–based word embedding method where the projection matrix has a positive expected value. The authors argue that this facilitates applying PPMI after projection (unlike standard zero-mean projections) and leads to computational efficiency compared to conventional co-occurrence matrix factorization.

Strengths
- The idea of aligning random projection with PPMI-style weighting is interesting and, if validated, could offer a lightweight alternative to heavier matrix factorization methods.
- Emphasis on efficiency is timely, and the approach could be practically useful for large vocabularies if the claims hold.

Major Concerns
1) Conceptual clarity and novelty: I could not clearly identify the core methodological difference between PoP and prior random projection approaches (e.g., Random Indexing, hashing-based projections, Achlioptas-style sparse projections, nonnegative projections). It remains unclear whether PoP uses strictly nonnegative entries, entries that are positive only in expectation, or some rectification step. Since PPMI requires nonnegative co-occurrence estimates, “positive in expectation” may not suffice for individual projected values, and the paper does not explain how this issue is resolved. Without a precise statement of what is new, it is difficult to assess the claimed advantages.

2) How PPMI is applied: The workflow is underspecified. Is PPMI computed on projected counts, or is the projection applied after PPMI weighting in the original space? If PPMI is computed post-projection, how are marginals, smoothing, and normalization defined in the projected space? What properties of PMI/PPMI are preserved under the proposed projection? A formal argument or theorem (even if high-level) would help.

3) Theoretical and empirical support: Several claims are asserted without sufficient justification or references. For instance:
- Lines 114 (“in addition”), 137 (“Since”), and 154 (“thus”) each introduce consequential claims or implications that need either a proof sketch, a citation, or an empirical demonstration.
- Efficiency claims should be supported by complexity analysis and runtime/memory measurements against baselines.

4) Accessibility and self-containment: The paper assumes familiarity with random projections but does not provide enough background or intuition for readers experienced in distributional semantics yet less versed in projection theory. A brief, self-contained overview (Johnson–Lindenstrauss intuition, distributional choices for projection matrices, effects of nonnegativity constraints) would improve readability.

5) Experimental evaluation: Evaluating only on MEN is insufficient for a new embedding method. At minimum, include WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words (RW), RG-65, and MTurk-771/287. Consider analogy benchmarks (Google, MSR) and, ideally, one or two extrinsic tasks. Also, include strong baselines (SVD on PPMI, SGNS/CBOW, GloVe, Random Indexing) and report statistical significance where appropriate. Ablations (with/without positivity constraint; sparse vs dense projections; dimensionality and seed sensitivity) would clarify where gains originate.

6) Writing quality: The paper contains numerous grammatical issues that impede comprehension. Examples:
- Line 86: “To a large extend” → “To a large extent”
- Line 142: “such PPMI” is unclear; consider “such as PPMI” or rephrase to specify the intended object.
More broadly, several sentences are incomplete or begin with transition words that imply missing premises (e.g., “In addition,” “Since,” “Thus”) without providing the necessary context or evidence.

Requests for clarification
- Precisely define the distribution of the projection matrix. Are entries strictly nonnegative, or only nonnegative in expectation? If the latter, how does the method ensure nonnegativity required by PPMI for individual entries?
- Where in the pipeline is PPMI computed? How are projected marginals and normalization handled?
- What are the formal properties preserved by PoP (e.g., approximate dot-product preservation, expected PMI relationships)?
- How does PoP compare theoretically and empirically to nonnegative matrix factorization or to projections with Bernoulli {0,1} or {1,2} distributions?
- Provide computational complexity (time and space) relative to baselines, including empirical wall-clock comparisons.

Suggestions for revision
- Rework the introduction to clearly state the problem, the precise methodological contribution, and why positivity (strict or in expectation) matters for PPMI.
- Add a method section with explicit notation, a step-by-step algorithm, and either a proposition with proof sketch or an intuitive derivation that connects the projection distribution to valid PPMI estimation.
- Expand experiments to multiple benchmarks and baselines with ablations, hyperparameter details, and sensitivity analyses. Include error bars or statistical tests.
- Improve organization: ensure each claim is supported by either a citation, a derivation, or an experiment. Move informal intuition to a dedicated subsection to avoid conflating it with results.
- Thoroughly proofread for grammar and clarity; consider professional language editing.

Overall recommendation
I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. The idea has potential, but the paper needs substantially clearer exposition, stronger theoretical justification, and a comprehensive empirical evaluation. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly and resubmit to a future venue.
