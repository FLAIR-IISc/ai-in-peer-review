Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random-projection-based method for constructing word embeddings using a projection matrix with strictly non-negative entries and E(R) > 0. The core claims are:
1) A practical contribution: by using positive-only random projections, PoP enables post-hoc weighting (e.g., PPMI) on the low-dimensional representations, which the authors argue is not possible for prior random projection methods with E(R) = 0.
2) An efficiency contribution: PoP supports incremental construction and avoids heavy training; combined with PPMI, it achieves MEN relatedness scores around 0.75 using low-dimensional vectors.
3) A methodological contribution: the paper argues for Kendall’s τb to measure similarity in the non-Gaussian spaces produced by PoP, and for Pearson’s r after PPMI transforms.

Strengths
- Computational motivation and simplicity. The paper targets a genuine pain point: neural embeddings and dense PPMI+SVD pipelines can be slow and resource-intensive. PoP offers a simple, incremental method that allegedly preserves much of the downstream performance while drastically reducing computation. This is an attractive proposition, especially for quick iteration or low-resource settings.
- Interesting idea: enabling weighting after projection. The central twist—designing a random projection with strictly positive entries to keep the “counts-like” structure and thus allow PPMI weighting in the reduced space—is novel and practically interesting if its assumptions hold. The observation that PPMI helps “Gaussianize” distributions and thereby makes Pearson correlation effective is also plausible and well-motivated.
- Empirical exploration of parameters. The paper provides useful intuition on how dimensionality m and sparsity s affect performance and variance across runs, and compares PoP to RI and to count-based models in a controlled setup. The results suggest PoP without weighting outperforms RI on MEN and that PoP+PPMI approaches strong baselines.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual clarity and justification of PPMI on projected spaces. The most significant concern is the lack of a rigorous argument that PPMI remains meaningful when applied to PoP-projected matrices. PPMI is defined on co-occurrence probabilities derived from counts. After random projection, the entries no longer have a clear probability interpretation. The paper argues that E(R) > 0 “enables” PPMI because row/column sums are positive, but positivity alone does not establish that the resulting quantities are faithful surrogates for probabilities or information content. This issue is central to the claimed contribution and requires a clear formal justification or, at minimum, compelling empirical evidence that PoP+PPMI preserves the relative signal of true co-occurrence PMI better than alternatives.
- Limited and narrow evaluation. The evaluation focuses almost exclusively on MEN (relatedness). This is insufficient to support broad claims. Standard similarity/relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999, Rare Words), as well as at least one syntactic/semantic analogy or downstream task, are needed. The paper asserts competitiveness with neural embeddings but does not provide controlled comparisons with SGNS/GloVe trained on the same corpus, with matched dimensionality and training budget. Statistical significance, variance across seeds, and practical runtime/memory benchmarks are also missing.
- Incomplete positioning relative to prior random projections. The distinction from RI/RRI/ISA/RMI is not explained with enough precision. For example: how exactly does the proposed T differ in distributional properties and in preservation of neighborhood structure; what is lost/gained compared to preserving inner products or L1 distances; how does PoP relate to known nonnegative random features or hashing variants? Without this, the reader cannot assess whether the advantages claimed stem from positivity, sparsity, different similarity metrics, or simply from the subsequent PPMI transform.
- Similarity function choice not convincingly justified. Using Kendall’s τb on raw PoP vectors is unconventional for embeddings. The paper motivates τb as nonparametric, but the consequences for ranking fidelity vs cosine/Pearson are not systematically evaluated beyond MEN. A broader and more rigorous comparison (cosine, Pearson, Spearman, τb) across datasets would clarify when τb truly helps and whether the gains are robust or specific to a single benchmark and parameter setting.
- Presentation and language issues impede comprehension. The paper is difficult to follow, with numerous grammatical errors, imprecise statements, and missing or dangling connective claims (e.g., sentences beginning with “In addition,” “Since,” “Thus” without sufficient support). Examples include “To a large extend” (extent) and “such PPMI.” The exposition should be tightened, notation standardized, and claims supported directly where introduced.

Questions to the authors
- Validity of PPMI post-projection: Please formalize why PPMI computed on the PoP-projected matrix is meaningful. Do the projected entries approximate sufficient statistics of co-occurrences or preserve PMI ordering in expectation? Can you provide a theorem (or empirical study) showing that PoP+PPMI better preserves rank relationships of PMI than RI or than applying PPMI pre-projection followed by a linear reduction?
- Comparisons and scope: Please evaluate on additional benchmarks (WordSim353, SimLex-999, Rare Words, and at least one analogy task or downstream classification) and compare against SGNS and GloVe trained on the same UKWaC subset. Include statistical significance and variance across runs.
- Runtime/memory: Quantify the claimed efficiency with wall-clock times and peak memory for (a) count+PPMI+SVD, (b) RI, (c) SGNS/GloVe, and (d) PoP and PoP+PPMI at several m and s on the same hardware.
- Similarity metrics: Report a systematic comparison of cosine, Pearson, Spearman, and Kendall τb on PoP (with and without PPMI) across datasets. When does τb help?
- Theoretical grounding: Even if tight bounds are out of scope, can you provide intuition or partial results on how positivity and sparsity of T affect distortion, neighborhood preservation, and the variance of estimates vs RI? Any connections to nonnegative random features or Johnson–Lindenstrauss variants for nonnegative projections?
- Reproducibility: Please provide full preprocessing details (tokenization, lemmatization, POS tags, stopword handling, frequency cutoffs), hyperparameters (m, s, nonzero counts), and number of runs; include code or pseudocode now rather than upon acceptance.

Overall, the idea of enabling post-projection weighting via positive-only random projections is intriguing and potentially impactful, but the paper needs substantially clearer exposition, stronger theoretical and empirical support for applying PPMI after projection, and a broader, more rigorous evaluation to substantiate its claims.
