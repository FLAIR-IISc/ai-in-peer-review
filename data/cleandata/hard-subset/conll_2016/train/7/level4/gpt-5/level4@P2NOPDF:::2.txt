The paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) method for word embeddings: a random projection whose expected projection matrix has positive entries. The authors argue this allows the use of PPMI (which they claim is not applicable when the expected value is zero) and yields computational efficiency typical of random projection methods.

My primary concern is clarity. Specifically:
1) Novelty and differences from prior work: I could not discern the essential distinction between PoP and existing random projection approaches. As a result, it is unclear how (or whether) the claimed advantages—particularly the applicability of PPMI and the computational benefits—follow from the proposed design. A precise comparison with prior random projection methods, including what changes when the expected value is positive versus zero, is needed.

2) Exposition and logical flow: The paper is difficult to follow from the introduction onward. Key ideas, assumptions, and their implications are not explained with sufficient intuition or formal justification. Clearer motivation, definitions, and step-by-step reasoning would help.

3) Unsupported claims: Several statements appear without adequate support or context:
- Line 114: Sentence beginning “In addition” introduces a claim without prior evidence or citation.
- Line 137: Sentence beginning “Since” assumes a premise that is not established.
- Line 154: Sentence beginning “Thus” draws a conclusion that does not clearly follow from the preceding text.
Each of these should be backed by derivations, empirical evidence, or references, and the logical connections made explicit.

4) Accessibility and self-containment: While I have experience in vector space models, I am not a specialist in random projections. The paper does not provide enough background or intuition to make the approach understandable to non-experts in this subarea. A self-contained presentation should include:
- A concise overview of random projections relevant to this work.
- An explanation of why a positive expected projection enables PPMI and what breaks when the expectation is zero.
- A clear description of how PoP integrates with PMI/PPMI computation and why it leads to efficiency gains.

5) Language and style: There are numerous English issues that impede readability. Examples include:
- Line 86: “To a large extend” → “To a large extent.”
- Line 142: “such PPMI” is ungrammatical in context.
A thorough proofreading pass is necessary.

Evaluation: The experiments appear to be limited to MEN. Given standard practice in word embedding evaluation, this is insufficient without strong justification. Results should be reported on multiple benchmarks (e.g., MEN, WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words, and possibly analogy tasks) and include comparisons to strong baselines, including established random projection methods and popular embedding models. If the focus is specifically on properties that only MEN captures, that rationale should be articulated and defended.

Recommendation: I encourage the authors to substantially revise the paper before resubmission. In particular:
- Clearly articulate the methodological novelty of PoP relative to prior random projection techniques.
- Improve the narrative and provide both intuition and formal support for key claims.
- Ensure that all assertions are justified with proofs, derivations, or citations.
- Expand the empirical evaluation to multiple standard datasets and competitive baselines, or provide a convincing reason for any narrower scope.
- Carefully edit the paper for grammar and clarity.

With these improvements, the contribution would be much easier to assess and could be of interest to the community.
