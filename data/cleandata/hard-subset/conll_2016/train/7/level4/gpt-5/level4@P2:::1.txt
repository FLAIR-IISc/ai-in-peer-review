Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a random projection method with E(R) > 0 that produces low-dimensional, non-Gaussian word vectors. The key claim is that, unlike prior random indexing variants (E(R) = 0), PoP spaces can be post-hoc weighted with PPMI to improve discriminativeness at low computational cost.
- The authors propose using Kendall’s τb for similarity in raw PoP spaces and argue that, after PPMI (which makes distributions more Gaussian-like), Pearson correlation is preferable. They report competitive MEN scores (≈0.75; up to 0.78 with tuning) with dramatically lower training cost than neural embeddings.
- The method is incremental and scalable, requiring only random vector generation and addition, with some empirical exploration of parameters (dimension m, sparsity s).

Primary contributions, in my view:
1) A simple random projection scheme with strictly non-negative entries enabling efficient, post-projection PPMI weighting in low-dimensional spaces.
2) An empirical observation about similarity metrics: τb works better in raw PoP spaces; Pearson works better after PPMI.
3) Practical efficiency: PoP+PPMI achieves MEN performance comparable to stronger baselines with much lower computation.

Strengths
- Efficiency and simplicity: The approach is easy to implement, incremental, and fast, avoiding expensive training or SVD on high-dimensional count matrices. This could be useful in resource-limited or streaming settings.
- Empirical gains over RI and raw counts: The paper shows PoP outperforming RI and count-based models on MEN, particularly for smaller windows, supporting the claim that E(R) > 0 plus appropriate similarity can help.
- Clear practical motivation: The paper targets a common bottleneck (costly training and weighting in high dimensions) and presents a plausible alternative that preserves enough distributional structure for subsequent weighting.
- Parameter study: The exploration of m and s provides some insight into behavior and stability, even if a full theory is missing.

Weaknesses
- Missing direct baselines to contemporary methods: There is no head-to-head comparison with standard strong baselines such as SGNS/word2vec, GloVe, or PPMI+SVD on the same data, with matched preprocessing and similarity metrics. The claim of being “comparable to state-of-the-art” remains unsupported without such comparisons.
- Similarity measure issues: The definition of Kendall’s τb given on p.3 is non-standard/unclear; please provide the exact formula and source. It is also unclear why τb is chosen for model similarity when evaluation uses Spearman’s ρ. A principled justification and consistency across training/evaluation would help.
- Evaluation scope: MEN is widely used but not sufficient alone. The community typically expects results on multiple benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, Rare Words), ideally including similarity and relatedness distinctions. As is, the empirical case is narrow.
- Limited evidence for the central claim about PPMI transformability: While the argument that E(R) > 0 enables PPMI is intuitive, the paper does not quantify how much of PoP’s final performance derives from PPMI (vs. the projection itself), nor does it compare to a strong PPMI+SVD baseline. If PPMI dominates, the novelty may be limited to making PPMI cheaper rather than improving quality.
- Theoretical grounding: The method’s properties are argued largely by analogy to other random projections, and the paper acknowledges the lack of theory. Even a partial analysis (e.g., expected sparsity, collision rate, or concentration bounds in terms of m and s) would strengthen the contribution.
- Presentation issues: Figure 1 appears to show only two lines despite the text referencing three. Clarify. Some notation is overloaded (T vs R), and a few claims would benefit from precise citations (e.g., the τb implementation details).

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Please provide the exact formula and citation for the version of Kendall’s τb you use, including tie handling. Is it standard τb (as in Kendall 1938/Knight 1966), or a modification?
- Why is τb preferable to Spearman’s ρ for computing similarities in raw PoP spaces? Since evaluation uses ρ, have you tried using ρ internally as well? Please report PoP results with cosine, ρ, τb, and Pearson consistently before/after PPMI.
- Can you add direct, controlled comparisons to: (a) SGNS/word2vec and GloVe; (b) PPMI+SVD; (c) RI/RRI/ISA with their recommended similarities? Include training time, memory, and MEN performance on the same corpus/preprocessing.
- Please report results on additional standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65, RW). Do conclusions hold across datasets?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of s and m beyond what is shown? Is there a principled way to set s relative to model sparsity and vocabulary size to avoid non-orthogonality while maintaining coverage?
- For PPMI on PoP spaces: do you guarantee that row/column sums needed for PPMI are non-zero for all dimensions? How do you handle zero marginals in practice?
- Reproducibility: Can you share code, random seeds, and full preprocessing details (windowing, tagging, cut-offs) for the reported numbers?

Additional comments (minor)
- Typo: “large extend” → “large extent.”
- Clarify Figure 1: if three methods are compared (count-based, RI, PoP), ensure all three curves are visible and labeled.
- Notation: Keep R/T consistent across sections; specify the distribution of tij precisely and justify α and s choices.
- Please align claims about “comparable to state-of-the-art” with actual reported baselines in tables/figures, or soften the claim.

Overall recommendation
The idea of positive-only random projections that allow efficient post-hoc PPMI is interesting and practically motivated, and the preliminary results are promising. However, the paper lacks direct comparisons to standard baselines, uses a non-standard similarity definition without a clear justification, and evaluates on a limited set. With stronger empirical comparisons, clarification of τb usage, and broader evaluation, the contribution would be much clearer.
