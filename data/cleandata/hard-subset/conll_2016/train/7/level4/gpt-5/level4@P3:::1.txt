Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Positive-only Projections (PoP), a random-projection-based method for building distributional semantic spaces. Unlike standard random indexing variants that use zero-mean projection matrices, PoP uses sparse, strictly nonnegative random matrices (E[R] > 0), enabling the application of post-hoc weighting schemes such as PPMI to the low-dimensional projected space. The authors also advocate KendallŌĆÖs Žäb as a similarity measure in the non-Gaussian spaces produced by PoP and report results on MEN using UKWaC.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A practical variant of random projection (PoP) with nonnegative entries that preserves the ability to apply PPMI after projection, yielding substantial efficiency gains over computing PPMI on high-dimensional count matrices.
2) An empirical study showing that PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves MEN scores (~0.75) comparable to popular baselines while being faster and incremental, with analysis of how dimensionality and sparsity affect performance.
3) A proposal to use KendallŌĆÖs Žäb for similarity in the unweighted PoP space, motivated by its nonparametric nature under non-Gaussian distributions.

Strengths
- Efficiency and incremental construction: The approach is computationally light (no training, streaming-friendly) and keeps vectors low-dimensional throughout. If substantiated with wall-clock timings and memory footprints against strong baselines, this could be a compelling engineering contribution.
- Enabling PPMI post-projection: The core ideaŌĆöusing a positive-only sparse projection to retain the applicability of PPMIŌĆöaddresses a known limitation of zero-mean random projections. This is a neat, practically useful design choice that could broaden the usability of random indexing-style methods.
- Competitive MEN results with minimal computation: The reported ŽüŌēł0.75 on MEN for PoP+PPMI+Pearson is in the ballpark of established baselines, suggesting a promising accuracyŌĆōefficiency trade-off, especially given the lack of neural training.
- Sensitivity analysis: The paper explores key parameters (dimension m, sparsity s/number of non-zero entries) and shows how performance and variance change. This helps practitioners understand how to set PoP hyperparameters.
- Clarity about theory gaps: The paper is candid about the lack of a theoretical error bound and positions PoP alongside prior randomized methods that were initially justified empirically. This framing is honest and pragmatic.

Weaknesses
- Missing direct, controlled comparisons to standard baselines: Despite claims of competitiveness, there is no head-to-head evaluation on the same corpus and preprocessing against (i) SGNS/word2vec or GloVe, (ii) PPMI+SVD, and (iii) stronger random indexing variants (e.g., ISA, RRI), all with matched settings. Without these, it is hard to assess the real advantage of PoP.
- Choice and coverage of evaluation benchmarks: MEN is widely used, but current practice includes WordSim-353, SimLex-999, Rare Words, RG-65, and recent SemEval benchmarks. Using only MEN limits generality, especially since MEN emphasizes topical relatedness more than strict similarity.
- Inconsistencies and potential errors in the KendallŌĆÖs Žäb definition: The text defines concordant/discordant pairs using consecutive indices (j, j+1) rather than all pairs (i<j), which is not the standard Žäb definition. This needs correction and could undermine the rationale for Kendall-based similarity if misunderstood.
- Questionable motivation for Kendall over Spearman or cosine: The argument for Žäb hinges on non-Gaussianity pre-PPMI, but the empirical comparisons mostly use cosine (or Pearson post-PPMI) and evaluate with Spearman at the dataset level. Please justify why Žäb, rather than Spearman (rank correlation) or even cosine, is the right choice for vector similarity in practice and show the empirical impact.
- Insufficient evidence for the central PPMI claim: The paper emphasizes that E[R]>0 allows PPMI after projection, but does not show a strong, controlled comparison between (a) PoPŌåÆPPMI and (b) PPMI-on-countsŌåÆSVD or feature hashingŌåÆPPMI. It is not yet clear why post-projection PPMI is preferable beyond speed, and whether the quality trade-off is negligible.
- Reproducibility details are thin: Code is promised only upon acceptance, and crucial details (wall-clock times, exact preprocessing, hardware, number of passes, random seeds, variance across runs for main reported numbers) are missing. Figures sometimes average over runs, but the headline MEN number would benefit from mean┬▒std and significance testing.

Questions to authors
- Please provide direct, controlled comparisons on the same corpus with: SGNS (skip-gram with negative sampling), GloVe, and PPMI+SVD, using matched windows and preprocessing. How does PoP+PPMI+Pearson fare on MEN, WordSim-353, and SimLex-999 against these?
- Can you report wall-clock time, memory usage, and throughput for PoP, count-based PPMI, PPMI+SVD, and SGNS (with typical hyperparameters), on the same hardware? This would substantiate the efficiency claims.
- Why is KendallŌĆÖs Žäb the preferred similarity metric in the unweighted PoP space versus Spearman (a rank correlation more commonly used in semantic tasks) or cosine? Please include empirical ablations.
- How does PoP relate to feature hashing (the hashing trick) and Count-Sketch? Both allow low-dimensional embeddings with optional weighting. Is PoP effectively a nonnegative variant with controlled sparsity, or does it offer distinct properties?
- Can you clarify the apparent discrepancy in Figure 1 (the text mentions three methods, but only two lines are visible)? Please ensure the third curve is present and legible.

Overall recommendation
Promising practical idea with solid efficiency potential, but the paper needs stronger, controlled empirical comparisons, corrected statistical definitions, and clearer justification for the chosen similarity measures. With these addressed, PoP could be a useful addition to the toolkit for fast, incremental semantic space construction.
