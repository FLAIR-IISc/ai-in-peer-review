- Main contribution: PoP uses a random projection matrix with positive expected value to enable post-projection weighting (e.g., PPMI), combining random-projection efficiency with weighted-model discriminative power.
- Claim: PoP offers computational efficiency and scalability, with reported model-building times in seconds/minutes.
- Empirical claim: PoP + PPMI achieves competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, improving over RI alone.

- Strength: Novelty — introducing a non-zero-expected-value random projection that permits weighting after projection.
- Strength: Potential scalability and efficiency from the random projection approach.
- Strength: Demonstrated competitive performance on the MEN dataset supports viability.
- Strength: Method is clearly explained and connected to existing random projection techniques.

- Weakness: Insufficient direct comparison to related work (e.g., SVD, GloVe, fastText) on performance, training time, and resources.
- Weakness: Unclear justification for emphasizing PPMI over other weighting schemes and why PPMI is critical.
- Weakness: Uses a non-standard Kendall’s τb formula and an insufficiently justified choice of similarity metric.
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to the MEN dataset; lacks results on standard benchmarks like WordSim353 or SimLex-999.
- Weakness: Figure 1 is unclear (mentions three lines but only two visible).

- Question: Provide head-to-head comparisons to baselines (SVD, GloVe, fastText) on standard datasets and timing metrics?
- Question: Elaborate why PPMI is especially important for PoP and how it compares to other weighting schemes?
- Question: Clarify the origin and justification of the non-standard Kendall’s τb formula?
- Question: How sensitive is PoP to the `s` parameter (sparsity of the projection matrix); provide a parameter-sensitivity analysis?

- Minor comment: Typo — "large extend" should be "large extent".
- Suggestion: Add discussion of PoP limitations (random-seed sensitivity, corpus size effects).
- Suggestion: Mention potential applications of PoP beyond semantic similarity in the conclusion.
