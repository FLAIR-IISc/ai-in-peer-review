The paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) method for constructing word embeddings, a random projection technique utilizing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors posit that this allows for the application of PPMI weighting, unavailable with traditional random projections (expected value of 0), while retaining computational efficiency inherent to random projection methods.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the exploration of a non-traditional random projection approach that *enables* post-projection weighting techniques like PPMI, thus bridging a gap between the efficiency of random projections and the performance benefits of weighted models. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of competitive performance on the MEN relatedness test, suggesting the viability of this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Combining Techniques:** The core idea of utilizing a positive-valued random projection to facilitate post-projection PPMI weighting is a novel combination. While random projections are well-established, and PPMI is frequently used, their application in this specific sequence addresses a limitation of standard random projection methods.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper correctly highlights the potential for computational savings. Random projections are known for their scalability, and the ability to apply PPMI efficiently to the lower-dimensional PoP space is a significant advantage, especially when contrasted with applying PPMI directly to high-dimensional count-based models.
3. **Competitive Performance:** Achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art methods on the MEN benchmark demonstrates the potential of PoP. This empirical validation provides evidence that the proposed method is not merely theoretically interesting but also practically useful. 
4. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (sparsity, high dimensionality of count-based models, and the inability to apply weighting techniques to standard random projections) and positions PoP as a solution to these challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Core Difference:** The fundamental distinction between PoP and existing random projection methods isn't sufficiently elucidated. The paper states the difference lies in the expected value of the projection matrix, but doesn't fully explain *why* this difference unlocks the ability to apply PPMI and the implications of this for the resulting vector space. A more detailed explanation of the mathematical basis is needed.
2. **Difficult-to-Follow Argumentation:** The flow of the introduction and subsequent sections is convoluted. The transitions between ideas and the motivations for specific design choices are not always clear, hindering comprehension. Restructuring and streamlining the presentation would significantly improve readability.
3. **Stylistic and Grammatical Issues:** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., lines 114, 137, 154, and 86, 142 as noted in the original review), which detract from the paper’s credibility and make it harder to understand the technical content. A thorough proofread is essential.
4. **Limited Evaluation:** Evaluating the method solely on the MEN dataset is insufficient. While MEN is a standard benchmark, a more comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets (e.g., WordSim, SimLex) is necessary to establish the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. Justification for limiting evaluation to MEN is lacking.
5. **Accessibility for Non-Experts:** The paper assumes a high level of prior knowledge regarding random projections. While it's acceptable to assume some familiarity with the field, the paper should be more self-contained and provide sufficient background information to make it accessible to researchers with experience in vector space modeling but not specialized in random projections.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed mathematical explanation of *why* a positive expected value in the random projection matrix enables the application of PPMI weighting, and what the implications are for the resulting vector space?
2. What specific choices were made in setting the value of 's' in Equation 2, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
3. Beyond MEN, what other datasets have you considered for evaluation, and why did you ultimately choose to focus on MEN?
4. Can you elaborate on the limitations of this approach, and potential scenarios where it might not perform well compared to other embedding techniques?
