## Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings based on random projections. The core idea is to utilize a random projection matrix with a non-zero expected value, enabling the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) after projection. The authors claim the primary contributions are: (1) a novel random projection technique (PoP) allowing for post-projection weighting, (2) computational efficiency and scalability due to the random projection approach, and (3) competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art algorithms. 

I believe the most significant contribution of this work is the observation that a random projection matrix with a positive expected value *allows* for the subsequent application of weighting techniques like PPMI, which is not possible with traditional random projection methods. This unlocks a pathway to combining the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of weighted models. The computational efficiency gains, while not fully quantified in comparison to other methods, are a noteworthy benefit. The empirical results, demonstrating performance comparable to established techniques, further support the viability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The use of a random projection matrix with a non-zero expected value is a genuinely novel idea.  Existing random projection methods, by design, prevent the application of weighting techniques. PoP elegantly bypasses this limitation, opening up new possibilities for combining the benefits of both approaches. This is a conceptually sound contribution.
2. **Potential for Scalability and Efficiency:** The authors correctly highlight the potential for scalability and computational efficiency inherent in random projection methods.  While a more detailed comparison to other methods (e.g., training time for comparable performance) would strengthen this argument, the principle is sound and a compelling motivation for the work. Building models in seconds/minutes, as claimed, is a significant advantage.
3. **Demonstrated Competitive Performance:** The reported results on the MEN relatedness test demonstrate that PoP, combined with PPMI, achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. This validates the core idea and suggests that PoP is a viable alternative to more computationally expensive approaches.  The improvements over RI alone are also a positive finding. 
4. **Clear Explanation of the Method:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the PoP method and its underlying principles. The connection to existing random projection techniques like RI is well-established, and the mathematical formulation is presented in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to related work. While the authors mention existing techniques, they don't provide a direct, head-to-head comparison of performance, training time, or resource usage. A table comparing PoP to several baselines (e.g., SVD, GloVe, fastText) on standard semantic similarity datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.  The claim of “comparable” performance needs concrete substantiation.
2. **Justification for PPMI Emphasis is Unclear:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the importance of being able to apply PPMI weighting, but the *why* remains somewhat unclear.  A more detailed explanation of why PPMI is crucial for achieving good performance and why other weighting schemes wouldn't suffice would be beneficial.  What specifically does PPMI bring to the table that justifies its central role in the argument?
3. **Non-Standard Kendall’s τb Formula & Similarity Metric Choice:** The formula for Kendall’s τb presented on page 3 is not the standard definition. The authors need to clarify its origin and justify its use. The choice of Kendall’s τb, especially given the discussion of Gaussian distributions, is also questionable. While the authors address this by later using Pearson correlation after PPMI transformation, the initial choice seems counterintuitive, and the justification is weak.  The standard Spearman correlation would have been a more natural choice.
4. **Limited Evaluation Dataset:** The evaluation is performed solely on the MEN dataset, which is not a standard benchmark for semantic similarity. Including results on more widely used datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) would significantly improve the robustness and generalizability of the evaluation.
5. **Figure 1 Clarity:** Figure 1 currently only displays two lines, making it difficult to interpret. The description mentions three, but they are not visible.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Comparison to Baselines:** Can you provide a direct comparison of PoP to other methods (SVD, GloVe, fastText) in terms of performance (on standard datasets like WordSim353 and SimLex-999) and training/inference time?
2. **PPMI Justification:**  Could you elaborate on why PPMI weighting is so critical for the performance of PoP? What specific advantages does it offer compared to other weighting schemes?
3. **Kendall’s τb Formula:** Please clarify the origin and justification for the non-standard formula used for Kendall’s τb.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is the performance of PoP to the choice of the `s` parameter (controlling the number of non-zero elements in the projection matrix)?  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of this parameter?

**Additional Comments:**

* The phrase "large extend" on page 3 should be "large extent".
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of PoP, such as potential sensitivity to the choice of random seed or the impact of corpus size.
* In the conclusion, briefly mention potential applications of PoP beyond semantic similarity assessment.
