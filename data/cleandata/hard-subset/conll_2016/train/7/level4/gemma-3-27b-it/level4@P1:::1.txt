We introduce positive-only projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces and word embeddings. The PoP method is based on random projections, making it highly scalable and computationally efficient. In contrast to previous methods that use random projection matrices R with an expected value of 0 (i.e., E(R) = 0), the proposed method uses R with E(R) > 0. We use Kendall’s τb distance to compute vector similarities in the resulting non-Gaussian spaces. Most importantly, since E(R) > 0, weighting methods such as positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI) can be applied to PoP-constructed spaces after their construction for efficiently transferring PoP embeddings onto spaces that are discriminative for semantic similarity assessments. Our PoP-constructed models, combined with PPMI, achieve an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test, which is comparable to results obtained by state-of-the-art algorithms.

1 Introduction
The development of data-driven methods in natural language processing begins with a foundational assumption – the distributional hypothesis: linguistic entities’ properties can be modeled by statistical observations in language data. This statistical information is then represented in a mathematical framework, and finally, tools from that framework are used to implement similarity-based logic to identify linguistic structures and/or verify the initial hypothesis. Harris’s distributional hypothesis is a well-known example of the first step, stating that the meanings of words correlate with their context. Vector space models and η-norm-based similarity measures are renowned examples for the subsequent steps – word space models or word embeddings.

However, as pointed out by Baroni et al. (2014), count-based models often lack the discriminative power to achieve satisfactory results, necessitating predictive models. To this end, an additional transformation step is often added, described by Turney and Pantel (2010) as a combination of weighting and dimensionality reduction. This transformation from count-based to predictive models can be implemented with heuristics, or with more sophisticated mathematical transformations, such as converting raw counts to probabilities and using matrix factorization techniques. Alternatively, leveraging modern computational power, this transformation can be achieved via neural word embedding techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013; Levy and Goldberg, 2014).

To a large extent, the need for such transformations arises from the heavy-tailed distributions frequently found in statistical natural language models – such as the Zipfian distribution of words in contexts when building word spaces. Consequently, count-based models are sparse and high-dimensional, leading to computational expense and reduced discriminative power (see Minsky and Papert, 1969, chap. 12).

Similar to considerations of feature weighting, selection, and engineering in statistical machine learning.

While neural networks achieve top performance, their usage is computationally costly: training is often time-consuming, and performance can vary depending on the objective function (Baroni et al., 2014).  Alternatively, methods based on random projections efficiently reduce dimensionality – such as RI (Kanerva et al., 2000), reflective random indexing (RRI) (Cohen et al., 2010), ISA (Baroni et al., 2007), and random Manhattan indexing (RMI) (Zadeh and Handschuh, 2014) – but they retain distances between entities in the original space. Additionally, because these methods use asymptotic Gaussian or Cauchy random projection matrices R with E(R) = 0, their resulting vectors cannot be adjusted or transformed using weighting techniques such as PPMI. Hence, these methods do not consistently outperform neural embeddings or combinations of PPMI weighting of count-based models followed by matrix factorization – such as truncation of weighted vectors using singular value decomposition (SVD).

To overcome these problems, we propose a new method called positive-only projection (PoP). PoP is an incremental semantic space construction method similar to RI in its use of random projections, avoiding the need for prior embedding computation. However, unlike RI and similar methods, PoP-constructed spaces *can* undergo weighting transformations like PPMI due to the use of random vectors containing only positive integer values. Because it is based on random projections, model building is incremental and efficient. Furthermore, the small dimensionality of PoP-constructed vectors (a few hundred dimensions) allows for faster application of weighting methods like PPMI compared to classical count-based models. Combined with a suitable weighting method such as PPMI, PoP yields competitive results concerning accuracy in semantic similarity assessment, comparable to neural network-based approaches and combinations of count-based models with weighting and matrix factorization. These results are achieved without requiring extensive computation – models can be built in seconds or minutes, rather than hours, days, or months. Notably, even without weighting transformation, PoP-constructed models outperform RI on tasks of semantic similarity assessment.

We describe the PoP method in § 2. In § 3, we report the performance of the PoP method in the MEN relatedness test. Finally, § 4 concludes with a discussion.

2 Method

2.1 PoP-based Model Construction
Any transformation from a count-based model to a predictive one can be expressed using a matrix notation:
Cp×n ×Tn×x = Pp×x. 

In Equation 1, C denotes the count-based model consisting of p vectors and n context elements (i.e., n dimensions). T is the transformation matrix that maps the p n-dimensional vectors in C to an x-dimensional space (often, but not necessarily, x ≠ n and x < n). Finally, P is the resulting x-dimensional predictive model. Note that T can be a composition of several transformations, e.g., a weighting transformation W followed by a projection onto a space of lower dimensionality R, i.e., Tn×x = Wn×n ×Rn×x.

In the proposed PoP technique, the transformation Tn×m (for m < n, e.g., 100 ≤ m ≤ 5000) is a randomly generated matrix. The elements tij of Tn×m have the following distribution:

in which U is an independent uniform random variable in (0, 1], s is a small number (e.g., s = 0.01) such that each row vector of T has at least one non-zero element (i.e.,∑m
i=1 tji ≠ 0 for each row vector tj ∈ T). For α, we choose α = 0.5. Given Equations 1 and 2 and using the distributive property of multiplication in matrices, the desired semantic space (i.e., P in Equation 1) can be constructed using the two-step procedure of incremental word space construction known from RI:

Step 1. Each context element is mapped to one m-dimensional index vector ~r. ~r is randomly generated such that most elements in ~r are 0 and only a few are positive integers (i.e., the elements of ~r have the distribution given in Equation 2).
Step 2. Each target entity being analyzed in the model is represented by a context vector ~v, initially set to 0. For each encountered occurrence of this target entity together with a context element (e.g., through a sequential scan of a corpus), we update ~v by adding the index vector ~r of the context element to it.

This process results in a model built directly at the reduced dimensionality m (i.e., P in Equation 1). The first step corresponds to the construction of the randomly generated transformation matrix T: each index vector is a row of T. The second step is an implementation of the matrix multiplication in Equation 1 which is distributed over addition: each context vector is a row of P, computed iteratively.

2.2 Measuring Similarity
Once P is constructed, similarities between entities can be computed using Kendall’s τb correlation (−1 ≤ τb ≤ 1). To compute τb, we define the following. Given vectors ~x and ~y of the same dimension, a pair of observations (xj, yj) and (xj+1, yj+1) is concordant if (xj < xj+1 ∧ yj < yj+1) ∨ (xj > xj+1 ∧ yj > yj+1). The pair is discordant if (xj < xj+1 ∧ yj > yj+1) ∨ (xj > xj+1 ∧ yj < yj+1). Finally, the pair is tied if xj = xj+1 ∨ yj = yj+1. Tied pairs are neither concordant nor discordant. We define n1 and n2 as the number of tied values in ~x and ~y, respectively. nc and nd denote the number of concordant and discordant pairs, respectively. If m is the dimension of the two vectors, n0 is the total number of observation pairs: n0 = m(m−1) / 2. Given these definitions, Kendall’s τb is:
τb = (nc − nd) / √( (n0 − n1)(n0 − n2) ) .

To compute τb, we use an implementation of the algorithm proposed by Knight (1966), with a computational complexity of O(n log n). Since the vectors resulting from the PoP method have low dimensionality, this complexity does not impact overall efficiency.

The choice of τb is motivated by generalizing the role that cosine plays for computing similarities between vectors derived from a standard Gaussian random projection. In random projections with R of (asymptotic) N(0, 1) distribution, despite the common interpretation of the cosine similarity as the angle between two vectors, cosine can be seen as a measure of the product-moment correlation coefficient between the two vectors. Since R and thus the obtained projected spaces have E = (0), Pearson’s correlation and the cosine measure have the same definition in these spaces (see also Jones and Furnas, 1987). Subsequently, one can propose that in Gaussian random projections, Pearson’s correlation is used to compute similarities between vectors.

However, the use of projections proposed here (i.e., T with a distribution set in Equation 2) will result in vectors that have a non-Gaussian distribution. In this case, τb becomes a reasonable candidate for measuring similarities (correlations between vectors) since it is a non-parametric correlation coefficient measure that does not assume a Gaussian distribution of projected spaces. Other similarity measures may be explored in future work. In particular, transformations of PoP-constructed spaces to induce vectors with Gaussian distributions (e.g., the log-based PPMI transformation used below) may be beneficial. If such a transformation is performed, Pearson’s correlation, which works under the assumption of Gaussian distribution, may yield better results than Kendall’s correlation (as confirmed by our experiments).

2.3 Some Delineation of the PoP Method
PoP is a randomized algorithm. In this class of algorithms, computational complexity is reduced by tolerating a tolerable loss in accuracy and using random decisions. For instance, Gaussian-based sparse random projections in RI replace eigenvector computation (often of complexity O(n2 log m)) with simpler random matrix construction (approximate complexity O(n)).  In return, a randomized algorithm such as RI and PoP can give different results for the same input. 

Assume the difference between the optimum result and the result from a randomized algorithm is δ. Much research focuses on specifying bounds for δ, often expressed as a function of the probability of errors. For instance, δ in Gaussian random projections is often derived from the lemma proposed by Johnson and Lindenstrauss (1984). Similar studies for random projections in `1-normed spaces and deep neural networks are Indyk (2000) and Arora et al. (2014), respectively.

Currently, we cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for specifying δ and for the results obtained by the PoP method. Instead, we rely on simulation outcomes and performance on an NLP task. This is not uncommon; for instance, Kanerva et al. (2000) proposed RI without mathematical justification.  It was only later that Li et al. (2006) proposed mathematical lemmas for justifying sparse Gaussian random projections such as RI (QasemiZadeh, 2015). Research on projection onto manifolds is vibrant in theoretical computer science and mathematical statistics. Our research will benefit from this in the near future. Concerning δ, it can be shown that it and its variance σ2δ are functions of the dimension m of the projected space, i.e., σ2δ ≈ 1/m, based on similar mathematical principles proposed by Kaski (1998) and Hecht-Nielsen (1994).

Our empirical research shows that projections using PoP exhibit similar behavioral patterns as other sparse random projections in α-normed spaces. The dimension m of random index vectors can be seen as the capacity of the method to memorize and distinguish entities.  Up to a certain number (100 ≤ m ≤ 4000) in our experiments, a PoP-constructed model with a large m shows better performance and smaller δ than a model with a small m. Since observations in semantic spaces have a long-tailed distribution, choosing different values of non-zero elements does not significantly affect performance; 2 or 3 non-zero elements are often sufficient. Furthermore, changes in the adopted distribution of tij only slightly affect performance due to the use of τb as a similarity measure.

3 Evaluation & Empirical Investigations

3.1 Comparing PoP and RI
For evaluation, we use the MEN relatedness test set (Bruni et al., 2014) and the UKWaC corpus (Baroni et al., 2009). The dataset consists of 3000 word pairs (from 751 distinct tagged lemmas). We use these resources because they are publicly available, large, and have been used for evaluating several word space models (e.g., Levy et al., 2015; Tsvetkov et al., 2015; Baroni et al., 2014; Kiela and Clark, 2014). We use Spearman’s rank correlation ρ as the metric, comparing human ranking to system rankings.

Figure 1 shows the performance of a simple count-based word space model for lemmatized context windows around lemmas from MEN. As expected, performance increases with window size up to a point, then gradually declines. However, ρ < 0.50 in all cases.

We performed the same experiments using the RI technique, averaging over 10 runs for each context window size, using 1000-dimensional index vectors with 4 non-zero elements. As shown in Figure 1, the average performance of RI is almost identical to the count-based model, successfully reducing dimensionality while maintaining distances between vectors. However, RI does not improve similarity assessment.

We then performed similar experiments using PoP, with the same context window sizes and dimensions as in the RI experiments, averaging over 10 runs. The performance is also reported in Figure 1. For PoP, we used τb for measuring similarity. PoP models converge faster than RI and the count-based method, outperforming them with smaller context windows.  One possible interpretation is that PoP is more sensitive to noise, which could be addressed by increasing the dimensionality of the index vectors. Regardless, the performance of PoP remains above 0.50 (ρ > 0.50).

3.2 PPMI Transformation of PoP Vectors
Although PoP outperforms RI and count-based models, its performance is still limited. To improve this, we apply association measures like positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI) for weight adjustment. This often enhances discriminatory power and increases performance in semantic similarity assessment (see Church and Hanks, 1990; Turney, 2001, 2008; Levy et al., 2015). PMI is interpreted as a measure of information overlap between vectors. Bouma (2009) states PMI is a mathematical tool for measuring how much the actual probability of a co-occurrence deviates from the expected probability under the assumption of independence.

Figure 2 shows the performance of PMI-transformed spaces for small context windows. A major problem with PMI is its computational cost; PMI-induced spaces are dense. Even for a small number of entities, the resulting spaces from large context windows are high-dimensional, making PMI weight computation intractable. However, even for small context windows, PMI+Cosine models outperform other techniques, including the introduced PoP method. The performance of PMI models can be further enhanced by normalization, discarding negative values and using Positive PMI (PPMI). Additionally, SVD truncation of PPMI-weighted spaces can slightly improve performance (see the references above), but requires computationally expensive eigenvector calculations. For a p× n matrix with elements vxy, 1 ≤ x ≤ p and 1 ≤ y ≤ n, we compute the PPMI weight as follows:
ppmi(vxy) = max(0, log(vxy * (∑p i=1 ∑n j=1 vij) / (∑p i=1 viy * ∑n j=1 vxj))).

The key benefit of PoP is that PoP-constructed models, unlike previous random projection-based methods, can be weighted using PPMI. In an RI-constructed model, the sum of row and column vector values is always 0 due to the asymptotic standard Gaussian distribution of the random projection matrix (E(R) = 0). Therefore, PPMI weights for RI-induced vector elements are undefined. In contrast, the sum of values in PoP-constructed models is greater than 0, because PoP uses a projection matrix R with E(R) > 0. By choosing a suitably large value of s, it can be guaranteed that the sum of column vectors is non-zero. Hence, vectors in PoP models can undergo PPMI transformation. Furthermore, the PPMI transformation is faster than on count-based models due to the low dimensionality of PoP vectors. Therefore, PoP allows benefitting from both the efficiency of randomized techniques and the accuracy of PPMI transformation.

Putting aside the information-theoretic interpretation of PPMI weighting, the logarithmic transformation converts long-tailed distributions to Gaussian-like distributions. From a statistical perspective, any variation of PMI transformation stabilizes variance and makes observations more Gaussian-like (a practice with a long history in biology and psychology).

To exemplify, Figure 3 shows histograms of weight distributions for the verb ‘abandon’ in various models. Raw frequencies in the count-based model have a long tail (Figure 3a). Log transformation yields a Gaussian distribution (Figure 3b). RI weights have a perfect Gaussian distribution with an expected value of 0 (Figure 3c). The PoP method largely preserves the long tail distribution (Figure 3d), which can be weighted using PPMI and transformed into a Gaussian-like distribution (Figure 3e).

Given that PPMI-transformed models have bell-shaped Gaussian distributions, we expect a correlation measure like Pearson’s r, leveraging prior knowledge about the distribution of data, to outperform the non-parametric Kendall’s τb for computing similarities. This is confirmed in Figure 2.

3.3 PoP’s Parameters and Performance
PoP is a randomized algorithm influenced by several parameters. We study PoP’s behavior under different parameter settings in the MEN relatedness test. We focus on context windows of size 4+4.

Figure 4 shows performance as the dimensionality m of the projected index vectors increases. For these experiments, index vectors are built using 4 non-zero elements. For each m (100 ≤ m ≤ 5000), the models are built 10 times, and the average, maximum, and minimum performances are reported. For PPMI-weighted spaces, increasing dimension boosts performance, and the variance decreases.

For the count-based PoP method without PPMI transformation (dash-dotted lines), increasing m over 2000 decreases performance. This is unexpected since increasing dimensionality usually improves performance. This might be due to a very small s; the number of non-zero elements may not be sufficient. To investigate, we fix the dimension m at 3000 and vary the number of non-zero elements.

Figure 5 shows the observed performances. For PPMI-weighted spaces, increasing the number of non-zero elements deteriorates performance. For unweighted PoP models, increasing s up to a point enhances performance. As shown in Figure 6, increasing s increases the chances of non-orthogonal vectors, potentially introducing distortions in similarities. These distortions can be controlled with a training procedure, but left to chance, they can lower performance. Evidently, s controls the resemblance between the distribution of data in the original and projected spaces. The sparsity of vectors in the original matrix influences the optimal value for s. For direct use of PoP with τb, we propose s > 0.002. For PoP models subject to weighting for stabilization, we propose using 1 or 2 non-zero elements.

4 Discussion
We introduced PoP, a new technique for incremental semantic space construction. PoP is a dimensionality reduction method based on a random projection matrix R with E(R) > 0.  PoP transfers vectors to lower dimensions without changing their distribution to a Gaussian shape with E = 0. Transformed spaces can be manipulated similarly to count-based models, but much faster and with less computational resources. 

PPMI weighting can be easily applied to PoP-constructed models. PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves a high performance in semantic relatedness tests, comparable to neural embeddings (Chen and de Melo, 2015; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) but without the time-consuming training of neural networks. The involved processes are all done on vectors of low dimensionality, dramatically enhancing the performance of distributional natural language analyses.

This research can be extended in several ways. Theoretical accounts for PoP still need to be investigated. New methods can be designed by combining PoP with various weighting techniques. Also, the low dimensionality allows computationally-intense techniques for stabilizing vector distributions.  PoP-constructed models can replace high-dimensional count-based models as input for neural embedding development. Finally, derandomization techniques can be used to make PoP models linguistically more informed.
