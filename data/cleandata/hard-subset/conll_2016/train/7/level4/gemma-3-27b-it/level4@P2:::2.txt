This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel word embedding technique based on random projections. The core idea is to utilize a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, enabling the application of weighting methods like Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) – a step not feasible with traditional random projection methods employing a zero expected value. The authors highlight the computational efficiency inherent in random projection techniques as a key advantage.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the exploration of a modified random projection approach that *allows* for post-hoc weighting with techniques like PPMI. While random projections themselves are not new, the specific constraint of a positive expected value to facilitate downstream weighting is a potentially valuable contribution, opening avenues for efficient semantic space construction. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that this combination – PoP coupled with PPMI – achieves competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks, approaching the results of more computationally intensive methods. Finally, the paper provides a detailed analysis of the method’s parameters and behavior, offering insights into its sensitivity and optimal configuration.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Combining Techniques:** The central idea of using a positive-valued random projection matrix to enable PPMI weighting is a genuinely interesting combination. Existing random projection methods, as the authors correctly point out, typically preclude this step. This offers a potential trade-off between the efficiency of random projections and the discriminative power of weighting schemes.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The authors consistently emphasize, and presumably demonstrate, the computational benefits of PoP. Given the increasing scale of NLP data, methods offering a reduction in computational cost are valuable. The claim of achieving comparable results to neural embeddings with significantly reduced computational effort is a strong point, assuming it is well-supported by experimentation.
3. **Detailed Parameter Analysis:** The thorough investigation of parameters like dimensionality (m) and the proportion of non-zero elements (s) is commendable.  This reveals important considerations for practical application and provides useful guidelines for users of the method. The observed trade-offs and limitations related to sparsity and orthogonality are valuable insights.
4. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively frames the problem of constructing efficient and discriminative semantic spaces, situating the proposed method within the broader context of distributional semantics and the limitations of count-based versus predictive models. The motivation for seeking alternatives to computationally expensive neural methods is well articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Conceptual Clarity:** The core difference between PoP and existing random projection methods remains unclear. The paper states this is the positive expected value, but doesn’t fully explain *why* this fundamentally changes the possibilities beyond enabling PPMI. A more intuitive explanation of how this positive expectation alters the resulting vector space would significantly improve understanding. Specifically, the impact on distance preservation or the underlying geometric properties of the space should be elaborated on.
2. **Disorganized Presentation & Flow:** The introduction is dense and struggles to establish a coherent narrative. It jumps between motivations and background information, making it difficult to grasp the paper's central argument. The logical connections between paragraphs are often weak, hindering readability. Restructuring the introduction to first clearly define the problem, then existing solutions, followed by the limitations of those solutions, and finally presenting PoP as a targeted approach would improve clarity.
3. **Editorial Issues and Grammatical Errors:**  Numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., “To a large extend”, “such PPMI”, sentence-initial conjunctions like "In addition," "Since," and "Thus") detract from the professionalism of the paper and impede comprehension. A careful proofread is essential.
4. **Limited Evaluation:** Evaluating solely on the MEN dataset is insufficient. While MEN is a standard benchmark, relying on a single dataset doesn’t provide a comprehensive assessment of the method’s generalizability.  Including results on other established semantic similarity benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) would strengthen the paper's conclusions.  The authors should justify the focus on MEN if they maintain this limited evaluation scope.
5. **Insufficient Comparison to Baseline Random Projections:** The paper compares PoP to count-based models and RI, but lacks a direct comparison to standard Gaussian random projection methods *with* subsequent weighting (if feasible with some workaround). This comparison is crucial for isolating the benefit of the positive expected value.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of *how* the positive expected value of the random projection matrix fundamentally alters the resulting vector space compared to standard random projections? What geometric properties are affected?
2.  What specific modifications would be required to apply PPMI weighting to standard Gaussian random projection methods? What are the limitations that PoP overcomes?
3. Why was only the MEN dataset used for evaluation? What justification can you offer for this limited scope, and do you plan to expand the evaluation to other standard benchmarks?
4.  Can you clarify the impact of dimensionality (m) and sparsity (s) on the computational complexity of both PoP construction and subsequent PPMI weighting?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation (e.g., Equation 3) could be improved for readability.
* The discussion of theoretical bounds (δ) in Section 2.3 feels incomplete and could be streamlined.
* The supplemental material section is a positive note, promising code and embeddings, which would be valuable for reproducibility.
