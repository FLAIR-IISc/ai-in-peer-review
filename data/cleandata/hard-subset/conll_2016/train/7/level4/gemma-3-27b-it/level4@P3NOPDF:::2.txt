## Review of Positive-Only Projection Word Embeddings

This paper introduces a novel word embedding method based on positive-only projection (PoP), a random projection technique utilizing a projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors posit that this approach facilitates the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) – a common practice hampered by the zero expected value of standard random projections – while maintaining computational efficiency inherent to random projection methods.

My primary concern with this submission revolves around its clarity and accessibility. While the core idea has potential, the current presentation significantly hinders understanding and evaluation. Specifically:

1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation:** The paper fails to adequately distinguish PoP from existing random projection techniques. The incremental benefit of a positive expected value in the projection matrix, and *how* this enables PPMI in a way that other methods cannot, remains unclear. Consequently, it’s difficult to assess the validity of the claimed advantages.

2. **Difficult to Follow Argumentation:** The overall flow of the paper, beginning with the introduction, is challenging to follow. The reasoning behind key design choices and the connection between different sections are not always apparent. A more cohesive and logically structured presentation is necessary.

3. **Stylistic Issues & Argumentation:** The writing contains instances of awkward phrasing and potentially unsupported claims. The paper includes sentences beginning with discourse markers ("in addition" – line 114, "since" – line 137, “thus” – line 154) that do not smoothly integrate into the surrounding text. These stylistic choices detract from the paper’s professionalism and readability.

4. **Limited Context & Self-Containment:** While familiarity with vector space modeling is common, the paper assumes a level of expertise in random projections that may not be universally held. As a reviewer with experience in the field but not specialized in random projections, I found it difficult to grasp the motivation and theoretical underpinnings of the proposed approach. A strong paper should be self-contained and accessible to researchers with a reasonable background in related areas. More detailed explanations and potentially a brief review of relevant prior work on random projections would be beneficial.

5. **English Language Quality:** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical and typographical errors that impede comprehension (e.g., “To a large extend” – line 86, “such PPMI” – line 142). A thorough proofreading pass is essential.

Beyond clarity, the evaluation methodology is also a point of concern. The exclusive use of the MEN dataset is insufficient justification for the claims made. While MEN is a valid benchmark, a comprehensive evaluation should include standard datasets like WordSim, SimLex, and others commonly used for assessing word embedding quality.  Evaluating on a single dataset raises questions about the generalizability of the method and requires a strong justification for the dataset selection – a justification that is currently lacking. 

In conclusion, while the idea of positive-only projection for word embeddings is intriguing, the current presentation falls short of the standards expected for CONLL. I recommend a substantial revision focusing on improved clarity, a more thorough explanation of the method’s novelty, rigorous editing for grammatical errors, and a more comprehensive evaluation.  I suggest the authors significantly improve the paper and consider resubmitting to a future conference after addressing these concerns.
