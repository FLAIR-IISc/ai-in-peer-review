## Review of [Paper Title] - CONLL 2016

This paper proposes a method for dimensionality reduction with a focus on maintaining semantic properties of word embeddings. The motivation – achieving faster training times compared to neural network-based approaches while preserving distributional information – is appealing, and I find myself partially convinced by this core idea. However, significant concerns regarding the methodology, experimental evaluation, and relation to prior work prevent me from fully endorsing the paper in its current form.

My primary concern lies in the justification for the core technique: the insistence on transformability via Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI). The paper does not adequately explain *why* maintaining PPMI transformability is crucial for downstream performance, or what specific advantages this constraint offers over other dimensionality reduction techniques. Without a clearer rationale, the methodological choices feel arbitrary.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a substantive comparison to related work. Simply stating that the proposed method differs from existing approaches is insufficient. A thorough discussion of how this method performs relative to established techniques – such as PCA, SVD, t-SNE, and other embedding compression methods – is essential to understand its strengths and weaknesses. This comparative analysis should be quantitative, demonstrating where the proposed approach excels and where it falls short.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **p.3: Kendall’s Tau Definition:** The definition of Kendall’s tau presented is non-standard. It deviates from the widely accepted formula, and the authors do not provide sufficient explanation for this modification or its source. This raises concerns about the validity and comparability of the reported results.
* **p.3: Correlation Metric:** The choice to use a non-standard variant of Kendall's tau during training, while employing Spearman correlation for evaluation, is puzzling. Given that Spearman correlation is the standard metric for evaluating semantic relatedness in NLP tasks, it would be more consistent and understandable to utilize it throughout the process. The rationale for this discrepancy should be clearly explained.
* **Evaluation Datasets:** While exploring alternative datasets is commendable, the evaluation relies on datasets not typically used for assessing semantic relatedness within the NLP community. To facilitate broader impact and comparability, I strongly recommend including results on standard benchmark datasets, such as WordSim353, SimLex-999, or RG-65. Reporting performance on these established resources will allow for a more meaningful comparison with existing approaches.
* **Figure 1:** The figure appears incomplete. The text mentions three lines, but only two are visible. This is a minor issue but needs to be addressed for clarity and completeness.
* **Related Work:** As previously stated, the lack of direct comparison to related work is a major weakness. A dedicated section detailing a comparative analysis, including quantitative results, is crucial for establishing the contribution of this work.
* **Typos:** “large extend” should be corrected to “large extent.”



In summary, while the motivation for exploring faster dimensionality reduction techniques is valid, the paper suffers from a lack of justification for key methodological choices, insufficient comparison to related work, and inconsistencies in the evaluation process. Addressing these concerns through a more thorough justification of PPMI transformability, a comprehensive comparative analysis, and the inclusion of standard evaluation benchmarks would significantly strengthen the paper.
