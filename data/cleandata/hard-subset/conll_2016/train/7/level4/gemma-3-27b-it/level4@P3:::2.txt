## Review: Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction

This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel random projection technique for constructing word embeddings. The core idea is to utilize a random projection matrix with a positive expected value, enabling the application of weighting methods like PPMI *after* projection – a step not typically possible with standard random projection methods that have an expected value of zero. The authors claim efficiency due to the random projection approach and competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks.

**Primary Contributions:**

I identify two main contributions of this work. First, the proposal of the PoP method itself, representing a variation on existing random projection techniques. While the novelty of altering the expected value of the projection matrix may be incremental, it opens up the possibility of applying post-projection weighting, which is a potentially valuable addition to the toolkit of efficient embedding construction. Second, the demonstration that applying PPMI weighting to PoP-constructed embeddings yields competitive results on the MEN relatedness test, achieving scores comparable to more computationally intensive methods. The empirical validation, even on a single dataset, provides some initial support for the approach. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Potential for Efficiency:** The use of random projections inherently offers computational advantages, especially for large-scale data. The authors correctly highlight this benefit and emphasize the speed of constructing models with PoP compared to neural embedding methods. This is a significant advantage in resource-constrained settings or for rapid prototyping.
2. **Novelty in Combining Techniques:** The key insight of enabling PPMI weighting after projection is a valuable contribution. Existing methods often either integrate weighting *during* the embedding learning process (e.g., in matrix factorization approaches) or do not allow it at all. PoP provides a unique combination of efficient dimensionality reduction *and* the ability to refine embeddings with PPMI.
3. **Clear Demonstration of PPMI’s Benefit:** The paper clearly shows that applying PPMI to PoP embeddings improves performance on the MEN task. This validates the core hypothesis and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Detailed Methodological Description:** The paper provides a reasonably detailed explanation of the PoP method, including the mathematical formulation and the process of model construction. This allows for potential reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity & Motivation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clear motivation and explanation of *why* a positive expected value in the projection matrix allows for PPMI weighting. The connection between the mathematical properties of PoP and the ability to apply PPMI is not sufficiently explained, making it difficult for readers unfamiliar with random projections to fully grasp the method's advantages. The introduction is particularly dense and lacks a compelling narrative.
2. **Insufficient Comparative Analysis:** Evaluating the method on only the MEN dataset is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the justification provided is weak. Comparison to a wider range of baselines, including simpler methods and other random projection techniques (beyond just RI), is crucial to establish the true value of PoP. A more comprehensive evaluation would also include metrics beyond Spearman’s rank correlation.
3. **Stylistic Issues & Presentation:** The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing, hindering readability. Sentences starting with conjunctions ("In addition," "Since," "Thus") are frequent and disrupt the flow of the text. Additionally, the organization could be improved to better highlight the core contributions and the experimental results. The frequent asides and historical context, while potentially interesting, often detract from the central argument.
4. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of a rigorous theoretical analysis for the performance bounds of PoP, this is a significant weakness. Providing some initial theoretical insights, even if incomplete, would strengthen the paper and establish a foundation for future research. The discussion of related work in randomized algorithms feels somewhat superficial. 
5. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The empirical investigation highlights a sensitivity to parameter choices (s and dimensionality) which isn’t fully explained or addressed. A deeper understanding of how these parameters interact and affect performance is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* a positive expected value in the projection matrix allows for the application of PPMI weighting, avoiding the constraint present in standard random projection methods?** A visual illustration or a simplified example might be helpful.
2. **What is the computational complexity of PoP compared to other random projection methods (e.g., RI, RRI) and PPMI weighting applied to count-based models?** A more detailed analysis of the computational benefits would be valuable.
3. **How does the performance of PoP vary across different languages and datasets?** Are there specific characteristics of the data that make PoP more or less effective?
4. **Have you explored other weighting methods beyond PPMI?** Are there potential benefits to using different weighting schemes in conjunction with PoP?

**Additional Comments:**

* Line 86: "To a large extend" should be "To a large extent".
* Line 142: "such PPMI" should be "like PPMI" or "PPMI".
* Consider adding a table summarizing the performance of PoP compared to different baselines on the MEN dataset.
* The discussion of Kendall’s τb and Pearson’s r feels somewhat disconnected from the main argument.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea with potential benefits. However, significant improvements in clarity, presentation, and experimental evaluation are needed before it would be suitable for acceptance. The authors should focus on clearly articulating the novelty of their approach, providing a more thorough comparative analysis, and addressing the weaknesses outlined above. I recommend a major revision.
