This paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) method for generating word embeddings. The approach utilizes a random projection technique employing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors posit that this design allows for the effective application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), a method incompatible with standard random projections that have an expected value of zero, while maintaining computational efficiency inherent in random projection methods.

However, my primary concern lies with the paper’s clarity. I found it challenging to fully grasp the core contributions of this work, specifically the distinctions between PoP and existing random projection techniques. Without a clear understanding of these differences, the purported advantages of the method remain unsubstantiated. 

Furthermore, the paper’s argumentation is difficult to follow, beginning as early as the introduction. Several instances of awkward phrasing disrupt the flow (e.g., lines 114, 137, and 154 begin with “in addition,” “since,” and “thus” respectively, appearing as somewhat disjointed transitions). While these are minor points individually, they contribute to the overall lack of clarity. 

The paper also lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with random projections. Although I possess a background in vector space modeling, I am not a specialist in this specific area. The paper did not adequately explain the motivation behind exploring this particular research direction, hindering my ability to assess its value. I believe a strong paper should be self-contained and accessible to researchers with general expertise in the field, even if they aren't specialists in the precise sub-area.

Finally, the manuscript contains numerous grammatical and typographical errors (e.g., line 86: “To a large extend,” line 142: “such PPMI”). These errors further detract from the paper’s readability and professionalism.

Beyond clarity and presentation, the evaluation methodology raises concerns. The exclusive use of the MEN dataset feels limited. A more comprehensive evaluation using standard benchmark datasets—such as MEN, WordSim, and SimLex—would provide a more robust assessment of the method's performance. Unless a compelling justification is provided for focusing solely on MEN, evaluating on multiple datasets is crucial for establishing the generalizability and effectiveness of any new word embedding technique.

In conclusion, I recommend the authors undertake a substantial revision focusing on clarity of presentation, improved argumentation, grammatical correctness, and a more thorough evaluation. Following these improvements, resubmission to another conference would be appropriate.
