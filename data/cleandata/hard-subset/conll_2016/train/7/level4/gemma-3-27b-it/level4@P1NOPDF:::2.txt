The paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) word embedding method, a random projection technique employing a random projection matrix with a positive expected value. The authors contend that this enables the application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI), which is incompatible with methods having an expected value of zero, while still maintaining the computational efficiency characteristic of random projection methods.

My primary concern with this paper centers on its clarity. Specifically:

1. I struggled to discern the key distinctions between the proposed method and existing random projection techniques. Consequently, I found it difficult to evaluate whether the claimed advantages are genuinely realized.
2. I found the paper’s arguments challenging to follow, beginning with the introduction. The overall presentation lacked a clear and logical flow.
3. Several arguments within the paper appear unsupported by their immediate context; for instance:
    - Line 114 begins with the transitional phrase “in addition,” without a clear connection to the preceding text.
    - Line 137 begins with “Since,” introducing a causal link that isn’t readily apparent.
    - Line 154 begins with “thus,” implying a conclusion that hasn’t been adequately established.
4. Although I have experience with vector space modeling, I am not a specialist in random projections and have not utilized them in my research. The paper did not adequately explain the rationale behind this research direction, making it difficult for me to understand the underlying motivation. I believe a paper should be self-contained and accessible to readers with a reasonable level of experience in the relevant field.
5. The paper contains numerous grammatical and typographical errors (e.g., Line 86: “To a large extend,” Line 142: “such PPMI”).

Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to the MEN dataset, which I find insufficient. Several standard benchmarks exist for evaluating word embeddings – including MEN, WordSim, and SimLex, among others. When presenting a novel method, a more comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets is crucial, unless a strong justification for focusing on a single dataset is provided. 

I recommend that the authors significantly revise the presentation of their work and resubmit it to a future conference.
