

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with positive expected values (E(R) > 0) instead of the standard E(R) = 0. This enables subsequent weighting with techniques like PPMI, which is not possible with existing random projection methods. The authors claim PoP achieves competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks (0.75 average score on MEN test) with significantly lower computational cost than neural network approaches.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A novel random projection method that enables PPMI weighting of the resulting spaces, addressing a key limitation in existing random projection techniques.
2. A demonstration that PoP with PPMI achieves competitive semantic similarity performance with orders-of-magnitude lower computational requirements than neural approaches.
3. An empirical analysis of the method's behavior across different parameter settings.

Strengths:
1. The computational efficiency claim is compelling and well-supported. The authors demonstrate that PoP models can be built in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural network approaches, which is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications.
2. The method's ability to enable PPMI weighting represents a genuine technical contribution that addresses a clear limitation in current random projection methods, making it possible to combine the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of weighting techniques.
3. The empirical results on the MEN test are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches, showing that the method is viable for semantic similarity tasks.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks direct comparisons to relevant related work, making it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of the proposed method. The authors mention that PoP outperforms RI and count-based models, but provide no comparison to other random projection methods (e.g., RRI, ISA, RMI) or to standard count-based approaches with PPMI weighting.
2. The definition of Kendall's tau used in the paper appears non-standard and differs from the original formula. The authors present a variant that is not explained or justified, which undermines the methodological rigor of their similarity measurement approach.
3. The evaluation uses the MEN test but fails to report results on more standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), limiting the generalizability of their claims about performance.
4. The importance of the PPMI transformability property is not sufficiently justified. The authors claim this is "most important," but don't explain why it matters for semantic similarity tasks or what would be lost without it.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide direct comparisons of PoP+PPMI to other state-of-the-art methods (e.g., GloVe, word2vec, fastText) on standard semantic relatedness benchmarks (WordSim-353, SimLex-999) in addition to the MEN test?
2. Why did you choose Kendall's tau instead of the more standard Spearman correlation for similarity measurement, especially since you use Spearman for evaluation?
3. How does the ability to apply PPMI specifically benefit semantic similarity tasks? What would be the consequence if this property wasn't available?

Additional comments:
- The paper contains a typo: "large extend" should be "large extent."
- The manuscript claims PoP "achieves an average score of 0.75 in the MEN relatedness test," but fails to provide sufficient context to evaluate whether this is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches.
- The claim that "PoP-constructed models display a better performance than RI on tasks of semantic similarity assessments" is not properly substantiated with comparative results.
- The discussion of random projection theory is insufficient; the authors acknowledge they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account for the error bounds (δ), which weakens the theoretical foundation of the method.
