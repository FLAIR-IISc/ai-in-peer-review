

**Summary**  
This paper introduces "positive-only projection" (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0), rather than the standard E(R) = 0. The authors claim this enables the application of weighting techniques like positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI) to the projected spaces, which is not feasible with conventional random projection methods. They demonstrate that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive performance (0.75 average MEN score) while being significantly more computationally efficient than neural network approaches.  

**Main Contributions**  
1. A novel random projection method (PoP) that preserves the ability to apply weighting techniques like PPMI to projected spaces.  
2. A computationally efficient approach for semantic space construction that avoids the time-intensive training required by neural methods.  
3. A demonstration that low-dimensional PoP-constructed spaces can achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods when combined with PPMI.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Computational efficiency**: The method's incremental nature and low-dimensional vectors enable model construction in seconds/minutes, rather than hours/days required for neural embeddings. This is a significant practical advantage.  
2. **PPMI compatibility**: The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing random projection methods (e.g., RI, RRI) that cannot apply PPMI due to their E(R) = 0 property. PoP overcomes this limitation, enabling a useful combination of techniques.  
3. **Empirical performance**: The reported MEN scores (0.75 average with PPMI+Pearson) are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the approach's viability for semantic similarity tasks.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of clarity in technical distinction**: The paper fails to clearly articulate how PoP differs from previous random projection methods (RI, RRI, etc.). The distinction between "positive-only" projection and standard approaches is not sufficiently explained, making it difficult to assess whether the claimed advantages hold.  
2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The authors acknowledge they cannot provide a detailed mathematical account of the error bound (δ) for PoP, unlike standard random projection methods where such bounds exist. This undermines confidence in the method's theoretical foundation.  
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper evaluates only on the MEN dataset without justification. Standard practice requires evaluation on multiple benchmarks (e.g., MEN, WordSim, SimLex) to demonstrate robustness across different semantic tasks.  
4. **Poor writing and organization**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142) and confusing phrasing that impedes comprehension. Key arguments are difficult to follow, particularly in the introduction and method sections.  
5. **Unjustified claims**: Some arguments lack supporting evidence. For example, the claim that "the vectors in PoP-constructed models are small" is not quantified, and the assertion that "PPMI can be applied to PoP-constructed spaces" is not sufficiently contrasted with prior methods.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Can you provide a clear, concise explanation of how PoP differs from RI/RRI in terms of both mathematical formulation and practical implementation?  
2. What is the theoretical basis for the error bound δ in PoP, and how does it compare to established random projection methods?  
3. Why was the evaluation limited to the MEN dataset? How does the method perform on other standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim, SimLex)?  
4. Could you clarify the relationship between the dimensionality of PoP index vectors (m) and the optimal number of non-zero elements (s)? Your Figure 5 suggests an optimal range, but the underlying rationale is unclear.  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper has significant potential, but its current presentation severely limits its impact. The method is promising, but the lack of clarity and theoretical grounding makes it difficult to assess its novelty and contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to improve clarity, provide better theoretical justification, and expand the evaluation to standard benchmarks. Without these changes, the paper's contribution remains unclear and its claims difficult to verify.
