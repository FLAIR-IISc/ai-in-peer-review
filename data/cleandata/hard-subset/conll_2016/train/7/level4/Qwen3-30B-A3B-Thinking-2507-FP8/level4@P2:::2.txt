

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with matrices having strictly positive expected values (E(R) > 0), enabling subsequent application of Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) weighting. The primary contribution is the demonstration that this approach allows efficient PPMI transformation on low-dimensional vectors—a capability absent in prior random projection methods (e.g., RI, RMI) due to their E(R) = 0 matrices. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of competitive performance (MEN score: 0.75) achieved with minimal computational cost compared to neural embeddings. The authors’ claim of "novel method" is less significant than the practical impact of enabling PPMI on randomized spaces.

Strengths:  
1. **Computational efficiency**: PoP constructs models in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural embeddings (e.g., 2-month training for Collobert’s model), making it viable for resource-constrained settings. This is a strong practical contribution, especially for incremental semantic space construction.  
2. **PPMI compatibility**: The core innovation—enabling PPMI application on random projection spaces—is validated through experiments (Figure 2). Unlike RI, PoP’s E(R) > 0 ensures PPMI weights are defined, yielding a MEN score of 0.75 (comparable to state-of-the-art) while avoiding the high cost of PPMI on dense count-based models.  
3. **Competitive accuracy**: PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves MEN scores (0.75) on par with neural methods (e.g., Chen & de Melo 2015) without training overhead, demonstrating that randomized methods can rival predictive models when combined with appropriate weighting.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Insufficient benchmarking**: Evaluation is limited to the MEN dataset alone. Standard practice requires multiple benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) to establish generalizability. The paper lacks justification for excluding these, weakening claims of broad applicability.  
2. **Clarity gaps in methodology**: The distinction between PoP and prior methods (e.g., RI) is obscured. For instance:  
   - Section 2.1 states PoP uses "random vectors with only positive integer values," but fails to clarify *why* this enables PPMI (vs. RI’s E(R)=0).  
   - Section 2.2’s justification for Kendall’s τb over cosine is underdeveloped (e.g., no comparison to Pearson on PoP+PPMI spaces before Figure 2).  
   The paper should explicitly contrast PoP’s E(R) > 0 with RI’s E(R)=0 in the method section to resolve reviewer confusion.  
3. **Theoretical underpinnings**: The paper acknowledges the lack of δ (error bound) analysis (Section 2.3), which is critical for randomized algorithms. While citing prior works (e.g., Johnson-Lindenstrauss), it does not derive or bound δ for PoP, making the efficiency claims less rigorous. The claim that "performance improves with m" (Figure 4) lacks theoretical support.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Why is evaluation restricted to MEN? Please report results on at least one additional standard benchmark (e.g., WordSim353) to validate generalizability.  
2. Clarify how PoP’s E(R) > 0 *specifically* enables PPMI (vs. RI’s E(R)=0). Provide a concise mathematical comparison in Section 2.1.  
3. The paper states PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieves 0.75 MEN score (Section 3.2), but Figure 2 shows 0.75 for PoP+PPMI+Pearson *and* dense PPMI. Please specify if PoP+PPMI+Pearson matches dense PPMI in performance *without* SVD truncation (as dense PPMI typically requires it).  

Additional Comments:  
- Grammar/typos: "To a large extend" (Line 86) → "To a large extent"; "such PPMI" (Line 142) → "such as PPMI."  
- The claim "PoP outperforms RI" (Section 3.1) is misleading: PoP+τb outperforms RI, but PoP+cosine (not used) would not. The paper should clarify similarity measures per method.  
- Figure 3’s histogram labels are inconsistent (e.g., "POP" vs. "PoP" in Figure 3d/e).  
- The paper’s focus on "incremental" construction is underemphasized; highlight how PoP’s low dimensionality (e.g., m=100–5000) enables true incremental updates vs. batch methods.
