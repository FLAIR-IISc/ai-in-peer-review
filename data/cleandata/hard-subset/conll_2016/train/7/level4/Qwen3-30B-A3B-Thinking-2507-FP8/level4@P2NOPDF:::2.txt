

The paper introduces a "positive-only projection" (PoP) method for word embeddings, leveraging a random projection matrix with a positive expected value to enable PPMI application (which typically requires non-negative matrices) while maintaining computational efficiency. However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its contribution and validity.

The primary concern is the lack of clear differentiation between PoP and existing random projection techniques. The authors fail to articulate how PoP's specific properties—beyond the positive expectation—yield the claimed advantages (e.g., PPMI compatibility). Without this distinction, it remains unclear whether the proposed method delivers novel benefits or merely repackages established approaches. This ambiguity permeates the entire argument, making it difficult to assess the technical merits.

The paper's presentation further hinders comprehension. The introduction and subsequent reasoning are disjointed, with logical gaps that disrupt the reader's understanding. Crucially, key arguments lack support:  
- Line 114 begins with "In addition" without context, weakening its linkage to prior claims.  
- Line 137 opens with "Since," implying an unsupported causal relationship.  
- Line 154 starts with "Thus," drawing a conclusion not sufficiently grounded in preceding evidence.  
These transitional flaws, compounded by grammatical errors (e.g., "To a large extend" at line 86; "such PPMI" at line 142), signal poor editing and undermine the paper’s credibility.

The authors’ justification for evaluating only on the MEN dataset is inadequate. Standard practice in word embedding research requires benchmarking across multiple datasets (e.g., MEN, WordSim, SimLex, RareWords) to establish robustness. Presenting a novel method with evaluation limited to a single dataset—without compelling rationale—renders the results inconclusive and fails to demonstrate general applicability. This omission significantly diminishes the paper’s contribution.

While the authors’ technical focus on random projections is reasonable, the paper assumes excessive prior knowledge. The logic behind the research direction is opaque to readers with standard vector space modeling experience (a common background in the field), violating the expectation that papers should be self-contained. The methodological rationale should be accessible to a broad audience, not restricted to specialists in random projections.

In summary, the paper’s core innovation is obscured by unclear methodology, unsupported arguments, and insufficient evaluation. The grammatical errors and disjointed presentation further detract from its scholarly value. While the PoP concept holds potential, its current formulation does not advance the field meaningfully. The authors must substantially revise the paper to:  
1) Explicitly contrast PoP with prior work and justify its advantages;  
2) Strengthen arguments with rigorous support and eliminate unsupported transitions;  
3) Evaluate on multiple standard benchmarks;  
4) Correct all grammatical and stylistic errors.  

Given these fundamental issues, the paper is not suitable for publication in its current form. I recommend rejection with a clear path for resubmission after major revisions to another venue.
