

Review:  
The paper proposes a method that trains significantly faster than neural networks while preserving key distributional properties during dimensionality reduction, which constitutes a compelling advantage. However, the necessity of requiring vectors to be transformable via PPMI (Pointwise Mutual Information) is not adequately justified. Crucially, the work lacks direct comparisons to relevant prior approaches, undermining the claimed performance gains.  

Specific concerns include:  
- **Kendall’s tau definition (p. 3)**: The formula provided diverges from the standard definition; its origin and rationale must be clarified to ensure methodological rigor.  
- **Spearman correlation (p. 3)**: The use of Kendall’s tau in the method description is inconsistent with its standard application in semantic tasks (and the authors’ own evaluation practice), warranting justification.  
- **Evaluation datasets**: While novel datasets are explored, results on established semantic relatedness benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999) are omitted. Including these would strengthen comparability with the broader NLP community.  
- **Figure 1**: Only two lines are visible, yet the caption references a third line; this discrepancy requires resolution.  
- **Typos**: "large extend" (p. 3) should be corrected to "large extent."  

The absence of direct comparisons to related work is the most significant limitation. For instance, the method’s speed and preservation claims cannot be contextualized without benchmarking against established dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA, t-SNE) or recent distributional methods. Without such comparisons, the contribution’s novelty and practical value remain unclear. The authors should explicitly address this gap by adding comparative experiments and discussing how their approach differs from or improves upon prior work.  

Additionally, the motivation for PPMI-based transformation lacks depth. Why is this specific transformation essential for the method’s success? Alternative transformations (e.g., SVD, random projections) could be explored to demonstrate necessity. The current justification appears tautological, as the method’s design inherently depends on PPMI without explaining *why* this dependency is advantageous over other approaches.  

The paper’s technical claims would benefit from greater precision. For example, the assertion that "some distributional properties are preserved" requires quantification—e.g., which properties (e.g., pairwise similarity, cluster structure) and how they are measured. Without this, the claim remains vague.  

In summary, while the training speed advantage is noteworthy, the work’s impact is diminished by insufficient justification for key design choices and the lack of rigorous comparative analysis. Addressing these issues—particularly adding direct comparisons to related work and clarifying the role of PPMI—would substantially strengthen the contribution. The current version does not sufficiently demonstrate how the proposed method advances the state of the art.
