

# Review for "Random Positive-Only Projections: PPMI-Enabled Incremental Semantic Space Construction"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with expected value > 0 (unlike previous methods with E(R)=0). The primary contribution is enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based embeddings, which was previously impossible due to the E(R)=0 constraint of prior methods. The second contribution is demonstrating that PoP combined with PPMI achieves competitive results (0.75 average score on MEN relatedness test) with dramatically reduced computational requirements compared to neural methods. The third contribution is providing insights into the relationship between vector distribution properties and appropriate similarity measures (Kendall's τb for non-Gaussian spaces, Pearson's r for PPMI-transformed spaces).

## Strengths
1. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The paper identifies and solves a fundamental limitation in existing random projection methods (E(R)=0 preventing PPMI application), which is a significant technical insight. The authors properly contextualize this within the broader literature on random projection methods and weighting techniques.

2. **Compelling Empirical Results**: The paper demonstrates that PoP+PPMI achieves state-of-the-art performance (0.75 on MEN) while being orders of magnitude more efficient than neural methods. The computational efficiency claim is well-supported by the paper's assertion that models can be built in "seconds or minutes" rather than "hours, days or months."

3. **Insightful Analysis of Similarity Measures**: The paper provides a thoughtful analysis of why Kendall's τb is appropriate for non-Gaussian spaces produced by PoP, while Pearson's correlation becomes more suitable after PPMI transformation. This theoretical justification strengthens the methodological contribution.

## Weaknesses
1. **Poor Presentation and Clarity**: The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous grammatical errors, unclear explanations, and poor organization. Key differences from prior work (e.g., RI) are not clearly articulated, making it challenging to assess the novelty. For example, the distinction between PoP and RI is obscured by technical jargon without clear comparative explanations.

2. **Inadequate Evaluation**: The paper evaluates only on the MEN dataset, which is insufficient for a method claiming to be competitive with state-of-the-art approaches. Standard practice requires evaluation on multiple benchmarks (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999, RareWords) to demonstrate robustness across different semantic relationships.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: Despite claiming to be a random projection method, the paper provides no theoretical bounds for the error δ (the difference between the optimum result and PoP's result). This is a significant gap for a method based on random projections, as theoretical guarantees are standard in this research area.

4. **Unclear Experimental Details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about parameter selection (e.g., why m=1000, s=0.01, etc.) and how these choices affect performance. The section on parameter sensitivity (Section 3.3) is insufficiently detailed to reproduce the experiments.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can the authors provide a clear, concise explanation of how PoP differs from RI in terms of mathematical formulation and implementation, with concrete examples that illustrate the E(R)>0 advantage for PPMI application?

2. Why did the authors choose to evaluate only on the MEN dataset? What evidence do they have that their method would generalize to other semantic similarity benchmarks?

3. The paper claims PoP-constructed models can be built "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes." Can the authors provide concrete timing measurements (e.g., on standard hardware) for building models on corpora of varying sizes (e.g., 10M, 100M, 1B words)?

4. The paper states that "the PoP method makes it possible to benefit both from the high efficiency of randomized techniques as well as from the high accuracy of PPMI transformation." Can the authors provide a quantitative comparison of the computational cost (time and memory) of applying PPMI to PoP-constructed spaces versus count-based models?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting technical insight about enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based embeddings, and the empirical results are promising. However, the poor presentation, insufficient evaluation, and lack of theoretical justification significantly undermine the contribution. The authors would need to substantially improve the paper's clarity, expand the evaluation to multiple standard benchmarks, and provide theoretical justification to make this work suitable for publication at CONLL. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the understanding that a substantially revised version addressing these issues could be reconsidered.
