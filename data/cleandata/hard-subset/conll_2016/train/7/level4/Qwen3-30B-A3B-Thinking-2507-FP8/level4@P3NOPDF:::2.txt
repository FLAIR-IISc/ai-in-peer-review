

The paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) method for word embeddings, leveraging a random projection matrix with a positive expected value to enable PPMI application—unlike standard random projections with zero-mean matrices. While the computational efficiency claim is plausible, the paper's core contribution and advantages remain unclear due to significant presentation issues, warranting major revisions before consideration at CONLL.

The primary concern is the lack of clarity in differentiating PoP from existing random projection methods. The authors assert that PoP enables PPMI usage, but fail to explicitly contrast it with prior work (e.g., random projections in Mikolov et al., 2013, or similar approaches). Without a clear technical comparison—such as a table detailing matrix properties, assumptions, or PPMI compatibility—the claimed advantages appear unsubstantiated. A concise section clarifying *how* PoP’s positive expectation resolves PPMI’s non-negativity constraints (vs. standard methods) is essential.

The paper’s argumentation is also problematic. Key sections begin with weak transitional phrases ("in addition," "since," "thus"), disrupting logical flow and suggesting incomplete reasoning. For instance, the rationale for PoP’s necessity (Section 3) reads as a series of disconnected assertions rather than a cohesive proof. The introduction similarly lacks a clear motivation: it does not establish *why* PPMI compatibility is a critical gap in existing embedding methods or how PoP uniquely addresses it. The paper should open with a problem statement explicitly linking PPMI’s limitations to the need for positive projections.

Furthermore, the evaluation strategy is inadequately justified. The authors test exclusively on MEN, omitting standard benchmarks like WordSim353, SimLex-999, and RG-65. While MEN is a common dataset, single-dataset validation is insufficient for a method claiming broader utility. The paper must either: (a) justify why MEN suffices (e.g., domain-specific relevance), or (b) expand to multiple benchmarks. Without this, the results lack credibility for general embedding applications.

Minor issues compound these concerns. The English contains grammatical errors (e.g., "to a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142), and the writing occasionally lacks precision (e.g., "random projection method" without specifying whether it’s *a* or *the* method). These distract from the technical content and undermine professionalism.

Crucially, the paper assumes familiarity with random projection theory, yet fails to provide sufficient context for readers outside this niche. A brief primer on why zero-mean projections conflict with PPMI, and how PoP’s positive expectation resolves this, would make the work accessible to the broader NLP community. The current presentation demands expertise the authors do not assume, violating the principle that papers should be self-contained for field-relevant audiences.

In summary, the technical idea has potential, but the execution is flawed. The paper must: (1) clearly differentiate PoP from prior work with concrete technical contrasts; (2) restructure arguments to avoid weak transitions and build logical proofs; (3) justify the evaluation scope with either domain-specific rationale or expanded benchmarks; and (4) correct grammatical errors and improve accessibility. Without these revisions, the contribution remains ambiguous and the evaluation unconvincing.

The authors should address these points thoroughly before resubmission. As currently written, the paper does not meet CONLL’s standards for clarity, rigor, or completeness. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit to a venue better aligned with the paper’s current scope (e.g., a specialized ML or NLP workshop).
