

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a novel method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with expected value E(R) > 0 (unlike standard methods with E(R) = 0). The primary contribution is enabling PPMI weighting on projected spaces—previously impossible with random projection methods due to negative values. The second contribution is the efficient incremental construction of semantic spaces (building in seconds/minutes rather than hours/days). The third contribution is the demonstration that PoP+PPMI achieves competitive results (0.75 MEN score) without neural network training.

**Strengths**  
1. **Computational efficiency**: PoP constructs semantic spaces in seconds/minutes (vs. hours/days for neural networks), as demonstrated by the authors' claim of building models "in a matter of a few seconds or minutes." This is a significant practical contribution for resource-constrained applications, especially given the growing scale of NLP tasks. The paper correctly identifies that neural embeddings require "highly costly" training (citing Baroni et al., 2014).

2. **PPMI compatibility**: The key innovation is enabling PPMI weighting on random projection-based spaces. The authors convincingly show that PoP's E(R) > 0 allows PPMI application (unlike RI, which has E(R) = 0), making it possible to combine the efficiency of random projections with the discriminative power of PPMI. Figure 2 demonstrates PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieving 0.75 MEN score—comparable to state-of-the-art methods—while being computationally more efficient than count-based PPMI+truncation.

3. **Empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation across multiple dimensions (context window size, vector dimensionality, sparsity). Figure 4 shows PoP+PPMI+Pearson's performance improves with higher dimensionality (m ≥ 3000), and Figure 5 demonstrates the sensitivity to non-zero elements in index vectors. The authors also report an improved score of 0.78 with additional tuning (Section 3.3), showing robustness.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of direct comparison to related work**: The paper claims PoP "yields competitive results" but provides no direct comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., GloVe, FastText, or recent PPMI-based approaches like Levy & Goldberg, 2014). Figure 1 compares only PoP, RI, and count-based models, omitting key baselines. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether PoP's 0.75 MEN score is truly competitive (e.g., GloVe achieves 0.77 on MEN).

2. **Misuse of Kendall's τb**: The authors define Kendall's τb using a non-standard formula (Equation 4), which differs from the standard definition. This is problematic because Kendall's τb is a rank correlation coefficient, but the authors use it as a similarity measure (like cosine), which is not conventional. The paper should either justify their variant or use the standard Spearman correlation (which they employ in evaluation).

3. **Non-standard evaluation dataset**: The MEN test is used, but the paper does not report results on more common benchmarks like SimLex-999 or WordSim-353, which are standard for semantic relatedness tasks. The authors claim MEN is "large" and "public domain," but omitting widely accepted benchmarks weakens their claim of competitiveness.

4. **Figure 1 ambiguity**: The paper states there are three lines in Figure 1 (PoP, RI, count-based), but only two lines are visible. This omission makes it difficult to assess the claimed "large margin" by which PoP outperforms others.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please provide direct comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., GloVe, FastText, or PPMI+truncation) on standard benchmarks (SimLex-999, WordSim-353), not just MEN. Without these, it's unclear whether PoP's 0.75 MEN score is truly competitive.

2. Justify the non-standard definition of Kendall's τb in Equation 4. Why is this variant appropriate for semantic similarity? If you intend to use standard Kendall's τb, please correct the formula.

3. Explain why PoP+PPMI+Pearson (0.75 MEN) outperforms PoP+Kendall (0.65 MEN) despite the authors' claim that τb is "a reasonable candidate for measuring similarities" in non-Gaussian spaces. Does this contradict your theoretical justification?

4. Clarify the missing line in Figure 1. Is it due to an error in figure generation or omission of a baseline?
