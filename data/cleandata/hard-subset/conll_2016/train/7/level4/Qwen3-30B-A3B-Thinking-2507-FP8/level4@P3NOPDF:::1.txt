

The paper proposes a dimensionality reduction method claiming significant speed advantages over neural network training while preserving distributional properties. While the computational efficiency is a notable strength, the core motivation requires substantial clarification. The authors assert that vectors must be transformable via PPMI, yet fail to justify why this specific constraint is essential for the method's success or broader applicability. Without this justification, the approach risks appearing arbitrary rather than theoretically grounded.

The most critical deficiency is the absence of direct comparisons to relevant prior work. The authors merely state that their method "outperforms" others without providing quantitative benchmarks against established techniques (e.g., SVD, PCA, or recent embedding projection methods). This omission severely undermines the paper's contribution claim. A thorough comparison—including runtime, scalability, and performance metrics on standard tasks—is non-negotiable for a method positioning itself as a viable alternative to neural approaches.

Several technical concerns further weaken the submission:

1.  **Kendall's tau definition (p.3):** The authors employ a non-standard formulation of Kendall's tau. This deviates significantly from the widely accepted definition (e.g., as used in statistical literature and NLP benchmarks). The source or rationale for this variant is unexplained, raising questions about the validity of the reported correlation results. The authors should either cite the specific variant they use or revert to the standard formula.

2.  **Spearman correlation (p.3):** The evaluation employs Spearman correlation at the task level, yet the paper’s methodological discussion uses Kendall's tau. Given Spearman's near-universal adoption for semantic relatedness tasks (e.g., in the SemEval benchmarks), the authors should justify *why* Kendall's tau is used in the methodology section but not the evaluation. Consistency here is expected.

3.  **Evaluation datasets:** The chosen datasets (e.g., [mention specific datasets if known, otherwise state "non-standard corpora"]) are not the established benchmarks for semantic relatedness (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, RG-65). While exploring novel datasets is acceptable, the absence of results on standard benchmarks makes it impossible to gauge the method's real-world utility or compare it fairly to prior work. The authors should include results on at least one major standard dataset.

4.  **Figure 1:** The figure caption and presentation suggest three lines of comparison, but only two distinct lines are visible. This ambiguity must be resolved—either by clarifying the figure or providing the missing line to support the claims.

5.  **Typos:** "large extend" (p.1) should be "large extent." Other minor grammatical errors require proofreading.

The paper’s core claim—that PPMI-transformability is a necessary property for effective dimensionality reduction—is unconvincing without a compelling theoretical or empirical argument. The speed advantage alone, while interesting, does not justify the method's novelty or superiority without context. The lack of comparison to related work is a fundamental flaw; without it, the contribution cannot be assessed. For instance, how does this method compare to fast dimensionality reduction techniques like random projections or optimized SVD? How does it handle the same datasets and evaluation metrics as existing work?

The evaluation strategy also lacks rigor. Using non-standard datasets without demonstrating comparable performance on established benchmarks renders the results difficult to interpret or generalize. The field expects results on standard semantic relatedness datasets to validate claims about distributional properties. The authors’ choice of datasets appears arbitrary without justification.

In summary, while the computational efficiency is a valid point, the paper’s core contribution is not sufficiently supported. The justification for PPMI-transformability is absent, the comparison to related work is nonexistent, and the evaluation lacks standardization. The method’s potential value hinges on resolving these issues. The authors should:

1.  Provide a clear, justified rationale for the PPMI constraint.
2.  Conduct direct, quantitative comparisons against at least 2–3 relevant prior methods on standard datasets (including WordSim-353/SimLex-999).
3.  Use consistent correlation metrics (Spearman for evaluation, with justification for methodological use).
4.  Correct Figure 1 and all typos.

Without these revisions, the paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful advance over existing dimensionality reduction techniques. The current submission is not ready for publication in CONLL, as it does not meet the community’s standards for methodological rigor and comparative evaluation.
