

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-Only Projection (PoP), a method for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with a positive expected value (E(R) > 0) instead of the conventional E(R) = 0. This enables application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces, which is infeasible with prior random projection methods. The authors claim PoP-constructed models combined with PPMI achieve MEN test scores of 0.75, comparable to state-of-the-art methods but with significantly lower computational cost.

Main contributions (as assessed by reviewer):
1. The compatibility of PoP with PPMI weighting, enabling efficient transformation of random projection spaces into discriminative semantic spaces (a key technical contribution).
2. The computational efficiency of PoP (building models in seconds/minutes vs. hours/days for neural methods), demonstrated through empirical results.

Strengths:
1. The PoP method successfully enables PPMI application to random projection spaces—a technical insight not present in prior methods like RI or RMI (which use E(R)=0 matrices). This is evidenced by the paper’s clear demonstration that RI-induced vectors have undefined PPMI weights due to zero column sums (Section 2.3), while PoP vectors maintain positive sums (Equation 3).
2. The empirical results for PoP+PPMI+Pearson achieve MEN scores of 0.75 (Section 3.2), matching neural methods while requiring minimal computation (e.g., models built in seconds vs. months for neural embeddings, as cited from Baroni et al. 2014). This demonstrates tangible efficiency gains.
3. The method’s incremental construction via random projections (Section 2.1) is well-justified for scalability, with dimensionality (m) directly controlling model capacity. The performance plateau at m=1000 (Figure 4) validates practical applicability.

Weaknesses:
1. The core novelty claim lacks sufficient differentiation from prior work. The paper fails to clearly articulate *how* PoP differs from existing methods (e.g., RI or RMI) beyond the E(R) > 0 condition. For instance, Section 1 states PoP is "similar to RI" but does not explain why the E(R) > 0 change fundamentally alters outcomes (e.g., why PPMI becomes feasible). This undermines the contribution’s significance.
2. Critical arguments are unsupported or poorly justified. Key claims lack evidence: (a) "To a large extend, the need for such transformations arises..." (Line 114) is presented as fact without data; (b) "Since the method is based on random projections..." (Line 137) assumes efficiency without quantification; (c) "Thus, instead of hours..." (Line 154) cites Baroni et al. 2014 without contextualizing PoP’s computational savings. These weaken the technical rigor.
3. Evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. The paper evaluates *only* on the MEN test (Section 3.1), omitting standard benchmarks like WordSim-353, SimLex-999, or RG-65. The authors provide no justification for this limitation, despite these being standard in semantic similarity literature (e.g., Levy et al. 2015, Tsvetkov et al. 2015). This raises concerns about generalizability.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims PoP is "similar to RI" (Section 1) but does not quantify how the E(R) > 0 condition changes the *mathematical properties* of the projection (e.g., distortion bounds, convergence). How does PoP’s distribution (Equation 2) differ from RI’s Gaussian distribution in terms of error guarantees (δ in Section 2.3)?
2. The MEN score of 0.75 (Section 3.2) is comparable to neural methods, but the paper omits results on other benchmarks. Please provide MEN results for the baselines (RI, count-based) to contextualize PoP’s improvement, and include at least one additional benchmark (e.g., WordSim-353) in the revision.
