

The paper introduces a positive-only projection (PoP) method for word embeddings, a random projection technique using a matrix with a positive expected value. The authors claim this enables the use of PPMI (which requires a non-zero expected value) and highlights computational efficiency as a key advantage. However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its lack of clarity, which undermines the core contributions. Specifically:

1.  **Unclear Novelty:** The paper fails to articulate the key distinction between PoP and prior random projection methods. Without this, it remains unclear whether the claimed advantages (e.g., enabling PPMI application) are valid or merely restatements of existing approaches.
2.  **Poor Exposition:** The argument flow is difficult to follow from the introduction onward. Key concepts are introduced without sufficient context or motivation, making the paper challenging to parse.
3.  **Unsupported Claims:** Several arguments lack logical support due to poor sentence structure:
    *   Line 114: "In addition" begins a sentence without preceding context.
    *   Line 137: "Since" begins a sentence, implying a premise that isn't established.
    *   Line 154: "Thus" begins a sentence, presenting a conclusion without prior justification.
4.  **Insufficient Self-Containment:** While I have experience with vector space modeling, the paper assumes prior, specialized knowledge of random projections that isn't adequately explained. A paper should be accessible to readers with standard field experience, not requiring deep, niche expertise.
5.  **Grammatical Errors:** The paper contains multiple grammatical mistakes (e.g., "To a large extend" on line 86, "such PPMI" on line 142), which further impede comprehension.

Additionally, the evaluation is severely limited. The authors report results solely on the MEN dataset, despite the existence of standard benchmarks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999). Evaluating a novel method on only one dataset without justification is insufficient; a comprehensive assessment across multiple established benchmarks is expected.

The core technical claims regarding PoP's relationship to PPMI and random projections are interesting but remain unconvincing due to the paper's presentation flaws. The method's potential merits are obscured by unclear explanations, unsupported reasoning, and poor writing. While the computational efficiency claim might hold, the paper does not demonstrate it convincingly or distinguish the method meaningfully from existing work.

I recommend the authors substantially revise the paper to:
*   Clearly define the novelty of PoP relative to prior random projection methods.
*   Reconstruct the argument flow for logical clarity, eliminating unsupported transitions.
*   Provide a self-contained explanation of the technical rationale for the approach.
*   Correct all grammatical errors.
*   Evaluate the method on multiple standard lexical similarity benchmarks.

Without these significant improvements, the paper's contribution remains unclear and its claims unsubstantiated. I therefore recommend rejection. The authors should address these fundamental issues before resubmitting to a different venue.
