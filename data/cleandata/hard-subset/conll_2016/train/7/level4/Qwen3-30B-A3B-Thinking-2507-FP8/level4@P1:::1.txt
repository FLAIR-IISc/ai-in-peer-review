

Summary: The paper introduces Positive-only Projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0 rather than the standard E(R) = 0. This key modification enables the application of weighting techniques like PPMI to the resulting spaces, which previous random projection methods couldn't support. The authors claim their approach achieves competitive performance (0.75 average score on MEN relatedness test) while being significantly more computationally efficient than neural network approaches.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. PoP enables the application of weighting techniques like PPMI to random projection-based semantic spaces—a capability missing in previous methods due to their E(R) = 0 constraint.
2. The method offers dramatic computational efficiency, building semantic spaces in seconds or minutes rather than hours, days, or months required for neural network training.
3. The approach achieves competitive performance on semantic relatedness tasks without requiring neural network training.

Strengths:
1. The computational efficiency claim is compellingly demonstrated. The authors show PoP can build models in seconds/minutes versus hours/days for neural networks, a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.
2. The insight that PoP enables PPMI weighting on random projection spaces is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in previous random projection approaches.
3. The results (0.75 average score on MEN) are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating that the approach can achieve strong performance without neural network training.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks direct comparisons to related work. While the authors claim PoP outperforms RI and count-based models, there are no comparisons to other random projection methods or to state-of-the-art approaches like neural embeddings or PPMI-weighted count-based models.
2. The use of Kendall's τb as the similarity measure is problematic. The paper provides a non-standard definition of τb (not matching the original formula) and fails to justify why it's superior to the standard Spearman's correlation used in semantic similarity tasks.
3. The evaluation datasets are not the standard ones preferred by the NLP community for semantic relatedness (e.g., no results on SimLex-999 or WordSim353), limiting the comparability of the results.
4. Figure 1 appears to show only two lines (RI and count-based models), but the authors claim to have three (including PoP). Without the PoP line visible, it's impossible to assess their performance claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify the definition of Kendall's τb used in the paper. The formula provided differs from the standard definition and isn't properly justified for this context.
2. Why did you choose Kendall's τb instead of the standard Spearman's correlation for semantic similarity assessment, which you use in your evaluation?
3. Could you provide results on standard semantic relatedness datasets like SimLex-999 or WordSim353 to better contextualize your performance claims?
4. Please clarify the missing line in Figure 1 and provide the PoP performance data to enable proper assessment of your method's relative performance.

Minor corrections:
- "large extend" should be "large extent"
- The paper's claim about "transforming vectors onto spaces that are discriminative for semantic similarity assessments" is not adequately demonstrated through direct comparisons to other methods.
