

Summary of the paper: The authors introduce positive-only projection (PoP), a novel technique for constructing semantic spaces using random projections with E(R) > 0, differing from previous methods that use matrices with E(R) = 0. The method enables application of positive pointwise mutual information (PPMI) weighting to the resulting low-dimensional spaces, achieving competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN relatedness test) with significantly lower computational requirements than neural network approaches.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. PoP provides a computationally efficient method for semantic space construction that requires only seconds or minutes to build models, as opposed to hours or days for neural network training.
2. The PoP method preserves the distributional properties of the original space (unlike Gaussian-based random projections), enabling application of weighting techniques like PPMI that are not applicable to previous random projection methods.
3. By constructing spaces at low dimensionality (a few hundred dimensions), PoP allows fast application of PPMI weighting, which would be computationally prohibitive on high-dimensional count-based models.

Strengths:
1. The computational efficiency of PoP is a substantial contribution, as it enables semantic space construction at a fraction of the time required for neural embedding methods, making it practical for resource-constrained applications.
2. The compatibility with PPMI weighting represents a meaningful technical advance, as it allows leveraging the discriminatory power of PPMI without the computational burden of applying it to high-dimensional count-based models.
3. The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance (0.75 average on MEN) compared to state-of-the-art methods, with the added benefit of significantly reduced computational requirements.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks direct comparison to related work, making it difficult to assess the novelty and relative performance of PoP compared to existing random projection methods.
2. The definition of Kendall's τb used in the paper deviates from the standard formula, and the authors do not provide justification for this variant or references to its use in the literature.
3. The evaluation uses non-standard datasets (MEN relatedness test) instead of the more commonly accepted benchmarks in semantic similarity assessment, limiting the generalizability of the claims.
4. Figure 1 appears to show only two lines (count-based and RI) rather than the three claimed (count-based, RI, and PoP), making it difficult to assess the comparative performance claims.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify the rationale behind the modified definition of Kendall's τb and provide references for this variant, as it differs significantly from the standard formula used in the literature.
2. Why was the MEN relatedness test selected over more standard semantic similarity benchmarks like WordSim353 or SimLex-999, which are commonly used in the field?
3. Please provide a direct comparison to related work, including a clear discussion of how PoP differs from and improves upon existing random projection methods like RI, RRI, ISA, and RMI.
4. The paper claims vectors must be transformable with PPMI to be useful, but does not convincingly establish why this is a critical advantage over other methods that don't support PPMI. Please clarify this important point in your revision.
