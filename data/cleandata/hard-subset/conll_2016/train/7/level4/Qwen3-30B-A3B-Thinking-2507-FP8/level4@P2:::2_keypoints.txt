- Summary: Introduces PoP—random projections with E(R)>0—to enable PPMI on low-dimensional vectors; shows MEN=0.75 with low compute; novelty is practical (PPMI compatibility) more than methodological.

- Strength: Computational efficiency—models built in seconds/minutes vs hours/days, suitable for resource-constrained and incremental use.

- Strength: PPMI compatibility—E(R)>0 allows PPMI on randomized spaces, validated experimentally (Figure 2), avoiding dense-count PPMI costs.

- Strength: Competitive accuracy—PoP+PPMI+Pearson attains MEN=0.75, comparable to some neural embeddings without expensive training.

- Weakness: Insufficient benchmarking—evaluation limited to MEN; missing other standard datasets (e.g., WordSim353, SimLex-999) for generalizability.

- Weakness: Method clarity gaps—paper inadequately explains why positive-only projections enable PPMI vs RI (E(R)=0) and gives weak justification for Kendall’s τb over cosine.

- Weakness: Missing theoretical guarantees—no δ/error-bound analysis for PoP; claims about performance scaling with m lack theoretical support.

- Question: Why restrict evaluation to MEN? Please report results on at least one additional standard benchmark (e.g., WordSim353).

- Question: Provide a concise mathematical explanation of how PoP’s E(R)>0 specifically enables PPMI compared to RI’s E(R)=0.

- Question: Does PoP+PPMI+Pearson truly match dense PPMI performance without SVD truncation, as Figure 2 seems to imply?

- Comment: Fix grammar/typos (e.g., "To a large extend" → "To a large extent"; "such PPMI" → "such as PPMI").

- Comment: Clarify the claim "PoP outperforms RI" by specifying which similarity measure is used for each method.

- Comment: Correct figure/label inconsistencies (e.g., "POP" vs "PoP" in Figure 3) and emphasize PoP’s incremental-update advantages due to low dimensionality.
