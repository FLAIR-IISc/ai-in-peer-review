This paper introduces a novel approach for multi-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) by incorporating features derived from Wikipedia. The method employs a cross-lingual Wikifier to map phrases in a target language to corresponding English Wikipedia articles, from which features are then extracted for the NER model. The experiments effectively demonstrate that these new features improve performance in both monolingual tasks and, more significantly, in the challenging direct transfer setting where a model trained on English is evaluated on a different target language.

This is a well-executed paper with several strengths. The primary contribution is the introduction of a novel and effective feature set for NER derived from a cross-lingual knowledge base, which is a valuable addition to the field. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, providing convincing evidence of the features' utility across various settings. The analysis of the features' impact on low-resource languages and languages with non-Latin scripts is particularly insightful and addresses a critical area in multilingual NLP.

While the results are promising, there are a few points that could be clarified or explored further to strengthen the paper:

1.  **Handling of Entities Not in Wikipedia:** The method's reliance on Wikipedia raises a question about its performance on named entities that do not have a Wikipedia entry. An analysis of how these "out-of-Wikipedia" entities are affected by the proposed features would be highly beneficial. For instance, does the model's performance on this specific subset of entities degrade or remain unchanged? This would help clarify the scope and potential limitations of the approach.

2.  **Impact of Wikifier Errors:** The model's performance is inherently dependent on the accuracy of the upstream cross-lingual Wikifier. As this component is a potential source of error, it would be valuable to understand the impact of Wikification errors on the final NER predictions. An error analysis that quantifies how often Wikification failures lead to downstream NER errors would provide a more complete picture of the system's robustness.

3.  **Regularization for Direct Transfer:** The paper notes a performance degradation in the direct transfer setting for Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are included, suggesting the model may be overfitting to source-language lexical items. This observation raises an interesting possibility for improvement. Have the authors considered applying differential regularization to the various feature classes? For example, by more strongly penalizing the weights of lexical features, the model could be encouraged to rely more on the cross-lingually robust Wikipedia features, potentially improving transfer performance for distant language pairs.
