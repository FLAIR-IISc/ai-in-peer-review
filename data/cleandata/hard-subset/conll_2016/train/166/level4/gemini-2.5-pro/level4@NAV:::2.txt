This paper investigates the direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models across languages by leveraging a state-of-the-art cross-lingual wikification system. From a conceptual standpoint, the work is more incremental than innovative, as it does not introduce a fundamentally new methodology. Instead, its primary contribution lies in effectively integrating two established lines of research: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging) and (2) recent advancements in cross-lingual wikification. Despite this, the paper is commendable for its coherent narrative, clear presentation, and a robust set of experiments that provide convincing empirical support for its conclusions. Nevertheless, I have several suggestions regarding the work's presentation and experimental setup.

**Related Work:** The related work section would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its relationship to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Furthermore, it is crucial to more explicitly articulate the distinctions between the proposed method and other NER systems that utilize Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge. While these differences are mentioned in the text, they should be emphasized more directly to help readers better contextualize the contribution.

The clarity of **Section 3.3** could be significantly improved with the addition of a running example. The current description of the modified Tsai and Roth model is not entirely transparent, and an illustrative example would help clarify its mechanics.

While the authors provide a rationale for excluding Part-of-Speech (POS) tags as features, the inclusion of experiments incorporating them (akin to the setup in Tackstrom et al.) would be highly informative. This would offer readers empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of such features across the different languages under investigation, particularly for those with available universal POS tagsets.

The authors repeatedly state that their approach is orthogonal to those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) and can be combined. To substantiate this claim and enrich the discussion, it would be beneficial to present preliminary results from a hybrid model on a subset of languages. Similarly, to better contextualize the performance and demonstrate the value of the wikification-based method, a comparison against a strong projection-based baseline is warranted.

The paper notes some intriguing cross-lingual transfer results, such as Dutch being the optimal source language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba. Is this merely a statistical artifact, or is there a more profound linguistic or data-driven reason? A more in-depth discussion in the analysis section exploring these specific findings would be a valuable addition.

The improvements reported in **Table 4**, while present, appear marginal and are not consistent across all experimental conditions. To bolster the authors' claims, it is recommended to include statistical significance tests for these results.

**Minor Comments:**
*   **Sect. 2.1:** The discussion on projection should acknowledge methods that do not rely on parallel corpora, such as those using bilingual semantic spaces (e.g., Peirsman and Pado, NAACL 2009; Vulic and Moens, EMNLP 2013). These approaches offer broader applicability, especially for low-resource languages.
*   The manuscript contains several typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "as a the base model" in the first sentence of Section 3; "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." on page 3; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on page 8). A thorough proofreading is necessary to improve readability.
