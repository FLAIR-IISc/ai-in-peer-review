### Summary

This paper presents a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) by generating language-independent features from a cross-lingual wikifier. The core idea is to ground all n-grams in a given text to English Wikipedia entries, regardless of the source language. The Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types associated with these linked entries are then used as features for a standard NER model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in two settings: (1) enhancing monolingual NER systems by adding these features, and (2) a direct transfer setting, where an NER model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied directly to a low-resource target language. The experiments, conducted across nine languages including several with non-Latin scripts, show significant performance improvements over strong baselines, particularly in the direct transfer scenario.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Feature Generation Method for Cross-Lingual NER:** The primary contribution is the method of using a cross-lingual wikifier to generate rich, language-independent features for NER. By treating wikification as an upstream process that provides signals for every n-gram (rather than a downstream task applied only to recognized entities), the authors create a powerful delexicalized feature set based on Wikipedia's structured knowledge (categories and FreeBase types). This approach cleverly bypasses the need for parallel corpora, which are central to many cross-lingual transfer methods.
2.  **Extensive Empirical Validation on Diverse Languages:** A second major contribution is the thorough evaluation of the proposed method across nine languages. This includes standard CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) as well as a diverse set of five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), some of which use non-Latin scripts. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method significantly outperforms comparable direct transfer baselines and provides a strong analysis of how feature quality correlates with the size of the target language's Wikipedia.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Performance Gains in Direct Transfer:** The proposed method achieves substantial improvements in the challenging direct transfer setting. As shown in Table 2, adding wikifier features boosts F1 scores dramatically over a strong baseline, with gains of over 12 points for Dutch, 6 for German, and over 17 for Spanish when trained on English. The method outperforms the prior state-of-the-art direct transfer model of Täckström et al. (2012) on all comparable languages. Furthermore, it surpasses the results of Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, despite requiring fewer resources (i.e., no parallel data or interaction with native speakers).
2.  **Broad Applicability and Simplicity:** The method is elegant and widely applicable, as its only requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump. This makes it readily deployable for any of the hundreds of languages present in Wikipedia, a significant advantage over methods that rely on scarce resources like large, sentence-aligned parallel corpora. The simplicity of integrating these features into a standard NER architecture also makes the approach easy to adopt.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results and includes valuable analysis. The experiments on low-resource and non-Latin script languages are particularly insightful, demonstrating both the potential and the limitations of the approach. Section 4.4 provides a compelling analysis that directly links the quality of the wikifier features to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the number of interlanguage links to English. This thoughtful investigation adds depth to the paper and helps explain the variance in performance across languages. The exploration of multi-source training in Section 4.5 further strengthens the paper's contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Handling of Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The method's effectiveness is intrinsically tied to the coverage of Wikipedia. Named entities that are not prominent enough to have a Wikipedia entry (e.g., names of local businesses, non-public figures, or emerging entities) will not benefit from the proposed features. While the model can still rely on base and gazetteer features, it would lose its primary source of cross-lingual signal for these entities. The paper does not include an analysis of the model's performance on out-of-Wikipedia entities, which represents a potentially significant failure mode.
2.  **Lack of Error Analysis regarding the Wikifier:** The NER model is the second step in a pipeline that begins with the cross-lingual wikifier. As such, its performance is highly dependent on the wikifier's accuracy. Errors made by the wikifier (e.g., linking a phrase to the wrong Wikipedia entry or failing to link it at all) will propagate and could lead to incorrect NER predictions. An analysis of this error propagation—for instance, a breakdown of NER errors that are directly attributable to wikification mistakes—is missing and would provide a more complete picture of the model's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper notes the poor performance of the direct transfer model on Bengali and Tamil when lexical features from the English-trained model are included (the "+Wikifier" F1 scores are much lower than the "Wikifier only" scores for BN and TA in Table 2). This suggests the model becomes over-reliant on English-specific lexical features that do not generalize. Have the authors considered methods to differentially regularize feature classes during training? For example, could one down-weight the lexical features to encourage the model to rely more on the language-independent wikifier features, potentially improving transfer to typologically distant languages with different scripts?
