This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from a source text in any language to corresponding English Wikipedia entries. The categories and FreeBase types associated with these entries are then used as features for an NER model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of these features in both monolingual and direct transfer settings, where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to various target languages.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective set of language-independent features for NER derived from cross-lingual wikification. By mapping phrases to a common, language-agnostic knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the model can access rich semantic type information (via FreeBase and Wikipedia categories) that is directly transferable across languages.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of nine languages, including high-resource CoNLL languages and low-resource languages with both Latin and non-Latin scripts. The experiments convincingly show that the proposed features significantly improve performance over strong baselines in both monolingual and direct-transfer scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **The proposed wikification-based features are highly effective and provide a strong delexicalized signal for NER.** The core idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier to generate features is powerful, as it abstracts away from surface lexical forms. The experimental results strongly support this; the "+Wikifier" rows in Table 2 show consistent and often dramatic F1 score improvements across all nine languages for both monolingual and direct-transfer settings. For instance, in the direct transfer setting, the features add over 12 F1 points for Dutch, German, and Spanish.
2.  **The paper presents a comprehensive and convincing evaluation on a wide and diverse range of languages.** The authors validate their approach not only on standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) but also on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), including two with non-Latin scripts. This thorough evaluation demonstrates the generalizability of the method and its applicability to challenging, real-world scenarios, strengthening the paper's claims. The model significantly outperforms comparable direct-transfer baselines and even surpasses a method that uses more resources for the low-resource languages.
3.  **The paper includes insightful analysis that enhances the practical value of the work.** The authors conduct an ablation study (Table 3) that clearly links the quality of the wikifier features to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with English Wikipedia. This analysis helps explain performance variations and provides a useful heuristic for predicting the method's effectiveness on new languages. Furthermore, the exploration of multi-source training (Table 4) demonstrates the potential for further performance gains by combining annotated data from multiple languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The model is inherently dependent on the coverage of Wikipedia, but the impact on out-of-Wikipedia entities is not analyzed.** The proposed features can only be generated for n-grams that can be successfully linked to a Wikipedia entry. This raises a crucial question about the model's performance on named entities that are not in Wikipedia (e.g., emerging entities, local businesses, or less-prominent individuals). An analysis breaking down performance on in-KB vs. out-of-KB entities would be necessary to fully understand the trade-offs of this approach. It is possible that the model becomes overly reliant on wikifier features and performs worse on entities that cannot be linked.
2.  **The method introduces a strong dependency on the upstream cross-lingual wikifier, yet the paper lacks an analysis of error propagation.** The entire feature generation process relies on the wikifier's accuracy. An incorrect or missed wikification will lead to noisy or missing features, which will in turn negatively affect the NER model's predictions. The paper does not provide any analysis of how wikifier errors correlate with NER errors. Quantifying this relationship would be crucial for understanding the model's failure modes and identifying areas for future improvement.
3.  **The handling of lexically dissimilar language pairs in the direct transfer setting seems suboptimal.** As shown in Table 2, including English-trained lexical features severely degrades performance on non-Latin script languages like Bengali and Tamil. The authors' proposed strategy is to simply discard all lexical features in such cases. This is a heuristic solution that requires a manual decision. A more principled approach, such as applying different regularization strengths to different feature groups (e.g., lexical vs. wikifier), could allow the model to automatically learn to down-weigh non-transferable features, resulting in a single, more robust model that does not require manual tuning for each language pair.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide an analysis of the model's performance on entities that are not present in Wikipedia? How does the introduction of wikifier features affect the recognition of these out-of-KB entities compared to a baseline that does not use them?
2.  Can you quantify the impact of wikification errors on the final NER performance? For instance, what percentage of NER errors in your direct-transfer experiments can be directly attributed to an incorrect or missing link from the wikifier?
3.  Regarding the poor performance on non-Latin script languages when including lexical features: have you considered alternative modeling approaches to automatically down-weigh these source-specific features, such as feature-group-specific regularization, rather than manually excluding them?
