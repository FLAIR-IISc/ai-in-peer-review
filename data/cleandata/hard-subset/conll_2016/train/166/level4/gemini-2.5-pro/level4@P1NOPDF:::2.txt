This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models that leverages a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea is not highly innovative, as it does not introduce a new fundamental technology, the contribution is a well-executed, incremental step that successfully combines two established research areas: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging) and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. Despite the incremental nature of the work, I found the paper to be compelling. It presents a coherent narrative supported by extensive experiments and empirical evidence, leading to convincing results. I do, however, have several suggestions for improving the presentation and strengthening the paper's contributions.

**Major Comments**

*   **Related Work:** The related work section would benefit from a more detailed description of how this work relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). The authors should also more clearly state the difference between their approach and other NER systems that rely on Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge. These distinctions, while mentioned elsewhere in the text, should be emphasized in this section to help readers better contextualize the work.

*   **Experiments with POS Tags:** The authors provide a rationale for excluding POS tags as features. However, it would be beneficial to see experiments including them, similar to the setup in Täckström et al. (2012). This would provide the reader with empirical evidence regarding the utility (or lack thereof) of such features across different languages, at least for those where universal POS tags are available.

*   **Clarity of Section 3.3:** Section 3.3 would benefit from a running example. As it stands, the description of how the edited model from Tsai and Roth is applied is not entirely clear, and an illustrative example would greatly improve comprehension.

*   **Combining with Orthogonal Approaches:** The authors mention that the approaches of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal and could be combined with their own. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include some preliminary results on a selection of languages demonstrating such a combination. This would add valuable depth to the discussion.

*   **Inclusion of a Projection Baseline:** Along the same lines, while acknowledged as an orthogonal approach, a comparison against a strong projection-based baseline would be valuable. This would further contextualize the results and more clearly demonstrate the advantages or limitations of wikification-based methods.

*   **Deeper Analysis of Results:** The paper reports some intriguing cross-lingual transfer results, such as Dutch being the best source for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba. Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or is there a deeper linguistic or data-driven reason? A paragraph or two discussing these findings in more detail would be a fascinating addition.

*   **Statistical Significance:** The improvements shown in Table 4, while positive, are modest and not observed in all scenarios. Reporting statistical significance tests for these results would help substantiate the authors' claims regarding the efficacy of their proposed enhancements.

**Minor Comments**

*   The discussion of projection in Section 2.1 could be expanded to include methods that do not require parallel data, which would broaden their applicability. Examples include the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct word alignments for transfer.

*   The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "as a the base model" in the first sentence of Section 3; "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." on Page 3; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
