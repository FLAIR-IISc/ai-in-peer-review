This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer for Named Entity Recognition (NER) by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea of marrying direct transfer with cross-lingual resources is not entirely novel, representing an incremental step rather than a breakthrough, the paper's contribution is significant. The work is well-executed, presenting a coherent narrative supported by extensive experiments and convincing empirical results. Overall, I found the paper to be a strong and valuable contribution, though I have several suggestions for improvement, primarily concerning the experimental context and presentation.

**Major Comments**

*   **Positioning and Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by more clearly situating its contributions within the existing literature.
    *   A more detailed description of how this work relates to, and differs from, that of Kazama and Torisawa (2007) is needed.
    *   Similarly, while the paper mentions other NER systems that use Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge, the distinctions could be more sharply drawn and emphasized. Highlighting these differences more explicitly would help readers better contextualize the specific contributions of this approach.

*   **Experimental Comparisons and Baselines:** The experimental section is solid but could be made more comprehensive with additional comparisons that would provide a fuller context for the results.
    *   The authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features. However, for the sake of completeness and to provide empirical evidence for this choice, it would be valuable to report results from an experiment that *does* include universal POS tags (for the languages where they are available), similar to the setup in Tackstrom et al. (2012). This would offer a clearer picture of the utility (or lack thereof) of these features in this cross-lingual transfer setting.
    *   The paper rightly notes that its approach is orthogonal to those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) and that the methods could be combined. To substantiate this claim and enrich the discussion, presenting even preliminary results from a combined model on a subset of languages would be highly beneficial.
    *   The evaluation would be more comprehensive with the inclusion of a strong projection-based baseline. This would provide a crucial point of comparison and help to better contextualize the performance of the wikification-based approach, highlighting its specific strengths and potential limitations.

*   **Clarity of Method:** The description of the adapted Tsai and Roth model in Section 3.3 is somewhat difficult to follow. Including a concrete running example would greatly enhance clarity and help the reader understand the mechanics of the proposed modifications.

*   **Analysis and Discussion:**
    *   The paper reports some intriguing cross-lingual transfer results, such as Dutch being the best source language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba. The discussion would be significantly strengthened by a deeper analysis of these findings. Are these merely statistical coincidences, or are there underlying linguistic or structural properties (e.g., in the Wikipedia graph, entity distribution) that explain these surprising pairings? A paragraph or two exploring these possibilities would be very insightful.
    *   The improvements shown in Table 4, while positive, appear to be quite small and are not consistent across all scenarios. To bolster the claims made based on these results, it is essential to report statistical significance tests. This would clarify whether the observed gains are statistically meaningful or potentially due to chance.

**Minor Comments**

*   In Section 2.1, the discussion of projection methods could be expanded to mention that some techniques do not require parallel data (e.g., those using bilingual semantic spaces, such as Peirsman and Pado, 2009; Vulic and Moens, 2013). This would offer a more complete picture of the landscape and make the model's applicability even clearer.
*   The manuscript would benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct several typos and grammatical errors. For example:
    *   Section 3, first sentence: "as a the base model"
    *   Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." (awkward phrasing)
    *   Page 8: "to disambiguate every n-grams" should be "every n-gram" or "all n-grams"
