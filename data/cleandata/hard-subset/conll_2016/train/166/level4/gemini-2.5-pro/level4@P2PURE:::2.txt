This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models, leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification system. The central idea, while effective, is not highly innovative; it does not introduce a new core technology. Rather, the contribution is largely incremental, effectively combining two established research lines: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks like NER, and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. Despite this, the paper is well-executed. It presents a coherent and clear narrative, supported by thorough experiments and convincing empirical evidence. Nevertheless, I have several suggestions for improving the presentation and strengthening the paper's contributions.

**Related Work:** The related work section should provide a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). It is also necessary to more clearly differentiate the proposed approach from other NER systems that leverage Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge. While these distinctions are mentioned in the text, they should be emphasized more explicitly in the related work section to help readers place the work in its proper context.

**Experiments and Analysis:**
*   While the authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features, it would be beneficial to report on experiments that include them, similar to the setup in Täckström et al. (2012). This would provide valuable empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of such features across different languages, at least for those where universal POS tags are available.
*   Section 3.3 would benefit significantly from a running example. The current description of the modified Tsai and Roth model is not entirely clear, and an illustrative example would greatly improve comprehension.
*   The authors repeatedly state that their approach is orthogonal to those of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012). To substantiate this claim and enrich the discussion, it would be beneficial to report even preliminary results from combining these models on a subset of languages.
*   Along the same lines, comparison against a strong projection-based baseline would be valuable. While also an orthogonal method, including such a baseline would better contextualize the results and more clearly demonstrate the specific advantages or limitations of the wikification-based transfer.
*   The paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of the cross-lingual transfer results. For instance, why is Dutch the best source language for Spanish, and Spanish the best for Yoruba? Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or are there more interesting linguistic or data-driven factors at play? A discussion of these surprising findings would be a welcome addition.
*   While the underlying idea is sound, the results in Table 4 are not entirely convincing, as they show only small improvements and not in all scenarios. Reporting statistical significance tests for these results would be crucial to substantiate the claims of improvement.

**Minor Comments:**
*   **Sec. 2.1:** The discussion of projection should acknowledge methods that do not require parallel data (e.g., Peirsman and Pado, 2009; Vulić and Moens, 2013). These approaches, which often use bilingual semantic spaces, are more broadly applicable, particularly for low-resource languages.
*   **Proofreading:** The paper requires careful proofreading to correct several grammatical errors and typos. Examples include: "as a the base model" (Sec. 3), "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." (p. 3), and "to disambiguate every n-grams" (p. 8).
