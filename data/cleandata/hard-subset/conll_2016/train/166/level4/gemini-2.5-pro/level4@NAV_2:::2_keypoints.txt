- Summary: Proposes direct-transfer NER using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features from Wikipedia, trained on source languages and applied to targets without target annotations; evaluated on nine languages including low-resource ones.

- Contribution 1: Empirically shows wikification-derived features are highly effective language-independent signals for direct-transfer NER, with significant gains over comparable methods.

- Contribution 2: Provides detailed analyses (e.g., Wikipedia size impact) and shows wikification features can modestly improve monolingual NER.

- Strength: Convincing empirical results and extensive evaluation—outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) and works on several low-resource and non-Latin-script languages.

- Strength: Well-motivated, coherent synthesis of direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification with a sound experimental setup.

- Strength: Valuable analyses and insights, including correlation between Wikipedia size and feature quality and exploration of multi-source training.

- Weakness: Insufficient contextualization with related work—differences from prior Wikipedia-based NER (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa 2007) not detailed enough.

- Weakness: Limited experimental comparisons—no strong projection-based baseline or experiments combining the features with orthogonal methods like cross-lingual clusters.

- Weakness: Lack of deeper analysis of cross-lingual transfer results—unexplained best-source/target pairings and unclear whether patterns are linguistic or statistical.

- Weakness: Description of the modified wikifier and feature generation (Section 3.3) is hard to follow; a running example is needed for clarity and reproducibility.

- Question: Have the authors tested whether the small multi-source training gains are statistically significant?

- Question: Could POS tags provide complementary signals to wikification features, and were they considered for languages with universal POS taggers?

- Question: Please clarify unexpected pairings in Figure 2 (e.g., Spanish as best source for Yoruba)—are these driven by linguistic similarity, training-data artifacts, or other factors?
