This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification system. The core idea, which combines direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks with advances in cross-lingual wikification, is not a major technological innovation. The contribution is thus primarily incremental. Despite this, the paper is well-executed, presenting a coherent narrative supported by extensive experiments and convincing empirical evidence. I have several comments and suggestions for improving the presentation of the work.

**Major Comments**

*   **Related Work**: The related work section would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Additionally, while the paper does mention differences from other NER systems that rely on Wikipedia, these distinctions should be more explicitly stated and emphasized. This would help readers better contextualize the paper's specific contributions within the existing literature.

*   **Additional Experiments**:
    *   The authors provide a rationale for excluding POS tags as features. However, reporting results from an experiment that *does* include POS tag features (similar to Täckström et al., 2012) would be valuable. Such an experiment would provide empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of these features in this cross-lingual setting, at least for the languages where universal POS tags are available.
    *   The authors correctly note that their approach is orthogonal to those of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) and could be combined. To strengthen this claim and enrich the discussion, it would be highly beneficial to report preliminary results from such a combined model, perhaps on a subset of languages.
    *   To better contextualize the results and demonstrate the specific advantages or limitations of the wikification-based method, a comparison against a strong projection-based baseline would be very informative.

*   **Clarity and Analysis**:
    *   The description of the adapted Tsai and Roth model in Section 3.3 is not entirely clear. Including a running example would significantly improve the clarity of this section and help the reader understand the model's mechanics.
    *   The results reveal some interesting language pairings, such as Dutch being the optimal source language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba. It is unclear if this is a statistical coincidence or if there are deeper linguistic or data-driven reasons. A more in-depth discussion of these cross-lingual transfer phenomena would make the analysis more compelling.
    *   The idea behind the experiment in Table 4 is sound; however, the reported improvements are marginal and not consistent across all scenarios. To substantiate the claims made based on these results, it is important to include statistical significance tests.

**Minor Comments**

*   In Section 2.1, it is worth noting that projection-based transfer can also be performed without parallel data (e.g., using bilingual semantic spaces), which increases its applicability. See, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulić and Moens (EMNLP 2013).

*   The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "as a the base model" in the first sentence of Section 3; the ungrammatical sentence on page 3 beginning "They avoid the traditional pipeline..."; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on page 8).
