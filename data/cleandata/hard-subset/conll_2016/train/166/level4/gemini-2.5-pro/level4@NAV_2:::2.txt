### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) based on a direct transfer approach. The central idea is to utilize a cross-lingual wikification system to generate language-independent features. By grounding n-grams from a text in any language to corresponding English Wikipedia entries, the system can extract features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. An NER model is then trained on one or more source languages using these features and applied directly to target languages without any target-specific annotated data. The authors perform an extensive evaluation on nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A strong empirical demonstration that features derived from a modern cross-lingual wikification system serve as highly effective language-independent signals for direct transfer NER. The approach is validated across a diverse set of languages, showing significant performance gains over comparable methods.
2.  A detailed analysis of the proposed method, investigating the impact of factors like Wikipedia size on feature quality. The work also shows that these language-independent features can provide a modest boost to monolingual NER systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Convincing Empirical Results and Extensive Evaluation:** The paper is supported by strong empirical evidence. The proposed model significantly outperforms the Täckström et al. (2012) baseline on the CoNLL languages and shows strong performance on five low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts. This comprehensive evaluation across a wide range of language families robustly supports the authors' claims about the effectiveness of wikification-based features.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Coherently Presented Approach:** Although the work combines two established research lines (direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification), the synthesis is logical and well-executed. The paper presents a clear narrative, convincingly arguing for the use of wikification as a source of delexicalized features, and the experimental setup is sound.
3.  **Valuable Analysis and Insights:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results by providing insightful analysis. Section 4.4, for example, effectively demonstrates the correlation between a language's Wikipedia size and the performance of the wikifier features, explaining performance discrepancies between high- and low-resource languages. The exploration of multi-source training further adds to the paper's depth.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Contextualization with Related Work:** The paper's relationship to prior work could be articulated more clearly. Specifically, the differences from other NER systems that use Wikipedia knowledge, such as Kazama and Torisawa (2007), are mentioned but not detailed enough. A more stressed comparison would better situate the paper's contribution within the existing literature.
2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Comparisons:** The authors note that their method is orthogonal to other techniques like projection-based transfer or using cross-lingual word clusters. To provide a more complete picture of the method's value, it would be beneficial to include a comparison against a strong projection baseline or to report preliminary results from combining the proposed features with an orthogonal approach like Täckström et al.'s clusters.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Cross-Lingual Transfer Results:** The results in Figure 2 reveal interesting language pairings (e.g., Dutch being the best source for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba). The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of why these specific pairings yield the best results. Without further analysis, it is unclear if this is due to linguistic properties, training data characteristics, or is merely a statistical artifact.
4.  **Clarity of the Wikification Feature Generation Process:** The description of the modified wikifier model in Section 3.3 is difficult to follow. A running example illustrating how features are generated and assigned to a specific token from the surrounding n-grams would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gains from multi-source training reported in Table 4 appear to be quite small and not entirely consistent across all target languages. Have you conducted statistical significance tests to confirm that these improvements are indeed meaningful?
2.  While you justify the exclusion of POS tags, it would be interesting to know if you believe they could provide a complementary signal to your wikification features, especially for the languages where universal POS taggers are available. Could you elaborate on this?
3.  Could you please clarify the findings in Figure 2? For instance, why do you think Spanish is the best source language for Yoruba? Is there a linguistic or data-driven reason for this specific cross-lingual pairing performing so well?
