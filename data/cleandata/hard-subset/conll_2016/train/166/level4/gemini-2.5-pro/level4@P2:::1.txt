### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to link n-grams in a source text, regardless of its language, to their corresponding English Wikipedia entries. From these linked entries, the model extracts features such as FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories, which are inherently language-agnostic. The authors demonstrate the utility of these "wikifier features" in two settings: (1) improving performance in standard monolingual NER, and (2) enabling a direct transfer model, where an NER system trained on a high-resource language like English can be applied directly to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in that language.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for Cross-Lingual NER:** The primary contribution is the method of using cross-lingual wikification as a source of powerful, language-independent features for NER. This reframes wikification as an upstream task that provides valuable semantic and type information, rather than a downstream task that consumes NER output.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results in a Direct Transfer Setting:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) and several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The proposed model significantly outperforms previous direct transfer methods and achieves competitive results against systems that require more resources, such as parallel corpora or native speaker interaction.

3.  **Extensive Analysis of Feature Utility:** The authors provide a valuable analysis of the factors influencing the performance of their method. They demonstrate the utility of the wikifier features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings and investigate how performance correlates with the size of a language's Wikipedia, offering important insights for applying the method to new languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Approach:** The central idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier to create a "lingua franca" of features (based on English Wikipedia) is both innovative and intuitive. By grounding words in any language to a common knowledge base, the model gains access to rich semantic information that transcends lexical and syntactic differences. The results in Table 2 clearly validate this approach, showing that adding wikifier features provides substantial gains in both monolingual (+1.5 to +4.2 F1) and direct transfer settings (+1.2 to +12.4 F1) for nearly every language.

2.  **Thorough and Wide-Ranging Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The evaluation covers nine languages with diverse characteristics, including three high-resource European languages and five low-resource languages, two of which (Bengali and Tamil) use non-Latin scripts. This diversity provides a robust test of the method's generalizability. The comparison against strong baselines, including Tä̈ckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), is compelling, showing that this method outperforms prior work in the direct transfer setting while requiring fewer resources.

3.  **Insightful Analysis, Particularly for Low-Resource Languages:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting scores and provides insightful analysis. The experiments in Section 4.4, which simulate a smaller Wikipedia by removing interlanguage links, effectively demonstrate the correlation between Wikipedia size and feature quality. Furthermore, the analysis of performance on non-Latin script languages, which reveals that lexical features can be detrimental in a direct transfer setting, is particularly interesting and provides practical guidance for applying the model in new scenarios.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Analysis on Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The method's effectiveness is predicated on the ability to link entities to Wikipedia. However, many named entities, such as newly prominent individuals, local businesses, or less-famous persons, may not have a Wikipedia page. The paper does not provide an analysis of how the model performs on these "out-of-Wikipedia" entities. This is a significant limitation, as the model would receive no signal from the wikifier for such cases, potentially leading to a high number of false negatives. An error analysis distinguishing between in-Wikipedia and out-of-Wikipedia entities would be crucial for understanding the model's practical limitations.

2.  **Unaddressed Impact of Wikifier Errors:** The NER model is positioned downstream of a cross-lingual wikifier, making its performance highly dependent on the wikifier's accuracy. An incorrect disambiguation from the wikifier (e.g., linking "Washington" the person instead of the location) would feed erroneous features into the NER model. The paper does not quantify the impact of this error propagation. It is unclear how robust the NER model is to noise from the wikifier, making it difficult to assess whether NER errors are due to the NER model itself or to upstream wikification mistakes.

3.  **Suboptimal Handling of Feature Classes in Cross-Script Scenarios:** The results for Bengali and Tamil in Table 2 show that the direct transfer model including all features performs exceptionally poorly (F1 < 7.0), while the "Wikifier only" model performs much better (F1 > 25.0). This indicates that the model, trained on English, becomes over-reliant on lexical features that are useless when transferring to a non-Latin script. The paper acknowledges this and suggests a strategy of only using wikifier features in such cases, but this is a post-hoc manual decision. A more robust model would be able to learn how to balance or regularize different feature groups automatically, rather than failing so dramatically when a feature class becomes irrelevant.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the model's performance on entities not covered by Wikipedia: could you provide an analysis of the model's recall on entities for which the wikifier returns NIL? What proportion of named entities in the test sets fall into this category, and how does this affect overall performance?

2.  The paper highlights the poor performance of the full model on non-Latin script languages in the direct transfer setting. Have you considered any mechanisms to automatically down-weight or regularize feature groups (e.g., lexical features) that may not transfer well across languages, particularly in cases of script mismatch?

3.  Could you provide some analysis on the impact of wikifier errors on the final NER performance? For example, based on a sample of the development data, what percentage of NER errors can be directly attributed to an incorrect entity link from the wikifier?
