### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to leverage a recent cross-lingual wikification system to generate language-independent features. By grounding n-grams from a source text in any language to English Wikipedia entries, the model extracts rich, delexicalized features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. The authors train a standard NER model on an annotated source language (e.g., English) using these features and apply it directly to target languages without any target-language annotations. The method is evaluated on nine languages, showing strong performance on standard CoNLL datasets and significant improvements for five low-resource languages.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent NER features.** The paper successfully reframes cross-lingual wikification not as a downstream task but as a source of powerful, delexicalized features for NER. This is a creative application of a recent technology that proves highly effective for direct transfer.

2.  **An extensive empirical validation of the proposed approach across a diverse set of languages.** The authors provide a thorough evaluation on both high-resource and low-resource languages (including those with non-Latin scripts). The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness over strong baselines and provide a valuable analysis of how performance correlates with the size of a language's Wikipedia.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its extensive and convincing experimental results. The proposed method is evaluated on nine different languages, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over comparable direct transfer baselines on the CoNLL datasets. Crucially, the evaluation on five low-resource languages shows that the approach outperforms a state-of-the-art system that requires more resources (e.g., parallel text and native speaker interaction). This thorough evaluation across a diverse language set provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and robustness.

2.  **Practicality and Broad Applicability:** The proposed model is highly practical as its only external resource requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump. It does not depend on parallel corpora, word alignments, or language-specific tools like POS taggers, which are often unavailable for low-resource languages. This makes the method easily and widely applicable to any language present in Wikipedia, representing a significant advantage over many existing cross-lingual transfer techniques.

3.  **Elegant and Coherent Approach:** The central idea of using cross-lingual wikification to create a shared semantic feature space for NER is elegant. The paper presents a coherent and well-structured narrative, clearly motivating the problem, explaining the methodology, and designing experiments that directly support its claims. The analysis of how feature quality depends on Wikipedia size (Section 4.4) is insightful and adds depth to the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the application is novel and the results are strong, the core technical contribution could be seen as incremental. The work effectively combines two established research lines—direct transfer with delexicalized features and cross-lingual wikification—rather than proposing a fundamentally new learning algorithm or model architecture. The main innovation lies in the clever engineering of features from a very recent wikification system.

2.  **Insufficient Contextualization and Comparison:** The paper could do more to position its contribution within the broader landscape of cross-lingual NER.
    *   The distinction from previous work using Wikipedia for NER, especially Kazama and Torisawa (2007), is mentioned briefly but should be elaborated upon more clearly in the related work section to highlight the novelty.
    *   The evaluation focuses solely on direct transfer. While comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) are provided, the inclusion of a strong projection-based baseline (even on a subset of languages with parallel data) would provide a more complete picture and better contextualize the performance of wikification-based direct transfer against other cross-lingual paradigms.

3.  **Analysis Lacks Depth in Some Areas:**
    *   The results of multi-source training (Table 4) show only marginal gains. These claims would be much stronger if supported by statistical significance tests, especially since performance does not improve in all scenarios.
    *   Figure 2 presents an interesting finding that Dutch is the best source for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba. However, this is presented without any discussion or hypothesis. A deeper analysis of why certain language pairs are more effective for transfer would be a valuable addition.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Section 3.3 describes modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier to handle all n-grams. The current description is somewhat brief. Could you clarify this process with a concrete, running example to illustrate how features are generated from an input n-gram?

2.  You rightly note that your approach is orthogonal to methods like Täckström et al.'s cross-lingual word clusters and Nothman et al.'s silver-standard data generation. This suggests they could be combined for even better performance. While a full evaluation is likely beyond the scope, have you considered running a preliminary experiment on one or two languages to demonstrate this potential synergy? This could significantly strengthen the impact of your work.

3.  Could you offer a hypothesis or a more detailed analysis for the cross-lingual transferability results in Figure 2? For example, why might Spanish be a better source language for Yoruba than English is? Is this simply a statistical artifact of the dataset, or is there a more interesting linguistic or data-driven reason?

### Additional Comments

The paper is well-written but would benefit from another round of proofreading to correct minor typos and grammatical errors. Examples include:
*   Section 3, first sentence: "...as a the base model."
*   Section 2.2, last paragraph: "...pipeline of NER then EL by..." (should be "than")
*   Section 5, first sentence: "...to disambiguate every n-grams." (should be "n-gram")
*   Section 2.1: It might be worth noting that projection can also be performed without parallel corpora, for instance, using bilingual semantic spaces, which further broadens its applicability.
