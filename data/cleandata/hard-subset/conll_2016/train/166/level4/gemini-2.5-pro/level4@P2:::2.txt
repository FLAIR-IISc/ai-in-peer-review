### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) based on the direct transfer paradigm. The core idea is to leverage a recent cross-lingual wikification system to generate language-independent features. For any given n-gram in a source text, the system attempts to link it to an English Wikipedia entry. The corresponding Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types of the linked entry are then used as features for a standard NER model. The model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and then directly applied to a target language without any annotated data in that language. The authors conduct an extensive empirical evaluation on nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, demonstrating significant improvements over comparable baselines.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Effective Application of Wikification for Direct Transfer NER:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification are highly effective for direct transfer NER. The work successfully shows that this approach provides a strong signal, particularly for languages with non-Latin scripts where lexical feature transfer fails completely.

2.  **Extensive Empirical Evaluation and Strong Results:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across a diverse set of nine languages, covering high-resource, low-resource, Latin-script, and non-Latin-script languages. The proposed model convincingly outperforms strong baselines, including the direct transfer model of Täckström et al. (2012) on CoNLL languages and the pattern-based model of Zhang et al. (2016) on low-resource languages.

3.  **Analysis of Cross-Lingual Feature Quality:** The paper provides a valuable analysis of the factors that influence the performance of the wikification-based features, empirically linking their quality to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper's strongest argument for acceptance is its impressive empirical performance. The proposed method significantly outperforms comparable direct transfer approaches on the standard CoNLL Spanish, German, and Dutch datasets. More notably, it achieves superior results on five low-resource languages compared to a competitive baseline that requires more resources (parallel data and native speaker interaction). The large performance gains (e.g., over 10 F1 points on Dutch and German when adding wikifier features) clearly demonstrate the practical value of the proposed features.

2.  **Simplicity and Generalizability:** The proposed approach is elegant in its simplicity. It does not rely on complex, language-specific resources like parallel corpora for projection or hand-crafted rules. Its only major requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump, making the method broadly applicable to any language with a Wikipedia presence. This generality is a significant practical advantage for extending NER capabilities to new languages.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors test the model in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, analyze the impact of individual feature groups, and explore multi-source training. The experiment in Section 4.4, which simulates a smaller Wikipedia by removing interlanguage links, provides clear evidence that the quality of the wikifier features is directly correlated with Wikipedia's size, offering a clear explanation for the performance variance across languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** While the engineering is solid and the results are strong, the core idea is an incremental step that combines two established research lines: direct transfer for NLP tasks and cross-lingual wikification. The work does not propose a new learning algorithm or a fundamentally new feature representation but rather demonstrates a very effective application of a recently developed tool (the Tsai and Roth 2016 wikifier) to the NER task.

2.  **Insufficient Contextualization and Comparison:** The paper could be strengthened by placing its results in a broader context.
    *   The authors state that their work is orthogonal to projection-based methods and Wikipedia-based data generation (e.g., Nothman et al., 2012). While true, comparing the results to a strong, standard projection baseline would provide a more complete picture of where this direct transfer approach stands in the wider landscape of cross-lingual NER.
    *   The distinction from prior work using Wikipedia for NER, particularly Kazama and Torisawa (2007), is mentioned briefly but could be elaborated upon more clearly in the Related Work section to better highlight this paper's specific contribution.

3.  **Lack of Depth in Analysis and Technical Description:**
    *   The description of the modified wikification model in Section 3.3 is abstract and could benefit from a concrete running example. It is not entirely clear how the constraints (e.g., not generating candidates from individual tokens for n>1) are implemented and how the final set of features is generated for a given token from the various n-grams that cover it.
    *   The analysis of the multi-source training results in Table 4 is underdeveloped. The performance gains are modest (and sometimes negative), yet the authors claim they "further improve the results." A statistical significance test is needed to substantiate this claim. Furthermore, the paper reports interesting cross-lingual transfer effects (e.g., Spanish being the best source for Yoruba) but offers no discussion or hypothesis, which is a missed opportunity for deeper insight.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The analysis in Section 4.4 clearly links performance to the size of the Wikipedia intersection. Have you considered whether linguistic typology or language family plays a role in the transfer results shown in Figure 2? For instance, why might Spanish (a Romance language) be a better source for Yoruba (a Niger-Congo language) than English? Is this purely an artifact of the training data/Wikipedia size, or could there be another explanation?

2.  The improvements from multi-source training reported in Table 4 are quite small. Are these results statistically significant compared to the single-best-source model (EN+ES)?

3.  Could you provide a brief, concrete example walking through the feature generation process (Section 3.3)? For example, for the German phrase "Albrecht Lehmann" from Figure 1, what n-grams are considered, what top candidates are returned by the modified wikifier, and what specific FreeBase and Wikipedia category features are generated for the token "Albrecht"? This would greatly clarify the mechanism.
