This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual NER via direct transfer, leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. The core idea, while effective, is not highly innovative; the contribution is largely incremental, combining two established research directions: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging) and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification.

Despite this, the paper is well-executed. It presents a coherent and clear narrative, supported by comprehensive experiments and convincing empirical evidence. However, I have several comments regarding the work's presentation and experimental design that could further strengthen the submission.

The related work section could be improved by providing a more detailed comparison to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Similarly, the distinctions between this work and other NER systems that leverage Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge should be more explicitly highlighted. While these differences are mentioned, emphasizing them more clearly would help readers better situate the paper within the existing literature.

While the authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features, reporting experiments that include them (similar to Täckström et al., 2012) would still be valuable. This would provide empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of POS features in this framework, at least for the languages where universal POS tags are available.

The description of the modified wikification model in Section 3.3 is not entirely clear. The section would benefit significantly from a running example to illustrate how the modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) system function in practice.

The authors correctly note that their method is orthogonal to the approaches of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012). To strengthen this point and enrich the discussion, it would be highly beneficial to report even preliminary results from combining these models on a few languages. Along the same lines, including a comparison against a strong projection-based baseline would provide a more complete context for the results and better demonstrate the specific advantages or limitations of the wikification-based approach.

The results in Figure 2 are intriguing, particularly that Dutch is the best source language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best for Yoruba. Is this a statistical coincidence, or is there a more interesting underlying reason? The paper would be more compelling with a brief discussion analyzing these specific cross-lingual transfer results.

The results for multi-source training in Table 4 show only modest improvements, and not consistently across all scenarios. While the idea is sound, the empirical gains appear marginal. Reporting statistical significance tests for these results would help to substantiate the claim that multi-source training is beneficial.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Section 2.1, it is worth noting that projection can also be performed without parallel data, making it applicable to a wider range of low-resource languages. For instance, the work of Peirsman and Pado (2009) and Vulić and Moens (2013) leverages bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct word alignments for transfer.
*   The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "as a the base model" in Section 3; "NER then EL" on page 3; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on page 8).
