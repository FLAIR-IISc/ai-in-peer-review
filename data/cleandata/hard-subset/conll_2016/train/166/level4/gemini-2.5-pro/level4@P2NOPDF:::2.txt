This paper presents an approach for cross-lingual direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models, leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification system. The core idea, which combines direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks with new developments in cross-lingual wikification, is a logical synthesis of existing research lines rather than a novel technological contribution. The paper's strength, however, lies not in groundbreaking innovation but in its excellent execution. It is built upon a coherent and clear narrative, supported by comprehensive experiments and empirical evidence that lead to convincing results. While I am supportive of the paper, I have several comments and suggestions for improvement, primarily concerning the presentation and experimental context.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Related Work and Positioning:** The related work section would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Furthermore, the paper needs to more explicitly differentiate its approach from other NER systems that also leverage Wikipedia's encyclopedic knowledge. While these differences are mentioned throughout the text, consolidating and highlighting them in the related work section would help readers better contextualize the paper's specific contributions.

2.  **Ablation Study on POS Features:** The authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features. However, reporting results from an experiment that includes them, similar to the setup in Tackström et al. (2012), would provide significant value. Such an experiment would offer empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of universal POS features for this task across the different languages studied, which would be a valuable finding for the community.

3.  **Clarity of Model Description:** The description of the adapted Tsai and Roth model in Section 3.3 lacks clarity. As currently written, it is difficult to fully grasp the mechanics of the modified system. The inclusion of a running example would greatly aid reader comprehension and make the technical contribution easier to follow.

4.  **Additional Baselines and Combined Models:** The authors correctly note that their method is orthogonal to the approaches of Tackström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) and could be combined with them. To further strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to include preliminary results from such a combined model, even if only on a subset of languages. This would add significant depth to the discussion and better demonstrate the potential of the proposed features. Similarly, a comparison against a strong projection-based baseline would provide a more complete picture, helping to situate the performance of wikification-based transfer and illuminate its advantages or limitations.

5.  **Deeper Analysis of Results:** The cross-lingual transfer results present some intriguing pairings (e.g., Dutch being the best source language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba). The paper would benefit from a discussion that explores these findings in more detail. Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or are there underlying linguistic or data-driven phenomena at play? A paragraph or two dedicated to analyzing these surprising results would make the paper more insightful.

6.  **Statistical Significance:** The improvements reported in Table 4, while promising, are modest and not observed in all scenarios. To substantiate the claims that the proposed features are beneficial, the authors should report statistical significance tests for these results. This would provide a more rigorous validation of the method's impact.

**Minor Comments**

*   **Section 2.1 (Related Work):** The discussion on projection-based transfer should mention that some methods do not require parallel data, making them more widely applicable. For instance, approaches by Peirsman and Padó (NAACL 2009) or Vulić and Moens (EMNLP 2013) exploit bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct word alignments for transfer.
*   **Proofreading:** The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors that should be corrected. A careful proofreading pass is recommended. Examples include:
    *   First sentence of Section 3: "...as a the base model..."
    *   Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." (This sentence is difficult to parse).
    *   Page 8: "...to disambiguate every n-grams" (should be "n-gram").
