This paper proposes an approach for multilingual named entity recognition using features derived from Wikipedia. By leveraging a cross-lingual Wikifier, the method identifies corresponding English Wikipedia articles for phrases in a target language and utilizes features based on those entries. Experiments demonstrate that this novel feature set improves performance not only in monolingual scenarios but also, crucially, in the direct transfer setting, where a model trained on English is evaluated on target languages.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) The demonstration of the effectiveness of language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification for NER, offering a promising alternative to methods relying on parallel corpora or language-specific rules. 2) A comprehensive experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich (Spanish, German, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The analysis on these low-resource and non-Latin script languages is particularly insightful. 3) The insightful analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size and the exploration of multi-source training, demonstrating practical considerations and further avenues for improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms comparable approaches on both CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages, showcasing the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The gains on low-resource languages are particularly notable, suggesting the approach’s potential in real-world scenarios where labeled data is scarce. The careful comparison to existing work (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) strengthens this claim.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, varying features, training languages, and languages themselves. The inclusion of languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) is commendable and highlights a robustness not always seen in cross-lingual NLP work. This wide-ranging evaluation builds confidence in the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis exploring the relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality is a significant strength. The experiments probing the impact of removing FreeBase links and interlanguage connections provide valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of the method and its limitations.  This careful analysis demonstrates a deep understanding of the system's behavior.
4. **Practical Methodology:** The approach has a clear practical advantage: it only requires a multilingual Wikipedia dump, avoiding the need for parallel corpora or expensive annotation efforts. This makes it readily applicable to a wide range of languages.
5. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is well-written and explains the methodology clearly. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the concept of cross-lingual wikification.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Dependency & Error Propagation:** The method’s performance is inherently dependent on the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. While the paper acknowledges this, a more detailed analysis of wikification errors and their impact on NER performance would be valuable. Quantifying the frequency of incorrect wikifications and their contribution to prediction errors would strengthen the evaluation.
2. **Handling of Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The approach struggles with named entities not present in Wikipedia. While a limitation inherent in the methodology, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of this issue and potential strategies for mitigation (e.g., incorporating gazetteers or other knowledge sources). A dedicated experiment evaluating performance on a set of out-of-Wikipedia entities would be beneficial.
3. **Feature Regularization:** The observation that adding lexical features degrades performance on Tamil and Bengali suggests a potential issue with feature weighting. Exploring different regularization techniques, such as feature-specific regularization, to prevent the model from over-relying on potentially misleading lexical features could be a valuable direction for future research. 
4. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are relevant, the field of cross-lingual NER has advanced since those works. Comparing against more recent and state-of-the-art approaches would provide a more rigorous assessment of the method’s competitiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors introduced by the cross-lingual wikifier? Specifically, what is the frequency of incorrect wikifications, and how do these errors contribute to the overall NER error rate?
2. What is the performance of your method on named entities that are *not* present in Wikipedia? Could you provide results on a dedicated test set of such entities?
3. Have you experimented with any form of feature regularization to address the issue of lexical features degrading performance on low-resource, non-Latin script languages? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation "W.−FB query" in Table 3 is somewhat unclear. Clarifying this notation would improve readability.
* The discussion of multi-source training in section 4.5 could be expanded with a table summarizing the performance gains from adding each additional language.
* Consider adding a discussion of computational cost associated with the cross-lingual wikification process, particularly for large documents or a large number of languages.
