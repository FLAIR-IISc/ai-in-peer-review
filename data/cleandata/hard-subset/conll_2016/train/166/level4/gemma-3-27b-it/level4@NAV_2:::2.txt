This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification technique. While the core idea isn’t groundbreaking – it combines established directions in direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification – the work presents a coherent and well-supported approach with compelling empirical results. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification for NER, achieving strong performance across diverse languages, including low-resource scenarios. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation that training on multiple source languages generally improves target language NER performance. Finally, the paper demonstrates that these language-independent features can enhance monolingual NER systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms comparable approaches on standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) and, crucially, shows significant improvements on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). This demonstrates the practical utility of the approach in challenging scenarios where annotated data is scarce. The comprehensive experimental setup and clear presentation of results build confidence in the findings.
2. **Effective Use of Cross-Lingual Wikification:** The paper effectively leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate valuable, language-independent features. The insight that grounding words in English Wikipedia provides a consistent representation across languages is well-motivated and experimentally validated. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase information, reducing interlanguage links) convincingly demonstrate the importance of these features.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, including both monolingual and cross-lingual transfer experiments, analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size, and exploration of multi-source training. The comparison to relevant baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) positions the work within the existing literature. The inclusion of low-resource languages adds significant value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The central concept of combining direct transfer with cross-lingual knowledge is not entirely novel. The contribution is incremental, effectively applying and validating an existing technology (cross-lingual wikification) in a new context (NER). While the results are strong, the core innovation is less pronounced.
2. **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section, while present, could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work differentiates itself from other systems leveraging encyclopedic knowledge for NER, particularly Kazama and Torisawa (2007).  A clearer articulation of the specific advantages of the proposed approach compared to existing knowledge-based NER systems is needed.
3. **Limited Exploration of Feature Combinations:** While the ablation studies are helpful, the paper would be strengthened by exploring combinations of the proposed features with other established NER features, such as POS tags. Including experiments with POS tags, even if they prove unhelpful, would provide a more complete picture of the feature landscape. A discussion of why POS tags were omitted, with empirical evidence, would also be useful.
4. **Modest Gains from Multi-Source Training:** The improvements observed from multi-source training (Table 4) are relatively small and not consistently significant. A statistical significance test would help to validate the claims regarding the benefits of using multiple source languages. Further analysis of why the gains are limited could provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper highlights that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish and Spanish is the best for Yoruba. Can you provide a deeper analysis of these results? Are there specific characteristics of these languages or the training data that explain these observations?
2.  Section 3.3 describes modifications to the wikifier system. Could you provide a more concrete example illustrating how the system operates on a sample sentence, clarifying the process of grounding n-grams and utilizing FreeBase types?
3.  You mention the orthogonality of your approach with methods like those from Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012). Have you conducted any preliminary experiments combining your approach with these methods? If so, could you share the results? If not, could you explain the challenges involved in such a combination?
4. Would the reported performance change if a strong projection baseline was included in the comparison?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Section 2.1 could acknowledge projection methods that don’t rely on parallel data, such as those leveraging bilingual semantic spaces (e.g., Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulic & Moens, 2013).
*   Several minor typos and grammatical errors were identified throughout the text (e.g., "as a the base model", "to disambiguate every n-grams"). Careful proofreading is recommended.
