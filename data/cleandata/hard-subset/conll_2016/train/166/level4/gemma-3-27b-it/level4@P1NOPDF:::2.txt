This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models, leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea is not particularly novel – it primarily combines existing research paths – the contribution is incremental but valuable. Specifically, it unites direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging) with recent advances in cross-lingual wikification technology. Overall, I found the paper well-written and compelling, presenting a coherent narrative supported by sufficient experiments and convincing results. However, I have several suggestions regarding its presentation.

Regarding related work, a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to that of Kazama and Torisawa (2007) would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper should clearly articulate the differences between this system and other related NER systems that utilize encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia. Although these distinctions are mentioned, emphasizing them would improve readability and contextualize the work more effectively.

While the authors provide justification for excluding POS tags as features, including experiments with POS tag features—similar to those conducted by Tackstrom et al.—would be informative. This would offer empirically supported insights into the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features across different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tagsets.

Section 3.3 could be enhanced by a running example. The current description of the Tsai and Roth model is not entirely clear, and an illustrative example would greatly aid understanding.

The authors repeatedly state that the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to their own and could be combined. Reporting preliminary results on a selection of languages using this combined approach would strengthen the discussion. Similarly, although acknowledging its orthogonal nature, a comparison with a strong projection baseline would further contextualize the results and demonstrate the benefits (or limitations) of a wikification-based approach.

The finding that Dutch performs best as a training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, is intriguing. Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or is there a more substantive explanation? A paragraph or two exploring these results in greater depth would be of significant interest.

While the underlying idea is sound, the improvements reported in Table 4 are modest and not consistently observed across all scenarios. Conducting a statistical significance test on the results presented in Table 4 would provide stronger support for the claims.

Minor comments:

- Sect. 2.1: It’s important to note that projection can also be achieved without relying on parallel data, making such models applicable to a wider range of languages, including those lacking parallel resources. See, for example, the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which utilize bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links for transfer.
- The text contains several typos and would benefit from careful proofreading (e.g., the first sentence of Section 3 reads "as a the base model"; this sentence is grammatically incorrect; Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..."; Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”).
