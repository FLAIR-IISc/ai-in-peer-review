This paper investigates cross-lingual transfer for Named Entity Recognition (NER) by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea of combining direct transfer with cross-lingual wikification is incremental rather than fundamentally novel, the paper presents a cohesive and well-supported argument for its effectiveness. The experiments are thorough and provide convincing empirical evidence for the approach. However, several aspects of the presentation could be improved.

The related work section would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its connection to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Furthermore, the paper should more clearly delineate its differences from existing NER systems that utilize Wikipedia knowledge. Although distinctions are mentioned, emphasizing these differences would better contextualize the contribution.

The authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features; however, including experiments with POS tag features, following the methodology of Tackstrom et al., would be valuable. Such experiments could provide empirical insight into the usefulness of POS tags for different languages, particularly those with available Universal POS tagsets.

Section 3.3 would be significantly enhanced by a running example to clarify the functionality of the Tsai and Roth model. The current description is not entirely clear, and a concrete illustration would greatly aid understanding.

Given the authors’ assertion that their approach is orthogonal to and potentially combinable with methods like those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012), preliminary results demonstrating such a combination across a selection of languages would strengthen the discussion. Similarly, a comparison against a strong projection baseline would further contextualize the results and highlight the benefits of the wikification-based approach.

The observation that Dutch performs best as a training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, is intriguing. The paper should dedicate a paragraph or two to discussing these findings in more depth, exploring whether they represent statistical anomalies or indicate a more meaningful phenomenon.

The improvements reported in Table 4 are modest and not universally observed. A statistical significance test for these results would provide stronger support for the claims made.

Finally, several minor issues require attention:

*   Section 2.1: The discussion of projection should acknowledge methods that do not rely on parallel data, such as those employing bilingual semantic spaces (e.g., Peirsman & Pado, NAACL 2009; Vulic & Moens, EMNLP 2013). These methods broaden the applicability of projection to languages lacking parallel resources.
*   The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several detected typos (e.g., Section 3, first sentence: "as a the base model"; Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..."; Page 8: "to disambiguate every n-grams”).
