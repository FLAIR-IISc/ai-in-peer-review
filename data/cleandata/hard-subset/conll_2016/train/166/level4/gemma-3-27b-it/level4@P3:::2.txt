This paper explores cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) via a novel approach leveraging cross-lingual wikification. The core idea is to utilize language-independent features derived from grounding words and phrases in English Wikipedia, enabling training on resource-rich languages and applying the model to others. While the underlying concepts aren’t entirely groundbreaking – building on direct transfer and recent advances in wikification – the work presents a coherent and well-supported investigation with convincing results, making a valuable incremental contribution to the field. The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear narrative, though some improvements in presentation and analysis would strengthen it.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) a demonstration of the effectiveness of cross-lingual wikification features for cross-lingual NER, achieving strong performance on both CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages; (2) a comprehensive empirical evaluation of this approach across nine languages, providing valuable insights into the role of Wikipedia size and the benefits of multi-source training; and (3) a practical methodology for leveraging readily available Wikipedia data to build NER systems for languages with limited annotated resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents consistently strong results across a diverse set of languages, outperforming comparable approaches on both standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, and Dutch) and several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil). This provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed method. The inclusion of low-resource languages is particularly valuable, as this is where cross-lingual transfer methods are most needed.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, exploring various aspects of the approach, including the impact of Wikipedia size, the benefits of multi-source training, and the effect of incorporating gazetteer features. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, varying interlanguage link density) are particularly insightful and help to understand the underlying mechanisms.
3. **Practical Methodology:** The proposed method relies solely on multilingual Wikipedia, a readily available resource, making it easily applicable to a wide range of languages. This practical aspect is a significant advantage over approaches requiring parallel corpora or language-specific rules.  The clear articulation of the methodology allows for straightforward replication.
4. **Clear and Coherent Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed approach. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the execution is strong, the core idea of combining direct transfer with wikification features is not inherently novel. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty relative to existing work, acknowledging its incremental nature while highlighting the specific contributions of this implementation.
2. **Insufficient Contextualization with Related Work:** The related work section, while covering relevant topics, lacks a detailed comparison with other knowledge-based NER systems utilizing Wikipedia. A more explicit discussion of the differences between this work and, for example, Kazama and Torisawa (2007) is needed.  Further exploration of how this work builds upon, or diverges from, other approaches that leverage encyclopedic knowledge would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Exploration of Feature Interactions:** The authors briefly mention the potential for combining their approach with other methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Nothman et al., 2012), but only propose preliminary experiments.  A more systematic investigation of the synergy between their wikification features and other techniques, such as projection or silver-standard data generation, would significantly strengthen the paper.  Specifically, a comparison with a strong projection baseline would provide valuable context.
4. **Inconclusive Multi-Source Training Results:** The results from Table 4, demonstrating the benefits of multi-source training, are modest and lack statistical significance testing. A statistical analysis is crucial to support the claim that the observed improvements are not due to chance. Further analysis exploring which language combinations yield the greatest gains would also be valuable.
5. **Lack of Insight into Language-Specific Training Effects:** The observation that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba raises an interesting question, but remains unexplained. A dedicated discussion exploring potential reasons for this phenomenon—beyond statistical coincidence—would significantly enhance the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a statistical significance test for the results presented in Table 4?
2.  What specific characteristics of Dutch and Spanish might explain their effectiveness as training languages for Spanish and Yoruba, respectively?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the cross-lingual wikification process, particularly for large documents or datasets?
4.   The description of the edited Tsai and Roth (2016) system in section 3.3 could benefit from more clarity. Can you provide a simple example illustrating how it operates?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Section 2.1:  Consider mentioning approaches to projection that don’t rely on parallel data, such as those employing bilingual semantic spaces (e.g., Peirsman & Pado, NAACL 2009; Vulic & Moens, EMNLP 2013).
*   Proofreading is recommended. Several typos were identified (e.g., “as a the base model”, “to disambiguate every n-grams”).



Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the work and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
