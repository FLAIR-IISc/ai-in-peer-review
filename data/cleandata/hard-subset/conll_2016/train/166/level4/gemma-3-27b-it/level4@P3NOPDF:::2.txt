This paper investigates cross-lingual transfer learning for Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea of combining direct transfer with cross-lingual wikification is incremental rather than fundamentally novel, the paper presents a well-structured and coherent investigation with sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims. Overall, I found the paper to be strong and the results promising, though several points regarding presentation and experimentation could be addressed to further strengthen the work.

The related work section would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this approach relates to existing literature, specifically the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Furthermore, a clearer delineation between this work and other NER systems that utilize encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia is needed. While differences are mentioned, emphasizing these distinctions would improve the reader’s understanding of the contribution and its positioning within the field.

The authors provide a rationale for excluding Part-of-Speech (POS) tags as features. However, given the availability of universal POS tags for many languages, including experiments with POS tag features, similar to those conducted by Tackstrom et al., would provide valuable empirical insight into their utility—or lack thereof—for cross-lingual NER performance. This would offer a more complete picture of feature importance.

Section 3.3 describing the Tsai and Roth model could be improved with a running example. The current description is not entirely clear, and a concrete illustration would aid comprehension. 

The authors rightly note the orthogonality of their approach to methods like those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012). To bolster this claim, including preliminary results demonstrating the combination of these methods with the proposed approach, even for a limited set of languages, would be beneficial. Similarly, a comparison to a strong projection baseline would provide additional context and highlight the advantages of utilizing a wikification-based approach.

The observed language-specific transfer performance – Dutch as the best source for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba – is intriguing. The paper would be significantly enhanced by a dedicated discussion exploring the potential reasons for these patterns. Is this merely a statistical anomaly, or does it suggest underlying linguistic or structural similarities driving these results? A paragraph or two delving into this observation would be highly valuable.

The improvements reported in Table 4 appear modest, and are not consistent across all scenarios. To strengthen the claims of improvement, reporting statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 4 is crucial.

Finally, several minor points require attention:

*   **Section 2.1:** The description of projection methods could be broadened to include approaches that do not rely on parallel data. Work such as Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013) leverage bilingual semantic spaces for transfer, making them applicable to languages lacking parallel resources.
*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** A careful proofreading is recommended to correct several detected typos and grammatical errors. Examples include: “as a the base model” (Section 3, first sentence); “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by…” (Page 3); and “to disambiguate every n-grams” (Page 8).



Addressing these points would further enhance the clarity, rigor, and impact of this already promising work.
