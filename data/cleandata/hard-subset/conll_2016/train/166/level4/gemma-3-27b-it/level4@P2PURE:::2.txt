This paper investigates cross-lingual transfer of NER models leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea is not particularly novel – representing an incremental contribution by combining direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks like NER, parsing, and POS tagging with recent advances in cross-lingual wikification – I found the paper generally well-executed. It presents a coherent narrative supported by sufficient experiments and empirical evidence, yielding convincing results. I do, however, have several suggestions for improving the presentation.

Regarding related work, a more detailed discussion of the paper’s relationship to the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007) would be beneficial. Furthermore, the authors should explicitly articulate the distinctions between their system and other NER systems that utilize encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia; while differences are mentioned, emphasizing them would improve clarity and contextualization.

Although the rationale for excluding POS tags as features is provided, exploring their inclusion in experiments, mirroring the approach of Tackstrom et al., would be insightful. This would provide empirically-grounded insight into the utility (or lack thereof) of POS tags across different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tagsets. 

Section 3.3 could be enhanced by a running example to clarify the operation of the Tsai and Roth model, as the current description is not entirely transparent. 

The authors assert the orthogonality of their approach to those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012), and suggest potential for combination. Reporting preliminary results on a selection of languages using a combined model would strengthen this claim and enrich the discussion. Similarly, a comparison with a strong projection baseline, despite its orthogonality, would provide further context and illuminate the benefits (or limitations) of the wikification-based approach.

The observation that Dutch performs optimally as a training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, is intriguing. A deeper analysis, perhaps a paragraph or two, exploring the underlying reasons for this phenomenon – whether statistical coincidence or a more substantive pattern – would be valuable.

While the approach is sound, the improvements observed in Table 4 are modest and not consistently observed across all scenarios. Reporting statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 4 would bolster the claims made.

Minor comments:
- Sect. 2.1: The discussion of projection should acknowledge methods that do not rely on parallel data, expanding the applicability of such models to resource-scarce languages. Examples include the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which utilize bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links for transfer.
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address several identified typos (e.g., Section 3: “as a the base model”; a non-parsable sentence; Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by…”; Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”).
