This paper introduces a novel approach to multi-lingual named entity recognition (NER) leveraging features derived from Wikipedia. The core idea is to utilize a cross-lingual wikifier to link phrases in a target language to corresponding English Wikipedia articles, and subsequently employ features extracted from those articles. The authors demonstrate that these Wikipedia-based features enhance NER performance in both monolingual and, crucially, direct transfer scenarios – where a model trained on English is evaluated on other languages.

The primary contribution of this work is the effective application of cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER. While cross-lingual wikification itself isn't novel, the authors uniquely utilize it not as a downstream task *following* NER, but as a foundational step *for* NER, generating potentially valuable clues regardless of the input language. A second contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation. The paper showcases consistent performance gains across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich (Spanish, German, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), providing strong evidence for the method’s generalizability. Finally, the analysis regarding the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality, and the exploration of multi-source training, offer valuable insights into the method’s behavior and potential for further improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Feature Engineering:** The use of cross-lingual wikification features for NER is a clever and effective approach. The authors rightly point out that grounding mentions in Wikipedia provides a valuable, language-agnostic representation. This is a genuinely insightful contribution to the field.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation, spanning multiple languages and experimental settings (monolingual, direct transfer, multi-source training). The inclusion of low-resource and non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil) is particularly commendable, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance improvements over baseline methods, particularly on the challenging direct transfer task, lend significant credibility to the proposed approach. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) reinforces these findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies and error analysis (specifically concerning Wikipedia size and the impact of lexical features) provide a deeper understanding of the method's strengths and limitations. The correlation between Wikipedia size and feature quality is a key takeaway.
5. **Practical Applicability:** The method’s reliance solely on Wikipedia makes it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring parallel corpora or extensive language-specific resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Dependency:** The performance is intrinsically linked to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. The paper doesn't fully quantify the error propagation – how frequently does wikifier error lead to incorrect NER predictions? Understanding this error rate would strengthen the analysis.
2. **Handling of Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The method’s effectiveness is likely diminished for named entities not present in Wikipedia. While this limitation is acknowledged, a more detailed discussion of this scenario, perhaps with illustrative examples, would be beneficial. Exploring techniques to address this – such as incorporating gazetteers or other external knowledge sources – could be a direction for future work.
3. **Feature Regularization:** The observation that adding lexical features degrades performance on certain low-resource languages (Tamil, Bengali) suggests potential issues with feature weighting. Investigating different regularization strategies, perhaps by assigning lower weights to lexical features for these languages, could improve performance.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The computational overhead of the wikification step, particularly for large corpora, is not thoroughly discussed. Providing a rough estimate of the computational cost would be useful for assessing the practicality of the approach.
5. **Presentation of Results:** The sheer number of tables and experiments makes the paper dense. While thoroughness is appreciated, some of the results could be presented more concisely without sacrificing key information.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the error propagation from the wikifier to the NER system? Specifically, what percentage of NER errors can be directly attributed to incorrect wikification?
2. How does the method perform on named entities that are not present in Wikipedia? Can you provide examples of such cases and discuss potential strategies to handle them?
3. Have you experimented with different regularization techniques to address the issue of over-reliance on lexical features, particularly for low-resource languages?
4. What is the computational cost of the wikification step for a large corpus (e.g., one million sentences)?
