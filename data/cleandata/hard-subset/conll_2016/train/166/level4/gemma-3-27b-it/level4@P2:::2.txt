This paper investigates cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) via a direct transfer approach leveraging recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. While the core idea of combining these two research areas – direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification – isn’t groundbreaking, the work presents a well-structured and empirically sound exploration of this combination, resulting in convincing performance gains. The paper’s contributions, as I see them, are threefold: (1) demonstrating the effectiveness of language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification for NER across diverse languages, including low-resource settings; (2) establishing a strong baseline for cross-lingual NER transfer learning that outperforms comparable methods, particularly for low-resource languages; and (3) showing the generalizability of the approach by demonstrating improvements to monolingual NER systems through the inclusion of these language-independent features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements across a range of languages, including both CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages. The results presented in tables are clear and support the claims made throughout the paper. The outperformance of existing direct transfer methods, particularly on low-resource languages where parallel data is scarce, is a significant achievement.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, exploring different feature combinations and analyzing the impact of Wikipedia size on performance. The inclusion of experiments with multiple training languages provides valuable insights into the transferability of the model.
3. **Clear and Coherent Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the methodology is explained in a straightforward manner. The inclusion of Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the wikification process.
4. **Practical Applicability:** The method's reliance solely on multilingual Wikipedia makes it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring expensive and often unavailable parallel corpora or manually annotated data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The primary contribution of the paper lies in the effective combination of existing techniques rather than the introduction of fundamentally new technology. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of this incremental approach and potential avenues for more radical innovation.
2. **Insufficient Contextualization in Related Work:** The discussion of related work, particularly concerning Kazama and Torisawa (2007), could be more detailed. A more explicit comparison of the proposed approach with other NER systems that utilize encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia is needed. While the differences are mentioned, they should be emphasized to better position this work within the broader research landscape.
3. **Limited Analysis of Multi-Language Training:** While the experiments with multiple training languages (Table 4) are interesting, the improvements observed are relatively small, and the lack of statistical significance testing weakens the claims. Further investigation into *why* certain language combinations are more effective (e.g., Dutch for Spanish, Spanish for Yoruba) would be valuable. A deeper analysis of the linguistic characteristics of these languages might reveal underlying reasons for these observations.
4. **Lack of Experimentation with POS Tags:** The authors justify the exclusion of POS tags, but a brief set of experiments—even with languages where universal POS tags are available—would provide empirical evidence to support this decision and address potential reader skepticism.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons behind the observed performance differences when training with different languages (as shown in Figure 2)? Is there a linguistic explanation for why Dutch is the best training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba?
2.  Would you consider conducting statistical significance tests on the results presented in Table 4 to provide more robust support for your claims regarding the benefits of multi-language training?
3.  Given the orthogonality of your approach with methods like projection (as you mention), would you be willing to present a preliminary comparison with a strong projection baseline to further contextualize your results?
4.  The description of the modified wikification system from Tsai and Roth (2016) in Section 3.3 could benefit from more clarity. Could you provide a more concrete example of how the modifications address the challenges of grounding every n-gram?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Section 2.1: The discussion of projection methods could acknowledge approaches that don't rely on parallel data, such as those exploiting bilingual semantic spaces (e.g., Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulic & Moens, 2013).
*   Minor typos and grammatical errors should be addressed through careful proofreading (e.g., "as a the base model," "to disambiguate every n-grams").
