This paper introduces a novel approach to multi-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging features extracted from Wikipedia. The core idea is to utilize a cross-lingual Wikifier to identify corresponding English Wikipedia articles for entities mentioned in a target language, and subsequently employ features derived from these linked articles. The experimental results demonstrate that incorporating these Wikipedia-based features improves NER performance, both in monolingual settings and, notably, in a direct transfer scenario where a model trained on English is evaluated on target languages.

I found this paper to be a valuable contribution. It successfully introduces a new feature for NER and provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation that clearly demonstrates its effectiveness. The analyses specifically focused on low-resource languages and non-Latin scripts are particularly insightful and strengthen the paper's overall impact. 

However, a potential limitation concerns the handling of named entities not present in Wikipedia. While the paper presents strong results, exploring the method’s behavior when encountering such entities would provide a more complete understanding of its capabilities. It would be beneficial to see results illustrating the impact of this limitation on performance.

The proposed method’s reliance on the cross-lingual Wikifier also raises an important question regarding error propagation. Introducing an additional component into the pipeline inevitably introduces potential sources of error. An analysis quantifying the frequency with which Wikifier errors contribute to incorrect NER predictions would be a valuable addition. Understanding this error rate is crucial for assessing the overall robustness of the approach.

Finally, the observed performance decline on Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are incorporated into the direct transfer setting suggests a potential for feature imbalance. Investigating techniques for differential regularization of feature classes – allowing the model to rely less heavily on potentially misleading lexical features in these specific languages – could be a promising avenue for future work and may lead to further performance improvements.
