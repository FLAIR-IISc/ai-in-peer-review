This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea isn't particularly novel – it primarily combines existing research paths of direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging) with recent advances in cross-lingual wikification – the contribution is valuable and incremental. I generally found the paper well-written and supported by a coherent narrative, sufficient experiments, and convincing empirical evidence. However, I have several suggestions regarding the presentation of the work.

Regarding related work, a more detailed discussion of how this paper relates to the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007) would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper should clearly articulate its distinction from other NER systems that utilize encyclopedic Wikipedia knowledge, even though these differences are currently mentioned in the text. Explicitly emphasizing these distinctions would aid reader comprehension and contextualization of the work.

Although the authors provide justification for excluding POS tags as features, including experiments with POS tag features—similar to those conducted by Tackstrom et al.—would be insightful. This would provide empirically-supported evidence regarding their utility (or lack thereof) for different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tagsets.

Section 3.3 would be strengthened by the inclusion of a running example. The current description of the Tsai and Roth model is not entirely clear, and an illustrative example would improve understanding.

The authors repeatedly state that approaches from Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to their own and potentially combinable. Reporting preliminary results on a selection of languages using a combination of these models would enhance the discussion. Similarly, comparing against a strong projection baseline, despite acknowledging its orthogonality, would further contextualize the results and demonstrate the benefits (or limitations) of a wikification-based approach.

The observed language-specific transfer performance—Dutch as the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba—is intriguing. A paragraph or two exploring the underlying reasons for these results, beyond mere statistical coincidence, would be highly valuable. 

While the overall idea is sound, the improvements reported in Table 4 are modest and not universally observed across scenarios. Performing and reporting a statistical significance test on the results in Table 4 would strengthen the support for the claims.

Minor comments:
- Sect. 2.1: The description of projection methods could be broadened to include techniques that do not rely on parallel data, which expands their applicability to languages lacking parallel resources. Examples include the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which leverage bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links for transfer.
- Several typographical errors were detected throughout the text. Careful proofreading is recommended to improve clarity and professionalism (e.g., first sentence of Section 3: "as a the base model"; This sentence is not 'parsable', Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...", "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
