**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) using a cross-lingual wikification technique. The model grounds words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, providing language-independent features for NER. The authors evaluate their model on a range of languages, including low-resource languages, and demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Language-independent features for NER**: The paper proposes a novel method for generating language-independent features for NER using cross-lingual wikification.
2. **Direct transfer of NER models**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of direct transfer of NER models across languages using the proposed features.
3. **Improved performance on low-resource languages**: The paper shows that the proposed model can improve NER performance on low-resource languages, which is a significant contribution.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear and coherent story**: The paper presents a clear and coherent story, with a well-structured narrative and sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims.
2. **Convincing results**: The authors provide convincing results, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach on a range of languages.
3. **Novel application of cross-lingual wikification**: The paper proposes a novel application of cross-lingual wikification for NER, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in core technology**: The paper does not propose any core new technology, but rather marries two existing research paths: direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification.
2. **Limited discussion of related work**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly in relation to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and other NER systems that rely on Wikipedia knowledge.
3. **Lack of experiments with POS tags**: The authors argue against using POS tags as features, but it would be interesting to see experiments with POS tags to support this claim.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the edited model from Tsai and Roth works, using a running example?
2. Why do you think Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba? Is this a statistical coincidence or is there something more interesting going on?
3. Can you report some preliminary results on combining your approach with orthogonal approaches, such as Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012)?
4. Can you provide a statistical significance test for the results in Table 4 to support your claims?

**Additional Comments**

* The paper could benefit from a more careful proofreading to eliminate typos and improve clarity.
* The authors may want to consider comparing their approach with a strong projection baseline to put their results into more context.
* The idea of combining the proposed approach with other techniques for multilingual NER is an interesting one, and the authors may want to explore this in future work.
