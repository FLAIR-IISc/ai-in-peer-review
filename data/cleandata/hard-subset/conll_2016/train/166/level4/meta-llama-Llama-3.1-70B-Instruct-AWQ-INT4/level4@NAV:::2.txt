This paper explores the direct transfer of NER models across languages using a state-of-the-art cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea may not be highly innovative, it successfully integrates two research paths: direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks and recent advancements in cross-lingual wikification technology. The paper presents a coherent narrative, supported by thorough experiments and empirical evidence, yielding convincing results. However, I have several suggestions for improving the presentation.

The related work section would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Kazama and Torisawa (2007), highlighting the distinct contributions of this paper. Additionally, the authors should emphasize the differences between their approach and other NER systems that rely on Wikipedia knowledge.

Including experiments with POS tags as features, similar to Tackstrom et al., would provide valuable insights into their usefulness across languages. 

A running example in Section 3.3 would enhance the clarity of the edited model from Tsai and Roth.

Given the authors' assertion that their approach can be combined with those of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012), preliminary results on combining these models would add depth to the discussion. A comparison with a strong projection baseline would also provide context for the wikification-based approach.

The paper's findings on the best training languages for Spanish and Yoruba warrant further discussion, exploring whether these results are statistically significant or indicative of a more interesting phenomenon.

The results in Table 4, although sound in concept, are not entirely convincing due to the small improvements observed. Conducting a statistical significance test would strengthen the claims made.

Minor suggestions include:

- In Section 2.1, the authors could note that projection methods exist that do not require parallel data, making them more widely applicable (e.g., Peirsman and Pado, NAACL 2009; Vulic and Moens, EMNLP 2013).
- A thorough proofreading is necessary to correct typos and improve the overall clarity of the paper (e.g., Section 3's first sentence, Page 3's description of the traditional pipeline, and the phrase "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
