This paper explores the cross-lingual direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the key idea is not highly innovative or creative, as it builds upon existing research rather than proposing novel technology, the contribution is still significant as it combines two research paths: direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging) and recent developments in cross-lingual wikification technology. Despite the incremental nature of the contribution, I found the paper engaging, as it presents a coherent and clear narrative supported by sufficient experiments and empirical evidence, yielding convincing results. However, I have several comments regarding the presentation of the work.

The related work section would benefit from a more detailed description of how this paper relates to the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Furthermore, it is essential to clearly state the differences between this work and other related NER systems that have utilized encyclopedic Wikipedia knowledge. Although the differences are mentioned in the text, they should be emphasized to facilitate reading and contextualization. 

The authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features, but it would be interesting to report experiments with POS tag features, similar to Tackstrom et al., to provide empirical evidence on the usefulness of such features for different languages (at least for languages with available universal POS tags). 

Section 3.3 could be improved with a running example, as the description of the modified model from Tsai and Roth is not entirely clear. 

Given that the authors mention the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to theirs and can be combined with the proposed approach, it would be beneficial to report preliminary results on a selection of languages using the combination of models. This would add depth to the discussion. Similarly, comparing the proposed approach with a strong projection baseline would provide a more comprehensive context and highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

It would be interesting to explore why Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba. Is this merely a statistical coincidence or is there a more intriguing explanation? A more in-depth discussion of these results would be valuable.

Although the idea is sound, the results from Table 4 are not entirely convincing, with only small improvements detected (and not in all scenarios). A statistical significance test reported for the results from Table 4 would help support the claims.

Minor comments:
- Section 2.1 should note that projection can also be performed via methods that do not require parallel data, making such models more widely applicable (even for languages without parallel resources), as seen in the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which exploit bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links for transfer.
- The paper would benefit from a more careful proofreading, as several typos were detected in the text (e.g., first sentence of Section 3: "as a the base model"; This sentence is not 'parsable', Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...", "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
