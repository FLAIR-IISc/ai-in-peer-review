This paper explores cross-lingual direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the key idea is not highly innovative, the paper presents a coherent and clear story with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence to support its claims, yielding convincing results.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual NER using a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate every n-gram. The model works on all languages in Wikipedia and requires only a Wikipedia dump. The authors study a wide range of languages in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, showing significant improvements over strong baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. **Cross-lingual wikification for NER**: The authors introduce a method that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER.
2. **Direct transfer of NER models**: The proposed model can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia, outperforming comparable approaches on the standard CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages.
3. **Improved results with multiple source languages**: The authors demonstrate that training on multiple source languages can further improve NER results.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of cross-lingual wikification**: The authors show that cross-lingual wikification can provide strong language-independent features for NER, outperforming other approaches.
2. **Extensive experimentation**: The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed model on various languages and settings, providing convincing results.
3. **Improved results on low-resource languages**: The authors demonstrate significant improvements on low-resource languages, which is a challenging task in NER.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited novelty**: The paper's key idea is not highly innovative, as it builds upon existing research in direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification.
2. **Lack of comparison with projection baselines**: The authors do not compare their approach with strong projection baselines, which would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
3. **Insufficient discussion on training language selection**: The paper does not provide a detailed explanation for why certain languages are better suited as training languages for specific target languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the edited model from Tsai and Roth works, including a running example to illustrate the process?
2. Have you considered combining your approach with other orthogonal methods, such as projection or generating training data from Wikipedia, and if so, what were the results?
3. Can you provide a statistical significance test for the results in Table 4 to support the claims of improved performance with multiple source languages?

**Additional Comments**

The paper could benefit from a more careful proofreading to correct typos and improve readability. Additionally, the authors may consider discussing the implications of their results on the selection of training languages for specific target languages.
