This paper explores the cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea may not be highly innovative or creative, as it primarily combines existing research paths, the paper is well-structured and effectively presents its claims with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence. The authors successfully merge two key research areas: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks, such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging, and (2) recent developments in cross-lingual wikification technology. Despite the incremental nature of the contribution, I found the paper engaging due to its clear narrative and convincing results.

However, I have several comments regarding the presentation of the work. In the related work section, a more detailed description of how this paper relates to the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007) is necessary. Additionally, it would be beneficial to clearly state the differences between this paper and other related NER systems that have relied on encyclopaedic Wikipedia knowledge. Although the differences are mentioned in the text, they should be further emphasized to facilitate reading and contextualize the work.

The authors justify their decision to exclude POS tags as features, but it would be interesting to report experiments with POS tags features, similar to Tackstrom et al. This would provide readers with empirical evidence regarding the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features for different languages, particularly for those with available universal POS tags.

The inclusion of a running example in Section 3.3 would enhance the reader's understanding of the edited model from Tsai and Roth. As it stands, the description is not entirely clear, and a concrete example would help to illustrate the concept.

Given that the authors mention the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) as orthogonal to theirs and potentially combinable, it would be beneficial to report preliminary results on a selection of languages using the combination of the models. This would add depth to the discussion and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach. Similarly, comparing the results with a strong projection baseline would help to contextualize the findings and highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

The observation that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba, warrants further discussion. Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or is there a more interesting explanation? A paragraph or two exploring these results in more depth would be valuable.

While the idea is sound, the results from Table 4 are not entirely convincing, with only small improvements detected (and not in all scenarios). Conducting a statistical significance test for the results from Table 4 would help support the claims and strengthen the paper's conclusions.

Minor comments include:

- Section 2.1 could be improved by noting that projection can also be performed via methods that do not require parallel data, making such models more widely applicable (even for languages without parallel resources). For example, see the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which exploit bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links to perform the transfer.
- A thorough proofreading is necessary to correct several typos and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., the first sentence of Section 3: "as a the base model"; Page 3: "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...", "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
