This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-lingual named entity recognition (NER) by leveraging features from Wikipedia. The method relies on a cross-lingual Wikifier to identify English Wikipedia articles for phrases in a target language and utilizes features derived from these Wikipedia entries. Experimental results demonstrate that this new feature not only improves performance in the monolingual case but also in the more challenging direct transfer setting, where an English model is tested on a target language.

The paper's contribution to the field of NER is notable, as it introduces a new feature and conducts a thorough set of experiments to validate its effectiveness. The analysis of low-resource languages and non-Latin scripts is particularly insightful, providing valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities in these under-explored areas.

However, the proposed method's reliance on Wikipedia raises questions about its applicability to named entities not present in Wikipedia. It would be beneficial to include an analysis of how these entities are affected by the proposed method, in addition to the results presented in the paper. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's strengths and limitations.

Furthermore, the success of the proposed method is heavily dependent on the accuracy of the cross-lingual Wikifier. The introduction of this additional step in the pipeline increases the risk of error propagation. It would be informative to investigate the frequency of errors in the prediction due to errors in the Wikifier and discuss potential strategies for mitigating these errors.

The experimental results also reveal an interesting trend, where the addition of lexical features leads to poor performance in the direct transfer setting for Tamil and Bengali. This suggests that the model may be over-relying on lexical features, which could be detrimental to its overall performance. It would be worth exploring the possibility of regularizing different feature classes separately, allowing the model to strike a better balance between lexical and non-lexical features.

Overall, this paper presents a compelling approach to multi-lingual NER and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area. Addressing the above concerns and exploring the proposed method's limitations and potential improvements would further strengthen the paper's contributions.
