This paper proposes a cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate words and phrases to the English Wikipedia. The model is designed to work on all languages in Wikipedia, with the only requirement being a Wikipedia dump. The authors evaluate their model on a wide range of languages in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, achieving significant improvements over strong baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A language-independent model for cross-lingual NER that can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia.
2. The use of a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate words and phrases to the English Wikipedia, providing useful language-independent features for NER.
3. An extensive evaluation of the model on a wide range of languages, including low-resource languages.

The strengths of this paper are:
1. The authors present a clear and coherent story, with enough experiments and empirical evidence to support their claims.
2. The model achieves significant improvements over strong baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in cross-lingual NER.
3. The use of a cross-lingual wikifier provides a novel and effective way to obtain language-independent features for NER.
4. The evaluation of the model on low-resource languages is a valuable contribution, as these languages are often neglected in NLP research.

The weaknesses of this paper are:
1. The idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier for NER is not highly innovative or creative, as it builds on existing work in cross-lingual wikification.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed description of how their model relates to other related work, such as Kazama and Torisawa (2007).
3. The authors do not report experiments with POS tags as features, which could provide additional insights into the usefulness of these features for different languages.
4. The results from Table 4, which show the effect of training on multiple languages, are not convincing, with only small improvements detected.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide more details on how your model relates to other related work, such as Kazama and Torisawa (2007)?
2. Why did you decide to leave out POS tags as features, and can you report experiments with POS tags to provide more insights into their usefulness?
3. Can you provide more details on how the edited model from Tsai and Roth (2016) works, and how it is used in your system?
4. Have you considered combining your approach with other orthogonal approaches, such as projection-based methods, to achieve even better results?
5. Can you provide more insights into why Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba?
6. Can you report statistical significance tests for the results from Table 4 to support your claims?
