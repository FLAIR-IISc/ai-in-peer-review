**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) using a cross-lingual wikification technique. The model is trained on a source language and can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia, without requiring parallel data or language-specific features. The key idea is to disambiguate words and phrases to the English Wikipedia, using the typing information from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase as language-independent features for NER.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Cross-lingual wikification**: The paper proposes a method to ground words and phrases in non-English languages to the English Wikipedia, providing language-independent features for NER.
2. **Direct transfer**: The model can be trained on a source language and applied to all languages in Wikipedia, without requiring parallel data or language-specific features.
3. **Improved performance**: The model outperforms comparable approaches on the standard CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear and coherent story**: The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with convincing results and empirical evidence to support its claims.
2. **Wide range of languages**: The paper studies a wide range of languages in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Robust evaluation**: The paper evaluates the model on various datasets, including CoNLL and low-resource languages, providing a comprehensive assessment of its performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper's key idea is not highly innovative, as it combines two existing research paths: direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification.
2. **Limited improvement**: The results in Table 4 show only small improvements when training on multiple languages, which may not be statistically significant.
3. **Typos and proofreading**: The paper could benefit from a more careful proofreading to eliminate typos and improve readability.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed description of how your approach relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and other related NER systems that relied on Wikipedia knowledge?
2. Why did you decide to leave out POS tags as features, and can you report experiments with POS tags features to provide empirical evidence on their usefulness?
3. Can you provide a running example to illustrate how the edited model from Tsai and Roth works?
4. Why is Dutch the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish the best language for Yoruba? Is this a statistical coincidence or something more interesting?
5. Can you report preliminary results on combining your approach with orthogonal methods, such as Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012), to provide a more comprehensive evaluation?
