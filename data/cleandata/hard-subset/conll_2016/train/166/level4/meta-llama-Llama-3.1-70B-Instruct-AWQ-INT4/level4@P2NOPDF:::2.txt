This paper explores the cross-lingual direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the key idea may not be highly innovative, as it does not introduce new core technology, the contribution is still valuable as it combines two research paths: direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks and recent developments in cross-lingual wikification technology. The paper effectively presents a coherent and clear story, supported by experiments and empirical evidence, yielding convincing results.

However, there are several aspects that require attention to improve the presentation of the work. Firstly, the related work section would benefit from a more detailed description of how this paper relates to the work of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). Additionally, it is essential to clearly state the differences between this work and other NER systems that have utilized Wikipedia knowledge. Although these differences are mentioned in the text, they need to be further emphasized to facilitate reading and contextualization.

The authors' decision to exclude POS tags as features is justified, but it would be interesting to include experiments with POS tags features, similar to those conducted by Tackstrom et al. This would provide readers with an empirical understanding of the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features for different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tags.

The inclusion of a running example in Section 3.3 would enhance the clarity of the edited model from Tsai and Roth. As it stands, the description is not entirely clear, and a concrete example would facilitate comprehension.

Given that the authors mention the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to theirs and can be combined, it would be beneficial to report preliminary results on a selection of languages using the combination of models. This would add depth to the discussion. Similarly, comparing the proposed approach with a strong projection baseline would provide a more comprehensive context and highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

The results showing that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba, warrant further discussion. It would be interesting to explore whether this is merely a statistical coincidence or if there are more profound underlying factors at play.

Although the idea is sound, the results from Table 4 are not entirely convincing, with only small improvements detected in some scenarios. Conducting a statistical significance test for the results from Table 4 would help support the claims.

Minor comments include:

- Section 2.1: Projection can also be performed using methods that do not require parallel data, making such models more widely applicable (even for languages without parallel resources). Examples of such work include Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which utilize bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links for transfer.
- A thorough proofreading is necessary to correct several typos detected in the text, such as "as a the base model" in the first sentence of Section 3, "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." on Page 3, and "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8.
