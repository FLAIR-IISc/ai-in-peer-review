This paper explores the cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the key idea is not highly innovative, as it builds upon existing research rather than proposing new core technologies, the contribution is still valuable as it marries two research paths: direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging) and recent developments in cross-lingual wikification technology. Despite the incremental nature of the contribution, the paper is well-structured and presents a coherent story, supported by sufficient experiments and empirical evidence, yielding convincing results.

I have several comments concerning the presentation of the work. In the related work section, a more detailed description of how this paper relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) would be beneficial. Additionally, it would be helpful to clearly state the differences between this work and other related NER systems that rely on encyclopedic Wikipedia knowledge. Although the differences are mentioned in the text, they need to be further emphasized to facilitate reading and contextualization.

The authors' decision to exclude POS tags as features is justified, but it would be interesting to include experiments with POS tags features, similar to Tackstrom et al., to provide empirical evidence on their usefulness (or lack thereof) for different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tags.

The addition of a running example in Section 3.3 would enhance the clarity of the edited model from Tsai and Roth, as the current description is not entirely clear. Furthermore, since the authors mention that the approaches from Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to theirs and can be combined, reporting preliminary results on a selection of languages using the combination of models would add depth to the discussion. A comparison with a strong projection baseline would also provide a more comprehensive context and highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

The finding that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba, raises questions. Is this merely a statistical coincidence, or is there a more interesting explanation? A more in-depth discussion of these results would be valuable.

Although the idea is sound, the results from Table 4 are not entirely convincing, with only small improvements detected (and not in all scenarios). Conducting a statistical significance test for the results from Table 4 would help support the claims.

Minor comments include:

- In Section 2.1, it is worth noting that projection can also be performed via methods that do not require parallel data, making such models more widely applicable (even for languages without parallel resources). Examples of such work include Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which exploit bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links for transfer.
- The paper would benefit from a more careful proofreading, as several typos were detected (e.g., "as a the base model" in the first sentence of Section 3, "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." on Page 3, and "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8).
