This paper explores the cross-lingual direct transfer of Named Entity Recognition (NER) models using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the key idea may not be highly innovative or creative, as it does not introduce any core new technology, the contribution is still significant as it combines two research paths: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (such as NER, parsing, or POS tagging), and (2) recent developments in cross-lingual wikification technology. Despite the incremental nature of the contribution, the paper is well-structured and presents a clear story, supported by sufficient experiments and empirical evidence, with convincing results.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed description of related work, particularly in relation to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). A clear distinction should be drawn between this work and other NER systems that have relied on encyclopedic Wikipedia knowledge. Although the differences are mentioned in the text, they need to be emphasized to facilitate reading and contextualize the work.

The authors' decision to exclude POS tags as features is justified, but it would be interesting to report experiments with POS tags features, similar to Tackstrom et al. This would provide empirical evidence on the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features for different languages, particularly those with available universal POS tags.

Section 3.3 could be improved with a running example to illustrate how the edited model from Tsai and Roth works. The current description is not entirely clear, and an example would help to clarify the process.

As the authors mention that the approaches from Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to theirs and can be combined, it would be beneficial to report preliminary results on a selection of languages using the combined models. This would add depth to the discussion and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach. Additionally, comparing the results with a strong projection baseline would help to contextualize the findings and highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

The paper's results show that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish is the best language for Yoruba. It would be interesting to explore these findings in more depth, discussing whether this is a statistical coincidence or if there are more underlying factors at play.

The results in Table 4 are not entirely convincing, with only small improvements detected in some scenarios. A statistical significance test reported for these results would help to support the claims and provide more robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Minor comments include:

* Section 2.1 could be expanded to mention that projection can also be performed via methods that do not require parallel data, making such models more widely applicable. For example, the work of Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013) exploits bilingual semantic spaces instead of direct alignment links to perform the transfer.
* The paper would benefit from a more careful proofreading to correct typos and improve clarity. For instance, the first sentence of Section 3 contains a typo ("as a the base model"), and the sentence "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by..." is unclear. Additionally, the phrase "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8 could be rephrased for better clarity.
