Summary and contributions:
- The paper tackles cross-lingual NER in a direct-transfer setting by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikifier to produce language-independent features (Wikipedia categories, Freebase types, and ranker scores) for every n-gram, and integrating these into an NER model trained on one or multiple source languages.
- In my view, the main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification yields strong delexicalized features that substantially improve direct-transfer NER across diverse languages, including low-resource ones; (2) an extensive empirical study over nine languages, with analyses of Wikipedia size effects and multi-source training; (3) practical adaptations to a cross-lingual wikifier to support dense n-gram grounding and its effective use in NER.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical evidence and breadth. The paper evaluates on CoNLL (DE, ES, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), showing clear gains from wikifier features both in monolingual and direct-transfer settings. The improvements over comparable direct-transfer baselines are consistent and often large (e.g., +10 F1 on several languages).
- Resource-light yet competitive. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel corpora, no native speaker interaction). Despite this modest resource footprint, it outperforms alternatives that rely on parallel data or manual effort (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016).
- Insightful analyses. The paper examines how Wikipedia size and English–target intersections affect wikifier feature quality, providing a plausible explanation for weaker gains on very low-resource Wikipedias. The analysis is careful and informative.
- Generality and orthogonality. The features are delexicalized and broadly applicable. The method is complementary to other cross-lingual techniques (e.g., projection, multilingual clustering), suggesting potential for further gains via combination.
- Multi-source training. The exploration of training on multiple languages is valuable. Although gains are modest, results show that adding annotated data in other languages generally helps and rarely harms.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty. The central idea combines known strands (direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification) without introducing a fundamentally new modeling approach. The contribution is primarily empirical and integrative.
- Clarity gaps in Section 3.3. The modifications to the Tsai & Roth wikifier and how features are extracted and used for every n-gram are not fully transparent. A running example would significantly improve comprehension.
- Missing baselines and combinations. Given the stated orthogonality, it would strengthen the case to include: (a) a POS-tag feature variant (as in Täckström et al.) for languages with reliable universal POS; (b) a strong projection baseline; (c) a combination with multilingual word clusters or silver data from Wikipedia (e.g., Nothman et al.). This would contextualize the gains and clarify the added value of wikification features.
- Statistical rigor. Improvements in Table 4 (multi-source training) are small and not always consistent; significance testing would bolster the claims.
- Related work positioning. The relationship to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-driven NER systems should be articulated more precisely, highlighting methodological differences and why this approach scales cross-lingually where others do not.
- Interpretation of language-pair findings. The observation that Dutch works best for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba is intriguing; the paper should explore whether this reflects script overlap, domain similarity, Wikidata/Wikipedia linkage density, or training set composition rather than chance.

Questions to authors:
- Section 3.3: Could you provide a concrete, step-by-step example showing how n-gram candidates are generated, filtered, linked, and how categories/types/ranker scores become features for tokens?
- POS features: For languages where universal POS tags are available with reasonable accuracy, what is the impact of adding POS features in your framework?
- Combinations: Can you report preliminary results combining your features with (i) multilingual word clusters induced from parallel text, and/or (ii) silver training data generated from Wikipedia (e.g., Nothman et al.)?
- Projection baseline: How does your model compare to a strong projection system (word/phrase alignment or alignment-free projection using cross-lingual embeddings) under comparable resources?
- Multi-source significance: Are the gains in Table 4 statistically significant? If not, can you characterize when multi-source training helps most?
- Training-language anomalies: Do you have hypotheses or evidence explaining why Dutch→Spanish and Spanish→Yoruba are best among non-target sources?

Additional comments:
- Broader view on projection: In Section 2.1, you may wish to mention projection methods that do not require parallel corpora but rely on bilingual semantic spaces or induced lexicons (e.g., Peirsman & Padó, NAACL 2009; Vulić & Moens, EMNLP 2013), which broadens applicability to truly low-resource settings.
- Proofreading: Several grammatical issues should be corrected, e.g., “as a the base model” (Section 3), “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by …”, “to disambiguate every n-grams” (p. 8), and other minor typos.
- Terminology: Standardize “Freebase” (capitalization) and ensure consistent use of entity type names.
- Reproducibility: Briefly state key hyperparameters and resources for the wikifier (version, dumps, intersection sizes), and clarify handling of scripts and tokenization in non-Latin languages.
