Summary
This paper proposes a multilingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification. Mentions (n-grams) in a target language are grounded to English Wikipedia entries, and their associated Wikipedia categories and Freebase types are used as language-independent features. The authors train on one or more source languages and evaluate both monolingually and in a direct transfer setting. The approach improves over strong baselines across multiple languages, including low-resource and non-Latin-script languages.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Introduction of wikifier-derived, language-independent features (from English Wikipedia categories and Freebase types) for NER, applicable across languages without parallel data.
2) Demonstration that these features substantially improve direct transfer performance, with additional evidence from monolingual settings and analyses on low-resource and non-Latin languages.
3) A thorough empirical study, including ablations (wikifier-only, base, +gazetteer, +wikifier) and multi-source training, that clarifies when and why the features help.

Strengths
- Clear, novel feature design: Using cross-lingual wikification to generate delexicalized features is simple, broadly applicable, and aligns well with the goal of language independence. The feature source (English Wikipedia/Freebase) enables transfer to many target languages without language-specific engineering.
- Comprehensive evaluation and ablation: The paper evaluates on 9 languages, comparing monolingual and direct transfer scenarios, and systematically adds feature groups (+gazetteers, +wikifier). The consistent gains from wikifier features, even on top of strong baselines, convincingly support the main claim.
- Relevance to low-resource and non-Latin languages: The analysis on languages with small Wikipedias and non-Latin scripts is particularly valuable. The results and discussion (e.g., the poor direct transfer performance in Bengali and Tamil when lexical features are included) highlight practical deployment considerations.
- Practicality of resources: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no sentence- or word-aligned parallel corpora, and no target-language annotation), which improves accessibility for genuinely low-resource settings.
- Multi-source training: The observation that training on multiple languages typically improves target performance (even if modestly) is useful and supports broader applicability.

Weaknesses
- Coverage and unseen entities: It is unclear how the method behaves for named entities not present in Wikipedia or lacking reliable interlanguage links/Freebase types. Since the features rely on successful grounding, entities outside Wikipedia (or with sparse metadata) may be disadvantaged. A quantitative breakdown of performance on in-Wikipedia vs out-of-Wikipedia entities would clarify any coverage bias.
- Error propagation from wikification: The approach depends heavily on the cross-lingual wikifier. It would help to quantify how often NER errors are attributable to wikifier errors (incorrect disambiguation vs NIL cases) and to show the effect of varying linker confidence thresholds. This would contextualize the reported gains and failure cases.
- Over-reliance on lexical features in non-Latin scripts: The paper notes poor direct transfer results on Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are included, suggesting the model can overweight features that do not transfer. It would be valuable to regularize or weight feature groups differently (e.g., group-wise regularization or feature priors) to prevent the model from overwhelming the wikifier signal in such settings.

Questions for the Authors
- How does the method perform on entities not present in Wikipedia or without interlanguage links/Freebase types? Can you provide F1 stratified by (a) entities successfully linked, (b) linked but incorrectly disambiguated, and (c) NIL/unlinked entities?
- What fraction of overall NER errors can be traced to wikifier errors? A controlled analysis (e.g., masking wikifier outputs, perturbing candidate rankings, or using gold links where available) would be informative.
- Have you tried group-wise regularization or feature reweighting (e.g., different L2 penalties per feature class, or prior weights) to mitigate over-reliance on lexical features, especially for non-Latin-script targets?
- Can you report core wikifier statistics per language (linking coverage, NIL rate, top-1 accuracy/confidence) on your NER test sets, and correlate these with NER gains?

Overall assessment
I liked this paper. It introduces a useful language-independent feature family, supports the claims with thorough experiments and ablations, and offers practical insights for low-resource and non-Latin-script languages. Addressing the questions above—especially coverage for unseen entities and error attribution to the wikifier—would further strengthen the work.
