Summary and Contributions
- The paper studies cross-lingual direct transfer for NER, proposing to derive language-independent features from a cross-lingual wikifier that grounds n-grams in any language to English Wikipedia entries (and their Freebase types and Wikipedia categories). The model is trained on one or more source languages and applied directly to target languages without parallel data or target-language annotation.
- Primary contribution: Demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification signals serve as strong, delexicalized features for NER, enabling effective direct transfer across a wide range of languages (including low-resource ones).
- Secondary contribution: A comprehensive empirical study on 9 languages showing consistent gains over comparable direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) and over a non-direct transfer method for low-resource languages (Zhang et al.), and showing that the wikifier features also improve monolingual NER.
- Tertiary contribution: Showing that multi-source training generally yields modest further improvements and analyzing how Wikipedia size affects feature quality and transfer effectiveness.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent approach requiring minimal resources. The method needs only multilingual Wikipedia and a cross-lingual wikifier; it avoids parallel corpora, alignment, or language-specific heuristics. This makes it broadly applicable, especially for low-resource settings.
- Strong and thorough empirical validation. Experiments cover CoNLL languages (ES, DE, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA). The study includes monolingual vs. transfer settings, ablations (base/gazetteer/wikifier-only), and a careful analysis of when features help most (e.g., Latin vs. non-Latin scripts).
- Competitive or superior results against strong baselines. The approach outperforms the direct-transfer baseline of Täckström et al. without relying on bitext-induced clusters, and also exceeds the expectation-learning approach of Zhang et al. on all five low-resource languages despite using fewer resources and no human-in-the-loop.
- Insightful analysis of feature quality and resource dependence. The paper convincingly links the effectiveness of wikifier features to Wikipedia size and intersection with English, with experiments that degrade interlanguage links to mimic low-resource conditions.
- Practical guidance. The study offers actionable recommendations: include all features for Latin-script targets; rely more on wikifier features for non-Latin scripts where lexical overlap is minimal.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The work is primarily an integration of two existing lines (direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification), with incremental methodological changes rather than a core new algorithmic contribution.
- Related work positioning needs strengthening. The paper should more clearly differentiate its approach from prior uses of Wikipedia-derived features for NER, notably Kazama and Torisawa (2007), and other NER systems leveraging encyclopedic knowledge. The distinctions are present but not sufficiently emphasized to help readers place the work.
- Missing POS-tag feature comparison. Although the authors explain why POS tags were excluded, reporting results with universal POS tags (where available) would give readers empirical evidence about their utility or lack thereof across languages, and would allow a more direct comparison to Täckström et al.
- Section 3.3 clarity. The description of the adapted Tsai and Roth wikifier would benefit from a running example to make the candidate generation and ranking modifications concrete, given the atypical use case of grounding every n-gram.
- Limited comparative baselines and combinations. Since the paper argues orthogonality to Täckström’s multilingual clusters and Nothman’s Wikipedia-derived silver data, it would be valuable to include preliminary combined results to assess complementarity. Likewise, comparing to a strong projection baseline (including projection methods that do not require direct parallel alignment) would better contextualize the gains and limitations of wikification-based transfer.
- Multi-source training gains are small and not always consistent. Table 4 shows modest improvements; reporting statistical significance would strengthen the claims.
- Training-language effects need more analysis. The finding that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, deserves deeper discussion beyond a brief report—are these coincidences or is there a typological/resource-driven explanation?

Questions to Authors
- Can you more explicitly contrast your approach with Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER methods? What specific aspects of your wikifier features and pipeline enable better robustness and cross-lingual transfer?
- Could you report experiments adding universal POS tags where available, following Täckström et al., and comment on their effect across scripts and language families?
- Please add a running example in Section 3.3 that walks through candidate generation and ranking for n-grams, illustrating the two modifications (whole-string queries for n>1; excluding mention-embedding features).
- Since you note orthogonality, can you share preliminary results combining your model with (a) multilingual clusters induced from bitext (as in Täckström) and/or (b) silver data generated from Wikipedia (as in Nothman)? Even a subset of languages would help.
- Can you compare against at least one strong projection baseline, including approaches that do not require sentence/word-aligned parallel text (e.g., using bilingual semantic spaces)?
- What explains Dutch→Spanish and Spanish→Yoruba being the best non-target training options? Is this due to annotation distributions, Wikipedia intersection sizes, domain overlap, or typological factors?
- Are the multi-source training gains in Table 4 statistically significant? If so, please report significance tests; if not, please discuss potential reasons.

Additional Comments
- Related work: Projection can also be performed without parallel data via bilingual semantic spaces; see Peirsman and Padó (NAACL 2009) and Vulić and Moens (EMNLP 2013). Including these helps situate your approach among transfer methods with minimal resource assumptions.
- Proofreading: there are several typos and phrasing issues that should be corrected, e.g., “as a the base model”; an unparsable sentence on Page 3 (“They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...”); “to disambiguate every n-grams” (Page 8). A careful pass will improve readability.

Overall Assessment
While not highly novel, the paper is well-executed, empirically solid, and presents a compelling, broadly applicable approach to cross-lingual NER with minimal resources. With stronger positioning in related work, added baselines/combinations, clearer exposition of the wikifier adaptations, and significance testing, it would make a valuable contribution.
