This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer for NER by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification system. Conceptually, the novelty is limited: it does not introduce fundamentally new techniques. The contribution is primarily incremental, integrating two established lines of work: (1) direct-transfer approaches for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging), and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. Nevertheless, I found the work well put together: the narrative is clear, the experimental coverage is substantial, and the results are persuasive. I do, however, have several remarks regarding the presentation.

Related work: please provide a more detailed account of how this work relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). It would also help to more explicitly articulate how this approach differs from other NER systems that exploit Wikipedia-derived encyclopedic knowledge. While the text mentions distinctions, highlighting them more prominently would make the positioning clearer.

Although the paper explains the decision to omit POS-tag features, it would still be useful to report experiments with POS features in the style of Tackstrom et al., at least for languages where universal POS tags are available. This would offer empirical insight into the value (or lack thereof) of such features across languages.

Section 3.3 would benefit from a concrete running example; as written, it remains somewhat unclear how the modified Tsai and Roth model functions after your changes.

Since the paper repeatedly notes that the approaches from Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to this work and could be combined with it, it would strengthen the contribution to include preliminary results on a subset of languages demonstrating such combinations. Along the same lines, even though projection is orthogonal, including a comparison against a strong projection baseline would situate the results more firmly and help illustrate the strengths and limitations of wikification-based methods.

Why does Dutch emerge as the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba? Is this merely a statistical quirk, or is there a more substantive explanation? A short analysis discussing these findings would be informative.

While the idea is sensible, the Table 4 gains are modest (and not uniform). Reporting statistical significance tests for Table 4 would help support the claims.

Minor comments:
- Sect. 2.1: Projection can also be achieved via methods that do not require parallel data, which broadens applicability to languages without parallel resources; e.g., Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links.
- There are multiple typos; careful proofreading would help (e.g., first sentence of Section 3: “as a the base model”; an ungrammatical sentence on Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...”; Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”).
