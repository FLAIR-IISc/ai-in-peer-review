- Proposes a language-independent NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to map n-grams to English Wikipedia titles and extract Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as delexicalized features.
- Trains on one or multiple source languages and applies the model to new languages without target-language supervision; experiments cover nine languages across scripts and resource levels with ablations on Wikipedia size and feature sources.

Contributions:
- Introduces a simple, language-agnostic feature family from cross-lingual wikification that substantially improves direct-transfer NER across typologically diverse languages.
- Shows wikifier-derived features also improve monolingual NER and that multi-source training yields further gains.
- Analyzes how Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage affect feature effectiveness, with ablation studies quantifying this dependence.

Strengths:
- Wikifier-derived delexicalized features produce large gains over direct-transfer baselines and outperform prior cross-lingual methods on both boundary detection and type assignment.
- Extensive experiments across nine languages, multiple settings (monolingual, direct transfer, multi-source), and informative ablations give a clear picture of when and why the method helps.
- Method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps (no parallel data, bilingual lexicons, or target-language annotation), making it practical and reproducible.
- Provides actionable guidance for practitioners on when to downweight lexical features (e.g., non-Latin scripts or very small Wikipedias) versus combining feature groups (Latin scripts).
- Demonstrates utility in monolingual training and added benefits from pooling multiple source languages.

Weaknesses:
- Performance depends on wikifier coverage and quality; results degrade for languages with small Wikipedias or weak interlanguage links, and the paper lacks quantification of error propagation from wikification to NER errors.
- Unclear handling of entities absent from Wikipedia (NIL/out-of-KB mentions); no targeted evaluation on such cases.
- Tag-set remapping for low-resource languages (removing/remapping MISC/TIME/TTL) may complicate comparability and could bias results without detailed reporting.
- Core NER model is a perceptron-based system with classic features; comparisons against stronger modern baselines (multilingual neural models, cross-lingual embeddings) and combinations with orthogonal transfer methods are missing.
- Potential efficiency and scalability concerns from wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4) are not addressed with runtime, throughput, or memory measurements.

Questions for authors:
- How does performance differ for mentions that link to English titles versus NIL mentions, and what is the proportion of NIL mentions per language?
- Can you quantify error propagation from the wikifier (e.g., bucket performance by wikifier confidence or NER F1 excluding low-confidence wikifier features)?
- Did you try group-wise regularization or feature reweighting to mitigate over-reliance on lexical cues (especially for Bengali/Tamil)?
- What are the runtime and resource costs of wikifying all n-grams (average processing speed per sentence/document and memory usage)?
- How feasible is replacing Freebase types with Wikidata types and would you expect similar gains?
- In multi-source training, did you try weighting sources by Wikipedia size, script similarity, or dev-set performance, and is there evidence of negative transfer for particular source-target pairs?

Additional comments:
- Extending the ablation linking interlanguage coverage to NER F1 with a predictive model (e.g., expected F1 vs. intersection size) could help practitioners estimate performance for new languages.
- A qualitative error analysis (boundary vs. type errors and common confusions) would complement quantitative findings and guide further improvements.
