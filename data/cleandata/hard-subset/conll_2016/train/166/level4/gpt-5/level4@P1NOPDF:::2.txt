This paper studies cross-lingual direct transfer for NER using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the central idea is not particularly novel—the work does not introduce a fundamentally new technique—the contribution is a solid, incremental integration of two research lines: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging), and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. I found the paper coherent and well-motivated, with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence to support its claims, and the results are generally convincing. I nonetheless have several comments on the presentation and evaluation.

Related work: Please expand the discussion of how this paper relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007), and clearly delineate differences from other NER systems that leverage Wikipedia or encyclopedic knowledge. While some distinctions are mentioned, they should be emphasized more strongly to help readers situate the contribution.

POS features: Although you justify excluding POS features, it would be valuable to report experiments with POS features similar to Täckström et al. This would give readers an empirically grounded view of the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features across languages, at least for those with universal POS annotations.

Section 3.3: This section would benefit from a running example. As written, it is difficult to understand precisely how the adapted model from Tsai and Roth operates in your setting. A step-by-step illustration would greatly clarify the method.

Orthogonality and combinations: Since you repeatedly note that the approaches of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal and can be combined with your method, it would strengthen the paper to include preliminary combined results for a subset of languages. This would make the discussion more concrete. Along the same lines, please compare against a strong projection-based baseline to further contextualize the gains and to highlight the strengths and limitations of the wikification-based approach.

Language pair anomalies: Why is Dutch the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish the best for Yoruba? Is this a statistical artifact or is there a linguistic or resource-driven explanation (e.g., domain, annotation conventions, Wikipedia coverage)? A brief analysis would be insightful.

Table 4: The improvements here appear small and not consistent across all scenarios. Please include statistical significance testing to substantiate these gains.

Minor comments:
- Section 2.1: Projection can also be done without parallel data, making such methods more broadly applicable (even to languages lacking parallel corpora). See, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulić and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links for transfer.
- Typos and clarity issues: The paper would benefit from careful proofreading. Examples include:
  - First sentence of Section 3: “as a the base model”
  - Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by ...” (sentence appears incomplete or ungrammatical)
  - Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear narrative and solid empirical work. Strengthening the related work positioning, clarifying Section 3.3 with an example, adding comparisons with orthogonal and projection-based baselines, discussing the surprising language-pair results, and supporting small improvements with significance tests would substantially improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
