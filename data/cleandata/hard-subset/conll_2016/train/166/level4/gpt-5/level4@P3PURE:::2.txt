This paper studies cross-lingual direct transfer of NER models using a very recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea is not highly innovative—it does not introduce a fundamentally new technique—the work makes an incremental contribution by combining two research directions: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging), and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. Despite the limited novelty, I found the paper coherent and clearly motivated, with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence to support its claims, and overall convincing results. I do, however, have several comments aimed at improving the presentation and contextualization.

Related work and positioning:
- Please provide a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007).
- Clearly articulate how your approach differs from other NER systems that leverage encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia. While differences are mentioned, they should be emphasized more explicitly to help readers situate the contribution.

Features and ablations:
- Although you argue for excluding POS tags as features, it would be valuable to report experiments that include POS features, following Täckström et al. This would offer an empirically grounded view of the utility (or lack thereof) of such features across languages, at least for those with universal POS annotations.

Clarity of the modeling section:
- Section 3.3 would benefit from a running example. The current description leaves it unclear how the adapted model from Tsai and Roth operates in your setup.

Orthogonality and baselines:
- Since you state that the approaches of Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to yours and can be combined, it would strengthen the paper to include preliminary combined results for a selection of languages. This would enrich the discussion.
- Along the same lines, please compare with a strong projection baseline to better contextualize your results and to highlight the usefulness (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

Analysis of cross-lingual transfer patterns:
- Why is Dutch the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish the best for Yoruba? Is this a statistical coincidence, or is there a deeper explanation (e.g., linguistic similarity, resource overlap, or properties of the wikification signal)? A brief analysis would be informative.

Results and statistical support:
- Although the approach is sound, the improvements reported in Table 4 are modest and not consistent across all settings. Please include statistical significance testing for Table 4 to substantiate the claims.

Minor comments:
- Section 2.1: Projection can also be performed without parallel data, which broadens applicability to low-resource settings. See, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulić and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which exploit bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links.
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to fix typos and unclear phrasing. Examples include:
  - First sentence of Section 3: “as a the base model”
  - An unparseable sentence (please review around that location)
  - Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by ...”
  - Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”

Overall assessment:
This is a well-executed, clearly presented empirical study with convincing results, albeit with incremental novelty. Strengthening the related work positioning, adding POS-feature experiments, clarifying Section 3.3 with a running example, including comparisons with orthogonal methods and a strong projection baseline, and reporting statistical significance for Table 4 would substantially improve the paper.
