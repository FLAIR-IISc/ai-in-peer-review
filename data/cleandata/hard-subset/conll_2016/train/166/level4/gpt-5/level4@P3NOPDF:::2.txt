Summary
This paper studies cross-lingual direct transfer for NER by leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the approach is largely incremental—linking two well-established lines of work (direct transfer for downstream tasks and cross-lingual wikification)—the paper is coherent, well motivated, and supported by a solid set of experiments. The empirical results are generally convincing and the narrative is clear. I believe the paper can make a useful contribution with some clarifications and additional analysis.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a coherent story connecting wikification to NER transfer.
- Broad and careful experimental setup with results that, in most cases, support the claims.
- Sensible positioning as complementary to prior transfer methods (e.g., projection-based or distant-supervision approaches).

Major comments and suggestions
1) Related work and positioning
- Please expand the discussion of how your approach relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007), and more broadly to NER systems that exploit Wikipedia/encyclopedic knowledge. The paper notes differences, but these should be made more explicit to help readers situate your contribution. For example, clarify what is novel in the way you operationalize cross-lingual wikification for direct transfer compared with earlier Wikipedia-aware NER methods (feature design, learning setup, or cross-lingual signal).

2) POS features
- Although you justify omitting POS tags, it would be valuable to report supplementary experiments with POS features in the style of Tackstrom et al. (2012), at least for languages with reliable universal POS tags. Even a small subset would provide empirical evidence on whether POS helps or is redundant when combined with your wikification signals, and whether effects are language-dependent.

3) Clarity of Section 3.3
- Section 3.3 would benefit from a running example that walks through how the edited model from Tsai and Roth is integrated and used. The current description is hard to follow; a step-by-step illustration (e.g., mention the input sentence, generated candidates, disambiguation step, and how the resulting features feed into NER) would substantially improve clarity.

4) Orthogonality and combinations
- You emphasize that the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal and potentially combinable with yours. Please substantiate this claim with preliminary combined results on a subset of languages. Even a small table of combined vs. individual methods would strengthen the paper and enrich the discussion.
- Along the same lines, include a strong projection baseline for context. This could be either word-alignment-based projection or, if parallel data are scarce, projection methods that do not require parallel corpora (see Minor comments). Such comparisons would more clearly show the benefits and limitations of wikification-driven transfer.

5) Language-pair transfer patterns
- The observation that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, is intriguing. Is this a statistical artifact or does it reflect properties such as Wikipedia coverage, entity type distributions, annotation conventions, lexical overlap, or domain similarity? A brief analysis or hypothesis—supported by corpus statistics if available—would add depth and help readers interpret cross-lingual transfer dynamics.

6) Statistical significance
- Table 4 shows small improvements in some settings, and not uniformly across scenarios. Please report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap resampling at the sentence level or approximate randomization) or variance over multiple runs. This is particularly important where gains are modest.

Minor comments
- Projection without parallel data: In Section 2.1, note that projection can be performed via methods that do not require parallel corpora, which increases applicability to low-resource languages. See, for instance, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignments for transfer.
- Proofreading: The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to fix typos and ungrammatical phrases. Examples include:
  - “as a the base model” (first sentence of Section 3)
  - “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...” (Page 3; the sentence is difficult to parse)
  - “to disambiguate every n-grams” (Page 8)
- When making claims about complementarity or orthogonality, briefly characterize the error profiles you expect from each method; this can guide readers on when combinations are most likely to help.
- If space permits, provide brief statistics on the coverage of the wikification system across languages (e.g., link density, entity coverage by type), as this may correlate with transfer performance.

Overall assessment
The paper’s main idea is incremental rather than fundamentally novel, but it is well executed and empirically grounded. With stronger positioning in related work, clearer exposition of the wikification integration (Section 3.3), additional baselines/combined results, statistical significance testing, and a short analysis of language-pair idiosyncrasies, the paper would present a compelling and practically useful contribution to cross-lingual NER transfer. I encourage the authors to incorporate these revisions.
