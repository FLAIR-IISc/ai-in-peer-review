This paper studies cross-lingual direct transfer for NER using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the central idea is not highly novel and does not introduce a fundamentally new technique, the work makes a solid incremental contribution by combining two lines of research: (1) direct transfer for downstream tasks (NER, parsing, POS), and (2) advances in cross-lingual wikification. Despite the incremental nature, I found the paper clear, coherent, and backed by sufficient experiments and convincing evidence. Below are suggestions to improve presentation and context.

Related work: The relationship to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) should be explained in more detail. Please also more explicitly differentiate your approach from prior NER systems that leverage Wikipedia or encyclopedic knowledge. While some distinctions are mentioned, emphasizing them more clearly would help readers situate the contribution.

POS features: Although the paper justifies excluding POS features, it would be informative to report experiments with POS features similar to those in Tackstrom et al., at least for languages with universal POS annotations. This would provide empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of POS across languages.

Section 3.3: This section would benefit from a running example. As written, the description of the modified model based on Tsai and Roth is not fully clear; a step-by-step example would clarify how the edited model operates.

Combining with orthogonal approaches: Since the methods of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are presented as orthogonal and combinable with your approach, it would strengthen the paper to include preliminary combined results for a subset of languages. Similarly, including a strong projection-based baseline would better contextualize performance and highlight the strengths or limitations of wikification-based methods.

Language transfer patterns: The finding that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish for Yoruba, merits discussion. Is this due to chance, typological similarity, resource coverage in Wikipedia, script, or named entity distribution? A brief analysis would add useful insight.

Table 4: The idea is sound, but the gains are modest and not consistent across all settings. Please include statistical significance testing for the Table 4 results to substantiate the claims.

Minor comments:
- Section 2.1: Note that projection can be performed without parallel data, broadening applicability to low-resource scenarios. See, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignments.
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading. Examples: Section 3 first sentence (“as a the base model”); an unclear sentence on Page 3 (“They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by ...”); and “to disambiguate every n-grams” on Page 8.
