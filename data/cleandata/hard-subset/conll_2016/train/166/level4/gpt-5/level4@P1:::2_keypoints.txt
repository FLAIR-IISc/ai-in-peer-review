- Paper proposes using a cross-lingual wikifier to derive language-independent features for direct-transfer NER, training on source languages and applying to targets without parallel data or annotations.
- Primary contribution: showing wikification signals are strong delexicalized features enabling effective cross-lingual NER transfer, including low-resource languages.
- Secondary contribution: comprehensive empirical study on 9 languages with consistent gains over direct-transfer and some non-direct-transfer baselines, and improvements for monolingual NER.
- Tertiary contribution: analysis of multi-source training (modest gains) and the impact of Wikipedia size/interlanguage links on feature quality and transfer.
- Strength: simple, coherent method requiring only multilingual Wikipedia and a wikifier, avoiding parallel corpora or language-specific heuristics.
- Strength: thorough experiments across CoNLL and low-resource languages, with ablations and analyses (scripts, feature subsets).
- Strength: competitive or superior to strong baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) while using fewer resources.
- Strength: insightful analysis linking wikifier effectiveness to Wikipedia resource size and interlanguage overlap.
- Strength: practical recommendations (use all features for Latin scripts; prioritize wikifier for non-Latin scripts).
- Weakness: limited novelty—mainly an integration of existing ideas rather than a new algorithmic advance.
- Weakness: related work positioning needs strengthening, especially versus prior Wikipedia-derived NER methods like Kazama & Torisawa (2007).
- Weakness: missing empirical comparison including universal POS-tag features (as used by Täckström et al.).
- Weakness: Section 3.3 (wikifier adaptations) lacks clarity and would benefit from a running example.
- Weakness: limited comparative baselines and no experiments combining with multilingual clusters or Wikipedia silver data to test complementarity.
- Weakness: multi-source training gains are small and inconsistent; statistical significance is not reported.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of surprising training-language effects (e.g., Dutch→Spanish, Spanish→Yoruba).
- Question: explicitly contrast this approach with Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER—what makes your features more robust for cross-lingual transfer?
- Question: please report experiments adding universal POS tags where available and their effects across scripts/families.
- Question: add a running example in Section 3.3 illustrating candidate generation/ranking and the two wikifier adaptations.
- Question: provide preliminary results combining your features with (a) bitext-induced multilingual clusters and/or (b) Wikipedia-derived silver data.
- Question: compare to at least one strong projection baseline that does not require sentence/word-aligned parallel text (e.g., bilingual semantic spaces).
- Question: explain why Dutch→Spanish and Spanish→Yoruba are the best non-target training choices—resource overlap, annotation bias, typology, or coincidence?
- Question: are multi-source training gains statistically significant? If not, discuss possible reasons.
- Additional comment: cite projection methods without parallel data (e.g., Peirsman & Padó 2009; Vulić & Moens 2013) to situate the approach among low-resource transfer methods.
- Additional comment: proofread for typos and unclear sentences (several instances noted).
- Overall: not highly novel but well-executed and empirically solid; with stronger related-work positioning, extra baselines/combinations, clearer wikifier exposition, and significance testing it would be a valuable contribution.
