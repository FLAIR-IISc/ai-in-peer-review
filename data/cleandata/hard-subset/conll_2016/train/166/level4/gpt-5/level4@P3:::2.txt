Summary and Contributions
This paper studies cross-lingual NER in a direct-transfer setting, leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) by grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia. The model combines these features with standard non-lexical and lexical features and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers. It is trained on one or multiple source languages and applied to many target languages without relying on parallel data.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) Empirical demonstration that wikification-derived, delexicalized features enable strong direct-transfer NER across languages, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) on CoNLL languages and achieving competitive performance on low-resource languages, with no parallel corpora or target supervision.
2) Analysis linking the quality of wikifier features to Wikipedia size and interlanguage/Freebase coverage, including informative ablations (removing Freebase queries and interlanguage links) that explain performance variation across languages/scripts.
3) Evidence that the same language-independent features improve monolingual NER and enable modest gains from multi-source training.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual results with minimal resources: Training only on English, the model substantially improves over direct-transfer baselines on Dutch, German, and Spanish, and beats Zhang et al. (2016) on Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil while assuming fewer resources (no parallel text, no native speaker). The wikifier features consistently add large gains in direct transfer (e.g., +10 F1 on several languages), highlighting their utility as delexicalized signals.
- Clear, coherent methodology and broad evaluation: The paper evaluates across nine languages spanning Latin and non-Latin scripts, and reports both monolingual and cross-lingual settings. The practical takeaway (include all features for Latin-script targets; rely more on wikifier features for non-Latin targets) is useful.
- Informative ablations: The study that removes Freebase queries and interlanguage links (Table 3) convincingly shows why low-resource languages with small Wikipedias or poor Freebase coverage gain less from wikifier features. This connects outcomes to measurable resource properties and supports the proposed explanation.
- Generality and simplicity: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no sentence/word alignments), and the wikifier modifications are lightweight. The model also improves monolingual NER for all nine languages when adding wikifier features to strong baselines.
- Multi-source training is feasible: Although gains are small, the experiments show that pooling labeled data from multiple languages does not typically hurt and can help, especially when including all CoNLL languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The work primarily integrates an existing cross-lingual wikifier into a standard NER framework with conventional features and Wikipedia gazetteers. The main contribution is empirical rather than algorithmic; this should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Incomplete comparisons with orthogonal methods: The paper emphasizes orthogonality to projection and multilingual clustering/silver-data approaches (Täckström et al., Nothman et al.), yet does not report combinations or strong projection baselines. Including even preliminary combined results (e.g., with multilingual clusters or WIKI-derived silver data) would better substantiate claims of complementarity and set a stronger context.
- POS features not evaluated: While the authors explain not using POS due to tagger quality and availability, an empirical ablation (where universal POS is available) would inform readers about the incremental utility (or lack thereof) of POS in this setting, especially relative to wikifier features.
- Clarity of Section 3.3: The description of the modifications to the Tsai & Roth wikifier for “wikify every n-gram” is terse. A small running example (step-by-step candidate generation, ranking features, how types are aggregated over overlapping n-grams) would make the pipeline reproducible and remove ambiguity.
- Multi-source training gains are small and not always positive: Table 4 shows modest improvements absent statistical significance testing. Without significance analysis, the practical value of multi-source training remains unclear. Additional discussion of when and why certain training languages help (e.g., Spanish→Yoruba) is also missing.

Questions to Authors
- Could you include an ablation with POS-tag features (for languages with universal POS available) to quantify their effect in both monolingual and direct transfer settings?
- You claim orthogonality with Täckström’s multilingual clusters and with WIKI-derived silver data (Nothman et al.). Can you report preliminary combined results on a subset of languages to substantiate complementarity?
- Please provide a detailed running example of the wikification-for-every-n-gram pipeline in Section 3.3: candidate generation restrictions, ranking features retained/removed, handling overlaps, and how features from the top-k candidates are aggregated into token-level NER features.
- The training-language heatmap suggests Dutch is best for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba. Is this due to data size/annotation style, typological proximity, Wikipedia intersection structure, or lexical overlap in gazetteers? Any analysis (e.g., feature weight transferability, coverage statistics) would be valuable.
- For Table 4, can you report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or McNemar) for the multi-source improvements? Also, are there cases where multi-source hurts, and why?

Additional Comments
- Related work: Please expand the comparison to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER approaches, clearly articulating methodological differences (robust disambiguation, cross-lingual applicability, feature types) and limitations.
- Projection without parallel data: Note that some projection/transfer methods leverage bilingual semantic spaces rather than explicit alignments (e.g., Peirsman & Padó, NAACL 2009; Vulić & Moens, EMNLP 2013). Positioning your approach relative to these would further clarify the landscape.
- Label mapping: You modify MISC to LOC based on Freebase types and drop others for low-resource evaluations. Please clarify the impact on comparability, and consider reporting a sensitivity analysis to this mapping.
- Proofreading: Several typos and awkward phrases should be corrected (e.g., “as a the base model,” “This sentence is not parsable,” “to disambiguate every n-grams,” and “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by ...”). A careful edit will improve readability.
- Practical aspects: A brief note on computational cost/latency of wikifying all n-grams, and failure modes (e.g., NIL rates, ambiguous titles), would help practitioners assess scalability.

Overall evaluation: A solid empirical paper with a clear story and strong results that demonstrate the value of wikification-derived features for cross-lingual NER with minimal resources. Addressing the missing comparisons, clarifying Section 3.3, and adding significance tests would substantially strengthen the submission.
