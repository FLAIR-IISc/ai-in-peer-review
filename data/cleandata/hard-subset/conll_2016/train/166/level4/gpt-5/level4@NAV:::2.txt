This paper investigates cross-lingual direct transfer for NER by leveraging a very recent cross-lingual wikification model. Overall, the central idea is not particularly novel, as it does not introduce fundamentally new technology. The contribution is largely incremental, combining two research strands: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging) and (2) recent advances in cross-lingual wikification. That said, I liked the paper: it presents a coherent, clearly articulated narrative with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence to substantiate its claims, yielding convincing results. I nonetheless have several comments regarding the presentation.

Related work: the related work section should more thoroughly explain how this paper relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). It should also more clearly differentiate this approach from other NER systems that, in various ways, rely on Wikipedia-derived encyclopedic knowledge. While these distinctions are mentioned, they should be emphasized further to aid readability and contextualization.

Although the authors motivate their decision to omit POS tags as features, it would still be informative to report experiments with POS tag features akin to Tackstrom et al. This would provide an empirically grounded overview of the usefulness (or lack thereof) of such features across languages (at least for those with universal POS tags available).

Section 3.3 would benefit from a running example, as it remains unclear how the modified Tsai and Roth model currently operates (the description is not entirely transparent).

Since the authors repeatedly note that the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal and can be combined with their method, it would be helpful to include preliminary results on a subset of languages using such combinations. This would enrich the discussion. Along the same lines, although also orthogonal, a comparison against a strong projection baseline would further contextualize the results and highlight the strengths (or limitations) of wikification-based approaches.

Why is Dutch the best source language for Spanish, and Spanish the best for Yoruba? Is this merely a statistical artifact, or is there a more interesting explanation? A brief discussion probing these findings would be valuable.

While the idea is sound, the results in Table 4 are not particularly compelling, showing only small improvements (and not across all settings). Reporting statistical significance tests for Table 4 would help substantiate the claims.

Minor comments:
- Sect. 2.1: Projection can also be performed with methods that do not require parallel data, broadening applicability to languages lacking parallel resources; see, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) or Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which leverage bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links.
- Several typos are present; the paper would benefit from careful proofreading (e.g., first sentence of Section 3: “as a the base model”; a sentence marked as not ‘parsable’; Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...”; Page 8: “to disambiguate every n-grams”).
