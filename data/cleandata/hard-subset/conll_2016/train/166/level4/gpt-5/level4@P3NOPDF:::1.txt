This paper proposes a multilingual named entity recognition (NER) approach that augments standard features with information derived from Wikipedia. Using a cross-lingual wikifier, the method links target-language mentions to English Wikipedia pages and extracts features from the linked entries. Experiments demonstrate that these features improve performance both in monolingual settings and, more importantly, in direct transfer (train on English, test zero-shot on a target language). The analysis on low-resource and non-Latin-script languages is especially compelling.

Strengths:
- Clear and practical idea: leverage cross-lingual entity linking to import rich, language-agnostic features from English Wikipedia.
- Solid empirical evaluation across multiple languages, covering both monolingual and direct transfer scenarios.
- The focus on low-resource and non-Latin-script languages increases the paper’s relevance; the gains in these challenging settings highlight the method’s potential.
- The experimental presentation makes a persuasive case that Wikipedia-derived features are complementary to standard NER features.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Coverage and long-tail entities: The approach presumes that mentions can be linked to Wikipedia. How does performance change on mentions not present in Wikipedia or for entities with very sparse coverage? An analysis stratified by linkability (linked vs. unlinked mentions), entity frequency/popularity, and entity types would clarify how much of the overall gain comes from well-covered entities and whether the method degrades or helps on the long tail.
- Error propagation from the wikifier: The method’s success is tightly coupled to the quality of cross-lingual linking. How often do wikifier errors lead to incorrect NER predictions, and in what ways (e.g., wrong type assignment, boundary errors, over-triggering of entity labels)? A controlled study—such as comparing performance with gold links on a subset, or ablations with confidence thresholds and back-off strategies—would quantify this dependency and guide practitioners on when to trust the features.
- Over-reliance on lexical features in transfer: The degradation in Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are added suggests the model overfits to language-specific surface forms. It would be useful to explore:
  - Feature-class-specific regularization (e.g., separate L2 strengths per feature group, group lasso) to downweight brittle lexical cues in favor of cross-lingual or Wikipedia-derived features.
  - Dropout or stochastic feature masking for lexical features during training to improve robustness.
  - Character/subword features that generalize better across scripts, potentially combined with adversarial or domain-invariant training to reduce source-language bias.
  - Calibration of feature weights using a small development set from the target language, if available, to mitigate negative transfer.
- Practical considerations: A brief discussion of runtime and resource requirements for cross-lingual wikification (e.g., throughput, model size, and pipeline complexity) would help assess deployability in real-world systems.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Add a detailed breakdown by mention linkability and entity popularity, including precision/recall changes for unlinked or out-of-Wikipedia entities.
- Provide an error analysis that isolates wikifier-induced errors, and evaluate simple mitigations (confidence thresholds, back-off to non-Wikipedia features, or marginalizing over multiple candidate links).
- Report ablations with per-feature-group regularization and/or feature dropout to test whether the Tamil/Bengali degradation can be reversed while maintaining gains elsewhere.
- If feasible, include results with gold links on a small subset to upper-bound the potential of the Wikipedia-derived features and to contextualize current performance.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated and empirically grounded contribution. The idea of importing cross-lingual knowledge via Wikipedia is straightforward yet effective, and the results in low-resource and non-Latin settings are noteworthy. The main concerns—coverage gaps and error propagation—are addressable with additional analyses and modest methodological tweaks. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on including the proposed diagnostics and clarifications.
