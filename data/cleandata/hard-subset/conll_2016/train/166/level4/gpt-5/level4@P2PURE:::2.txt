This paper addresses cross-lingual direct transfer for NER using a very recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core idea is not highly novel and does not introduce a fundamentally new technology, the contribution is a solid incremental step that combines two research threads: (1) direct transfer for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., NER, parsing, POS tagging), and (2) advances in cross-lingual wikification. Despite the relatively incremental nature of the contribution, I found the paper well-motivated and clearly presented, with sufficient experiments and empirical evidence to support its claims and generally convincing results. I nonetheless have several comments on the presentation.

Related work: The paper should more thoroughly explain how it relates to Kazama and Torisawa (2007). It should also more clearly articulate how it differs from other NER systems that leverage encyclopedic knowledge from Wikipedia. While these distinctions are present in the text, they should be emphasized to help readers situate the work.

POS features: Although the authors explain why they omit POS tags as features, it would still be useful to report experiments with POS features in the style of Tackstrom et al. This would provide empirical evidence on the utility (or lack thereof) of such features across languages, at least for those with universal POS annotations.

Section 3.3: This section would benefit from a running example. I am still not entirely clear on how the modified Tsai and Roth model operates; the current description remains somewhat opaque.

Orthogonal methods and baselines: Since the authors repeatedly note that the approaches of Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal and can be combined with their method, it would be valuable to include preliminary combined results on a subset of languages. This would enrich the discussion. Along similar lines, even though it is also orthogonal, a comparison against a strong projection baseline would further contextualize the results and clarify the strengths and limitations of wikification-based approaches.

Language pairing observations: Why is Dutch the best training language for Spanish, and Spanish the best for Yoruba? Is this merely a statistical artifact, or is there a more interesting underlying pattern? A brief analysis of these findings would be insightful.

Table 4: Although the idea is sound, the gains in Table 4 are small and not universal. Reporting statistical significance tests for these results would strengthen the claims.

Minor comments:
- Section 2.1: Projection can also be performed without parallel data, making such models applicable to languages lacking parallel resources. See, for example, Peirsman and Pado (NAACL 2009) and Vulic and Moens (EMNLP 2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces rather than direct alignment links.
- The paper contains several typos and would benefit from careful proofreading (e.g., first sentence of Section 3: “as a the base model”; the sentence on Page 3: “They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL by...”; and “to disambiguate every n-grams” on Page 8).
