Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a language-independent approach to named entity recognition (NER) that leverages cross-lingual wikification. A cross-lingual wikifier grounds n-grams in any target language to English Wikipedia titles; the resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types are used as delexicalized features for NER. The model is trained on one or multiple source languages and directly applied to new languages without target-language supervision. Experiments span nine languages (CoNLL: EN, ES, DE, NL; low-resource: TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), covering both Latin and non-Latin scripts, and include ablations on Wikipedia size and feature sources.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A simple, language-agnostic feature family derived from cross-lingual wikification (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) that substantially improves direct transfer NER across typologically diverse languages.
2) Empirical evidence that these wikifier features also improve monolingual NER and that multi-source training (combining several source languages) further boosts performance.
3) Analysis showing how Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage mediate the effectiveness of the wikifier features, with ablations that quantify this dependence.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual transfer via delexicalized features: Wikifier-derived features deliver large gains over a comparable direct-transfer baseline and outperform prior art (e.g., Täckström et al.’s cross-lingual clustering approach on CoNLL languages; Zhang et al. on low-resource languages). The evidence that the same feature family improves both boundary detection and type assignment is compelling.
- Breadth and depth of experiments: The study covers nine languages across scripts and resource levels, includes both monolingual and direct transfer settings, evaluates multi-source training, and performs informative ablations (e.g., removing Freebase queries, subsampling interlanguage links). This gives a clear picture of when the approach helps and why.
- Minimal resource assumptions and practicality: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps and no parallel data, bilingual lexicons, or human annotation/interaction in the target language. This makes the approach practical for many languages and easier to reproduce.
- Clear insights for practitioners: The paper provides actionable guidance (e.g., for non-Latin scripts or very small Wikipedias, relying on wikifier features and downweighting lexical features can be preferable; with Latin scripts, combining all feature groups works best).
- Monolingual benefits and multi-source training: Demonstrating gains in purely monolingual training and incremental gains from pooling multiple source languages increases the utility and generality of the contribution.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on wikifier coverage and quality: Performance degrades for languages with small Wikipedias or weak interlanguage links (e.g., Yoruba). The approach inherits errors from the cross-lingual wikifier, yet the paper does not quantify how often NER errors are due to wikification errors versus model limitations. A more explicit error propagation analysis would strengthen the claims.
- Limited treatment of entities absent from Wikipedia: It is unclear how well the method handles novel or local entities without Wikipedia entries. While gazetteers and lexical features can help in some settings, a targeted evaluation on out-of-KB entities (or on NIL-linked mentions) is missing.
- Tag-set and evaluation mismatches: For low-resource languages, the paper removes or remaps MISC/TIME/TTL labels to align with CoNLL tags. While practical, this may complicate comparability and could bias results. Detailed reporting on the proportion of remapped/removed entities and its effect on metrics would help.
- Baseline scope and modern comparisons: The core NER model is a perceptron-based system with classic features. While fine for isolating the proposed feature contribution, comparisons against stronger modern baselines (e.g., multilingual neural models or cross-lingual embeddings) would better contextualize the gains. Similarly, combining with orthogonal transfer methods (e.g., projection, multilingual clusters) is suggested but not demonstrated.
- Efficiency and scalability: Wikifying every n-gram up to length four is potentially expensive. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or memory usage, nor the impact of wikifier latency on end-to-end inference.

Questions to Authors
- How does the approach perform on entities that are not in Wikipedia (or lack interlanguage links)? Can you report NER performance separately for mentions that link to English titles versus NIL, and the proportion of NIL mentions per language?
- Can you quantify error propagation from the wikifier? For example, bucket performance by wikifier confidence, or report NER F1 when excluding low-confidence wikifier features.
- Given the poor direct transfer on Bengali/Tamil when lexical features are included, did you try group-wise regularization or feature reweighting (e.g., different L2 penalties per feature group, or training without lexical features) to prevent over-reliance on lexical cues?
- What are the runtime and resource costs of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4)? Please provide average processing speed per sentence/document and memory usage.
- Freebase is deprecated. How feasible is replacing Freebase types with Wikidata types, and do you expect similar gains?
- In multi-source training, did you explore weighting sources by Wikipedia size, script similarity, or development-set performance? Any evidence of negative transfer for particular source-target pairs?

Additional Comments
- The paper’s ablation linking reduced interlanguage coverage to lower NER F1 is valuable; extending this with a simple predictive model (e.g., expected F1 as a function of intersection size) could guide practitioners in estimating expected performance for a new language.
- A qualitative error analysis (boundary vs. type errors; common confusions) would complement the quantitative findings and suggest avenues for feature or model refinement.
