

# Review for "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core insight is that grounding words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries provides valuable FreeBase types and category information that can serve as strong language-independent features for NER. The authors demonstrate that an English-trained NER model using these features can be directly applied to multiple target languages without target language annotations.

The main contributions, as I see them (ordered by strength):
1. The effective demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides robust language-independent features for NER, significantly improving performance on both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual settings, especially for low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts.
2. The practical implementation of a language-agnostic NER model requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, without needing parallel text or language-specific resources, making it broadly applicable across all languages with Wikipedia coverage.
3. The thorough empirical analysis showing how Wikipedia size impacts feature quality and demonstrating that training on multiple source languages (beyond English) further improves results.

## Strengths
1. The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that cross-lingual wikification features significantly improve NER performance across diverse languages, including low-resource ones with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil). The results on Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil show clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods that require parallel data or native speaker interaction. For instance, the approach outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) by 8-14 points on Turkish and Bengali despite using far fewer resources.

2. The approach is remarkably simple yet effective, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump without any parallel data or language-specific features. This contrasts with other methods that require parallel text, language-specific heuristics, or expensive entity linking systems. The authors clearly demonstrate how their method can be applied to all languages with Wikipedia coverage.

3. The paper provides a thorough analysis of feature quality, showing how Wikipedia size directly impacts performance (Table 3). The analysis reveals why wikifier features work better for languages with larger Wikipedia coverage and why they become the dominant signal for non-Latin script languages where lexical features fail completely. This provides valuable insights for future work.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper fails to compare with strong projection baselines that don't require parallel text (e.g., work by Peirsman and Pado, or Vulic and Moens), which would better contextualize the contribution. The comparison is limited to direct transfer methods (Täckström et al., 2012) and Wikipedia-based approaches (Nothman et al., 2012), but not to the most relevant alternative: projection methods that don't require parallel data.

2. The improvements over baselines, particularly in Table 4 (training on multiple languages), are marginal (typically 1-2 points F1), and the paper lacks statistical significance tests to confirm these differences are meaningful. For example, the 0.3-1.0 point improvements when training on multiple languages might not be statistically significant, undermining the claimed contribution.

3. The paper claims the approach is orthogonal to projection methods and could be combined with them, but provides no empirical evidence of this synergy. Including even preliminary results combining the approach with projection would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution claim and provide more concrete evidence of the method's utility.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why does Spanish serve as the best training language for Yoruba and Dutch for Spanish (Figure 2), rather than English? Is this a statistical coincidence or does it reflect specific linguistic relationships between these language pairs?

2. The improvements in Table 4 are marginal (0.3-1.0 points F1). Could you provide statistical significance tests to confirm these differences are meaningful rather than noise?

3. Have you considered combining your approach with projection methods (e.g., Vulic and Moens' work) to see if the combined approach yields further improvements? Even preliminary results would help clarify the relationship between these approaches.

## Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the cross-lingual wikifier was modified for n-gram processing (Section 3.3), as the current description is somewhat unclear.
- The comparison with Kazama and Torisawa (2007) should be strengthened to clarify the key differences, especially regarding the robustness of the wikification approach.
- The paper contains several minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "as a the base model" on page 3, "disambiguate every n-grams" on page 8) that should be corrected before publication.
