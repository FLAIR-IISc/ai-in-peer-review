

# Review for "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features. The authors ground words and phrases in any language to English Wikipedia entries, extracting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The main contributions are:
1. A method for generating language-independent features for NER using cross-lingual wikification, which only requires multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data or language-specific resources).
2. Demonstration that these features significantly improve cross-lingual NER performance on standard CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).
3. Evidence that these language-independent features can enhance monolingual NER systems across all 9 languages tested.

## Strengths
1. The paper presents a genuinely language-independent approach to NER that does not require parallel data or language-specific resources. The method only depends on multilingual Wikipedia, making it broadly applicable to any language with Wikipedia coverage. This is a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP, particularly for low-resource languages where parallel data is scarce.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured, covering both high-resource languages (CoNLL datasets) and low-resource languages (including non-Latin scripts like Bengali and Tamil). The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms strong baselines across all languages, with particularly impressive results on low-resource languages where other methods struggle. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance provides valuable insights for future work.

3. The paper makes a compelling case for the utility of language-independent features beyond cross-lingual transfer. The monolingual experiments show that wikifier features improve performance for all 9 languages, suggesting these features capture meaningful linguistic patterns that complement traditional lexical features. The demonstration that training on multiple source languages further improves results is also a significant contribution.

## Weaknesses
1. The approach is critically dependent on the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier, yet the paper provides minimal analysis of how wikifier errors affect the final NER performance. The authors note that "disambiguation is not perfect" in Figure 1, but don't quantify how often wikifier errors propagate to NER errors. This is a significant concern since the entire approach relies on the wikifier's accuracy.

2. The paper does not address a major limitation of the approach: named entities that are not present in Wikipedia. For example, the paper shows improvement on entities like "Albrecht Lehmann" (which was not in Wikipedia), but doesn't analyze how the method handles entities that have no Wikipedia representation at all. This is particularly problematic for low-resource languages where Wikipedia coverage is limited.

3. The paper doesn't adequately explain or address the poor performance on Bengali and Tamil when lexical features are included. The authors note that "for both Bengali and Tamil, this is the strongest signal, and gets the highest score" when using only wikifier features, but performance drops significantly when lexical features are added. This is a critical issue for the practical application of the method, yet the authors don't propose any solutions or analysis for this problem.

4. The comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods for low-resource NER is incomplete. The paper compares with Zhang et al. (2016), but doesn't address more recent approaches that might better handle low-resource scenarios, such as those leveraging multilingual language models or semi-supervised learning techniques.

## Questions to Authors
1. How often do errors in the wikifier propagate to errors in the NER output? Can you provide a detailed analysis of the error correlation between the wikifier and the NER system?

2. The paper notes that the method performs well on entities not present in Wikipedia (like "Albrecht Lehmann"), but doesn't explain how it handles entities that have no Wikipedia representation at all. What is the expected performance on entities with no Wikipedia coverage?

3. The paper observes that lexical features hurt performance on Bengali and Tamil, but doesn't explain why or propose a solution. Could the authors discuss potential approaches to mitigate this issue, such as feature weighting or regularization?

4. The paper compares with Zhang et al. (2016), but doesn't address more recent low-resource NER methods (e.g., those using multilingual BERT). How would your approach compare with these newer methods that leverage pre-trained multilingual models?
