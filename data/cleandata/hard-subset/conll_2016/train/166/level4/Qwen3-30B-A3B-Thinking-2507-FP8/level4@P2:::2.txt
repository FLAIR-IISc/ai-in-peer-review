

Summary: This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The authors ground words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, extracting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features. The model is trained on English data and applied to multiple languages, including standard CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The main contributions are: (1) a language-independent NER model using cross-lingual wikification features, (2) empirical validation across diverse languages demonstrating significant improvements over strong baselines, and (3) demonstration that these language-independent features can enhance monolingual NER systems.

Strengths: 
1. The paper presents a clear, coherent approach with strong empirical validation across a wide range of languages. The authors evaluate on both high-resource CoNLL languages and low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts, showing consistent improvements over baselines.
2. The paper provides convincing evidence that wikifier features serve as effective language-independent features, with the approach outperforming state-of-the-art methods on all evaluated languages, including low-resource ones where Wikipedia size is limited.
3. The authors demonstrate that the proposed features can be used both for direct transfer across languages and to enhance monolingual NER systems, showing the versatility of the approach.

Weaknesses:
1. The core innovation is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper primarily combines existing techniques (direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification) rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. The contribution would be stronger if it addressed a more significant gap in the literature.
2. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Kazama and Torisawa (2007), which also used Wikipedia category features for NER. The authors should clarify how their approach addresses the limitations of this earlier work, particularly regarding robustness to ambiguity.
3. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the improvements shown in Table 4, making it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the key differences between your work and Kazama and Torisawa (2007) in the related work section? Specifically, how does your approach address the robustness limitations mentioned in their work?
2. Would you consider including statistical significance testing for the results in Table 4 to better support the claim of consistent improvements?
3. How would the results change if POS tags were included as features, as in Täckström et al. (2012)? The authors mention they couldn't get universal POS tags for all languages, but would such features improve performance for languages where they are available?

Additional Comments: The paper has several minor grammatical errors (e.g., "as a the base model" on page 3) and typos that should be addressed in the final version. The discussion of Dutch being the best training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba is intriguing but lacks deeper analysis. A brief exploration of these patterns would strengthen the paper.

The paper makes a solid contribution to cross-lingual NER, with a well-executed approach that demonstrates clear benefits across multiple languages. While the core innovation is incremental, the thorough evaluation and strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to the field. The paper would be significantly improved by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the clearer differentiation from prior work and statistical validation of results.
