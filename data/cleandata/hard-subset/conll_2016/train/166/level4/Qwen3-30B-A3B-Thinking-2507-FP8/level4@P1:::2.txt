

Summary: This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The primary contribution is demonstrating that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories obtained through cross-lingual wikification serve as effective language-independent features for NER, enabling direct transfer of NER models across languages without requiring parallel data or language-specific resources. The authors show that their method outperforms strong baselines on standard CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and provides meaningful improvements on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).

Strengths:
1. The paper provides robust empirical evidence supporting the value of cross-lingual wikifier features for both direct transfer and monolingual NER. The results across nine languages demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, with particularly compelling results on low-resource languages where the approach significantly outperforms methods requiring parallel data or native speaker interaction.
2. The method is remarkably simple yet effective, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump as input without needing parallel text, language-specific rules, or complex feature engineering. This accessibility makes the approach highly practical for deployment across many languages.
3. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality provides valuable insights into the limitations and potential of the approach, explaining why certain low-resource languages show less dramatic improvements.

Weaknesses:
1. The contribution appears incremental rather than innovative, as it primarily combines existing techniques (direct transfer and cross-lingual wikification) rather than introducing fundamentally new methods. The core insight that Wikipedia categories can serve as language-independent features is not sufficiently novel, as Kazama and Torisawa (2007) previously explored similar concepts.
2. The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach from Kazama and Torisawa (2007), despite claiming this as a key distinction. The authors should more clearly articulate how their method overcomes the limitations of previous work, particularly regarding ambiguity handling.
3. The small improvements reported in Table 4 (multi-source training) lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess the practical importance of these gains. The paper would be strengthened by providing p-values or confidence intervals for the reported improvements.
4. The claim that the approach is orthogonal to Täckström et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) requires stronger justification. The authors should report preliminary results combining their approach with these methods to demonstrate the claimed orthogonality and potential for further improvement.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with Kazama and Torisawa (2007), specifically highlighting how your approach overcomes their limitations regarding ambiguity handling and monolingual focus?
2. The paper excludes POS tags as features but acknowledges their importance in NER. Would you consider reporting experiments with POS tags (similar to Täckström et al.) to assess their utility across languages where universal POS tags are available?
3. The paper observes that Dutch is the best training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba. Could you provide additional insight into these specific language transfer patterns? Are these results statistically significant or potentially coincidental?

Additional Comments:
- The description of the modified wikifier in Section 3.3 could be clarified with a concrete example to improve understanding of the technical modifications.
- The paper mentions "n-grams" but does not consistently specify the maximum n (n=4), which should be clearly stated early in the methodology.
- The claim that the approach "requires no sentence-aligned or word-aligned parallel text" is accurate, but the paper could better acknowledge that some cross-lingual wikification approaches do use parallel data for training, creating a more precise distinction.
- Several minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasings should be corrected (e.g., "as a the base model" on page 3, "disambiguate every n-grams" on page 8).
