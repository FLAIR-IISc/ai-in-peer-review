

This paper introduces a multi-lingual named entity recognition (NER) approach that leverages cross-lingual Wikipedia features through a cross-lingual Wikifier. The method maps target-language phrases to their corresponding English Wikipedia entries, extracting semantic and contextual features from these articles to enhance NER performance. Experimental results demonstrate that these features improve accuracy not only in monolingual settings but also in the more challenging direct transfer scenario, where an English-trained model is applied to target languages without language-specific adaptation. The authors provide a thorough evaluation, with particularly valuable insights into low-resource languages and non-Latin scripts, addressing critical practical challenges in multilingual NER.

The work presents a strong contribution with well-executed experiments. The novelty of the feature set and its demonstrated utility across diverse linguistic settings—especially in resource-constrained and non-Latin contexts—make this a meaningful advancement. The analysis of low-resource languages, in particular, offers actionable insights for real-world applications where labeled data is scarce.

However, three key concerns merit discussion. First, the approach’s effectiveness hinges on Wikipedia’s coverage of entities. How does the method handle entities absent from Wikipedia, and what is the impact on performance for such cases? A systematic analysis of Wikipedia coverage gaps and their effect on NER accuracy would strengthen the evaluation. Second, the reliance on the cross-lingual Wikifier introduces a potential error cascade. Since the wikifier’s mapping accuracy directly influences feature extraction, it is crucial to quantify how often errors in this stage propagate to final NER predictions. A detailed error analysis at the wikifier stage would clarify the robustness of the pipeline. Third, the observed performance degradation in Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are added suggests over-reliance on these features. Could differential regularization—such as applying stronger regularization to lexical features or adjusting their weight—mitigate this issue and improve generalization across languages? Exploring such strategies would address the observed trade-offs in specific language pairs.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution that advances multi-lingual NER through a practical, scalable feature engineering approach. The work’s emphasis on low-resource and non-Latin languages is especially commendable. Addressing the outlined concerns would further solidify its impact, particularly by clarifying the method’s limitations in Wikipedia-coverage-dependent scenarios and refining its robustness across diverse linguistic settings. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance with minor revisions.
