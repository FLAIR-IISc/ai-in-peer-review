

This paper introduces a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification. The authors propose grounding phrases in any language to English Wikipedia entries, extracting language-independent features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. The key contribution is demonstrating that these features significantly improve NER performance across multiple languages, both in monolingual settings and when transferring an English-trained model to target languages without target-language annotations. The authors further show that these features enhance both direct transfer and monolingual NER systems, with particularly strong results on low-resource languages like Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across nine languages, including five low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts. The authors convincingly demonstrate that wikifier features provide meaningful signal even when lexical features fail (e.g., for Bengali and Tamil), showing that Wikipedia-derived features can effectively bridge language gaps where traditional lexical approaches fail.

2. The method is remarkably simple and elegant, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia as input with no need for parallel text or language-specific resources. This makes the approach broadly applicable to any language with Wikipedia coverage, which is a significant practical advantage over competing methods that require parallel data or language-specific heuristics.

3. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance (Section 4.4) is particularly insightful. The authors clearly demonstrate the direct relationship between Wikipedia size, quality of wikifier features, and final NER performance, providing valuable guidance for practitioners on when this approach will be most effective.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how errors in the cross-lingual wikifier propagate to NER results. Since the entire approach depends on accurate disambiguation to Wikipedia entries, it's crucial to understand how often wikification errors lead to NER errors. The authors should quantify the error rates at the wikification stage and correlate them with NER performance.

2. The paper doesn't consider how the method handles named entities that don't appear in Wikipedia (e.g., newly created organizations, obscure locations, or recent events). For instance, if a person mentioned in a news article hasn't been added to Wikipedia yet, how does the approach handle that entity? The authors should provide analysis of this scenario, perhaps by examining cases where wikification fails and how NER performance is affected.

3. For languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), the model performs poorly when lexical features are included, suggesting the model over-relies on lexical features that don't transfer well. The authors don't explore potential solutions like feature weighting or regularization to balance the contributions of different feature types, which could significantly improve performance on these languages.

Questions to authors:
1. How often does the cross-lingual wikifier fail to disambiguate phrases correctly, and how does this impact the NER results? Could you provide a breakdown of error types (e.g., false positives, false negatives, incorrect FreeBase types)?

2. Can you share examples of named entities that don't appear in Wikipedia and how your system handles them? How do these cases compare to entities that do appear in Wikipedia?

3. For non-Latin script languages like Bengali and Tamil, would different feature weighting or regularization strategies improve the model's ability to balance lexical and wikifier features? What would be the computational cost of implementing such adjustments?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER by demonstrating the utility of Wikipedia-derived features. While the approach shows strong empirical results, the authors would significantly strengthen their work by addressing the limitations related to wikification errors, Wikipedia coverage, and feature balance for non-Latin script languages. The proposed method has clear potential for practical application in multilingual NLP systems, particularly for low-resource languages where traditional approaches struggle.
