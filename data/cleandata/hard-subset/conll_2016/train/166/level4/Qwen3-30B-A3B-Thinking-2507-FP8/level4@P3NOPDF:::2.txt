

This paper presents a cross-lingual NER transfer approach leveraging a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core methodology represents a modestly incremental contribution—integrating established direct transfer techniques for NER with contemporary wikification advances—the work is well-executed, coherent, and empirically grounded. The authors provide compelling results and a clear narrative, making the paper a solid addition to the field. However, several refinements would significantly strengthen the manuscript.  

**Major Comments:**  
1. **Related Work Clarification:** The paper must explicitly position itself against Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER systems. The differences (e.g., reliance on encyclopedic knowledge vs. wikification-driven disambiguation) are noted but require sharper articulation. A dedicated paragraph contrasting methodologies would better contextualize the novelty and avoid potential confusion with prior work.  

2. **Feature Analysis & Baselines:** The exclusion of POS tags as features is justified, but including ablation studies with POS (as in Tackström et al., 2012) would add value. Reporting performance differences across languages (where universal POS exists) would empirically validate the feature choice and enrich the discussion. Similarly, preliminary results combining the proposed approach with orthogonal methods (e.g., Tackström et al., 2012; Nothman et al., 2012) would strengthen the claim of complementarity. Crucially, a strong projection baseline (e.g., using unsupervised methods like Vulic & Moens, 2013) is needed to contextualize the wikification-based gains and demonstrate their relative efficacy.  

3. **Language Pair Analysis:** The observation that Dutch best supports Spanish NER and Spanish best supports Yoruba NER warrants deeper exploration. Is this a statistical fluke, or do linguistic/structural parallels (e.g., morphological similarity, resource availability) drive these patterns? A brief analysis of these pairs would transform an interesting footnote into a meaningful insight.  

4. **Statistical Rigor:** The marginal improvements in Table 4 (e.g., +0.3 F1 for Spanish→Yoruba) lack statistical validation. A significance test (e.g., paired t-test) would substantiate whether these gains are meaningful beyond noise, addressing reviewer skepticism about the practical impact.  

**Minor Comments:**  
- **Section 2.1 (Projection Methods):** Note that projection can bypass parallel data requirements (e.g., via bilingual semantic spaces; Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulic & Moens, 2013), broadening applicability to low-resource language pairs. This strengthens the motivation for the proposed approach.  
- **Proofreading:** Several typos and grammatical errors require correction (e.g., "as a the base model" in Section 2.1; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8; "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL" on Page 3). A careful proofread is essential.  
- **Section 3.3 Clarity:** A concrete running example would clarify the modified Tsai & Roth model. The current description leaves ambiguity about how the wikification layer integrates with the NER pipeline.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper’s strengths lie in its clear methodology, thorough empirical evaluation, and timely integration of wikification into cross-lingual NER. The incremental contribution is valid, and the results are generally convincing. Addressing the above points—particularly the contextualization against prior work, statistical validation, and deeper analysis of language pairs—would elevate the work from a competent incremental study to a more impactful contribution. The minor technical suggestions (POS features, baselines, projection methods) are straightforward to implement and would substantially bolster the paper’s technical soundness.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept** (with minor revisions). The paper is well-structured, empirically rigorous, and addresses a relevant problem. The requested revisions are feasible and would significantly enhance clarity and impact. The authors have delivered a solid, publishable contribution that advances the state of cross-lingual NER transfer.
