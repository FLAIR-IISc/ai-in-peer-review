

This paper explores cross-lingual NER transfer using a recent cross-lingual wikification model. While the core approach is incremental—combining direct transfer for NLP tasks (NER, parsing, POS) with advances in cross-lingual wikification—it is well-executed and supported by thorough experiments. The paper presents a coherent narrative with convincing empirical results, though several improvements would strengthen its contribution.  

**Major Comments:**  
1. **Related Work:** The paper must clarify its relationship to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and explicitly differentiate its use of Wikipedia knowledge from prior NER systems. Current distinctions are present but need greater emphasis to contextualize the work.  
2. **POS Features:** The exclusion of POS tags as features is justified, but including ablation experiments (e.g., comparing with Tackstrom et al.’s approach) would provide valuable insight into their utility across languages, especially where universal POS tags exist.  
3. **Section 3.3 Clarity:** A concrete example is needed to clarify how the modified Tsai and Roth model operates, as the current description is insufficiently clear.  
4. **Orthogonal Approaches:** The claim that Tackstrom et al. (2012) and Nothman et al. (2012) are orthogonal to this work merits empirical validation. Preliminary results combining these methods would bolster the discussion. Similarly, a comparison with a strong projection baseline (e.g., Vulic and Moens, EMNLP 2013) would better contextualize the value of wikification-based transfer.  
5. **Language Transfer Patterns:** The observation that Dutch outperforms Spanish as a training language for Spanish NER, and Spanish for Yoruba, requires deeper analysis. This is not merely a statistical quirk; a discussion of linguistic or data-driven factors (e.g., shared vocabulary, resource availability) would add significant insight.  
6. **Statistical Significance:** Results in Table 4 show only marginal gains across scenarios. Reporting statistical significance tests would strengthen the claims regarding the method’s effectiveness.  

**Minor Comments:**  
- **Section 2.1:** Projection methods can operate without parallel data (e.g., Peirsman and Daelemans, NAACL 2009; Vulic and Moens, EMNLP 2013), leveraging bilingual semantic spaces. This broader applicability should be acknowledged.  
- **Proofreading:** Several typos require correction (e.g., "as a the base model" in Section 2.1; "They avoid the traditional pipeline of NER then EL" on Page 3; "to disambiguate every n-grams" on Page 8). A careful proofread would substantially improve readability.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a valid contribution to cross-lingual NER via wikification, but its impact would be elevated by addressing the above points. The core idea is not groundbreaking, but the execution is sound. With the suggested revisions—particularly clarifying the novelty, validating orthogonal approaches, and deepening the analysis of language transfer patterns—the work would better justify its incremental advance. The empirical evidence is generally strong, but statistical rigor and contextual depth are needed to fully support the claims.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept with Minor Revisions** (addressing all major points above). The paper is promising but requires refinement to fully realize its potential.
