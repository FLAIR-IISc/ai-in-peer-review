

Summary of the paper: This work addresses cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) by proposing a language-independent model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to ground words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries. The main contributions are: (1) a language-independent NER model that uses Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features, (2) empirical demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms direct transfer methods on standard CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and improves low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) without requiring target language annotations, and (3) the demonstration that these language-independent features enhance monolingual NER systems across nine languages.

Strengths: 
1. The paper provides compelling empirical evidence through comprehensive experiments across multiple language settings, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines in both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual settings. The results are well-supported by detailed analysis of feature contributions and Wikipedia size effects.
2. The approach is conceptually sound and practical, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps with no need for parallel text or language-specific resources, making it broadly applicable across languages in Wikipedia.
3. The paper thoroughly analyzes the relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality, providing valuable insights for future work on cross-lingual NER in low-resource settings.

Weaknesses:
1. The related work section inadequately distinguishes this work from Kazama and Torisawa (2007), who also used Wikipedia category features for NER. A clearer statement of differences is needed to properly position this contribution.
2. The paper does not address why Dutch is the optimal training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba, which are interesting observations that could lead to deeper insights about language similarity and transfer learning patterns.
3. The results in Table 4 showing marginal improvements when training on multiple source languages require statistical significance testing to properly support the claim that these improvements are meaningful.
4. The paper does not include experiments with POS tags as features, which would help contextualize the decision to omit them and provide empirical evidence about their utility across languages.
5. Section 3.3 (Cross-lingual Wikifier Features) lacks a concrete example to clarify how the modified wikifier works, making it difficult to fully understand the technical implementation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide a detailed analysis of why Dutch is the best training language for Spanish and Spanish for Yoruba, and whether this pattern holds across other language pairs?
2. Would it be possible to include statistical significance tests for the results in Table 4 to demonstrate that the marginal improvements from multi-source training are statistically meaningful?
3. Could you provide a concrete example illustrating how the modified cross-lingual wikifier processes n-grams (n>1) to avoid incorrect entity linking, as described in Section 3.3?
