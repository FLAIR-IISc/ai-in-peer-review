### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a "direct transfer" setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to a target language without any annotated data. The core idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from any language to English Wikipedia entries. The corresponding Freebase types and Wikipedia categories of these English entries serve as powerful, language-independent features. The authors integrate these features into a standard perceptron-based NER model and demonstrate its effectiveness in two scenarios: 1) improving monolingual NER systems across 9 diverse languages, and 2) significantly outperforming existing direct transfer methods when training on English and testing on CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), including those with non-Latin scripts. The paper also shows that training on multiple source languages can further boost performance.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent NER features using cross-lingual wikification.** The key insight is to invert the traditional NLP pipeline where NER precedes entity linking. By using wikification as a feature source for NER, the authors create a rich, delexicalized feature set derived from the structured knowledge in Wikipedia and Freebase. This approach elegantly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora, a major requirement for many competing cross-lingual methods.

2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across a wide typological range of languages.** The study is not limited to closely related European languages. By including Turkic, Austronesian, Niger-Congo, and Indo-Aryan languages (some with non-Latin scripts), the authors provide compelling evidence for the generalizability of their method. The results show substantial gains over strong baselines, including those that require more resources like parallel text or human interaction.

3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependency on Wikipedia's data conditions.** The authors conduct a targeted ablation study (Table 3) that demonstrates how the quality of the wikifier features, and thus NER performance, degrades as the size of the intersection between the target language's Wikipedia and English Wikipedia is reduced. This provides a clear and valuable understanding of the method's limitations and prerequisites for success in new languages.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The core idea of leveraging cross-lingual wikification for NER feature generation is highly innovative and elegant. It grounds the NER task in a vast, multilingual knowledge base, effectively converting a lexical recognition problem into a concept-typing problem. This is a powerful conceptual shift that requires minimal resources (only a Wikipedia dump) compared to projection-based methods that depend on scarce parallel corpora.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance gains. On the standard CoNLL datasets, the proposed method significantly outperforms the strong Täckström et al. (2012) baseline, even their variant that uses word clusters derived from parallel data (e.g., 60.97 vs 59.3 F1 on German). The gains are even more compelling on the low-resource languages, where the method surpasses Zhang et al. (2016), a system which requires significantly more resources, including parallel text and an hour of a native speaker's time.

3.  **Thorough and Methodical Experiments:** The experimental design is excellent. The authors evaluate their features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, clearly isolating the contribution of the wikifier features. The inclusion of nine languages with varying script types and resource levels makes the claims of language independence highly credible. The analysis in Section 4.4, which directly links performance to Wikipedia size, is a sign of mature scientific work, as it proactively investigates and explains the method's failure modes.

4.  **Broad Applicability and Potential for Impact:** Because the only requirement is a Wikipedia instance for the target language, this method is immediately applicable to hundreds of languages. This has the potential to democratize NER capabilities for a vast number of low-resource languages, which is a significant contribution to the field. The finding that these features also help in the monolingual setting further broadens the work's impact.

### Weaknesses
1.  **High Dependency on a Complex "Black Box" Wikifier:** The success of the entire method is contingent on the performance of the Tsai and Roth (2016) cross-lingual wikifier. This system is a sophisticated piece of work in its own right. The paper does not provide sufficient analysis of this dependency. How robust is the NER performance to different wikifiers? Would a simpler, more transparent wikification approach (e.g., based on inter-language links and string matching) suffice, or is the full power of a state-of-the-art ranker necessary? This dependency makes it difficult to disentangle the contributions of the core idea from the specific implementation of the wikifier used.

2.  **Inability to Handle Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The feature generation process is fundamentally tied to entities that exist in Wikipedia. The model will likely struggle with emerging entities, non-notable named entities (e.g., local businesses, names of individuals in a news article not famous enough for a Wikipedia page), or entity types that are poorly represented in Wikipedia/Freebase. While standard lexical features can capture some of these (e.g., capitalization), the model's heavy reliance on wikification for its strongest signal is a potential point of failure in real-world applications where text contains many such OOW (Out-of-Wikipedia) entities.

3.  **Potential for Extreme Computational Cost:** The paper states that "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" is passed to the wikifier. This suggests a massive computational burden at inference time. Wikifying a single mention is already a non-trivial task involving candidate generation and ranking. Performing this for every 1-, 2-, 3-, and 4-gram in a document seems computationally prohibitive for any large-scale application. The paper lacks any discussion of runtime complexity or practical inference speed, which is a critical omission for a method proposed as a general solution.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your results to the specific choice of the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier? Have you experimented with any other, perhaps simpler, cross-lingual grounding methods? This would help clarify whether the core concept of wikification-as-features is robust, or if it depends heavily on a specific, high-performance wikifier.

2.  How does the model perform on entities that are novel or not present in Wikipedia? Does the strong signal from wikifier features for known entities cause the learned model to underweight the standard lexical features that might help identify these OOW entities?

3.  Could you provide an analysis of the computational cost of your method at test time? Specifically, what is the inference time overhead of adding the wikifier features (which requires processing all n-grams) compared to the "Base+Gazetteers" model? Is this approach feasible for processing large document collections?

4.  The handling of the MISC tag (re-mapping to LOC based on Freebase type or deleting) seems like a necessary but potentially impactful heuristic. Could you comment on how sensitive the low-resource language results are to this tagset harmonization strategy?
