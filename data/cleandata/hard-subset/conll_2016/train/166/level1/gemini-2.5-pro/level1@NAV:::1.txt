**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents a novel direct-transfer approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The core contribution is a method that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from any source language text to English Wikipedia entries. The types and categories associated with these English Wikipedia entries (e.g., from FreeBase) are then used as language-independent features for a standard NER model. The authors demonstrate that a model trained with these features on a high-resource language (e.g., English) can be effectively applied to low-resource languages without any target-language annotations, outperforming strong baselines. The method is evaluated on nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel and effective technique of using cross-lingual wikification as a source of powerful, language-agnostic features for NER. This intelligently inverts the traditional pipeline where NER is a prerequisite for wikification.
2.  A second key contribution is the extensive empirical validation across a diverse set of nine languages. The strong results, coupled with a detailed analysis of how performance correlates with Wikipedia size, provide compelling evidence for the method’s utility and clearly define its limitations.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The central idea of grounding all n-grams in a universal knowledge base to create semantic features is elegant and highly novel for the NER task. It provides a principled solution to creating delexicalized features that capture entity type information across languages.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results within the direct transfer paradigm. It shows significant F1 score improvements over established baselines on CoNLL datasets (e.g., +15 on Dutch, +13 on Spanish over the Täckström baseline) and also outperforms a more resource-intensive method on five low-resource languages.
3.  **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough analysis that supports its claims. The ablation studies demonstrating the impact of Wikipedia size and inter-language links are particularly insightful, as are the experiments showing the benefits of multi-source training. This adds significant depth and credibility to the work.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Heavy Dependence on Wikipedia:** The method's effectiveness is fundamentally constrained by the size and quality of a target language's Wikipedia and its link-density to the English version. This is a major limitation for truly low-resource languages that lack a significant Wikipedia presence, making the approach less generalizable than suggested.
2.  **Lack of Error Analysis:** The entire system's performance is contingent on the upstream cross-lingual wikifier. The paper lacks an error analysis of this component, making it unclear how wikification errors (e.g., incorrect disambiguation or failure to link an entity) propagate and affect the final NER output.
3.  **Limited Contextual Comparison:** While the comparison to other direct transfer methods is excellent, the paper would benefit from a broader discussion and contextualization against other cross-lingual transfer paradigms, such as annotation projection, which leverages different resources (parallel corpora) and represents a major competing approach.

**Questions to Authors**
The process of wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 seems computationally expensive. Could you elaborate on the inference-time latency of this feature extraction process and how it scales compared to standard NER models?
