**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that uses a language-independent method based on cross-lingual wikification. The model grounds words and phrases in non-English text into English Wikipedia entries, which provides strong language-independent features for NER. The authors evaluate their model on a wide range of languages, including high-resource languages (English, German, Spanish, and Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil). The results show that the proposed model outperforms comparable approaches on both high-resource and low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Cross-lingual wikification for NER**: The authors propose a novel method for cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification, which grounds words and phrases in non-English text into English Wikipedia entries.
2. **Language-independent features**: The authors show that the features obtained from cross-lingual wikification are language-independent and can be used to train a single NER model that can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia.
3. **Evaluation on low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their model on low-resource languages, which is a challenging task due to the lack of annotated data.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification, which is different from traditional approaches that rely on parallel data or language-specific features.
2. **Strong results**: The authors report strong results on both high-resource and low-resource languages, outperforming comparable approaches.
3. **Language independence**: The authors show that their approach can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia, making it a promising solution for low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on Wikipedia**: The authors' approach relies heavily on Wikipedia, which may not be available or up-to-date for all languages.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on a limited set of languages, and it is unclear how well the model will perform on other languages.
3. **No comparison to state-of-the-art**: The authors do not compare their model to the state-of-the-art models in NER, making it difficult to assess the significance of their results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of Wikipedia availability and quality for low-resource languages?
2. Can you provide more details on how you plan to extend your approach to other languages and evaluate its performance?
3. How does your approach compare to other state-of-the-art models in NER, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of your approach?
