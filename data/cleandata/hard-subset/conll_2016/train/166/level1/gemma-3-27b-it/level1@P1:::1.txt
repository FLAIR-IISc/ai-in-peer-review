This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground mentions in any language to English Wikipedia, utilizing the associated categories and FreeBase types as features for NER. The approach is evaluated on CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, demonstrating strong performance and outperforming existing methods.

**Summary of the Paper and Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides effective, language-independent features for NER, enabling strong transfer learning capabilities. Specifically, the ability to ground mentions in any language to English Wikipedia allows for a single model to be trained on resource-rich languages and effectively applied to low-resource languages. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both standard CoNLL languages and low-resource languages, showcasing the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method. A third contribution, albeit less prominent, is the analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality and the benefits of multi-source training.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of utilizing cross-lingual wikification as a primary source of language-independent features for NER is novel and effectively addresses the challenge of low-resource NER. Existing approaches often rely on parallel corpora or language-specific heuristics, which this method circumvents. The paper convincingly demonstrates that disambiguating words to Wikipedia entries *is* a useful step, reversing the traditional pipeline of NER followed by wikification.

2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on both standard CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages. The consistently strong performance, surpassing existing baseline methods, validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results on low-resource languages are particularly noteworthy, as they demonstrate the method’s ability to generalize to languages with limited annotated data.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, including ablation studies (e.g., examining the impact of FreeBase and interlanguage links) and experiments with different training language combinations. This comprehensive evaluation provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method and allows for a deeper understanding of its behavior. The analysis of Wikipedia size’s impact is also a strong point.

4. **Practical Applicability:** The method relies solely on multilingual Wikipedia, making it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring expensive parallel corpora or language-specific resources. This practical applicability is a significant advantage.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The approach is heavily reliant on the quality and coverage of English Wikipedia. While the authors acknowledge this, the limitations are potentially significant. Languages with poor representation in English Wikipedia may suffer reduced performance. The experiment in Section 4.4 does demonstrate this, but further discussion of mitigation strategies would be beneficial.

2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The paper details a fairly complex set of features, including various gazetteer and wikifier-based features. While effective, the complexity may hinder reproducibility and make it difficult to adapt the model to other NLP tasks. A more streamlined feature set could improve clarity and maintainability.

3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the model (e.g., incorrect entity boundaries, misclassification of entity types) would provide valuable insights for future improvement. A qualitative analysis of failure cases would strengthen the evaluation.

4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to existing methods are positive, they are not always to the most state-of-the-art baselines. Specifically, recent advancements in multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., multilingual BERT) might offer stronger baselines for comparison, though adapting these to NER directly may require further resources.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier itself? Have you investigated the impact of wikification errors on NER performance?
2. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of common error types and potential strategies for addressing them?
3. Given the reliance on English Wikipedia, have you considered any techniques for augmenting the features with information from the target language Wikipedia directly, even if the coverage is limited?
4. How does the performance of this model compare to more recent multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g. mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa) when fine-tuned for NER?
5. What is the computational cost associated with generating the wikifier features, particularly for large documents or corpora?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The figures and tables are informative and well-presented.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The use of a consistent notation throughout the paper is commendable.
* The authors could provide a link to the code and data used in the experiments to facilitate reproducibility.
