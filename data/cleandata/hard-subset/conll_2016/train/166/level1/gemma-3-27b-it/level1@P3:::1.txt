This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The central idea – utilizing the structured knowledge embedded within Wikipedia to bridge the gap between languages – is both innovative and effective, as demonstrated by the strong experimental results. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and thoroughly evaluated, making a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Feature Engineering via Cross-Lingual Wikification:** The core contribution lies in demonstrating the utility of features derived from cross-lingual wikification (Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types) for NER. This approach moves beyond simple lexical transfer and exploits the semantic richness of Wikipedia. The thorough ablation studies (Table 2, Table 3) convincingly show the importance of these features, especially for low-resource languages.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on standard CoNLL datasets and significantly outperforms existing methods on low-resource languages. The consistent performance gains across multiple languages validate the robustness of the proposed approach.  Outperforming Zhang et al. (2016) which *requires* native speaker interaction is a strong point.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Source Transfer Learning:**  The paper showcases the benefits of training on multiple source languages, illustrating that combining knowledge from different languages can further improve NER performance. This is a practical and valuable finding for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The approach of using wikification *as a feature engineering step* rather than a downstream task is a novel and insightful perspective. This is a clever inversion of the typical NER-then-entity-linking pipeline.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, covering both high-resource (CoNLL) and low-resource languages. The ablation studies are particularly valuable in dissecting the contributions of different features. The inclusion of experiments with varying Wikipedia sizes and training languages further strengthens the analysis.
3. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in detail, and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner. Figure 1 is a helpful visual aid.
4. **Practical Applicability:** The method is readily applicable to any language supported by Wikipedia, making it highly practical for real-world applications dealing with multilingual text. The minimal resource requirements (only a Wikipedia dump) are a significant advantage.
5. **Thorough Related Work Discussion:** The paper provides a thorough review of related work, positioning the proposed approach within the broader context of cross-lingual NER research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Wikification Quality:** The performance of the model is inherently tied to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. While the paper acknowledges this, a more detailed error analysis of wikification errors and their impact on NER performance would be beneficial. Imperfect disambiguation, even if it provides some signal, can introduce noise.
2. **Limited Exploration of Feature Combinations:** While ablation studies are present, a more systematic exploration of different feature combinations (e.g., interactions between lexical features and wikifier features) might reveal further performance improvements. The current feature set seems somewhat conservative.
3. **Freebase Dependence:** The reliance on FreeBase introduces a potential point of failure and limits the applicability of the method if FreeBase becomes unavailable or is not well-maintained. While the authors address this partially in Table 3, exploring alternative knowledge bases or methods for generating language-independent semantic representations would be worthwhile.  The impact of Freebase unavailability on lower resource languages seems particularly acute.
4. **Suboptimal Handling of Non-Latin Scripts:** The experiments reveal lower performance on languages with non-Latin scripts. While the authors acknowledge this and partially attribute it to the lack of lexical overlap, further investigation into techniques for handling different scripts (e.g., character embeddings, transliteration) could improve results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the wikification process, specifically focusing on common types of errors and their impact on NER performance?
2. Have you explored interactions between the lexical features and the wikifier features? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3. What is the computational cost of the wikification step, and how does it scale with the size of the input text? Is it a bottleneck for processing large datasets?
4.  Regarding the ablation study in Table 3, are the reduced performance values from removing interlanguage links purely due to a loss of Freebase type information, or do the Wikipedia category features also suffer from link removal?
5. The paper mentions potential for combining this approach with techniques like parallel projection. Could you elaborate on how these methods might complement each other in a practical setting?

**Additional Comments:**

* The code and data used in the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility.
* It would be interesting to see how the proposed approach performs on NER datasets with more fine-grained entity types.
* While the paper focuses on NER, the language-independent features generated by the wikifier could potentially be applied to other NLP tasks as well. Exploring these possibilities could be a direction for future research.
