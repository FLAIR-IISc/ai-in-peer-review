## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding words in English Wikipedia to provide consistent feature representations across languages – is well-motivated and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly for low-resource languages.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper addresses the challenge of NER in low-resource languages by transferring knowledge from English (and other source languages) using a novel feature engineering technique. The key contributions, in order of significance, are:

1. **Effective Use of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:** The central contribution is the demonstration that grounding words in English Wikipedia via cross-lingual wikification provides surprisingly strong, language-independent features for NER. This is not simply applying wikification as a pre-processing step for entity linking; it’s using the *process* of disambiguation and the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as direct features in the NER model. This is a clever and effective approach.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Languages:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil) without relying on parallel corpora or native speaker interaction, which is a significant achievement. The experiments convincingly show the benefit of this approach where traditional methods struggle.
3. **Demonstration of Feature Transferability & Multi-Source Training:** The paper shows that the language-independent features not only improve cross-lingual transfer but also enhance monolingual NER models and benefit from training on multiple source languages. This illustrates the versatility and robustness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The core idea of utilizing cross-lingual wikification *as a feature generator* for NER, rather than just a pre-processing step, is novel and insightful. It reframes the relationship between wikification and NER in a productive way.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results are consistently strong across a diverse set of languages, particularly for the low-resource scenarios. The performance gains on these languages demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method. The comparisons with existing methods (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) are well-conducted and supportive of the claims.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper includes a thorough set of experiments. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase support, varying interlanguage links) provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the system.  The analysis of multi-source training is also a strong point.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The figures (e.g., Figure 1) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The descriptions of the features and experimental setup are detailed and easy to follow.
5. **Practicality & Scalability:**  The method relies only on multilingual Wikipedia, making it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring expensive parallel data or linguistic expertise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Wikifier Quality:** While the paper acknowledges the importance of Wikipedia size, the performance is ultimately limited by the accuracy and coverage of the cross-lingual wikifier. Errors in wikification can introduce noise and negatively impact NER performance. The paper doesn't extensively analyze the types of wikification errors and their impact.
2. **Limited Feature Set:** The features used are relatively simple (base features from Ratinov & Roth, gazetteers, and wikifier features). While effective, the model could potentially benefit from more sophisticated features, such as character-level embeddings or contextualized word representations. The paper acknowledges this in the discussion.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:**  Although the experiments are comprehensive, the paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails would be valuable for future improvement. For instance, analyzing errors related to ambiguous entities or rare named entity types could reveal avenues for further refinement.
4. **Freebase Dependency:** The reliance on Freebase, which is no longer actively maintained, could be a long-term concern. While the experiment testing removal of Freebase shows some resilience, alternative knowledge bases or methods for obtaining entity type information should be considered.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the types of errors made by the cross-lingual wikifier and how these errors affect NER performance? Specifically, are there certain entity types or languages where the wikifier struggles more?
2.  Have you experimented with incorporating character-level embeddings or contextualized word representations (e.g., BERT, multilingual embeddings) into the model? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using the BIO labeling scheme? Were other tagging schemes considered, and why was BIO chosen?
4.  Given the reliance on English Wikipedia as the grounding point, how might the inherent biases present in English Wikipedia affect the performance of the model in different languages and cultural contexts?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is thorough and well-placed.
*   The paper’s strong focus on low-resource languages is a significant strength.
*   The provided code/data availability would greatly enhance reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.
