This paper presents a cross-lingual NER system leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea of grounding mentions in different languages to English Wikipedia and utilizing the resulting categories and FreeBase types as features is novel and effectively demonstrated. The contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) a novel approach to cross-lingual NER relying on comprehensive wikification of all n-grams, 2) strong empirical results on both CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages, outperforming existing approaches, and 3) demonstration of the usefulness of the proposed features for enhancing monolingual NER systems, and for multi-source transfer learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The central idea of using wikification as a foundational step for cross-lingual NER, rather than as a post-processing step, is a significant insight.  Transforming the task into leveraging existing knowledge graphs via wikification provides a unique and promising solution to the cross-lingual transfer problem. This approach avoids the need for parallel corpora or complex alignment techniques.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides convincing empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness. The system outperforms comparable direct transfer approaches on standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch), and crucially, demonstrates impressive performance on genuinely low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).  The results on low-resource languages are particularly compelling.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Analysis:** The authors conduct thorough experimentation, including analyses of the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality and experiments with multiple source languages. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase querying, reducing interlanguage links) provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components. The inclusion of comparisons against existing baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method relies solely on multilingual Wikipedia, a readily available resource, making it highly practical and scalable to numerous languages.  The ability to process any language supported by Wikipedia is a major advantage.
5. **Positive Impact on Monolingual Performance:** The finding that the language-independent features also improve monolingual NER systems is an unexpected and valuable result, demonstrating the broader applicability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:** While leveraging English Wikipedia is a strength in terms of resource availability, it introduces a potential bias. The quality and coverage of information in English Wikipedia may not be representative of all languages, potentially affecting performance, especially for languages with significantly different cultural contexts. This dependence isn't thoroughly discussed.
2. **Wikification Errors:** The paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation by the wikifier. Although the features seem robust to some errors (as demonstrated in Figure 1), the impact of systematic wikification errors on performance isn’t quantified or explicitly addressed. The performance degradation observed with smaller Wikipedias hints at this issue.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The features used, while standard for English NER, may not be optimally suited for all languages. The paper acknowledges this, but further investigation into language-specific feature engineering or adaptation of existing features could potentially yield further improvements.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While results are presented, detailed error analysis, particularly for the low-resource languages, would be valuable. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific entity types, ambiguities) could guide future development.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by relying on English Wikipedia, and how these biases might affect performance in different languages?
2.  What strategies could be employed to mitigate the impact of wikification errors, and have you explored any such strategies?
3.  Have you experimented with adapting the feature set to better suit the characteristics of non-English languages? If so, what were the results?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, particularly focusing on the types of errors made in the low-resource languages?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   Table 2 is very helpful in summarizing the experimental setup and results.
*   The inclusion of related work is comprehensive and provides good context.
*  It would be beneficial to include a discussion of computational cost and scalability of the wikification step, especially for very large datasets.
