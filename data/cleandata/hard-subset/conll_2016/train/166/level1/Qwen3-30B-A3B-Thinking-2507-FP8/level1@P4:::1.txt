

Summary of the paper: The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. The authors ground words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, using the resulting categories and types as features for NER across languages. The method requires only a multilingual Wikipedia dump and no parallel text.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1. A language-independent NER feature set derived from cross-lingual wikification that enables direct transfer between languages without requiring parallel text or language-specific adaptation.
2. Comprehensive empirical validation across 9 languages (4 CoNLL languages and 5 low-resource languages), demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods in both high-resource and low-resource settings.
3. Analysis showing that the quality of the wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size, with a clear understanding of the limitations of the approach.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features. The results on the CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) consistently outperform previous direct transfer methods, with improvements of 10+ points F1 on several languages. The comparison with Täckström et al. (2012) is particularly convincing, showing clear improvements without requiring parallel text for word clustering.

2. The paper demonstrates a novel application of cross-lingual wikification for NER that goes beyond the traditional pipeline (NER followed by wikification). The authors show that wikification features alone can provide strong signals for NER, even when disambiguation is imperfect, which is a significant insight not previously exploited in the literature.

3. The analysis of Wikipedia size as a factor affecting performance (Table 3) provides valuable insights into the limitations of the approach. The authors show that for languages with small Wikipedia sizes, the quality of the wikifier features degrades significantly, which explains the performance gap between high-resource and low-resource languages. This analysis helps establish realistic expectations for the method's applicability.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims the method can be applied to "all languages in Wikipedia" but fails to address the significant performance drop for languages with very small Wikipedia sizes (Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not propose any strategies to improve performance for these languages, making the claim of universal applicability misleading.

2. The paper does not adequately address the scalability to languages without Wikipedia (e.g., languages with no Wikipedia or extremely small Wikipedia). The authors state "the only requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump," but this creates a fundamental limitation for the approach, as it cannot be applied to languages without Wikipedia, which includes many low-resource languages.

3. The paper's claim that the features are "truly delexicalized" is problematic. The authors still use lexical features (word forms, affixes, capitalization) in their model, which are language-dependent. The paper does not sufficiently explain why these lexical features are included in a "language-independent" approach, creating a contradiction in the method's core claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. For languages with no Wikipedia (e.g., less than 100 articles), how would your approach perform, and what modifications would be necessary to make it applicable?
2. The paper states that their model "outperforms comparable approaches on the standard CoNLL datasets" but doesn't provide sufficient comparison with recent methods (e.g., those using neural networks). How do your results compare with state-of-the-art neural NER models trained on multilingual data?
3. The paper mentions that "the only requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump," but the cross-lingual wikification process described requires English Wikipedia as a reference. How would your approach handle languages with no interlanguage links to English Wikipedia?
