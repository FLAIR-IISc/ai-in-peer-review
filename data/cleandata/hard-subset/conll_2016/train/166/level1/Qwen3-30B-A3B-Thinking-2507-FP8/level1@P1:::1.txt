

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core insight is that grounding words and phrases in any language to English Wikipedia entries provides valuable category and FreeBase type information that can serve as robust features for NER across languages. The authors demonstrate that their method works effectively for both high-resource languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), outperforming existing direct transfer approaches. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel method for generating language-independent features through cross-lingual wikification, which enables NER models trained on English to be applied to all languages with Wikipedia coverage.
2. Comprehensive empirical validation showing the approach's effectiveness across a wide range of languages, including low-resource languages with limited Wikipedia coverage.
3. Demonstration that these language-independent features can enhance monolingual NER systems, improving results across all 9 tested languages.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation across diverse languages**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), which is a significant contribution. The results show consistent improvement over strong baselines, particularly for low-resource languages where the approach significantly outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) despite requiring substantially fewer resources. This broad coverage demonstrates the method's generalizability and practical utility.

2. **Novel use of wikification for language-independent features**: The paper makes a compelling argument that cross-lingual wikification provides valuable language-independent features for NER, rather than using it as a downstream task. The insight that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories can serve as strong signals for NER even with imperfect disambiguation (as shown in Figure 1) is well-supported by the results. This approach avoids the need for language-specific features or parallel data, making it more broadly applicable.

3. **Practical implementation with minimal resource requirements**: The method only requires multilingual Wikipedia dumps, making it highly accessible and deployable. The paper demonstrates that the approach works well even with limited Wikipedia coverage (e.g., for Yoruba), which is particularly valuable for low-resource language scenarios. The authors show that training on multiple source languages further improves results, providing a practical strategy for resource-constrained settings.

4. **Analysis of Wikipedia size impact**: The paper provides valuable insights into how Wikipedia size affects feature quality, as shown in Section 4.4. The experiments removing interlanguage links and FreeBase queries demonstrate a clear correlation between Wikipedia coverage and performance, which helps explain the method's limitations and provides guidance for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with related Wikipedia-based approaches**: While the paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), it does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from Kazama and Torisawa (2007) or Nothman et al. (2012), both of which also use Wikipedia for NER. The paper claims its method is "most closely related" to Kazama and Torisawa, but doesn't clearly explain how it improves upon their work, particularly regarding robustness to ambiguity. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient detail on wikifier modifications**: The paper mentions modifying the cross-lingual wikifier to handle n-grams, but provides minimal technical detail about these modifications. The explanation of why they need to generate candidates only from whole strings (not individual tokens) is insufficient. Without clear implementation details, it's difficult to assess the feasibility and robustness of this approach, particularly for languages with different word segmentation.

3. **Limited evaluation of low-resource language performance**: The evaluation of low-resource languages is limited to just five languages, with no clear indication of how representative these are of the broader low-resource NER landscape. The paper shows that performance drops significantly for Bengali and Tamil, but doesn't explore why these languages are particularly challenging beyond the script difference. More detailed analysis of these specific challenges would strengthen the paper.

4. **No comparison with recent language-agnostic approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with more recent approaches to language-agnostic NER that use multilingual embeddings or other techniques (e.g., those based on XLM or mBERT). While the paper's resource requirements are minimal, it would be valuable to understand how it compares with these newer methods that have emerged since 2016.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the wikifier features compare to other language-independent features that have been proposed for cross-lingual NER (e.g., character n-grams, multilingual embeddings)? Could you provide a more detailed comparison of feature importance?

2. The paper mentions modifying the wikifier to handle n-grams, but doesn't explain the exact implementation. Could you provide more details about how the candidate generation and ranking process was modified to handle n-grams without introducing false entities?

3. For Bengali and Tamil, the paper states that the lack of FreeBase support causes significant performance drops. How would the approach perform if FreeBase supported these languages, and what would be the practical implications of this limitation?

4. The paper demonstrates that training on multiple source languages improves results. Could you provide more insight into which languages are most beneficial as training sources for different target languages, and why?
