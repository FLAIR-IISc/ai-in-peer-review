

# Peer Review: "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary and Main Contributions:**  
This paper proposes a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification. The authors ground words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, extracting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features. The key contributions are: (1) demonstrating that wikifier features provide strong language-independent signals for NER, (2) showing that a single English-trained model can effectively recognize entities across diverse languages (including low-resource languages), and (3) proving that these features improve both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual NER systems. The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, making it broadly applicable.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Strong empirical validation across diverse language settings:** The paper provides comprehensive experiments across 9 languages (4 CoNLL languages and 5 low-resource languages), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines. The direct transfer results (62.10 F1 for Dutch) significantly outperform Täckström et al.'s (2012) direct transfer baseline (48.4 F1), showing the practical utility of their approach. The analysis of why features work better for some languages (e.g., correlation with Wikipedia size) is particularly valuable.

2. **Truly language-independent feature engineering:** Unlike most cross-lingual NER approaches requiring parallel data or language-specific resources, this method relies solely on multilingual Wikipedia, which is widely available. The demonstration that wikifier features remain effective even when lexical features fail (e.g., for Bengali/Tamil) is compelling evidence of their language-independence.

3. **Dual utility of features:** The paper convincingly shows that wikifier features benefit both cross-lingual transfer (improving direct transfer by 10+ points on German/Dutch) and monolingual NER (improving all 9 languages by 0.5-5.5 F1 points). This dual utility significantly strengthens the contribution beyond just cross-lingual transfer.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited applicability to truly low-resource languages:** While the method works for languages with Wikipedia (even small ones), the performance on Yoruba (6.72 F1) and Bengali/Tamil (6-7 F1) remains very poor. The paper doesn't adequately address whether this represents a fundamental limitation of the approach or if additional techniques (e.g., combining with semi-supervised learning) could improve performance for these languages. The claim that the method "can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia" is misleading since many languages with small Wikipedias yield unusable results.

2. **Insufficient comparison with contemporary methods:** The paper compares against Zhang et al. (2016) but doesn't address more recent approaches that might have emerged by 2016. For instance, the paper doesn't compare against work like Lample et al. (2016) on neural NER, which would have been relevant for the CONLL 2016 context. This limits the perceived novelty and significance of the contribution.

3. **Lack of analysis on failure modes:** The paper doesn't examine when the wikifier fails (e.g., for proper names that aren't in Wikipedia) and how these failures impact NER performance. The authors claim "even with imperfect disambiguation, FreeBase types still provide valuable information," but don't quantify how often the wikifier fails for entities and whether these failures correlate with NER errors.

4. **Underestimation of computational requirements:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of their approach, which involves processing all n-grams in a document through a wikifier. For large-scale applications, this could be a significant bottleneck compared to simpler feature extraction methods.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. The paper shows wikifier features improve monolingual NER systems, but how do these features compare to other language-independent features (e.g., character n-grams or morphological features) that have been used in monolingual NER?  

2. For languages like Yoruba with very low Wikipedia size (31K articles), what specific types of entities are most challenging to recognize, and could these be addressed by combining wikifier features with other techniques (e.g., semi-supervised learning on small parallel corpora)?  

3. The paper mentions that "FreeBase does not contain entities in Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil," but doesn't explore how this limitation could be mitigated (e.g., using alternative knowledge bases or generating synthetic entity types). Could you elaborate on potential solutions for this problem?  

4. How does the performance of your method scale when applied to languages with even smaller Wikipedia sizes (e.g., less than 10K articles), where the wikifier features would be extremely sparse?  

This paper makes a valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER by demonstrating the utility of language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification. While the approach has clear limitations for very low-resource languages, the empirical evidence supporting its effectiveness across a diverse language set is strong. With more thorough analysis of the limitations and comparisons to contemporary methods, this work would be even more impactful.
