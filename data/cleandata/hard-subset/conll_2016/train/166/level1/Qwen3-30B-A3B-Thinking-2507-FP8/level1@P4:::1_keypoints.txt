- Summary: Proposes cross-lingual NER using wikification to extract language-independent features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump and no parallel text.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a language-independent NER feature set from cross-lingual wikification enabling direct transfer between languages without parallel text or language-specific adaptation.

- Contribution 2: Provides empirical validation across 9 languages, showing significant improvements over prior direct-transfer methods in both high- and low-resource settings.

- Contribution 3: Analyzes dependence of feature quality on Wikipedia size and characterizes limitations for small-Wikipedia languages.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical results on CoNLL datasets, often outperforming prior direct-transfer methods by large F1 margins without parallel text.

- Strength 2: Novel use of wikification as primary NER features (rather than a post-processing step), showing robustness even with imperfect disambiguation.

- Strength 3: Clear analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance, explaining performance gaps across languages.

- Weakness 1: Claim of applicability to “all languages in Wikipedia” is misleading given large performance drops for very small Wikipedias (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) and no mitigation strategies proposed.

- Weakness 2: Method does not scale to languages with no or extremely small Wikipedias, contradicting the stated single-requirement premise.

- Weakness 3: Claim of “truly delexicalized” features is contradicted by use of lexical, language-dependent features (word forms, affixes, capitalization) without sufficient justification.

- Question 1: How would the approach work or be modified for languages with virtually no Wikipedia (e.g., <100 articles)?

- Question 2: How do the results compare with recent state-of-the-art neural multilingual NER models?

- Question 3: How does the method handle languages lacking interlanguage links to English Wikipedia, given reliance on English as a reference?
