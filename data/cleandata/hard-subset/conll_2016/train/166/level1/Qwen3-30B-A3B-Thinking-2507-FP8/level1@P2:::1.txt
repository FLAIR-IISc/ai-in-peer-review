

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features from Wikipedia. The authors ground words and phrases in any language to English Wikipedia entries, then use Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER. The key insight is that these features provide strong signals for NER even with imperfect disambiguation. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Novel use of cross-lingual wikification for language-independent NER features** - The paper demonstrates that cross-lingual wikification can generate features that are truly language-independent, enabling direct transfer of NER models without target language annotations.

2. **Strong empirical results across diverse languages** - The approach outperforms existing methods on standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) and achieves meaningful results on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) without any target language training data.

3. **Analysis of Wikipedia size impact on feature quality** - The paper provides a thorough analysis showing that the quality of wikifier features directly correlates with Wikipedia size, explaining performance differences across languages.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and resource-efficient approach** - The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, no parallel data or target language annotations, making it highly practical for low-resource languages. This is a significant advantage over existing approaches that require parallel data or manual intervention. The authors convincingly show that their method outperforms approaches requiring parallel text (Zhang et al., 2016) and language-specific heuristics (Nothman et al., 2012).

2. **Strong empirical validation across diverse languages** - The paper evaluates on both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages, providing a comprehensive assessment of the approach's strengths and limitations. The consistent improvements across languages (10+ F1 points on Dutch, German, Turkish) demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

3. **Insightful analysis of feature quality and Wikipedia size relationship** - The analysis in Section 4.4 is particularly strong, showing that the performance drop on low-resource languages directly correlates with Wikipedia size. The experiments removing interlanguage links (Table 3) provide compelling evidence that Wikipedia size is the key factor, not language family or script type.

4. **Demonstrated applicability to monolingual NER** - The authors show that their language-independent features improve monolingual NER systems across all 9 languages, extending the value of their contribution beyond just cross-lingual settings.

5. **Clear comparison with strong baselines** - The paper provides thorough comparisons with relevant baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016), demonstrating that their approach consistently outperforms these methods with fewer resources.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with more recent methods** - While the paper compares well with 2012-2016 baselines, it doesn't compare with approaches that have become standard for cross-lingual tasks since then, such as multilingual embeddings (e.g., XLM, mBERT). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion but don't address how their approach might complement or differ from these more recent methods.

2. **Insufficient detail on wikifier modifications** - The paper mentions modifying the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier for their purposes but doesn't provide enough detail on these modifications. It's unclear how significant these changes were or whether they could be applied to other wikification systems.

3. **Limited discussion of computational complexity** - The paper doesn't address the computational cost of using the wikifier for NER. For large-scale applications, the time and resources required to wikify every n-gram could be a significant drawback that needs to be weighed against the performance gains.

4. **No discussion of failure cases** - The paper doesn't analyze specific cases where the approach fails, which would help identify the limitations of the method and guide future improvements.

5. **Inadequate exploration of language-specific challenges** - While the paper notes that non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil) perform worse, it doesn't deeply explore why or how to address these challenges, which is particularly relevant for low-resource languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach perform if combined with multilingual embeddings (e.g., mBERT), which have become standard for cross-lingual tasks since 2016? Would your language-independent features complement such approaches, or would they be redundant?

2. You note that English-based gazetteers don't work well for non-Latin script languages. Could you elaborate on why this is the case (beyond the obvious script difference), and what specific modifications might improve gazetteer matching for these languages?

3. In Figure 2, Spanish is the best training language for Yoruba. Could you explain why Spanish (a Romance language) outperforms English (the most resource-rich language) for Yoruba (a Niger-Congo language)? Is this due to lexical similarity, Wikipedia size, or something else?

4. The paper mentions removing MISC tags when training on CoNLL data for low-resource languages. How does this affect the overall evaluation, and would the results change if the authors had included MISC tags in the low-resource languages?

5. How would your approach scale to languages with extremely small Wikipedias (e.g., fewer than 10,000 articles)? What alternative strategies would you recommend for such languages?

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and practical approach to cross-lingual NER that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The empirical results are convincing, and the analysis of Wikipedia size impact is particularly valuable. With minor improvements addressing the weaknesses noted above, this would make an excellent contribution to CONLL.
