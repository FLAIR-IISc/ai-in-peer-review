## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in all languages to English Wikipedia to extract informative features – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective. The paper thoroughly investigates this approach, evaluating it on both standard CoNLL datasets and a diverse set of low-resource languages.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of using cross-lingual wikification *as a feature engineering technique* for NER, rather than solely as a downstream task for entity linking. The authors convincingly show that even imperfect disambiguation yields valuable signals from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, enabling strong transfer performance. A second significant contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation across a wide range of languages, including several low-resource scenarios where the method outperforms existing approaches. The ablation studies, particularly those examining the impact of Wikipedia size and FreeBase integration, provide valuable insights into the method’s behavior. Finally, the demonstration that these language-independent features can *enhance* monolingual NER systems is a noteworthy result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Wikification:** The paper uniquely positions wikification as a core component of feature engineering for NER, moving beyond its traditional role in entity linking. This is a fresh perspective with clear benefits.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance gains across multiple languages, particularly the strong results on low-resource languages, are a significant achievement. The comparisons to existing methods (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) are well-executed and highlight the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation studies (varying Wikipedia size, removing FreeBase links) provide valuable insights into the method’s strengths and weaknesses, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Testing on both CoNLL languages and a diverse set of low-resource languages strengthens the generalizability of the findings.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The method’s dependence on English Wikipedia as the grounding point introduces a potential bias. While the authors acknowledge this, exploring alternatives (e.g., multilingual embeddings or grounding to multiple Wikipedia languages) could be a valuable direction for future work.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, is relatively complex and relies on careful tuning. A more streamlined feature representation could improve the method’s practicality.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the ablation studies are informative, a more detailed error analysis of the model’s predictions would provide further insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.  Specifically, understanding *what types* of errors the model makes in low-resource settings would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the possibility of combining your approach with parallel projection techniques. Could you elaborate on how you envision this integration working in practice?
2.  The experiments show that training on multiple source languages improves performance. Is there a point of diminishing returns when adding more source languages?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier itself? Have you explored using different wikification systems or techniques?
