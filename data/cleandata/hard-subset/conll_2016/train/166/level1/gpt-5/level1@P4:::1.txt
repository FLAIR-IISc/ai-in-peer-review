Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer, language-independent NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to ground words and phrases in any language to English Wikipedia entries and extract language-agnostic signals (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories). The model integrates these wikifier-derived features with a standard NER architecture (averaged perceptron BIO tagging), plus delexicalized base features and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers. Experiments cover nine languages (CoNLL: EN/DE/ES/NL; and low-resource: TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), evaluate monolingual and cross-lingual settings, include ablations on wikifier components, and explore multi-source training.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikifier-derived features provide strong delexicalized signals for NER, enabling direct transfer without parallel data and outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines on CoNLL languages and competitive results on low-resource languages.
2) Empirical analysis across nine languages, including ablations that link performance to Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage, and showing that the wikifier features also improve monolingual NER and benefit from multi-source training.
3) Practical adaptations of a cross-lingual wikifier to dense per-n-gram grounding and its integration as features for NER.

Strengths
- Strong gains in direct transfer with minimal resources: The cross-lingual setting trained on English and tested on DE/ES/NL shows substantial improvements over a strong direct-transfer baseline and over prior work (e.g., outperforming Täckström et al. with bitext clusters). The wikifier features add >10 F1 on several languages (Dutch, German, Turkish), supporting the central claim that wikification-derived signals are effective delexicalized features.

- Broad, systematic evaluation including low-resource languages: The study spans nine languages, including non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) and very small Wikipedias (Yoruba). Results are consistently improved by adding wikifier features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings. The method outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, despite using fewer resources (no parallel data or native-speaker interaction), indicating robustness.

- Informative ablations and analysis: The paper quantifies how removing Freebase queries or interlanguage links reduces F1, directly connecting performance to resource coverage (Table 3). This clarifies failure modes and explains weaker gains in truly low-resource settings. The multi-source training experiments, while modest in gains, show that combining languages generally helps or at least does not harm.

- Simple, scalable assumptions: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia and a cross-lingual wikifier; no sentence/word-aligned corpora, POS taggers, or language-specific rules are required. This makes the method attractive for new languages, especially compared to projection-based or heuristic-heavy silver-data generators.

- Practical wikifier adaptations: The modifications to candidate generation (restricting multi-token n-grams to whole-string candidates) and to the ranking model (removing dependence on other mentions when densely wikifying) are reasonable, and they enable the per-n-gram grounding strategy needed to extract pervasive features.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior use of Wikipedia-derived semantic types for NER: Using Wikipedia categories/Freebase types as features for NER is not new (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa, and joint NER+EL work). The main novelty here is the cross-lingual application with dense per-n-gram wikification and direct transfer. This is a meaningful extension, but conceptually incremental.

- Dependence on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage: The approach’s effectiveness degrades with smaller Wikipedias and sparse interlanguage links. The ablation confirms heavy reliance on English-intersected titles and Freebase types, which are missing or sparse for some low-resource languages/scripts. This limits applicability precisely where annotations are scarcest. The paper acknowledges this but does not propose mitigation beyond analysis.

- Tagset alignment and evaluation confounds: For low-resource datasets, the paper modifies tags (e.g., mapping some MISC to LOC via Freebase types, removing TIME/TTL). Such preprocessing risks conflating system features with label alignment, potentially biasing evaluations. Further, gazetteers are derived from Wikipedia/Freebase types, introducing possible overlap with test-time signals. A clearer, more controlled protocol (and reporting of precision/recall per tag) would strengthen claims.

- Model and baselines are somewhat dated and incomplete: The NER backbone is an older perceptron with greedy decoding. The paper does not compare against contemporary neural transfer methods available by 2016 (e.g., multilingual embeddings for NER), nor combine with other orthogonal signals (e.g., bitext clusters in this paper’s own experiments) to establish stronger upper bounds. This makes it harder to position the contribution against the state-of-the-art landscape at the time.

- Efficiency and scalability not addressed: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 is computationally heavy. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or memory profiles, nor strategies for pruning candidates or thresholding by confidence. Practical adoption would benefit from such details and from error analysis of wikifier-induced boundary/type errors.

Questions to authors
- What are the runtime and memory costs of wikifying every n-gram (n ≤ 4) per document across languages? Please report average processing time per document and scalability to larger corpora.

- Do you apply any confidence thresholding or pruning for wikifier-derived features (e.g., only include features when ranker confidence exceeds a threshold), beyond using top-2 candidates and ranker features? How sensitive are results to the number of candidates?

- How are tokenization and capitalization handled for non-Latin scripts, and how do these choices affect base and gazetteer features? Are results robust across tokenizers?

- In the low-resource setups, can you quantify how many labels were modified (e.g., MISC→LOC) and assess the impact of this mapping on F1 per class? Could you provide a version of the evaluation without such relabeling for comparison?

- In multi-source training, how do you balance training instances across languages to avoid domination by English? Did you try fine-tuning on the best source language identified per target?

Additional comments
- Reporting precision/recall per entity type and significance tests would improve the empirical section.

- Releasing the adapted wikifier code, feature extraction scripts, and constructed gazetteers would aid reproducibility.

- Combining this approach with multilingual embeddings/clusters or projection could likely yield stronger results; a small-scale combination experiment would strengthen the paper’s positioning.
