Summary
The paper proposes a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER that uses cross-lingual wikification to map n-grams in any language to English Wikipedia entries, harvesting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as delexicalized features. Built atop a Ratinov & Roth-style perceptron NER, the method improves both monolingual NER and, more notably, direct transfer from English to other languages, including five low-resource languages. Analyses probe the dependence of gains on Wikipedia size, Freebase coverage, and multi-source training.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification-derived types/categories constitute strong, truly delexicalized features for NER, yielding substantial gains in direct transfer across diverse scripts and resources.
- Secondary: A careful empirical study across nine languages, including low-resource settings, with ablations (e.g., removing Freebase/link intersections) that explain performance drivers.
- Tertiary: Practical multi-source training showing modest but consistent improvements and evidence that the features also boost monolingual systems.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual effectiveness under minimal assumptions: Training on English and using only multilingual Wikipedia, the approach outperforms prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., +12–24 F1 over Täckström with bitext clusters on NL/DE) and surpasses expectation-learning on low-resource languages without parallel data or native-speaker interaction.
- Convincing delexicalization: Wikifier-only features preserve a robust signal across languages/scripts; adding them yields double-digit gains in several targets (e.g., NL/DE/TR), aligning with the claim of language independence.
- Thorough analysis of resource dependencies: The experiments varying interlanguage links and Freebase queries show performance degrades with smaller intersections, explaining weaker results for Yoruba/Bengali/Tamil and validating the causal role of wikifier features.
- Practical scalability across languages and training regimes: The method applies to all Wikipedias, requires no alignment, and benefits from multi-source training with small but consistent gains; it also improves monolingual NER across all nine languages.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage: Performance drops markedly with limited interlanguage links or absent Freebase types; the approach may underperform for truly low-coverage languages or non-Wikipedia domains.
- Label-set and evaluation inconsistencies: Mapping/removing MISC and other tags for cross-corpus comparability introduces potential bias; fairness of comparisons could be affected.
- Limited baselines and model diversity: No comparisons to modern multilingual embeddings or joint NER-EL/neural approaches, limiting the strength of the claim relative to contemporary methods.
- Efficiency/latency concerns: Wikifying all n-grams (n≤4) can be computationally heavy; no runtime or throughput analysis is provided.

Questions to Authors
- How costly is full-document n-gram wikification in practice (time/memory), and can it be pruned without losing accuracy?
- How do results change when replacing Freebase with Wikidata, given Freebase’s deprecation?
- Have you evaluated on out-of-domain text (non-news) to assess domain robustness and Wikipedia bias?
- Can you quantify statistical significance for key gains and provide more granular error analysis on linking noise vs boundary/type errors?
