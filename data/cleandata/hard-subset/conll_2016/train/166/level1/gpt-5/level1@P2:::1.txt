Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer, language-independent approach to NER that leverages cross-lingual wikification to ground n-grams in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries and derives language-agnostic features from Wikipedia categories and Freebase types. The model is trained on one or multiple source languages and tested on target languages without target-language supervision. Evaluations cover CoNLL (es, de, nl) and five low-resource languages (tr, tl, yo, bn, ta), with additional monolingual experiments.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides strong delexicalized features for NER that substantially improve direct transfer across many languages, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines and performing well on low-resource settings.
2) Systematic empirical study across nine languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts), with ablations that quantify how Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage drive gains; and practical guidance on when to favor/de-emphasize lexical features.
3) Multi-source transfer results showing that combining training languages typically yields further gains; and showing that wikifier-derived features also improve monolingual NER.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea with practical impact. Reversing the usual NER→EL pipeline to use entity linking signal (Freebase types, Wikipedia categories) as language-independent NER features is simple, broadly applicable, and requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data, no language-specific rules). This substantially lowers the barrier to deploying NER in new languages.

- Strong empirical gains in direct transfer with careful baselines. On CoNLL target languages, adding wikifier features gives large F1 boosts over an English-trained baseline and surpasses Täckström et al. (2012)’s bitext-cluster model, despite requiring fewer resources (no bitext). The gains are largest where transfer is hardest (e.g., Dutch, German, Turkish), indicating the features are truly delexicalized and complementary to gazetteers.

- Broad coverage and insightful analysis. The paper evaluates nine languages spanning scripts and Wikipedia sizes, shows consistent monolingual improvements, and provides ablations that directly tie performance to (i) Freebase availability and (ii) interlanguage link density. The controlled removal of Freebase queries and interlanguage links convincingly explains why low-resource languages with small Wikipedias lag, and gives practitioners actionable guidance (e.g., rely more on wikifier features when scripts differ).

- Orthogonality and extensibility. The approach is orthogonal to projection and multilingual clustering, making it easy to combine with other cross-lingual techniques. The multi-source experiments show small but consistent improvements and suggest the method benefits from more annotated sources.

- Methodological clarity for the key novelty. The modifications to the cross-lingual wikifier to handle all n-grams (n ≤ 4) and avoid spurious matches are sensible and well motivated, and the feature construction (top-k candidates’ types/categories plus ranker features) is described concretely enough to reimplement.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage limits low-resource performance. The approach’s effectiveness hinges on interlanguage links and Freebase typing. For truly low-resource languages, especially with non-Latin scripts, the gains decrease sharply. While the ablation analyses diagnose this well, it constrains applicability where Wikipedia and Freebase coverage are sparse. The reliance on Freebase (which has since been deprecated) raises sustainability concerns; Wikidata might be a better long-term backbone.

- Evaluation design introduces label-set inconsistencies. For low-resource languages, the paper removes tags (TIME/TTL) and remaps MISC to LOC using Freebase types, then evaluates without MISC for CoNLL. This conflates system performance with label-space engineering and may bias comparisons across settings. A cleaner setup would harmonize labels uniformly across all datasets or report per-type metrics to separate boundary detection from type mapping effects.

- Limited comparison to stronger recent cross-lingual baselines. The paper compares to Täckström (2012) and a projection-style system (Zhang et al., 2016), but not to more recent cross-lingual representation approaches (e.g., multilingual embeddings without bitext, joint EL-NER models). Although orthogonal, such baselines would strengthen the claim that wikifier features are competitive in 2016 beyond the specific comparators. Also, the table presentation makes it difficult to align columns across methods; some numbers and claims (e.g., outperforming on all five low-resource languages) would benefit from clearer, directly comparable reporting.

- Scalability and error analysis are underexplored. Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 can be computationally heavy; there is no runtime/memory discussion, nor analysis of how the top-k candidate choice affects precision/recall. More detailed error analysis (boundary errors vs. type errors; failure modes from ambiguous links) would clarify when the features help or hurt.

- “Language-independent” claim is diluted by reliance on language-specific gazetteers and lexical features. While the central contribution is delexicalized, the full system’s strongest results for Latin-script targets rely on Wikipedia-derived gazetteers and lexical cues. This is practical, but the evaluation sometimes mixes settings, making it harder to isolate the contribution of the truly language-agnostic features.

Questions to authors
- Can you report per-type precision/recall and boundary vs. typing error breakdowns, especially for low-resource languages, to pinpoint where wikifier features help most?

- What is the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per document (throughput, memory)? How sensitive are results to n and to the number of top candidates used?

- Have you tried replacing Freebase with Wikidata properties/classes, or using only Wikipedia categories/infobox templates, for languages lacking Freebase coverage?

- Could you provide a version of the low-resource evaluation with a harmonized label set across all languages (e.g., PER/LOC/ORG only) without remapping using Freebase?

- How robust are the gains if you remove language-specific gazetteers and lexical features, i.e., using only non-lexical + wikifier features, to better isolate the delexicalized signal?

Overall assessment
Novelty is moderate but meaningful: prior work used Wikipedia categories for monolingual NER or joint EL/NER, but the cross-lingual grounding of all n-grams to extract language-agnostic type features and the breadth of evaluation are new and valuable. The method is simple, well motivated, and empirically strong, with thorough analyses that illuminate its operating conditions. Despite limitations in low-resource settings and some evaluation issues, this submission meets CoNLL criteria well and merits acceptance.
