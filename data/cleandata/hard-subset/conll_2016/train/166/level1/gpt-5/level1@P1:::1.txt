Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to derive language-independent features. Each n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language sentence is grounded to English Wikipedia titles via a multilingual wikifier; the corresponding Wikipedia categories and Freebase types are then used as delexicalized features in a linear NER tagger (Ratinov & Roth, 2009). The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel corpora) and can be trained on one or multiple source languages and applied to any language with a Wikipedia. Experiments on CoNLL (ES, DE, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA) show sizable gains over direct-transfer baselines and competitiveness with methods that assume more resources. The same features also improve monolingual NER.

Main contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification-derived type/category features provide strong, language-independent signals for NER, significantly improving direct transfer across diverse languages and scripts without parallel data.
2) A practical, scalable cross-lingual NER framework applicable to any Wikipedia language, with ablations tying performance to Wikipedia size/interlanguage links and multi-source training that further improves transfer.
3) Evidence that the same features consistently improve monolingual NER across nine languages, confirming complementary value to standard lexical and gazetteer features.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual performance with minimal assumptions. Training on English only, the model substantially improves over direct-transfer baselines on ES/NL/DE and beats Täckström et al.’s bitext-cluster approach, despite using no parallel corpora. On five low-resource languages, it outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) while assuming fewer resources (no native speaker interaction), underscoring practicality.
- Language-independence via grounding to English Wikipedia. Mapping mentions to English provides a unified type/category space, avoiding language-specific engineering. Wikifier-only features already yield reasonable F1, especially where Wikipedia is larger, validating the central hypothesis.
- Careful ablations and analyses. The monolingual vs. transfer breakdown, “wikifier only” rows, and stepwise addition of gazetteers and wikifier features make the contributions of each component clear. The degradation study that reduces interlanguage links or Freebase support quantitatively ties performance to resource size, explaining weaker results for very small Wikipedias.
- Breadth and realism of evaluation. Nine languages spanning Latin and non-Latin scripts, with both standard CoNLL settings and low-resource conditions, offer a convincing picture of generality. The multi-source training matrix and experiments demonstrate additional gains and provide useful guidance on source-language selection.
- Conceptual simplicity and compatibility. The approach is orthogonal to and likely synergistic with other resource-light transfer methods (e.g., multilingual clusters/embeddings, projection), and could be ported to modern neural taggers.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Wikipedia and Freebase limits low-resource effectiveness. While the paper acknowledges this, performance drops sharply for languages with small Wikipedias or sparse interlanguage links, and the reliance on Freebase types is problematic (Freebase coverage is uneven and, by 2016, being sunset). It is unclear how results would look using Wikidata-only type/category signals or without Freebase at all.
- Fairness and comparability of low-resource evaluations. The authors modify label sets (dropping TIME/TTL, removing MISC, and mapping some MISC to LOC via Freebase). It is not fully clear whether baselines adopt identical preprocessing, and removing labels reduces task difficulty. More details are needed to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with Zhang et al. (2016).
- Computational and practical costs of “wikify every n-gram.” Generating candidates and ranking for every n-gram up to length 4 can be quadratic in sentence length and potentially heavy in practice. The paper does not quantify runtime, memory, or throughput, nor discuss caching or pruning strategies, which matter for scalability to large corpora.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work using Wikipedia categories for NER. The key novelty is in robust cross-lingual wikification and applying these signals for transfer. However, the use of Wikipedia categories/types as features (Kazama & Torisawa, 2007) and joint NER-EL (Sil & Yates, 2013) are known; the contribution is more in the cross-lingual operationalization and broad evaluation than in modeling.
- Missing implementation details and error analysis. Important choices are under-specified: confidence thresholds for the “linker,” how ambiguous/multi-typed entities are handled, tokenization across scripts, feature templates for category/type aggregation, and hyperparameters. Qualitative error analyses (e.g., boundary vs. type errors) would clarify when wikifier features help or hurt.

Questions to authors
- Resource dependency: Can you report results replacing Freebase with Wikidata (or removing Freebase entirely), and quantify the impact? Given Freebase’s deprecation, this seems important for longevity.
- Runtime: What is the computational cost (per sentence or document) to wikify all n-grams up to 4? What proportion of tokens/n-grams are linked, and what caching/pruning is used to keep inference feasible?
- Disambiguation features: Please detail the ranking features retained after your modifications, the confidence thresholding strategy, and how NILs are handled. Do you ever suppress features for low-confidence links?
- Feature construction: How are multiple categories/types per candidate aggregated? Do you normalize or cap the number of features per token to avoid bias toward entities with many categories?
- Label set harmonization: Did all baselines for low-resource languages apply the same MISC removal and TTL/TIME dropping? If not, could you provide matched evaluations?
- Multi-source training: How are training instances balanced across languages (sampling, weighting)? Do gains persist if the non-English sources are much smaller than English?
- Generalization to neural taggers: Have you tried plugging the wikifier features into a BiLSTM-CRF or similar? If not, do you anticipate similar gains?

Additional comments
- The gazetteers derived from target-language Wikipedia are a useful addition but are language-dependent resources; please clarify availability/size in truly low-resource settings and whether they are used at test time only.
- Consider reporting per-entity-type F1 and boundary vs. classification errors to better understand where cross-lingual signals help most.
