Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to derive language-independent features from English Wikipedia (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) for mentions in any language. The NER backbone is a Ratinov and Roth-style perceptron model with BIO decoding. The authors: (i) augment the model with gazetteers induced from multilingual Wikipedia titles, and (ii) introduce wikifier-derived features by grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia using a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016), and feeding the top-k candidates’ types and categories as features. They evaluate on CoNLL 2002/2003 (DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), in monolingual and direct-transfer settings, and show improvements vs. strong baselines and prior cross-lingual methods. They also demonstrate modest gains from multi-source training and analyze how Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage affect performance.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification features provide strong delexicalized signals for NER, enabling direct transfer across diverse languages, including low-resource settings.
2) A practical, scalable pipeline that only requires multilingual Wikipedia dumps, with thorough empirical evaluation across nine languages and analysis (effects of Wikipedia size, Freebase availability, and multi-source training).
3) A simple integration of multilingual Wikipedia gazetteers and wikifier features that also improves monolingual NER.

Strengths
1) Clear, consistent cross-lingual gains with minimal assumptions. The addition of wikifier features substantially boosts direct-transfer F1 on DE/NL/ES (e.g., +14–22 points over base transfer; Table 2) and helps low-resource languages where lexical transfer is ineffective (BN/TA: wikifier-only is best). The approach requires only Wikipedia, making it broadly applicable.

2) Careful ablation and analysis. The paper disentangles contributions from base, gazetteer, and wikifier features in both monolingual and cross-lingual regimes. The controlled experiments that downsample interlanguage links and remove Freebase queries (Table 3) convincingly show that Wikipedia size and English intersection density are key drivers of success.

3) Multi-source transfer evidence. Although gains are modest, the matrix of train/test language pairs and multi-source training results show the method benefits from pooling high-resource languages, a valuable practical insight for cross-lingual NER.

4) Orthogonal to prior direct-transfer and projection methods. The paper situates itself well: the method compares favorably to Täckström et al. (2012) bitext clusters on CoNLL transfer, and to expectation-learning (Zhang et al., 2016) on low-resource languages, while being complementary to both (can be combined with clusters or projected data).

5) Practicality and scalability. Modifications to wikification for dense n-gram grounding are simple, and the featureization via top-k categories/types is easy to integrate into standard CRF/perceptron NER codebases. The approach also improves monolingual models across all nine languages, underscoring utility beyond transfer.

Weaknesses
1) Heavy reliance on Freebase and Wikipedia coverage. The strongest signals come from Freebase types and interlanguage links; when these are reduced, performance drops sharply (Table 3). Freebase has been sunset; the paper does not discuss alternatives (e.g., Wikidata properties/classes) or portability to languages with sparse interlanguage links. The core contribution is thus tied to specific KB infrastructure whose availability varies by language and over time.

2) Limited novelty in methodology. The work primarily combines known components: a standard NER model, Wikipedia-derived gazetteers, and cross-lingual wikification signals. While the empirical demonstration is strong, the algorithmic novelty is modest; a deeper joint model of NER and wikification, or principled handling of NIL/noisy links, would strengthen the contribution.

3) Evaluation mismatches and label mapping choices may confound comparisons. The authors remove TIME/TTL in low-resource datasets and remap/strip MISC tags (including converting some MISC to LOC via Freebase types). It is not fully clear whether the baselines are evaluated under the same remappings. Such preprocessing can materially affect F1 and may favor a system that uses Freebase types during both training and evaluation.

4) Potential noise from aggressive n-gram wikification. Grounding every n-gram up to length 4 is likely to introduce a large number of false positives (e.g., function words, frequent phrases). Although the authors restrict candidate generation and use ranker features as a “linker,” there is no quantitative analysis of NIL rates, disambiguation accuracy on these dense candidates, or runtime/throughput. The cost-benefit tradeoff of this dense EL is not characterized.

5) Missing baselines with modern cross-lingual representations. The paper does not compare to cross-lingual embedding-based NER transfer or recent neural sequence models with multilingual embeddings, which were emerging by 2015–2016. Such comparisons would better position the approach against current alternatives that also require minimal resources.

Questions to authors
1) How are baselines for low-resource languages evaluated with respect to your tag remapping (removal of TIME/TTL, MISC-to-LOC heuristics)? Are the same mappings applied to Zhang et al. (2016) outputs to ensure comparability?

2) What is the computational overhead of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4)? Please report average documents per second, candidate counts per token, and percentage of tokens/n-grams with NIL links.

3) How sensitive are results to the number of top candidates (k = 2)? Did you tune k, and how does precision/recall trade off with larger k?

4) Can you replace Freebase with Wikidata classes and properties? Have you tested the method’s portability when only Wikipedia categories (without KB types) are available?

5) Do wikifier features help specific entity types more (PER vs ORG vs LOC)? A per-type analysis would be useful, especially in low-resource scripts.

6) How is tokenization handled for non-Latin scripts (BN/TA)? Does the wikifier depend on language-specific tokenizers, and how do segmentation errors affect the features?

Additional comments
- It would be valuable to report precision/recall breakdowns, and a short qualitative error analysis of wikifier-induced boundary/type errors.
- Consider combining with cross-lingual clusters or embeddings to test orthogonality claims in a single model.
- The idea is promising for joint modeling of NER and EL; exploring structured inference or multi-task training could provide further gains.
