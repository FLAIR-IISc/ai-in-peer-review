## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding words in multiple languages to English Wikipedia and utilizing the resulting categories and FreeBase types – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of cross-lingual wikification features for direct transfer learning in NER, achieving strong performance on both standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages. A second notable contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation, showcasing the benefits of this approach across a diverse set of languages and demonstrating its ability to enhance even monolingual NER systems. Finally, the analysis of Wikipedia size and its impact on feature quality provides valuable insights into the limitations and potential improvements of the method.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing against relevant baselines (including Täckström’s work and Zhang et al.’s approach) and providing detailed ablation studies. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly for low-resource languages where annotated data is scarce. The analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size is particularly insightful, highlighting a key factor influencing performance. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The reliance on English Wikipedia as the grounding point introduces a potential bias. While the authors acknowledge this implicitly, a more detailed discussion of its implications would be beneficial. The feature engineering, while effective, appears somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic exploration of feature combinations and weighting strategies could potentially yield further improvements. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a discussion of computational costs associated with the wikification process, especially for large-scale applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by grounding all languages to English Wikipedia, and how these biases might affect performance in specific scenarios?
2.  Have you explored more sophisticated feature selection or weighting techniques to optimize the combination of wikifier features with existing NER features?
3.  What are the computational costs (time and resources) associated with the cross-lingual wikification process, particularly when applied to large corpora?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The thorough experimental evaluation and insightful analysis make a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
