## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in any language to English Wikipedia to exploit its rich semantic information – is a compelling approach to address the data scarcity problem in cross-lingual NER, particularly for low-resource languages. The paper demonstrates strong performance on both CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, outperforming existing methods in many scenarios.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification, traditionally a downstream task, can be effectively utilized to generate strong, language-independent features for NER. This shifts the perspective on wikification and opens up new avenues for leveraging its capabilities.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents robust experimental results on both standard CoNLL datasets and a diverse set of low-resource languages.  The consistent improvements over baseline methods, especially for low-resource settings, validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages strengthens the findings.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Quality Impact:** The insightful analysis of how Wikipedia size impacts the performance, and the ablation study showing the importance of Freebase links within the wikification process, provide valuable insights into the limitations and potential improvements of the method.  This demonstrates a good understanding of the factors influencing the model’s performance.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  The idea of using wikification *as* a feature engineering step for NER, rather than a post-processing step, is a fresh perspective.  This distinguishes the work from traditional direct transfer methods and Wikipedia-based approaches.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper goes beyond typical CoNLL evaluation and includes a substantial set of low-resource languages, showcasing the model’s applicability in challenging scenarios. This is a significant strength, as low-resource NER is a critical area of research.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The experimental setup is described in detail, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.
4. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The description of the features used in the model, including the base features, gazetteer features, and wikifier features, is thorough and well-explained. The modifications made to the wikifier system to handle n-grams are also clearly described.
5. **Ablation Studies:**  The ablation studies in Section 4.4 are particularly strong, convincingly demonstrating the importance of different components of the system (Freebase links, interlanguage links).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails, and *why*, would provide valuable insights for future improvement. Are there specific entity types that are consistently misclassified? Are there languages where the model struggles more than others, and if so, why?
2. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:**  The dependence on English Wikipedia as the grounding point introduces a potential bias.  The quality and coverage of English Wikipedia may not perfectly reflect the semantic nuances of other languages. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but further discussion on mitigating this bias (e.g., using multilingual embeddings or alternative knowledge bases) would be beneficial.
3. **Feature Complexity:** The combination of various feature sets (base, gazetteer, wikifier) might make the model difficult to interpret and potentially prone to overfitting. A more streamlined feature selection process or a discussion of feature importance could be helpful.
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is provided, it would be beneficial to compare against more recent and competitive cross-lingual NER models, particularly those leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting common error patterns and languages where the model performs poorly?
2. Have you considered strategies for mitigating the potential bias introduced by relying solely on English Wikipedia?  (e.g. using multilingual knowledge graphs)
3.  Regarding the feature set, have you conducted any feature ablation studies to identify the most important features for performance?
4.  How does the performance of your model compare to more recent cross-lingual NER methods that leverage pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g. mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 could benefit from a clearer explanation of the FreeBase types and their relevance to NER.
*   The terminology "Wikifier only" in Table 2 is slightly ambiguous.  It would be clearer to specify exactly which features are included in this setting (e.g., "Previous Tags + Wikifier Features").
*   The phrase "Latin script" is used consistently, but it might be more accurate and inclusive to use "Roman script".
