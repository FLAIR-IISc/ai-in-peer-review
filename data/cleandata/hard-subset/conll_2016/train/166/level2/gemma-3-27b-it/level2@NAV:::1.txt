## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a novel approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea—grounding words in all languages to English Wikipedia and utilizing the resulting categories and Freebase types—is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on both standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, establishing the effectiveness of their approach.

The primary contribution is the successful application of cross-lingual wikification features for direct transfer NER, achieving strong performance without relying on parallel corpora or language-specific features. A second notable contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including low-resource scenarios where the method proves particularly valuable. Finally, the demonstration that these language-independent features can *enhance* monolingual NER systems adds to the practical utility of the work.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear presentation, comprehensive experimentation, and insightful analysis. The ablation studies (Table 3) clearly demonstrate the impact of different components, particularly the importance of the Freebase connection. The multilingual experiments (Figure 2, Table 4) offer valuable insights into the benefits of multi-source training and the influence of Wikipedia size. The connection to existing literature is well-established, and the motivation for choosing specific evaluation datasets is clearly stated.

However, the paper has weaknesses. While the experimental results are strong, the improvement on some low-resource languages (e.g., Yoruba) remains relatively modest. A deeper analysis into *why* the method struggles in these cases beyond simply Wikipedia size would be beneficial. Additionally, the paper doesn’t address the potential for bias inherent in the English Wikipedia representation. Grounding all languages in a single, potentially biased knowledge base could introduce unforeseen issues. 

Finally, while the authors acknowledge limitations regarding lexical subtleties, further discussion of potential solutions (e.g., incorporating character-level features or subword information) would strengthen the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by grounding all languages to the English Wikipedia, and how these biases might impact NER performance in different languages?
2.  What specific challenges led to the comparatively smaller gains on languages like Yoruba? Are there language-specific characteristics that make wikification less effective?
3.  Have you considered incorporating character-level features or subword information to mitigate the reliance on perfect wikification, especially in low-resource settings?
