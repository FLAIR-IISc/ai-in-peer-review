## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in all languages to English Wikipedia to utilize its rich semantic information – is well-motivated and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly for low-resource languages. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Summary of Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:** The primary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of using cross-lingual wikification as a source of strong, language-independent features for NER. This is a novel application of wikification, traditionally considered a downstream task, and showcases its potential as a foundational component for cross-lingual transfer.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Languages:** The model achieves notable improvements on several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil), outperforming existing methods that rely on parallel text or native speaker interaction. This addresses a significant challenge in NER and highlights the practical value of the proposed approach.
3. **Improvement to Monolingual Systems:**  Demonstrating that the language-independent features can enhance *monolingual* NER systems adds further value and broadens the applicability of the research beyond purely cross-lingual scenarios.  The ability to leverage Wikipedia information to boost performance even with language-specific training data is a valuable finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The reframing of cross-lingual wikification from a downstream task to a feature engineering technique is insightful. The paper convincingly argues that disambiguating mentions to Wikipedia entities provides valuable signal for NER, even if imperfect.
2. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, covering both CoNLL languages and a diverse set of low-resource languages. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, reducing interlanguage links) are particularly strong and provide a detailed understanding of the feature contributions.
3. **Clear Explanation and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of detailed information regarding datasets and evaluation metrics facilitates reproducibility.
4. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The results are compared against relevant baselines, including the Täckström et al. (2012) work and the Zhang et al. (2016) model, demonstrating a clear advantage for the proposed approach, particularly in low-resource settings.
5. **Analysis of Wikipedia Size & Multi-Language Training:** The analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size and the benefits of training on multiple source languages provide valuable insights and further strengthen the contribution of the work.  Identifying Spanish as a particularly useful source language for Yoruba is a noteworthy observation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** While the core idea is clever, the reliance on the *English* Wikipedia as the central knowledge source introduces a potential bias. The paper acknowledges this in the discussion, but a deeper investigation into how this bias affects performance for languages significantly different from English could be beneficial.
2. **Wikifier Quality:** The performance is directly tied to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier.  While the paper utilizes a state-of-the-art wikifier, errors in disambiguation can propagate and negatively impact NER performance. A more detailed error analysis of the wikifier itself, specifically regarding the types of errors made and their impact on NER, would strengthen the paper.
3. **Limited Feature Set:** The feature set used is relatively simple, drawing from the Ratinov and Roth (2009) model. Exploring more sophisticated features, potentially incorporating contextual information from the wikified entities, could further improve performance.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis on Low-Resource Languages:** While demonstrating improved performance on low-resource languages, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis specific to these languages. Understanding the *types* of errors the model makes in these scenarios (e.g., incorrect entity type assignment, boundary errors) would provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the reliance on English Wikipedia introduces a potential bias. Could you elaborate on potential mitigation strategies for this bias, beyond exploring alternative knowledge bases?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the cross-lingual wikifier, particularly focusing on the types of errors that most frequently impact NER performance?
3.  The experimental results show that training on multiple languages improves performance. Is there a diminishing return with adding more source languages? What is the optimal number of source languages for maximizing performance on a given target language?
4.  You remove the MISC tag in low resource languages due to differences in annotation schemes. How sensitive are the results to this decision? Would a different approach, like mapping MISC tags to LOC if the wikifier supports it, affect the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Table 2 could be improved for readability (e.g., clarifying abbreviations).
*   Consider discussing the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram, as this could be a limitation for very large datasets.
*  Figure 2 could benefit from a clearer legend or explanation of the color scheme.



Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed approach is innovative, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and easy to understand. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
