

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The primary contribution is demonstrating that grounding n-grams to English Wikipedia entries provides strong language-independent signals for NER, allowing a model trained on English to perform well across languages without requiring target-language annotations. The secondary contribution is showing these features enhance both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual NER systems.

Strengths: The method is truly language-independent, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps with no parallel data or annotated target-language resources. The experiments are comprehensive, covering both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), with statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (e.g., +12.5 F1 on German compared to Täckström et al.). The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance provides valuable practical insights for practitioners.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't adequately address the impact of wikifier inaccuracy on performance, particularly for low-resource languages where Wikipedia size is limited. The comparison with recent multilingual transformer models (e.g., mBERT) is missing, which would better position the work against state-of-the-art. The claim that their method outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) without explaining why their native speaker interaction approach is inferior is underdeveloped.

The paper acknowledges limitations regarding Wikipedia size but could better discuss the trade-offs between using wikifier features versus lexical features for non-Latin script languages. While the approach is promising, the authors should clarify whether the observed improvements are primarily due to the wikifier features or the combined feature set, especially for languages with large Wikipedia coverage.

Questions to authors: Could you provide more details on the wikifier's accuracy rates for different languages, particularly low-resource ones? How would the method perform if the target language has no Wikipedia (e.g., for endangered languages)?
