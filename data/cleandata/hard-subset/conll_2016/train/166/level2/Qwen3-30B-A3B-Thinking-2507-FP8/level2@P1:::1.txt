

Summary of the paper: The paper introduces a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER. The key contribution is using Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types from cross-lingually grounded n-grams as features, enabling direct transfer of NER models across languages without requiring parallel text or language-specific heuristics. The second contribution is demonstrating that these features improve both cross-lingual NER (outperforming state-of-the-art on CoNLL languages and low-resource languages) and monolingual NER. The third contribution is showing that training on multiple source languages further improves results.

Strengths:
1. The core insight that Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types provide strong language-independent features for NER is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated. The authors effectively show that even with imperfect disambiguation (as in Figure 1), these features carry meaningful signals for NER, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. The comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse language types (CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages) is a major strength. The paper not only shows improvements on standard benchmarks but also provides valuable analysis on why wikifier features work better for some languages than others (based on Wikipedia size), offering practical guidance for practitioners.

3. The method's simplicity and low resource requirements (only needing multilingual Wikipedia dumps, no parallel text) make it highly practical and broadly applicable. This is a significant advantage over many previous approaches that require parallel data or language-specific heuristics.

4. The demonstration that the language-independent features enhance monolingual NER systems (Table 2, "+Wikifier" row) is an important secondary contribution that extends the applicability of the approach beyond cross-lingual settings.

5. The analysis of training on multiple source languages (Section 4.5) is insightful, showing that adding data from other high-resource languages can improve performance without significant downsides.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with Täckström et al. (2012) is insufficiently detailed. While the paper claims significant outperformance, it doesn't explain why their approach works better - is it specifically due to the wikifier features, or are there other differences in the model architecture or feature sets? A more thorough comparison would strengthen the claim of novelty.

2. The evaluation of low-resource languages is limited to languages with relatively substantial Wikipedia sizes (e.g., Turkish has 269K articles). It's unclear how the method would perform on languages with much smaller Wikipedia coverage (e.g., languages with fewer than 10,000 articles), which is a significant gap for truly low-resource settings.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the wikification process. For large-scale applications, the time and resources required to wikify every n-gram in a document could be prohibitive, and this practical consideration is missing from the discussion.

4. The paper claims the method "can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia," but the evaluation is limited to languages with Wikipedia articles. It would be valuable to discuss how the method would handle languages without Wikipedia or with minimal Wikipedia coverage, which is common for many low-resource languages.

5. The paper cites Zhang et al. (2016) as a baseline for low-resource languages, but doesn't compare with more recent approaches to cross-lingual NER that have emerged since 2016, which would provide better context for the contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. In Figure 2, Spanish appears to be the best training language for Yoruba. Can you elaborate on why Spanish (a Romance language) would be better than English for training a model to recognize entities in Yoruba (a Niger-Congo language)? Is this due to language family similarity or something else?

2. The paper mentions that for Bengali and Tamil (non-Latin scripts), the "Wikifier only" approach performs better than models with lexical features. Could you clarify why this is the case, and whether this pattern would hold for other non-Latin script languages?

3. Could you provide more details about the computational requirements of the wikification process, particularly for large-scale document processing? How does it scale with document size and language?
