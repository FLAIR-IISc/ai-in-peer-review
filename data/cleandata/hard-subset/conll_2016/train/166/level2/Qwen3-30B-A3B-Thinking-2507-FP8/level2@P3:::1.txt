

Summary: The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The main contributions are: 1) demonstrating that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories derived from cross-lingual wikification provide strong language-independent features for NER, 2) showing that these features improve both direct transfer NER across languages and monolingual NER systems, and 3) establishing that Wikipedia size directly affects feature quality, with significant improvements on low-resource languages using the proposed method.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across 9 languages, including both high-resource CoNLL languages and challenging low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts. The consistent improvement over baselines (including Täckström et al. 2012 and Zhang et al. 2016) across all language types demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

2. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality (Table 3) provides valuable insights into the limitations and requirements of the approach. This is particularly important for understanding why performance varies across languages, which is a critical consideration for practical deployment.

3. The demonstration that the language-independent features enhance monolingual NER systems (Table 2) represents a significant contribution beyond the direct transfer setting, showing broader applicability of the approach.

4. The method's minimal requirements (only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, no parallel text) make it highly practical and accessible for implementation, which is a major advantage over approaches requiring parallel data or language-specific resources.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently explain why the approach works better for some languages than others beyond Wikipedia size. For example, the performance on Yoruba (33.63 in monolingual) is substantially worse than on Spanish (83.51) despite having comparable Wikipedia size (31K vs. 1.3M articles), suggesting other factors may be at play that warrant further investigation.

2. The comparison with related work is somewhat superficial. While the paper mentions Täckström et al. (2012), it doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed method differs in terms of feature engineering or why it achieves better results. A more detailed analysis of the methodological distinctions would strengthen the contribution claim.

3. The paper does not address potential failure cases where the wikifier might make incorrect disambiguations that could negatively impact NER performance. For instance, in languages with different cultural references, the English Wikipedia grounding might produce misleading category information.

4. The evaluation of low-resource languages lacks detailed error analysis. It would be valuable to understand what specific types of entities are most challenging for the system in these languages, as this could guide future improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper shows that English works best for most languages as a training language (Figure 2), but why does Spanish perform better than English for Yoruba? Is this due to linguistic similarity or something about the Wikipedia content?

2. In the monolingual experiments, why does the "Wikifier only" model perform better on Bengali and Tamil than on other languages despite their non-Latin scripts? This seems counterintuitive given the paper's argument about script differences.

3. The paper mentions that FreeBase doesn't contain entities in Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil, but doesn't explain how the system handles this limitation. What happens when the wikifier fails to find a FreeBase ID?

4. How does the proposed method compare to approaches that use Wikipedia to generate training data (like Nothman et al., 2012) when both are applied to the same low-resource language?
