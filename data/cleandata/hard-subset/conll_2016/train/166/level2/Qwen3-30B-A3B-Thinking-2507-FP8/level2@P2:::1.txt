

Summary of main contributions: The paper introduces a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. The primary contribution is demonstrating that these features enable direct transfer of NER models across languages without target language annotations, with significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The second contribution is showing that these features can enhance monolingual NER systems across multiple languages. The third contribution is analyzing how Wikipedia size affects feature quality and demonstrating that training on multiple source languages improves results.

Strengths: 
1. The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to cross-lingual NER that doesn't require parallel data or language-specific heuristics. The insight that cross-lingual wikification can provide strong language-independent features (as shown in Figure 1) is compelling and well-supported by experiments. The method is simple yet effective, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps.

2. The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, covering both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), which is crucial for demonstrating the method's real-world applicability. The paper shows significant improvements over strong baselines (Täckström et al. and Zhang et al.) across all languages, with particularly impressive gains on low-resource languages where other methods fail.

3. The analysis of Wikipedia size effects (Section 4.4) provides valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and how to address them. The experiments in Table 3 clearly demonstrate that smaller Wikipedia sizes result in worse feature quality, which explains the performance gap between high-resource and low-resource languages.

4. The demonstration that the approach can be used to enhance monolingual NER systems (Table 2, "+Wikifier" row) is an important secondary contribution that extends the paper's applicability beyond just cross-lingual transfer.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's claim of "language-independent features" is misleading. The features are actually derived from English Wikipedia, so they're only language-independent in the sense that they don't depend on word forms. The quality of these features is highly dependent on the target language's Wikipedia size and quality (as shown in Section 4.4), which contradicts the implication of universal applicability. The paper should clarify that the approach works best for languages with substantial Wikipedia coverage.

2. The comparison with Täckström et al. (2012) is problematic because their method uses parallel text to induce multilingual word clusters, which is orthogonal to the authors' approach. The paper claims to outperform their "baseline" but doesn't explain why they're using this particular baseline when their method is fundamentally different. This comparison undermines the significance of their claimed improvements.

3. The paper fails to adequately address why their approach outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) despite both using cross-lingual wikification. The paper states that "in our model, the features from the wikifier are used in both detecting entity mention boundaries and the entity types" but provides no evidence that this is the key differentiator. The paper should clarify this distinction more precisely.

4. The paper's claim that the method "can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia" is overstated. For languages with very small Wikipedia (like Yoruba), the performance is poor (Table 2), and the paper doesn't provide a clear solution for languages with virtually no Wikipedia content. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and suggest potential extensions.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would you handle languages with no Wikipedia content (e.g., some indigenous languages with no digital presence)? Would you consider combining this approach with other techniques like projection or automatic data generation?
2. The paper mentions that FreeBase doesn't contain entities in Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. How much of the performance difference in these languages is due to the lack of FreeBase types versus the smaller Wikipedia size?
3. The paper shows that training on multiple source languages improves results (Table 4), but doesn't explain why this happens. Is it because of more training data, better feature coverage, or something else?
