**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to generate language-independent features by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from a text in any language to corresponding English Wikipedia entries. The Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types associated with these entries serve as powerful, delexicalized features for a standard NER model. The authors train this model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and apply it directly to target languages. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation across nine languages, including high-resource CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts). The results show that this approach significantly outperforms comparable methods and that the proposed features are also beneficial in a standard monolingual setting.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent NER features via cross-lingual wikification.** The key contribution is the insight to use wikification, traditionally a downstream task, as a source of features for NER itself. By grounding all n-grams to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the method creates a powerful and truly language-agnostic representation for identifying named entities, which proves highly effective for direct transfer.
2.  **A thorough empirical study across a diverse set of languages, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and analyzing its limitations.** The paper provides a strong and convincing evaluation on nine languages, including those with non-Latin scripts where lexical transfer fails completely. The experiments not only establish state-of-the-art results against comparable baselines but also include insightful analyses, such as the direct impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality, which strengthens the paper's conclusions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Novel Idea:** The central idea of inverting the standard NER->Wikification pipeline is both simple and powerful. It provides a principled way to create language-independent features by mapping surface forms from any language to a shared semantic space. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves substantial improvements over strong baselines. On the CoNLL languages, it outperforms the direct transfer method of Täckström et al. (2012), even their version that uses parallel corpora for word clustering. For the five low-resource languages, it outperforms a more complex system that requires additional resources and human interaction. The consistent gains observed from adding the wikifier features, even in the monolingual setting, underscore their general utility.
3.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Analysis:** The paper's analysis is a key strength. The ablation studies clearly isolate the impact of the proposed features. The experiment in Section 4.4, which simulates a low-resource setting for high-resource languages by degrading the knowledge base, provides compelling evidence that Wikipedia size is the main factor limiting performance on languages like Yoruba and Tamil. This level of analysis adds significant depth and credibility to the work.
4.  **Broad Applicability and Practicality:** The proposed approach is highly practical as it does not rely on parallel corpora, which are scarce for most language pairs. Its only requirement is the existence of a Wikipedia for the target language, making it applicable to hundreds of languages. This significantly lowers the barrier to developing NER systems for a wide range of low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Wikifier and Knowledge Base Quality:** The method's performance is fundamentally capped by the quality of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier and the coverage of the target language's Wikipedia. As the authors show, performance degrades significantly for languages with smaller Wikipedias. While this is analyzed, the paper could benefit from a more qualitative discussion of the wikifier's failure modes. For example, what kinds of disambiguation errors are most common, and how do they translate into specific NER errors (e.g., boundary errors vs. type errors)?
2.  **Handling of Out-of-Knowledge-Base Entities:** The approach is built on grounding mentions to existing Wikipedia entries. It is unclear how the model performs on entities that do not have a Wikipedia page (e.g., non-notable persons, local organizations). While other features (lexical, gazetteer) might provide some signal, the core contribution of the paper—the wikifier features—would be absent for such entities. An analysis comparing performance on in-KB vs. out-of-KB entities would provide a more complete picture of the method's capabilities and limitations.
3.  **Potential for Noise from Dense Wikification:** The system wikifies every n-gram up to length 4, which is a departure from standard wikification that targets candidate mentions. This dense approach could potentially introduce significant noise by linking many non-entity phrases to Wikipedia concepts (e.g., linking "in Germany" to the page for "Germany"). While the model may learn to handle this, a discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio and whether any filtering is performed would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more insight into the model's ability to recognize out-of-knowledge-base entities? Since the wikifier features are the strongest signal, especially in the cross-lingual setting, how does the model generalize to entities that cannot be grounded to Wikipedia?
2.  In your experiments with non-Latin script languages (Section 4.3), you found that using only wikifier features was optimal, suggesting that the lexical features learned from English were detrimental. Did you explore any strategies to mitigate this, such as using a different regularization strength for lexical features when transferring to a typologically distant language?
3.  The process of wikifying every n-gram in a document seems computationally intensive. Could you comment on the runtime performance of your system compared to a baseline model that does not use wikifier features? Is this approach feasible for processing large text corpora?
