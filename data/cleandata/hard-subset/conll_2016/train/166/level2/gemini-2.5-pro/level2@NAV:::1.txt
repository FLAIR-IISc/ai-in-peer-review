### Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel direct transfer method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The central idea is to generate language-independent features by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from text in any language to their corresponding English Wikipedia entries. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with these entries are then used as features to train an NER model on a high-resource language (e.g., English), which can be directly applied to low-resource target languages.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective feature generation method for cross-lingual NER.** By leveraging cross-lingual wikification, the paper introduces a powerful way to create language-agnostic features, cleverly reversing the traditional NER-then-wikification pipeline. This approach only requires a multilingual Wikipedia dump, bypassing the common need for parallel corpora.
2.  **Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse languages.** The authors demonstrate the method's strength through extensive experiments on nine languages, including several low-resource and non-Latin script languages. The results show significant improvements over strong, comparable baselines in the direct transfer setting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The core idea is simple yet highly effective. Using wikification as a universal feature source for cross-lingual NER is a clever insight that elegantly solves the problem of feature representation across languages without complex alignment or projection techniques.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results compared to relevant direct transfer baselines. The performance gains on the standard CoNLL datasets are particularly impressive (e.g., +12-15 F1 points over the Täckström baseline), and the model outperforms a more resource-intensive method on five low-resource languages.
3.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes a valuable analysis that connects the quality of the proposed features to the size of the target language's Wikipedia. The ablation study in Section 4.4, which simulates smaller Wikipedia intersections, provides a clear and insightful explanation for the performance variations across languages.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependency on the Wikifier:** The method’s performance is entirely dependent on the underlying wikification system. The paper treats the wikifier as a black box and does not discuss how its errors (e.g., incorrect disambiguation, failure to link) might propagate and affect the final NER output. An error analysis would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Potential Computational Overhead:** Wikifying every n-gram (up to n=4) in a document appears computationally expensive. The paper lacks a discussion of the inference time or computational cost compared to baseline models, which is a key practical consideration for deployment.
3.  **Handling of Non-Latin Scripts:** The paper observes that lexical features harm performance on non-Latin script languages and suggests an ad-hoc strategy of only using wikifier features in these cases. This approach is not principled; a more robust method for combining features across different script systems would be more compelling.
