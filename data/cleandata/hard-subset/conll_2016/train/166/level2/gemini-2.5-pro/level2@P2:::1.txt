### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), specifically in the direct transfer setting where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in that language. The central idea is to generate a rich set of language-independent features by leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier. For any given text, the method attempts to link every n-gram to a corresponding English Wikipedia entry. This grounding provides access to semantic information from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, which serve as powerful, delexicalized features for an NER classifier. The authors show that this approach not only works well when transferring from English to other languages but that the features also improve performance in standard monolingual settings.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent features for NER.** The key insight is to use cross-lingual wikification as a pre-processing step to featurize text, effectively inverting the traditional pipeline where NER is a prerequisite for entity linking. This provides a principled way to inject rich semantic knowledge into an NER model that is applicable to any language with a Wikipedia presence.
2.  **State-of-the-art results in direct-transfer cross-lingual NER.** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on nine languages, including standard CoNLL benchmarks and several low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts). The proposed model significantly outperforms strong prior work that relies on parallel corpora (Täckström et al., 2012) or human-in-the-loop annotation (Zhang et al., 2016), despite requiring fewer resources.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependencies and capabilities.** The authors provide a strong analysis correlating the quality of the wikifier features, and thus NER performance, with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. The paper also demonstrates the model's flexibility by showing that training on multiple source languages can further improve performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The core idea of using dense wikification to create features for NER is highly novel and effective. It provides a powerful bridge between lexical items in any language and a canonical, language-agnostic knowledge base (English Wikipedia/FreeBase). This approach elegantly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for many cross-lingual transfer methods, thus significantly extending the reach of high-quality NER to a much wider range of languages.
2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The method is tested on a diverse set of 9 languages, covering both high- and low-resource scenarios and different writing systems. The reported results show substantial gains over very strong and well-established baselines in the direct transfer setting. For example, the F1 score on Spanish improves from 30.9 to 48.4 with the addition of wikifier features, and the model outperforms Täckström et al.'s parallel-data-dependent model by a large margin on all CoNLL languages. The success on non-Latin script languages, where lexical transfer fails completely, is particularly compelling.
3.  **Thorough Analysis and Explanation:** The paper includes an excellent analysis in Section 4.4 that investigates *why* the method works and what its limitations are. By systematically degrading the resources available to the wikifier (removing FreeBase queries and interlanguage links), the authors clearly demonstrate that performance is tied to the quality of the cross-lingual grounding. This ablation study provides strong support for the paper's central claims and gives readers a clear understanding of the factors that influence the model's performance.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the method is explained well, and the experiments are logically structured. Figure 1 provides an excellent motivating example, and the tables are well-organized and packed with useful information.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Computational Cost:** The method requires running a cross-lingual wikifier on every n-gram (up to n=4) in the input text. This seems computationally intensive, likely much more so than a standard NER model that relies on local lexical and orthographic features. The paper does not address the practical issue of runtime performance or scalability. For the method to be widely adopted, especially for processing large corpora, it is important to understand the trade-offs between its high accuracy and its potential computational expense.
2.  **Dependence on Wikifier Quality:** The entire approach is predicated on the performance of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier. While the paper shows the features are robust to some disambiguation errors (Figure 1), it lacks a deeper analysis of how wikifier errors propagate to the final NER output. For example, are certain types of wikification errors (e.g., false positives on common nouns) particularly harmful? A more quantitative error analysis could strengthen the paper by better characterizing the robustness of the feature extraction process.
3.  **Ad-hoc Handling of the MISC Category:** For the cross-lingual experiments, the authors handle the MISC entity type by either converting it to LOC based on FreeBase types or removing the tag entirely. This heuristic feels somewhat arbitrary and could lead to information loss or conflation of distinct entity types. Furthermore, the evaluation in Figure 2 ignores MISC tags for CoNLL languages, which makes direct comparison to standard reported scores (which typically include all 4 types) more difficult. A more detailed justification for this choice, or an exploration of alternative strategies for handling tagset mismatches, would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the computational overhead of your method, particularly the inference time, compared to the baseline models? Is the dense wikification step a significant bottleneck for practical application?
2.  The strategy for handling MISC tags seems important for the cross-lingual setting. Have you experimented with alternative approaches, such as simply removing all MISC examples from the training data, and how did that affect performance on the low-resource languages?
3.  Your results in Table 2 suggest an interesting strategy: using all features for Latin-script languages but only wikifier features for non-Latin ones. Did you consider creating a single, unified model that could learn when to rely on lexical vs. wikifier features, perhaps by incorporating features about the script or language family?
