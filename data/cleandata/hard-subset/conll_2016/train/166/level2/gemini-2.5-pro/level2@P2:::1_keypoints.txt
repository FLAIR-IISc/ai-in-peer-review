- Task/setting: Direct-transfer cross-lingual NER trained on a high-resource language and applied to low-resource targets with no target annotations.
- Method: Use a cross-lingual wikifier to link every n-gram to English Wikipedia, extracting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as language-independent, delexicalized features.
- Contributions: Introduces a novel wikification-based feature pipeline, achieves state-of-the-art direct-transfer results on nine languages, and analyzes dependencies (Wikipedia size, interlanguage links) and multi-source training benefits.
- Novelty/Impact: Dense wikification is a novel, effective way to inject language-agnostic knowledge, avoiding the need for parallel corpora.
- Experiments: Comprehensive evaluation across 9 languages (including non-Latin scripts) showing large F1 gains and outperforming strong baselines.
- Analysis: Strong ablation studies showing NER performance correlates with wikifier/resource quality and demonstrating robustness to some degradations.
- Clarity/Presentation: Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well-illustrated with useful figures and tables.
- Computational cost: Running a wikifier on all n-grams (up to n=4) is likely computationally expensive; runtime and scalability are not addressed.
- Dependence on wikifier quality: Approach relies heavily on wikifier accuracy and lacks detailed analysis of how specific wikification errors affect NER.
- MISC handling: Ad-hoc heuristics for MISC (convert to LOC or remove) and ignoring MISC in some evaluations may distort comparisons and lose information.
- Question — overhead: Need clarification on inference time and whether dense wikification is a practical bottleneck.
- Question — MISC alternatives: Have authors tried other strategies (e.g., removing MISC from training) and how did that affect low-resource performance?
- Question — unified model: Did authors consider a single model that learns when to rely on lexical vs. wikifier features (e.g., using script/language indicators)?
