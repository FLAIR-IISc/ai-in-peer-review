**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) using a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate every n-gram. The model is trained on annotated documents in one or multiple source languages and can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia. The paper introduces a novel feature extraction method that grounds words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, providing language-independent features for NER. The model is evaluated on a wide range of languages in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, showing significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Cross-lingual wikifier**: The paper introduces a novel feature extraction method that grounds words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, providing language-independent features for NER.
2. **Language-independent NER model**: The paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual NER that can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia.
3. **Evaluation on low-resource languages**: The paper evaluates the model on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil), showing significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel feature extraction method**: The paper introduces a novel feature extraction method that grounds words and phrases in non-English text to English Wikipedia entries, providing language-independent features for NER.
2. **Language-independent NER model**: The paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual NER that can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia.
3. **Strong evaluation results**: The paper evaluates the model on a wide range of languages in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, showing significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on Wikipedia size**: The paper shows that the quality of wikifier features depends on the Wikipedia size of the test language, which may limit the model's performance on low-resource languages.
2. **Limited evaluation on low-resource languages**: While the paper evaluates the model on five low-resource languages, the evaluation is limited to a small set of languages, and more extensive evaluation is needed to fully assess the model's performance.
3. **No comparison to state-of-the-art models**: The paper does not compare the proposed model to state-of-the-art models for cross-lingual NER, which makes it difficult to assess the model's performance relative to other approaches.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model perform on languages with limited Wikipedia coverage, and are there any plans to extend the model to such languages?
2. Can the model be adapted to other NLP tasks, such as part-of-speech tagging or dependency parsing?
3. Are there any plans to release the code and data used in the paper to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a novel approach to cross-lingual NER that has shown promising results on a wide range of languages. However, further evaluation is needed to fully assess the model's performance, particularly on low-resource languages. Additionally, the model's dependence on Wikipedia size may limit its performance on languages with limited Wikipedia coverage.
