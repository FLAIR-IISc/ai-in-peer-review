Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER that leverages cross-lingual wikification to English Wikipedia. By grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target language to English titles, the system extracts Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as delexicalized features. The model is trained on one or multiple source languages and directly applied to target languages without parallel data or target-side annotations. The authors show:
1) A cross-lingual NER method that uses wikifier-derived, language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) enabling effective direct transfer across languages.
2) Extensive empirical evaluation across nine languages, including five low-resource languages, with ablations and analyses. The method outperforms prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) on CoNLL and compares favorably to Zhang et al. (2016) for low-resource settings, while requiring fewer resources.
3) Analysis of the dependence on Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage, multi-source training benefits, and evidence that the wikifier features also improve monolingual NER.

Strengths
- Clear, practical idea with broad applicability: Using cross-lingual wikification to obtain language-independent features is conceptually simple and resource-lean (no parallel data, only multilingual Wikipedia). The modifications to wikify all n-grams are sensible and well-motivated.
- Strong empirical results with careful ablations: The paper evaluates across 9 diverse languages and reports a thorough feature ablation (+gazetteers, +wikifier). Wikifier features consistently help both direct-transfer and monolingual settings; they are especially impactful for cross-lingual transfer to Latin-script languages, and even dominate in non-Latin scripts where lexical features do not carry over.
- Competitive comparisons: The method substantially outperforms Täckström’s direct-transfer baselines (and even bitext-based clusters) on CoNLL targets and performs better than Zhang et al. (2016) in low-resource settings, despite using fewer resources and no target-language native interaction.
- Informative analyses: The paper quantifies the role of Wikipedia/Freebase coverage via controlled ablations (removing Freebase queries and interlanguage links), and explores multi-source training, showing small but consistent gains. The train–test matrix gives useful guidance on source language selection.
- Relevance and generality: The approach applies to “all languages in Wikipedia” and can complement other methods (e.g., projection or multilingual embeddings), making it a useful building block.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage: Performance degrades for languages with small Wikipedias or sparse interlanguage links; Yoruba shows especially weak wikifier-only performance. The approach currently depends on Freebase types (now deprecated), and the paper does not explore replacements (e.g., Wikidata). This limits applicability to truly low-resource languages with sparse coverage.
- Comparability and label-set adjustments: For low-resource datasets, the removal or remapping of entity types (e.g., dropping TIME/TTL; mapping some MISC to LOC and removing the rest) complicates comparability across settings. It would help to report results under standardized label sets or provide per-type breakdowns and more justification for these choices.
- Baselines could be stronger: The main direct-transfer comparison is to Täckström (2012), which predates modern neural transfer methods (e.g., cross-lingual embeddings and LSTM-CRF). While the method is orthogonal and could be combined, readers would benefit from comparisons to more recent transfer baselines when feasible.
- Missing details and reproducibility concerns: Key implementation details of the wikifier modifications (candidate pruning thresholds, handling overlapping n-grams, tokenization across scripts, NIL handling, confidence thresholds) and the NER training setup (hyperparameters, feature templates by language, gazetteer construction/cleaning) are not fully specified. Statistical significance of improvements is not reported.
- Computational considerations and error propagation: Wikifying every n-gram may be computationally heavy and susceptible to noise from pervasive disambiguation errors; the paper offers limited runtime/memory analysis or error analysis on typical failure modes (e.g., over-linking common phrases, boundary errors induced by noisy top-2 candidates).

Questions to authors
- Coverage and knowledge base choice: Given Freebase’s deprecation, can the method be migrated to Wikidata or other KBs? Do you have preliminary results replacing Freebase types with Wikidata properties/classes?
- Disambiguation at scale: What is the runtime and memory footprint for wikifying all n-grams on typical CoNLL and low-resource test sets? How do you handle overlapping n-grams and conflicts? Is there a pruning threshold for candidate generation/ranking confidence?
- NIL and noise handling: How often does the wikifier return NIL, and how are NILs integrated into features? Do you filter certain category/type namespaces to reduce noise? How sensitive is performance to using top-1 vs top-2 candidates?
- Tokenization and scripts: How do you tokenize and generate n-grams for non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil)? Any language-specific normalization applied? Does segmentation quality affect wikifier recall?
- Label-set harmonization: Could you report per-entity-type F1 and clarify how the label-set modifications influence the scores? Any experiments that keep MISC or TTL/TIME to show robustness?
- Baselines and combinations: Have you tried combining your wikifier features with cross-lingual embeddings or modern neural NER architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) to test complementarity?
- Multi-source training: How are training instances from multiple languages weighted or balanced? Did you try sampling strategies or language-adaptive weighting?

Additional comments
- The idea is timely and useful; considering a migration from Freebase to Wikidata should strengthen long-term impact.
- An error analysis (e.g., boundary vs type errors, entity class-specific performance) would help guide future improvements.
- Releasing code/models or detailed configuration would greatly aid reproducibility and adoption.
