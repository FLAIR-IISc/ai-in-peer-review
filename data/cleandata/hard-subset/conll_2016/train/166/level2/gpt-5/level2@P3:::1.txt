Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The method grounds every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in text to English Wikipedia titles, and uses associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories, along with wikifier ranker confidence, as features in an averaged perceptron BIO NER model. The work evaluates both monolingual and direct transfer settings across nine languages (CoNLL: EN, DE, ES, NL; low-resource: TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), analyzes dependence on Wikipedia size and interlanguage links, and explores multi-source training.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) Demonstration that wikifier-derived type/category features are strong, truly delexicalized signals for NER, substantially improving direct transfer without parallel data and also boosting monolingual models.
2) A practical cross-lingual wikification setup adapted to dense span coverage (every n-gram), with modifications to candidate generation and ranking features, and an analysis quantifying how Freebase access and interlanguage link coverage affect performance.
3) Evidence that multi-source training yields additional, albeit modest, gains, and that the approach outperforms prior cross-lingual baselines under comparable or weaker resource assumptions.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains in both monolingual and transfer settings. Adding wikifier features improves monolingual F1 for all nine languages (e.g., DE: 68.74→72.97; average: 72.91→74.80). In direct transfer from English, wikifier features yield large improvements (e.g., NL: 35.06→47.14; DE: 55.04→60.97; TR: 64.07→65.32), confirming that the features carry cross-lingual signal beyond lexical overlap.
- Competitive and sometimes superior to prior cross-lingual baselines. The direct transfer results outperform Täckström et al. (2012) with bitext clusters on NL (47.14 vs. 40.4), DE (60.97 vs. 59.3), and are comparable or better across the CoNLL languages under similar training data. On low-resource languages, the model surpasses Zhang et al. (2016) across all five (e.g., TR 65.32 vs. 51.3; BN 41.31 vs. 26.0) while requiring no parallel text and no human annotation in the target language.
- Careful analysis of resource dependence. The ablation showing the effects of removing Freebase queries and randomly dropping interlanguage links (Table 3) explains why Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil lag, and demonstrates that performance scales with intersection size with English Wikipedia. This provides actionable guidance for where the approach will be most effective.
- Generality and practicality. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia and avoids POS tagging, parallel corpora, or language-specific heuristics. The same feature machinery benefits both boundary detection and type assignment, and multi-source training (EN+ES+NL+DE) yields consistent improvements over EN-only training.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Wikipedia and Freebase coverage. Performance degrades substantially when interlanguage links or Freebase types are sparse (as shown by Table 3 and the weaker “wikifier only” results for YO/BN/TA). The approach may struggle for languages with very small or nascent Wikipedias, limiting applicability. A reasonable enhancement would be to incorporate Wikidata types/relations and back-off strategies when Freebase typing is unavailable.
- Computational footprint and precision risks in “wikify every n-gram.” Linking all n-grams up to length 4 is potentially expensive and risks spurious links for common phrases, even with the modified candidate generation. The paper does not present runtime, throughput, or NIL-rate statistics, nor thresholds/filters for rejecting low-confidence links at scale. Reporting these and exploring lightweight pre-filters (e.g., character-type heuristics, simple span proposals) would strengthen the case for deployability.
- Label set alignment and evaluation comparability. For cross-corpus experiments, MISC labels are dropped or remapped to LOC using wikifier signals; CoNLL evaluation ignores MISC. This complicates comparability with prior CoNLL reports and may inflate scores relative to standard settings. Per-entity-type F1, with and without remapping, and a sensitivity analysis would clarify the impact of these choices.
- Scope of baselines. While comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are informative, contemporaneous neural cross-lingual baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings/clusters) are not included. Since the method is orthogonal, a comparison or combination with cross-lingual embeddings would help position the contribution within the 2016 landscape.
- Reproducibility details. Key elements of the feature templates (exact ranker features used, NIL rejection criteria), handling of overlapping spans, and training details (hyperparameters, regularization, averaging) are only briefly described. Clearer specification and release of code/models would enhance impact and adoption.

Questions to authors
- What proportion of tokens/spans receive at least one candidate link in each language, and what is the NIL rate? How is the linker confidence threshold chosen, and how sensitive are results to this threshold?
- What is the end-to-end runtime for wikifying every n-gram (per document or per million tokens), and how does runtime scale with Wikipedia size? Are there pruning strategies that preserve most of the gains?
- What is the marginal utility of longer n-grams (n=2,3,4) over unigrams? An ablation by n would clarify the cost-benefit trade-off.
- In multi-source training, are languages weighted equally or by data size? Does up/down-weighting by language similarity (e.g., via dev-set tuning) further help?
- For low-resource scripts where Freebase lacks coverage, have Wikidata or category-only features been tested as substitutes for Freebase types?

Additional comments
- Reporting per-type F1 (PER/LOC/ORG) and statistical significance would strengthen the empirical section.
- The idea of augmenting wikifier features with graph-based signals (relations between entities, page text) is promising and already noted as future work; even a small-scale pilot could be illuminating.
- Combining the proposed features with multilingual embeddings or cluster features (as in Täckström et al.) could further improve robustness when Wikipedia coverage is thin.
