Summary:
The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features (Wikipedia categories and Freebase types) by grounding n-grams in any language to English Wikipedia. Trained on one or multiple source languages (often English), the model directly transfers to target languages and also improves monolingual NER. Experiments across 9 languages (CoNLL and low-resource) show consistent gains, with analyses linking performance to Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, language-agnostic featureization for NER from cross-lingual wikification that enables strong direct transfer without parallel data.
2) Comprehensive evaluation, including low-resource languages and multi-source training, with ablations isolating the role of Freebase and interlanguage links.
3) Demonstration that the same features also improve monolingual NER.

Strengths:
- Strong cross-lingual transfer with minimal resources: Using only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text, no native speakers), the approach substantially outperforms prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) on CoNLL and exceeds Zhang et al. on five low-resource languages. The gains from adding wikifier features are large and consistent.
- Clear, reusable idea with orthogonal synergy: The features are truly delexicalized and orthogonal to projection/embedding methods, making the approach easy to combine with other techniques.
- Solid analyses: The authors present informative ablations (removing Freebase queries, downsampling interlanguage links) that convincingly explain performance variation via Wikipedia coverage; they also explore multi-source training, showing small but reliable improvements.
- Practical utility: The method improves monolingual NER across all nine languages, suggesting usefulness beyond cross-lingual transfer.

Weaknesses:
- Dependence on Freebase and Wikipedia coverage: Freebase types are central to the gains; low-resource languages lacking Freebase coverage or small interlanguage intersections see degraded performance. The reliance on a deprecated KB (Freebase) raises maintainability concerns; Wikidata could be a better target.
- Evaluation caveats in label mapping: Heuristic remapping/removal of MISC, TTL, and TIME tags may bias comparisons across datasets and complicate interpretation of cross-lingual scores.
- Limited baselines and significance: No comparison to strong modern neural cross-lingual NER (e.g., multilingual embeddings or pretraining) and no statistical significance testing; runtime/memory and error propagation from wikifying all n-grams (n≤4) are not reported.
- Robustness and NIL handling: The approach’s sensitivity to wikifier errors is acknowledged but not deeply quantified; NIL/linker thresholds and domain shift effects are underexplored.

Questions to authors:
- Can the approach be migrated from Freebase to Wikidata types and properties? Any preliminary results?
- What are the runtime and memory costs of wikifying all n-grams per document, and how does inference latency scale with Wikipedia size?
- How are NIL decisions thresholded in practice, and how sensitive are results to the linker confidence?
- Are gains statistically significant across runs, and can you report significance vs. key baselines?
- In multi-source training, how are languages weighted, and does curriculum or sample balancing matter?
