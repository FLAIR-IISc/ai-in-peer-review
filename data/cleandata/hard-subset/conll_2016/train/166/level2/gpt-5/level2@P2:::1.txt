Summary and key contributions
- A simple, language-agnostic featureization for NER based on cross-lingual wikification: every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in any target language is grounded to English Wikipedia, and the resulting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories are used as delexicalized features. This enables direct transfer without parallel data or target-language annotation.
- Extensive empirical study across nine languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts), showing: (a) substantial gains for direct transfer on CoNLL languages, (b) competitive to strong weakly supervised methods on several low-resource languages, and (c) consistent gains when augmenting monolingual models with wikifier features.
- Analysis of the dependency of performance on Wikipedia coverage and preliminary evidence that multi-source training typically helps.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, generally applicable idea: Using cross-lingual entity types and categories as delexicalized features is intuitive and orthogonal to projection/embeddings. The paper convincingly shows these signals are strong even with imperfect linking.
- Strong empirical gains in direct transfer on CoNLL: Adding wikifier features improves English→Dutch/German/Spanish over a solid baseline; gains are large for Dutch and German (Table 2), and ablations isolate the effect of each feature group.
- Broad evaluation with informative analysis: The study spans 9 languages across scripts, and includes (i) monolingual vs. direct transfer, (ii) wikifier-only/baseline/gazetteer vs. +wikifier ablations, and (iii) controlled experiments demonstrating how shrinking the English–target intersection and removing Freebase queries degrade performance (Table 3). This ties improvements clearly to the proposed signal and to resource availability.
- Practical guidance: The observation that for non-Latin scripts the wikifier-only configuration can outperform models that include English-lexicalized features is valuable for practitioners deciding what to deploy in zero-shot scenarios.
- Multi-source transfer: Results show modest but generally positive trends when training on multiple languages, suggesting the approach is compatible with multilingual supervision.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Wikipedia coverage and Freebase: The method’s core signal depends on the English–target interlanguage link graph and Freebase typing. The paper shows performance drops sharply as this intersection shrinks (Table 3), and that Freebase coverage gaps severely hurt certain languages (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali). Freebase has been sunset; reproducibility and future applicability would benefit from moving to Wikidata. The practical limits of the approach for very small Wikipedias remain unclear.
- Evaluation protocol and comparability ambiguities: For low-resource languages, the tagset is altered (TIME/TTL removed; MISC removed or remapped to LOC in training; MISC ignored for CoNLL evaluation). While understandable, this complicates comparability to prior work and across languages. The paper should report per-type F1 and clarify precisely which tags are evaluated for each condition. Additionally, the comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is hard to interpret given the column ordering in Table 2; for Spanish, it is not obvious the proposed system outperforms bitext clusters. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) appears to mix settings: e.g., for Yoruba/Bengali the best configuration is “wikifier only,” but the highlighted +wikifier setting is much worse; please compare using the best configuration per target to ensure fairness.
- Incomplete details about the wikifier and efficiency: The approach depends critically on Tsai and Roth (2016), but the paper does not provide enough specifics for replication after the stated modifications (feature set, thresholds/linking confidence, NIL handling, training resources for the ranker). Grounding all n-grams up to length 4 is potentially expensive; run-time and memory costs are not reported. These details are important if the claim is “works for all languages in Wikipedia.”
- Missing modern baselines and significance tests: By 2016, cross-lingual embeddings and early neural NER models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with character features) were emerging. While the method is model-agnostic, a comparison to a cross-lingual embedding baseline (or integrating embeddings into the current model) would strengthen claims of generality. No statistical significance is reported for improvements (especially for multi-source training where gains are small).
- Feature configuration/tuning per language: The paper recommends dropping lexical features for non-Latin scripts, yet most aggregate numbers include them. A clear, principled selection protocol (e.g., dev-set based) per target language would avoid conflating configuration choices with method quality.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the column mapping for the Täckström rows in Table 2 and confirm whether your system outperforms their bitext clusters on Spanish. Similarly, for the Zhang et al. comparison, can you report best-per-language configurations (e.g., wikifier-only for YO/BN) to make a fair comparison?
- What training data and features does your modified wikifier use? Are code and a frozen KB snapshot available? How are NIL predictions handled when grounding all n-grams?
- What are the run-time costs of grounding all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per sentence and the overall NER pipeline across languages? Any pruning heuristics?
- How robust are results if Freebase is replaced by Wikidata instance-of/subclass-of types? Do you expect improvements for low-resource languages?
- Can you provide per-entity-type F1 and an analysis of boundary vs. type errors to better understand where wikifier features help/hurt?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting a simple regression relating F1 to English–target intersection size to quantify the dependency.
- Explore an oracle that uses only types vs. only categories to assess which contributes most; current ablations remove Freebase queries or interlanguage links jointly.
- A CRF or constrained decoding could avoid invalid BIO sequences; your greedy decoding with previous-tag features may suffer from error propagation.
- Minor: terminology consistency (“FreeBase” vs “Freebase”); clarify footnotes; a few typos (e.g., “languageindependent”).
- Releasing code, gazetteers, and wikifier resources would greatly enhance impact.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, broadly applicable idea with solid empirical evidence and useful analysis. Addressing the comparability issues, detailing the wikifier, and future-proofing the KB dependence (e.g., Wikidata) would substantially strengthen the paper.
