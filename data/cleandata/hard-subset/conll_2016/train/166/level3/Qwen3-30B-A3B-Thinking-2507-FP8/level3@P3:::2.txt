

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The method grounds words and phrases from any language to English Wikipedia entries, leveraging Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables direct transfer NER (training on English, testing on other languages) and improves monolingual NER across 9 languages, including low-resource ones with small Wikipedias.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification generates effective language-independent features for NER, enabling direct transfer across languages without requiring parallel data
2. Comprehensive evaluation across 9 languages (4 CoNLL languages and 5 low-resource languages) showing consistent improvements over strong baselines
3. Insight that wikifier features are particularly valuable for non-Latin script languages where lexical features fail

## Strengths
1. The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that wikifier features work well across multiple languages, including low-resource ones with small Wikipedias. The results on Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil are particularly valuable for practical NLP applications in resource-constrained settings.

2. The approach is elegant in its simplicity, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps without needing parallel data or language-specific resources. This makes the method broadly applicable to any language with Wikipedia coverage.

3. The analysis of why wikifier features work better for some languages than others (e.g., non-Latin scripts) provides valuable insights into feature utility across language families and scripts.

## Weaknesses
1. The related work section lacks sufficient comparison with Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and doesn't clearly distinguish this work from other Wikipedia-based NER systems. The authors claim their method is "most closely related" to Kazama & Torisawa, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address the differences in their approach to wikification and handling ambiguity.

2. The paper fails to include POS-tag features in experiments, despite claiming these features are important in direct transfer settings (Tackström et al.). The authors state they couldn't get universal POS tags, but this is a significant omission that weakens their comparison with other direct transfer methods.

3. The claim that approaches from Tackström et al. and Nothman et al. are orthogonal needs empirical validation. The authors should provide preliminary combined-model results to support this claim before making such a strong assertion.

4. The paper lacks comparison with strong projection baselines that don't require parallel data, such as Peirsman & Pado (2009) and Vulić & Moens (2013), which use bilingual semantic spaces. This omission makes it difficult to contextualize the strengths and limitations of the wikification-based approach.

5. Table 4 reports small, inconsistent improvements without statistical significance tests, making it hard to assess whether the gains are meaningful. The paper should report p-values or other statistical measures for the reported differences.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims approaches from Tackström et al. and Nothman et al. are orthogonal, but provides no combined-model results. Could you provide preliminary results combining your method with theirs to validate this claim?

2. The language-pair results in Figure 2 show surprising patterns (e.g., Dutch best for Spanish, Spanish best for Yoruba). Could you provide an analysis of why these particular language pairs work best?

3. The paper mentions that projection can be done without parallel data using bilingual semantic spaces (Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulić & Moens, 2013). Could you add a comparison with these methods to better contextualize your approach?

The paper has a coherent story with convincing experiments but would benefit from addressing these weaknesses. The incremental contribution is valuable for cross-lingual NER, but the paper would be stronger with the suggested improvements.
