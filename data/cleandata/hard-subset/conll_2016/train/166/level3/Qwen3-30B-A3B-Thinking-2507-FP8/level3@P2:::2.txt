

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The main contribution is the demonstration that disambiguating words to English Wikipedia entries (via FreeBase types and categories) provides strong, language-agnostic features for NER. The second contribution is the empirical validation of this approach across 9 languages, including both high-resource CoNLL languages and low-resource languages with small Wikipedia coverage. The third contribution is showing that these features can improve monolingual NER systems, not just direct transfer scenarios.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), which is particularly valuable for cross-lingual NLP. The results consistently show significant improvements over strong baselines, especially for languages where lexical features fail (e.g., Bengali and Tamil).

2. The approach is remarkably simple yet effective, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps without needing parallel data or language-specific resources. This makes the method broadly applicable to any language covered by Wikipedia, which is a significant practical advantage over approaches requiring parallel corpora or language-specific heuristics.

3. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality (Section 4.4) is insightful and well-executed. The experiments showing how removing FreeBase queries or interlanguage links degrades performance provide concrete evidence about the components that make the approach work.

**Weaknesses**

1. The contribution is incremental rather than technically novel. The core idea of using Wikipedia categories for NER is not new (Kazama & Torisawa, 2007), and the paper's approach builds directly on existing wikification techniques (Tsai & Roth, 2016) with minimal methodological innovation. The related work section fails to adequately distinguish the paper's contribution from prior work.

2. The paper omits a critical comparison to the widely used POS-tag features (as in Täckström et al., 2012), which are standard in direct transfer NER. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the authors' features complement or replace standard approaches. The claim that "lexical features are useful only if the target language is close to the training language" isn't empirically supported without this comparison.

3. The improvements from training on multiple languages (Table 4) are marginal (average +1.2 F1) and inconsistent across languages, yet the paper presents them as a significant contribution. Statistical significance tests are missing, making it impossible to assess whether these small improvements are meaningful.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The paper claims that the approach is orthogonal to projection methods, but doesn't provide evidence. Could you include preliminary results showing that combining your features with a projection baseline (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016) yields further improvements?

2. The paper notes that Spanish performs best as a training language for Yoruba (Figure 2), while English is best for most other languages. Could you provide a brief linguistic explanation for this surprising result?

3. Why doesn't the paper include POS-tag features (as in Täckström et al., 2012) in the experiments, given that they're standard in direct transfer NER? Would their inclusion change the results significantly?

4. The paper claims that the proposed method "allows us to train on multiple source languages, typically improving NER results on the target languages." However, the improvements are small (Table 4). Could you clarify what "typically" means in this context, and whether there are specific language pairs where multi-source training provides substantial gains?
