

Review: The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The primary contribution is demonstrating that wikifier-derived FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories serve as robust, language-agnostic features for NER, enabling direct transfer from English to 9 languages (including low-resource ones like Yoruba and Bengali) without parallel data. This is supported by strong empirical results: the model outperforms prior direct-transfer methods on CoNLL languages (e.g., +12.1 F1 on German vs. Täckström baseline) and achieves state-of-the-art results on low-resource languages where prior work required parallel data or native speaker interaction. The secondary contribution is showing that these features also enhance monolingual NER systems across all languages, improving results by up to 4.7 F1 points. The third contribution is the practical demonstration that training on multiple source languages (e.g., English + Spanish) further boosts performance, particularly for low-resource targets like Yoruba.

Strengths: First, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The paper tests 9 languages spanning Latin/non-Latin scripts, including five low-resource languages where Wikipedia size is limited (e.g., Yoruba: 31K articles). The model consistently outperforms strong baselines (e.g., +12.1 F1 on German vs. Täckström, +14.3 F1 on Turkish vs. Zhang et al.), proving wikifier features are highly effective for cross-lingual transfer. Second, the monolingual enhancement results are significant: adding wikifier features to English-trained models improves F1 by 4.7–5.2 points across all languages, demonstrating their utility beyond cross-lingual settings. Third, the methodology is practical and minimally resource-intensive—only requiring multilingual Wikipedia dumps, no parallel data or language-specific heuristics, making it broadly applicable.

Weaknesses: First, the related work section inadequately distinguishes this work from Kazama & Torisawa (2007), who also used Wikipedia categories for NER. The paper claims their method "is not robust to ambiguity," but fails to empirically compare against it (e.g., on English or German), weakening the novelty claim. Second, the omission of POS features is a critical flaw. Täckström et al. (2012) show POS tags significantly improve direct transfer, yet this paper excludes them due to "unavailability" of universal POS tags. The authors should report results with POS features (e.g., using multilingual POS tags) to validate their claim that lexical features are "not useful" in cross-lingual settings. Third, the claim that their approach is "orthogonal" to Täckström’s clustering method lacks evidence. The paper should include preliminary results combining both methods (e.g., training on English + Spanish with cross-lingual clusters) to substantiate this. Fourth, Table 4’s marginal gains (e.g., +0.5 F1 for EN+ES+NL+DE vs. EN) lack statistical significance testing, making the "improvement" claim unsubstantiated. Fifth, Section 3.3’s model modifications (e.g., n-gram processing) are unclear; a running example would clarify how they prevent over-linking (e.g., "in Germany" → "Germany" as a single entity).

Questions to Authors: 1) How does the model’s performance compare to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) on English/German, where their method was applied? 2) Could you provide results using universal POS tags to assess their impact on cross-lingual transfer? 3) What is the statistical significance (e.g., p-values) for the improvements in Table 4? 4) Can you elaborate on the surprising language-pair results (e.g., Spanish as best training language for Yoruba, Dutch for Spanish) with linguistic or Wikipedia-size-based analysis? 5) Please clarify the model modification in Section 3.3 with a concrete example (e.g., processing "Albrecht Lehmann" in German).
