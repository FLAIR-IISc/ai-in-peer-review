

This paper presents a novel approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The primary contribution is the demonstration that grounding words and phrases from any language to English Wikipedia entries provides strong language-independent features (FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) that significantly improve NER performance across multiple languages. The second contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across 9 languages (including low-resource and non-Latin script languages), showing that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods in direct transfer settings without requiring target language annotation. The third contribution is the analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality, revealing that smaller Wikipedia sizes lead to weaker features but still provide meaningful improvements.

Strengths: The paper's most compelling strength is its effective use of cross-lingual wikification to create truly language-independent features. The authors show that even with imperfect disambiguation (as demonstrated in Figure 1), FreeBase types provide strong signals for NER across languages. This insight is novel and valuable, as it demonstrates that Wikipedia's structured information can be leveraged without requiring language-specific resources. The second strength is the comprehensive evaluation across 9 languages, including Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. The results for low-resource languages are particularly impressive, showing significant improvements over methods that require parallel text or native speaker interaction. The third strength is the insightful analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality (Table 3), which provides valuable guidance for future work on cross-lingual NER.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the insufficient analysis of how entities not present in Wikipedia affect the model's performance. The paper does not address how the model handles novel entities or proper names not found in Wikipedia, which is a critical limitation for NER. The second weakness is the lack of detailed analysis on the error propagation from the wikifier to the NER model. The authors do not quantify how often wikifier errors lead to NER prediction errors, which is crucial for understanding the method's reliability. The third weakness is the inadequate explanation for the poor performance on Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are included (Table 2). The authors note the issue but don't provide sufficient analysis or potential solutions for this problem. The fourth weakness is the lack of detail on how the wikifier handles non-Latin scripts, particularly for languages without FreeBase support (Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), which affects the quality of features extracted.

Questions to Authors: 
1. How does the model handle entities not present in Wikipedia, and what is the impact on overall NER performance for such entities?
2. Can you provide a detailed error analysis showing the correlation between wikifier errors and NER prediction errors?
3. What specific modifications would you propose to improve performance on Tamil and Bengali when lexical features are included?
4. How do you handle the situation where the wikifier fails to find any Wikipedia entry for a phrase (e.g., for very new entities), and what features does the model use in such cases?

The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that Wikipedia-derived features can be effectively used for cross-lingual NER. The experimental results are strong and well-analyzed, particularly for low-resource languages. While the weaknesses noted above should be addressed, the core idea is sound and has significant potential for future work in cross-lingual NLP. The method's requirement of only a Wikipedia dump (no parallel data or native speaker interaction) is particularly appealing for low-resource settings. The authors should clarify their approach to handling entities absent from Wikipedia and provide more detailed analysis of error propagation from the wikifier to NER. With these clarifications, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
