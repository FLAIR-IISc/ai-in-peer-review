

# Review for "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification. The authors propose a novel feature set derived from grounding target language phrases to English Wikipedia entries, leveraging Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as language-independent signals. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A method for extracting language-independent features from cross-lingual wikification that can be used to train NER models applicable across all languages with Wikipedia coverage, without requiring parallel text or language-specific resources.

2. Comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating that these features significantly improve both monolingual NER systems and cross-lingual transfer NER systems across 9 languages, including challenging low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts.

3. Insightful analysis showing that the quality of the wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size and language script, with particularly strong results for languages with larger Wikipedia coverage.

## Strengths

1. The paper presents a genuinely language-independent approach to NER that requires only multilingual Wikipedia, eliminating the need for parallel data or language-specific resources. This is a significant contribution to cross-lingual NLP, as it enables NER for languages with no annotated training data using only Wikipedia as a resource.

2. The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough, covering 9 languages across multiple settings (monolingual, cross-lingual transfer, multiple training languages), with detailed analysis of performance differences based on Wikipedia size and language script. The results on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) are particularly impressive given their small Wikipedia sizes.

3. The paper demonstrates that the proposed features enhance both monolingual and cross-lingual NER systems. The 4.3-5.2 point improvement over the strongest baseline (Täckström et al.) on CoNLL languages and the superior performance on low-resource languages compared to Zhang et al. (2016) is compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper does not adequately address how the method handles entities not present in Wikipedia, which is particularly problematic for low-resource languages. For example, when the wikifier returns "NIL" for a mention (as shown in Figure 1), the approach has no way to handle it, potentially leading to NER errors. The authors should clarify how they handle such cases and what impact this has on overall performance.

2. The method's heavy dependence on the cross-lingual wikifier's accuracy is not sufficiently analyzed. The paper shows that wikifier features work well, but it doesn't explore how often wikifier errors lead to NER prediction errors. This is critical for understanding the method's limitations and potential failure modes.

3. The paper demonstrates poor performance on Bengali and Tamil when lexical features are included, yet doesn't provide a clear explanation for this phenomenon. Given that these languages use non-Latin scripts, the authors should investigate why lexical features are harmful here and propose potential solutions.

4. The paper doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods beyond those cited (Zhang et al., 2016 and Täckström et al., 2012). The field has advanced since these works, and the evaluation would be stronger with comparison to more recent approaches.

5. The analysis of why wikifier features work better for some languages than others is limited. The paper shows that Wikipedia size correlates with performance, but doesn't provide deeper linguistic or structural insights into why certain languages benefit more from these features.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do errors in the cross-lingual wikifier (e.g., incorrect disambiguation of phrases) specifically affect NER predictions? Could you provide an error analysis showing the correlation between wikifier errors and NER errors?

2. How do you handle entities that aren't present in Wikipedia, especially for low-resource languages with small Wikipedia? What's the impact on the overall performance, and how does this affect your method's applicability?

3. The paper shows that including lexical features harms performance on Bengali and Tamil, but doesn't explain why. Could you provide more insight into this phenomenon and suggest potential solutions to make the method more robust to non-Latin scripts?
