Summary
The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that derives language-independent features by mapping every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target text to English Wikipedia via a cross-lingual wikifier. It then uses Wikipedia categories and Freebase types from the top candidates as features in an averaged-perceptron NER model. The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps (no parallel data), supports multi-source training, and is evaluated in both monolingual and direct-transfer settings across nine languages, including low-resource and non-Latin scripts. The authors present thorough analyses, including ablations showing how Wikipedia size and interlanguage link availability drive performance, and demonstrate gains over prior direct-transfer baselines and weakly supervised approaches.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Establishing cross-lingual wikification as a strong, language-independent feature source for NER, yielding substantial gains in direct transfer over comparable methods without relying on parallel text or language-specific engineering.
2) A systematic empirical study across 9 languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts), with ablations that quantify how Wikipedia size and interlanguage links affect NER performance; plus evidence that multi-source training provides additional improvements.
3) Demonstration that wikifier-derived features also improve monolingual NER across all tested languages, indicating broad utility beyond the cross-lingual setting.

Strengths
- Strong direct-transfer results with minimal resources: Training only on English and transferring to Spanish/Dutch/German substantially outperforms prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) and beats Zhang et al. on five low-resource languages without requiring parallel corpora or native speaker interaction. The “+Wikifier” features add 10+ F1 for several targets in direct transfer, which is a large and practically meaningful improvement.
- Clear, well-designed ablations and analyses: The “Wikifier only” configuration isolates the cross-lingual signal and shows competitive performance where scripts differ markedly from English. The experiments that remove Freebase queries and randomly drop 50%/90% of interlanguage links convincingly tie performance to Wikipedia coverage and interlanguage links, explaining why low-resource settings (e.g., Yoruba) lag. This analysis goes beyond reporting scores and provides diagnostic value.
- Broad language coverage, including non-Latin scripts: Evaluations on Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil (with entirely different scripts in BN/TA) make a compelling case for generality. The finding that lexical features can hurt non-Latin transfer is important and practically actionable.
- Practicality and portability: The method requires only Wikipedia dumps and a cross-lingual wikifier (no POS tags, no parallel data, minimal language-specific engineering). This makes the approach attractive for rapid deployment and for truly low-resource scenarios.
- Evidence that features are complementary: Wikifier-derived features consistently improve monolingual baselines and help in multi-source training, indicating they capture signals not already provided by standard lexical/gazetteer features.

Weaknesses
- Heavy dependence on wikifier coverage and accuracy: The approach degrades with sparse Wikipedia and weak interlanguage links, and the paper does not quantify how often gold entities are missing from Wikipedia or how NIL/coverage gaps impact recall. Since “Wikifier only” drives results in non-Latin settings, coverage limitations can be a hard bottleneck.
- Limited error attribution for wikification: While ablations are strong, there is no breakdown of how often incorrect or low-confidence links lead to boundary/type errors in NER. Given that top-2 candidate types are used, the noise profile matters; a confusion analysis would help understand error propagation and where to focus robustness improvements.
- Over-reliance on lexical features hurts non-Latin transfer: The paper observes that adding lexical features reduces performance for Bengali/Tamil, but does not propose a principled mechanism to balance feature classes. Group-wise regularization, feature scaling, or training-time reweighting could mitigate this and make the model more robust across scripts.
- Outdated base architecture and missing neural baselines: The model is a Ratinov & Roth–style perceptron with handcrafted features. By 2016, strong neural NER baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with char encoders) were available. It remains unclear whether the wikifier features would still deliver the same magnitude of gains when combined with modern encoders; a comparison would strengthen claims.
- Computational cost and scalability not discussed: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 can be expensive. There is no reporting of runtime, throughput, or memory usage, nor discussion of practical trade-offs (e.g., pruning, caching, batching) needed for large-scale or real-time settings.

Questions to Authors
- Coverage and NIL rates: What fraction of gold entities (by language) have at least one linked Wikipedia candidate (top-1/top-2)? What is the NIL rate, and how does it correlate with recall and F1?
- Error propagation: Among NER false positives/negatives, how many are attributable to incorrect wikifier links vs. NIL cases vs. other feature classes? Can you provide a confusion analysis by entity type?
- Feature balancing: Did you try group-wise regularization, feature scaling, or early stopping to prevent lexical features from dominating in cross-script transfer? How sensitive are results to reweighting feature templates?
- Runtime: What is the end-to-end cost of wikifying every n-gram (tokens/sec per language, model size)? Are there optimizations or practical constraints that limit deployment?
- Freebase reliance and future-proofing: Freebase has been deprecated; can your features be replicated with Wikidata properties and categories, and do results change materially?

Additional Comments
- Reporting the proportion of tokens/mentions successfully linked per language would help reproduceability and guide practitioners on when to expect gains.
- Clarify the handling of MISC tags and conversions when training across datasets to ensure fair comparability.
- Releasing code/models and the exact Wikipedia dump versions used would benefit the community.
- Exploring character-level encoders and multilingual word embeddings in tandem with wikifier features could further improve robustness in non-Latin settings.
