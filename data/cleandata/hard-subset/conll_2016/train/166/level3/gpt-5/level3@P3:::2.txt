Summary
The paper proposes a direct-transfer NER approach that derives language-independent features from cross-lingual wikification: for each n-gram (n ≤ 4), the system links to English Wikipedia and extracts Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features. The model is trained on one or more source languages and applied to any language covered by Wikipedia. Experiments on CoNLL (DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA) show gains over a delexicalized baseline and competitive performance relative to prior work; monolingual models also benefit from the wikifier features. Analyses relate performance to Wikipedia size and examine multi-source training.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Primary: An empirical demonstration that cross-lingual wikification-derived types/categories are strong delexicalized features for direct-transfer NER across diverse scripts and resource conditions.
2) Secondary: Broad, well-executed evaluation across nine languages, including low-resource, with analyses on Wikipedia size and source-language choice; demonstration that multi-source training brings modest additional gains.
3) Tertiary: Practical modifications to Tsai & Roth’s cross-lingual wikifier to handle dense all-n-gram linking and use of top-k candidate features.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent idea with strong empirical signal: The wikification features yield large improvements in direct transfer on DE/NL/ES/TR and remain useful where lexical features fail (BN/TA). The ablations that downsize interlanguage links highlight a plausible causal link between Wikipedia coverage and performance.
- Broad multilingual evaluation: Results on nine languages, including non-Latin scripts, strengthen claims of language independence and practicality (requiring only Wikipedia).
- Orthogonality to other lines of work: The approach does not require parallel data or language-specific heuristics, and could be combined with projection/bitext-cluster methods (e.g., Tackström et al.) or Wikipedia-derived silver data (Nothman et al.).
- Multi-source training and monolingual benefits: Although modest, the consistent gains from multi-source training and the improvements in monolingual settings show the features’ general utility beyond cross-lingual transfer.
- Reproducible building blocks: The feature set is straightforward; the paper cites a known wikifier and explains two key modifications.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty and missing comparisons: The core idea (Wikipedia-based features for NER) is not novel; the contribution is in cross-lingual application and engineering. The paper lacks a strong projection baseline (e.g., Wang & Manning 2014) and does not combine with bitext clusters (Tackström et al.) or Wikipedia silver data (Nothman et al.) to substantiate claims of orthogonality via combined gains.
- Related work coverage is uneven: The comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) needs deeper analysis (e.g., how your disambiguation and top-k typing overcome their ambiguity issues; cross-lingual vs monolingual scope; feature granularity). The distinctions from other Wikipedia-based NER systems should be clarified. Also mention projection without parallel corpora using bilingual semantic spaces (Peirsman & Pado 2009; Vulić & Moens 2013) and position your work relative to them.
- Missing POS-tag feature experiments: Given Tackström et al.’s findings, the dismissal of POS features due to tagger accuracy is unconvincing without empirical evidence. Please run and report experiments with universal POS features (e.g., UPOS), including noisy tags, to assess whether they help or hurt across languages.
- Section 3.3 is underspecified: The edits to Tsai & Roth’s model and their implications for precision/recall trade-offs are hard to follow. A running example showing candidate generation, ranking features, top-2 types/categories, and how linker confidence is used would greatly improve clarity.
- Statistical support and effect sizes: Several gains (e.g., in Table 4 multi-source results) are small/inconsistent. No statistical significance tests are reported. Please run entity-level paired bootstrap or approximate randomization tests to support claims.
- Analysis gaps: The surprising language-pair patterns (e.g., Dutch best for Spanish; Spanish best for Yoruba) are asserted but not explained. Provide analyses of Wikipedia intersection coverage, category/type overlap, annotation guideline effects (e.g., MISC→LOC mapping), capitalization/signal similarities, and script effects.
- Practical dependencies: Heavy reliance on Freebase (now deprecated) raises concerns for longevity. Clarify whether Wikidata properties or schema-aligned types can replace Freebase and how results might change.

Questions to Authors
- Section 3.3 details: Please provide a step-by-step running example of the modified candidate generation and ranking when wikifying all n-grams, including how you avoid over-linking stopword n-grams and how the linker confidence rejects low-confidence predictions.
- POS features: Which UPOS taggers did you try? Can you include results with noisy POS (projected or off-the-shelf) to quantify the trade-off across languages?
- Orthogonality claims: Can you show preliminary combined results for a subset of languages by adding (a) bitext-induced clusters (Tackström et al.), and (b) Wikipedia-silver data (Nothman et al.) to your model?
- Projection baselines: Can you add a strong projection baseline (e.g., Wang & Manning 2014 expectation projection) and, if possible, a projection-without-parallel variant using bilingual semantic spaces (Peirsman & Pado; Vulić & Moens), to contextualize your approach?
- Statistical testing: Which test will you use for F1 (e.g., bootstrap over documents)? Please report confidence intervals or p-values for key comparisons.
- Feature inventory: How many distinct types/categories fire per token on average? Are features binarized or weighted? Do top-2 candidates ever conflict, and how is conflict resolved?

Additional Comments
- Related work: Deepen the comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and clarify distinctions from other Wikipedia-based NER/EL systems; add discussion of bilingual semantic spaces for projection without parallel text.
- Error and ablation analyses: Include tag-wise breakdowns (PER/LOC/ORG), boundary vs type errors, and sensitivity to k in top-k candidates. Report runtime of “wikify every n-gram” and pruning heuristics.
- Significance: Add statistical tests for all tables where claims are made, especially Table 4; consider reporting macro-averaged gains and variance across seeds.
- Typos/clarity: Fix issues such as “languageindependent,” “as a the base model,” inconsistent capitalization, stray footnote markers (e.g., “n-gram 1”), and awkward phrasing. Proofread tables/figures (e.g., table captions, column labels).
- Sustainability: Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata item classes and describing the mapping.
- Reproducibility: If possible, release code, feature extraction scripts, and the cross-lingual gazetteers.
