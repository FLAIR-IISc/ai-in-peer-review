Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes a language-independent NER approach that derives features by mapping target-language n-grams to English Wikipedia entries via a cross-lingual wikifier. These Wikipedia-derived categories and Freebase types are used as delexicalized features, improving both monolingual NER and direct transfer (English→target). The claimed scope spans CoNLL languages and several low-resource/non-Latin languages, with analysis of Wikipedia size effects and multi-source training.

Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength):
1) Empirical demonstration that wikifier-derived, language-agnostic features yield large gains in both monolingual and cross-lingual NER, outperforming strong direct-transfer baselines and competing approaches without target annotation.
2) A practical, broadly applicable featureization pipeline that requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no bitext), including modifications to wikify all n-grams and to expose ranker confidence as features.
3) Careful, valuable analysis across resource levels and scripts, including ablations on Wikipedia size/Freebase availability and multi-source training effects.

Strengths:
- Strong cross-lingual gains and competitive monolingual improvements: Wikifier features substantially lift direct transfer on Dutch, German, Turkish, and maintain signal even where lexical overlap is minimal (Bengali/Tamil), outperforming Täckström’s clusters and Zhang et al. for low-resource cases.
- Minimal resource requirements: The method relies only on multilingual Wikipedia, increasing practicality for many languages, and avoids language-specific heuristics or parallel corpora.
- Thorough experimentation and analysis: Ablations that remove Freebase queries or interlanguage links convincingly explain performance variations; analysis across Latin vs. non-Latin scripts is informative.
- Multi-source training: Demonstrates small but consistent gains, suggesting robustness and extensibility.

Weaknesses:
- Coverage gaps for entities not in Wikipedia/Freebase are not quantified; it is unclear how NIL entities affect recall and error modes (boundary vs. type).
- Heavy dependency on the cross-lingual wikifier: The paper does not report how often wikifier errors propagate to NER errors or how confidence is calibrated to mitigate noise.
- Feature-group imbalance: In direct transfer for Tamil/Bengali, adding lexical/gazetteer features hurts, suggesting the learner overfits to English-lexical cues; no principled regularization per feature class is explored.
- Reliance on Freebase types (deprecated) and English-centric categories raises concerns about sustainability and cross-cultural bias; alternatives (e.g., Wikidata) are not evaluated.

Questions to authors:
- What proportion of mentions in each dataset are NIL (no Wikipedia entry), and how does performance break down on NIL vs. linked mentions?
- Can you report the correlation between wikifier top-1 accuracy/confidence and NER precision/recall, and quantify error propagation?
- Have you tried feature-group regularization (e.g., separate L2/orthogonalization, group dropout, or confidence-weighted training) to prevent lexical over-reliance, especially for non-Latin scripts?
- Could Wikidata-based typing replace Freebase, and with what impact?
