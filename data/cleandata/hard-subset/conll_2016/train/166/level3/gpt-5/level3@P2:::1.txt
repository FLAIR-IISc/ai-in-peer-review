Summary and claimed contributions:
This paper proposes a language-independent approach to cross-lingual NER by grounding n-grams in any target language to English Wikipedia titles via a cross-lingual wikifier, and turning the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types into delexicalized features. The model is built on a strong English NER baseline and evaluated both monolingually and in direct-transfer settings across nine languages, including five low-resource/non-Latin languages. The authors also study the impact of Wikipedia size and multi-source training.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
- Primary: Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification yields effective, language-independent features (FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories, and ranker confidence) that substantially improve NER in both monolingual and direct-transfer scenarios, outperforming prior direct-transfer methods.
- Secondary: Broad empirical study across 9 languages (including low-resource and non-Latin scripts) with careful ablations isolating the wikifier features, analyses of Wikipedia size and interlanguage links, and exploration of multi-source training.
- Tertiary: Practical insights on when to include lexical/gazetteer features vs rely primarily on wikifier features (e.g., for non-Latin scripts), and evidence that these features also help monolingual NER.

Strengths:
- Clear, impactful idea with strong empirical support: Turning cross-lingual wikification signals into NER features is conceptually clean and practically valuable. The ablations show that these features carry substantial predictive power by themselves and add consistent gains on top of gazetteers and base features across 9 languages. In transfer, the +wikifier setting delivers large gains (e.g., >10 F1 on several CoNLL languages), underscoring the utility of the delexicalized signal.
- Broad and rigorous evaluation: The paper goes beyond the usual CoNLL trio to include five low-resource languages (including non-Latin scripts), and reports both monolingual and direct-transfer results. Comparisons to strong prior work (e.g., Täckström et al. in CoNLL, Zhang et al. in low-resource) are favorable despite requiring fewer resources and no parallel text. The study further probes multi-source training, yielding small but consistent improvements.
- Insightful analysis of coverage and failure modes: The experiments that remove FreeBase queries and randomly prune interlanguage links convincingly demonstrate how Wikipedia size and English intersection density affect performance, explaining weaker gains on low-resource languages. The “wikifier-only” setting is particularly informative and aligns with observed script/coverage differences.
- Practicality and generality: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel, sentence/word alignment, or target-language supervision), and is applicable to all languages present in Wikipedia. The modifications to wikify all n-grams are well-motivated and make the pipeline usable as a general feature extractor.

Weaknesses:
- Dependence on wikifier coverage and FreeBase: Performance degrades when interlanguage links or FreeBase types are scarce (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), and for entities not present in Wikipedia. The paper shows macro effects via pruning experiments, but does not quantify coverage at the mention level (e.g., linking/type-attachment rates per dataset) nor how often NILs occur and how they impact precision/recall per entity type.
- Limited error propagation analysis: Since the method leans heavily on cross-lingual entity linking, it would help to quantify how wikifier errors (wrong title, wrong type, NIL) translate into NER boundary/type errors. Although the model includes ranker confidence features (a “linker”), there is no analysis of calibration or how thresholding/feature scaling mitigates bad links.
- Over-reliance on lexical features harms transfer in non-Latin scripts: The direct-transfer results on Bengali/Tamil improve when lexical features are removed (wikifier-only). This points to feature-group imbalance during training on English. The paper acknowledges this qualitatively but does not explore remedies (e.g., group-wise regularization/reweighting, feature dropout, or curriculum that upweights delexicalized features).
- Evaluation design details could be clearer: The relabeling of MISC to LOC (or removal) for cross-corpus consistency is reasonable, but may confound cross-paper comparability. A short sensitivity check (e.g., with/without relabeling) or more detail on how many instances were affected would strengthen the claims. Statistical significance testing is not reported.

Questions to authors:
- Coverage and linking statistics: What proportion of tokens/spans receive at least one wikifier-derived type/category feature per language? What is the distribution across PER/LOC/ORG? How often do NILs occur?
- Error propagation: Among NER errors, what fraction are attributable to wikifier mistakes versus missing links versus misleading types/categories? Can you share confusion matrices with and without wikifier features?
- Entities absent from Wikipedia: How does the system behave for emerging or highly local entities with no Wikipedia page? Do gazetteer patterns or contextual signals compensate, and to what extent?
- Feature balancing: Did you try regularizing feature groups differently (e.g., group lasso, per-group learning rates, or reweighting) to avoid overemphasis on lexical features in transfer? Any preliminary results?
- Resource choice: Given the observed limitations for FreeBase types in some languages, can the pipeline easily switch to Wikidata (or similar) for types? Would the results likely improve in low-resource settings?

Additional comments:
- Consider reporting per-class (PER/LOC/ORG) F1 for monolingual and transfer settings to reveal where wikifier features help most, especially for low-resource scripts.
- A simple feature-group reweighting schedule (e.g., annealing lexical features or adding feature dropout) might improve transfer to non-Latin scripts without hurting Latin-script languages.
- Please include runtime and memory costs for wikifying all n-grams and feature extraction at test time; this will help assess scalability.
- The study on multi-source training is encouraging. It would be useful to detail how training data were mixed (balanced vs proportional) and whether simple sample weighting by Wikipedia size or target similarity improves further.
- Releasing feature extraction code and the language-specific gazetteers would greatly aid reproducibility and adoption.

Overall, I find the paper clear, convincing, and practically useful. The idea of cross-lingual wikifier-derived features for NER is well executed and supported by thorough experiments. Addressing the coverage/error analyses and exploring simple feature-group balancing would further strengthen the work.
