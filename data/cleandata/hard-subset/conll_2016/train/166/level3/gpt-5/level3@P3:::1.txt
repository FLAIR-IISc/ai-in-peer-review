Summary
This paper presents a direct-transfer, language-independent NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification: every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language document is mapped to English Wikipedia titles and associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories. These derived signals are used as features in a standard NER model. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data), and is evaluated across CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), with additional monolingual and multi-source training experiments. Results show consistent gains from the Wikipedia-derived features in both monolingual and direct-transfer settings, with insightful analysis on how Wikipedia size affects performance and how multi-source training helps.

Main Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, language-independent featureization of NER using cross-lingual wikification to English Wikipedia, yielding strong delexicalized signals (Freebase types, categories) for mention boundary detection and typing in direct-transfer scenarios.
2) A thorough empirical study across nine languages, including low-resource and non-Latin scripts, with clear analyses linking gains and failure modes to Wikipedia size, interlanguage link coverage, and wikifier behavior.
3) Demonstration that the same features also improve monolingual NER across all nine languages, indicating general utility beyond transfer.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with strong empirical gains: Adding wikifier features substantially improves direct-transfer performance (e.g., +10 F1 or more on Dutch, German, Turkish over base+gazetteer), surpasses Täckström et al.’s direct-transfer baseline and bitext cluster model on CoNLL languages, and outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages without requiring parallel data or native speaker time. In monolingual settings, the features consistently improve over base+gazetteers.
- Breadth and relevance of evaluation: The study covers nine languages, including non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) and very small Wikipedias (Yoruba), providing an unusually broad picture for 2016. The multi-source training and training–test matrix further strengthen the claims.
- Insightful analysis of resource constraints: The authors explicitly quantify how performance degrades as interlanguage links and Freebase queries are removed, tying low-resource performance to coverage. This is valuable for practitioners deciding when/where the approach will work.
- Minimal resource assumptions and portability: Requiring only Wikipedia (no sentence-/word-aligned corpora) makes the approach easy to deploy. The method is orthogonal to clustering/projection and could be combined with them.
- Practical modifications to wikification for NER: The adjustments to generate candidates only for whole n-grams and to omit global mention-embedding features are reasonable, and the use of ranker confidence as a linker is a nice touch.

Weaknesses
- Heavy dependence on wikifier quality and coverage: Performance correlates strongly with Wikipedia size and interlanguage link density. For languages with sparse coverage (e.g., Yoruba) or non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil with weaker Freebase linkage), gains shrink and absolute performance can be low. The paper stops short of quantifying how often wikifier errors directly cause NER errors, or how NIL (no-link) cases impact recall and boundary detection.
- Entities absent from Wikipedia: The approach does not directly address entities that lack Wikipedia pages (e.g., local businesses, emerging persons). While categories/types help even with imperfect disambiguation, it remains unclear how NIL rates affect NER recall, especially for tail entities in low-resource languages.
- Over-reliance on lexical features in transfer: The results show that adding lexical features hurts direct transfer on Tamil and Bengali (non-Latin); the model appears to overweight English lexical features learned in training. The paper would benefit from stronger regularization or feature balancing across classes/templates to mitigate this.
- Limited discussion of runtime/scale: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 can be computationally heavy and may introduce many false candidates. The paper does not report throughput, memory usage, or pipeline costs at scale.
- Fragility of Freebase reliance: Freebase was being deprecated around this period; a discussion of porting to Wikidata (or another KB) and any expected impact on features would strengthen the long-term utility.

Questions to Authors
- Coverage and error propagation: What fraction of gold mentions are linked to the correct Wikipedia entity (top-1/top-2)? What is the overall NIL rate by language? Among NER errors, what proportion can be attributed to (i) incorrect links, (ii) NIL, or (iii) noisy categories/types?
- Handling non-Wikipedia entities: Do you use any specific back-off strategies when the wikifier returns NIL (e.g., rely more on character/subword, morphology, or context-only features)? Could a NIL-confidence feature help?
- Feature regularization: Have you tried template-wise regularization (e.g., group lasso/elastic net per feature class), per-class learning rates, or early stopping that prioritizes delexicalized features to prevent lexical overfitting in transfer? Any results on reweighting lexical vs wikifier features for non-Latin targets?
- Runtime: What is the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per sentence and the overall throughput? Any pruning or caching strategies to make it production-ready?
- Knowledge base portability: If Freebase types are replaced by Wikidata properties/classes, do you expect similar gains? Any preliminary evidence?

Additional Comments
- The label harmonization (e.g., mapping/removing MISC, converting some MISC to LOC using Freebase types) is reasonable but could introduce biases; please quantify its effect on results.
- Consider augmenting NIL cases with cross-lingual subword/character features or multilingual embeddings to better cover entities outside Wikipedia.
- The multi-source training gains are modest but positive; reporting statistical significance would help. Also, a simple weighting scheme favoring high-coverage languages might further help.
- Future work could explore joint inference between NER and wikification (lightweight, scalable variants) to reduce cascading errors without sacrificing portability.

Overall Evaluation
I like this paper. The core idea is simple and effective, the experiments are broad and carefully analyzed, and the contribution is clear: cross-lingual wikification provides strong, language-independent features that improve both monolingual and direct-transfer NER. Addressing the dependence on wikifier coverage and better regularizing feature classes to avoid lexical overfit (especially for non-Latin scripts) would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance.
