Summary and Contributions
- Primary: A coherent empirical study demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification yields strong, language-independent features for direct-transfer NER across nine languages, improving both monolingual and cross-lingual performance.
- Secondary: Practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier to densely link n-grams and inject Freebase types/Wikipedia categories as delexicalized NER features.
- Tertiary: Broad evaluation (CoNLL + five low-resource languages) and analyses relating performance to Wikipedia size and multi-source training.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness: The wikifier features provide sizable gains in direct transfer, outperforming Täckström et al. (baseline and bitext clusters) on CoNLL and Zhang et al. (2016) on low-resource languages despite using fewer resources.
- Generality and simplicity: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia and integrates cleanly with a standard NER model; it also benefits monolingual NER.
- Diagnostic analyses: The study relating performance to intersection size with English and ablations removing Freebase queries/interlanguage links is insightful and supports the method’s mechanism.
- Multi-source training: Results show that adding annotated data from multiple languages is usually safe and can help.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The contribution is incremental; the core idea (Wikipedia-derived features for NER) is known, and the technical changes are modest.
- Related work gaps: Insufficient comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and unclear positioning vs. other Wikipedia-based NER systems (e.g., Nothman et al.).
- Missing baselines/ablations: No POS-tag feature experiments (as in Täckström et al.) to assess cross-lingual utility; no strong projection baselines, including projection without parallel data via bilingual semantic spaces (Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulić & Moens, 2013).
- Unclear methodology: Section 3.3 (edits to Tsai & Roth) is hard to follow; a running example is needed to make candidate generation/ranking changes precise.
- Claims of orthogonality untested: No preliminary combined-model results with Täckström-style clusters or Nothman silver data to substantiate orthogonality.
- Analysis and reporting: Table 4 improvements are small/inconsistent; no statistical significance tests. Surprising training–test language pair findings (e.g., Dutch→Spanish, Spanish→Yoruba) lack explanation.
- Presentation: Several typos and awkward sentences; cited examples need correction.

Questions to Authors
- Please add POS-tag feature experiments (universal tags) and report their impact across languages.
- Provide combined results with (i) Täckström et al. clusters and (ii) Nothman-style silver data to support the orthogonality claim.
- Include strong projection baselines, including semantic-space projection without parallel data (Peirsman & Pado; Vulić & Moens), and position your approach accordingly.
- Clarify Section 3.3 with a step-by-step running example of your modified candidate generation/ranking.
- Explain the cross-language training anomalies (Dutch best for Spanish; Spanish best for Yoruba).
- Report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or AR) for Table 4 and key comparisons.
- Proofread carefully and fix the cited examples.
