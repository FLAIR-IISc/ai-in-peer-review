- Summary: Proposes direct-transfer NER using cross-lingual wikification features (Freebase types/Wikipedia categories) on all n-grams, trained on source languages and applied to any Wikipedia-covered language; evaluated on 9 languages with analyses.

- Primary contribution: Empirical demonstration that wikification-derived types/categories are strong delexicalized features for direct-transfer NER across scripts and resource settings.

- Secondary contribution: Broad evaluation across nine languages (including low-resource) with analyses on Wikipedia size and multi-source training effects.

- Tertiary contribution: Practical modifications to Tsai & Roth’s wikifier to handle dense all-n-gram linking and top-k candidate features.

- Strength: Clear idea with strong empirical gains on several languages and plausible link between Wikipedia coverage and performance.

- Strength: Broad multilingual evaluation (nine languages, non-Latin scripts) supports language-independence and practicality.

- Strength: Orthogonal to other methods—doesn’t require parallel data and could combine with projection/bitext-cluster or Wikipedia-silver approaches.

- Strength: Multi-source training and monolingual experiments show consistent, if modest, additional gains.

- Strength: Reproducible, straightforward feature set and documented wikifier modifications.

- Weakness: Incremental novelty—Wikipedia-based features are not new; novelty largely engineering and cross-lingual application.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to strong projection baselines (e.g., Wang & Manning 2014) and no combined experiments with bitext clusters or Wikipedia silver data.

- Weakness: Related work coverage uneven—needs deeper comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER systems, plus bilingual semantic-space projection work.

- Weakness: POS-tag feature dismissal is unconvincing—empirical experiments with universal POS (noisy or projected) are missing.

- Weakness: Section 3.3 underspecified—edits to Tsai & Roth’s model and precision/recall implications need a clear running example.

- Weakness: No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals reported for small/inconsistent gains.

- Weakness: Analysis gaps—unexplained surprising language-pair transfer patterns; lacking analyses of Wikipedia intersection, annotation guidelines, capitalization, and script effects.

- Weakness: Practical dependency on Freebase (deprecated) raises longevity concerns; need discussion of Wikidata replacement.

- Question: Provide a step-by-step running example of modified candidate generation/ranking for all n-grams, including stopword handling and confidence thresholds.

- Question: Which UPOS taggers were tried; please include experiments with noisy or projected POS tags across languages.

- Question: Can you show preliminary combined results adding (a) bitext-induced clusters and (b) Wikipedia-silver data for some languages to support orthogonality claims?

- Question: Can you add a strong projection baseline (e.g., expectation projection) and projection-without-parallel variants to contextualize the approach?

- Question: Which statistical test will you use for F1 (e.g., paired bootstrap) and please report confidence intervals/p-values for key comparisons.

- Question: How many distinct types/categories fire per token on average; are features binarized/weighted; how are conflicting top-k candidates resolved?

- Additional: Deepen related-work comparisons (Kazama & Torisawa, bilingual semantic spaces) and clarify distinctions from other Wikipedia-based systems.

- Additional: Include detailed error/ablation analyses (PER/LOC/ORG breakdowns, boundary vs type errors, sensitivity to k) and runtime/pruning details for wikifying all n-grams.

- Additional: Add statistical tests for all tables, report macro-averaged gains and variance across random seeds.

- Additional: Fix typos/clarity issues, proofread figures/tables, and clean up formatting/footnotes.

- Additional: Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata classes and describe the mapping and its effect.

- Additional: Release code, feature extraction scripts, and cross-lingual gazetteers to improve reproducibility.
