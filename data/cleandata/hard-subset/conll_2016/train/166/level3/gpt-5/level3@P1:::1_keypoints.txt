Summary:
- Proposes cross-lingual NER using wikifier-derived, language-independent features from n-grams mapped to English Wikipedia categories/Freebase types, trained with an averaged-perceptron.
- Requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps (no parallel data), supports multi-source training, and shows gains across nine languages with thorough ablations.

Contributions:
- Establishes cross-lingual wikification as a strong, language-independent feature source for NER without parallel text or language-specific engineering.
- Provides a systematic empirical study across 9 languages with ablations quantifying effects of Wikipedia size and interlanguage links, and shows multi-source training gains.
- Shows wikifier-derived features also improve monolingual NER, indicating utility beyond cross-lingual transfer.

Strengths:
- Strong direct-transfer improvements (10+ F1 in some cases) using only English training and Wikipedia-based features, outperforming prior baselines.
- Clear, well-designed ablations isolating wikifier signal and linking performance to Wikipedia coverage and interlanguage links.
- Broad language coverage including non-Latin scripts, with actionable finding that lexical features can hurt non-Latin transfer.
- Practical and portable approach needing only Wikipedia dumps and a wikifier, suitable for low-resource deployment.
- Wikifier features complement standard lexical/gazetteer features and aid multi-source training and monolingual baselines.

Weaknesses:
- Heavy dependence on wikifier coverage and interlanguage links; sparse Wikipedia hurts performance and recall, especially for low-resource languages.
- No detailed analysis of how incorrect or low-confidence wikifier links cause NER boundary/type errors.
- Adds lexical features that degrade non-Latin transfer without proposing principled balancing mechanisms (e.g., group regularization).
- Uses an older perceptron-based architecture and lacks comparisons to contemporary neural NER baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF).
- Does not discuss computational cost or scalability of wikifying every n-gram (runtime, throughput, memory).

Questions to Authors:
- What fraction of gold entities per language have at least one linked Wikipedia candidate (top-1/top-2) and what are the NIL rates?
- Among NER errors, how many are due to incorrect wikifier links vs. NIL cases vs. other feature errors (confusion analysis by type)?
- Did you try group-wise regularization, feature scaling, or reweighting to prevent lexical features from dominating cross-script transfer?
- What is the runtime and resource cost of wikifying every n-gram (tokens/sec, model size) and are there practical optimizations?
- Can Freebase-based features be replicated with Wikidata properties/categories, and would results change materially?

Additional Comments:
- Reporting proportion of tokens/mentions successfully linked per language would aid reproducibility and practitioner expectations.
- Clarify handling/conversion of MISC tags across datasets for fair comparison.
- Releasing code, models, and exact Wikipedia dump versions would benefit the community.
- Exploring character-level encoders and multilingual embeddings alongside wikifier features could improve non-Latin robustness.
