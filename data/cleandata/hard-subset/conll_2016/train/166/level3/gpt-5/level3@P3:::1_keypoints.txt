- Summary: Proposes language-independent NER using cross-lingual wikification to map n-grams to English Wikipedia + Freebase types/categories as features, evaluated on 9 languages with monolingual and direct-transfer experiments.

- Contribution 1: Practical, language-independent featureization using wikification-derived delexicalized signals for mention detection and typing.

- Contribution 2: Thorough empirical study across nine languages (including low-resource and non-Latin scripts) with analyses linking performance to Wikipedia coverage and wikifier behavior.

- Contribution 3: Shows wikifier features improve both direct-transfer and monolingual NER, demonstrating general utility beyond transfer.

- Strength: Clear, simple idea with strong empirical gains (large F1 improvements vs baseline and prior work) without parallel data or native-speaker effort.

- Strength: Broad and relevant evaluation including non-Latin scripts and very small Wikipedias, plus multi-source training experiments.

- Strength: Insightful analysis quantifying how performance depends on interlanguage link and Freebase coverage.

- Strength: Minimal resource assumptions and portability; approach is orthogonal to projection/clustering methods.

- Strength: Practical wikifier modifications for NER (n-gram-only candidates, ranker confidence use) improve applicability.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on wikifier quality and Wikipedia coverage, reducing gains for sparse languages and non-Latin scripts.

- Weakness: Does not handle entities absent from Wikipedia well; unclear impact of NIL rates on recall and tail entities.

- Weakness: Over-reliance on lexical features in transfer causes degradation on Tamil/Bengali; needs better regularization or feature balancing.

- Weakness: Limited discussion of runtime/scale costs for wikifying all n-grams and handling many candidates.

- Weakness: Fragility from reliance on Freebase (deprecated); lacking discussion of porting to Wikidata or other KBs.

- Question: What fraction of gold mentions are correctly linked (top-1/top-2) and what are NIL rates by language?

- Question: How often do NER errors stem from incorrect links, NIL, or noisy categories/types?

- Question: Are there back-off strategies for NIL cases (subword/morphology/context-only features) or NIL-confidence features?

- Question: Have the authors tried template-wise regularization or reweighting to prevent lexical overfitting for non-Latin targets?

- Question: What is the computational cost/throughput of wikifying all n-grams and are there pruning/caching strategies for production?

- Additional comment: Label harmonization choices (e.g., MISC handling) may introduce bias and should be quantified.

- Additional comment: Augmenting NIL cases with cross-lingual subword/character features or multilingual embeddings could help tail entities.

- Additional comment: Report statistical significance for multi-source gains and consider weighting schemes favoring high-coverage languages.

- Additional comment: Future work could explore lightweight joint inference between NER and wikification to reduce cascading errors.

- Overall evaluation: Strong, well-analyzed paper with a simple effective idea and broad experiments; recommend acceptance subject to addressing coverage/regularization and KB portability concerns.
