Summary and contributions
This paper studies direct-transfer cross-lingual NER by leveraging cross-lingual wikification to obtain language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for every n-gram in a document. Trained on one or more source languages, the model is applied to target languages without target-side supervision, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump. Experiments span CoNLL languages (de, es, nl) and five low-resource languages (tr, tl, yo, bn, ta), with additional analysis on Wikipedia size and multi-source training.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that wikifier-derived semantic features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) are strong, language-agnostic signals for NER, substantially improving direct-transfer performance over comparable approaches across 9 languages.
2) A simple, scalable direct-transfer NER architecture that relies only on multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text, taggers, or language-specific heuristics), with evidence that multi-source training typically helps.
3) A broad experimental study, including low-resource settings and analyses linking performance to Wikipedia size/coverage, plus evidence that the same features also improve monolingual NER.

While the method is incremental rather than technically novel, the overall story is coherent and the empirical results are convincing.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual performance with minimal resources. Training on English and applying to es/nl/de yields consistent and often large gains over direct-transfer baselines (e.g., improvements over Täckström’s baselines; competitive or better than Nothman et al. without language-specific heuristics). On low-resource languages, the approach outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) while assuming fewer resources (no parallel data, no native-speaker interaction), notably on tr, tl, and bn.
- Clear evidence that wikifier features are language-agnostic and complementary. The “Wikifier only” rows, and the consistent gains when adding wikifier features on top of base+gazetteers (monolingual and cross-lingual), support the central claim. The analysis varying interlanguage link coverage (Table 3) is particularly informative: it ties performance to Wikipedia/FreeBase coverage and explains weaker gains for very small Wikipedias (e.g., yo).
- Practicality and scalability. The approach requires only Wikipedia dumps and a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai & Roth, 2016), with no reliance on POS taggers, parsers, or parallel data. Modifications to wikify all n-grams (n ≤ 4) make the feature extraction pipeline conceptually simple and widely applicable.
- Breadth of evaluation and analysis. Experiments cover 9 languages, multiple settings (monolingual, direct transfer, multi-source), and include ablations (e.g., removing FreeBase queries and interlanguage links). The observed trends (e.g., Latin vs. non-Latin scripts, dependence on Wikipedia size) are useful guidance for practitioners.
- Multi-source transfer results indicate generality. Although gains are modest, training on multiple source languages typically helps and rarely harms, which supports the claim that the features are shared and transferable.

Weaknesses
- Missing key baselines and combinations. The paper positions Täckström et al. and Nothman et al. as orthogonal, but does not present preliminary combined results (e.g., wikifier features + multilingual clusters; or + silver Wikipedia data). Similarly, there is no comparison with strong projection baselines, including approaches based on bilingual semantic spaces without sentence-aligned parallel data (e.g., Peirsman & Padó, 2009; Vulić & Moens, 2013). These omissions make it hard to fully contextualize the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
- Methodological clarity in Section 3.3. The description of modifications to Tsai & Roth (2016) for “wikify all n-grams” is terse and hard to follow. A step-by-step running example would clarify candidate generation, ranking, confidence features, and how overlapping n-grams/types are aggregated into token-level features. Given that this is the core novelty, the current exposition is insufficient.
- Limited assessment of POS-tag features. The paper states an earlier experiment suggested POS did not help, but this is not reported. Since Täckström et al. used POS (even gold in some settings), an empirical comparison with POS-tag features (universal taggers for as many languages as feasible) would strengthen the claims about delexicalized features and fairness of comparisons.
- Analysis gaps and statistical support. Some cross-language findings are surprising (e.g., Dutch best for Spanish; Spanish best for Yoruba). A deeper analysis (e.g., overlap in Wikipedia categories, topical/domain distributions, gazetteer coverage, annotation guidelines) would help interpret these results. Additionally, improvements in Table 4 (multi-source training) are small and sometimes inconsistent; statistical significance tests are needed to substantiate claims.
- Related work coverage and positioning. The discussion of Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and other Wikipedia-based NER systems is brief; clearer distinctions are needed (e.g., robustness to ambiguity vs. their mention-level categories, monolingual vs. cross-lingual scaling). The paper should also discuss projection without parallel sentences (bilingual semantic spaces) and where this method fits in that landscape.

Questions to authors
- POS features: Can you report experiments adding universal POS tags as features across languages (even if noisy) to directly compare with Täckström et al.? Do results change for Latin vs. non-Latin scripts?
- Combinations: Since you argue orthogonality, can you provide preliminary results combining your wikifier features with (a) multilingual word clusters (Täckström) and/or (b) silver training data from Wikipedia (Nothman et al.) on a subset of languages?
- Projection baselines: Can you include a strong projection-based baseline that does not require sentence-aligned data (e.g., bilingual semantic space methods such as Peirsman & Padó, 2009; Vulić & Moens, 2013) to better contextualize your approach?
- Section 3.3 clarity: Please add a running example showing candidate generation for overlapping n-grams, the ranking features used, how top-k candidates’ FreeBase types/categories are chosen, and how conflicts/overlaps are resolved at token level. How sensitive is performance to k?
- Feature ablations: What is the relative contribution of FreeBase types vs. Wikipedia categories vs. ranker confidence? In low-resource languages where FreeBase coverage is sparse, how much do categories alone contribute?
- Language-pair anomalies: Can you analyze why Dutch is best for Spanish and Spanish best for Yoruba (e.g., category overlap, domain, named-entity distributions, annotation scheme differences)?
- Significance and variance: Please provide significance tests (e.g., bootstrap) for key comparisons (Table 2, Table 4) and report variance over multiple runs.
- Practicality: What is the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per document? Are there heuristics to prune candidates without hurting performance?

Additional comments
- Please proofread thoroughly; there are several typos and awkward sentences, and Figure/Table references occasionally read ambiguously. Also fix the German sentence example and ensure consistency of tag mappings (e.g., MISC to LOC conversions) and their implications for evaluation.
