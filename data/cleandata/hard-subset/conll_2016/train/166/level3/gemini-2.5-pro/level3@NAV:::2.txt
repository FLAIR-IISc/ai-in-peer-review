This paper proposes a direct-transfer approach for cross-lingual NER, leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features by grounding n-grams to English Wikipedia. This allows an NER model trained on a high-resource language to be applied to target languages without parallel data. The main contribution is a strong empirical demonstration of this method’s effectiveness across nine languages, including several low-resource ones. While the technical contribution is incremental, the paper presents a coherent system with convincing results.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper’s key strength lies in its comprehensive experimental evaluation. By testing on both standard CoNLL datasets and genuinely low-resource languages (e.g., Yoruba, Tamil), the authors convincingly validate the robustness and broad applicability of their approach.
2. The proposed method is well-motivated and elegant, using Wikipedia as a language-agnostic knowledge source. Its minimal resource requirements—only a Wikipedia dump—give it significant practical value for languages where annotated or parallel data are unavailable.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The evaluation lacks crucial context. A comparison against a strong projection-based baseline is necessary to properly situate the performance of the wikification-based method within the field. The related work section is also insufficient, failing to clearly distinguish the work from other Wikipedia-based systems like Kazama & Torisawa (2007) or mention projection methods that do not require parallel data.
2. Several claims are unsubstantiated or underdeveloped. The small improvements from multi-source training in Table 4 require statistical significance tests to be convincing. The authors should also provide preliminary results to support their claim that the approach is orthogonal to methods like Tackström et al.’s. Furthermore, surprising findings, such as Dutch being the best training language for Spanish, are presented without any analysis.
3. The technical description is unclear in places. Section 3.3, detailing modifications to the wikifier, is difficult to follow and would benefit greatly from a running example to illustrate the process. Finally, the authors should perform a careful proofreading to correct several typos and awkward sentences.
