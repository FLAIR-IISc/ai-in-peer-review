### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a direct transfer method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The core idea is to leverage a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from a document in any language to corresponding English Wikipedia entries. The features derived from these entries—specifically, FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories—are inherently language-independent. The authors use these features to train a standard NER model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and apply it directly to low-resource target languages without any target-language-specific training data or tools. The method is evaluated on nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, German, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The results show significant improvements over comparable direct transfer baselines. The paper also demonstrates that these wikifier features can enhance monolingual NER models and that performance can be further improved by training on multiple source languages.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Effective Application of Wikification for Cross-Lingual NER Features:** The primary contribution is the successful and compelling demonstration that cross-lingual wikification can be repurposed to generate powerful, language-independent features for direct-transfer NER. While the constituent technologies (wikifiers, perceptron-based NER) are not novel, the paper presents a well-executed and coherent study showing this approach outperforms comparable methods. The accompanying analysis that correlates feature quality with Wikipedia size is a valuable finding for the community.
2.  **A Broad and Challenging Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of nine languages. This includes not only closely related European languages but also truly low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts. This extensive experimental setup serves as a strong benchmark and provides important insights into the practical strengths and limitations of direct transfer methods in varied linguistic settings.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents convincing results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The model significantly outperforms the direct transfer baseline from Täckström et al. (2012) on the CoNLL languages (e.g., +12 F1 on Dutch, +15 F1 on German over their baseline). More impressively, it also surpasses the resource-intensive method of Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages, despite requiring significantly fewer resources (no parallel text or native speaker interaction). These substantial gains highlight the practical utility of the proposed features.
2.  **Simple and Generalizable Method:** The core idea is elegant, intuitive, and highly generalizable. By grounding all languages in a common semantic space (English Wikipedia), the approach sidesteps the need for parallel corpora or language-specific resources like POS taggers. This makes the method easily applicable to any of the hundreds of languages present in Wikipedia, representing a significant advantage for low-resource NLP.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Feature Quality:** The paper includes a valuable analysis in Section 4.4 that connects the quality of the wikifier features to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. By simulating smaller Wikipedias through the removal of interlanguage links, the authors provide a clear and compelling explanation for the performance drop on languages like Yoruba, strengthening the paper's conclusions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison and Positioning with Related Work:** The paper’s positioning against prior work could be stronger. The comparison to Kazama and Torisawa (2007) is too brief, given its direct relevance of using Wikipedia categories for NER. A deeper discussion on the key differences (e.g., robustness of the wikifier, the cross-lingual setting) is needed. Furthermore, the related work section overlooks an entire class of relevant methods for projection that do not require parallel corpora, such as those using bilingual semantic spaces or cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., Peirsman & Pado, 2009; Vulić & Moens, 2013).
2.  **Missing Baselines and Ablations:** The experimental evaluation is missing some key comparisons that would better contextualize the results. The authors state they do not use POS tags (unlike Täckström et al.), but do not provide an experiment with universal POS tags to empirically justify this choice or to assess the relative contribution of their features against other common delexicalized features. Additionally, including a comparison to a strong projection-based baseline would provide a more complete picture of where this direct transfer approach stands.
3.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Deeper Analysis:** Certain sections of the paper are difficult to follow or lack sufficient analysis. Section 3.3, describing the modifications to the wikifier, is opaque and would benefit immensely from a concrete running example. The analysis of the language-transfer matrix in Figure 2 is also superficial; surprising results (e.g., Dutch being the best source for Spanish) are noted but not explained. Finally, the claims of improvement from multi-source training in Table 4 are based on small and inconsistent gains, which require statistical significance testing to be convincing.
4.  **Unsubstantiated Orthogonality Claims:** The authors claim their method is orthogonal to the bitext clustering of Täckström et al. (2012) and could be "used in tandem to get even better scores." This is a strong, speculative claim that should be supported by at least a preliminary experiment combining the approaches on one or two languages. Without such evidence, the claim of complementarity remains unproven.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding Section 3.3, could you provide a step-by-step example of how an n-gram like "in Germany" is processed by your modified wikifier, clarifying how candidates are generated and why it is not incorrectly linked to the "Germany" entity?
2.  The improvements from multi-source training in Table 4 are modest. Have you performed statistical significance tests to confirm that these gains are statistically significant?
3.  Could you offer any hypotheses to explain the more surprising cross-lingual transfer results in Figure 2, such as why Spanish is a better source for Yoruba than English, or why Dutch is best for Spanish? Is this due to lexical overlap, typological similarity, or an artifact of the training data or wikifier features?
4.  To substantiate your claim of orthogonality, would it be possible to provide results for a system combining your wikifier features with Täckström et al.'s bitext clusters on at least one CoNLL language?

### Additional Comments

The paper is generally well-written but would benefit from another round of proofreading to correct several typos and awkward phrasings. For example, in the abstract, "yielding, as we show, strong languageindependent features" is slightly clunky. In Section 3.3, the first sentence of the second bullet point appears to be grammatically incorrect.
