### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) based on a direct transfer approach. The central idea is to generate language-independent features by linking n-grams in a given text (regardless of language) to English Wikipedia entries using a cross-lingual wikifier. The corresponding FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories of the linked entries are then used as features in a standard NER model. The authors demonstrate that this approach is effective in two settings: 1) enhancing monolingual NER systems by adding these wikifier features, and 2) enabling cross-lingual NER by training a model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applying it directly to a low-resource language. The method is evaluated on nine languages, including standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel language-independent feature representation for NER derived from cross-lingual wikification.** The key insight is to use entity linking as a pre-processing step to generate rich, delexicalized features for the NER task, effectively inverting the traditional NER-then-linking pipeline. This provides a powerful way to transfer knowledge from a central knowledge base (English Wikipedia) to any language covered by the wikifier.
2.  **A comprehensive and rigorous empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages.** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the method's effectiveness on high-resource (German, Spanish, Dutch) and low-resource (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) languages. The inclusion of non-Latin script languages is particularly valuable and highlights the robustness of the approach.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's performance in relation to knowledge base size.** The authors clearly demonstrate that the quality of the wikifier features, and thus the overall NER performance in low-resource settings, is directly correlated with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. This analysis is crucial for understanding the applicability and limitations of this type of approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Effectiveness of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and effective use of cross-lingual wikification for NER. By grounding phrases to a language-agnostic knowledge base, the method creates powerful semantic features that are not available to models relying solely on lexical or distributional signals. The results in Table 2 provide compelling evidence of its impact. For instance, in the direct transfer setting, adding wikifier features boosts F1 scores dramatically from 49.66 to 62.10 on Dutch and from 55.04 to 60.97 on German, demonstrating that these features capture a signal that is crucial for cross-lingual transfer.
2.  **Extensive and Thorough Experimentation:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors test their model on nine different languages, covering a wide range of language families, resource levels, and writing systems. They compare their results to strong and relevant baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) and show superior performance, often with fewer resource requirements (e.g., no need for parallel corpora or native speaker input). The inclusion of both monolingual and cross-lingual experiments (including multi-source training) provides a complete picture of the features' utility.
3.  **Valuable Analysis of Low-Resource Scenarios:** The paper presents a thoughtful analysis of the method's limitations in Section 4.4. The experiment that simulates a smaller knowledge base for Spanish and German by removing inter-language links convincingly shows that performance degradation on languages like Yoruba is tied to the limited size of its Wikipedia. This honest and insightful analysis is a significant contribution in itself and offers valuable lessons for the community working on low-resource NLP.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Heavy Reliance on the Wikifier Without Error Analysis:** The model's performance is critically dependent on the upstream cross-lingual wikifier, yet there is no analysis of how errors from this component propagate to the final NER predictions. For example, if the wikifier incorrectly links a common noun phrase to a Wikipedia page, it will likely introduce noisy features that could lead to a false positive NER tag. A qualitative or quantitative error analysis would be highly beneficial to understand the failure modes and the robustness of the feature extraction process.
2.  **Unclear Handling of Entities Not in Wikipedia:** The paper does not address how the system performs on named entities that do not have a corresponding English Wikipedia entry. For these "out-of-knowledge-base" entities, the wikifier would presumably fail to produce a link, meaning the model would receive no wikifier features for them. This is a potentially significant limitation, as real-world text often contains emerging or less prominent entities. An analysis of the model's recall on this subset of entities would be necessary to assess its real-world applicability.
3.  **Ad-hoc Strategy for Non-Latin Script Languages:** The authors correctly identify that English-trained lexical features hurt performance when transferred to non-Latin script languages like Bengali and Tamil. Their solution is to manually exclude these features for those specific languages. This approach is brittle and not generalizable. A more principled solution would be to explore methods that can automatically learn the importance of different feature groups, such as using group-specific regularization (e.g., group lasso). This would allow the model to down-weight unhelpful feature classes without requiring language-specific manual intervention.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide an error analysis that clarifies the relationship between wikifier errors (e.g., wrong entity link, or failure to link an in-KB entity) and the final NER errors? What are the most common failure cases?
2.  How does the model perform on entities that are not present in Wikipedia? Is the model able to identify them using the base and gazetteer features, or does the lack of wikifier features significantly harm recall for this category of entities?
3.  Instead of manually removing lexical features for non-Latin script languages, did you consider using regularization techniques that could automatically learn to ignore or down-weight these features during training, thus creating a single, more robust model for all transfer scenarios?
