### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource source language and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in the target. The core idea is to leverage a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams from any language text into English Wikipedia entries. The corresponding FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories of the linked entries serve as powerful, language-independent features. The authors demonstrate this method by training a perceptron-based NER model on English and evaluating it on the three CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). They show that these features significantly improve performance in the direct transfer setting, can also enhance monolingual NER systems, and allow for effective multi-source training.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and broadly applicable method for generating language-independent features for NER.** The paper presents a well-motivated approach that repurposes cross-lingual wikification, a task typically downstream of NER, as a source of powerful features for NER itself. This method's sole reliance on Wikipedia makes it scalable to a large number of languages.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating strong performance across diverse languages.** The experiments are extensive, covering nine languages with varying resource levels and script types. The proposed model is shown to substantially outperform a strong direct-transfer baseline and a competitive weakly-supervised system, providing convincing evidence of its effectiveness.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the relationship between feature quality and Wikipedia size.** The authors conduct ablation studies that clearly link the performance of the wikifier features to the size of a language's Wikipedia and its intersection with English Wikipedia, offering a valuable diagnostic for predicting the method's success on new languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The central idea of grounding phrases to a language-independent knowledge base (English Wikipedia) to extract semantic features is simple, intuitive, and highly effective. It cleverly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora, which are central to many cross-lingual transfer techniques, making the approach more widely applicable, especially for truly low-resource languages.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results within the direct transfer paradigm. On the CoNLL languages, it significantly outperforms the baseline and the more complex clustering-based model of Täckström et al. (2012). More impressively, on five low-resource languages, it surpasses a system by Zhang et al. (2016) that requires parallel data and human interaction. The fact that the features also provide consistent gains in monolingual settings further underscores their robustness and utility.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The evaluation across nine languages, including those with non-Latin scripts, is a major strength. The paper does not just report scores but also provides a thoughtful analysis in Section 4.4 that investigates *why* performance varies across languages. By simulating low-resource conditions for high-resource languages, the authors compellingly demonstrate that the quality of the wikifier features is directly tied to the availability of inter-language links, which is crucial for understanding the method's limitations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Alternative Cross-Lingual Paradigms:** The paper frames its contributions entirely within the "direct transfer" setting and omits comparison to the other dominant paradigm for this task: annotation projection. A comparison to even a standard projection-based baseline would be essential to contextualize the method's performance and highlight the relative strengths and weaknesses of the two approaches (e.g., direct transfer's simplicity vs. projection's potential accuracy with good parallel data). The related work also misses projection methods that do not require parallel corpora (e.g., using bilingual semantic spaces).
2.  **Unsubstantiated Claims and Missing Experiments:**
    *   The authors claim their approach is orthogonal to methods like Täckström et al.'s clusters and Nothman et al.'s silver-standard data generation. While plausible, this claim is not supported by experiments. A preliminary experiment combining wikifier features with cross-lingual clusters on one or two languages would significantly strengthen this argument and the paper's contribution.
    *   The decision to exclude POS tags is justified by reference to an "earlier experiment" without presenting any data. Given that strong baselines rely on them, a more formal ablation study is needed to empirically justify their exclusion across different languages.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Clarity:**
    *   The description of the modified wikifier in Section 3.3 is too brief and difficult to follow. Crucial details about how candidate generation and ranking are adapted for "all n-grams" are missing, hindering reproducibility. A running example would be very beneficial here.
    *   The paper presents intriguing results in Figure 2 (e.g., Dutch being the best source for Spanish, Spanish for Yoruba) but provides no analysis or hypotheses for these non-obvious findings. This is a missed opportunity for deeper insight.
    *   The gains from multi-source training reported in Table 4 are marginal and inconsistent. Without statistical significance testing, the claim that this technique "further improves the results" is not convincingly supported.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the modifications made to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier for grounding all n-grams? Specifically, how does the candidate generation and ranking process work in this setting? A concise example would be very helpful.
2.  You argue that your wikifier features are orthogonal to other delexicalized features like cross-lingual word clusters. To substantiate this, could you provide results from a preliminary experiment that combines your features with the bitext clusters from Täckström et al. (2012) for one or two of the CoNLL languages?
3.  Figure 2 shows that English is not always the optimal source language for transfer (e.g., Dutch for Spanish, Spanish for Yoruba). Do you have any linguistic or data-driven hypotheses that might explain these specific cross-lingual synergies, beyond general observations about Wikipedia or training set size?
4.  The F1 improvements from multi-source training in Table 4 are quite small (many are <1 F1 point). Have you conducted statistical significance tests to verify that these gains are not due to random variation?
