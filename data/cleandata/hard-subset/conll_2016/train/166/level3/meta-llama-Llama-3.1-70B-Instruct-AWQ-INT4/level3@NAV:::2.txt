**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a language-independent model for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) using a cross-lingual wikifier. The model is trained on annotated documents in one or multiple source languages and can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia. The authors use a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate every n-gram in the document, which provides language-independent features for transferring an NER model directly. The model is evaluated on a wide range of languages, including low-resource languages, and shows significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Language-independent features for cross-lingual NER**: The authors propose a novel approach to generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using a cross-lingual wikifier.
2. **Cross-lingual wikifier for NER**: The authors use a cross-lingual wikifier to disambiguate every n-gram in the document, which provides useful information for NER.
3. **Evaluation on low-resource languages**: The authors evaluate their model on a wide range of languages, including low-resource languages, and show significant improvements over strong baselines.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to cross-lingual NER**: The authors propose a novel approach to generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using a cross-lingual wikifier.
2. **Strong performance on low-resource languages**: The authors show significant improvements over strong baselines on low-resource languages.
3. **Robustness to language variation**: The authors demonstrate that their model is robust to language variation, including differences in script and lexical features.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparison to related work**: The authors do not provide a thorough comparison to related work, including Kazama and Torisawa (2007) and Nothman et al. (2012).
2. **Lack of analysis on surprising language-pair results**: The authors do not provide a thorough analysis on surprising language-pair results, such as Dutch being the best for Spanish and Spanish being the best for Yoruba.
3. **Small improvements in some experiments**: The authors report small improvements in some experiments, such as the addition of Spanish training data yielding the best score on Yoruba.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the limited comparison to related work in future research?
2. Can you provide a more thorough analysis on surprising language-pair results, such as Dutch being the best for Spanish and Spanish being the best for Yoruba?
3. How do you plan to improve the performance of your model on low-resource languages, such as Yoruba and Bengali?
