- Proposes a language-independent cross-lingual NER model that uses a wikifier to disambiguate every n-gram and can be applied to all Wikipedia languages.  
- Trains on annotated documents in one or multiple source languages and evaluates across many target languages, including low-resource ones, showing notable gains.  

Contributions:
- Introduces cross-lingual wikification to ground non-English text to English Wikipedia as features for direct-transfer NER.  
- Demonstrates that Wikipedia category and Freebase typing information provide useful language-independent NER features.  
- Evaluates the approach on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) with improved results over prior methods.  

Strengths:
- Effectively leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features for NER.  
- Shows improved performance on several low-resource languages compared to existing baselines.  
- Model can be trained on multiple source languages, offering flexibility and possible performance gains.  

Weaknesses:
- Approach is incremental and builds on prior work, lacking strong technical novelty.  
- Comparison to related work is insufficient; key prior studies (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa 2007, Nothman et al. 2012) are not thoroughly addressed.  
- Explanation of the edited model from Tsai and Roth (2016) is unclear and needs a running example.  
- Reported improvements (e.g., Table 4) are small/inconsistent and lack statistical significance testing.  

Questions to authors:
- Please expand comparisons to related work such as Kazama & Torisawa (2007) and Nothman et al. (2012).  
- Provide a clearer, example-driven explanation of the edited Tsai and Roth (2016) model.  
- Include statistical significance tests to support claims in Table 4.  
- Discuss limitations of the approach and plans to address them in future work.  

Additional comments:
- Paper contains typos and awkward sentences; careful proofreading is needed.  
- Consider integrating other multilingual NER techniques (e.g., parallel projection, automatically generated Wikipedia training data).
