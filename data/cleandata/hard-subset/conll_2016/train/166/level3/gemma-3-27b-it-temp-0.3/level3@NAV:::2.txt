## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper explores cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in all languages to English Wikipedia – is sound and the experimental setup is reasonably comprehensive, covering both CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages. The paper demonstrates that this approach can effectively transfer knowledge from English to other languages, outperforming existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The contribution lies primarily in the systematic application and evaluation of cross-lingual wikification features for NER, rather than a fundamentally novel algorithmic approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich and low-resource settings. This breadth of evaluation is a significant strength, demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed approach.
2. **Practical Approach:** The method relies solely on Wikipedia, a readily available resource, making it highly practical and accessible for languages with limited annotated data. This is a key advantage over methods requiring parallel corpora or extensive language-specific rules.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on performance (Table 3) provides valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and highlights the importance of resource availability. The exploration of multi-lingual training (Section 4.5) is also a positive contribution.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The results consistently outperform existing direct transfer methods and are competitive with more complex approaches, especially considering the simplicity of the proposed features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the application of cross-lingual wikification to NER is well-executed, the core idea builds upon existing work in both wikification and direct transfer. The novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section lacks a detailed comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007), as noted by the supervisor. A clearer distinction from other Wikipedia-based NER systems (beyond simply mentioning them) is also needed.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Table 4 shows small and inconsistent improvements. Reporting statistical significance tests is crucial to substantiate the claims of improvement and determine whether the observed gains are statistically meaningful.
4. **Unclear Model Details:** Section 3.3, describing the modified Tsai and Roth model, is unclear and would benefit significantly from a running example to illustrate the modifications made.
5. **Missing Combined Model Results:** The claim of orthogonality between the proposed approach and methods like Tackström et al. and Nothman et al. requires empirical support. Preliminary results combining these models would strengthen this claim.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a running example illustrating the modifications made to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier, as described in Section 3.3?
2.  Please provide statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 4.
3.  Have you explored combining your approach with methods like Tackström et al. (2012) or Nothman et al. (2012)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4.  Can you elaborate on the surprising language-pair results (e.g., Dutch best for Spanish, Spanish best for Yoruba) with a more detailed analysis of potential linguistic or cultural factors?




**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains several typos and awkward sentences that require careful proofreading. For example, the citation of “Tackström et al.” is inconsistent throughout the paper.
