## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in any language to English Wikipedia – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The thorough experimental evaluation, spanning multiple languages including several non-Latin script languages, is a significant strength. The authors clearly demonstrate performance gains over existing methods in both direct transfer and monolingual settings, and the analysis of Wikipedia size’s impact on feature quality is insightful. Overall, this is a strong submission with clear contributions.

The primary contribution lies in the successful application of cross-lingual wikification as a feature engineering technique for NER. While wikification has been used in NLP previously, this work uniquely utilizes it to create truly language-independent features, enabling effective transfer learning. A second key contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation. The experiments are well-designed, covering a diverse set of languages and experimental setups, providing robust evidence for the method’s effectiveness. Finally, the analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on performance provides valuable insights into the limitations and potential improvements of the approach.

Several aspects of the paper are particularly commendable. The inclusion of low-resource and non-Latin script languages is crucial, as these are often neglected in cross-lingual NLP research. The ablation studies, such as removing FreeBase information, are helpful in understanding the contribution of different components. The comparison to existing baselines, including Täckström et al. and Zhang et al., provides a solid benchmark for the proposed method.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. The reliance on the cross-lingual wikifier is a potential bottleneck. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed analysis of Wikifier errors and their impact on NER predictions would be beneficial. Specifically, how often do Wikifier errors *directly* lead to incorrect NER predictions? Additionally, the performance on Tamil and Bengali is relatively low, and the authors suggest that lexical features hinder performance in these languages. Exploring alternative regularization strategies, as suggested in the guidelines, to prevent over-reliance on lexical features would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of Wikifier errors? Specifically, what types of errors are most common, and how frequently do these errors lead to incorrect NER predictions?
2.  Have you explored alternative regularization techniques to mitigate the negative impact of lexical features on languages like Tamil and Bengali? If so, what were the results?
3.  The paper mentions that the model can be trained on multiple source languages. Could you elaborate on the selection criteria for choosing which source languages to include in the training data?
