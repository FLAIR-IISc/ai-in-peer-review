## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) by leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in any language to English Wikipedia and utilizing the resulting categories and Freebase types – is innovative and demonstrably effective. The experimental setup is thorough, evaluating performance across a diverse set of languages, including several low-resource and non-Latin script languages. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, method, and results, making the paper easy to follow.

The primary contribution lies in the successful application of cross-lingual wikification features for direct transfer NER, achieving strong results, particularly in low-resource scenarios where traditional methods struggle. A second significant contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation, showcasing the effectiveness of the approach across a broad linguistic spectrum and providing valuable insights into the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality. Finally, the demonstration that these features also enhance monolingual NER systems is a noteworthy benefit, broadening the applicability of the proposed method.

The strengths of this submission are numerous. The thorough experimentation, including ablation studies and comparisons to strong baselines (including Täckström et al. and Zhang et al.), lends credibility to the findings. The analysis of low-resource languages and the impact of Wikipedia size are particularly valuable. The clear presentation and organization of the paper contribute to its readability and impact.

However, some weaknesses warrant consideration. While the authors acknowledge the dependence on the cross-lingual Wikifier, the extent to which Wikifier errors propagate to NER prediction errors remains unclear.  How robust is the method to incorrect or ambiguous wikifications? What percentage of errors are attributable to the Wikifier?  Furthermore, the performance on languages like Tamil and Bengali suffers when lexical features are added, suggesting an over-reliance on these features. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of Wikifier errors and their impact on NER performance? Specifically, what is the error rate of the Wikifier on your test sets, and how does this correlate with NER prediction errors?
2.  The paper notes that adding lexical features hurts performance on some languages. Could you explore regularization techniques or feature weighting strategies to mitigate this effect and prevent over-reliance on lexical features?
3.  For entities *not* present on Wikipedia, how does the method perform? Does the absence of a Wikipedia entry inherently limit the ability to identify such entities?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be interesting to see a qualitative error analysis to better understand the types of errors the model makes and how they relate to the limitations of the wikification process.
*   The choice of using English Wikipedia as the central grounding point is reasonable, but a brief discussion of the potential biases introduced by this choice might be beneficial.
