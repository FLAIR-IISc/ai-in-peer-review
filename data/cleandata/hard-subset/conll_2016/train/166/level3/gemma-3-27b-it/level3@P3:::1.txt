## Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

This paper presents a compelling approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in all languages to English Wikipedia – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective, particularly for low-resource languages. The thorough experimental evaluation, covering a diverse set of languages including several non-Latin script languages, is a significant strength. Overall, this is a strong paper with clear contributions, and I recommend acceptance.

**Summary:**

The paper addresses the challenge of NER in languages with limited annotated data by proposing a direct transfer method. The method utilizes a cross-lingual wikifier to map phrases in any language to English Wikipedia articles, extracting features from associated Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. This allows the training of an NER model on resource-rich languages (primarily English) and its application to others, significantly improving performance compared to existing direct transfer approaches, and even enhancing monolingual performance. The authors demonstrate improvements across CoNLL datasets and five low-resource languages.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Cross-Lingual Feature Representation:** The most significant contribution is the innovative use of cross-lingual wikification to create language-independent features for NER. Mapping mentions to English Wikipedia allows the extraction of rich semantic information (Wikipedia categories, FreeBase types) regardless of the source language.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse range of languages, including low-resource and non-Latin script languages. This robust evaluation, with detailed analysis, clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison against existing methods, including strong baselines, is well-executed.
3. **Demonstrated Effectiveness in Low-Resource Settings:** The method exhibits particularly strong performance on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil), surpassing existing state-of-the-art results for these languages, and highlighting the practicality of the approach in data-scarce scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Robust Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and well-designed. The inclusion of both CoNLL languages and a set of low-resource languages provides a valuable and balanced assessment of the method’s capabilities. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries) further strengthen the analysis.
2. **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes insightful analyses, such as the investigation into the impact of Wikipedia size on performance. This analysis adds depth to the understanding of the method’s limitations and potential areas for improvement. The exploration of multi-language training is also a valuable addition.
3. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The method is clearly explained, and the experimental results are presented in a straightforward manner.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The approach addresses a critical challenge in NLP – adapting NER models to languages with limited resources. The reliance on readily available Wikipedia data makes the method highly practical and accessible.
5. **Strong Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison to baselines, including those requiring parallel data or native speaker interaction (Zhang et al., 2016), convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Error Dependence:** The method’s performance is intrinsically linked to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. The paper does not adequately address the potential impact of incorrect wikifications on NER prediction errors. How frequently do Wikifier errors *directly* lead to incorrect entity classifications, and can this effect be mitigated? A more detailed error analysis, quantifying the effect of false positives and false negatives from the wikifier, would be valuable.
2. **Handling of Non-Wikipedia Entities:** The approach inherently struggles with entities not present in Wikipedia. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't fully explore strategies to address it. Further discussion on how the model performs on OOV entities, and potential solutions like incorporating character-level features or external knowledge bases, would be beneficial.
3. **Over-reliance on Lexical Features:** As highlighted by the results on Tamil and Bengali, the method can become overly reliant on lexical features when direct transfer is attempted. The suggestion to regularize feature classes differently, perhaps down-weighting lexical features, is a valid and important observation. The poor performance when adding lexical features to the Bengali and Tamil models clearly indicates a need for more careful feature weighting.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the wikifier and their impact on the NER performance? Specifically, what percentage of NER errors can be attributed to incorrect wikifications?
2.  How does the method perform on entities that are not present in Wikipedia? Do you have any preliminary results or ideas on how to address this limitation?
3.  Have you experimented with different regularization strategies for the feature classes, specifically down-weighting lexical features, particularly in the context of low-resource, non-Latin script languages like Tamil and Bengali? If so, what were the results?
