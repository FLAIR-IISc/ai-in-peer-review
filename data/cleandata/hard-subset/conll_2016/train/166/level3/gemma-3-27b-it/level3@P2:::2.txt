## Review for CONLL 2016 Paper: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

This paper presents a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in multiple languages to English Wikipedia to extract useful features – is a reasonable and well-executed approach. While the technical novelty is incremental, the paper’s strength lies in a coherent story supported by convincing experiments across a diverse set of languages, including several low-resource scenarios. The contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) a demonstration of the effectiveness of cross-lingual wikification features for direct-transfer NER, achieving strong results on both CoNLL languages and low-resource languages; (2) a comprehensive empirical evaluation across nine languages, showcasing the adaptability of the proposed method; and (3) evidence that these language-independent features can also benefit monolingual NER systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms comparable approaches on Spanish, German, and Dutch CoNLL datasets, and importantly, demonstrates significant gains on low-resource languages like Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. This is a substantial achievement, particularly given the challenges inherent in low-resource NER. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016), which requires native speaker interaction, is particularly compelling.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation spans nine languages, providing a robust assessment of the method’s generalizability. The inclusion of languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) is a valuable addition and highlights the potential of the approach to handle diverse linguistic structures. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase links in Table 3) are well-designed and provide insights into feature importance.
3. **Practical Applicability:** The system’s reliance only on multilingual Wikipedia makes it easily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring parallel corpora or extensive language-specific resources. This broad applicability is a significant practical advantage.
4. **Demonstrated Feature Utility:** The experiments clearly demonstrate that the wikification-derived features are informative, even in isolation (though less so than combined with other features), and are beneficial for both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual NER. The analysis relating Wikipedia size to feature quality (Table 3) is insightful.
5. **Multi-Source Training:** Showing the benefits of training on multiple source languages is a valuable addition, providing a pathway to further improvement and leveraging available data more effectively.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the approach is effective, the core idea of using Wikipedia for cross-lingual transfer isn’t entirely new. The paper could benefit from a deeper comparison to prior work, specifically Kazama & Torisawa (2007). The distinctions from other Wikipedia-based NER systems (e.g., Nothman et al., 2012) should be more clearly articulated.
2. **Incomplete Comparison to Related Work:**  The related work section mentions projection methods, but doesn’t adequately contextualize them within the landscape of cross-lingual NER. It would be beneficial to include methods that perform projection *without* parallel data (e.g., Peirsman & Pado 2009; Vulić & Moens 2013) and position these approaches relative to the wikification-based method.
3. **Unclear Implementation Details:** Section 3.3, describing the modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier, lacks clarity. A running example illustrating the changes would greatly improve understanding.
4. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Table 4 shows relatively small improvements when training on multiple languages. Without statistical significance tests, it’s difficult to determine whether these improvements are meaningful or due to random chance.
5. **Unexplained Language-Pair Results:** The results in Figure 2 are surprising in some cases (e.g., Dutch best for Spanish, Spanish best for Yoruba). A brief discussion analyzing these counterintuitive findings would strengthen the paper.
6. **Missing POS Tag Experiments:** The paper mentions not using POS tags due to difficulties obtaining them for all languages, but doesn't present any experiments *specifically* to evaluate their impact, contrasting with work like Tackström et al. (2012). This leaves a potential avenue for improvement unexplored.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a running example illustrating how the modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier (Section 3.3) affect the disambiguation process and feature generation?
2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 4 to demonstrate whether the improvements from multi-source training are statistically significant?
3. What factors might explain the surprising language-pair relationships observed in Figure 2 (e.g., Dutch being the best training language for Spanish)?
4. Have you experimented with combining your approach with a strong projection baseline? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains several typos and awkward sentence constructions. A careful proofread is recommended. The cited examples (e.g., Figure 1) could be more clearly presented. Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as potential biases in Wikipedia or challenges with highly ambiguous mentions.
