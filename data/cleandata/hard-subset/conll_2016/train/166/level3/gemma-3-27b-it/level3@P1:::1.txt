## Review of Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

This paper presents a novel approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in any language to English Wikipedia – is insightful and effectively utilized. The experimental setup is comprehensive, demonstrating improvements over strong baselines in both monolingual and cross-lingual transfer scenarios, including a valuable analysis of performance on low-resource and non-Latin script languages. Overall, this is a strong submission with clear contributions and a well-executed evaluation.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of language-independent features derived from cross-lingual wikification for NER. While wikification has been traditionally considered downstream of NER, this paper successfully inverts that paradigm, showing how disambiguation to Wikipedia entries provides valuable signals *for* NER. A second noteworthy contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich CoNLL languages and low-resource languages with varying script types. This detailed analysis provides important insights into the effectiveness of the approach under different linguistic conditions and demonstrates the robustness of the proposed features. Finally, the paper demonstrates that these language-independent features are not only useful for direct transfer learning but can also enhance the performance of monolingual NER systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Effective Approach:** The core idea of using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features is novel and surprisingly effective. The paper convincingly demonstrates that grounding mentions in English Wikipedia, even with imperfect disambiguation, provides valuable information for NER, circumventing the need for large amounts of annotated data in the target language.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a significant strength. The authors evaluate their model on a diverse range of languages, including Spanish, German, Dutch (CoNLL datasets) and Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil (low-resource languages). This broad coverage allows for a robust assessment of the method’s generalizability. The inclusion of languages with non-Latin scripts is particularly commendable and highlights the adaptability of the approach.

3. **Detailed Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting results. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality (demonstrated in Table 3) and explore the benefits of using multiple source languages for training (shown in Figure 2). This in-depth analysis provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of the method and helps to understand its strengths and limitations.

4. **Strong Baselines and Comparisons:** The authors compare their approach to relevant baselines, including direct transfer methods from Täckström et al. (2012) and the expectation learning model from Zhang et al. (2016). The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, especially on low-resource languages where it outperforms approaches requiring parallel data and native speaker interaction.

5. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method relies solely on multilingual Wikipedia, a readily available resource. This makes the approach highly practical and scalable to a large number of languages. The authors explicitly address the computational efficiency of their system and demonstrate its applicability to diverse linguistic settings.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on Wikifier Quality:**  A primary concern is the dependence on the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. The paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation (Figure 1), but does not thoroughly investigate the impact of wikifier errors on NER performance. How frequently do mis-linked entities lead to incorrect NER predictions? A more detailed error analysis, perhaps with manual inspection of misclassifications, would strengthen the paper.

2. **Handling of Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The approach relies on grounding mentions in English Wikipedia. What happens to entities that are *not* present in Wikipedia? While Freebase types are used, their coverage may be limited.  The paper could benefit from a discussion of strategies for handling such entities, or an analysis of their frequency and impact on performance.

3. **Lexical Feature Dominance:** The experiments reveal a somewhat counterintuitive result: adding lexical features degrades performance on low-resource languages (Bengali and Tamil). This suggests that the model may be overly reliant on lexical features, which are uninformative when transferring from English to languages with significantly different writing systems or vocabularies.  Consider exploring techniques like feature regularization or weighting to prevent the model from over-emphasizing lexical information in the cross-lingual setting.  Perhaps penalizing the lexical feature weights could yield improved results.

4. **Limited Scope of Wikipedia Features:** While the authors utilize Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, the paper acknowledges the potential for leveraging other Wikipedia information (e.g., document context, relations between titles). Exploring these avenues could further enhance the quality of the language-independent features.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis to quantify the impact of wikifier errors on NER performance? Specifically, what percentage of NER errors can be attributed to incorrect wikification?
2. How does the model handle entities that are not present in English Wikipedia? What is the frequency of such entities in the test sets, and how does their presence affect overall performance?
3. Have you experimented with feature regularization or weighting to mitigate the dominance of lexical features, particularly in low-resource language scenarios? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

- The writing is clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized.
- Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the wikification process and the associated features.
- The discussion of related work is comprehensive and contextualizes the contribution of the paper effectively.
- Consider adding a table summarizing the main experimental results for quick reference.
