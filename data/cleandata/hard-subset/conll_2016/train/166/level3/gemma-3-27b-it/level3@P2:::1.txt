## Review for CONLL 2016 - Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in non-English text into English Wikipedia entries to obtain category and Freebase type information – is insightful and demonstrably effective. The experimental evaluation is extensive, covering both CoNLL languages and a diverse set of low-resource languages, and includes a thoughtful analysis of feature contributions. Overall, this is a strong submission with clear contributions to the field.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper addresses the challenge of NER in languages with limited annotated data by proposing a direct transfer method. Rather than relying on parallel corpora or projecting annotations, the authors utilize a cross-lingual wikifier to map mentions to English Wikipedia entries. The resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types serve as language-independent features for an NER model trained primarily on English data. The approach is evaluated on standard CoNLL datasets and five low-resource languages, consistently demonstrating improvements over comparable methods, including state-of-the-art systems requiring more substantial resources. The authors also show the benefits of these features for enhancing monolingual NER systems and utilizing multiple source languages for training.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A novel approach to cross-lingual NER based on cross-lingual wikification:** This is the primary contribution. By framing NER as a grounding problem – linking mentions to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia) – the authors bypass the need for language-specific features and direct annotation transfer. This is a significant conceptual shift.
2. **Demonstration of the effectiveness of Wikipedia-derived features:** The extensive experiments convincingly demonstrate that features derived from English Wikipedia, obtained via cross-lingual wikification, are surprisingly effective for NER in a variety of languages, including low-resource settings.  The analysis showing feature importance is particularly valuable.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation and analysis:** The paper’s thorough experimental setup, including multiple languages, ablations, and analysis of Wikipedia size impact, strengthens the validity and impact of the findings. The exploration of multi-source training is also a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The idea of using cross-lingual wikification as a foundational step for cross-lingual NER is innovative. The paper correctly identifies the potential of Wikipedia as a source of language-independent knowledge and successfully harnesses it.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing methods on a diverse set of languages, including challenging low-resource scenarios. The performance gains, particularly in the low-resource settings, are impressive.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental design is robust and well-considered. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contributions of different feature sets. The investigation into the impact of Wikipedia size provides valuable insights into the limitations and dependencies of the approach.
4. **Practical Applicability:** The method relies only on a multilingual Wikipedia dump, making it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without requiring expensive parallel resources or native speaker involvement. This accessibility is a key strength.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the key results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Dependency & Error Propagation:** The performance of the proposed system is inherently tied to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. The paper doesn’t fully address the potential impact of wikifier errors on NER performance. How often do incorrect or ambiguous wikifications lead to incorrect NER predictions? A more detailed analysis of these error cases would be beneficial.
2. **Handling of Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** The method relies on grounding mentions in English Wikipedia. What happens when a mention refers to an entity that is not present in Wikipedia? The paper briefly mentions this but lacks a detailed discussion of how the model handles such cases and the resulting impact on performance.  A more in-depth analysis of these "NIL" cases is needed.
3. **Feature Regularization:** The experiments show that incorporating lexical features can sometimes *decrease* performance, especially in low-resource languages. This suggests that the model may be over-relying on these features, particularly when the lexical overlap between English and the target language is limited. Exploring different regularization techniques to prevent this over-reliance could improve performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the wikifier and how these errors impact the NER performance? Specifically, can you quantify the correlation between wikifier errors and NER prediction errors?
2. How does the model handle mentions that cannot be linked to an English Wikipedia entry (NIL cases)? Are there any strategies employed to mitigate the negative impact of these cases?
3. Have you experimented with different regularization techniques to control the weight given to lexical features, particularly in the low-resource scenarios?

**Additional Comments:**

- It would be helpful to include a discussion of the computational cost of the wikification step.
-  The paper mentions using FreeBase types. Is there any consideration given to the evolving nature of knowledge bases like FreeBase, and the potential for inconsistencies or outdated information?
