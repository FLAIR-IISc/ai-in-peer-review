## Review of “Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification”

This paper explores cross-lingual NER through a wikification-based approach, leveraging the rich information contained within Wikipedia to build language-independent features. The core idea – grounding mentions in multiple languages to a common English Wikipedia space – is sound and intuitively appealing. While the technical novelty is incremental, the paper presents a coherent story, thorough experimentation across a diverse set of languages (including low-resource ones), and demonstrates competitive performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation of Wikification for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper provides a systematic and broad evaluation of using cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features for NER, spanning both CoNLL languages and a significant set of low-resource languages. This evaluation, including ablation studies and varied training configurations, is the most substantial contribution.
2. **Demonstration of Cross-Lingual Transfer Effectiveness:** The work convincingly shows that a model trained on English can achieve strong performance on multiple target languages via wikification, often outperforming existing methods particularly in low-resource scenarios.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Wikipedia Feature Quality:** The investigation into the impact of Wikipedia size and the role of Freebase links provides valuable insights into the factors affecting the effectiveness of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Broad Language Coverage:** The inclusion of both standard CoNLL languages *and* a diverse range of low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) is a significant strength. This demonstrates the potential of the method in realistic, challenging scenarios.
2. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a range of experiments, including monolingual improvements, transfer learning with various source languages, and ablation studies to assess the contribution of different feature sets. The consistent experimental setup strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the work, the method, and the experimental setup. The example in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the grounding process.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive, and often superior, performance compared to established baselines, particularly in low-resource settings where data scarcity is a major challenge. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) is particularly compelling given their strong baseline using extensive resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core method builds on existing techniques (cross-lingual wikification, feature-based NER). The primary contribution lies in the application and extensive evaluation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic development.
2. **Insufficient Related Work Comparison:** The discussion of related work lacks depth. The paper mentions Kazama & Torisawa (2007) but doesn’t adequately explain the differences beyond stating their method is not “robust to ambiguity.” A more detailed comparison of their feature engineering, wikification strategies, and experimental setups is needed.  Similarly, the distinctions from other Wikipedia-based NER systems (e.g., Nothman et al., Kim et al.) aren’t sufficiently clear.  A table summarizing the key differences would be helpful.
3. **Missing POS Tag Experiments:** The authors acknowledge the potential benefits of POS tag features (citing Tackström et al.) but dismiss them due to difficulties obtaining universal POS tags. This is a significant omission. It would be valuable to conduct experiments with POS tags *even if* a less-than-ideal tagger is used, to empirically assess their impact across languages and justify the decision to exclude them.
4. **Unclear Model Description (Section 3.3):** The description of the modified Tsai and Roth (2016) system is vague. A running example, illustrating how the candidate generation and ranking processes are modified for n-grams (n>1), would significantly improve clarity.
5. **Lack of Combined Model Results:** The paper claims that the approaches from Tackström et al. and Nothman et al. are orthogonal. However, no preliminary results demonstrating the benefit of combining these approaches are presented. Including even limited experiments combining these models would lend support to this claim.
6. **Inadequate Statistical Significance Testing:** Table 4 shows small and inconsistent improvements. Without statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests), it’s difficult to determine if these improvements are statistically meaningful or simply due to chance.
7. **Missing Discussion of Non-Parallel Projection Methods:** The related work section focuses heavily on parallel corpus projection. The paper should acknowledge and discuss methods for projection without parallel data, such as Peirsman & Pado (2009) and Vulić & Moens (2013) which utilize bilingual semantic spaces, and position this work within that landscape.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison to Kazama & Torisawa (2007), specifically outlining the differences in their wikification process, feature engineering, and experimental setup?
2. Can you provide a running example illustrating the modifications made to the Tsai and Roth (2016) system for n-gram wikification, particularly addressing candidate generation and ranking?
3. Could you include preliminary results demonstrating the performance of a combined model incorporating elements from Tackström et al. (2012) or Nothman et al. (2012)?
4.  Will you perform statistical significance tests on the results presented in Table 4 to confirm the statistical significance of the reported improvements?
5.  Why do you observe the surprising language-pair results, specifically Dutch performing best for Spanish and Spanish performing best for Yoruba? What linguistic or Wikipedia-related factors might explain these observations?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper contains several typos and awkward phrasing (e.g., “Latin Script Non-Latin Script” in Table 2’s column headers). Careful proofreading is recommended.
* The citation of "Zhang et al. (2016)" is inconsistent (sometimes with page numbers, sometimes without).
* Clarify whether the reported performance numbers are micro-averaged or macro-averaged F1 scores.
