This paper presents a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model leveraging cross-lingual wikification. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, and use the associated Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as language-independent features for NER. The model is trained on annotated data in source languages (primarily English) and applied to target languages, including low-resource ones. The authors claim the primary contributions are: 1) a novel use of cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER, 2) demonstrating significant performance improvements over comparable direct transfer methods on both CoNLL datasets and low-resource languages, and 3) showing that these language-independent features improve both direct transfer and monolingual models, and facilitate multi-source training.

I see the primary contribution as the demonstration of the surprisingly effective use of comprehensive n-gram wikification as a feature source for cross-lingual NER, *particularly* in the low-resource setting. While the idea of using Wikipedia for cross-lingual transfer isn’t new, the thoroughness of grounding *every* n-gram and demonstrating its benefit, especially without relying on parallel data or native speaker interaction, is a significant empirical contribution. The ablation studies (Table 3) convincingly illustrate the importance of both the wikification process and the link to Freebase. The experimental results, specifically the improvements on the low-resource languages, are impressive and highlight the practical value of the approach. The demonstration that these features also enhance monolingual models is a valuable secondary result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over baselines, especially on the challenging low-resource languages. The performance gains are substantial enough to suggest a real impact. The inclusion of results on a diverse set of languages strengthens the conclusions.
2. **Novelty in Application:** While the components (cross-lingual wikification, Wikipedia features) are known, the specific application of grounding *every* n-gram and using the resulting features for NER in a direct transfer setting is novel and effective. This comprehensive approach distinguishes it from previous work.
3. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies (Tables 2 & 3) are well-designed and provide clear insights into the contribution of different components of the model, specifically demonstrating the necessity of the Freebase link and the impact of Wikipedia size. This adds credibility to the claims.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation for the approach and the experimental setup. The use of Figure 1 as an example is effective in illustrating the core idea.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide enough detail regarding the datasets, features, and experimental setup to facilitate replication of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the ablation studies are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *what types* of entities are still challenging and *why* the model fails in certain cases would provide valuable insights and directions for future work.
2. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The model's effectiveness is heavily reliant on the quality and coverage of the English Wikipedia and Freebase. While this is acknowledged, the paper doesn’t fully explore potential biases introduced by this reliance or alternative approaches to mitigate them (e.g., using multiple Wikipedia languages).
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, is relatively complex and might require significant effort to adapt to new languages or domains. A discussion of the computational cost of feature extraction and potential simplification strategies would be helpful.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art for Low-Resource Languages:** While the comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) is presented, a more comprehensive comparison to other recent approaches specifically designed for low-resource NER would provide a stronger benchmark.
5. **Impact of n-gram size:** The choice of n<=4 is justified, but there's no discussion on how performance changes with different n-gram size limits. Investigating this could reveal more optimal settings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of entities where the model still struggles and potential reasons for these errors?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and coverage of the English Wikipedia? Have you explored strategies for mitigating potential biases introduced by relying on a single language Wikipedia?
3. What is the computational cost of extracting the wikifier features, and are there potential strategies for simplifying the feature set without significantly sacrificing performance?
4.  In Figure 2, what is the reasoning behind selecting the specific training language for each test language? Is there a correlation between linguistic similarity and the performance achieved with different training languages?
5. How do the results change if the n-gram size limit is adjusted (e.g., n<=3, n<=5)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the computational resources used for training and evaluation.
*   The phrase "direct transfer" is used frequently. Perhaps clarifying the different types of transfer learning to which this work relates would be beneficial.
*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.
