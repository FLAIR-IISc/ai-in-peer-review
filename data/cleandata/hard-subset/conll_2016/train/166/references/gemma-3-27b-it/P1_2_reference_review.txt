## Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, using the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for a NER model trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and achieve state-of-the-art results on five low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that grounding text in Wikipedia via a cross-lingual wikifier provides useful, language-independent features for NER, enabling effective transfer learning to low-resource languages. This is the strongest contribution, offering a practical and relatively simple way to leverage the wealth of information in Wikipedia.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Diverse Languages:** The authors conduct thorough experiments across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich (CoNLL) and low-resource languages, providing a robust evaluation of their proposed method.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Quality Impact:** The analysis showing the relationship between Wikipedia size and the effectiveness of the wikifier features offers valuable insight into the limitations and potential improvements of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently reported performance gains on both CoNLL and low-resource languages are compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The fact that it outperforms state-of-the-art methods for low-resource languages *without* relying on parallel corpora or native speaker interaction is significant.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the work.  The idea of using wikification not as a post-processing step but as a feature engineering technique for NER is insightful and logically sound.
3. **Thorough Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, reducing interlanguage links) provides valuable insight into the contribution of different components of the system.  This demonstrates a deep understanding of the method's behavior.
4. **Practicality and Scalability:** The reliance on readily available resources (Wikipedia, a cross-lingual wikifier) makes the approach practically applicable and scalable to a wide range of languages.  The system doesn't require complex language-specific rules or extensive annotated data.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Limitations:** The authors openly acknowledge and discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly the dependency on Wikipedia size and the challenges with non-Latin script languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Wikification Component:** While the *application* of cross-lingual wikification to NER is novel and effective, the wikification system itself (Tsai and Roth, 2016) is not a core contribution of this work.  The paper relies heavily on the performance of an existing system, and improvements to the wikifier itself could likely further enhance the NER results.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** While the core idea is elegant, the feature set (including base features, gazetteer features, and wikifier features) is quite complex. It’s not entirely clear which features contribute most significantly to the performance gains, and further simplification or feature selection might be beneficial.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed feature importance analysis.
3. **Limited Comparison to More Recent NER Models:** The baseline NER model (Ratinov and Roth, 2009) is relatively dated. Comparing against more recent and powerful NER architectures (e.g., those based on transformers like BERT or multilingual BERT) would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the contribution of the wikifier features. It's possible a stronger underlying NER model would lessen the benefits of the added features.
4. **English-Centric Approach:**  The heavy reliance on grounding everything to *English* Wikipedia introduces a potential bias. While the authors demonstrate this works well, it’s unclear if grounding to another central language (e.g., Spanish, German) would yield comparable or even superior results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Feature Importance:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of feature importance? Which features contribute most significantly to the performance gains in different languages and settings?
2.  **Alternative Wikifiers:** Have you experimented with different cross-lingual wikifiers, and if so, how do the results compare?  Would using a more advanced wikifier significantly improve performance?
3.  **Transformer-Based Models:** Have you considered incorporating these features into a more modern NER architecture, such as a BERT-based model? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the challenges to doing so?
4. **Non-English Grounding:** Have you explored grounding to Wikipedia editions in languages other than English?  Would this mitigate the potential bias introduced by English-centricity?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The table formatting is a bit cramped; consider using wider table layouts or breaking them into smaller tables for better readability.
*   The description of the n-gram grounding modification is a bit dense. A visual example illustrating the difference in candidate generation would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights into the use of Wikipedia for transfer learning.  While the novelty of the wikification component itself is limited, the successful application of this technique to NER and the comprehensive evaluation make this a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
