- Proposes cross-lingual NER using a wikifier to generate language-independent features by grounding n-grams to English Wikipedia.
- Uses Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features and shows improved performance on CoNLL and low-resource languages.
- Reframes wikification as a preprocessing feature source for NER rather than a downstream task.

- Demonstrates strong empirical gains, especially in low-resource scenarios, and competes well with prior work requiring native speaker interaction.
- Provides comprehensive experiments: monolingual, cross-lingual transfer, and low-resource evaluations.
- Includes thorough ablation studies that clarify the contribution of Freebase queries, interlanguage link density, and Wikipedia size.
- Clear motivation and exposition; helpful figures illustrate the method and process.
- Practical and broadly applicable approach that leverages existing wikification tools.

- Performance depends heavily on the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier; error propagation is a concern and lacks deep analysis.
- Base feature set is limited compared to state-of-the-art monolingual NER and omits potentially useful language-specific features like POS tags.
- Insufficient error analysis for low-resource languages; reasons for failures (scripts, Wikipedia coverage) are not fully explored and MISC tag removal is under-discussed.
- Reliance on English Wikipedia introduces cultural and coverage bias; alternative multilingual KBs are not sufficiently considered.

- Request detailed analysis of wikification error types and their impact on precision/recall.
- Ask whether language-specific features were experimented with alongside wikifier features and results of such experiments.
- Request deeper error analysis for low-resource languages and strategies to mitigate common failures.
- Ask about using alternative multilingual knowledge bases (e.g., Wikidata) and trade-offs compared to English Wikipedia grounding.

- Table 2 notation is dense and needs a key; conclusion references Figure 2 but should refer to Figure 1.

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel paper with compelling results and minor revisions requested; recommendation to accept.
