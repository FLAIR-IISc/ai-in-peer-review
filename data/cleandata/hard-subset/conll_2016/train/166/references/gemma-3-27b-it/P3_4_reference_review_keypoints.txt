- Presents a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a wikifier to generate language-independent features grounded in English Wikipedia.  
- Uses Wikipedia categories and Freebase types from grounded n-grams as features for an English-trained NER model.  
- Shows significant performance gains over baseline direct-transfer methods and achieves state-of-the-art on several low-resource languages.  

Main contributions:
- Demonstrates wikification as an effective source of language-agnostic features for cross-lingual NER.  
- Produces strong empirical results across resource-rich and low-resource languages.  
- Provides analysis of Wikipedia size effects and benefits of multilingual training.  

Strengths:
- Novel application of wikification as a feature-engineering step for direct-transfer NER.  
- Thorough experimental validation on diverse languages including low-resource datasets.  
- Insightful analysis linking Wikipedia coverage to performance (Table 3).  
- Clear and well-organized presentation with helpful illustrations.  

Weaknesses:
- Individual components (wikifier, averaged perceptron NER) are not novel; novelty is primarily in the combination.  
- Heavy dependence on English Wikipedia coverage and potential bias, with limited mitigation strategies explored.  
- Complex feature set; lacks fine-grained analysis of individual wikifier feature contributions (e.g., specific Freebase types or n-gram effects).  
- Limited qualitative error analysis detailing which entity types or linguistic phenomena cause failures.  

Questions for authors:
- Have you considered using multiple central Wikipedias or methods to mitigate English Wikipedia bias?  
- Which Freebase types and n-gram lengths contribute most to performance—can you provide feature importance?  
- Can you provide qualitative examples of typical misclassification errors and their causes?  
- What is the computational cost of generating wikifier features for large corpora and is it practical in real-world settings?  

Additional comments:
- Clarify the distinction vs. prior work claiming scalability limitations when both use pre-trained NER systems.  
- It would be interesting to compare or combine these features with modern transformer-based NER models.  

Overall assessment:
- Strong paper with a practical, effective approach; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the noted weaknesses.
