## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams in various languages to English Wikipedia entries and utilize the associated FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate performance gains on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) using a direct transfer approach trained on English data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikifier Feature Effectiveness:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that features derived from cross-lingual wikification can significantly improve cross-lingual NER performance, particularly in a direct transfer setting. This is a practical finding with potential for wider application.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a relatively broad evaluation across multiple languages, including low-resource scenarios, providing a useful benchmark for future work. The ablation studies regarding the Freebase link and Wikipedia size are insightful.
3. **Practical System & Baseline Comparison:** The authors provide a working system and compare against established baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.), showcasing a noticeable improvement, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The experimental results are the strongest aspect of this paper. The consistent improvements over baselines on both CoNLL and low-resource languages, as reported in Table 2, are compelling. The detailed ablation studies demonstrating the importance of Freebase and Wikipedia size strengthen these results.
2. **Clear Methodology:** The methodology is clearly explained and well-structured. The paper details the feature engineering process and the modifications to the cross-lingual wikifier, allowing for reproducibility. The chosen evaluation metric (F1-score) is standard and appropriate.
3. **Relevance to a Practical Problem:** Cross-lingual NER with limited resources is a significant challenge in NLP. This paper directly addresses this challenge with a practical and relatively straightforward approach. The reliance on Wikipedia makes the method accessible without requiring extensive parallel corpora.
4. **Thorough Baseline Comparison:**  Comparison to relevant prior work, including Täckström’s direct transfer approach and Zhang et al’s expectation-based method, provides a strong context for evaluating the contribution. The reported improvements are noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the application to NER is valuable, the core idea of leveraging Wikipedia for cross-lingual transfer is not entirely novel (see related work). The authors acknowledge this, but the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight *what* makes their approach distinct beyond the specific features and comprehensive evaluation.  The wikification *as a feature engineering step* is the core contribution, but feels incremental.
2. **Over-Reliance on English as Source:** The consistent focus on training exclusively on English data is a limitation. While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of transferring *from* English, the potential for multi-lingual training (as hinted at in the conclusion) needs more exploration.  The impact of the source language’s linguistic distance from target languages isn’t adequately addressed.
3. **Wikifier Performance as a Bottleneck:** The performance of the cross-lingual wikifier itself is a potential bottleneck. Imperfect disambiguation, as acknowledged in Figure 1, introduces noise into the features. The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the wikifier's impact on NER performance, especially for the low-resource languages where the wikifier is likely to struggle the most.
4. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** While the paper clearly describes the features, the overall feature set seems quite large. A more focused analysis on the most impactful features would be beneficial. The features are largely based on English Wikipedia structure - how well does this translate to languages with vastly different knowledge organization?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the cross-lingual wikifier, specifically regarding the types of errors made and their impact on NER performance, especially for the low-resource languages?
2.  Have you experimented with training on multiple source languages simultaneously (beyond the experiment in Section 4.4)? If so, what results did you obtain, and what is the optimal strategy for selecting source languages?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with generating and incorporating these wikifier features? Is the process scalable to very large datasets?
4.  You mention the possibility of using other information from Wikipedia. Can you elaborate on potential avenues for exploration, and the challenges associated with them?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, but could benefit from more in-depth discussion of the limitations.
* The reference to "LDC2014E115 3LDC2015E13,LDC2015E90,LDC2015E83,LDC2015E91" in the table is unclear and should be clarified.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER. While the core idea isn’t groundbreaking, the comprehensive evaluation, practical system, and demonstrated performance gains make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I lean towards acceptance, pending satisfactory responses to the questions raised.
