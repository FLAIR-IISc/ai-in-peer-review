This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea—using Wikipedia as a bridge to transfer knowledge between languages—is interesting and potentially valuable, especially for low-resource languages. The experiments are comprehensive, covering both CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages, and the ablation studies are well-designed and insightful.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Cross-Lingual Transfer via Wikification:** The primary contribution is a strong empirical demonstration that features derived from cross-lingual wikification significantly improve NER performance in a direct transfer setting, particularly for low-resource languages.  The consistent performance gains across a variety of languages are compelling.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including ablation studies that isolate the impact of different feature sets and investigations into the relationship between Wikipedia size and performance. This rigor strengthens the validity of their findings.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Wikipedia Quality:** The analysis regarding the dependency of performance on Wikipedia size and the impact of Freebase accessibility in different languages (particularly Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil) provides valuable insights into the limitations and potential improvements of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported F1 scores, particularly on the low-resource languages, consistently outperform existing baselines, showcasing the practical benefit of the proposed approach. The improvement over Zhang et al. (2016), which requires native speaker interaction, is notable.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of low-resource NER and positions the proposed method as a viable solution that avoids the need for parallel corpora or extensive language-specific engineering.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:**  The feature set is well-considered, combining standard NER features with novel wikifier features, and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component. The modifications to the wikifier system to handle n-grams are a clever and important detail.
4. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation experiments are crucial for understanding *why* the model works. Showing the impact of removing Freebase information and varying interlanguage link density is particularly helpful.
5. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are performed on a relatively large and diverse set of languages, strengthening the generalizability of the conclusions.  The multilingual training experiments are also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core NER Model:** The underlying NER model (Ratinov and Roth, 2009) is a well-established approach. While the *application* of wikifier features to this model is novel, the core architecture doesn't represent a significant advance in NER modeling itself.  The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging this and focusing the contribution claim on the effective feature engineering and cross-lingual transfer methodology.
2. **Dependency on English Wikipedia:** The reliance on English Wikipedia as the central hub introduces a potential bias.  The quality and coverage of English Wikipedia may not be representative of all languages, and this could limit the performance in languages with significantly different cultural or linguistic characteristics. While the paper acknowledges this, a more in-depth discussion of this potential bias would be beneficial.
3. **Scalability Concerns:**  Wikifying all n-grams (up to n=4) can be computationally expensive, especially for large documents.  The paper doesn’t discuss the scalability of this approach or explore potential optimizations.  Mentioning the computational cost and potential strategies for addressing it (e.g., filtering, approximation) would be valuable.
4. **Presentation of Figure 2:** While the multilingual training result is good, the heat map in Figure 2 is visually somewhat cluttered and difficult to interpret quickly.  A clearer visualization or a table summarizing the key findings from this experiment would be more effective.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered exploring alternative cross-lingual wikification systems beyond the one used in Tsai and Roth (2016)?  Could different wikifiers lead to improved performance or robustness?
2.  Could you quantify the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams for a typical document? What strategies could be used to reduce this cost without significantly impacting performance?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the interlanguage links between Wikipedia articles in different languages?  Are there specific languages where the interlanguage links are known to be inaccurate or incomplete, and how does this affect the results?
4.  Given the dependency on English Wikipedia, have you considered ways to mitigate potential biases introduced by its content or structure? For example, could you incorporate information from other language Wikipedias directly into the feature set?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "silver-standard" data appears a few times. Consider replacing this with "automatically generated" or "weakly supervised" data, as it's more descriptive in this context.
*   The introduction could be slightly more concise, focusing more directly on the specific challenges and contributions of the paper.
*   The related work section is comprehensive but could be streamlined to focus more on the most directly relevant prior work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis is insightful. While the core NER model isn’t particularly novel, the effective integration of cross-lingual wikification features represents a valuable advance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The paper has the potential to be highly influential, particularly for researchers working on low-resource NLP tasks.
