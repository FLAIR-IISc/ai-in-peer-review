## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language into the English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over direct transfer baselines on CoNLL data and several low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper effectively shows that grounding text in a shared knowledge base (English Wikipedia) via wikification can provide useful signals for NER across languages, even without parallel data. This is a pragmatic and potentially impactful finding.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach across a relatively diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich and low-resource scenarios, and provide comparisons to relevant baselines. The ablation studies examining the contribution of different feature sets are also valuable.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Size Impact:** The analysis highlighting the dependency of performance on the size of the Wikipedia for the target language is insightful and provides a practical consideration for applying this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Results:** The reported performance gains, particularly on low-resource languages where direct transfer is challenging, are encouraging. The consistent improvement over baselines across multiple languages strengthens the claim that the proposed method is effective.
2. **Well-Defined Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the model architecture, feature engineering process (including the modifications to the wikifier), and experimental setup. This facilitates reproducibility and allows for a clear understanding of the approach.
3. **Practical Approach:**  The reliance on readily available resources – multilingual Wikipedia and a cross-lingual wikifier – makes this method relatively accessible and easy to implement. The avoidance of parallel corpora is a significant advantage.
4. **Detailed Ablation Studies:**  The careful ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contribution of the various feature components, making it clear *why* the approach works. The experiments removing Freebase support and interlanguage links are particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the application to NER is sensible, the core idea of using Wikipedia/Wikification for cross-lingual transfer is not entirely novel (see related work). The paper doesn’t adequately differentiate its contribution beyond applying existing techniques to a specific task. The "novelty" of grounding *every* n-gram, while interesting, feels more like an implementation detail than a substantial theoretical advancement.
2. **Over-reliance on English Wikipedia:** The entire system hinges on the English Wikipedia as the central knowledge base. This introduces a significant bias. The assumption that concepts are best represented and disambiguated within the English Wikipedia context may not hold true for all languages and domains. The paper acknowledges this with the Wikipedia size analysis, but doesn't explore alternatives.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., specific entity types, linguistic phenomena) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Knowing the types of errors will also help the community understand where the method can be utilized.
4. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Typos:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of bold font, spacing issues in tables) and typos (e.g., “Schwierigkeiten beim nachvollziehenden Verstehen” in Figure 1’s caption seems out of place). While minor, these detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** While the results appear promising, the paper lacks statistical significance testing to demonstrate that the improvements over baselines are not due to chance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your model? Specifically, what are the common failure cases for low-resource languages?
2.  Have you considered exploring alternative knowledge bases beyond the English Wikipedia? Would using multilingual knowledge graphs or grounding in language-specific Wikipedias improve performance?
3.  The modification to the wikifier to process every n-gram seems to introduce significant computational overhead. Can you quantify this overhead and discuss its implications for scalability?
4. The paper mentions training on multiple source languages; could you elaborate on the strategy for selecting source languages and weighting their contributions during training?
5.  Why was POS tagging not included in the final model despite being mentioned in Section 3.1? Was it simply due to unavailability for all languages, or did preliminary experiments show it was detrimental?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure captions are somewhat terse. Providing more context would improve understanding.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly concerning languages with limited Wikipedia coverage or significantly different linguistic structures.
*   The reference list could be formatted more consistently.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed investigation of using cross-lingual wikification for NER. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the comprehensive experimental evaluation and insightful analysis of the Wikipedia size impact are valuable contributions. However, the lack of substantial novelty, the reliance on the English Wikipedia, the absence of error analysis, and the minor formatting issues prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection**, with encouragement to address the weaknesses raised in this review and resubmit for future consideration. The core idea has merit, but requires further refinement and validation.
