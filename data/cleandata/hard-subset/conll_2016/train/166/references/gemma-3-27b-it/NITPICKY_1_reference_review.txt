## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams to the English Wikipedia and utilize the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER in low-resource languages, trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate performance improvements on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Wikification Features:** The paper provides a relatively comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that features derived from cross-lingual wikification *can* improve cross-lingual NER performance, particularly in a direct transfer setting. This is a valuable contribution, as it offers a practical way to incorporate external knowledge from Wikipedia.
2. **Analysis of Wikipedia Size Impact:** The analysis highlighting the dependency of wikification feature quality on Wikipedia size in the target language is a useful observation. It provides a nuanced understanding of the method’s limitations and suggests potential areas for improvement.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The work includes a reasonably extensive set of experiments across multiple languages and configurations (monolingual, direct transfer, multi-source training) which adds to the robustness of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-defined, utilize standard datasets (CoNLL, REFLEX, LORELEI), and include clear baselines for comparison (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.).  The evaluation metric (F1-score) is appropriate.
2. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is described in sufficient detail, allowing for replication. The use of Figure 1 to illustrate the wikification process is helpful.
3. **Practical Approach:** The proposed method is relatively straightforward to implement, relying on readily available resources like Wikipedia and existing wikification tools. This practicality enhances the potential impact of the work.
4. **Insightful Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, reducing interlanguage links) offer valuable insights into the contribution of different components of the model. These analyses support the claims made about the importance of Wikipedia size and feature quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application of cross-lingual wikification to NER is presented as a key contribution, the underlying concept – leveraging external knowledge for cross-lingual transfer – is not entirely novel. Prior work, as acknowledged in the related work section, has explored Wikipedia and entity linking for similar tasks. The novelty lies in *how* the wikification is utilized (grounding *all* n-grams) but this feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity & Lack of Justification:**  The feature set appears somewhat complex and ad-hoc.  The rationale behind certain feature choices (e.g., specific window sizes, inclusion of FreeBase types) is not always clear and could benefit from further justification. A more systematic feature selection process would strengthen the work.
3. **Overreliance on English as Source Language:** The authors primarily focus on transfer *from* English. While this is a common approach, it limits the generalizability of the findings. Exploring transfer from other high-resource languages (e.g., Spanish, German) could reveal important insights.  The analysis of training language effectiveness, while present, feels somewhat cursory.
4. **Suboptimal Handling of Low-Resource Languages:**  The removal of MISC tags in low-resource languages is a concerning simplification. It introduces a bias into the evaluation and potentially masks the true performance of the model. A more robust approach to handling differing tagsets would be preferable. The complete removal of lexical features from some low-resource languages, based on script type, feels overly drastic and potentially harmful.
5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:**  The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors (e.g., inconsistent hyphenation, missing spaces, grammatical errors). While these do not fundamentally affect the technical content, they detract from the overall polish and credibility of the work. For example, “Schwierigkeiten beim nachvollziehenden Verstehen” appears randomly in the middle of the text.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind grounding *all* n-grams to Wikipedia, rather than first performing mention detection? What are the trade-offs associated with this approach?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of wikifier? Have you experimented with alternative cross-lingual wikification systems, and if so, how do the results compare?
3.  You mention that the inclusion of POS tags did not improve performance. Did you consider using multilingual POS taggers, or exploring POS tags from other languages as features?
4.  Regarding the removal of MISC tags in low-resource languages, could you provide a more detailed justification for this decision?  Have you considered alternative strategies for handling tagset discrepancies?
5.  Could you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with the wikification process, particularly for large datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please thoroughly proofread the manuscript to address the numerous formatting and typographical errors.
*   The related work section could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive overview of relevant research.
*   The use of abbreviations (e.g., NER, NLP) should be consistent throughout the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a competent and well-executed study on the use of cross-lingual wikification for NER. While the core method lacks significant novelty, the empirical evaluation is thorough and provides valuable insights. However, the aforementioned weaknesses—particularly the limited novelty, feature engineering complexity, and suboptimal handling of low-resource languages—raise concerns. **I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns raised in this review.** A stronger focus on justifying feature choices, exploring alternative training strategies, and addressing the limitations of the low-resource language experiments could significantly improve the impact of this work.
