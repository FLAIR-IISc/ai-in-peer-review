This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams in test documents to English Wikipedia, utilizing the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over direct transfer baselines on CoNLL datasets and achieve competitive results on several low-resource languages.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical demonstration of the utility of readily available cross-lingual wikification for cross-lingual NER, particularly in low-resource scenarios. While the core idea isn't entirely novel (linking to knowledge bases for NER is well-established), the application of wikification as a primary feature *generation* technique, and its surprisingly strong performance, is noteworthy. A secondary contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across a range of languages, including several truly low-resource settings. The ablation studies exploring the impact of Wikipedia size and training language combinations are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirically Sound:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple languages and datasets. The inclusion of both CoNLL languages and genuinely low-resource languages strengthens the findings. The ablation studies (varying Wikipedia size, training languages) are well-executed and provide useful insights.
2. **Practical Approach:** The method relies on readily available resources (Wikipedia, a cross-lingual wikifier) and doesn’t necessitate parallel corpora or extensive language-specific engineering. This makes the approach relatively easy to implement and deploy.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The reported results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, particularly in the low-resource settings where the approach seems most beneficial.  Outperforming systems relying on parallel data and native speaker interaction is a strong indicator of the method's efficacy.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The example in Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the core concept.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The fundamental idea of using knowledge base information to enhance NER isn’t new. The primary novelty lies in the specific application of *cross-lingual wikification* as a feature generation method. While effective, the leap from existing knowledge-based NER approaches isn't substantial enough to warrant strong claims of innovation. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of related work and a clearer articulation of the unique contribution.
2. **Wikifier Dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of *wikification errors* isn’t sufficiently explored. Errors in linking will inevitably introduce noise into the features and could degrade performance.  A more detailed error analysis of the wikifier's output, specifically focusing on its impact on NER accuracy, is needed.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The model relies on the Ratinov and Roth (2009) NER model with a fixed set of base features. While this provides a solid baseline, it limits the potential for synergistic improvements. Exploring more advanced NER architectures or incorporating additional features (e.g., character-level embeddings, contextualized word embeddings) could further enhance performance. The authors briefly mention this in the conclusion, but it should be acknowledged as a limitation.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the ablation studies offer some insight, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples of incorrectly identified entities would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach and guide future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the cross-lingual wikifier and their impact on NER performance? Specifically, how does performance degrade when the wikifier links to incorrect entities?
2.  Have you experimented with more advanced NER architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, Transformers) in conjunction with the wikifier features? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3.  The paper mentions removing MISC tags when training on CoNLL and testing on low-resource languages.  How sensitive are the results to this tag removal process?  Could a different strategy for handling tag discrepancies be more effective?
4.  The results in Table 3 show a significant drop in performance when the FreeBase component is removed. Does this indicate that the Wikipedia categories alone are insufficient for achieving good results?



**Additional Comments:**

*  Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the wikification process.
*  The formatting of Table 2 is somewhat dense. Consider breaking it down into multiple tables for improved readability.
* The phrase "approaches that include base features perform poorly" in Section 4.3 is a strong claim that needs further justification.  "Poorly" compared to what?
