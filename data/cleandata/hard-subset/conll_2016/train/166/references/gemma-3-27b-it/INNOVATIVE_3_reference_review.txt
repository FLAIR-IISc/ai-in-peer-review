This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets and achieve competitive results on several low-resource languages.

The primary contribution is the *novel application of cross-lingual wikification as a feature engineering technique for direct transfer NER*. While wikification and entity linking are established fields, repurposing them not as a downstream task *but as a fundamental component for creating language-agnostic features* is a significant shift in perspective. A second, supporting contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including low-resource scenarios, demonstrating the method’s robustness. The thorough ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly illustrate the value of the proposed wikifier features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Idea:** The central premise of utilizing cross-lingual wikification *before* NER – turning a typically downstream process into a feature generator – is a genuinely novel approach. This isn't simply applying existing techniques; it fundamentally alters how cross-lingual NER is framed.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements, particularly on the low-resource languages where transfer learning is most crucial. Outperforming a system requiring native speaker interaction (Zhang et al., 2016) is a particularly strong result.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation studies and exploration of Wikipedia size (Table 3) provide valuable insights into the factors influencing performance.  The analysis clearly demonstrates *why* the method works and where its limitations lie, specifically the dependency on the quality of cross-lingual links.  This is evidence of thoughtful experimentation, not just result chasing.
4. **Practicality & Scalability:** The method’s reliance solely on Wikipedia makes it highly practical and scalable to numerous languages without requiring parallel corpora or extensive language-specific engineering. The clear explanation of the system modification to handle n-grams is a good sign of implementation care.
5. **Demonstrated Generalization:** The ability to combine training data from multiple languages (Table 4) highlights the potential for further performance gains and emphasizes the model’s flexibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Dependency & Error Propagation:** The entire system is contingent on the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of wikification errors isn’t deeply investigated.  A more detailed error analysis – specifically, identifying cases where incorrect wikification leads to incorrect NER predictions – would be valuable.
2. **Limited Novelty in Core NER Model:** The paper utilizes a well-established NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009). While a solid baseline, the core model itself isn't a contribution. The innovation lies almost entirely in the feature engineering. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but the paper's impact is somewhat limited by its reliance on an existing architecture.
3. **Freebase Reliance:** The strong performance seems tied to the availability of Freebase types.  The degradation in performance observed when removing Freebase querying (Table 3) raises concerns about the method's applicability to languages/entities poorly represented in Freebase.  A discussion on alternative knowledge sources would be beneficial.
4. **Presentation of Results:** While comprehensive, the results tables (especially Table 2) are dense and somewhat difficult to quickly interpret. A clearer visual presentation, potentially with highlighted performance gains, would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, illustrating specific examples where wikification errors lead to incorrect NER predictions? What steps could be taken to mitigate the impact of such errors?
2.  You mention that the features are designed for English. How sensitive is the performance to languages drastically different from English in terms of morphology and syntax?  Are there any modifications that could improve performance in such cases?
3.  Given the reliance on Freebase, what alternative knowledge sources have you considered, and how would their integration impact the model's performance and portability?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presents its methodology and results.  The experimental setup is robust. While minor methodological improvements are possible (as highlighted in the weaknesses), the novelty and promising results warrant acceptance. The bold idea and empirical validation are significant contributions to the field of cross-lingual NER.
