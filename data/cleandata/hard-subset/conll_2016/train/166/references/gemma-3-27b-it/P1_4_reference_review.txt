## Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, and use the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). They also show that these features can enhance monolingual models and benefit from multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The most significant contribution is showcasing the utility of cross-lingual wikification *beyond* its traditional role in entity linking as a downstream task. The work convincingly argues and demonstrates that the disambiguation process inherent in wikification provides valuable signals for NER, even when imperfect.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Low-Resource Languages:** The performance gains on the five low-resource languages, especially considering the lack of target language training data and the comparative simplicity of the approach, are noteworthy. Outperforming state-of-the-art methods which rely on parallel corpora or native speaker interaction represents a substantial advancement.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper offers a thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies, analysis of Wikipedia size impact, and exploration of multi-source training, providing a robust assessment of the proposed method.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:**  The central idea of utilizing cross-lingual wikification features for NER is novel and intuitively appealing.  Framing wikification as a source of language-independent features rather than a post-processing step is a valuable shift in perspective. The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind this approach and provide supporting evidence.
2. **Strong Performance, Especially in Low-Resource Settings:** The results consistently show improvements over baseline methods, particularly for low-resource languages. This is crucial as the primary motivation for cross-lingual transfer is to address data scarcity.  The improvement on languages with non-Latin scripts is particularly promising.
3. **Well-Designed Experiments and Analysis:** The experiments are well-structured and cover a diverse range of languages and scenarios.  The ablation studies (e.g., removing Freebase querying, reducing interlanguage links) provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components.  The analysis of Wikipedia size correlation with performance is insightful and justifies further exploration.
4. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental results. The inclusion of Figure 1 effectively illustrates the concept.
5. **Reproducibility:** While a full implementation isn't provided, the description of the method and the use of readily available resources like Wikipedia and the Tsai & Roth (2016) wikifier promotes the potential for reproducibility.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core NER Model:** The paper builds upon an existing NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) and primarily focuses on feature engineering. While the features are novel in the context of cross-lingual NER, the underlying NER architecture itself lacks significant innovation. This weakens the overall claim of a highly innovative contribution.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The heavy reliance on the English Wikipedia as the grounding point is a limitation. The quality and coverage of the English Wikipedia may introduce biases and potentially limit the model's performance on languages with significantly different cultural contexts or knowledge representation in their respective Wikipedias. 
3. **Superficial Exploration of Feature Interactions:** While the ablation studies are helpful, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the interactions between the wikifier features and the baseline features. Understanding how these features complement or conflict with each other could lead to further performance improvements.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed error analysis.  Identifying common error cases and analyzing the reasons behind them (e.g., ambiguity, incorrect wikification, lack of lexical features) could provide valuable insights for future research.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Bias in English Wikipedia:** How do you anticipate the reliance on English Wikipedia impacting performance on languages with very different cultural contexts or knowledge representation? Have you considered methods to mitigate potential biases?
2. **Feature Interaction:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how the wikifier features interact with the baseline features? Are there specific feature combinations that consistently lead to higher or lower performance?
3. **Alternative Wikification Systems:** Have you experimented with other cross-lingual wikification systems? Would using a different wikifier significantly impact the results?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting common types of errors made by the model and the potential reasons behind them?  Specific examples would be very helpful.




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is fairly comprehensive, but could perhaps be more critical in highlighting the specific gaps that this work aims to address.
*   The table layouts are slightly cumbersome due to the width.  Consider simplifying the presentation or breaking them into smaller tables.
* It would be good to mention computational cost of wikification process, especially for large documents.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER.  While the underlying NER model isn’t groundbreaking, the innovative use of cross-lingual wikification features, coupled with the strong empirical results – particularly on low-resource languages – makes this paper worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the potential bias introduced by the reliance on English Wikipedia and a more in-depth feature analysis, would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
