## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia and utilize the resulting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER, trained on resource-rich languages and applied to low-resource ones. 

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification Feature Effectiveness:** The primary contribution is a clear empirical demonstration that features derived from cross-lingual wikification are valuable for cross-lingual NER, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The consistent performance gains across multiple languages are noteworthy.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a relatively thorough evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including CoNLL languages and a set of genuinely low-resource languages. The comparison against existing methods is reasonable.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Size Impact:** The investigation into the effect of Wikipedia size on the quality of wikification features is a valuable insight, highlighting a crucial dependency for the method’s success.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The paper consistently shows performance improvements over strong baselines, particularly on the low-resource languages. This provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement on Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil is particularly encouraging given the script differences.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The premise of leveraging the rich knowledge base of Wikipedia, accessed through wikification, is a logical and potentially impactful strategy for cross-lingual NER. The idea of treating wikification *as* a feature engineering step, rather than a pre-processing one, is a novel perspective.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The description of the feature set is comprehensive. The explanation of how wikifier features are integrated into the existing NER model is clear and well-structured.  The modifications to the wikification system to handle n-grams are sensible.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the influence of Wikipedia size on performance is a strong point, demonstrating a clear understanding of the method’s limitations and dependencies. The investigation of training language impacts is also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the *application* of cross-lingual wikification to NER is a reasonable approach, the underlying NER model (Ratinov & Roth 2009) and the core idea of using Wikipedia-derived features are not fundamentally new. The paper largely builds upon established techniques rather than presenting a groundbreaking methodological advancement.  The primary novelty resides in the specific feature combination and the thorough empirical evaluation.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails—types of entities frequently misidentified, common disambiguation errors—would be valuable. What kinds of entities *benefit* most from the wikifier features? This analysis is crucial for understanding the method's limitations and guiding future improvements.
3. **Wikification System Details Lacking:** The paper relies heavily on an existing cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai & Roth 2016). However, details about the performance *of the wikifier itself* on the target languages are scarce. Reporting precision and recall of the wikification step would strengthen the paper. Imperfect wikification introduces noise, and the impact of this noise on NER performance needs to be better understood. The modifications to the wikifier are mentioned, but a quantitative comparison of the modified vs. original performance would be helpful.
4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several instances of inconsistent formatting (e.g., varying spacing, uneven heading styles).  Table 2 is overly dense and difficult to parse quickly. A more visually organized presentation of the data would enhance readability.  The reference list is also somewhat inconsistent in style.
5. **Overstated Claims Regarding State-of-the-Art:** The claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art “which assumes parallel text and some interaction with a native speaker of the target language” (Introduction) requires careful scrutiny. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) is valuable, but the description of their method feels dismissive. A more nuanced discussion of the tradeoffs between different approaches is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically identifying common error types and illustrating how the wikifier features contribute to these errors?
2.  What are the precision and recall of the cross-lingual wikifier on the languages used in your experiments, *before and after* your modifications to handle n-grams?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the underlying wikifier? Could a different, potentially less accurate, wikifier significantly impact the results?
4. Can you elaborate on the computational cost of grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia? Is this scalable for very large documents?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding ablation studies to quantify the individual contribution of different feature sets (base features, gazetteers, wikifier features).
*   The motivation for using the BIO labeling scheme, converting from the original IOB1, could be briefly explained.
*   In the introduction, briefly define “NIL” as it relates to the wikifier.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to cross-lingual NER. While the core methodology isn't groundbreaking, the thorough empirical evaluation and insightful analysis demonstrate the value of using cross-lingual wikification features. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—particularly the lack of error analysis and more detailed reporting on wikification performance—would significantly strengthen the paper.  Despite the minor formatting issues, I believe the paper merits acceptance, contingent upon addressing the reviewer's concerns.
