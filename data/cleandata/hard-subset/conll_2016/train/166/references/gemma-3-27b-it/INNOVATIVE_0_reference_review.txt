## Review

This paper presents a surprisingly effective, and refreshingly straightforward, approach to cross-lingual NER leveraging cross-lingual wikification. The core idea – to use Wikipedia disambiguation as a source of language-independent features for NER – is genuinely novel and bypasses the typical complexities of parallel corpora or intricate projection methods. While the method itself isn't groundbreaking in its individual components (it utilizes a pre-existing NER model and a cross-lingual wikifier), the *combination* and application to this problem space are what make it stand out. The results, particularly the performance on low-resource languages exceeding state-of-the-art methods requiring more resources, are compelling.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Cross-Lingual Wikification:** The paper demonstrates the surprisingly powerful utility of simply *grounding* all n-grams to English Wikipedia as a means of creating transferable NER features. This is a shift in perspective – traditionally wikification is a downstream task *following* NER, here it’s foundational to the entire process.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Low-Resource Languages:** The significant performance gains on languages with limited annotated data (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) are a major strength, demonstrating the practical benefit of the proposed approach.
3. **Demonstration of Multi-Lingual Training:** Showing that training on multiple source languages simultaneously can improve performance is a valuable, if somewhat expected, finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold & Novel Idea:** The paper’s strength lies in its central conceit. It’s a relatively simple idea, but one that hasn’t been explored before in this manner. The authors identify a gap in current methods and propose an elegant solution. This is the kind of "thinking outside the box" that the field needs.
2. **Excellent Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments, across both CoNLL languages and a diverse set of low-resource languages, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation studies (e.g., removing Freebase links) are particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the core concept. The experimental setup is clearly explained.
4. **Resource Efficiency:** The method’s reliance solely on Wikipedia (and a pre-trained NER model) makes it highly accessible and scalable. It avoids the need for expensive parallel corpora or native speaker expertise.
5. **Outperformance of Strong Baselines:** The consistent outperformance of established methods (including Zhang et al., 2016) on low-resource languages is a strong indicator of the approach's value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Dependency & Error Propagation:** The performance is inherently limited by the accuracy of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier. Errors in disambiguation will directly impact NER performance. While the paper acknowledges this, a more thorough error analysis examining *types* of wikification errors and their impact would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Analysis of Feature Importance:** While ablation studies are present, a more detailed analysis of which specific Wikipedia features (Freebase types, categories) are *most* informative for NER would strengthen the understanding of the model's behavior.
3. **English-Centric Approach:** The grounding to English Wikipedia introduces a potential bias. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore alternatives (e.g., grounding to a more neutral, interlingual representation). This dependency on English might limit performance in languages significantly different from English.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent NER Models:** The use of the Ratinov and Roth (2009) NER model as a base feels somewhat dated. While not a critical flaw, comparing against more recent state-of-the-art NER architectures (e.g., transformer-based models) could provide a more robust evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the wikifier, focusing on the types of disambiguation errors that most negatively impact NER performance?
2. Which specific Freebase types and Wikipedia categories are most predictive of NER tags in your experiments?  A feature importance analysis would be very valuable.
3. Have you considered alternative strategies for grounding n-grams, such as using a multilingual embedding space instead of relying solely on English Wikipedia?
4. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier? Is there a threshold below which performance degrades significantly?



**Additional Comments:**

* The table formatting could be improved for readability (e.g., better column alignment).
* While the paper is thorough, a discussion of the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram would be useful.



**Overall:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a highly innovative approach and compelling results. The novelty of the idea, combined with the demonstrated performance gains, particularly on low-resource languages, outweigh the flaws. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above. The paper has the potential to significantly influence future research in cross-lingual NER.
