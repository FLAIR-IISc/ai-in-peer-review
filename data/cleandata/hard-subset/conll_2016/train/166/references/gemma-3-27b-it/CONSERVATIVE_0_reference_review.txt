## Review of “Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification”

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams in test documents to English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER, trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate performance improvements on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and several low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification Features for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper effectively demonstrates that features derived from cross-lingual wikification *can* be beneficial for transferring NER models to new languages, particularly in a low-resource setting. This is a reasonably solid empirical finding.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a fairly extensive evaluation across a variety of languages, including several low-resource languages, offering a broader picture than many cross-lingual transfer papers.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Size Impact:** The investigation into how Wikipedia size influences wikification feature quality is a valuable, albeit expected, observation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of NER in low-resource languages and positions its contribution within the context of existing approaches (projection, Wikipedia-based methods, direct transfer).
2. **Strong Empirical Results (CoNLL):** The reported performance gains on the CoNLL datasets, particularly German and Spanish, are substantial enough to warrant attention. The improvements over the Täckström baseline are notable.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper provides a thorough description of the features used, including the baseline features, gazetteer features, and the novel wikifier features. This level of detail is crucial for reproducibility.
4. **Robustness Analysis (Wikipedia Size):** The ablation study demonstrating the correlation between Wikipedia size and feature effectiveness strengthens the validity of the findings and highlights a key limitation.
5. **Multi-Language Training Exploration:** The investigation into combining training data from multiple source languages offers a practical direction for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** The concept of using Wikipedia information for NER is *not* new. Many prior works (cited in the paper) have explored similar ideas. While grounding n-grams via wikification to create language-independent features is a reasonable extension, it’s not a fundamentally novel breakthrough. The claim of a “key contribution” in this area feels overstated.
2. **Over-Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The entire system hinges on the quality and coverage of the *English* Wikipedia. This creates a strong bias and limits the applicability to languages with poor links to the English Wikipedia, as evidenced by the poorer performance on Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. The proposed solution of simply adding more languages to the training data doesn't fully address the underlying problem.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to Täckström are provided, more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual transfer methods (e.g., those employing multilingual pre-trained language models like mBERT or XLM-RoBERTa) are missing. It is unclear how the proposed method stacks up against these state-of-the-art approaches.  Demonstrating competitive performance against these models would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Are errors primarily due to disambiguation errors in the wikifier? Are certain entity types more challenging than others?
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The process of wikifying every n-gram (up to n=4) can be computationally expensive, especially for large documents. The paper does not address the scalability of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of your model compare to recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER models based on multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa)? Please provide quantitative comparisons.
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the common types of errors made by your model and their potential causes?
3. What is the computational cost of the wikification step, and how does it scale with document length and vocabulary size?
4. You mention the possibility of combining your approach with parallel projection. Have you experimented with this combination, and if so, what were the results?
5. The Freebase API seems crucial. What happens if that API is unavailable, or becomes significantly limited in the future? Is there a fallback mechanism?
