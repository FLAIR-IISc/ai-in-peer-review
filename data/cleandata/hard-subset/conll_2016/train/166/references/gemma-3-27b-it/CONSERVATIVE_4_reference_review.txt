## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams in various languages to English Wikipedia, utilizing the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER, trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly shows that linking words to English Wikipedia, even with imperfect disambiguation, provides useful signals for NER in a cross-lingual transfer setting. This is the most significant aspect of the work.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct experiments on a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich and low-resource scenarios, providing a reasonably thorough evaluation of their approach.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Quality Impact:** The investigation into the relationship between Wikipedia size and the effectiveness of the wikification features is a valuable insight.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The overall approach is logically sound. Utilizing wikification to create language-agnostic features is a sensible strategy for cross-lingual transfer, particularly given the lack of parallel corpora in many language pairs. The use of established features from Ratinov & Roth (2009) as a baseline is appropriate and facilitates comparability.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the CoNLL datasets, especially the significant gains on Spanish, Dutch, and German, are encouraging. The improvements on the low-resource languages, while smaller, are still notable considering the challenges involved.  The comparison to existing direct transfer methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) demonstrates a clear advantage.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors clearly describe the features used, including the novel wikification features. The explanation of how the cross-lingual wikifier was adapted for this task (handling n-grams, modifying the ranking model) is helpful.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies and the analysis regarding the impact of Wikipedia size (Table 3) provide valuable understanding of the model’s behavior and limitations. This demonstrates a thoughtful investigation beyond simply reporting performance numbers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of wikification features to cross-lingual NER is a reasonable idea, the core concept isn’t groundbreaking. Wikification and entity linking have been extensively studied, and the idea of using Wikipedia as a knowledge source for NER is not new (see related work). The primary contribution is demonstrating the *effectiveness* of this approach, rather than introducing a fundamentally novel technique.
2. **Over-Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The entire system is predicated on the quality and coverage of the *English* Wikipedia.  This introduces a bias, and the performance on languages with weak links to the English Wikipedia (as shown in the analysis) suffers. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a significant limitation. A more robust approach might involve grounding to multiple Wikipedia editions or utilizing other multilingual knowledge bases.
3. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While the comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are helpful, a more comprehensive comparison to more recent and stronger cross-lingual NER baselines is needed. More recent methods utilizing multilingual embeddings or pre-trained language models (e.g., multilingual BERT) would provide a more challenging test of the proposed approach. Simply stating that these methods "assume parallel text and some interaction with a native speaker" doesn’t justify not including them in the evaluation.
4. **Feature Complexity & Scalability:** The feature set, while clearly explained, is relatively complex. The n-gram grounding and inclusion of Freebase types and Wikipedia categories for multiple n-gram lengths could lead to a high-dimensional feature space. The authors don’t explicitly discuss the computational cost of this feature engineering process or its scalability to even more languages.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of your model compare to recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER methods that utilize multilingual embeddings (e.g., multilingual BERT) or other pre-trained language models?  Please provide a quantitative comparison.
2.  You mention that the quality of wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size. Have you explored strategies to mitigate the performance drop for low-resource languages, such as data augmentation or transfer learning from related high-resource languages?
3.  Can you provide more detail on the computational resources required for feature extraction and training, particularly concerning the n-gram grounding process?
4.  The paper highlights the dependence on English Wikipedia. Have you considered alternative approaches that do *not* rely on linking to English Wikipedia, such as creating multilingual embeddings directly from Wikipedia content or utilizing other multilingual knowledge bases?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 2 is slightly awkward, making it difficult to quickly grasp the key results.
*   The mention of “MISC” tags and their handling seems a bit convoluted. A clearer explanation of why these tags were removed and how the modification was performed would be helpful.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper. The core idea is reasonable, the experiments are thorough, and the analysis is insightful. However, the lack of significant novelty and the over-reliance on English Wikipedia are notable weaknesses. Addressing the questions above and providing a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art baselines would strengthen the paper considerably. I currently lean towards acceptance, but with a request for major revisions.
