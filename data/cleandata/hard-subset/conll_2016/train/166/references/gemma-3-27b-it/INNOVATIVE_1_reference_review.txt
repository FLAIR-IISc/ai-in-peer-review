This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground all n-grams to the English Wikipedia and utilize the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages compared to existing methods.

The primary contribution is the *concept* of systematically utilizing cross-lingual wikification not as a downstream task *following* NER, but as a foundational step *for* NER, generating features independent of the input language. While cross-lingual wikification and Wikipedia-based features aren’t new, framing it as a source of universally applicable features for direct transfer NER is genuinely novel. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation across a relatively diverse set of languages, including a good suite of low-resource languages, demonstrating the practical viability of the approach. Finally, the analysis regarding the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality is insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The reversal of the traditional NER-then-wikification pipeline is a bold and interesting idea. It challenges conventional wisdom and opens up new avenues for cross-lingual transfer learning. The paper correctly identifies the potential of wikification as a source of language-agnostic features.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently reported improvements, especially on low-resource languages where data scarcity is a major hurdle, lend credibility to the proposed approach. Outperforming methods requiring parallel data or native speaker interaction is a significant achievement.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimentation across multiple languages, including both high- and low-resource settings, strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, reducing interlanguage links) are particularly valuable for understanding the contribution of individual components.
4. **Clear Presentation & Analysis:** The paper is well-written and the experimental setup is clearly explained. The analysis section provides insightful observations regarding the impact of Wikipedia size and the benefits of multi-lingual training.  Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the core concept.
5. **Practicality & Scalability:** The method’s reliance solely on Wikipedia data makes it inherently scalable to many languages without requiring expensive annotated resources or parallel corpora.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Wikifier Robustness:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper doesn’t delve deeply into the *types* of errors the wikifier makes and how these errors impact NER performance. Are certain languages more prone to disambiguation errors?  How do NIL links affect the results? A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.
2. **Feature Engineering:** While the core idea is novel, the feature set used (Ratinov & Roth’s features + Wikipedia features) feels somewhat conservative. More exploration of potentially richer features derived from the wikified entities – e.g., relationships between entities in the knowledge graph – could lead to further improvements.
3. **Limited Comparison to Modern Approaches:** While the comparison to Täckström et al. is useful, the paper would benefit from a comparison to more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual transfer learning methods, particularly those utilizing pre-trained language models (e.g., multilingual BERT) and fine-tuning strategies. The field has moved rapidly, and a stronger benchmark would better position the work.
4. **Superficial Discussion of FreeBase:** The dependence on FreeBase is acknowledged, but the implications of FreeBase's known issues (completeness, curation) are not fully discussed.  The ablation study showing the importance of FreeBase is good, but doesn’t address the *quality* of the FreeBase data itself.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the cross-lingual wikifier, specifically focusing on the types of disambiguation errors it makes and their impact on NER performance for different languages?
2. Have you explored more sophisticated features derived from the wikified entities, such as relationships between entities in the knowledge graph? If so, what were the results?
3. How does your model compare to more recent cross-lingual transfer learning approaches that utilize pre-trained language models like multilingual BERT?  (Even a brief comparison would be valuable.)
4. Regarding the removal of MISC tags in the low-resource languages, could you elaborate on the rationale behind this decision and the potential impact on the overall evaluation?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 2 is a little dense and could be improved for readability.
*   The concluding remarks about future work are somewhat generic. More specific directions for future research would be appreciated.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor methodological shortcomings, this paper presents a novel and compelling approach to cross-lingual NER. The core idea is genuinely insightful, and the empirical results are encouraging, particularly for low-resource languages. I believe the novelty of the approach outweighs the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above. This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of cross-lingual NLP.
