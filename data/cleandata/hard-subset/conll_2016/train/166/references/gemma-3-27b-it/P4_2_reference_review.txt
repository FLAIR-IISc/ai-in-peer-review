This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea—using wikification to provide features for NER in low-resource languages—is sound and well-motivated, addressing a crucial problem in NLP. The authors claim the primary contributions are: 1) a method to utilize cross-lingual wikification for language-independent features in NER, 2) demonstrating significant performance gains on CoNLL datasets and achieving state-of-the-art results on low-resource languages, and 3) showing the utility of these features for both direct transfer and improving monolingual models.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification Feature Effectiveness:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of features derived from cross-lingual wikification for cross-lingual NER, especially in a direct transfer setting. The extensive experimentation across multiple languages, including low-resource ones, provides strong evidence supporting this claim.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing against multiple baselines (including a strong Täckström baseline) and conducting ablation studies. The analysis of Wikipedia size’s impact on feature quality adds valuable insight.
3. **Practical & Scalable Approach:**  The approach is relatively simple to implement (relying on existing wikification tools) and scalable to any language covered by Wikipedia, making it a practical solution for resource-poor languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance improvements, particularly on the low-resource languages, are compelling. Outperforming the state-of-the-art model that requires native speaker interaction is a substantial achievement. The numbers presented in the tables are clear and convincing.
2. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies regarding FreeBase and interlanguage link removal (Table 3) provide valuable insight into *why* the method works and the importance of different components. This adds credibility to the findings.
3. **Extensive Language Coverage:** Evaluating the model on a diverse set of languages, including those with non-Latin scripts, demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach and addresses a key challenge in cross-lingual NLP.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The experimental setup and results are presented in a logical and accessible manner. Figure 1 provides a helpful example of the wikification process.
5. **Analysis of Training Language Impact:** The investigation into the optimal training language for different target languages (Figure 2) offers valuable practical guidance and reveals interesting relationships between languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Wikification:**  The cross-lingual wikification component itself is not novel; the authors acknowledge this and appropriately cite relevant work (Tsai and Roth, 2016). The contribution lies in *how* wikification is used, but the fundamental wikification technology isn’t advanced by this paper.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The method relies heavily on linking to the English Wikipedia. This introduces a bias towards concepts well-represented in the English Wikipedia and may hinder performance for languages/concepts underrepresented there.  While acknowledged in the discussion, the limitations of this dependency aren’t fully explored.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** While the model itself is conceptually simple, the feature engineering process (especially with different n-gram lengths and combinations of Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types) could be complex to replicate and optimize for new languages.  A clearer discussion of the feature selection process would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While quantitative results are strong, a qualitative error analysis would provide a deeper understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses. Identifying common error patterns could guide future improvements.
5. **Limited Comparison to Recent SOTA:** Although the comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is appropriate for that time, the field has moved on. A comparison to more recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER models, particularly those employing pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., XLM-RoBERTa, mBERT), would be valuable to contextualize the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with wikifying all n-grams (up to n=4), especially for large documents? How does this impact the scalability of the approach?
2.  You mention a bias towards concepts well-represented in the English Wikipedia. Have you investigated whether performance is significantly lower for languages/domains with limited coverage in English Wikipedia? If so, what mitigation strategies could be employed?
3.  Given the dependence on Wikipedia's structure, how robust is the model to changes in Wikipedia's content or organization?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of wikification system? Did you experiment with other cross-lingual wikifiers, and if so, how did they compare?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of monolingual improvement could be strengthened by providing more specific examples of how the wikifier features contribute to better performance in that setting.
*   The phrase "direct transfer" is used frequently. Consider briefly defining this term more explicitly early in the paper for readers unfamiliar with the terminology.
*   There are a few minor grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., “WIKI-2” should be “Wiki-2”). A careful proofread would be beneficial.
