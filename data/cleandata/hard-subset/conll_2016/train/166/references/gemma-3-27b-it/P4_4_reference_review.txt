This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – using Wikipedia disambiguation as a feature source for NER across languages – is presented clearly and motivated well. The experiments are extensive, covering CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages, and demonstrate consistent performance gains from the proposed features. The contribution lies primarily in demonstrating the effectiveness of cross-lingual wikification features for direct transfer NER, and providing a comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse languages. A secondary contribution is the thorough analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on the quality of the resulting features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms comparable direct transfer methods on both CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The gains are statistically significant and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approach. The comprehensive experimental setup, including varying training languages and ablation studies, strengthens the validity of these results.
2. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The ablation studies presented in Tables 2 & 3 offer a detailed understanding of the contribution of each feature set (base, gazetteer, wikifier) and the impact of Wikipedia size. This analysis helps pinpoint the conditions under which the proposed method performs best and provides insights into the underlying mechanisms driving the performance gains. The investigation of training language choices further enhances the understanding of the model’s behavior.
3. **Novel Application of Wikification:**  While cross-lingual wikification is not a new technique, its application as a source of language-independent features for direct transfer NER is a novel contribution. The approach elegantly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora or extensive language-specific engineering, relying solely on the ubiquitous resource of Wikipedia. The framing of wikification as a feature *generation* process, rather than a pre-processing step, is a noteworthy shift in perspective.
4. **Comparison to Relevant Baselines:** The paper establishes a clear comparison to existing methods, including Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), demonstrating its advantage over previous work. Explicitly stating the limitations of prior approaches and highlighting the benefits of the proposed method is well done.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The individual components – NER with averaged perceptron, feature engineering from Wikipedia, and cross-lingual wikification – are not themselves novel. The primary novelty lies in the *combination* of these components and the demonstration of their effectiveness in the specific context of direct transfer NER. This somewhat limits the overall impact of the work.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The model’s reliance on linking to the English Wikipedia introduces a potential bias. While the authors acknowledge the impact of Wikipedia size, the inherent English-centric perspective could limit the model’s performance on languages with significantly different conceptual structures or knowledge representation in their respective Wikipedias. This wasn't fully investigated.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, appears relatively complex.  The description of feature generation (Section 3) is dense, and the number of different feature types could make the system difficult to adapt or extend. A more streamlined feature representation might improve practicality.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the ablation studies provide valuable insights, a more detailed error analysis of the model’s predictions (e.g., identifying common error patterns in low-resource languages) would strengthen the paper. This would help understand the limitations of the approach and suggest directions for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The authors mention the limitation of using English Wikipedia due to potential bias. Did you explore methods to mitigate this bias, such as using a multilingual embedding space or incorporating information from multiple Wikipedia languages simultaneously?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the wikification process, particularly for long documents or languages with large Wikipedia entries? Is this a significant bottleneck for real-time applications?
3.  You observe that gazetteer features are less effective in low-resource languages. Did you investigate alternative methods for creating or adapting gazetteers in these settings, such as leveraging machine translation or cross-lingual entity linking?
4.  In Figure 2, Spanish appears as the best training language for Yoruba. Can you offer an explanation for this observation? Are there linguistic or cultural factors that might contribute to this phenomenon?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The formatting of Table 2 is somewhat cramped. Consider using a wider table layout for improved readability.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough but could be slightly more focused on the most directly relevant prior studies.
*   It's unclear what the “W.−FB−50% inter.” and “W.−FB−90% inter.” rows in Table 3 are measuring exactly - a clearer description would be helpful.
