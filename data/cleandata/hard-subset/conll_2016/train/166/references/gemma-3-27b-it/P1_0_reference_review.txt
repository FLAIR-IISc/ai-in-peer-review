## Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for a NER model trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over baseline direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and achieve state-of-the-art results on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). They also show the benefit of these features for monolingual models and multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Feature Engineering with Cross-Lingual Wikification:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that features derived from cross-lingual wikification, specifically Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, are surprisingly effective for cross-lingual NER, particularly in a direct transfer setting. This is a practical and insightful application of wikification beyond its traditional role as a downstream task.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Languages:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging set of low-resource languages, highlighting the potential of this approach for languages where annotated data is scarce.  This is a particularly valuable contribution.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and comparisons to relevant baselines, providing strong evidence for the efficacy of their proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Wikification:**  The central idea of using cross-lingual wikification *as a feature generator* for NER, rather than a pre- or post-processing step, is genuinely novel and well-motivated. It flips the traditional pipeline and demonstrates that disambiguation itself provides valuable signals for NER.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative results across multiple languages are compelling. The improvements over baseline methods are substantial, particularly on the low-resource languages where the impact is most significant.  The consistent performance across the CoNLL languages also builds confidence in the robustness of the approach.
3. **Detailed Ablation Studies & Analysis:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, varying interlanguage links) provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the model and the characteristics of the wikification process.  This level of analysis demonstrates a deep understanding of the system and helps identify potential areas for improvement.
4. **Practicality & Scalability:**  The method requires only a multilingual Wikipedia dump, making it readily applicable to a wide range of languages without the need for parallel corpora or language-specific resources.  This is a significant advantage in a cross-lingual setting.
5. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of detailed results and discussion facilitates reproducibility and understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core NER Model:** The core NER model used (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) is a well-established approach. The primary novelty lies in the feature engineering, not in a new NER architecture. While effective, this limits the overall contribution in terms of algorithmic advancement.
2. **Dependency on English Wikipedia:** The model’s reliance on the English Wikipedia as the grounding point introduces a potential bias. The quality and completeness of the English Wikipedia may not perfectly represent all concepts and entities across all languages.  This could lead to suboptimal performance for languages that are significantly different from English in terms of cultural context or knowledge representation.
3. **Wikification Errors:** The authors acknowledge imperfect disambiguation. While the experiments demonstrate that the model is robust to some degree of error, the impact of more significant wikification errors on NER performance is not fully explored.  A more detailed error analysis of the wikification process itself would be beneficial.
4. **Feature Combination:** The paper demonstrates adding wikifier features improves performance, but doesn't dive deeply into *why* certain features interact well with others. A more nuanced analysis of feature interactions could reveal further optimization opportunities.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Bias in English Wikipedia:**  How does the reliance on the English Wikipedia potentially affect performance for languages with significant cultural or conceptual differences from English? Have you considered strategies to mitigate this bias, such as using a multilingual knowledge graph?
2. **Wikification Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the cross-lingual wikifier and their impact on NER performance?  Specifically, are certain types of errors (e.g., linking to incorrect categories) more detrimental than others?
3. **Feature Interaction:** Have you explored techniques for identifying and leveraging synergistic interactions between the wikifier features and the base NER features?
4. **Scalability of Wikification:**  How does the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram scale with document length and the size of the Wikipedia?  Are there potential optimizations to address scalability concerns for very large documents?




**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is comprehensive.
* Table 2 is very helpful for understanding the data and results.
* The conclusion clearly summarizes the contributions and outlines potential future directions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed method is novel, effective, and practical. While the core NER model itself is not new, the innovative application of cross-lingual wikification as a feature engineering technique is significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
