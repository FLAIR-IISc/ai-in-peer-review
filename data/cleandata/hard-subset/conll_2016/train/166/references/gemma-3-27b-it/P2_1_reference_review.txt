## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground every n-gram in a document to the English Wikipedia, then utilize the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over baselines on both CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages, arguing that their approach is particularly effective in scenarios with limited annotated data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The primary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of using cross-lingual wikification to *create* informative, language-independent features for NER. This shifts the perspective from wikification being a downstream task *following* NER, to being a beneficial pre-processing step *for* NER.
2. **Strong Empirical Results, Particularly in Low-Resource Settings:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments, demonstrating consistent performance gains across various languages, notably in low-resource scenarios where traditional methods struggle. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016), which requires native speaker interaction, is particularly compelling.
3. **Thorough Analysis of Feature Importance:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing Freebase queries, varying interlanguage link density) provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components of their system and highlight the dependence on Wikipedia quality.

---

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:**  The idea of utilizing cross-lingual wikification in this way is genuinely novel. It's a clever adaptation of existing tools (wikification, Wikipedia) to solve a challenging problem in cross-lingual NLP.  The insight that disambiguation itself provides useful signal for NER is well-supported by the experimental results.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a diverse range of experiments, including monolingual tests, direct transfer across multiple languages, and analysis on low-resource languages. This thoroughness strengthens the claim that the proposed method is robust and generalizable.  The comparison to existing methods, particularly Täckström et al. and Zhang et al., provides a strong benchmark.
3. **Clear Explanation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivations behind the work, the proposed method, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the process.
4. **Detailed Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies in section 4.3 are particularly strong.  They allow for a nuanced understanding of which components of the system contribute most to performance and the limitations of the approach.  The exploration of Wikipedia size is insightful.
5. **Practical Relevance:** This work directly addresses a critical challenge in NLP – adapting NER models to languages with limited labeled data. The method is relatively simple to implement (leveraging existing wikification tools) and has the potential for wide applicability.

---

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Wikifier Quality:** While the authors acknowledge this, the performance is inherently tied to the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier.  Errors in wikification will propagate as incorrect features, impacting NER performance. The paper doesn't extensively analyze the *types* of wikification errors and their impact on results. A deeper dive into error analysis would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Feature Set:** The base features used are somewhat limited compared to state-of-the-art monolingual NER models. While the focus is on language-independent features, it’s possible that incorporating more sophisticated (language-specific) features alongside the wikifier features could further improve performance. The paper mentions not using POS tags due to difficulties obtaining them for all languages, but exploring methods for cross-lingual POS tag projection could be considered.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis on Low-Resource Languages:**  The results on low-resource languages are encouraging, but the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the model struggles in these languages (e.g., script issues, lack of Wikipedia coverage) would be valuable for future research.  The removal of MISC tags seems like a workaround, and its impact on overall performance isn’t fully discussed.
4. **English Wikipedia Bias:** The model is heavily reliant on the English Wikipedia as the central grounding point. This introduces a potential bias, as concepts and entities may be represented differently in other cultures and languages.  Exploring alternative grounding strategies or multilingual knowledge bases could be a direction for future work.



---

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Wikification Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the cross-lingual wikifier and their impact on NER performance? Specifically, what types of entities are most often mis-wikified, and how does this affect precision and recall?
2.  **Feature Engineering:** Have you experimented with incorporating other, potentially language-specific, features alongside the wikifier features? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
3.  **Low-Resource Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the model's performance on the low-resource languages? What are the most common types of errors, and what strategies might be used to address them?
4. **Alternative Knowledge Bases:** Have you considered using other multilingual knowledge bases (e.g., Wikidata) as a grounding point instead of solely relying on the English Wikipedia? What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of this approach?




**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Table 2 is a little dense. Providing a key or more explanation would improve readability.
* The reference to "Figure 2" in the conclusion is a typo – it should refer to Figure 1.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and insightful approach to cross-lingual NER. The experimental results are compelling, particularly in low-resource settings. While there are some weaknesses, they are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
