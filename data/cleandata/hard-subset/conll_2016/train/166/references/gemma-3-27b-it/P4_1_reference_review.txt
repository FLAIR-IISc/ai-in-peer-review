This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea – utilizing wikification as a source of features for NER, particularly in low-resource scenarios – is interesting and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing direct transfer methods and state-of-the-art models on CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages. The most significant contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation, showcasing the effectiveness of wikifier features across a diverse set of languages and experimental settings. A second, valuable contribution is the demonstration that the proposed method *improves* even monolingual models, suggesting the features are generally informative. Finally, the detailed analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on feature quality is insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results. The consistent improvements on both CoNLL languages and, crucially, low-resource languages demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approach. The comparisons against established baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) are thorough and convincingly show the benefits of the method. The inclusion of five low-resource languages is a notable strength.
2. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase queries, reducing interlanguage links) are well-designed and provide a nuanced understanding of how the different components of the model contribute to performance. The analysis regarding Wikipedia size and its impact on feature quality is particularly valuable, offering actionable insights.
3. **Novel Application of Wikification:** The paper reframes wikification from a downstream task to a crucial feature engineering step for NER. This perspective is original and enables a direct transfer approach without relying on parallel corpora or extensive language-specific knowledge. The systematic grounding of n-grams, rather than just identified mentions, is a reasonable and effective approach.
4. **Demonstrated Generalizability:** The success of the method across languages with varying scripts (Latin vs. non-Latin) and resource levels suggests a degree of robustness and generalizability. The results with Bengali and Tamil, despite their reliance solely on wikifier features, are particularly encouraging.
5. **Clear Presentation and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The experimental setup and results are clearly presented, and the discussion is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of cross-lingual wikification and NER is novel, the individual components themselves are not. Cross-lingual wikification and direct transfer NER are both well-established research areas. The paper does not introduce a new wikification algorithm or a fundamentally new NER architecture. The innovation lies in the specific application and feature engineering, rather than breakthrough algorithmic advances.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The reliance on the English Wikipedia as the grounding point is a potential limitation. While practical given current resources, it introduces a bias towards English-centric knowledge representation. The performance degradation observed when removing FreeBase queries (Table 3) highlights this dependency. A future direction could explore grounding to a multilingual knowledge graph.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, is relatively complex. The inclusion of various features (base, gazetteer, wikifier) and their combinations might make it challenging to interpret the precise contribution of each feature. A more targeted feature selection or ablation study focusing on the wikifier features themselves could provide further insights.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the model fails to identify named entities could reveal patterns and guide future improvements. Understanding the types of errors made could illuminate weaknesses in the wikification process or the feature representation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the modification to the wikifier system to handle n-grams (Section 3.3), could you provide more details on the impact of this change on wikification accuracy? Did you observe any increase in disambiguation errors due to this modification?
2.  The paper highlights the importance of Wikipedia size. Have you investigated the impact of Wikipedia *quality* (e.g., completeness, accuracy) on the performance of the model?
3.  In Section 4.4, you discuss training on multiple languages. Could you elaborate on the strategies used to balance the training data across different languages when training on a mixed dataset? Were any weighting schemes employed?
4.  You mention that the existing features are designed for English. Have you considered adapting or augmenting the feature set to better capture language-specific characteristics in the target languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 2 is a useful visualization, but the legend could be more clearly presented.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough but could benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of existing approaches.
*   Consider adding a section discussing the computational cost of the cross-lingual wikification process.
