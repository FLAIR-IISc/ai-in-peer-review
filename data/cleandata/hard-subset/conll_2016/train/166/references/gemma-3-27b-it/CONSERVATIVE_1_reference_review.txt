This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline methods on CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical demonstration of the utility of cross-lingual wikification features for direct transfer NER. While the core idea isn't entirely novel (linking to knowledge bases for NER is well-established), the authors successfully integrate this into a direct transfer setting and demonstrate its effectiveness across a range of languages, including low-resource scenarios. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies demonstrating the importance of the FreeBase component and analysis of performance based on Wikipedia size. Finally, the experiments showing benefits of multi-lingual training are a reasonable extension and a positive result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements over baseline methods on multiple datasets, including challenging low-resource languages. The improvements are statistically significant in many cases, indicating a real effect of the proposed features. The inclusion of results from Zhang et al. (2016) provides a valuable comparison point, particularly for the low-resource languages.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a relatively thorough evaluation, including ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of different features (wikifier, gazetteers), analysis of Wikipedia size's impact, and experiments with multi-lingual training. This systematic approach increases confidence in the results.
3. **Practical Approach:** The method is appealingly practical, relying solely on readily available resources (Wikipedia) and avoiding the need for parallel corpora or language-specific rules. This makes it potentially applicable to a wide range of languages.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. Figure 1 effectively illustrates the core idea of grounding words to Wikipedia.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The underlying concept of utilizing knowledge base information (in this case, Wikipedia) for NER is not new. The key innovation lies in applying this within a *direct transfer* framework using *cross-lingual wikification*. However, the direct transfer approach itself is also well-established, and the specific technique of using wikification features feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of related work, acknowledging the existing body of research that leverages knowledge bases for NER.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The entire system is reliant on the English Wikipedia as the central knowledge source. This introduces a potential bias, as the English Wikipedia's coverage and categorization schemes may not perfectly align with other languages and cultures. The ablation study in Table 3 highlights this vulnerability, demonstrating performance degradation when links to the English Wikipedia are reduced. A potential improvement would be to explore strategies for leveraging multiple Wikipedia editions or incorporating language-specific knowledge bases.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while comprehensive, relies heavily on hand-engineered features (e.g., gazetteers, POS tags, word forms). In an era of end-to-end deep learning, this approach feels somewhat dated. It's unclear whether the performance gains are attributable to the wikification features specifically or to the overall quality of the engineered feature set. A comparison against a strong, modern deep learning baseline (e.g., a multilingual BERT-based NER model) would be valuable.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails and *why* would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific entity types or linguistic phenomena that the model struggles with? Are there systematic errors related to the quality of the wikification process?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The ablation study in Table 3 shows a significant drop in performance when FreeBase querying is disabled. Could you provide more details about the types of entities for which FreeBase information is most crucial? Are there specific entity types where the model relies heavily on FreeBase types for accurate prediction?
2.  You mention the potential for combining your approach with parallel projection or automatic data generation from Wikipedia. Could you elaborate on how these techniques could be integrated with your current model? What specific challenges would need to be addressed?
3.   You state that lexical features become weak in the cross-lingual setting. Is this due to a lack of lexical overlap, or something else? Could character-level embeddings be used to alleviate this issue and improve performance in low-resource settings?
4.  The performance on Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil is notably lower. Given the reliance on English Wikipedia, could exploring methods to incorporate language-specific knowledge bases or resources for these languages improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Zhang et al. (2016)" is a bit vague. Please provide a complete citation.
*   The motivation for choosing n=4 for n-gram grounding isn't clearly explained. Was this determined empirically, or is it a standard practice in wikification?
