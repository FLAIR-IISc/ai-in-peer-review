## Review of “Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification”

**Summary:** This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, and then utilize the associated Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over direct transfer baselines on CoNLL datasets and achieve state-of-the-art results on several low-resource languages, without relying on parallel corpora or native speaker interaction.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that cross-lingual wikification is not merely a downstream task for NER, but a valuable source of language-independent features that significantly boost cross-lingual transfer performance. This is a novel and impactful application.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Low-Resource Languages:**  The consistent improvements achieved on the challenging low-resource languages, particularly outperforming a baseline requiring substantial human interaction, is a significant contribution. This showcases the practical value of the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies (analyzing feature contributions) and investigation of the impact of Wikipedia size, demonstrating a deep understanding of the proposed model and its limitations.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The paper's central idea – treating wikification as a feature engineering step for cross-lingual NER – is a genuinely novel contribution.  It reframes the traditional NER-then-wikification pipeline, and demonstrates the benefits of this reversed perspective. The observation that disambiguation is useful for NER, not just the other way around, is insightful.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistent improvements across multiple languages (both resource-rich and resource-poor) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison with competitive baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.) clearly demonstrates the advantages of the wikifier features.
3. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The ablation studies (section 4.3) are particularly valuable. They clearly show the contribution of the various feature sets (base, gazetteers, wikifier) and highlight the importance of Wikipedia size for wikifier feature quality.  This demonstrates a good understanding of the model’s inner workings.
4. **Practicality and Scalability:**  The method relies solely on publicly available Wikipedia dumps, making it highly practical and easily scalable to numerous languages. The avoidance of parallel corpora or native speaker expertise is a major advantage for real-world applications.
5. **Thorough Experiment Design:** The extensive experiments including multilingual training, and the careful ablation studies indicate a rigorous and thorough evaluation of the proposed method.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Wikification Errors:** While the paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation (Figure 1), it lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the cross-lingual wikifier and their impact on NER performance.  Understanding *why* the wikifier fails in certain cases could lead to further improvements.  For example, are there specific entity types or languages where the wikifier consistently struggles?
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, appears somewhat complex (especially the inclusion of n-gram types).  A more streamlined feature set that retains the core benefits might be desirable for practical implementation. While the authors justify the inclusion of n-grams, the impact of limiting ‘n’ beyond 4 isn’t investigated.
3. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:**  The reliance on the English Wikipedia as the central hub raises potential biases. Entities that are poorly represented or have different conceptualizations in English might be unfairly penalized. While the paper mitigates this somewhat with Freebase types, it would be valuable to discuss this limitation further and explore potential alternatives (e.g., multilingual Freebase).
4. **Monolingual Improvement Not Fully Explained:** The paper mentions that the features improve monolingual models as well, but the explanation feels somewhat cursory. A more detailed analysis of *why* the language-independent features benefit even monolingual NER would strengthen the argument. Is it primarily helping with ambiguous entities, or providing complementary information?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Wikification Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the cross-lingual wikifier? What types of entities or languages are most prone to errors, and how do these errors impact NER performance?
2. **Feature Set Simplification:** Have you explored simplifying the feature set (e.g., reducing the n-gram size or combining features)?  Would such simplification significantly impact performance?
3. **Bias Mitigation:**  How do you address the potential biases introduced by relying on the English Wikipedia? Have you considered exploring alternatives, such as leveraging multilingual Freebase directly?
4. **Monolingual Improvement Mechanism:**  Could you elaborate on *why* the language-independent features improve monolingual NER? Is it primarily due to disambiguation of ambiguous entities, or are there other factors at play?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
* The inclusion of both CoNLL languages and low-resource languages is a strength.
* Table 2 is a valuable resource, providing a clear overview of data sizes and experimental results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The novel application of cross-lingual wikification, the compelling experimental results, and the thorough analysis all contribute to its high quality. While some weaknesses remain (particularly regarding wikification error analysis and feature set simplification), these do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above.
