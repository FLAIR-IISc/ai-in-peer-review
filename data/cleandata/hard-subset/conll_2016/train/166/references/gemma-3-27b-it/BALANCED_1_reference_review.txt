This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – utilizing Wikipedia as a bridge for transferring NER capabilities across languages – is promising and well-motivated, given the resource scarcity for many languages. The approach is thoroughly investigated through experiments on both CoNLL languages and a set of low-resource languages.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Wikification Features for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper demonstrates that incorporating features derived from cross-lingual wikification significantly boosts the performance of a direct transfer NER model, particularly for low-resource languages. This is the strongest contribution, as it presents a practical and effective method for leveraging readily available resources (Wikipedia) for a challenging NLP task.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a broad experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both standard CoNLL datasets and several low-resource languages. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the validity of the proposed approach and provides valuable insights into its performance characteristics. The ablation studies (e.g., removing FreeBase information) are particularly insightful.
3. **Analysis of Wikipedia Quality & Multi-Language Training:** The investigation into the relationship between Wikipedia size and wikification feature quality, alongside the exploration of multi-language training, offer a nuanced understanding of the model’s dependencies and potential improvements. This demonstrates a strong analytical approach beyond simply presenting results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model consistently outperforms comparable direct transfer methods and, importantly, the state-of-the-art on the low-resource languages, even considering the resource requirements of those baselines. The gains are substantial enough to be considered significant.
2. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility. Figure 1 is an excellent illustration of the core idea.
3. **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work in cross-lingual NER, covering projection, Wikipedia-based approaches, and direct transfer, positioning their work effectively within the existing literature.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The proposed approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia, eliminating the need for parallel corpora or native speaker interaction, making it highly practical for low-resource language scenarios.
5. **Detailed Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies, specifically those around the FreeBase information and the removal of interlanguage links, are crucial for understanding *why* the method works and identifying potential limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** While leveraging English Wikipedia is a strength in terms of accessibility, the model’s performance is implicitly tied to the quality and coverage of the *English* Wikipedia for all languages. This creates a potential bottleneck, and the paper could benefit from a discussion of how biases or limitations in the English Wikipedia might impact results.  The analysis in Section 4.4 touches on this, but a broader discussion would be valuable.
2. **Limited Exploration of Wikifier Alternatives:** The paper relies on a single cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016). While this choice is justified, a brief exploration of other wikification tools and their potential impact on performance would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while comprehensive, is somewhat complex and relies heavily on pre-existing English-centric features (Ratinov and Roth, 2009). It’s not immediately clear how easily these features could be adapted or extended to other languages with significantly different linguistic structures.
4. **Modest Gains with Multi-Language Training:** While the multi-language training experiments show a positive trend, the gains are relatively modest. A more in-depth analysis of which language combinations are most beneficial and why would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier? Have you explored the impact of using different confidence thresholds for the wikification links?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases present in the English Wikipedia and how they might affect the performance of the model for different languages and entity types?
3.  The paper mentions that the features are designed for English. Could you discuss potential approaches for adapting or extending these features to better suit languages with significantly different linguistic properties?
4.  You've shown that training on multiple languages can be helpful.  Is there a point of diminishing returns – a point where adding more source languages doesn't significantly improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Table 2 could be more consistent (e.g., abbreviations for language names).
*   In the introduction, briefly mentioning the types of errors the model makes would provide a more complete picture of its limitations.
*   The phrase "Latin Script Non-Latin Script" in Table 2 is somewhat unclear. Perhaps "Script Type" would be more informative.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The authors have conducted a thorough experimental evaluation and provided insightful analysis. While there are some minor weaknesses, the contributions are significant and the paper is well-deserving of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
