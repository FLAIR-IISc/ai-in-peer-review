## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia and utilize the corresponding FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and achieve state-of-the-art results on several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) in a direct transfer setting – training solely on English data.  The authors also explore the benefits of incorporating these features into monolingual models and multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Strong Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that grounding text in Wikipedia via a cross-lingual wikifier provides valuable, language-independent signals for NER, particularly in low-resource scenarios.  This moves beyond simply using Wikipedia as a gazetteer.
2. **Effective Direct Transfer without Parallel Data:** The work presents a competitive approach to cross-lingual NER *without* relying on parallel corpora or language-specific rules, addressing a significant challenge in NLP for low-resource languages.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both standard CoNLL languages and truly low-resource languages with different script types. This breadth is valuable for understanding the generalizability of the approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Intuition:** The central idea of using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-agnostic features is intuitive and presents a novel perspective. Framing wikification *as a feature engineering step* rather than a prerequisite to NER is a key insight.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results are compelling. The consistent improvement over baseline methods on multiple languages, especially the SOTA performance on the low-resource languages, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies effectively highlight the contribution of the different feature sets.
3. **Well-Defined Experimental Setup & Thorough Analysis:** The experimental setup is clearly defined, and the authors conduct a reasonable number of experiments to validate their hypothesis. The analysis regarding Wikipedia size and the effect of removing Freebase information is particularly insightful.
4. **Practical Applicability:**  The reliance on readily available Wikipedia data and a publicly available wikifier makes this approach highly practical for application to a wide range of languages without requiring expensive linguistic resources.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:**  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setup.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Wikification Errors:** While the paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation (Figure 1), there’s limited discussion of how *specific* wikification errors impact performance.  A deeper analysis of common error types and their effect on NER accuracy would strengthen the findings.  Are certain entity types more susceptible to mis-linking?
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The approach is inherently tied to the English Wikipedia. While this leverages a massive resource, it introduces a potential bias.  Exploring alternative anchor languages or methods for creating multilingual knowledge graphs could mitigate this dependency. The English centric approach could be a major limitation for languages structurally very different from English.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The described feature set, while effective, appears relatively complex, involving various combinations of Freebase types, Wikipedia categories, and n-gram features.  The authors don't discuss the computational cost of generating and processing these features, or potential avenues for feature selection and simplification.
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is fair, more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual transfer learning methods (e.g., those utilizing multilingual pre-trained language models like mBERT or XLM-RoBERTa) should be considered as baselines.  A direct comparison would better position the contribution of this work.  (Though this may be difficult if those models are not directly comparable regarding the no-parallel data constraint).




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of common errors made by the wikifier and their impact on NER performance? Specifically, are certain entity types more frequently mis-linked, and how does this affect the overall accuracy?
2. **Bias of English Wikipedia:**  Have you considered the potential biases inherent in relying solely on the English Wikipedia and its influence on NER performance in languages with different cultural or structural characteristics?  What steps could be taken to mitigate these biases?
3. **Computational Cost:** Can you provide some insights into the computational cost of generating and processing the extensive feature set described in the paper?  Are there potential strategies for feature selection or simplification to reduce this cost?
4. **Comparison to Modern Cross-Lingual Models:**  How would your model perform compared to more recent cross-lingual transfer learning approaches based on multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa), considering your constraint of not using parallel data?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure showing different training/test language pairs (Figure 2) is very helpful for visualizing the transfer performance.
*   The discussion of removing Freebase information and interlanguage links in Table 3 is particularly insightful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER.  While there are some weaknesses regarding analysis of errors and comparison to state-of-the-art methods, the compelling results and practical applicability of the proposed technique make it a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
