## Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

**Summary:**

This paper presents a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia and use the resulting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for a NER model trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate improved performance on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) compared to baseline methods. They also show improvements to monolingual models with these features and demonstrate the benefit of multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that grounding text in Wikipedia via cross-lingual wikification provides surprisingly effective language-independent features for NER, especially in low-resource scenarios. This is a practical and relatively simple approach that bypasses the need for parallel corpora or complex linguistic transfer learning.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Diverse Languages:** The evaluation is a strong point of the paper.  Testing on both established CoNLL languages *and* a set of genuinely low-resource languages, including those with non-Latin scripts, lends significant weight to the findings.
3. **Detailed Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies regarding the use of Freebase and interlanguage links are informative and reveal important insights into the feature contribution of the wikification process.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show improvements over existing methods, particularly in the low-resource settings where cross-lingual transfer is most crucial.  The gains on languages like Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil, which are notoriously difficult for NLP, are particularly noteworthy.
2. **Practical and Scalable Approach:** The method relies on readily available resources (Wikipedia) and a relatively straightforward feature engineering process.  It avoids the complexities of creating parallel corpora or language-specific rules, making it highly practical and scalable to many languages.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation studies (Section 4.4) demonstrate a solid understanding of the method’s inner workings.  Investigating the impact of Freebase and interlanguage links is crucial for understanding *why* the approach works and how it can be improved.  The analysis of training language combinations (Figure 2) is also insightful.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the approach. The use of Figure 1 to illustrate the wikification process is effective.
5. **Addresses a Real-World Problem:**  The paper tackles a significant challenge in NLP – applying NER to languages with limited annotated resources.  The proposed solution has clear potential for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Wikification:** While the *application* of cross-lingual wikification to NER features is novel and effective, the underlying cross-lingual wikification component itself is not a primary contribution.  The authors rely on existing systems (Tsai and Roth, 2016) and do not propose any advancements in wikification technology. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the existing wikifier, especially its error rate, would be valuable.
2. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The model relies on carefully engineered features. While effective, it’s not clear how easily these features could be adapted to other NER models or tasks.  A discussion of the potential for automated feature learning, or the use of more modern feature representations (e.g., embeddings), would be beneficial.
3. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The method is inherently tied to the English Wikipedia as a central hub. While this isn’t necessarily a flaw, it raises questions about potential biases present in the English Wikipedia and their impact on NER performance in other languages.  This dependency should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed. The paper touches on this slightly with the Wikipedia size analysis, but could be expanded.
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are relevant, it would be valuable to compare the proposed method against more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual NER approaches that utilize techniques like multilingual embeddings or transfer learning with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, XLM-RoBERTa).



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the chosen wikifier disambiguates to the *English* Wikipedia. What percentage of wikification attempts are successful (i.e., link to a valid English Wikipedia page) across the different languages tested?  How does the success rate correlate with performance?
2.  You mention the potential for biases within the English Wikipedia. Could you elaborate on how these biases might manifest in the NER results (e.g., over-representation of certain entity types, inaccurate linking for certain geographic regions)?
3.  Have you explored using multilingual embeddings or pre-trained language models in conjunction with the wikifier features?  If so, what were the results?  If not, why not?
4.  Regarding the limitation of the existing wikifier, what is the error analysis of the cases that it fails on? How are those errors reflected in the NER performance?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is mostly good, but a consistent style for referencing (e.g., consistently using et al.) would improve readability.
*   The description of the training/test language matrix (Figure 2) could be made more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a practical and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. While the core wikification component isn't novel, the application of these features to NER, along with the thorough evaluation and analysis, is a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
