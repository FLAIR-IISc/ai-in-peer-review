## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features for NER. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia and utilize the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features for training an NER model. The authors demonstrate strong performance on both CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), outperforming several baselines including state-of-the-art approaches that rely on parallel data or native speaker interaction.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Strong Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly shows that leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate features is a highly effective approach for zero-shot cross-lingual NER, particularly for low-resource languages. This is the primary contribution.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including both resource-rich and low-resource scenarios. The inclusion of five low-resource languages is particularly valuable.
3. **Analysis of Feature Importance & Wikipedia Size:**  The paper goes beyond simply presenting results and provides insightful analysis of the importance of Freebase information and the impact of Wikipedia size on performance, offering valuable practical insights.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented are compelling. The model consistently outperforms strong baselines, especially on the challenging low-resource languages.  The gains over methods requiring parallel data or native speaker input are significant, demonstrating the practical benefit of this approach. The tables are clear and the comparisons are well-defined.
2. **Novel and Practical Approach:** The idea of shifting the focus from NER-to-Wikification to Wikification-to-NER is a clever one.  It leverages the wealth of information contained in Wikipedia in a novel way and offers a practical solution for cross-lingual transfer without requiring expensive parallel resources. The method doesn't rely on complex linguistic pipelines or language-specific rules.
3. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing Freebase information, reducing interlanguage links) provide valuable insights into *why* the model works and which components are most important.  This demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying mechanisms.  The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance is particularly strong.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation Setup:** The authors clearly define their experimental setup, datasets, and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of experiments combining multiple training languages further strengthens the analysis.
5. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly explained, and the experimental results are presented in a logical and organized manner.  Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the core concept.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of cross-lingual wikification with NER is novel and effective, the individual components (cross-lingual wikification, feature-based NER) are well-established. The core technical novelty lies in the application and demonstration of effectiveness of this specific combination.  The authors acknowledge related work, but could more explicitly discuss how their work builds upon and differentiates from prior research.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The model’s reliance on the English Wikipedia as a central hub introduces a potential bias. While the paper acknowledges this and mitigates it to some extent with Freebase integration, the effectiveness of the approach is inherently tied to the completeness and accuracy of the English Wikipedia's coverage of entities across different languages. Further investigation into the impact of this dependency would be valuable.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while effective, appears somewhat hand-engineered and could benefit from more automated feature selection or learning techniques. While the authors justify their choice of features based on prior work, exploring learned features could potentially lead to further improvements.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the model fails and *why* would provide valuable insights for future research.  For example, are there specific entity types or languages where the model struggles more?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the cross-lingual wikifier itself?  Have you experimented with different wikifiers, and if so, what impact did they have on the results?
2.  You mention that the Freebase component is particularly important for languages with smaller Wikipedia sizes.  Could you elaborate on strategies for mitigating the reliance on Freebase for languages where Freebase coverage is limited?
3.  Given the reliance on English Wikipedia, have you observed any biases in the model's performance towards entities that are well-represented in the English Wikipedia but less prominent in other cultures or languages?  Could you provide some examples?
4. The paper mentions the possibility of combining this approach with parallel projection.  Have you conducted any preliminary experiments in that direction, and what were the initial findings?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be slightly more focused on the intersection of wikification and NER.
*   A more detailed description of the cross-lingual wikifier system used (Tsai and Roth, 2016) would be helpful for reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation and provided insightful analysis. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and demonstration of their effectiveness, particularly in low-resource scenarios, is a significant contribution.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
