## Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as features for NER. The authors demonstrate significant performance gains over baseline direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and achieve state-of-the-art results on several low-resource languages. They also show improvements to monolingual models by incorporating these features and highlight the benefits of multi-lingual training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective application of cross-lingual wikification for NER:** The paper demonstrates a novel and effective way to utilize cross-lingual wikification *not* as a post-processing step (entity linking), but as a feature engineering technique for improving NER performance directly. This is a substantial shift in perspective.
2. **Strong empirical results on low-resource languages:** The reported results on languages like Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil are compelling, particularly given the lack of reliance on parallel data or native speaker interaction, beating a strong baseline.
3. **Comprehensive experimental analysis:** The paper includes thorough experimentation, ablation studies, and analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size and multi-lingual training, strengthening the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The core idea of utilizing wikification as a feature source for NER is genuinely novel. Many cross-lingual transfer methods rely on parallel corpora or complex alignment techniques. This approach is comparatively simple, elegant, and scalable, relying only on readily available Wikipedia data.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The consistent performance improvements across multiple languages, especially the noteworthy results on low-resource languages, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The performance gains on the CoNLL languages are substantial, and the SOTA results on the low-resource languages are particularly impressive.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation studies (e.g., removing Freebase information, varying inter-language link density) clearly demonstrate the importance of different components of the proposed method and provide valuable insights into its workings. The analysis of the influence of Wikipedia size is insightful and highlights the limitations and potential improvements of the approach.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the method, experiments, and results. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the wikification process.
5. **Practicality & Scalability:**  The method is relatively easy to implement and scale to new languages as it requires only a Wikipedia dump. This makes it highly practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The reliance on grounding all n-grams to English Wikipedia introduces a potential bias and information bottleneck. While the authors acknowledge this, the consequences aren’t fully explored. Specifically, the quality of the English Wikipedia and its coverage of concepts relevant to other languages could limit performance.  The assumption that English Wikipedia provides a comprehensive and unbiased representation of all concepts is questionable.
2. **Limited Comparison to Recent Advances:** While the authors compare to relevant baselines (Täckström et al., Zhang et al.), the field of cross-lingual NER is rapidly evolving, especially with the rise of pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa). A comparison to these state-of-the-art models would be crucial to properly contextualize the contribution of this work.  It’s important to understand how this wikification-based approach performs *relative* to models that have learned cross-lingual representations.
3. **Wikifier Error Propagation:** The paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation by the wikifier, but doesn’t thoroughly analyze the impact of incorrect linking on NER performance.  How sensitive is the model to errors in the wikification step? This needs further investigation.
4. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** While the overall approach is elegant, the feature set described in Section 3.3 seems complex and relies heavily on specific details of the wikifier output.  The optimal configuration of these features might require significant tuning for each language.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **English Wikipedia Bias:** How do you think the inherent bias and limitations of English Wikipedia affect the performance of your model, particularly for languages with very different cultural contexts or knowledge representation? Do you have any plans to mitigate this bias (e.g., by incorporating features from other Wikipedia languages)?
2. **Comparison to Pre-trained Models:**  Have you experimented with incorporating the features proposed in this paper into a pre-trained multilingual language model (e.g., fine-tuning mBERT with your wikifier features)?  How do the results compare to using the pre-trained model alone or with other cross-lingual adaptation techniques?
3. **Wikifier Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the wikifier and how these errors impact NER performance?  Are there specific types of entities that are more susceptible to mis-linking?
4. **Feature Selection:**  What is the relative importance of the different features you’ve used (Base, Gazetteer, Wikifier)?  Have you experimented with feature selection techniques to identify the most informative features for each language?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the low-resource languages is a strong point.
*   Table 2 is very helpful and provides a clear overview of the experimental results.
*   Consider adding a discussion section addressing the limitations of the work and potential future directions in more detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The empirical results are compelling, and the analysis is thorough. While some weaknesses exist (primarily related to the reliance on English Wikipedia and lack of comparison to recent pre-trained models), the contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
