## Review of “Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification”

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, utilizing the associated FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for a NER model trained primarily on English data. The authors demonstrate strong performance gains on both CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and several low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). They also show improvements in monolingual models by incorporating these features and the benefits of multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Utilization of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:**  The paper convincingly demonstrates that cross-lingual wikification isn’t merely a downstream task for NER but can be powerfully leveraged to *create* useful features for NER, especially in cross-lingual scenarios. This shifts the perspective on wikification’s role.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on Low-Resource Languages:** The reported gains on the challenging low-resource languages are significant and represent a valuable contribution.  Outperforming a baseline requiring native speaker interaction is particularly notable.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The paper offers a thorough set of experiments, including ablation studies (monolingual, feature analysis), investigation into Wikipedia size impact, and exploration of multi-source training. This strengthens the validity of the findings.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Practical Approach:** The idea of using wikification as a feature engineering step for cross-lingual NER is both novel and highly practical. It sidesteps the need for expensive parallel corpora or extensive language-specific annotation. The reliance on readily available Wikipedia data makes it easily adaptable to many languages.
2. **Strong Performance & Competitive Results:**  The reported results are impressive, consistently outperforming baseline methods, including direct transfer baselines and a competing state-of-the-art system (Zhang et al., 2016) on the low-resource languages.  The performance improvement on CoNLL languages is also substantial.
3. **Thorough Evaluation & Ablation Studies:** The authors go beyond simply demonstrating the performance of their model. The ablation studies – examining the impact of each feature set (base, gazetteer, wikifier) – provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component. The analysis of Wikipedia size’s impact is also a key strength.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The details provided (model architecture, feature sets, evaluation metrics) suggest reasonable reproducibility. The readily available use of Wikipedia is a strong advantage.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Wikipedia Quality:** The experiments investigating the impact of FreeBase integration and interlanguage links highlight the importance of Wikipedia data quality and provide actionable insights for improving the system further.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Wikification Errors:** While the paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation (Figure 1), it doesn't delve deeply into the impact of *incorrect* wikification links on performance.  A systematic analysis of common wikification errors and their effect on NER accuracy would strengthen the paper.  How robust is the system to mislinked entities?
2. **Over-Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The model's complete dependence on the English Wikipedia as the grounding point is a potential limitation.  While pragmatic, it introduces a bias towards English-centric knowledge.  Exploring multilingual Wikipedia embeddings or a more flexible cross-lingual grounding strategy could be valuable future work.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set is fairly complex, and it’s not entirely clear which wikifier features are *most* crucial.  A more targeted analysis of feature importance (e.g., using feature selection techniques) could help streamline the model and identify the most informative features.  The large number of features could also lead to overfitting.
4. **Limited Comparison to Recent Advances:** The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), which are reasonable baselines, but doesn’t engage with more recent advances in cross-lingual transfer learning, particularly those leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., multilingual BERT). A comparison to these models would position the work more effectively within the current landscape.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, specifically focusing on examples where incorrect wikification links led to NER errors? What types of entities or languages are most susceptible to these errors?
2. **Feature Importance:** What are the most informative wikifier features according to a feature importance analysis (e.g., using information gain or similar methods)?  Are certain FreeBase types or Wikipedia categories consistently more predictive than others?
3. **Multilingual Embeddings:**  Have you considered exploring multilingual Wikipedia embeddings (e.g., those generated by multilingual BERT) as features, either in addition to or instead of the current approach?
4. **Generalization to Other Knowledge Bases:** Could the framework be adapted to leverage other multilingual knowledge bases besides FreeBase? What challenges would arise in such a scenario?




**Additional Comments:**

* The notation could be more consistent. For example, “W.−FB query” in Table 3 is not immediately clear.
* A brief discussion of the computational cost of the wikification step would be helpful.
* The section on training languages and Figure 2 are useful, but could be presented more clearly. Perhaps a concise table summarizing the best training language for each test language would be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The comprehensive experimental evaluation and insightful analysis make a convincing case for the benefits of leveraging cross-lingual wikification. While some weaknesses remain, they are not critical and can be addressed through future work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
