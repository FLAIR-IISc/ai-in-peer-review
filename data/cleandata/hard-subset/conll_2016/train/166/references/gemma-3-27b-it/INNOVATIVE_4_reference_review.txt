This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to English Wikipedia, and use the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for NER. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets and achieve competitive results on low-resource languages. 

The primary contribution is the demonstration that cross-lingual wikification *can* be a strong source of language-independent features for NER, enabling effective zero-shot transfer. A secondary contribution is a comprehensive experimental evaluation across a diverse set of languages, including several low-resource scenarios, and a thorough ablation study exploring the impact of various features and Wikipedia size. Finally, the demonstration that combining training data from multiple languages can further improve performance is a valuable, though less novel, finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea – using wikification *as a feature engineering step* rather than a post-processing step – is genuinely novel and insightful. While wikification and entity linking are well-explored, framing it as a way to generate language-agnostic features for NER is a compelling perspective. This shifts the paradigm and opens up new avenues for cross-lingual NLP.
2. **Broad Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct extensive experiments across a wide range of languages, including several low-resource languages, which is commendable. The inclusion of CoNLL languages alongside languages like Yoruba and Tamil significantly strengthens the evaluation and demonstrates the generalizability (and limitations) of the approach. The ablation studies are also well-executed, providing valuable insights into the relative importance of different features.
3. **Strong Results on Low-Resource Languages:** Achieving competitive results on low-resource languages, outperforming methods requiring parallel data or native speaker interaction, is a significant achievement. This highlights the potential of the proposed approach for practical applications in scenarios where labeled data is scarce.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental setup. The inclusion of an example (Figure 1) is particularly helpful for illustrating the core idea.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on English Wikipedia:** The entire system fundamentally depends on the quality and coverage of English Wikipedia and its interlanguage links. The ablation study (Table 3) clearly demonstrates this dependence, raising concerns about the applicability of the method to languages with limited representation in English Wikipedia. While acknowledged, the implications of this are understated; the method's effectiveness is inextricably tied to the richness of English Wikipedia, which is a considerable bottleneck.
2. **Limited Analysis of Wikification Errors:** The paper acknowledges imperfect disambiguation by the wikifier, but doesn’t delve deeply into the *types* of errors and their impact on NER performance. Understanding whether the errors are systematic (e.g., consistently mislinking certain entity types) or random would be valuable for identifying potential improvements to the wikification process.
3. **Feature Engineering Remains Key:** While the wikification features are valuable, the model still relies on a substantial set of traditional, language-dependent features (e.g., lexical features, gazetteers). The extent to which the wikification features truly *decouple* the model from language-specific knowledge isn’t fully demonstrated. It’s possible that the gains are partly due to improved feature representation rather than complete language independence.
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) are somewhat dated. More recent and stronger direct transfer baselines should be considered to more accurately assess the relative performance of the proposed method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the model can be trained on documents from multiple languages together.  Can you provide more detail about the training procedure when using multiple source languages?  For example, do you balance the amount of training data from each language?
2.  The ablation study in Table 3 highlights the dependency on English Wikipedia.  Have you explored alternative strategies for mitigating this dependency, such as using a different anchor language or incorporating information from other Wikipedias directly?
3.  Could you elaborate on the types of wikification errors observed and their potential impact on NER performance? Are certain entity types more prone to mislinking?
4.  The paper focuses on leveraging Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types. Have you experimented with incorporating other information from Wikipedia, such as the surrounding text in the Wikipedia article or relationships between entities?
