## Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) model that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is to ground n-grams from any language to the English Wikipedia, utilizing the resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types as features for training an NER model. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing direct transfer methods on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and achieve state-of-the-art results on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). They also show benefits of these features for monolingual models and multi-source training.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Wikification as a Powerful Feature Source for Cross-Lingual NER:** The paper convincingly shows that simply grounding words in multiple languages to the English Wikipedia and using the associated knowledge (Freebase types, Wikipedia categories) provides surprisingly strong signals for NER, outperforming existing methods, especially in low-resource scenarios.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:**  The work provides a thorough evaluation on a diverse set of languages, including both well-resourced and low-resourced languages.  This extensive evaluation strengthens the claims about the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
3. **Insights into the Importance of Wikipedia Size & Interlanguage Links:** The analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size and the role of interlanguage links in the wikification process provides valuable insights into the limitations and dependencies of the proposed method.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Intuition:** The core idea of leveraging wikification *as a feature generator* rather than a downstream task is a clever and insightful contribution. It shifts the perspective on how knowledge from Wikipedia can be utilized for NER.  The intuition that disambiguation provides useful clues for NER, even without perfect accuracy, is well-supported by the results.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance gains across diverse languages, particularly the strong results on low-resource languages, are compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Outperforming a system relying on native speaker interaction (Zhang et al., 2016) is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation studies, examining the impact of different feature combinations (wikifier features alone, with base features, with gazetteers), and the analysis of Wikipedia size and interlanguage links demonstrate a deep understanding of the method's behavior. This is a key strength of the paper.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.  The explicit mention of the specific wikification system (Tsai and Roth, 2016) and the availability of resources (Wikipedia dumps) aid in potential reproducibility.
5. **Practicality and Scalability:** The approach is relatively simple to implement and scales well to new languages as it only requires a Wikipedia dump, avoiding the need for parallel corpora or language-specific resources.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Wikification System:** The paper relies heavily on an existing cross-lingual wikification system (Tsai and Roth, 2016). While correctly cited, the novelty primarily lies in *how* the wikification output is used, rather than the wikification process itself.  This makes the overall novelty incremental.
2. **Dependence on English Wikipedia:** The grounding to English Wikipedia introduces a potential bias.  The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories are inherently English-centric.  While the authors acknowledge this and show it impacts performance with smaller Wikipedias, a more in-depth investigation of how this bias affects NER quality across languages would be valuable.
3. **Suboptimal Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while standard (Ratinov & Roth, 2009), could potentially be improved.  The authors acknowledge not using POS tags due to difficulties obtaining them across all languages. Exploring more language-agnostic feature engineering techniques (e.g., character-level embeddings) could potentially boost performance further, particularly for low-resource languages.  
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., specific entity types, ambiguous cases) could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  Why does the model struggle with certain languages even with wikifier features?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Bias Analysis:** Could you elaborate on potential biases introduced by grounding all n-grams to the English Wikipedia? Have you considered strategies to mitigate this bias, such as using multilingual FreeBase or creating language-specific knowledge bases?
2. **Feature Exploration:**  Have you explored other language-agnostic features, such as character-level embeddings or subword units (e.g., Byte Pair Encoding)?  If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide a brief error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors made by the model? Are there specific entity types or languages where the model consistently underperforms?
4. **Scalability with Larger N-grams:**  You limit n-grams to a size of 4. Have you explored using larger n-grams? If so, did you find any trade-offs in performance or computational cost?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The table formatting is a bit awkward. Consider improving readability by using more consistent spacing and alignment.
*   The discussion of the training/test language combinations (Figure 2) is a bit brief. A more detailed explanation of the observations made would be helpful.
*   The reference to LDC resources would benefit from full LDC identifiers.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to cross-lingual NER. The core idea of utilizing wikification as a feature generator is clever and effective, and the comprehensive evaluation strengthens the claims. While the novelty is somewhat incremental due to the reliance on an existing wikification system, the strong empirical results and insightful analysis make this a worthy contribution to the field.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses raised above.
