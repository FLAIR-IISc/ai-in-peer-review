This paper presents a cross-lingual NER model leveraging cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea – utilizing Wikipedia as a bridge for transferring NER capabilities across languages – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in NLP, particularly for low-resource languages. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming baselines and, in many cases, state-of-the-art methods, especially in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Integration of Cross-Lingual Wikification for NER:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification, traditionally a downstream task, can be effectively repurposed as a feature engineering step for NER, yielding substantial performance gains, particularly in a zero-shot transfer setting. The clever use of Wikipedia categories and Freebase types obtained through wikification as language-independent features is a key insight.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Languages:** The model achieves competitive results on a diverse set of low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), surpassing existing methods that require parallel data or native speaker interaction. This is a notable accomplishment and highlights the practical value of the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments, varying datasets, features, and training languages, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed model and insightful analysis of the factors influencing performance (e.g., Wikipedia size).  The ablation studies (Table 2 and 3) are particularly helpful in understanding the contribution of different components.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:**  While cross-lingual wikification exists, its application *as a primary feature generation method* for NER is novel and demonstrates a shift in perspective. The observation that disambiguation aids entity recognition, rather than vice-versa, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains across various languages, especially the consistent outperformance of baselines, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The results on low-resource languages are particularly compelling.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed analysis of the impact of Wikipedia size on performance (Table 3) and the benefits of multi-lingual training (Figure 2 and Table 4), which demonstrate a deep understanding of the factors affecting the model. This provides valuable insights for future research.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The experiments are clearly described, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner. Figure 1 is a helpful illustration of the process.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details regarding the datasets, features, and experimental setup, which should facilitate reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on English Wikipedia:** The reliance on linking to *English* Wikipedia is a limitation. While pragmatic, it introduces potential biases and may not be optimal for all languages.  The experiments highlighting this dependency (Table 3) demonstrate the issue, but a discussion of strategies to mitigate this (e.g., using a more central Wikipedia like German) would be beneficial.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** While the features are effective, they are somewhat complex and rely on an external wikification system (Tsai and Roth, 2016). A deeper discussion of the computational cost and potential bottlenecks associated with the wikification process would be valuable.
3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods is somewhat limited. While the results are competitive, a more direct comparison with more recent and advanced NER models (especially those leveraging pre-trained language models like BERT or XLM-RoBERTa) would strengthen the paper. While the authors mention their method could be combined with such approaches, demonstrating this empirically would be a strong addition.
4. **Monolingual Experiments Not Fully Justified:** While the monolingual experiments demonstrate the value of the features, their purpose in the context of a *cross-lingual* transfer learning paper isn't entirely clear.  A stronger connection should be made explaining *why* improving monolingual performance is relevant to the core contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you explored using other Wikipedia languages (e.g., German) as the central hub for wikification, and if so, how did the performance compare to using English Wikipedia?
2. Could you provide more details on the computational cost and scalability of the cross-lingual wikification process, especially for very large corpora?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., XLM-RoBERTa) into your NER model, and if so, how did the performance compare to the results presented in the paper?
4. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the choice of BIO tagging scheme and whether other schemes (e.g., BILOU) were considered?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to "Schwierigkeiten beim nachvollziehenden Verstehen Albrecht Lehmann läßt Flüchtlinge und Vertriebene in Westdeutschland" seems extraneous and doesn’t contribute to the paper's content.
* The notation in Table 2 (#inter.) is unclear and should be explained more thoroughly.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
