### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), focusing on the direct transfer setting where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to a low-resource language without any target-language training data.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for Cross-Lingual NER:** The core contribution is the use of a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. By grounding every n-gram in a sentence to the English Wikipedia, the model extracts features from corresponding FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories. This cleverly turns wikification, often a downstream task, into a powerful feature source for an upstream task, creating a truly delexicalized and language-agnostic signal.
2.  **Extensive and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across nine languages, including four high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts). The proposed method significantly outperforms strong, comparable baselines for direct transfer NER (Täckström et al., 2012) and a state-of-the-art system for low-resource NER that uses more resources (Zhang et al., 2016).
3.  **Insightful Analysis of the Method's Properties and Limitations:** The authors conduct several well-designed experiments beyond the main direct transfer task. These include showing the features' utility in a monolingual setting, analyzing the direct relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality, and demonstrating the model's ability to benefit from multi-source training. This thorough analysis strengthens the paper's claims and provides valuable insights for the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Effective Core Idea:** The central idea of using cross-lingual wikification to create language-independent features is both simple and powerful. It directly addresses the main challenge of direct transfer—the lexical gap between languages—by mapping words and phrases from any language into a shared semantic space (the English Wikipedia knowledge graph). The large performance gains shown in Table 2 (+12-15 F1 points on CoNLL languages over a strong baseline) are compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.

2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental design is a major strength.
    *   **Language Diversity:** Evaluating on nine languages with varying resource levels and script systems (Latin vs. non-Latin) provides a robust test of the method's generalizability. The strong performance on languages like Turkish and Tagalog is particularly impressive.
    *   **Strong Baselines:** The paper compares against the most relevant and competitive baselines for this task. Outperforming Täckström et al. (2012), which uses cross-lingual word clusters derived from parallel corpora, is a significant achievement, especially since the proposed method does not require parallel text.
    *   **Thorough Ablations and Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their model's performance. The experiment in Section 4.4, which simulates a smaller Wikipedia by removing inter-language links, provides a clear and convincing explanation for why the features are less effective on languages like Yoruba. This kind of careful analysis is often missing and adds significant value.

3.  **Resource-Light Approach:** Compared to competing methods, the proposed system has minimal requirements: only a multilingual Wikipedia dump is needed. It does not rely on parallel corpora (like projection methods or Täckström's clustering), nor does it require interaction with a native speaker (like Zhang et al., 2016). This makes the approach highly scalable and easily applicable to any of the hundreds of languages present in Wikipedia.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost and Scalability:** The paper states that it wikifies "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" in a document. This suggests a very high computational overhead, as the number of n-grams is quadratic in sentence length. The paper provides no discussion of runtime, efficiency, or whether this brute-force approach is practical for processing large corpora. This is a significant omission, as the potential computational expense could be a major barrier to adoption. A more efficient strategy, such as only wikifying candidate phrases, is not explored.

2.  **Dependency on Wikipedia Quality and Coverage:** The method's performance is fundamentally tethered to the size and quality of a language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. This is acknowledged in the analysis (Section 4.4) but remains a critical limitation. For truly low-resource languages with minimal or non-existent Wikipedia presence, the method would be inapplicable. While this is a limitation of the approach itself, a more explicit discussion of the "floor" or minimum Wikipedia size needed for reasonable performance would be beneficial.

3.  **Somewhat Dated Base Model:** The NER model is based on Ratinov and Roth (2009), which uses an averaged perceptron with discrete features. While this is a fair choice for comparing with contemporaneous work like Täckström et al. (2012), the field has largely moved to neural architectures (BiLSTM-CRF, Transformers). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these powerful, language-independent features might integrate with or complement modern contextual embeddings from models like mBERT. It is unclear if the gains would be as dramatic on top of a much stronger neural baseline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please comment on the computational cost of the proposed feature extraction? Specifically, what is the runtime overhead of wikifying all n-grams compared to the baseline feature extraction, and have you explored more efficient candidate generation strategies to make the system more scalable?

2.  The modifications to the wikifier are crucial for preventing spurious links from common words (e.g., "in Germany" -> Germany). Could you clarify how unigrams that are common words but also named entities (e.g., the country "Turkey" vs. the bird) are handled? Does the ranker's context modeling sufficiently disambiguate these cases without special handling?
