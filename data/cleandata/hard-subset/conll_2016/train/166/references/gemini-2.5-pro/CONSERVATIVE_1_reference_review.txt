**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to train an NER model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) using language-independent features and apply it directly to a target language without any annotated data. The main contribution is the introduction of a new set of features derived from a cross-lingual wikifier, which links n-grams in any language to the English Wikipedia. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with the linked English entries serve as delexicalized features.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration of using cross-lingual wikification for direct transfer NER.** The paper successfully adapts an existing cross-lingual wikification system to generate features that yield significant improvements over a well-established direct transfer baseline on standard CoNLL datasets.
2.  **A thorough analysis of the method's dependency on Wikipedia size.** The authors conduct insightful ablation studies that correlate the quality of the wikifier features, and thus the model's performance, with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. This provides a clear explanation for the performance variance across languages.
3.  **An extensive evaluation across a typologically diverse set of languages.** The method is tested not only on related European languages but also on five low-resource languages, including two with non-Latin scripts, which rigorously assesses its generalizability.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Performance Against a Relevant Baseline:** The proposed method significantly outperforms the direct transfer model of Täckström et al. (2012) on the CoNLL Spanish, Dutch, and German datasets. For example, the F1 score on German improves from 45.6 to 60.97, and on Dutch from 23.5 to 47.14. These are substantial gains over a widely recognized and appropriate baseline, providing compelling evidence for the efficacy of the wikifier features.
2.  **Methodological Soundness in Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.4 is a key strength. Instead of just reporting lower scores on low-resource languages, the authors investigate the cause by simulating a low-resource setting (by removing interlanguage links) for high-resource languages. This experiment convincingly demonstrates that the performance degradation is tied to the availability of linkage information in Wikipedia, which is a methodologically rigorous way to support their hypothesis.
3.  **Resource-Light Approach:** Compared to alternative cross-lingual methods, the proposed approach has minimal requirements. It relies only on multilingual Wikipedia dumps and avoids the need for parallel corpora (unlike projection methods or Täckström's bitext clusters) or native speaker interaction. This makes the method highly practical and easily applicable to any of the hundreds of languages present in Wikipedia.
4.  **Solid Base Model and Feature Design:** The work builds upon a well-established and high-performing monolingual NER model (Ratinov and Roth, 2009). The cumulative addition of features in the experiments (Base -> +Gazetteers -> +Wikifier) clearly isolates and demonstrates the contribution of the novel features.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Wikifier is Treated as a Black Box:** The entire method's success hinges on the performance of the cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016). However, this critical component is not evaluated or analyzed in its own right. The paper provides no metrics on the wikifier's precision or recall, nor does it discuss the signal-to-noise ratio introduced by linking *every* n-gram. It is plausible that many n-grams are linked incorrectly or not at all, and the impact of this noise on the downstream NER model is not quantified. This lack of scrutiny of the core enabling technology is a significant omission.
2.  **Questionable Comparison to a Non-Comparable System:** The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) on low-resource languages is presented as a major result, but the two systems operate under fundamentally different assumptions. Zhang et al. is not a direct transfer model; it uses parallel data, a large monolingual corpus, and human-in-the-loop interaction. Claiming superiority is misleading when the resource requirements and problem formulation are so different. A more appropriate framing would be to position the proposed method as a strong baseline for settings where such additional resources are unavailable.
3.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application is effective, the core idea of using entity types from a knowledge base as features for NER is not new. The main innovation is the use of a *cross-lingual* wikifier to enable this in a *direct transfer* setting. As such, the work feels more like a successful and thorough application of an existing tool (the wikifier) to a known problem, rather than a fundamentally new methodology for NER itself.
4.  **Practical Concerns are Not Addressed:** The process of wikifying every n-gram up to size 4 for every word in a document seems computationally prohibitive. The paper offers no discussion of the system's efficiency, runtime performance, or scalability. For NER to be a practical pre-processing step, its computational cost is a critical factor, and the absence of this information makes it difficult to assess the method's real-world viability.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide some intrinsic evaluation of the wikifier on these datasets? For instance, what is the accuracy of the top-1 linked entity for actual named entities in the test sets? What percentage of all n-grams (n<=4) are successfully linked to a Wikipedia concept versus being returned as NIL? This would help in understanding how much noise the feature generation process introduces.
2.  Given the significant disparity in resource assumptions, could you re-contextualize the comparison with Zhang et al. (2016)? Would it be more accurate to present your system as a strong zero-shot baseline rather than a direct competitor that "outperforms" a semi-supervised system?
3.  Can you provide details on the computational cost of the wikification feature generation step? How does the total processing time of your system compare to the baseline models that do not use these features?
