### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to leverage a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. Instead of following the traditional pipeline where NER precedes wikification, the authors propose to wikify every n-gram in a given text, linking it to the English Wikipedia. The types and categories associated with the resulting Wikipedia entries (e.g., from FreeBase) are then used as powerful delexicalized features for a standard NER model trained on a high-resource language like English. The method is evaluated on a wide range of languages, from high-resource CoNLL languages to several low-resource languages, including some with non-Latin scripts, and shows significant improvements over strong baselines.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Conceptual Re-imagining of the NER/Wikification Relationship:** The primary contribution is the bold inversion of the standard NLP pipeline. Using wikification as a universal pre-processing step to generate rich semantic features for a foundational task like NER is a genuinely novel and powerful concept. It reframes a downstream application as an upstream feature source and opens a new direction for injecting world knowledge into cross-lingual models.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results with a Resource-Light Approach:** The paper provides a compelling empirical demonstration that this new feature set leads to significant performance gains. The proposed model substantially outperforms prior work in direct transfer NER, including methods that rely on parallel corpora or human-in-the-loop annotation, which are far more demanding resources than the multilingual Wikipedia dumps required here.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The paper’s central thesis is its greatest strength. The idea of flipping the NER->Wikification pipeline is innovative and thought-provoking. It moves beyond the typical delexicalization strategies (e.g., word shapes, cross-lingual word clusters) towards using a structured knowledge base to provide language-agnostic, semantic type information for any substring. This is exactly the kind of high-impact, paradigm-shifting work that the field needs.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across nine languages with diverse typological properties and resource levels. The model’s consistent and significant improvements in both monolingual and, more importantly, direct transfer settings are very convincing. The gains over the Täckström et al. baseline are impressive, especially considering that baseline requires parallel corpora. Surpassing Zhang et al., which uses even more resources including human feedback, further underscores the power of this approach.
3.  **Simplicity and Broad Applicability:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. It augments a standard, well-understood NER classifier with a new class of features. It does not rely on complex, task-specific architectures. The only required resource is Wikipedia, making the approach readily applicable to hundreds of languages without the need for parallel text or annotated data in the target language, which are major bottlenecks in cross-lingual NLP.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.4, which directly links the quality of the wikifier features to the size of the target language's Wikipedia, is valuable. It provides a clear-eyed view of the method's limitations and offers a useful heuristic for predicting its effectiveness on a new language. The experiments with multi-source training also add to the completeness of the study.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Dependency on Wikipedia Coverage:** The paper's primary strength is also its Achilles' heel. The method's effectiveness is fundamentally bound to the quality and coverage of a language's Wikipedia and its interlanguage links. As the authors’ own analysis shows, for languages with small Wikipedias (like Yoruba), the feature signal is weak, and the gains are minimal. This reliance makes the approach less viable for the long tail of truly low-resource languages that lack a significant Wikipedia presence, which is a crucial limitation for a cross-lingual NER method.
2.  **Unaddressed Computational Expense:** The proposal to wikify *every n-gram up to n=4* is a brute-force approach that seems computationally massive. Standard wikification is already a non-trivial task, and applying it to every possible substring in a document could make inference prohibitively slow for any practical application. The paper completely omits any discussion of runtime, efficiency, or computational cost, which is a major oversight for assessing the practicality of the method.
3.  **Use of a Dated Base Model:** While I appreciate that the focus is on the novelty of the features, the choice of a 2009-era averaged perceptron model as the base classifier feels dated. It leaves open the question of how these features would interact with modern, powerful neural architectures (e.g., mBERT). It is possible that large pretrained models already capture some of the semantic information provided by wikification, potentially leading to less dramatic gains. A small experiment or at least a discussion of this point would have made the work more forward-looking.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the computational cost: could you provide an analysis of the inference speed of your full model compared to the baseline model? Is the "wikify-everything" approach feasible for processing large text corpora, or do you envision it being used in a different context?
2.  Your results for Bengali and Tamil show that the "Wikifier only" model outperforms the full model trained on English lexical features. This suggests your training strategy is suboptimal for languages with different scripts. Did you consider a more adaptive approach, such as training a model for non-Latin scripts that explicitly excludes English lexical features, rather than relying on the learner to ignore them?
3.  The preprocessing step of re-mapping or deleting all MISC tags in the CoNLL training data seems like a rather blunt instrument. How sensitive are your results to this decision? Could this potentially inflate performance on LOC entities and make comparisons to prior work that may have handled this category differently less direct?
