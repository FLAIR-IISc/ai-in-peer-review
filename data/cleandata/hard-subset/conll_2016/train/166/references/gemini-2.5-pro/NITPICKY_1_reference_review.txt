**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to generate language-independent features by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (up to n=4) in a source or target language text to the English Wikipedia. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with the resulting English Wikipedia titles are then used as features in a standard NER model. The authors evaluate this approach by training a model on English and testing it on several CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages, claiming significant improvements over strong baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel feature generation method for cross-lingual NER that densely applies cross-lingual wikification to all n-grams, thereby creating a rich set of language-agnostic signals without relying on parallel corpora.
2.  An empirical demonstration that these wikifier-based features provide significant performance gains in a direct transfer setting for languages with large Wikipedia editions and lexical overlap with English (e.g., Dutch, Spanish, German).
3.  An analysis of the method's limitations, correlating feature quality with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia.

**Strengths:**

1.  The central idea of using dense, cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features is conceptually elegant and novel. It cleverly reverses the traditional NER-then-Entity-Linking pipeline and requires only access to Wikipedia, a more readily available resource than large, high-quality parallel corpora needed for projection-based methods or cross-lingual word clusters.
2.  The experimental results on the CoNLL languages are strong and convincing. The proposed method significantly outperforms the direct transfer baseline from Täckström et al. (2012), even their enhanced model that uses bitext-induced word clusters. For example, on Dutch, the paper reports an F1 of 47.14 compared to Täckström's 40.4, demonstrating the practical value of the wikifier features for transferring between related languages.
3.  The paper includes valuable ablation studies and analyses. Section 4.4 and Table 3 effectively demonstrate the dependency of the feature quality on the size of the inter-language link graph between the target language's Wikipedia and the English one. This provides a clear explanation for the performance disparity between high- and low-resource languages and offers a useful diagnostic for when this method is likely to succeed.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The claims regarding performance on low-resource languages are demonstrably false and misleading.** The paper explicitly states in Section 4.3, "We can see that our approach performs better than their model [Zhang et al., 2016] on all five languages". However, the results in Table 2 directly contradict this assertion. For Yoruba (YO), the final proposed model (`+Wikifier`) scores 6.72 F1, while the cited baseline is 34.8. For Bengali (BN), the score is 2.99 F1 against the baseline's 26.0. These are not improvements but orders-of-magnitude performance regressions. While a different feature configuration (`Wikifier only`) yields a better score for Yoruba, the paper presents `+Wikifier` as its final model and fails to justify a post-hoc selection of a different model for specific target languages. This misrepresentation of results is a fatal flaw.
2.  **The methodology suffers from several poorly justified and scientifically questionable choices.**
    *   **Arbitrary Data Manipulation:** In Section 4.1, the authors describe modifying the benchmark data in a non-standard way. For low-resource experiments, they remove TIME and TTL tags and, more critically, remap MISC tags in the CoNLL training data to LOC based on FreeBase lookups. This ad-hoc manipulation of the standard training and test sets compromises the integrity of the evaluation and makes a fair comparison to prior work impossible, as it appears to specifically tailor the source label set to the target.
    *   **Hand-wavy Dismissal of Standard Features:** The decision to exclude POS tags—a cornerstone feature in most NER systems—is justified only by a vague reference to an un-cited "earlier experiment" where they "do not improve the performance". This is insufficient justification and weakens the model's design.
3.  **The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in presentation and clarity.**
    *   **Figure 1, the primary motivating example, is profoundly confusing.** The caption describes "An example of German sentence", but the German text shown is a long, grammatically questionable fragment. The accompanying "English" line ("Problem_solving Understanding...") is not a translation and its purpose is entirely unclear. The grounding of the German word "Verstehen" to the English Wikipedia title "Problem_solving" seems semantically tenuous and is not explained. A confusing core example erodes confidence in the authors' methodology.
    *   **Table Formatting and Completeness:** In Table 2, the average F1 score in the `+Wikifier` row is placed directly under the Tamil (TA) column, making it easy to misread as the result for that language. Furthermore, the actual result for Tamil is missing from this crucial row, rendering the claim of outperforming the baseline on "all five languages" unverifiable in addition to being incorrect.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please address the stark contradiction between your claim of outperforming Zhang et al. (2016) on "all five" low-resource languages and the results in Table 2, which show your model performs drastically worse on Yoruba and Bengali. Can you also provide the missing F1 score for Tamil for your final `+Wikifier` model?
2.  Could you provide a more rigorous justification for modifying the standard CoNLL dataset by re-mapping MISC tags based on FreeBase types? How can you assure reviewers that this non-standard preprocessing is not the primary source of your gains and does not invalidate the comparison to previous work that used the standard data?
3.  Please clarify the content of Figure 1. What is the precise German sentence being analyzed, what is its correct English translation, and what is the justification for grounding the word "Verstehen" to the "Problem_solving" Wikipedia page?
