### Summary
This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a target language without annotated data. The core contribution is the use of a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. For any given text, the method attempts to link all n-grams (up to n=4) to their corresponding English Wikipedia pages. The semantic types (e.g., FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) associated with the linked English pages are then used as features in a linear NER model. This approach effectively circumvents the need for parallel corpora, requiring only access to multilingual Wikipedia. The authors conduct an extensive evaluation on nine languages, including four CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, demonstrating significant improvements over strong baselines in both monolingual and direct transfer scenarios.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel feature engineering method for cross-lingual NER based on wikification.** The key contribution is the idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier to generate rich, delexicalized semantic features for NER. By grounding n-grams in any language to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the method creates a powerful, language-agnostic signal for identifying entity types. This approach innovatively inverts the traditional pipeline where NER is a prerequisite for entity linking.

2.  **Extensive empirical validation on a diverse set of languages.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across nine languages with varying resource levels and script systems (Latin and non-Latin). This comprehensive testing demonstrates the robustness and broad applicability of the proposed features, showing consistent gains over baselines that rely on lexical features or parallel corpora.

3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependencies and limitations.** The authors perform crucial experiments correlating the performance of the wikifier features with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the number of inter-language links to English. This analysis provides a clear and objective understanding of the conditions under which the method is effective, adding significant value to the empirical results.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Practicality.** The central idea of leveraging cross-lingual wikification for NER feature generation is highly novel. It presents a practical and effective alternative to projection-based methods or techniques requiring large parallel corpora. As the only resource required is Wikipedia, the approach is scalable to a large number of languages, significantly lowering the barrier for developing NER systems for new languages.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The proposed model achieves substantial improvements over established baselines. On the CoNLL datasets, it outperforms the direct transfer method of Täckström et al. (2012) by a large margin (e.g., +13.7 F1 on Dutch, +15.3 F1 on German over the baseline). Furthermore, on five low-resource languages, it surpasses the performance of Zhang et al. (2016), a system which requires more resources, including parallel text and human interaction. These results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the wikifier-based features.

3.  **Thorough Analysis and Ablation Studies.** The paper includes a well-executed analysis that strengthens its conclusions. The experiments in Section 4.4, which simulate smaller Wikipedias by removing inter-language links, compellingly demonstrate the link between knowledge base size and feature quality. The multi-source training experiments in Section 4.5 further explore the capabilities of the model. This level of analysis provides valuable insights into the method's behavior.

4.  **Clarity and Organization.** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methodology is explained logically, and the experiments are described in sufficient detail to understand the setup and results.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Heavy Reliance on a Complex Upstream System.** The success of the entire method is contingent on the performance of the cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016). This wikifier is a complex system in its own right, and the paper does not discuss its performance characteristics or computational overhead. The process of wikifying every n-gram up to n=4 in a document is likely to be computationally expensive, which may limit the practical applicability of the method for large-scale processing. An analysis of the method's efficiency and scalability is missing.

2.  **Comparison with Baselines is Becoming Dated.** The base NER model is a feature-based linear classifier from 2009. While the comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are relevant, the paper lacks a comparison or even a discussion in relation to more contemporary neural approaches for cross-lingual transfer, such as those using multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT). While the work may have been done concurrently with the rise of such models, their absence makes it difficult to position the contribution within the current state of the art.

3.  **Handling of Non-Latin Scripts is Heuristic.** For languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), the authors find that including lexical features trained on English is detrimental and propose simply using only the wikifier features. This strategy feels like a post-hoc fix rather than a principled solution. It prevents the model from capturing any lexical regularities, such as transliterated entities. A more integrated approach for handling script differences would strengthen the model's design.

4.  **Limited Analysis of Wikification Errors.** The approach of grounding all n-grams is susceptible to noise from incorrect wikification links. While the paper claims the features are useful even with imperfect disambiguation, it does not provide a qualitative or quantitative analysis of how wikification errors propagate to NER errors. An error analysis could provide deeper insight into the model's robustness and failure modes.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide details on the computational cost of the feature generation process? Specifically, what is the runtime overhead of wikifying all n-grams in a document compared to the baseline model that uses only lexical and gazetteer features?
2.  The method's performance clearly degrades as the size of the target language's Wikipedia shrinks. Based on your analysis, is there an approximate "tipping point" (e.g., in terms of number of articles or inter-language links) below which the wikifier features cease to be beneficial?
3.  The proposed features are shown to be very effective in a linear model. Have you considered how these features could be integrated into a modern neural NER architecture (e.g., as additional inputs to a BiLSTM-CRF)? Do you hypothesize that the performance gains would be additive, or would the neural model implicitly learn similar representations?
