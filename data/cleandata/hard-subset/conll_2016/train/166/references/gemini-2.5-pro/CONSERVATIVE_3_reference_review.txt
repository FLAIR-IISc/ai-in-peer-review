**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to link n-grams from a source document (in any language) to the English Wikipedia. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with the linked English Wikipedia articles are then used as language-independent features in a standard sequence labeling model for NER. The model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied directly to low-resource target languages without any target-language-specific training data. The authors evaluate this approach on nine languages, showing improvements over established baselines in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that features derived from a cross-lingual wikifier provide a strong, language-independent signal for direct transfer NER.
2.  An extensive analysis across a diverse set of languages, including several low-resource and non-Latin script languages, which effectively highlights both the potential and the limitations of the proposed method (e.g., its dependence on Wikipedia size).
3.  A direct comparison showing that this Wikipedia-based feature engineering approach can outperform a strong baseline that relies on parallel corpora for inducing cross-lingual word clusters.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Baselines.** The paper demonstrates significant and consistent performance gains over a well-regarded direct transfer baseline (Täckström et al., 2012). For instance, on the CoNLL languages, the proposed method improves F1 scores by 3.7 to 13.7 points over the baseline's bitext cluster model. This provides compelling evidence that the proposed features are highly effective for the task. The comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) on low-resource languages further strengthens the paper by showing competitiveness against a different class of methods.

2.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis.** The evaluation across nine languages is a major strength. It moves beyond the standard set of European languages and tests the method's robustness on languages with varying script systems and resource levels. The ablation studies in Table 2 are well-designed, clearly isolating the contribution of the base, gazetteer, and wikifier features. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 4.4, which correlates performance with Wikipedia size and the availability of inter-language links, is excellent and provides a crucial understanding of the method's dependencies.

3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Accessibility.** The proposed approach is, at its core, a form of feature engineering applied to a standard, well-understood NER model (Averaged Perceptron). It does not require complex new architectures. The primary resource dependency is Wikipedia, which is more broadly available than the large-scale parallel corpora required by some competing approaches. This makes the method relatively straightforward to understand and potentially easier to apply in new language settings, assuming a functional wikifier is available.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Heavy Dependency on an External, Opaque Component.** The entire method's performance is critically dependent on the cross-lingual wikifier of Tsai and Roth (2016). This component is treated as a black box. The paper provides insufficient detail on the wikifier's own performance characteristics (e.g., its linking accuracy on the test languages), its potential biases, or the sensitivity of the final NER results to its output quality. Without this information, it is difficult to determine whether the contribution is a generalizable feature engineering principle or an artifact of a specific, high-performing, and complex upstream system. The replicability of the work is also hampered by the brief description of the modifications made to the wikifier.

2.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty.** The idea of using Wikipedia-derived information, such as entity categories, as features for monolingual NER is well-established (e.g., Kazama and Torisawa, 2007). The primary novelty here is the extension of this concept to a cross-lingual, direct-transfer setting via a modern wikification system. While the engineering and evaluation are solid, the work is more of an incremental, albeit effective, application of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach to cross-lingual NER.

3.  **Ad-hoc Data Processing and Modeling Decisions.** The paper employs several heuristics that raise concerns about the generalizability of the results. The pre-processing step of converting some MISC tags in the CoNLL training data to LOC while deleting others, based on wikification results, seems tailored to the specific low-resource test sets and may not be a principled general strategy. Furthermore, the conclusion that non-Latin script languages are best served by a "wikifier only" model feels like a post-hoc workaround for the failure of standard lexical features, rather than an integrated part of the proposed method. A more robust model would learn to weight or ignore uninformative features automatically.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide metrics on the performance of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier (e.g., precision/recall for entity linking) on the test languages, particularly the low-resource ones? How strongly does the final NER F1 score correlate with the wikifier's accuracy? This would help clarify the relationship between the quality of the upstream component and the success of your method.

2.  The modification of MISC tags in the training data is a potentially confounding variable. Could you report the performance on the low-resource languages when training on the original, unmodified CoNLL dataset? This would allow for a clearer assessment of the wikifier features' contribution, independent of this data-specific heuristic.

3.  The proposed strategy for non-Latin script languages is to manually exclude lexical and gazetteer features. Did you experiment with a single, unified model for all languages? It seems that a standard learning algorithm with proper regularization (e.g., L1/L2) should be able to learn to ignore the uninformative lexical features for languages with different scripts, without needing a special-cased model configuration.
