### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to leverage a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The method involves processing a document in a target language by attempting to link every n-gram (up to n=4) to a corresponding entry in the English Wikipedia. From the top-ranked Wikipedia candidates for each n-gram, features such as FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories are extracted. These "wikifier features" are then integrated into a standard feature-based NER model, which is trained on a high-resource source language (e.g., English) and applied directly to the target language text. The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on nine languages, including high-resource European languages from the CoNLL datasets and five low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts. The results demonstrate that the proposed features provide significant performance improvements over strong baselines in both monolingual and, more notably, direct transfer settings. The paper also includes an analysis of how performance correlates with the size of the target language's Wikipedia.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel and effective feature representation for direct transfer NER based on cross-lingual wikification.** The key contribution is the idea of using entity linking to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia) as a source of powerful, language-agnostic semantic features. This reverses the traditional pipeline where NER is a prerequisite for entity linking and establishes wikification as a valuable upstream process for cross-lingual understanding tasks.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical validation of the proposed method across a diverse set of languages.** The authors evaluate their model on nine languages from different language families and writing systems (including Latin, Cyrillic-based, and Indic scripts). This extensive evaluation demonstrates the generalizability of the approach and its effectiveness for both high-resource and genuinely low-resource languages, outperforming prior work that often relies on more extensive resources like parallel corpora.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the relationship between knowledge base size and feature quality.** The paper provides a clear analysis showing that the effectiveness of the wikifier features is directly correlated with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and, specifically, the number of articles that have interlanguage links to the English Wikipedia. This analysis offers a practical understanding of the method's limitations and provides a way to predict its potential success for a new language.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Novelty and Potential Impact:** The paper's central idea of using wikification to generate features for NER, rather than as a downstream task, is a novel and compelling contribution. By grounding all languages in a common semantic space (English Wikipedia), it offers an elegant solution to the feature-scarcity problem in direct transfer. The substantial performance gains shown suggest this approach could have a significant impact on cross-lingual NLP, particularly for low-resource languages where parallel data is unavailable.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The method is evaluated thoroughly and demonstrates state-of-the-art results for direct transfer NER. It significantly outperforms the comparable direct transfer method of Täckström et al. (2012) on CoNLL languages (e.g., achieving 62.1 F1 vs. 58.4 on Dutch) and a projection-based method from Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages. The consistency of improvements across all nine test languages underscores the robustness of the proposed features.
3.  **High Scalability and Low Resource Requirements:** Unlike projection-based methods, the proposed approach does not require parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for most languages. The only required resource is a multilingual Wikipedia dump, which is readily available for hundreds of languages. This makes the method highly scalable and broadly applicable, representing a significant practical advantage for tackling NER in the long tail of low-resource languages.
4.  **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The experimental design is sound, and the analyses are insightful. The ablation study in Section 4.4, which artificially reduces the size of the Wikipedia intersection to simulate a low-resource setting, is particularly effective at demonstrating the source of the feature's power.

### Weaknesses

1.  **High Dependency on a Specific Wikification System:** The success of the entire method is contingent on the performance of the cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016). The paper treats this component as a black box, offering limited details about its architecture or error patterns. It is unclear how robust the approach is to wikification errors or how the results would generalize if a different wikification system were used. An error analysis illustrating how incorrect disambiguation by the wikifier impacts final NER predictions would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of robustness.
2.  **Potential for Prohibitive Computational Cost:** The feature generation process requires wikifying every n-gram up to n=4 in the input documents. This appears to be a computationally intensive process, likely orders of magnitude slower than standard lexical feature extraction. The paper provides no discussion of the computational overhead, runtime performance, or memory requirements. This omission is a critical weakness, as high computational costs could render the method impractical for large-scale application, which is a primary use case for NER.
3.  **Unclear Ablation of Wikipedia-Derived Features:** In the main results table (Table 2), the contribution of the wikifier features is shown cumulatively on top of both base features and gazetteer features. The gazetteers, which are also derived from Wikipedia titles, provide a very substantial performance boost on their own. This experimental design makes it difficult to disentangle the contribution of the dynamic wikifier features from the static gazetteer lists. An experiment directly comparing "Base Features + Wikifier" against "Base Features + Gazetteers" would provide a clearer picture of the relative power and potential redundancy of these two Wikipedia-derived feature sets.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding computational cost: Could you please report the runtime and memory usage of your feature extraction process compared to the baseline model without wikifier features? Have you considered or experimented with strategies to make this process more efficient, such as only wikifying candidate n-grams identified by a simple heuristic (e.g., capitalization) instead of exhaustively processing all n-grams?
2.  The procedure for modifying MISC tags in the CoNLL training data when testing on low-resource languages appears to use your wikifier system to relabel the training data ("modify a MISC-tagged word to LOC tag if it is grounded to an entity with location as a FreeBase type"). This seems to introduce a potential information leak, where the features are used to alter the gold labels they are being trained to predict. Could you clarify the exact process and justify this methodological choice?
3.  The performance of the model relies heavily on the intersection between a target language's Wikipedia and the English Wikipedia. Does this grounding to a single, culturally specific knowledge base (English Wikipedia) introduce any observable biases in the NER output? For example, does the system perform better on entities with global prominence versus those that are only locally known within the target language's culture but may still have a Wikipedia page in that language?
