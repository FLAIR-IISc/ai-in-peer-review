### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to a target language with no annotated data. The core contribution is the use of a cross-lingual wikifier to generate a rich set of language-independent features. The method involves processing a document in any language and linking every n-gram (up to n=4) to its corresponding English Wikipedia entry. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with these entries are then used as features for a standard NER classifier. This approach circumvents the need for parallel corpora or language-specific resources, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump. The authors conduct an extensive evaluation on nine languages, including high-resource CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts), demonstrating significant improvements over strong direct transfer baselines.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Feature Generation Method for Cross-Lingual NER:** The primary contribution is the innovative idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier not as a downstream task, but as a core component for generating dense, language-independent NER features. By grounding all n-grams to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the model gains access to semantic type information that is consistent across languages, effectively delexicalizing the task.
2.  **Extensive Empirical Validation and State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical study across a diverse set of nine languages (English, German, Dutch, Spanish, Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil). The proposed method significantly outperforms previous direct transfer models that rely on word clusters from parallel corpora (Täckström et al., 2012) and also surpasses a method for low-resource languages that requires more resources like parallel text and native speaker interaction (Zhang et al., 2016).
3.  **Insightful Analysis of the Method's Dependencies:** The authors provide a valuable analysis (Section 4.4) demonstrating the correlation between the performance of the wikifier features and the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. This analysis transparently explains the performance variations across languages and highlights the method's primary dependency.

### Strengths
1.  **High Impact and Novelty:** The approach is conceptually elegant and highly effective. It cleverly reframes the problem of finding language-independent features by leveraging the structured, multilingual knowledge in Wikipedia. The idea of wikifying all n-grams, rather than just pre-identified candidate mentions, provides a dense feature signal that is robust to the absence of a target-language NER system. The substantial F1 score improvements over strong baselines (e.g., from 40.4 to 62.1 on Dutch, 59.3 to 61.0 on German compared to Täckström's bitext model) demonstrate a significant advance for zero-shot cross-lingual NER.
2.  **Minimal Resource Requirements and High Generalizability:** A key strength of this method is its low barrier to entry. Unlike methods requiring large, sentence-aligned parallel corpora or interaction with native speakers, this approach only requires a multilingual Wikipedia dump. This makes the model readily applicable to the hundreds of languages present in Wikipedia, representing a major practical advantage for truly low-resource scenarios.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors evaluate on both high-resource and genuinely low-resource languages, including those with non-Latin scripts where lexical transfer completely fails. Including monolingual experiments validates that the wikifier features are additive and capture a signal not present in standard lexical and gazetteer features. The direct comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are appropriate and clearly position the work within the literature.
4.  **Strong Ablation and Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.4, which simulates a low-resource setting by artificially reducing the number of inter-language links for Spanish and German, is excellent. It provides convincing evidence that the quality of the features is directly tied to the coverage of the multilingual knowledge base. This kind of analysis adds significant depth to the paper and helps the reader understand the method's mechanics and limitations.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Detail on the Wikifier Component:** The entire method hinges on the cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016), but the paper treats it largely as a black box. Key details are missing: How does the wikifier handle ambiguity when multiple English Wikipedia pages are potential candidates? What is the computational overhead of wikifying every n-gram up to n=4 in a document? The modifications made to the system are briefly mentioned but not fully justified or explained. Given its centrality to the method, a more detailed description of the wikifier's mechanism and performance characteristics is necessary for reproducibility and a deeper understanding of potential failure points.
2.  **Unclear Feature Engineering Details:** The description of how wikifier outputs are converted into features in Section 3.3 is vague. The text states, "...we use the types of wi, wi+1, and wi−1, and the types of the n-grams which contain wi as features." This is ambiguous. For a given token, does it receive features from the 1-gram, the 2-gram starting at it, the 3-gram, the 4-gram, as well as n-grams *ending* at it? A concrete example illustrating exactly which features are generated for a specific token in a phrase would greatly improve clarity. Furthermore, the "ranker features in wikifier" are mentioned as being used, but there is no explanation of what these features are.
3.  **Missing Comparison with a Key Orthogonal Approach:** The paper categorizes prior work into projection, Wikipedia-based, and direct transfer. This work is a hybrid of the latter two. However, it misses a direct comparison with a pure Wikipedia-based approach, such as training a monolingual NER model on silver-standard data generated from Wikipedia links for the target language (as done by Nothman et al., 2012). This is a crucial comparison. The authors' method transfers a model's *weights*, while the other approach transfers *data*. It is not obvious which is superior, and comparing the two would provide significant insight into the best way to leverage Wikipedia for low-resource NER.
4.  **Handling of NILs and Out-of-KB Entities:** The caption of Figure 1 mentions that the wikifier can return NIL if no link is found. The paper does not explain how these NIL outputs are handled in the feature vector. Is a specific "NIL" feature added? Or is there simply an absence of wikifier features for that n-gram? This is a critical detail, as many n-grams (especially non-entities) will likely result in a NIL prediction. The model's ability to distinguish between entities and non-entities heavily depends on how this is handled.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide a more concrete example of the feature generation process from Section 3.3? For the token "Lehmann" in Figure 1, what would the full set of wikifier-based features be, considering it's a 1-gram and part of the 2-gram "Albrecht Lehmann"?
2.  What is the computational cost of this method at test time, specifically the wikification step for all n-grams, compared to a baseline model without these features? Is this practical for processing large volumes of text?
3.  How are NIL outputs from the wikifier represented as features? Is the absence of a link an informative signal that is explicitly provided to the classifier?
