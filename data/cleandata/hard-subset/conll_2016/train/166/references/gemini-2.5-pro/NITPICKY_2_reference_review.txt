**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper presents a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in the latter. The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. For any given text, all n-grams are grounded to the English Wikipedia, and the FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories of the resulting concepts are used as features for a standard NER model. This approach inverts the traditional pipeline where wikification is a downstream task of NER. The authors evaluate this method by training on English and testing on eight other languages (Spanish, Dutch, German, Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), demonstrating significant improvements over comparable direct transfer baselines.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel and effective method for creating language-agnostic NER features by leveraging cross-lingual wikification. This is a clever approach that avoids the need for parallel corpora.
2.  A comprehensive empirical study across a diverse set of nine languages, including several with non-Latin scripts, which rigorously benchmarks the proposed method in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings.
3.  An analysis correlating the method's performance with the target language's Wikipedia size and its intersection with the English Wikipedia, providing a clear characterization of the approach's limitations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this work is its core contribution: using wikification as a source of delexicalized features for NER. This is a conceptually simple yet powerful idea that elegantly sidesteps the complexities of projection-based methods that rely on often-scarce parallel corpora. By grounding all languages to a single knowledge base (English Wikipedia), it creates a truly shared feature space for transfer.
2.  **Extensive and Challenging Evaluation:** The authors perform an impressively thorough evaluation. They test on high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, including two (Bengali, Tamil) with non-Latin scripts. This diverse testbed provides convincing evidence of the method's broad applicability. The comparisons against Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are appropriate and demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art performance for this specific task formulation.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Limitations:** The analysis in Section 4.4, which investigates the impact of Wikipedia size and inter-language link density, is commendable. By simulating a low-resource setting (removing inter-language links), the authors provide a clear-eyed view of their method's dependencies and failure modes. This adds significant depth to the paper and helps readers understand when and why the approach is likely to succeed.
4.  **Demonstrated Value in Monolingual Settings:** The authors show that the proposed wikifier features are not just useful for cross-lingual transfer but also provide a consistent performance boost in standard monolingual NER settings (Table 2). This demonstrates that the features capture valuable semantic information not fully covered by traditional lexical and gazetteer features.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Questionable Label Pre-processing:** The method for handling labels in the low-resource datasets is a significant concern. The authors state that they "modify a MISC-tagged word to LOC tag if it is grounded to an entity with location as a FreeBase type, and remove all the other MISC tags". This procedure uses the wikifier—the source of the proposed features—to alter the ground-truth labels used for evaluation. This introduces circularity and risks artificially inflating the reported F1 scores, as the model is evaluated on data that has been "corrected" to align with its own feature representation. The evaluation should have been performed on the original, unmodified data.
2.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Clarity:** Several key implementation details are vague or contradictory.
    *   In Section 3.3, the authors state that for n-grams (n>1), they "generate title candidates only according to the whole string, not individual tokens." However, the illustrative example in Figure 1 appears to contradict this, showing "Albrecht" and "Lehmann" linked to separate person entities. This inconsistency is confusing.
    *   Figure 1 itself is poorly explained. It seems to show a mix of wikification ("Westdeutschland" -> "Western_Germany") and simple translation ("Flüchtlinge" -> "Refugee"). The meaning of the "Problem_solving" type is unclear. A figure intended to clarify the approach instead introduces ambiguity.
    *   The paper does not specify how the numerous FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories are encoded as features. Are they binary indicators? Hashed? This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and understanding the model's complexity.
3.  **Inconsistent Experimental Protocol:** The experimental setup for direct transfer is inconsistent across languages. For Bengali and Tamil (non-Latin scripts), the authors use *only* wikifier and previous-tag features, whereas for Latin-script languages, the full feature set (including lexical features) is used. This decision is post-hoc and tailored to the target language type. While justified by the poor performance of lexical features on non-matching scripts, it undermines the claim of a single, unified "direct transfer" model. A truly robust model should not require a different feature template based on the target language's script.
4.  **Minor Presentation Flaws:** While individually small, a number of minor flaws detract from the paper's overall quality.
    *   The date of the Wikipedia dump is specified as "March 2016", which is not precise enough for a resource that changes daily. A specific dump date (e.g., 2016-03-01) is required for reproducibility.
    *   Averaging F1 scores across datasets with different sizes and complexities (the "AVG" column in Table 2) is not a statistically sound practice and can be misleading.
    *   The choice of an averaged perceptron with greedy decoding is somewhat dated. While effective, a comparison or justification against a standard CRF-based model, which explicitly models sequence dependencies, would have strengthened the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the apparent contradiction between the wikifier modification described in Section 3.3 (using "the whole string, not individual tokens" for n>1) and the behavior shown in Figure 1, where individual tokens within a name are linked?
2.  Can you justify the decision to modify the ground-truth labels in the low-resource datasets using information from your proposed feature source? Have you conducted an evaluation on the original, unmodified labels? If so, what were the results? This seems critical for a fair assessment of your method's contribution.
3.  The experimental protocol uses different feature sets for Latin vs. non-Latin script languages in the direct transfer experiments. Could you comment on whether this per-language-type tuning weakens the "direct transfer" paradigm? Did you experiment with a single, unified model that learns to ignore lexical features when appropriate?
