### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a direct transfer method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The central idea is to link n-grams in a given text to English Wikipedia concepts, regardless of the source language. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with these linked concepts serve as rich, delexicalized features for a standard NER classifier. This model is trained on an annotated source language (e.g., English) and can be directly applied to any target language present in Wikipedia without requiring annotated data in that language.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A novel and effective application of cross-lingual wikification for direct transfer NER.** The work cleverly inverts the traditional NLP pipeline (NER before entity linking) by using wikification as a source of powerful, language-agnostic features. This provides an elegant way to ground diverse linguistic expressions to a unified semantic space (English Wikipedia), effectively bypassing the need for parallel corpora or complex projection methods.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages.** The authors validate their approach on nine languages, spanning high-resource languages from CoNLL datasets and five low-resource languages, including some with non-Latin scripts. This extensive evaluation demonstrates the method's robustness and broad applicability.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the key factors influencing the model's performance.** The paper provides a clear analysis linking the quality of the wikifier features, and thus NER performance, to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the density of its interlanguage links to English Wikipedia. This analysis is crucial for understanding the method's limitations and predicting its utility for a new language.

### Strengths

1.  **The core idea is simple, intuitive, and highly effective.** Using a cross-lingual wikifier to generate features is a powerful approach for creating delexicalized representations. It avoids the heavy resource requirements of competing methods like projection (which needs parallel data) or automatic annotation generation (which often requires language-specific heuristics). The impressive performance gains shown in the direct transfer experiments—for example, outperforming the Täckström et al. (2012) bitext cluster model by a large margin on CoNLL languages (e.g., 60.97 vs. 59.3 F1 on German; 62.10 vs. 58.4 on Spanish)—strongly validate the practical utility of this approach.

2.  **The experimental setup is thorough and the results are convincing.** The paper presents a well-structured evaluation that systematically builds its case.
    *   The monolingual experiments (Table 2) effectively demonstrate that wikifier features provide a signal complementary to strong lexical and gazetteer features, improving performance across all nine languages.
    *   The direct transfer experiments are well-designed, comparing against strong and relevant baselines. The model's superior performance on low-resource languages compared to Zhang et al. (2016), despite using fewer resources (no parallel text or human interaction), is a significant result.
    *   The ablation study in Section 4.4, which simulates the effect of smaller Wikipedia sizes by removing interlanguage links, provides a compelling explanation for the performance variance across languages and strengthens the paper's conclusions.

3.  **The method has broad applicability, especially for truly low-resource languages.** A key advantage of this approach is its minimal resource requirement: only a multilingual Wikipedia dump is needed. This makes the model readily applicable to hundreds of languages. The results on Bengali and Tamil are particularly noteworthy; while the absolute F1 scores are low, the wikifier-only model provides by far the strongest signal, demonstrating its value in scenarios where lexical-based transfer completely fails due to script differences.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The work is heavily dependent on a pre-existing, complex wikification system, with limited discussion of its properties and computational cost.** The proposed method's success is entirely contingent on the performance of the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier. However, the paper provides few details about this system, its potential failure modes, or the practical implications of its use. Wikifying *every n-gram up to n=4* in a document seems computationally intensive. A discussion of the inference time and computational trade-offs would be necessary for assessing the method's practicality. Without this, the contribution feels more like a successful application of an existing tool rather than a fully self-contained and reproducible method.

2.  **The strategy for handling non-Latin script languages is a manual, post-hoc heuristic.** The paper observes that for non-Latin script languages like Bengali and Tamil, including the base (lexical) features learned from English severely degrades performance, and thus proposes a "strategy" of only using wikifier features. This suggests a negative interference between the feature sets. However, the paper does not explore this phenomenon in depth or propose a principled solution. Why does performance drop so drastically (e.g., from 41.81 to 2.30 for Bengali)? A more robust approach might involve feature-group-specific regularization or an adaptive mechanism to weigh feature importance based on language or script similarity, rather than requiring a manual decision based on the target language's script.

3.  **The evaluation protocol for low-resource languages involves a training data modification that could inflate performance.** To handle the tag set mismatch between CoNLL (which has MISC) and the low-resource datasets (which do not), the authors modify the CoNLL training data by converting some MISC tags to LOC based on FreeBase types and removing the rest. While pragmatic, this tailors the source training data to the target test sets, potentially leaking information about the target label distribution. This slightly undermines the claim of a "pure" direct transfer setting. It would be important to quantify the impact of this preprocessing step to understand the model's performance on the original, unmodified source data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide details on the computational cost of your method, specifically the inference time required to process a document by wikifying all n-grams (n≤4)? How does this scale with document length and compare to a standard direct transfer NER model that does not use a wikifier?

2.  Your results show that including lexical features from an English-trained model is highly detrimental for non-Latin script languages. Have you considered more adaptive methods for combining feature sets to avoid the manual decision of dropping lexical features? For instance, could a model learn to down-weight lexical features when script mismatch is detected?

3.  Regarding the modification of MISC tags in the CoNLL training data: what is the performance on the low-resource languages if you train on the original, unmodified CoNLL dataset? This would help clarify the true, "out-of-the-box" capability of your method and the significance of this data preprocessing step.
