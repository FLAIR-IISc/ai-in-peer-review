### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a direct transfer method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER), where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to low-resource target languages without any annotated data in those languages. The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. For any given text, all n-grams (up to n=4) are linked to the English Wikipedia. The Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types associated with the resulting English Wikipedia titles are then used as features in a greedy BIO-based NER classifier. The authors show that these wikifier-derived features provide a strong, delexicalized signal that significantly improves performance over strong baselines on nine different languages, including those with non-Latin scripts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent features for NER.** The core contribution is the idea of reversing the typical NLP pipeline (NER then Entity Linking) by using a cross-lingual wikifier as a feature source for NER. This provides a rich set of semantic features that are inherently language-agnostic, as they are all grounded in a single knowledge base (English Wikipedia).
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages.** The paper presents a strong set of experiments on nine languages, including standard CoNLL benchmarks and five low-resource languages (two with non-Latin scripts). The consistent and significant gains over relevant baselines, including methods that use parallel corpora, strongly validate the proposed approach.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependency on knowledge base size.** The authors conduct an ablation study that directly links the quality of the wikifier features, and thus NER performance, to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia. This provides a clear understanding of the method's applicability and limitations.

### Strengths

1.  **The core idea is elegant, effective, and well-motivated.** Leveraging cross-lingual wikification to create a universal semantic feature space for NER is a clever approach to the direct transfer problem. It neatly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora or language-specific resources, requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump. The large performance gains shown in Table 2 (e.g., improving from 49.66 to 62.10 F1 on Dutch, and from 55.04 to 60.97 on German) provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of these features. The method is a significant step forward for zero-shot cross-lingual NER.

2.  **The experimental setup is thorough and convincing.** The authors evaluate their model on a wide and challenging range of languages, demonstrating its robustness. The comparisons are made against appropriate and strong baselines for both the CoNLL languages (Täckström et al., 2012) and the low-resource languages (Zhang et al., 2016). Notably, the proposed model outperforms these baselines while requiring fewer resources (e.g., no parallel text or native speaker interaction, unlike the baselines). The inclusion of monolingual experiments, multi-source training experiments, and detailed feature ablations further strengthens the paper's conclusions.

3.  **The paper provides valuable insights for practical application.** The analysis reveals that for languages with non-Latin scripts where lexical features do not transfer, using *only* the wikifier features yields the best results. This is a practical and non-obvious finding. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 4.4, which simulates smaller Wikipedias by removing inter-language links, clearly demonstrates the source of the features' power and provides a useful heuristic for predicting the method's potential success on a new language.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The computational cost of the proposed method is not addressed.** The paper states that "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" is passed to the cross-lingual wikifier. This suggests a very high computational overhead, as the number of n-grams is a large multiple of the document length. This could make the system impractical for processing large corpora or in time-sensitive applications. A discussion of the runtime performance and a comparison to the baseline models would be necessary to assess the practical viability of this approach.

2.  **The work relies heavily on a specific, complex wikifier system.** The entire method's success is predicated on the performance of the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier. While using a state-of-the-art system is reasonable, the paper provides little detail about this component, making it a "black box." This raises concerns about reproducibility and understanding the method's failure modes. It is unclear how sensitive the results are to the specific choice of wikifier or its internal architecture. The contribution would be stronger if the authors had demonstrated that the *idea* of using wikification for features is robust, perhaps by comparing against a simpler wikification baseline.

3.  **The handling of entity types, particularly MISC, seems ad-hoc.** To evaluate on low-resource languages, the authors alter the CoNLL training data by re-mapping some MISC entities to LOC and removing the rest. This data pre-processing step is non-standard and feels engineered to fit the target datasets. It is not clear how this decision affects performance or how it would generalize to a new language with a different tag set. An ablation study showing the impact of this heuristic (e.g., by training a 3-class model from scratch on the original CoNLL data) would have made the evaluation more transparent and the results more generalizable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the computational complexity of your feature extraction process? Specifically, what is the processing time per document for your full model compared to the "Base + Gazetteers" model, and how does this scale with document length?
2.  The modification of MISC tags in the CoNLL training data is a key pre-processing step for the low-resource experiments. Have you tried an alternative approach, such as training a 3-class (PER, LOC, ORG) model on the CoNLL data from the outset (ignoring MISC entities entirely during training)? How would this simpler approach affect performance on both the CoNLL test sets and the low-resource languages?
3.  Your analysis in Section 4.4 clearly shows that performance degrades as the Wikipedia intersection size decreases. Does this allow you to estimate a practical lower bound on the Wikipedia size (or number of inter-language links) below which your method is no longer effective compared to simpler baselines? This would be very valuable for researchers looking to apply your method to new, truly low-resource languages.
