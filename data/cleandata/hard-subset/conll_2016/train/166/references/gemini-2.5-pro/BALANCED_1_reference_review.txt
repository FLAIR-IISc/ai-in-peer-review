**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in that target language. The core idea is to generate language-independent features by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram in a text to its corresponding English Wikipedia page. The semantic types (e.g., from FreeBase) and categories associated with the linked English Wikipedia page are then used as features in a standard NER model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on nine languages, showing significant improvements over strong baselines, particularly for languages with reasonably sized Wikipedias.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and highly effective method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER. The key insight is to repurpose cross-lingual wikification, which is typically a downstream task, as a feature source, thereby providing rich, delexicalized signals for entity identification and classification.
2.  An extensive and convincing empirical evaluation across a diverse set of nine languages, including several low-resource languages and two with non-Latin scripts. The experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art results compared to comparable direct transfer and projection-based methods, validating the broad applicability of the proposed features.
3.  A strong analysis of the method's primary dependency: the size and connectivity of the target language's Wikipedia. This provides valuable insight into the method's limitations and helps explain the performance variance across different languages.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Effectiveness of the Core Idea:** The central idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier to create delexicalized NER features is both elegant and powerful. Reversing the traditional NER -> Wikification pipeline is a clever conceptual shift. The effectiveness of this idea is strongly supported by the experimental results. For instance, in the direct transfer experiments in Table 2, adding the wikifier features provides a dramatic F1 score increase of +12.44 for Dutch, +15.93 for German, and +17.51 for Spanish over the model with base and gazetteer features. This clearly demonstrates that the features capture crucial, language-independent information that is missed by other feature types.

2.  **Thorough and Broad Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their method across a wide and challenging range of languages, from high-resource CoNLL languages (EN, NL, DE, ES) to five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), including those with non-Latin scripts. This diverse testbed strengthens the paper's claims of generality. The comparisons are made against appropriate and strong baselines for the different settings (Täckström et al., 2012 for CoNLL; Zhang et al., 2016 for low-resource languages), and the proposed model consistently outperforms them, often by a large margin.

3.  **Excellent Analysis of Feature Quality and Limitations:** The analysis in Section 4.4 is a significant strength. By simulating smaller Wikipedias through the removal of interlanguage links (Table 3), the authors convincingly demonstrate the correlation between feature quality and the size/connectivity of the target language's Wikipedia. This ablation provides a clear, data-driven explanation for why the method is less effective on languages like Yoruba compared to Spanish or German. This thoughtful analysis adds depth to the paper, giving readers a clear understanding of the conditions under which this method is expected to perform well.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Opacity and Dependence on the Underlying Wikifier:** The success of the entire method is critically dependent on the performance of the cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016). However, the paper provides very few details about this component. What is its accuracy? What is its computational cost, especially when run on every n-gram up to length 4? This lack of detail makes the work harder to reproduce and evaluate. It is unclear if the success is due to the general idea of using wikification features, or the specific power of a particular, potentially complex and computationally expensive, wikifier. A brief characterization of the wikifier's performance and runtime would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Ad-hoc Handling of Mismatched Tagsets:** The strategy for harmonizing the NER tagsets between the CoNLL source data and the low-resource target data seems problematic. The authors state they "modify a MISC-tagged word to LOC tag if it is grounded to an entity with location as a FreeBase type" in the CoNLL training data. This process uses an external knowledge base (FreeBase) to re-label the source training data to better match the target data's label distribution. This introduces a form of information leakage from the target domain into the training process, making the direct transfer setup less "clean." It would be more methodologically sound to use a fixed, universal tagset (e.g., PER, LOC, ORG) and simply discard all MISC entities from the training data. This would allow for a clearer assessment of the contribution of the wikifier features alone.

3.  **Lack of Detail on Feature Implementation:** While the paper outlines the types of features derived from the wikifier (FreeBase types, categories, ranker scores), the description of their implementation is sparse. The text states, "we use the types of wi, wi+1, and wi−1, and the types of the n-grams which contain wi as features." This suggests a potentially massive feature space. A more concrete description of how these are encoded (e.g., as binary indicators, feature names) is needed. Furthermore, an ablation study breaking down the contribution of different wikifier feature components (e.g., FreeBase types vs. Wikipedia categories, top-1 vs. top-2 candidates, ranker features) would provide valuable insights into what makes this approach so effective.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the computational overhead of your method? What is the approximate processing time for a document when including the wikification of all n-grams, compared to the baseline model that uses only base and gazetteer features?

2.  Your method for harmonizing the MISC tag in the CoNLL training data involves re-labeling some entities to LOC based on FreeBase types. Could you clarify the motivation for this and report results for a simpler strategy, such as removing all MISC-tagged entities from the CoNLL training data? This would help isolate the performance gains attributable purely to your proposed feature set from those due to this specific data pre-processing choice.

3.  For the non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil), the "Wikifier only" model performs best, suggesting that lexical features from the English training data are actively harmful. Did you consider alternative training strategies for such cross-script scenarios, such as aggressively regularizing or entirely excluding lexical features during training, to prevent the model from overfitting to source-language signals that do not transfer?
