**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents a novel approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer (zero-shot) setting. The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate a rich set of language-independent features. For any given text in a target language, the method attempts to link all n-grams (up to n=4) to their corresponding English Wikipedia entries. From these links, features such as FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories are extracted and used in a standard NER model. The model is trained on an annotated source language (e.g., English) and can then be directly applied to a target language without any target-specific annotated data. The authors evaluate their method on nine languages from the CoNLL, REFLEX, and LORELEI datasets, demonstrating strong performance in both direct transfer and monolingual settings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for generating delexicalized features for cross-lingual NER.** The core contribution is the insight to use cross-lingual wikification not as a downstream task, but as a source of rich, semantic, and language-independent features for the NER model itself. This provides a powerful way to transfer knowledge from a high-resource language to a low-resource one, relying only on the existence of multilingual Wikipedia.
2.  **A comprehensive and strong empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages.** The paper validates its approach on nine languages, including those with non-Latin scripts and varying levels of Wikipedia coverage. The experiments show significant improvements over strong, comparable baselines in the direct transfer setting (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012) and demonstrate the utility of the proposed features for improving standard monolingual NER models as well.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependencies and limitations.** The authors conduct a targeted analysis (Sec 4.4) to quantify how the quality of the wikifier features, and thus the model's performance, depends on the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the number of inter-language links to English. This adds significant value by providing a clear understanding of when and why the method is expected to perform well.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The idea of inverting the traditional NER -> Entity Linking pipeline to use a wikifier for feature generation is clever and highly novel. The approach is conceptually simple yet powerful, providing a principled way to ground words and phrases from any language into a shared, language-agnostic semantic space (the English Wikipedia knowledge graph). The only resource requirement is a Wikipedia dump, making the method broadly applicable.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves impressive results, significantly outperforming the direct transfer model of Täckström et al. (2012) on the CoNLL languages (e.g., F1 of 60.97 vs. 45.6 for German, and 62.10 vs. 48.4 for Dutch). It also surpasses the results of Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, notably with far fewer resource requirements (no parallel text or native speaker interaction needed). The gains are consistent and substantial across most languages.
3.  **Thoroughness of Analysis:** The paper provides a well-rounded evaluation. Beyond the main direct transfer task, the authors demonstrate that the wikifier features also boost performance in monolingual settings, showing they capture information complementary to traditional lexical features. The experiments exploring multi-source training and the detailed ablation study on the impact of Wikipedia size (Table 3) make the paper's conclusions more robust and informative.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Critical Dependency on Wikipedia Coverage:** The method's effectiveness is fundamentally tied to the quality and size of the target language's Wikipedia, as the authors acknowledge. For truly low-resource languages, where Wikipedia is sparse, has few inter-language links, or is non-existent, the approach would likely fail. The F1 scores for Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil when using the full feature set (6.72, 2.99, respectively) are extremely low, highlighting this fragility. While the analysis in Sec 4.4 is good, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the method's performance floor and potential mitigation strategies for languages on the extreme low-resource end of the spectrum.
2.  **Lack of Clarity on Computational Cost and Feature Implementation:** The paper states that "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" is wikified. On the surface, this appears to be a computationally intensive process, which could be a significant barrier to practical application. A discussion of the computational overhead, including any pruning or optimization strategies employed, is needed to assess the method's feasibility. Additionally, the description of the feature set derived from the wikifier is somewhat abstract. A more concrete example of the full feature vector generated for a token would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Feature Combination:** The authors rightly note that their method is orthogonal to other direct transfer techniques, such as using cross-lingual word clusters (Täckström et al., 2012). While outperforming this baseline is a strong result, a crucial missing experiment is one that combines the proposed wikifier features with such orthogonal features. Demonstrating that the features are complementary and lead to further gains when used in tandem would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution by positioning it as a key component in a state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER system, rather than just an alternative approach.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram up to n=4? Was this process prohibitively slow for long documents, and were any heuristics or pruning strategies necessary to make it tractable?
2.  The results in Table 2 for non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil) suggest that including lexical features from an English-trained model is highly detrimental. Your proposed strategy is to simply omit these features. Have you considered a more adaptive approach, such as a model that learns to gate or weight the influence of lexical vs. wikifier features, perhaps conditioned on script similarity or other language properties?
3.  Your analysis simulating smaller Wikipedias by removing interlanguage links is very insightful. Does the underlying wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016) use any other cross-lingual signals (e.g., string similarity of titles, cognates) that would remain active even after links are removed? If so, how might this affect the interpretation of the results as a proxy for performance on a genuinely small, isolated Wikipedia?
