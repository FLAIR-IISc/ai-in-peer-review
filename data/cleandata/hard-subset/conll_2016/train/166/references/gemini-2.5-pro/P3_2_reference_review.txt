### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to train an NER model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) using a set of language-independent features, and then apply this model directly to a low-resource target language without any target-language training data.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Representation for Direct Transfer NER:** The primary contribution is the development and application of features derived from a cross-lingual wikifier. By linking n-grams in any source text to the English Wikipedia, the authors extract language-agnostic signals like FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories. This cleverly inverts the traditional NLP pipeline where NER precedes entity linking, instead using linking-style signals to perform NER.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that this approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art direct transfer methods on standard CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German). Furthermore, it shows strong performance on five low-resource languages, surpassing a competitive baseline that requires more resources (parallel text and native speaker interaction).

3.  **Thorough Analysis of Feature Utility:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the proposed features. They demonstrate their utility not only in the cross-lingual setting but also for improving monolingual models. Crucially, they conduct an insightful ablation study (Sec 4.4) that directly links the quality of the wikifier features, and thus NER performance, to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with English Wikipedia.

### Strengths

1.  **Effectiveness and Simplicity of the Core Idea:** The central idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features is both elegant and highly effective. The massive F1-score improvements shown in Table 2 (e.g., from 49.66 to 62.10 on Dutch, 55.04 to 60.97 on German) over a strong baseline with gazetteers are compelling. This represents a significant step forward for zero-shot cross-lingual NER, relying only on Wikipedia, which is a more readily available resource than large parallel corpora.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors evaluate their model on a wide and diverse set of languages, from widely-studied European languages to truly low-resource languages, including two with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil). This diversity makes their claims much more robust and generalizable. The comparisons against strong and appropriate baselines (`Täckström et al., 2012`; `Zhang et al., 2016`) are well-executed and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.

3.  **Insightful Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting results. The analysis in Section 4.4, which simulates a low-resource setting by artificially reducing the number of inter-language links, provides a clear and convincing explanation for the performance variance across languages. This deepens the reader's understanding of the method's dependencies and limitations. Similarly, the analysis of performance on non-Latin script languages, and the conclusion that lexical features are harmful in that specific scenario, is a subtle but important finding.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clearly laid out with an illustrative example in Figure 1. The model and features are described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are structured logically. This clarity makes the paper's contributions easy to appreciate and facilitates reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost:** The methodology involves running a cross-lingual wikifier on *every n-gram (n ≤ 4)* in a document. This appears to be a computationally intensive process, especially at inference time. The paper does not provide any discussion of the runtime performance, scalability, or potential trade-offs (e.g., could one achieve similar performance by only wikifying candidate noun phrases?). For practical applications, this is a significant consideration that has been omitted.

2.  **Dependence on Wikipedia Quality and English-Centricity:** The method's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality and size of a language's Wikipedia, as the authors' own analysis shows. This limits its applicability for languages with very nascent Wikipedia communities. Furthermore, the reliance on the English Wikipedia as the central knowledge hub may introduce cultural or topical biases. While this is a practical choice, a discussion of the potential drawbacks or alternatives (e.g., using Wikidata as a language-agnostic pivot) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Use of a Classical Model Architecture:** The paper employs a well-established but somewhat dated linear model (averaged perceptron). While this choice is justifiable for isolating the effect of the novel features, the field of NER has largely transitioned to neural architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, Transformers). It remains an open question how these powerful, language-independent features would interact with modern neural models. Demonstrating their utility in a state-of-the-art neural architecture would make the contribution even more impactful and relevant to the current research landscape.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational overhead of the proposed method at inference time? Specifically, what is the runtime cost of wikifying every n-gram compared to the baseline NER model, and have you explored strategies to reduce this cost, such as only wikifying spans identified by a simple chunker, without significant loss in performance?

2.  Your analysis convincingly links performance to the size of the intersection with English Wikipedia. For a language with a very small or non-existent Wikipedia (e.g., many indigenous languages), your method would presumably not work well. Do you have any thoughts on how this approach could be adapted or what alternative sources of cross-lingual knowledge could be leveraged in such truly data-starved scenarios?

3.  The features from the top 2 candidates from the wikifier are used. Was there an empirical reason for choosing k=2? How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter?
