### Review

**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model trained on a high-resource language is applied to a target language with no annotated data. The core idea is to generate language-independent features by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram in a text to the English Wikipedia. The types and categories associated with the linked English Wikipedia pages (e.g., from FreeBase) serve as robust, delexicalized features. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through extensive experiments on both high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, including some with non-Latin scripts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and highly effective feature generation method for zero-shot cross-lingual NER.** The key contribution is the idea of using cross-lingual wikification over all n-grams—not just pre-identified mentions—as a source of powerful, language-agnostic semantic features. This simple yet elegant approach significantly outperforms previous direct transfer methods that rely on lexical similarity or parallel corpora.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across a diverse set of languages.** The paper provides a strong empirical validation of the method on nine languages, importantly including several truly low-resource and non-Latin script languages. This broad evaluation, complete with insightful analysis of the method's dependency on Wikipedia size, provides a realistic picture of its capabilities and limitations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simple, Effective, and Novel Approach:** The central idea of leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier to create a shared semantic feature space (rooted in English Wikipedia) is innovative and compelling. It elegantly sidesteps the need for parallel data required by many cross-lingual methods (e.g., word cluster projection). The empirical results are very strong, showing substantial gains over strong baselines on multiple languages (e.g., improving F1 from 48.4 to 62.1 on Dutch and from 45.6 to 60.97 on German compared to Täckström's bitext cluster model). This demonstrates the high utility of the proposed features.
2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Experiments:** The evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their method on a wide variety of languages, from well-resourced CoNLL languages to low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), which is a significant strength. Comparing against strong and relevant baselines like Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) effectively contextualizes the contribution. Furthermore, the inclusion of monolingual and multi-source training experiments demonstrates the general utility and robustness of the wikifier features.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Method Limitations:** The paper does not shy away from the limitations of its approach. The analysis in Section 4.4, which ablates the components of the wikifier and simulates smaller Wikipedia sizes, provides a clear and valuable understanding of *why* the method's performance varies across languages. It convincingly ties performance to the size of the target language Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia, which is a crucial insight for researchers looking to apply this method to new languages.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Impact:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is described clearly. By repurposing an existing NLP tool (wikification) for a different stage of the pipeline (feature generation for NER), this work opens up a promising direction for cross-lingual transfer learning. The approach is generalizable and could potentially be applied to other sequence labeling tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Heavy Reliance on an Unanalyzed "Black Box" Wikifier:** The success of the entire method is critically dependent on the performance and characteristics of the Tsai and Roth (2016) cross-lingual wikifier. However, the paper provides little discussion of this component's potential drawbacks. Specifically, the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram up to n=4 could be prohibitive, making the approach impractical for large-scale application. A discussion on the efficiency trade-offs and the sensitivity of the NER model to the underlying wikifier's quality is missing. It is unclear if any competent cross-lingual linker would suffice, or if specific properties of the chosen system are essential.
2.  **Ambiguity in Feature Engineering from Overlapping N-grams:** The paper states that for each word *wi*, it uses the types of n-grams containing *wi* as features. This process is underspecified. A single word can be part of multiple n-grams (e.g., "York" in "New York Times"), each potentially linking to entities with different, even conflicting, types (e.g., PER, LOC, ORG). The paper does not explain how these potentially numerous and conflicting features are aggregated or represented for a single token. This lack of detail makes a crucial part of the methodology difficult to understand and reproduce.
3.  **Ad-hoc Handling of the MISC Entity Type:** To align the CoNLL training data with the low-resource language test sets, the authors apply a heuristic: MISC entities are re-labeled as LOC if their wikified entity has a location type, otherwise they are removed. While pragmatic, this data manipulation feels ad-hoc and its impact is not analyzed. It would strengthen the paper to report how many MISC entities were converted versus deleted and to compare this approach against a simpler baseline, such as removing all MISC entities, to ensure the heuristic itself isn't a major, unacknowledged contributor to performance.
4.  **Limited Discussion of Alternative Cross-Lingual Approaches:** While the paper's focus is on direct transfer, the discussion of related work could be strengthened by more directly contrasting the pros and cons of this approach with annotation projection, especially in the context of the low-resource languages tested. The paper compares against Zhang et al. (2016), but that method has very different dependencies (e.g., native speaker interaction). A clearer positioning against projection-based methods (which require parallel data but may be more robust for distant language pairs) would provide a more complete picture of the cross-lingual NER landscape.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed method? Specifically, what is the overhead of wikifying every n-gram (up to n=4) compared to the baseline feature extraction, and how does this affect the practicality of your approach for processing large corpora?
2.  Could you please clarify how you handle the feature representation for a token when it is part of multiple n-grams with potentially conflicting type information? For instance, for the token "York" in "New York Times", how are the features from the wikification of "York", "New York", and "New York Times" combined?
3.  Regarding your preprocessing of the MISC tag in the CoNLL training data: could you provide statistics on the proportion of MISC entities that were converted to LOC versus those that were removed? Have you evaluated the impact of this heuristic against a simpler baseline, such as removing all MISC tags from the training data?
