**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data in that language. The central proposal is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The method involves linking n-grams in the source and target text to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia) and using the associated metadata (e.g., FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) as features for a standard sequence labeling model. The authors evaluate their approach on CoNLL datasets (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages, demonstrating significant improvements over established direct transfer baselines that do not require parallel data and outperforming a comparable method that does. The paper also includes analysis on the impact of Wikipedia size and the effect of multi-source training.

**Contributions**

1.  **An extensive empirical study of a direct transfer NER system using wikification-derived features.** The primary contribution is the thorough evaluation of this approach across a wide range of languages, including several with non-Latin scripts. The paper provides a strong demonstration that features from a cross-lingual wikifier can serve as effective delexicalized signals for NER.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on standard cross-lingual NER benchmarks.** The proposed model significantly outperforms the widely-cited direct transfer baseline from Täckström et al. (2012) on the CoNLL languages. This provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed feature set compared to methods based on cross-lingual word clusters.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodologically Sound and Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors compare against the correct and most relevant baselines for the direct transfer task (Täckström et al., 2012). The inclusion of nine languages, covering different language families and scripts, provides a comprehensive view of the method's applicability. The ablation studies (e.g., "Wikifier only" features) and analysis of the correlation between performance and Wikipedia size (Section 4.4) are well-executed and add significant value to the paper.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains over the Täckström et al. baseline are substantial (e.g., over 15 F1 points on German, over 6 F1 points on Dutch). Outperforming a strong baseline that requires parallel corpora (Täckström's bitext clusters) with a method that only requires Wikipedia is a significant achievement and a strong argument for acceptance. The results on the low-resource languages are also convincing, beating a more resource-intensive method from Zhang et al. (2016).
3.  **Practicality and Simplicity of the Approach:** The proposed method has a clear practical advantage: it does not require parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for many cross-lingual transfer techniques. By relying only on Wikipedia, the approach is, in principle, applicable to a large number of languages. The feature engineering itself is straightforward, building upon a well-established NER model (Ratinov and Roth, 2009).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Concept:** The idea of using Wikipedia categories or types as features for monolingual NER is not new, as acknowledged by the authors' citation of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). The main conceptual shift here is the application to the cross-lingual, direct-transfer setting. While the engineering and evaluation are solid, the paper primarily presents a novel application and combination of existing components (a standard NER model and a pre-existing cross-lingual wikifier) rather than a fundamentally new NER methodology.
2.  **Heavy Reliance on an External "Black Box" System:** The success of the entire method is contingent on the performance of the cross-lingual wikifier by Tsai and Roth (2016). The paper treats this component as a given, with limited analysis of its own failure modes or how its specific architecture contributes to the results. The quality, coverage, and biases of this wikifier are paramount, yet they are not deeply interrogated. The work feels more like a successful application of a powerful tool than a self-contained, foundational contribution to NER. The strong dependency on the English Wikipedia as a central hub may also introduce an inherent anglocentric bias.
3.  **Questionable Practicality for Genuinely Low-Resource Languages:** While the method is tested on several low-resource languages, the absolute performance on languages with non-Latin scripts and small Wikipedias is extremely low (e.g., F1 of 6.72 for Bengali, 2.99 for Tamil in the full direct-transfer setting). Although this outperforms the baseline (which fails completely), such low scores are of limited practical use. The paper's analysis confirms that performance is highly correlated with Wikipedia size, which suggests the method is not a robust solution for the long tail of truly low-resource languages that lack a significant Wikipedia presence.
4.  **Use of a Dated Base Model:** The base NER model is from Ratinov and Roth (2009), an averaged perceptron model. While a strong model for its time, it predates the widespread adoption of neural architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) which have since become the standard for NER. It is unclear if the dramatic improvements from wikifier features would hold when added to a more powerful, modern baseline that may already capture more abstract representations. A comparison using a contemporary neural model as the base would have made the contribution more robust and relevant to the current state of the art.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the conceptual novelty of your approach in light of prior work like Kazama and Torisawa (2007)? Is the primary contribution the demonstration that this established idea is highly effective for the *cross-lingual direct transfer* setting, or is there a more fundamental methodological innovation?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the specific choice of the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier? The paper's contribution seems tightly coupled with this particular system. Have you considered how performance might vary with a different cross-lingual entity linker, and what properties of a wikifier are most crucial for this task (e.g., precision, recall, ranking confidence)?
3.  Given the extremely low F1 scores for Bengali and Tamil, do you see a path forward for this methodology for truly low-resource languages, or does this represent a hard limit for approaches that rely on the intersection with English Wikipedia?
