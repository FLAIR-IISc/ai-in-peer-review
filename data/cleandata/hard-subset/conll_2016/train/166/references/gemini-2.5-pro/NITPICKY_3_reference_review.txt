**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes a direct transfer method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to ground all n-grams in a text to the English Wikipedia. The Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types associated with the resulting English Wikipedia titles are then used as language-independent features. This approach reverses the traditional NER-then-wikification pipeline. The model is trained on a high-resource language (primarily English) and applied to target languages with no annotated data. The authors evaluate the method on CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, including two with non-Latin scripts.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The novel use of cross-lingual wikification as a source of dense, language-independent features for direct transfer NER. This is a clever and well-motivated idea.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation across nine languages demonstrating that these wikification-based features provide a strong signal, significantly outperforming prior direct transfer methods and even a system that uses parallel data and human interaction.
3.  A useful analysis of the method's dependency on the size of the target language's Wikipedia, which sheds light on the approach's limitations and applicability.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The core concept of using a wikifier to generate features for NER, rather than as a downstream task, is highly innovative. It elegantly sidesteps the need for language-specific lexical features by mapping diverse surface forms to a canonical, feature-rich English knowledge base.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves substantial gains over the Täckström et al. (2012) baseline on CoNLL languages. More impressively, it outperforms the system of Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, despite using significantly fewer resources (no parallel data or human-in-the-loop interaction). This demonstrates the practical value and efficiency of the proposed approach.
3.  **Broad Language Coverage:** The experiments cover a diverse set of languages, including those with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil). This provides a challenging testbed and strengthens the claims of the method's generalizability. The analysis of performance degradation for languages with small Wikipedias (Section 4.4) is insightful and adds credibility to the study.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the promising results, the paper is marred by a lack of methodological precision, inconsistent descriptions, and questionable experimental choices that undermine the work's reproducibility and rigor.

1.  **Methodological Ambiguity:** The description of the feature engineering, the heart of the paper's contribution, is insufficiently detailed.
    *   In Section 3.3, the authors state they use "types of wi, wi+1, and wi−1, and the types of the n-grams which contain wi as features." This is vague. It is unclear how features from multiple n-grams containing `wi` (e.g., `(wi)`, `(wi-1, wi)`, `(wi, wi+1)`) are aggregated or represented. How are the types from the top 2 candidates from the wikifier combined? Are they distinct features (e.g., `rank1_type=X`, `rank2_type=Y`)? This lack of detail makes the work very difficult to reproduce.
    *   The modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier are not clearly specified. The claim that including other mentions as features "makes no sense" because of TF-IDF context words is a justification, not a precise description of what was changed in the ranking model.

2.  **Inconsistent Descriptions:** There is a critical contradiction between the example and the method description. Figure 1's caption states, "We ground each word to the English Wikipedia," and the diagram shows word-level linking. However, Section 3.3 explicitly states the goal is to "ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4)". This is a fundamental inconsistency regarding the basic unit being processed by the wikifier.

3.  **Questionable Data Processing:** The pre-processing of training data described in Section 4.1 is concerning. The authors "modify a MISC-tagged word to LOC tag if it is grounded to an entity with location as a FreeBase type, and remove all the other MISC tags" from the CoNLL training data when testing on low-resource languages. This uses the wikifier—the core component of the proposed method—to alter the "gold" training labels. This introduces a strong circularity, as the model is effectively being trained on information derived from its own features. The impact of this step is not quantified, and it may unfairly inflate performance by tailoring the training data to what the wikifier can easily recognize.

4.  **Flawed Analysis for Non-Latin Scripts:** The paper observes that including lexical features causes a catastrophic performance collapse on Bengali and Tamil and concludes that the strategy should be to use "wikifier features only" for such languages. This is presented as a strategic choice rather than a major flaw. A robust model should learn to assign low weights to non-transferable lexical features. The averaged perceptron's complete failure to do so suggests a significant modeling weakness (e.g., lack of regularization) that is not discussed or analyzed. The recommendation to simply omit features feels like an ad-hoc fix for a deeper problem.

5.  **Minor Formatting and Clarity Issues:**
    *   In Table 2, the "AVG" column, an average of F1 scores across disparate datasets, is not a standard or meaningful metric and should be removed.
    *   The comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is slightly obscured. A direct comparison should be made between the authors' "Base Features" and the "Täckström baseline", but the text does not emphasize this.
    *   Footnote 4 provides a raw URL without an access date, which is improper citation practice.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the CoNLL data modification (Section 4.1), could you clarify what percentage of MISC tags were converted to LOC versus being removed entirely? How do you justify using your system's core component to alter the training data, and have you measured the performance impact of this single pre-processing step?
2.  Could you please resolve the contradiction between Figure 1 (word-level wikification) and Section 3.3 (n-gram wikification)? What is the precise input to the wikifier?
3.  The model's catastrophic failure on non-Latin script languages when lexical features are included suggests a major modeling issue. Did your averaged perceptron implementation include any regularization? Why do you believe the model is unable to learn to ignore these zero-overlap features?
4.  Please provide a concrete example of the full feature vector generated for a single token, `wi`, specifying how types from multiple n-grams and the top two wikifier candidates are encoded. For instance, for the word "Lehmann" in Figure 1, what would the exact wikifier-based features be?
