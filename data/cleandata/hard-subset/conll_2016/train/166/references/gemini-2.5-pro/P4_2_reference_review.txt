### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a direct transfer model for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER). The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate a rich set of language-independent features. For a given text in a target language, the method attempts to link every n-gram (up to n=4) to an entry in the English Wikipedia. The Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types associated with the linked English entries are then used as features for a standard sequence labeling NER model. The model is trained on an annotated source language (e.g., English) and can be directly applied to any target language present in Wikipedia without requiring parallel corpora or target-language annotations. The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on nine languages, including four high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts), demonstrating significant performance gains over strong baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER.** The core contribution is the repurposing of cross-lingual wikification, typically a downstream task, as a feature engineering step for NER. By grounding all n-grams to a common, language-agnostic knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the model can leverage rich semantic type information that transfers effectively across languages.
2.  **An extensive empirical validation of the proposed method.** The paper presents a strong set of experiments across a wide and diverse set of nine languages. The method is shown to substantially outperform comparable direct transfer models on CoNLL datasets and a state-of-the-art weakly-supervised system on five low-resource languages, despite using fewer resources (e.g., no parallel text).
3.  **An insightful analysis of the relationship between knowledge base quality and model performance.** The authors conduct experiments simulating smaller or less-connected Wikipedias, providing clear evidence that the effectiveness of the proposed features is directly correlated with the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the density of its interlanguage links to English. This analysis helps to characterize the method's limitations and applicability.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel and effective approach to feature engineering for cross-lingual transfer. Using a wikifier on all n-grams to create dense, delexicalized features is a clever way to bridge the gap between languages. This approach elegantly sidesteps the need for parallel corpora, a major bottleneck for many cross-lingual methods. The technical implementation, which integrates these features into a well-established NER architecture, is sound and well-explained.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The proposed model significantly outperforms the direct transfer baselines from Täckström et al. (2012) on the CoNLL languages (e.g., achieving 62.10 F1 on Dutch vs. the baseline's 58.4). More impressively, it outperforms the model of Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages, even though the competing method requires more resources, including parallel data and interaction with a native speaker. These results strongly support the efficacy of the proposed wikifier features.

3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive and well-designed. The inclusion of nine languages, spanning different language families, resource levels, and writing systems (Latin and non-Latin), demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The paper includes multiple experimental settings (monolingual, direct transfer, multi-source transfer) that provide a holistic view of the method's performance. The ablation study in Table 3, which analyzes the impact of Wikipedia size and interlanguage links, is particularly insightful and adds significant depth to the paper by proactively addressing the method's boundary conditions.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, aided by the illustrative example in Figure 1. The methodology, experimental setup, and analysis are described with sufficient detail to allow for critical assessment and potential replication.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The proposed method requires running a cross-lingual wikifier on every n-gram (n≤4) in the input text. This process appears to be computationally expensive, especially when compared to the feature extraction in standard NER models. The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational cost (e.g., processing time, memory usage) associated with this feature generation step. This is a significant practical consideration that could limit the model's applicability to large-scale or time-sensitive tasks.

2.  **Dependence on Wikipedia Quality:** As the authors' own analysis confirms, the model's performance is critically dependent on the size and quality of the target language's Wikipedia and its linkage to the English Wikipedia. For truly low-resource languages where Wikipedia coverage is sparse or non-existent, the method would be ineffective. While the paper is transparent about this limitation, it could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of potential failure modes or mitigation strategies (e.g., using other multilingual knowledge bases like Wikidata). This dependency limits the method's claim of being applicable to "all languages in Wikipedia," as performance for many will be poor.

3.  **Ambiguity in Tag Schema Harmonization:** For the low-resource language experiments, the authors perform several pre-processing steps on the entity tags: removing `TIME` and `TTL` tags, and converting `MISC` tags in the CoNLL training data to `LOC` if their FreeBase type is location-related, while removing all other `MISC` tags. This ad-hoc harmonization simplifies the label space and may conflate evaluation. It is unclear what proportion of `MISC` entities were removed versus converted, making it difficult to assess the true difficulty of the task and the impact of this decision on the reported F1 scores.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you quantify the computational overhead of your feature extraction process? For instance, what is the average processing time per document or sentence for the full model (`+Wikifier`) compared to the baseline model (`Base Features`)?

2.  Regarding the tag schema harmonization for the low-resource experiments: what percentage of `MISC` entity instances in the CoNLL training data were removed, and what percentage were converted to `LOC`? Could this pre-processing step have contributed to the performance gap by simplifying the task for the model trained on CoNLL data?

3.  The cross-lingual wikifier is described as grounding strings to the "intersection of the English and the target language Wikipedia." Does this imply that an entity must have a page in both the target and English Wikipedias to be a candidate? If so, could this limitation hurt recall on entities that are prominent in the target language but not notable enough for an English Wikipedia entry?
