**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a low-resource target language. The core idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to link n-grams in the input text to English Wikipedia entries. The FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories associated with these entries are then used as language-independent features for a standard NER classifier. The authors evaluate this approach on nine languages, showing significant improvements over direct transfer baselines that rely on lexical or cross-lingual word cluster features.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  A novel and effective method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER by leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier. This creatively inverts the traditional pipeline where NER precedes entity linking.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation on a diverse set of languages, including CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts), demonstrating the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed features.
3.  A valuable analysis demonstrating the correlation between the performance of the wikifier features and the size of the target language's Wikipedia, providing insight into the method's limitations and applicability.

**Strengths**

1.  The central idea of using wikification to generate features for NER, rather than as a downstream task, is elegant and compelling. It provides a principled way to tap into a vast, multilingual knowledge base (Wikipedia/FreeBase) to create rich, delexicalized features that can bridge the gap between languages.

2.  The experimental results are strong and convincing. The proposed model significantly outperforms the Täckström et al. (2012) baseline on the CoNLL languages, even their stronger model which requires parallel text for inducing word clusters. The gains are substantial (e.g., >10 F1 points on Dutch and German), demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

3.  The method's primary dependency is on a multilingual Wikipedia dump, making it highly generalizable and applicable to a wide range of languages without the need for parallel corpora. This is a significant advantage over projection-based methods or those requiring cross-lingual word embeddings trained on parallel text. The paper effectively demonstrates this by applying the method to languages with disparate scripts like Bengali and Tamil.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility.** The paper omits several crucial details about the feature engineering and experimental setup. The description of how wikifier features are generated is vague: "we use the types of `wi`, `wi+1`, and `wi−1`, and the types of the n-grams which contain `wi` as features." This implies a feature conjunction over all n-grams (up to n=4) containing a word, which could lead to a feature explosion. The paper does not specify how this is handled. Furthermore, the modifications to the wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016) are mentioned but not sufficiently justified or evaluated. Crucial parameters, such as setting n=4, are relegated to a footnote, which is poor practice. These omissions make the work very difficult to reproduce.

2.  **Weakened Baseline Model.** The authors claim to use the model from Ratinov and Roth (2009) as their base, but explicitly state they do not use POS tags, which are a core and powerful feature in the original model. The justification provided (inability to get universal POS tags) is a logistical limitation, not a principled modeling choice. This decision weakens the "Base Features" baseline, potentially overstating the relative improvement gained from the gazetteer and wikifier features. A more rigorous comparison would have involved using a state-of-the-art universal POS tagger to create a stronger baseline.

3.  **Questionable Pre-processing of Gold Labels.** The procedure for handling entity tags in the low-resource language experiments is concerning. The authors state: "We modify a MISC-tagged word to LOC tag if it is grounded to an entity with location as a FreeBase type, and remove all the other MISC tags in the training data." This constitutes modifying the gold training labels using information from the very knowledge base (FreeBase) that is used to generate the proposed features. This introduces circularity and confounds the evaluation, as the model is trained on labels that have been "pre-aligned" with its feature space. The paper fails to report how many MISC tags were converted versus removed, making it impossible to assess the impact of this decision.

4.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues.** The paper suffers from a number of minor but distracting presentation flaws. Figure 1 is not as clear as it could be; the listed FreeBase types (e.g., "hobby", "media_genre") do not intuitively map to the PER/LOC NER tags shown, obscuring the figure's point. Table 2 is extremely dense, attempting to present dataset statistics, monolingual results, and direct transfer results with multiple baselines in a single block, hindering readability. The citation style appears inconsistent, and as noted above, critical details are hidden in footnotes.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding your base model: Could you report results for a "Base Features" model that *does* include universal POS tags? This would provide a clearer picture of the true performance gain attributable to your wikifier features on top of a stronger, more standard NER feature set.

2.  Concerning the modification of MISC tags in the CoNLL training data: Can you please quantify the number of MISC tags that were converted to LOC versus the number that were removed entirely? Can you justify this decision further and discuss its potential to bias the training process in favor of your FreeBase-derived features?

3.  Could you provide a more concrete specification of the wikifier features? For a single token, what is the exact set of features generated from all overlapping n-grams? How did you manage the potential for combinatorial feature explosion?
