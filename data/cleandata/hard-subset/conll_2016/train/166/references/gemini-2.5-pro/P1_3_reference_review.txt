### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language and applied to a low-resource target language. The core idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to link n-grams in the source and target languages to a common knowledge base (English Wikipedia). The types (e.g., FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) associated with the linked Wikipedia entries are then used as language-independent features for an NER model. This approach cleverly inverts the traditional NER-then-wikification pipeline. The method is evaluated on nine languages, showing significant improvements over strong baselines that require either parallel data or manual intervention.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER by leveraging a cross-lingual wikifier. This provides a principled way to bridge the lexical gap between languages without requiring parallel corpora.
2.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation on a wide range of languages, including CoNLL languages (Spanish, German, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), demonstrating the robustness and generalizability of the proposed features.
3.  An insightful analysis of the method's performance relative to the size of the target language's Wikipedia, clarifying the conditions under which the wikifier features are most effective.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The central idea of using wikification as a pre-processing step to generate features for NER is a significant conceptual contribution. It reverses the standard pipeline and directly tackles the key challenge of direct transfer: finding potent, delexicalized features. By grounding words and phrases in a shared, language-agnostic semantic space (English Wikipedia concepts), the method offers an elegant solution that is both powerful and intuitive. This approach has the potential to become a standard technique for low-resource NLP tasks beyond NER.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results in the direct transfer setting. It significantly outperforms the direct transfer model of Täckström et al. (2012) on CoNLL languages, for instance achieving 60.97 F1 on German vs. their 45.6 F1 (without clusters). More impressively, it outperforms the model of Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages, despite requiring fewer resources (no parallel text or native speaker interaction). These strong results across a diverse set of languages provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the wikifier-based features.

3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Accessibility:** A major strength of this work is its minimal resource requirements. The method relies only on multilingual Wikipedia dumps, which are readily available for hundreds of languages. Unlike many cross-lingual methods, it obviates the need for scarce resources like sentence-aligned parallel corpora or bilingual lexicons. This makes the approach highly practical and scalable to a vast number of low-resource languages, significantly lowering the barrier to developing NER systems for them.

4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting final numbers. The analysis in Section 4.4, which systematically degrades the interlanguage link information to simulate smaller Wikipedias, provides a clear and insightful connection between knowledge base size and feature quality. Furthermore, the experiments with multi-source training (Section 4.5) demonstrate the model's flexibility and potential for further improvement by combining signals from multiple high-resource languages. This analytical depth adds significant value to the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Opaqueness of the Core Wikifier Component:** The success of the entire method is critically dependent on the performance of the underlying cross-lingual wikifier from Tsai and Roth (2016). However, the paper provides insufficient analysis of this component. The wikifier's performance (e.g., precision, recall, and failure modes) on the different languages, especially the low-resource ones, is not discussed. For languages with low Wikipedia coverage or complex morphology, the wikifier might return NIL for a large proportion of n-grams, rendering the features sparse and ineffective. A more in-depth analysis of the wikifier's behavior and its direct impact on NER performance would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Potentially Outdated or Incomplete Baselines:** The comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) is relevant, but the field of cross-lingual representation learning has advanced significantly since. A more contemporary and challenging baseline would be a direct transfer model that uses pre-trained static cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., MUSE, VecMap). These models can be trained on monolingual corpora only and often serve as very strong baselines for cross-lingual transfer tasks. The absence of such a comparison makes it harder to situate the contribution in the context of modern cross-lingual methods.

3.  **Lack of Detail on Feature Engineering and Complexity:** The paper states that "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" is wikified. This brute-force approach seems computationally very expensive, and the paper lacks any discussion of runtime or scalability. Furthermore, it is unclear how features from multiple, overlapping n-grams that cover the same token are aggregated. For a given token, it could receive conflicting type information from different n-grams (e.g., `(w_i)` vs. `(w_{i-1}, w_i)`). The paper does not specify how these potentially numerous and contradictory features are handled by the model, which is a crucial implementation detail.

4.  **Ad-hoc Handling of the MISC Tag:** The decision to modify the CoNLL training data by converting or removing MISC tags based on the schema of the target low-resource languages is problematic. This pre-processing step introduces knowledge about the test set's label distribution into the training process, which weakens the claim of a pure, "direct transfer" model. This raises questions about the model's generalizability to a new target language where the tag set is unknown or contains a MISC category. A more principled approach to handling tag set mismatches would be more convincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide statistics on the wikifier's coverage for the different languages? Specifically, what percentage of n-grams in the test sets were successfully linked to an English Wikipedia entity, and how does this coverage correlate with the final F1 scores? This would help clarify the relationship between wikifier performance and NER performance.
2.  The modification of MISC tags in the source training data seems tailored to the specific low-resource datasets used in this paper. How do you propose your system would handle a new target language that has a different label schema (e.g., one that includes MISC or other entity types not present in the modified CoNLL data)? Does this data manipulation step limit the general applicability of your trained models?
3.  The process of wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 seems computationally intensive. Could you provide details on the feature extraction time for a typical document? Have you explored alternative, more efficient strategies, such as using a high-recall, low-precision mention candidate generator before running the wikifier?
