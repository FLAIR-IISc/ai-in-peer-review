### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model trained on a high-resource language is applied to a target language with no annotated data. The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The authors propose a novel approach that inverts the traditional NLP pipeline: instead of using NER to identify mentions for wikification, they wikify all n-grams in a sentence and use the resulting information (e.g., FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories from the linked English Wikipedia page) as features for the NER model. They demonstrate the effectiveness of these features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings across nine languages, showing significant improvements over strong baselines that often require more resources like parallel corpora.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and conceptually powerful paradigm for cross-lingual feature generation.** The core contribution is the idea of using cross-lingual wikification not as a downstream task, but as a universal feature extractor that projects text from any language into a shared, rich semantic space (English Wikipedia). This is a bold and elegant reversal of conventional thinking.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of the proposed method's practicality and scalability.** The paper shows that this approach, which relies only on the widely available multilingual Wikipedia, outperforms methods requiring more constrained resources like parallel text or human annotation, across a diverse set of languages including several with non-Latin scripts.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependencies and limitations.** The authors clearly demonstrate the correlation between the quality of the generated features and the size of the target language's Wikipedia, providing a clear understanding of the method's applicability and a roadmap for future improvements.

### Strengths

This is an excellent paper with a genuinely novel idea at its core. I am strongly in favor of acceptance.

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this paper is its central concept. Re-purposing a wikifier as a feature generation engine for a foundational task like NER is a brilliant and non-obvious insight. It elegantly sidesteps the need for complex, language-pair-specific resources like parallel corpora by leveraging the implicit alignment present in Wikipedia's interlanguage links. This kind of creative, paradigm-shifting work is precisely what our community needs more of. It provides a completely new and promising direction for research in cross-lingual transfer learning.

2.  **Impressive Results with Minimal Resource Assumptions:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results in the direct transfer setting while requiring only Wikipedia dumps—a far more accessible resource than the parallel corpora or native speaker interaction required by the baselines they outperform (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016). Beating stronger-resourced methods is a powerful testament to the efficacy of the idea. This makes the approach not just academically interesting but also highly practical and scalable to a vast number of the world's languages.

3.  **Thorough and Challenging Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on a wide and diverse set of nine languages. Crucially, this includes not only the standard, relatively similar European languages from CoNLL but also five low-resource languages, including some with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) which pose a significant challenge for transfer from English. While performance on the latter is expectedly lower, the paper convincingly shows that the wikifier features still provide a substantial boost over baselines. This demonstrates the robustness and generality of the approach.

4.  **Excellent Analysis of Failure Cases:** The experiment presented in Table 3 is a highlight of the paper. By artificially degrading the resources for high-resource languages (Spanish, German) to simulate a low-resource setting, the authors provide direct, compelling evidence for their hypothesis that performance is tied to the size and connectivity of the target language's Wikipedia. This kind of careful analysis adds significant depth and credibility to the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

While my overall assessment is highly positive, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. I view these not as reasons for rejection, but as points for discussion and future work, as they do not detract from the core novelty and impact of the submission.

1.  **Unexamined Computational Cost:** The proposed method requires wikifying every n-gram (up to n=4) in the input text. This seems to be a computationally intensive feature generation process. The paper does not discuss the runtime performance or complexity of this step. For NER to be practical in many applications, it must be reasonably fast. The potential computational bottleneck is a significant practical concern that is not addressed.

2.  **Opaqueness of the Wikifier Component:** The entire system's performance is fundamentally dependent on the underlying cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016). While the results are a testament to its effectiveness in this pipeline, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the wikifier's properties. For instance, what are its common failure modes? How robust is it to ambiguity and out-of-vocabulary terms? This would help to better delineate the contribution of the wikifier itself from the contribution of the paper's novel use of its output.

3.  **Evaluation with a Classic, Non-Neural Model:** The authors build upon a pre-neural, perceptron-based NER model from 2009. While this is a perfectly valid choice for isolating the impact of the new features, it leaves open the question of how these features would interact with modern neural architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, Transformers). It is possible that large pretrained models like mBERT already capture some of the cross-lingual semantic information provided by the wikifier, potentially reducing the feature's impact. A discussion of how this approach fits into the modern neural landscape would have strengthened the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide some analysis of the computational overhead of the n-gram wikification feature generation step at test time? How does the runtime compare to a baseline model without these features?

2.  The paper shows that for non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil), using *only* the wikifier features works best, as lexical features trained on English are useless. This suggests a simple but effective heuristic for applying the model to a new language. Have you considered a more sophisticated method for automatically weighting or selecting feature sets based on properties of the target language (e.g., script, lexical overlap with the source)?

3.  How does the system handle cases where an n-gram is correctly identified as an entity in the target language but has no corresponding entry in that language's Wikipedia (or the English Wikipedia)? Does the model learn to rely on other features in these cases, or does the absence of a strong wikifier signal hurt its ability to recognize such novel entities?
