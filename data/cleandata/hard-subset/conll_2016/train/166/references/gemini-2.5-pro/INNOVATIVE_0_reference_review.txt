### Review

**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the task of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The central and highly novel idea is to invert the traditional NLP pipeline where NER precedes Wikification/Entity Linking. Instead, the authors propose using a cross-lingual wikifier as a feature-generation engine for an NER model. By grounding every n-gram in a source text to the English Wikipedia, they extract language-independent features (e.g., FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) that prove to be powerful signals for identifying named entities. The method is evaluated by training a model on English and testing it on eight other languages, including high-resource CoNLL languages and several low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts. The results demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines that often require more resources like parallel corpora.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel conceptual framework for cross-lingual NER:** The primary contribution is the bold and elegant idea of using wikification as a *pre-processing step* to create a delexicalized, semantic feature space for NER. This reframes the problem from one of cross-lingual lexical matching to one of cross-lingual semantic grounding, representing a significant and refreshing new direction.
2.  **A highly effective and resource-light direct transfer method:** The paper presents a system that achieves state-of-the-art results in its setting, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia. It convincingly outperforms prior work that relies on more demanding resources like large parallel corpora or interaction with native speakers, making it a practical and highly scalable approach.
3.  **Comprehensive empirical validation and analysis:** The authors provide a strong and thorough evaluation across a diverse set of nine languages. The detailed analysis, including monolingual experiments, an investigation into the effect of Wikipedia size, and multi-source training, provides a clear picture of the method's strengths and limitations.

**Strengths**
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Impact:** The paper's core idea is its greatest strength. Reversing the NER -> Wikification pipeline is a genuinely new and insightful approach. It moves beyond incremental improvements on existing architectures and introduces a powerful new paradigm for leveraging knowledge bases in cross-lingual transfer. The idea is simple, elegant, and the results show it is highly effective. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact work that should be highlighted at a top conference.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves large and significant F1 score improvements over strong baselines. For instance, on the CoNLL languages (NL, DE, ES), adding the wikifier features boosts performance by 12-15 F1 points over a direct transfer baseline and handily beats the Täckström et al. (2012) system that requires parallel text for word clustering. The strong performance on low-resource languages, even outperforming a system that uses native speaker input, further underscores the power and efficiency of this approach.
3.  **Strong Analysis and Generality:** The paper does an excellent job of analyzing *why* and *when* the method works. The experiment in Section 4.4, which simulates a low-resource setting by removing inter-language links, provides a clear and convincing explanation for the performance gap between high- and low-resource languages. Furthermore, the experiments across languages with different scripts (Latin, Bengali, Tamil) and from different families demonstrate the general applicability of the features. The fact that these features also improve monolingual models is a strong testament to their value.

**Weaknesses**
While this is a strong paper with a clear accept recommendation, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. My criticisms are offered more as points for discussion and future work, as they do not detract from the significance of the core contribution.

1.  **Use of a Dated Base Model:** The experiments are built upon the Ratinov and Roth (2009) model, which is a classic but now-dated linear classifier (averaged perceptron). While the massive gains on this model impressively isolate the power of the proposed features, it leaves a crucial question unanswered: how do these explicit semantic features interact with modern, pre-trained neural architectures (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, BERT)? It is possible that large language models already implicitly capture some of this semantic information, potentially leading to smaller relative gains. Demonstrating complementarity with modern architectures would have made the contribution even more compelling.
2.  **Computational Cost of Brute-Force Wikification:** The strategy of wikifying every n-gram (up to n=4) in a document seems computationally intensive. A typical sentence could generate dozens of calls to a complex wikification system. The paper does not discuss the computational cost or efficiency of this feature generation process. While this may be acceptable in an academic context to prove a concept, it raises questions about the practicality of the approach for large-scale, real-world deployment. A discussion of potential optimizations (e.g., using a simple gazetteer to prune candidate n-grams) would have been beneficial.
3.  **Lack of Feature Ablation:** The "Cross-lingual Wikifier Features" are a composite of several information sources: coarse and fine-grained FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories, and internal features from the wikifier's ranker. An ablation study breaking down the contribution of each of these components would have provided deeper insight. For example, is the primary signal coming from coarse FreeBase types (e.g., `person`, `location`), or is the fine-grained information from Wikipedia categories and ranker confidence scores also critical? Such an analysis would strengthen the paper's explanatory power.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you comment on the computational overhead of the "wikify everything" approach? Have you considered or experimented with more efficient strategies for generating candidate n-grams to wikify, and how do you think that would impact performance versus runtime?
2.  Given the strength of the proposed features with a linear model, do you have any intuition or preliminary findings on how they would perform in concert with modern pre-trained language models? Do you believe these explicit knowledge-based features offer information that is orthogonal and complementary to what is learned by models like mBERT?
