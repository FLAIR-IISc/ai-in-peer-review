### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting, where a model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applied to a low-resource target language without any annotated data. The central idea is to use a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram in a document to the English Wikipedia. The resulting Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types serve as powerful, language-independent features for a standard sequence labeling model. This approach cleverly inverts the traditional NER-then-Wikification pipeline, using disambiguation signals to aid entity identification. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of these features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, showing significant improvements over strong baselines on CoNLL languages and a diverse set of five low-resource languages, including some with non-Latin scripts.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER.** The core idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier on all n-grams to extract semantic type information is innovative and shown to be highly effective. It elegantly bypasses the need for parallel corpora, which are a major bottleneck for many cross-lingual transfer methods.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation across a wide typological range of languages.** The paper provides a strong empirical case for the method's utility by testing on nine languages, from high-resource European languages to low-resource Asian and African languages with different scripts. The results convincingly outperform a strong prior direct-transfer baseline (Täckström et al., 2012) and a method that requires more resources (Zhang et al., 2016).
3.  **An insightful analysis of the method's dependencies and generalization.** The authors provide valuable analysis showing the correlation between feature quality and the size of the target language's Wikipedia, and demonstrate that the model can be further improved by training on multiple source languages.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The conceptual shift from using NER to enable wikification to using wikification to enable NER is elegant and powerful. The paper convincingly argues that entity type information, derived from grounding n-grams in a language-agnostic knowledge base (English Wikipedia), is a more robust signal for cross-lingual transfer than features based on lexical similarity or word clusters derived from parallel text. The state-of-the-art results on the direct transfer task demonstrate the high impact of this contribution.
2.  **Practicality and Scalability:** The method's primary dependency is the multilingual Wikipedia dump, a readily available resource for hundreds of languages. By avoiding the need for sentence-aligned parallel corpora, the approach is significantly more practical and scalable than projection-based methods or those requiring cross-lingual word embeddings trained on parallel data. This is a crucial advantage for truly low-resource languages where such parallel resources are non-existent.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and well-designed.
    *   The inclusion of monolingual experiments (Section 4.2) effectively isolates the contribution of the wikifier features, showing they provide complementary information even when strong lexical features are present.
    *   The direct transfer experiments (Section 4.3) are the core of the paper and show impressive gains over relevant baselines. The comparison against Täckström et al. (2012) is particularly strong, as it shows the wikifier features are more effective than features from cross-lingual word clusters.
    *   The analysis of feature quality vs. Wikipedia size (Section 4.4) is insightful, providing a clear explanation for performance variations and setting realistic expectations for the method's application to new languages.
    *   The multi-source training experiments (Section 4.5) further demonstrate the robustness and flexibility of the feature representation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Expense is Unaddressed:** The proposed feature extraction process involves running a cross-lingual wikifier on *every n-gram (up to n=4)* in the corpus. This seems computationally prohibitive. For each n-gram, this involves candidate generation, feature extraction, and ranking. The paper makes no mention of the computational complexity or practical runtime of this approach. This omission is a significant weakness, as the method's practicality is a key selling point, and a slow, resource-intensive feature extraction process could be a major barrier to adoption.
2.  **Limited Analysis of the Wikifier's Role:** The wikifier itself is treated as a black box. The paper's performance is critically dependent on the quality of this component, yet there is no analysis of the wikifier's standalone performance (e.g., entity linking accuracy) on these languages. It is unclear how wikification errors (e.g., incorrect disambiguation, linking non-entities to concepts, failing to link true entities) propagate and affect the final NER performance. A more in-depth error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper by providing insight into the failure modes and potential areas for improvement.
3.  **Missing a Key Baseline Comparison:** The paper positions itself as a Wikipedia-based approach but does not compare against the most direct alternative: generating silver-standard NER training data from the target language's Wikipedia itself (e.g., following Nothman et al., 2012). This method also only requires Wikipedia and would allow for training a standard monolingual model with language-specific lexical features. While the authors argue their method avoids language-specific heuristics, a direct empirical comparison is necessary to substantiate the claim that their transfer approach is superior to in-language silver-standard training. This is a major missing piece in the related work and experimental comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide details on the computational cost of your feature extraction? Specifically, what is the runtime for processing the test sets compared to the baselines, and how does this scale with document length?
2.  Your analysis shows that performance degrades as the size of the intersection with English Wikipedia shrinks. Have you performed any analysis on the wikifier's accuracy itself? For instance, for the gold entities in the test sets, what percentage are correctly linked to the right English Wikipedia page by the wikifier, and how does this metric correlate with the final F1 score across languages?
3.  Could you elaborate on the decision not to compare against the strong baseline of creating silver-standard training data from the target language's Wikipedia links? It seems like a very relevant alternative that uses the same resource. Do you have intuitions on how your direct transfer approach would fare against a model trained on such automatically-labeled target-language data?
