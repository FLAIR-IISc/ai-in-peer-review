**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) based on a direct transfer paradigm. The core idea is to train an NER model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) using a set of language-independent features and then apply it directly to a low-resource target language.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel feature set for NER derived from a cross-lingual wikifier. By grounding n-grams in any language to the English Wikipedia, the model extracts language-agnostic features like FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories, which serve as strong signals for entity type and boundaries.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation across nine languages, including the CoNLL 2002/2003 datasets and five low-resource languages (some with non-Latin scripts), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed features in a direct transfer setting.
3.  A well-executed analysis that directly links the quality of the wikifier features, and thus the model's performance, to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and its intersection with the English Wikipedia.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Simplicity:** The proposed approach builds upon a well-established NER model (Ratinov and Roth, 2009) and introduces a novel, yet conceptually straightforward, set of features. The core advantage is that it does not require parallel corpora, which are a significant bottleneck for many cross-lingual projection methods. The demonstration that these features improve performance not only in the cross-lingual setting but also in the monolingual setting (Table 2) provides strong evidence of their utility.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results against Relevant Baselines:** The paper compares its approach against Täckström et al. (2012), a seminal work in direct transfer for NER. The proposed model, augmented with wikifier features, shows clear improvements on Dutch and German, even when compared to Täckström et al.'s model that uses cross-lingual word clusters derived from parallel text. Furthermore, the model outperforms the system of Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, despite requiring fewer resources (no parallel text or native speaker interaction). This is a strong argument for the paper's acceptance.

3.  **Insightful Analysis of Feature Quality:** Section 4.4 provides a compelling analysis of why the wikifier features are less effective for some low-resource languages. By systematically degrading the resources available to the wikifier for high-resource languages (i.e., removing FreeBase queries and inter-language links), the authors successfully simulate the performance drop seen in languages with smaller Wikipedias. This analysis adds significant value by prospectively identifying the conditions under which the proposed method is likely to succeed or fail.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Inaccurate and Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper's central claim of outperforming established methods is not fully supported by its own results. In Section 4.3, the authors state, "we see that for each language, we significantly outperform their approach," referring to Täckström et al. (2012)'s bitext cluster model. However, Table 2 shows that for Spanish (ES), the proposed model achieves an F1 score of 48.41, while Täckström et al. report 58.4. This represents a substantial underperformance of over 10 F1 points. This discrepancy is not mentioned, let alone discussed, in the text. Such an omission undermines the rigor of the evaluation and the credibility of the paper's claims. A thorough comparison requires transparently reporting and analyzing all results, including negative ones.

2.  **Limited Novelty of Core Concept:** The use of Wikipedia categories as features for NER is not a new idea, as acknowledged by the authors' citation of Kazama and Torisawa (2007). The primary novelty here is the application of these features in a *cross-lingual direct transfer* setting via a modern wikification system. While the engineering and empirical validation are valuable, the conceptual contribution is an incremental extension of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach to the problem.

3.  **Ambiguity in the Wikification Process:** The paper states that it wikifies "every n-gram (n ≤ 4)" in a document. This approach seems computationally expensive and prone to generating significant noise from common, non-entity phrases (e.g., "in the case of"). The modifications made to the wikifier are briefly mentioned but lack sufficient detail. It is unclear how the model distinguishes between a confident link for a true entity and a noisy, low-confidence link for a common phrase. A more detailed description of this feature extraction process, including how "NIL" outputs are handled and the potential for noise, would be necessary for methodological reproducibility and soundness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please address the significant performance gap on the Spanish dataset when comparing your method (F1 48.41) to Täckström et al. (2012)'s bitext cluster result (F1 58.4)? Please correct the claim in Section 4.3 and provide a discussion of why your model might be underperforming on this specific language.

2.  The performance of your method is clearly correlated with the size of the target language's Wikipedia. Does this imply that the method is not suitable for truly low-resource or endangered languages that have a minimal or non-existent Wikipedia presence? How would you position your work in relation to methods that do not rely on such knowledge bases?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the computational overhead of wikifying every n-gram up to length 4? How does this feature extraction time compare to the training/inference time of the base NER model, and what steps were taken to manage the potential noise introduced by this exhaustive approach?
