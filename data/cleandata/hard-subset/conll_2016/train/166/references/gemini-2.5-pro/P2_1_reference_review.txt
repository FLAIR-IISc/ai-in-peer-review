### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method for cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) in a direct transfer setting. The core idea is to leverage a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features. The model is trained on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and can be applied directly to a target language without any annotated data in that language. The method works by wikifying every n-gram (n≤4) in the input text, linking it to the English Wikipedia. The Wikipedia categories and Freebase types associated with the resulting Wikipedia articles are then used as features for a standard NER classifier. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through extensive experiments on CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), showing significant improvements over strong baselines, including methods that require more resources like parallel corpora.

### Contributions
As a reviewer, I see the main contributions as follows:

1.  **A Novel and Effective Method for Generating Language-Independent Features:** The primary contribution is the clever idea of using a cross-lingual wikifier not as a downstream task, but as a feature engineering tool for NER. By grounding all n-grams to a single knowledge base (English Wikipedia), the authors create a rich set of delexicalized features (types, categories) that are inherently language-agnostic. This is an elegant solution to the core challenge of direct transfer.

2.  **Extensive and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across a wide and diverse set of nine languages. This includes standard high-resource benchmarks (CoNLL) and challenging low-resource languages, some with non-Latin scripts. The model significantly outperforms a very relevant and strong direct transfer baseline (Täckström et al., 2012) and even surpasses a method that uses more resources like parallel text and human interaction (Zhang et al., 2016). This provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness and practicality.

3.  **Insightful Analysis of the Method's Dependencies:** The authors provide a valuable analysis in Section 4.4 that directly links the quality of the wikifier features, and thus the model's performance, to the size of the target language's Wikipedia and the density of its inter-language links to English Wikipedia. This analysis transparently explains the performance variations across languages and highlights the method's primary limitation, which is a sign of strong scientific work.

### Strengths

1.  **Simplicity and Elegance of the Core Idea:** The central concept of reversing the typical NER-then-Wikification pipeline is novel and powerful. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, a widely available resource, making it highly accessible and applicable to a vast number of languages without the need for parallel corpora or language-specific tools like POS taggers.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Results in Direct Transfer:** The experimental results are a key strength. The model achieves substantial gains over the Täckström et al. (2012) baseline on the CoNLL languages (e.g., +12.3 F1 on Dutch, +15.4 on German over their baseline). Even more impressively, it outperforms the results of Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages, despite the latter's reliance on more expensive resources (parallel data, monolingual corpora, and native speaker input). This clearly demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.

3.  **Thoroughness of Experiments:** The paper's evaluation is comprehensive. Beyond the primary direct transfer experiments, the authors include monolingual experiments to show the features are beneficial even with in-language training data. They also experiment with multi-source training, demonstrating that the model can effectively combine knowledge from multiple annotated languages. The inclusion of languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) provides a strenuous test of the "language-independent" claim and yields interesting insights.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand. Figure 1 provides an excellent and intuitive example of the core idea. The related work is well-contextualized, and the experimental setup is clearly described, making the work reproducible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost:** The method requires wikifying *every n-gram up to n=4*. This seems computationally intensive, as it involves generating candidates and running a ranker for a quadratic number of substrings in a document. The paper does not provide any discussion of the method's runtime, efficiency, or scalability. This is a significant practical concern that could limit its application to large-scale text processing.

2.  **Dependence on an Opaque Wikifier:** The success of the entire model is critically dependent on the performance of the Tsai and Roth (2016) cross-lingual wikifier. While the analysis in Section 4.4 connects performance to Wikipedia size, it does not discuss how wikifier errors (e.g., incorrect disambiguation, or failing to link an entity) propagate and affect the final NER predictions. Providing some metrics on the wikifier's accuracy for the target languages would strengthen the paper by making the error analysis more direct.

3.  **Limited Discussion on Out-of-Wikipedia Entities:** By its nature, this method is biased towards entities that are notable enough to have a Wikipedia page. The paper does not discuss how the model handles named entities that are not in Wikipedia (e.g., names of local people, small businesses). While the base lexical features might provide some signal for Latin-script languages, the model's primary source of power would be absent for such entities, which could be a major limitation in domains outside of newswire.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please comment on the computational overhead of your feature generation process? Specifically, what is the runtime for processing the test sets compared to the baseline models, and do you see this as a potential bottleneck for real-world application?

2.  Your analysis shows that performance depends on Wikipedia size. Could you provide a more direct analysis of how the underlying wikifier's accuracy (e.g., precision/recall on an entity linking task) on a given language correlates with the downstream NER F1 score? This could help disentangle the effect of feature density from the effect of wikifier errors.

3.  How does the model perform on entities that are not present in the intersection of the target and English Wikipedias? Have you performed any analysis on the model's ability to recognize these "out-of-knowledge-base" entities, and is there a significant performance gap compared to entities that are well-covered by Wikipedia?
