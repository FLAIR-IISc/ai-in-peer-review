- Proposes cross-lingual NER that wikifies every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia and uses Freebase types/Wikipedia categories as language-independent features for direct transfer without parallel data.
- Evaluates on CoNLL (EN, ES, NL, DE) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA) with analyses of monolingual gains, direct transfer, Wikipedia size/interlanguage link dependence, and multi-source training.

- Contribution: a simple, delexicalized cross-lingual feature design using wikification (types/categories from top-k candidates).
- Contribution: extensive empirical study across nine languages showing wikifier features improve monolingual and cross-lingual NER and can beat prior direct-transfer baselines.
- Contribution: analyses quantifying the impact of Wikipedia size/interlanguage links and exploring multi-source training effects.

- Strength: Conceptually simple and practical—uses only multilingual Wikipedia, works across scripts, and wikifies n-grams sensibly for NER.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains on CoNLL and several low-resource languages, outperforming some methods that use parallel data or native speakers.
- Strength: Broad evaluation and diagnostic analyses (monolingual/cross-lingual, low-resource, multi-source, ablations tying performance to Wikipedia coverage).
- Strength: Orthogonal to other techniques (projection, clustering, silver data), making it a useful baseline/component.
- Strength: Adds value even in monolingual settings, outperforming strong baselines and gazetteers.

- Weakness: Heavy reliance on Freebase and interlanguage links harms coverage and reproducibility; no experiment replacing Freebase with Wikidata.
- Weakness: Wikifying every n-gram risks high noise; limited analysis of mention-linking precision, candidate sizes, and linker confidence thresholds.
- Weakness: Evaluation clarity issues—tag set alignment (remapping MISC) and unclear comparisons to Täckström et al. may confound results.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty relative to prior Wikipedia-based NER/EL; needs stronger positioning against recent neural cross-lingual baselines.
- Weakness: Potential computational cost and scalability concerns for wikifying all n-grams; no runtime/throughput/memory reported.
- Weakness: Steep drops in performance when removing Freebase/interlanguage links indicate fragility for some target languages.

- Question: Clarify Täckström comparison columns and ensure like-for-like evaluation (same tag sets/test splits), especially for Spanish.
- Question: How many n-gram candidates are generated per token/sentence, what is runtime per sentence/document, and are there pruning heuristics beyond the two listed?
- Question: How is linker confidence used—do you threshold the top candidate or include top-1/top-2 features—and can you ablate top-1 vs top-2 vs confidence thresholds?
- Question: Can the pipeline use Wikidata (instance-of/subclass-of) and category graphs instead of Freebase, and what would the expected impact be on coverage for YO/BN/TA?
- Question: For low-resource languages, can you provide per-entity-type precision/recall and error analyses to separate boundary detection vs type errors and sensitivity to the MISC-to-LOC remapping?

- Overall: A simple, effective, and broadly applicable cross-lingual NER approach with solid experimental breadth; main shortcomings are dependence on Freebase/interlanguage links and unclear comparisons and efficiency details—replacing Freebase with Wikidata and clarifying evaluations would strengthen the work.
