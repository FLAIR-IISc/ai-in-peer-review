Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the input to English Wikipedia, and uses the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features. The model is trained on annotated data in one or more source languages and directly transferred to target languages without parallel corpora or target-language annotation. Experiments cover CoNLL (EN, ES, NL, DE) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA). Key analyses examine monolingual gains, direct transfer performance, the dependence on Wikipedia size/interlanguage links, and multi-source training.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, broadly applicable delexicalized feature design for cross-lingual NER based on cross-lingual wikification (types/categories from top-k candidates), enabling direct transfer without parallel data.
2) Extensive empirical study across nine languages, including low-resource and non-Latin scripts, demonstrating that wikifier features improve both monolingual and cross-lingual NER and can outperform prior direct-transfer baselines.
3) Analyses that quantify how Wikipedia size/interlanguage link coverage impact performance, and a study of multi-source training effects.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and clear practicality: Using only multilingual Wikipedia (no sentence/word-aligned bitext), the method yields language-independent features (Freebase types, categories) and works across scripts. The modifications to wikify all n-grams are sensible for NER and easy to reproduce in principle.
- Strong empirical evidence for cross-lingual transfer: On CoNLL transfer (EN→ES/NL/DE), adding wikifier features yields large gains over base+gazetteer features (e.g., NL 47.14, DE 60.97, ES 48.41 with +Wikifier), and on several low-resource languages the proposed model beats expectation-based methods that require parallel data and native speaker involvement (e.g., TR 65.32 vs 51.3 in Zhang et al. 2016).
- Breadth of evaluation and analyses: The paper evaluates both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, covers five low-resource languages (including non-Latin scripts where lexical transfer fails), explores multi-source training, and provides ablations tying performance to Wikipedia size/interlanguage links (Table 3), which is valuable diagnostic insight for practitioners.
- Orthogonality to other techniques: The approach is compatible with projection, multilingual clustering, and silver data harvested from Wikipedia. This suggests the method can serve as a strong, simple baseline or a complementary component in more complex pipelines.
- Monolingual utility: Even within a single language, adding wikifier features consistently improves over strong baselines and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers, suggesting the features encode information not captured by conventional lexical/gazetteer cues.

Weaknesses
- Heavy dependence on Freebase and interlanguage links harms coverage and reproducibility: The features rely critically on Freebase types accessed via interlanguage links to English; Freebase is deprecated and has poor coverage for some target languages (YO/BN/TA). The paper shows steep drops when removing Freebase queries/interlanguage links (Table 3), but this raises concerns about practical deployment today (Wikidata would be more appropriate). A path to replacing Freebase with Wikidata and quantifying the impact is not provided.
- Potential noise from “wikify every n-gram” with limited analysis of precision/recall trade-offs: While intuitively useful, linking every n-gram (up to 4) risks high false positives and error propagation (e.g., function-word n-grams). The paper makes two adjustments to reduce noise but does not quantify mention-linking precision, candidate-set size, or the impact of top-2 vs top-1 vs confidence thresholding. A more thorough ablation on linker confidence and feature sparsity would strengthen the case.
- Evaluation choices and clarity issues: 
  - Tag set alignment is handled by removing MISC and remapping some tags via Freebase signals, which may confound comparability to prior work and bias results on low-resource languages. More detail and alternatives (e.g., reporting per-tag scores) would help.
  - Comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) are hard to interpret: the table ordering and claims appear inconsistent (e.g., it is unclear whether the proposed model truly surpasses their bitext-cluster results for all CoNLL targets; ES seems lower). Clearer mapping of columns and a like-for-like setup are needed.
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior Wikipedia-based NER/EL: The core idea—using Wikipedia-derived types/categories as features for NER—has been explored monolingually (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa 2007; Sil & Yates 2013). The novelty lies in robust cross-lingual wikification and using it as the primary delexicalized signal for transfer. Given this, stronger positioning versus recent neural cross-lingual baselines (e.g., multilingual embeddings, projection + BiLSTM-CRF) is missing.
- Computational cost and scalability: Wikifying all n-grams up to length 4 could be expensive. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or memory usage, which is important for practical adoption and for comparing to lighter-weight delexicalized baselines.

Questions to authors
- Can you clarify the Täckström comparison columns and ensure a like-for-like evaluation (same tag sets, same test splits)? For Spanish, the numbers in Table 2 seem to contradict the text claim of surpassing their approach.
- How many n-gram candidates per token/sentence are typically generated, and what is the runtime per sentence/document? Any pruning heuristics beyond the two listed?
- How is the linker confidence used? Do you threshold the top candidate (i.e., a learned linker) or always include top-1/top-2 features? An ablation of top-1 vs top-2 vs confidence-thresholded features would be informative.
- Freebase is deprecated. Can your pipeline use Wikidata (instance-of, subclass-of) and category graphs instead? If so, what is the expected impact on coverage in YO/BN/TA?
- For low-resource languages, can you report per-entity-type P/R and error analyses to assess boundary detection vs type errors? Also, how sensitive are results to your MISC-to-LOC remapping heuristic?

Overall
The paper offers a simple, effective, and broadly applicable cross-lingual NER approach with solid experimental breadth and helpful analyses. Its main limitation is reliance on Freebase/interlanguage links and lack of clarity in some comparisons and efficiency details. Addressing these issues (especially replacing Freebase with Wikidata and clarifying the Täckström comparison) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
