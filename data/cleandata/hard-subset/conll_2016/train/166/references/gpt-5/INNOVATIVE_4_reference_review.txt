Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer cross-lingual NER framework that leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel corpora, alignments, or native-speaker intervention). The core idea is to flip the usual EL-after-NER pipeline: disambiguate every n-gram to English Wikipedia and feed Freebase types and Wikipedia categories, plus wikifier confidence signals, as features to a standard NER model.

As I see it, the key contributions are:
1) Conceptual contribution: using cross-lingual wikification as delexicalized features to enable direct transfer NER to “any” Wikipedia language, including low-resource ones, without parallel data. This is a bold, clean idea that inverts the traditional NER→EL pipeline.
2) Empirical validation across 9 languages (CoNLL and five low-resource languages), showing consistent gains from wikifier features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, and outperforming strong direct-transfer baselines and an expectation-learning system that uses additional resources.
3) Analysis of resource effects and training regimes: careful study of how Wikipedia size and interlanguage coverage affect performance, plus evidence that multi-source training offers small but consistent improvements.

Strengths
- Novel, generalizable idea with minimal resource assumptions: The paper offers a simple and broadly applicable mechanism to produce language-agnostic features by grounding text to English Wikipedia and using type/category signals. Importantly, it dispenses with parallel data and language-specific rules, which is rare for cross-lingual NER. This “EL-as-features” perspective is conceptually strong and has clear downstream potential beyond NER.

- Strong cross-lingual results with delexicalized features: In direct transfer from English, adding wikifier features yields substantial gains (e.g., +10 F1 or more for Dutch, German, Turkish) and beats Täckström et al.’s bitext-induced cluster model on CoNLL target languages. Against Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, the proposed approach performs better despite using fewer resources. The “wikifier only” setting retaining useful signal across scripts is particularly compelling.

- Monolingual improvements and orthogonality: Even in monolingual settings, wikifier features consistently improve over strong baselines with gazetteers, showing genuine complementary signal. The method is orthogonal to projection and silver-data generation, making it easy to combine with other techniques.

- Honest and informative analysis of limitations: The paper quantifies how performance degrades with smaller Wikipedias and when Freebase lookup is unavailable, and uses controlled ablations (removing interlanguage links, Freebase queries) to explain the Yoruba/Bengali/Tamil behavior. This clarity strengthens the empirical claims.

- Multi-source training and practical guidance: The language-pair matrix and multi-source experiments offer actionable insights (e.g., when to include lexical features, which training language helps which target, value of pooling CoNLL training data).

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Freebase and interlanguage links: The approach hinges on Freebase types and English interlanguage mappings; when these are sparse or unavailable (as in some truly low-resource languages), the gains shrink. Freebase is deprecated; the paper does not discuss transitioning to Wikidata or other KBs. Reproducibility and sustainability would benefit from a clear path away from Freebase.

- Efficiency and scalability of “wikify every n-gram”: The method grounds all n-grams up to length 4. While the authors modified candidate generation to mitigate egregious errors, runtime and memory footprint are not quantified. Throughput and practicality at scale (e.g., web-scale corpora or long documents) should be reported, or at least estimated.

- Comparisons not fully aligned and limited modern baselines: The Täckström numbers are taken from their paper and use different feature/training setups (e.g., POS, bitext). There are no contemporary neural transfer baselines or cross-lingual embedding approaches. While I appreciate the orthogonality, a stronger suite of baselines would firm up the empirical case.

- Label-set harmonization and tag manipulations: For low-resource datasets, the paper removes certain tags (TIME, TTL) and remaps MISC→LOC based on Freebase types when training on CoNLL. This may bias the evaluation and could conflate type supervision with label-set tuning. More detail on the impact of these decisions (e.g., an ablation without remapping) would help.

- Limited discussion of NIL handling, overlapping spans, and confidence calibration: The use of top-2 candidates and ranker features is briefly described, but the policy for NILs, overlapping n-grams, and the “linker” rejection threshold is under-specified. Given the centrality of wikifier quality, these details matter.

Questions to the authors
- KB dependency: Can your method be ported from Freebase to Wikidata? If so, what would change in feature extraction, and do you anticipate similar coverage/quality?

- Runtime/practicality: What is the average time to process a document for “wikify every n-gram” (by language and dataset)? What pruning/parallelization strategies did you employ?

- NIL and overlap policy: How do you handle NIL predictions for n-grams? How do you resolve overlapping n-grams that link to different entities? Is there a confidence threshold or structured decision to avoid flooding the model with noisy features?

- Robustness without categories: If Wikipedia categories are sparse or noisy in a target language, how much of the gain remains from entity types alone? Have you tried (or can you report) an ablation separating types vs categories vs ranker features?

- Low-resource mitigation: For non-Latin scripts and tiny Wikipedias, did you attempt simple transliteration-based gazetteer expansion or cross-lingual type propagation via Wikidata properties? Any preliminary results?

- Release: Will you release code, gazetteer lists, and the feature extraction pipeline to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, despite some limitations in dependencies and baseline breadth, this work introduces a compelling, broadly useful idea—using cross-lingual wikification as the backbone for delexicalized NER transfer—and demonstrates clear gains across diverse languages. I view the conceptual novelty and empirical evidence as strong enough to merit acceptance.
