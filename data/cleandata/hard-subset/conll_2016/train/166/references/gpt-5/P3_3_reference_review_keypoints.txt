- Proposes direct-transfer cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification to derive delexicalized, language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories for n-grams up to 4).

- Requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data) by grounding target text n-grams to English Wikipedia.

- Evaluates broadly across 9 languages (CoNLL EN/DE/ES/NL and LRLs TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), showing gains in monolingual and direct-transfer settings versus strong baselines and a weakly supervised LRL method.

- Analyzes performance relative to size of target-language Wikipedia-English intersection, ablates Freebase/link coverage, and includes a small multi-source training study.

- Strength: Simple, broadly applicable mechanism that yields consistent empirical gains, especially large direct-transfer improvements (e.g., +10 F1 on DE/NL/TR).

- Strength: Thorough evaluation across scripts and resource levels, including informative “wikifier only” lower-bound experiments.

- Strength: Useful ablations linking method sensitivity to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage, explaining smaller LRL gains.

- Strength: Minimal supervision assumptions for LRLs; outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) on five LRLs without parallel text or native speaker interaction.

- Strength: Multi-source training experiments show modest but practical benefits and variation in best source languages by target.

- Weakness: Novelty is incremental given prior use of Wikipedia categories/types and EL-informed NER; main methodological advance is cross-lingual wikification and n-gram-wide application.

- Weakness: Reliance on Freebase and English intersection undermines language-independence for very low-resource languages; Wikidata would be a preferable modern alternative.

- Weakness: Label manipulation (removing/mapping MISC) compromises comparability to standard CoNLL results and may introduce bias from using the same knowledge source at test time.

- Weakness: Base model is a greedy BIO perceptron; lacks comparison to stronger CRF or neural (BiLSTM-CRF) direct-transfer baselines, limiting conclusions about absolute efficacy.

- Weakness: Practicality concerns—wikifying all n-grams is computationally heavy and noisy; paper omits runtime, candidate counts, NIL rates, and confidence thresholds.

- Question: Did you compute standard 4-class CoNLL F1 (PER/LOC/ORG/MISC) for EN→DE/ES/NL comparisons to Täckström, and which baseline settings were used?

- Question: How sensitive are LRL results to the MISC→LOC mapping heuristic; can you report numbers without relabeling?

- Question: What fraction of tokens/n-grams receive types, what NIL/confidence cutoffs are used, and how sensitive are results to top-1 vs top-k candidates?

- Question: What is the average runtime per document for n-gram wikification and feature extraction, and what pruning strategies were applied beyond skipping stopwords?

- Question: Can the pipeline switch from Freebase to Wikidata without parallel text; if so, please provide small-scale results for low-resource languages (especially YO/BN/TA).

- Additional comment: Recommend adding a BiLSTM-CRF baseline with multilingual embeddings, per-type scores, significance tests, code and data release, and brief error analysis on overlinking and boundary errors.

- Overall verdict: Accept borderline to weak accept — method is simple and empirically useful but novelty and evaluation rigor (comparability, baselines, practical details) need strengthening.
