Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that relies on cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features. A standard NER model (averaged perceptron with BIO decoding) is trained on one or more source languages and applied directly to target languages. The key idea is to wikify every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target text to English Wikipedia titles (via a cross-lingual wikifier) and use the resulting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories, plus the wikifier’s ranking signals, as delexicalized features. The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data or target-language annotations). Experiments on CoNLL (EN/ES/NL/DE) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA) show that wikifier features improve both monolingual and direct-transfer performance, outperform selected prior direct-transfer baselines. Analyses link performance gains to Wikipedia size/coverage, and multi-source training provides modest additional gains.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification yields strong, broadly applicable, delexicalized features for NER in a direct-transfer setting, improving over strong baselines without parallel data or target-language supervision.
2) Practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier to enable dense coverage (wikifying all n-grams) and an analysis attributing performance variations to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage, with ablations removing interlanguage links and Freebase queries.
3) Empirical evidence that multilingual training (combining multiple source languages) provides incremental gains and that the proposed features also help monolingual NER.

Strengths
- Clear, resource-light cross-lingual signal: The paper convincingly shows that wikifier-derived types/categories are effective delexicalized features, yielding large direct-transfer gains, especially for Latin-script languages (e.g., +10 F1 on NL/DE/TR over base+gazetteers). This is valuable since it avoids parallel data and language-specific tools.
- Thorough empirical coverage and ablations: The work spans nine languages across different scripts and resource levels, and includes ablations that isolate the role of Freebase and interlanguage links. The correlation between Wikipedia size and performance is carefully demonstrated and informative for practitioners.
- Sensible system design and integration: Modifications to the wikifier (whole-string candidate generation for n>1, removal of mention-embedding features) are motivated by the need to wikify all n-grams. Feature engineering (using top-2 candidates, context n-grams, ranker confidence) is pragmatic and well-justified.
- Practical insights for deployment: The paper offers actionable guidance (e.g., favor full features in Latin scripts; prefer wikifier-only in non-Latin scripts) and shows that multi-source training usually helps and rarely hurts, which is useful for real-world cross-lingual NER setup.
- Comparison to relevant prior non-neural baselines: Against Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), the method shows consistent improvements while using fewer or comparable resources.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to modern multilingual baselines: The paper evaluates against older direct-transfer methods. Given today’s landscape, zero-shot transfer with multilingual transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) is a strong baseline that also does not require parallel data or target-language annotations. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true competitiveness of the approach.
- Reliance on Freebase and replicability concerns: Freebase is now deprecated. The approach’s dependence on Freebase types is central to its gains (as shown by ablations). The paper should clarify how to reproduce results with Wikidata (or other KBs), including mappings and coverage differences, and report the impact of substituting Freebase with Wikidata types/instance-of properties.
- Evaluation protocol and label-set harmonization: For cross-lingual experiments, MISC is dropped for CoNLL languages and TTL/TIME removed for low-resource data; some MISC tokens are remapped to LOC via Freebase types. These choices may shift the task definition and comparability. More detail is needed on how evaluation aligns with prior work and whether remapping inflates performance on specific categories.
- Incomplete analysis of error types and robustness: While coverage analyses are provided, there is limited discussion of error patterns (e.g., boundary errors introduced by noisy n-gram linking, confusion among ORG/LOC/PER), class-wise performance, or the impact of wikifier errors on NER. Runtime/efficiency of “wikify all n-grams” is also not reported, which matters for scalability.
- Modeling choices lag behind current practice: The NER model is a greedy averaged perceptron without sequence-level inference beyond BIO constraints. Stronger decoders (CRF) and neural encoders are commonplace. At minimum, a CRF decoding comparison would help separate feature utility from modeling limitations.

Questions to authors
1) Modern baselines: How does your approach compare to zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R on the same datasets and label mapping? Can you also report a simple neural+CRF baseline with delexicalized features to isolate modeling effects?
2) Knowledge base substitution: Can the pipeline be reimplemented with Wikidata (e.g., P31/P106/P17, sitelinks) instead of Freebase? What is the quantitative impact on monolingual and transfer results?
3) Wikifier details: What are the wikifier’s coverage and accuracy per language when applied to “all n-grams”? How are overlapping n-grams reconciled in feature extraction (e.g., conflicts among candidates at different spans)?
4) Efficiency: What is the runtime of the wikification step per sentence/document and per language? Are there pruning heuristics or caching strategies to make “wikify all n-grams” scalable?
5) Label-set consistency: Please provide precise evaluation scripts and counts for remapped/removed labels across datasets. Can you report per-entity-type F1 and a sensitivity analysis without the MISC→LOC remapping?
6) Feature ablations: How sensitive are results to (a) the number of top candidates (top-1 vs top-2 vs top-3), (b) maximum n-gram length (n=3 vs 4), and (c) using only categories vs only types vs both?
7) Target-language gazetteers: In direct transfer, do you allow target-language gazetteers at test time? If so, please clarify this assumption and its availability for truly low-resource languages.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and empirically supported idea that leverages cross-lingual wikification effectively. Addressing the modern baseline gap, KB dependency, and evaluation clarity would substantially strengthen the submission.
