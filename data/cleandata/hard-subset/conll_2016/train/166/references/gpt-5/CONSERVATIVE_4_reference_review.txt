Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that augments a standard feature-based NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) with features derived from a cross-lingual wikifier. Mentions (n-grams up to length 4) are grounded to English Wikipedia; the top candidates’ Freebase types and Wikipedia categories, along with ranker confidence, are used as language-independent features. The model is evaluated in monolingual and direct-transfer settings on CoNLL (EN/DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA). Ablations relate performance to Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage; multi-source training is also explored.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that cross-lingual wikification-derived type/category features are effective delexicalized signals for direct-transfer NER, improving over prior direct-transfer baselines on several languages.
2) An analysis linking performance to Wikipedia resource size and interlanguage link availability, with ablations on type signal (Freebase) and Wikipedia intersections.
3) Practical observations on multi-source training and monolingual gains from the same features.

Strengths
- Broad empirical coverage and clear analyses: The paper evaluates across nine languages, including five low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts, and provides useful ablations (e.g., removing Freebase query and interlanguage links). The correlation between Wikipedia size and the utility of wikifier features is convincingly demonstrated.
- Resource-light transfer setting: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia, no sentence- or word-aligned bitext. For several targets (e.g., DE/NL/TR), the wikifier features yield sizable gains over delexicalized baselines, showing genuine cross-lingual signal.
- Competitive results against established baselines: On CoNLL direct transfer, the model outperforms Täckström et al.’s baseline and even their bitext-clustered variant (though orthogonal). For several low-resource targets, the “wikifier-only” configuration is strong, sometimes exceeding Zhang et al. (2016) despite using fewer resources.
- Simplicity and compatibility with established methods: Building on a well-known feature-based NER pipeline, the proposal integrates smoothly with existing systems, and the featureization (top-2 candidates, neighbor n-grams) is straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior Wikipedia/EL-based NER: Using Wikipedia categories/types for NER is not new (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa, 2007; Nothman et al., 2012; Sil & Yates, 2013). The cross-lingual extension via EL is natural but incremental. The technical changes to the wikifier (candidate gen and ranker features) are minor and lack detailed validation.
- Heavy reliance on deprecated/opaque resources and missing EL metrics: The method depends on Freebase types and a specific cross-lingual EL system (Tsai & Roth, 2016). Freebase is deprecated; reproducibility and future utility are unclear without a Wikidata-based replacement. No EL accuracy (e.g., P@1) or runtime is reported, despite “wikify every n-gram up to 4” being potentially noisy and computationally expensive.
- Incomplete and potentially cherry-picked evaluation: The paper’s claims versus Zhang et al. (2016) appear to hinge on selecting the best configuration per target (e.g., “wikifier-only” for non-Latin scripts), yet the narrative often references the full model (+Gazetteers/+Wikifier). This needs to be explicit. No statistical significance testing is provided. Moreover, there are no comparisons to modern strong cross-lingual NER baselines (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot, aligned multilingual embeddings), which are now standard; this makes it hard to assess the practical value of the approach.
- Label mapping and evaluation fairness: The handling of MISC (ignored in evaluation for CoNLL) and the heuristic remapping of MISC→LOC using Freebase when training for low-resource transfer may bias results and change the task definition. It is unclear how comparable the reported scores are to prior work under these altered label sets.
- Sensitivity to resource size: The approach degrades sharply as Wikipedia shrinks; for YO/BN, the full model performs poorly and only “wikifier-only” helps. This limits utility in truly low-resource settings and suggests the method’s core dependency may be fragile.

Questions to authors
1) Which configuration constitutes your “direct transfer model” when claiming improvements over Zhang et al. (2016)? Please report “best-per-target” results explicitly and ensure comparisons use the same label inventories and evaluation protocols.
2) Can you replace Freebase with Wikidata and report results? How sensitive are your features to the specific KB (type system, category coverage)?
3) What is the EL accuracy of your modified wikifier on your corpora, and what is the runtime/throughput of “wikify every n-gram up to 4”? Please provide P@1, candidate recall, and processing time per document.
4) Are the reported gains statistically significant? Please add significance testing, especially for cross-lingual improvements over the strongest baselines.
5) Can you include a modern zero-shot baseline (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuned on English CoNLL) and/or aligned fastText/MUSE features to contextualize your gains?
6) How robust is your approach to tokenization and segmentation in non-Latin scripts (BN/TA)? Did you apply any normalization or language-specific preprocessing?
7) Will you release code, the gazetteers, and feature extraction scripts to enable replication?

Overall, the paper presents a sensible, largely established approach, with careful empirical work and some useful insights. However, the methodological novelty is limited, key dependencies are dated, and the evaluation lacks comparison to strong contemporary baselines and statistical rigor. I encourage the authors to address the reproducibility/KB issues, clarify comparisons, report EL metrics and runtime, and add modern baselines. These would substantially strengthen the case for acceptance.
