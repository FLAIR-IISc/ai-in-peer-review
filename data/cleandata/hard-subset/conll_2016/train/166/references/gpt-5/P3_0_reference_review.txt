Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language document to English Wikipedia. It then uses the top-k candidates’ Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features within a Ratinov & Roth-style NER model.
- The authors show that wikifier-derived features substantially improve both monolingual and direct-transfer NER, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines on CoNLL (ES/NL/DE) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), without using parallel data or target-language annotation.
- The paper provides analyses of when these features help (dependency on Wikipedia size and English interlanguage links), and shows modest gains from multi-source training.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with strong empirical payoff: Turning entity linking “upstream” and using cross-lingual grounding to derive delexicalized features is elegant. The feature design (types and categories from top-2 candidates, local context propagation) works surprisingly well across languages and scripts, improving both monolingual and direct transfer.
- Minimal assumptions/resources: The approach does not require parallel corpora, bilingual lexicons, or target-language annotation—just multilingual Wikipedia and an off-the-shelf cross-lingual wikifier. This makes it applicable to many languages and fits the low-resource setting.
- Solid empirical results and breadth: The model improves over strong baselines on CoNLL (including Täckström’s direct transfer and bitext-cluster baselines) and exceeds Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages despite using fewer resources. The monolingual gains confirm the general utility of wikifier features.
- Useful analyses and ablations: The ablation isolating “Wikifier only” helps understand signal strength; Table 3 shows how Freebase coverage and interlanguage link density drive performance; the multi-source training experiment is informative and practical.
- Practical implications: The findings establish a robust, language-agnostic feature family (EL-derived types/categories) that can be plugged into NER systems and plausibly into other sequence tasks (e.g., mention detection), with potential to combine with orthogonal signals (parallel projection, cross-lingual embeddings).

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior EL+NER work: Using Wikipedia categories/types for NER is not new (Kazama & Torisawa 2007; Sil & Yates 2013), and the key technical dependency (cross-lingual wikifier) is external (Tsai & Roth 2016). The novelty lies in the cross-lingual use and the decision to wikify all n-grams, but the methodological leap is moderate.
- Evaluation choices may confound comparability: The paper remaps/removes labels (e.g., converting certain MISC to LOC via Freebase, dropping TIME/TTL) and ignores MISC in some cross-lingual matrices. It is unclear whether the CoNLL direct-transfer comparisons (including to Täckström) are done under identical label sets. This needs clarification to ensure fairness and reproducibility.
- Dependence on Freebase and English-centric KB signals: The approach relies heavily on Freebase types and Wikipedia categories via English interlanguage links. For very low-resource Wikipedias, performance degrades sharply (as the authors show). Freebase’s retirement raises maintainability concerns; Wikidata or DBpedia types might be more sustainable and broader-coverage.
- Limited analysis of errors and types: Results are only aggregate F1; no per-entity-type breakdown, precision/recall, or qualitative error analysis is provided. It would be valuable to know whether gains are concentrated in PER/LOC vs ORG, whether mention boundary detection or typing benefits more, and how linking errors propagate.
- Missing stronger contemporary baselines: While comparisons to Täckström and Zhang et al. are appropriate, the paper does not compare against direct-transfer models using cross-lingual word embeddings or modern neural sequence taggers (e.g., multilingual BiLSTM-CRF with aligned embeddings). Even if orthogonal, these are increasingly standard and may change the competitive landscape.

Questions to authors
- Label mapping and evaluation: For the CoNLL direct-transfer results in Table 2, are all four labels (PER/LOC/ORG/MISC) evaluated, and is this consistent with the Täckström baselines? For low-resource languages, how exactly were tags remapped and how might this affect cross-language comparability?
- Wikifier coverage and efficiency: What proportion of tokens/n-grams receive NIL vs linked candidates across languages, and what is the runtime cost of wikifying all n-grams up to length 4? Are there heuristics to prune spans without losing recall?
- Error profile: Do most gains come from better typing (PER/LOC/ORG) or boundary detection? Can you provide per-type P/R or a brief error analysis illustrating common failure modes (e.g., ORG vs LOC confusions, overlinking function n-grams)?
- Sustainability of KB features: Have you tried Wikidata instance-of/subclass-of types or DBpedia ontologies as a drop-in replacement for Freebase? How sensitive are results to the number of candidate titles (top-1 vs top-2 vs top-3)?
- Fairness to baselines: Did Täckström’s baselines have access to target-language gazetteers from Wikipedia? If not, can you provide a variant without target-language gazetteers to isolate the contribution of wikifier features?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a CRF or globally normalized decoder; greedy decoding can propagate local linking errors into tag sequences.
- Explore combining with cross-lingual embeddings and/or silver data projected from Wikipedia (e.g., Nothman et al.)—orthogonal to your features.
- Report per-entity-type breakdowns and statistical significance to strengthen claims.
- A brief qualitative case study (success and failure examples) would help interpretability.
- Switching from Freebase to Wikidata would likely improve coverage and long-term viability.
