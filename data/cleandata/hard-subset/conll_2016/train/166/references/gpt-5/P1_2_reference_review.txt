Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a cross-lingual NER approach that relies on cross-lingual wikification to derive language-independent features. A model trained on one or more source languages (primarily English) is directly transferred to target languages without target-language supervision or parallel text. The key idea is to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia titles using a cross-lingual wikifier and to use the resulting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features, along with ranker confidence features. The authors also build language-specific gazetteers from Wikipedia titles. Experiments cover CoNLL (EN/DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), with ablations and multi-source training analyses.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Methodology: A practical, delexicalized feature set for NER based on cross-lingual wikification that can be applied broadly with only multilingual Wikipedia (no bitext or target annotations). The modifications to wikify all n-grams and to use top-2 candidates/types and ranker features are central.
2) Empirical evidence across nine languages showing consistent gains in both monolingual and direct transfer settings, including improvements over strong baselines (Täckström et al. 2012) and a weakly supervised cross-lingual system (Zhang et al. 2016) on low-resource languages.
3) Analysis: Insightful ablations showing performance correlation with Wikipedia size and interlanguage-link coverage; study of the impact of Freebase availability; and evidence that multi-source training adds modest further gains.

Strengths
- Clear, broadly applicable idea with minimal assumptions: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia and an existing cross-lingual wikifier. It avoids relying on parallel data, language-specific heuristics, or target supervision, yet yields strong cross-lingual signals. This makes it highly practical for scaling NER to many languages.

- Consistent and sizable improvements in direct transfer: Wikifier features add 10+ F1 on multiple languages (e.g., NL/DE/TR) and substantially outperform prior direct-transfer baselines. On low-resource languages, the method surpasses Zhang et al. (2016) while using fewer resources (no bitext, no native speaker interaction), a notable empirical result.

- Sensible ablations and diagnostics: The experiments convincingly demonstrate why gains vary by language. Removing Freebase queries and interlanguage links degrades performance toward the levels observed for low-resource languages, validating the claim that Wikipedia size/coverage drives wikifier feature quality. This is a helpful and honest analysis.

- Contributions to monolingual NER and multi-source transfer: Wikifier features improve monolingual models across all nine languages on top of strong baselines. The multi-source training results are modest but positive and actionable, showing that adding annotated data from other languages typically does not hurt and can help.

- Methodological clarity and reasonable design choices: Modifying the wikifier to avoid token-based candidate generation for multi-token n-grams, using top-2 candidates, and including ranker confidence as features are well-motivated and practically effective for dense feature generation.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Freebase and English-centric resources: The approach leans on Freebase types and English Wikipedia categories; Freebase’s limited coverage for certain languages is shown to be a bottleneck, and Freebase has been deprecated. The paper would be stronger if it replaced/augmented Freebase with Wikidata and analyzed the effect, especially for non-Latin-script and low-resource languages.

- Limited novelty relative to prior use of Wikipedia for NER: While the cross-lingual application and integration with an EL system are valuable, the core idea—leveraging Wikipedia-type/category signals for NER—has precedents (e.g., Kazama and Torisawa 2007; Nothman et al. 2012). The novelty is primarily in the cross-lingual wikification at scale and the specific feature engineering, which may be seen as incremental.

- Computational and scalability considerations not addressed: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 is potentially expensive. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or memory usage for the wikifier or overall pipeline, nor discuss strategies for pruning, caching, or handling long documents. This affects practical adoption.

- Fairness and clarity of resource usage in transfer: In direct transfer, models are trained on English but test-time features include target-language gazetteers and wikifier outputs derived from target-language Wikipedia. While consistent with the paper’s premise, the comparison to prior direct-transfer baselines that do not exploit target-language Wikipedia is somewhat asymmetric. An ablation that removes target gazetteers (and/or restricts to English-only resources) would clarify the contribution of each component.

- Label space and evaluation choices: For cross-lingual comparisons, the paper removes/retags MISC labels and maps some MISC to LOC via Freebase grounding. This is pragmatic but may introduce biases and complicate comparability with prior work; more discussion or an additional evaluation with original label spaces would help. Significance testing is also not reported.

Questions to authors
- Can the method use Wikidata instead of Freebase? Do results change materially for low-resource languages if Wikidata properties are used for typing?

- What is the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (time per document, memory)? Any pruning strategies (e.g., restricting to content words, candidate thresholds) without harming accuracy?

- How many tokens/n-grams are successfully linked per language, and what fraction of final NER features come from types vs. categories vs. ranker scores?

- In direct transfer, what is the incremental contribution of target-language gazetteers vs. wikifier features? Please provide ablations to disentangle these.

- How sensitive is performance to using top-1 vs. top-2 vs. top-k candidates, and to the choice of n-gram maximum length?

- Do the wikifier features primarily aid boundary detection or type classification? Per-entity-type breakdowns would be useful.

- Could the approach be combined with modern neural sequence models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and cross-lingual embeddings, and do you expect complementary gains?

Overall, this is a solid, practical paper with clear empirical value and good analyses. Addressing the Freebase dependency and clarifying computational and resource-usage aspects would further strengthen it.
