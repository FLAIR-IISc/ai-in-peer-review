Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that augments a standard sequence tagging model with language-independent features derived from a cross-lingual wikifier. The wikifier grounds every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia entries; the system then uses associated Freebase types, Wikipedia categories, and ranker features as signals for mention detection and typing. The approach assumes only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that cross-lingual wikification-derived features can substantially improve both monolingual and direct-transfer NER across nine languages, including several low-resource, non-Latin-script languages.
2) Analyses isolating the impact of Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage on performance, and a small multi-source training study.
3) A practical recipe for building target-language gazetteers from Wikipedia titles and integrating them as delexicalized features.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains over direct-transfer baselines on CoNLL languages. The wikifier features consistently add 10+ F1 on several languages in the zero-shot scenario (e.g., nl/de/tr), and improve monolingual models as well. The comparisons against Täckström et al. (2012) (baseline and bitext-cluster variants) are favorable and on standard data.
- Broad, multilingual evaluation including low-resource settings. Testing on Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil provides evidence beyond the common CoNLL set. The observation that wikifier-only features dominate when scripts diverge (Bengali/Tamil) is actionable and aligns with intuition.
- Ablation and robustness analysis. The experiments that remove Freebase queries and randomly drop interlanguage links convincingly demonstrate (i) how much of the signal comes from types/categories and (ii) the dependence on Wikipedia size/coverage. This is informative for practitioners contemplating deployment in genuinely low-resource languages.
- Orthogonality to parallel-data methods and simplicity. The approach uses established components (averaged perceptron NER; feature-based modeling) and requires only Wikipedia dumps, making it easy to integrate with other transfer techniques, and avoiding the cost/availability issues of bitext.
- Multi-source training shows modest but consistent gains. While small, the study indicates that adding annotated data from other languages generally helps and rarely hurts, and it identifies when English is not the best source.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior Wikipedia-based NER and EL/NER integration. Using Wikipedia categories/Freebase types for NER (Kazama & Torisawa, 2007; Nothman et al., 2012) and joint/linked EL–NER signals (Sil & Yates, 2013) are well-established. The main new piece is applying cross-lingual wikifier features for direct transfer and doing dense n-gram linking, which is incremental. The choice to link every n-gram up to 4 without stronger justification raises concerns about noise and computational burden.
- Heavy dependence on Freebase and English-centric resources. The method relies critically on Freebase types and interlanguage links to English; performance drops sharply when these are weakened. Freebase has been deprecated; it is unclear whether Wikidata can serve as a drop-in replacement with comparable gains. For truly low-resource languages (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), coverage gaps undermine the core signal.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline coverage for the current state of the field. There are no comparisons to more recent cross-lingual neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings, multilingual BERT/XLM-R zero-shot transfer), which are strong and widely used. Without these, claims of state-of-the-art performance in direct transfer are not compelling by today’s standards. Similarly, target-language gazetteers constructed from Wikipedia are used in “direct transfer,” which may give an advantage over baselines that do not leverage target-language Wikipedia resources; this raises fairness questions.
- Experimental clarity and reproducibility gaps. Handling of MISC: the paper mentions ignoring MISC in some analyses and remapping MISC→LOC under certain conditions for low-resource evaluations; this ad-hoc relabeling could bias results and makes cross-setting comparisons hard. Tokenization, wikifier coverage (e.g., fraction of tokens/n-grams linked), and runtime/compute costs for “wikifying every n-gram” are not reported. No per-entity-type breakdowns or significance testing are provided. Hyperparameters for the perceptron model and thresholds for the linker confidence are under-specified.
- Risk of label leakage and over-reliance on type features. If a mention is linked and typed as person/organization/location by Freebase/Wikipedia, the NER task becomes close to a lookup. This might be acceptable if the goal is practical extraction with Wikipedia support, but it muddies comparison to delexicalized transfer methods that do not access target-side knowledge bases.

Questions to authors
- Baseline fairness: In the direct transfer setting, do you consider using target-language Wikipedia (for gazetteers and cross-lingual wikification) as part of the allowed resources? Could you report results without target-language gazetteers to align more closely with Täckström’s setup?
- Contemporary baselines: Can you add zero-shot mBERT/XLM-R and a BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings as baselines on CoNLL and at least Turkish/Tagalog? This would contextualize the gains.
- Label handling: For the main CoNLL results in Table 2, are MISC entities included in evaluation? For low-resource sets, precisely how many tokens/entities are affected by the MISC→LOC remapping and removals? Please provide per-type F1.
- Wikifier details: What proportion of tokens/n-grams receive non-NIL links per language? What is the top-1 precision of the wikifier on these corpora? Why top-2 candidates for features, and how sensitive are results to k?
- Resources and efficiency: What is the runtime and memory footprint to wikify all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per document? Are code, gazetteers, and processed resources released to support reproducibility?
- Freebase/Wikidata: Have you attempted replacing Freebase with Wikidata properties/types? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper presents a sensible and practically useful application of cross-lingual wikification to NER with thorough multilingual experiments and informative analyses. However, the methodological novelty is modest, the approach is tightly coupled to now-deprecated Freebase and English-centric resources, and the evaluation omits strong contemporary baselines and clearer fairness controls. I would encourage substantial revisions addressing these concerns.
