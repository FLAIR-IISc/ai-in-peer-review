Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes reversing the traditional NER→EL pipeline: it first cross-lingually wikifies every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a document to English Wikipedia and then uses the top candidates’ Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features for NER. This enables direct transfer from source-language supervision to many target languages without parallel data or target-language annotation.
- It introduces practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier to make “wikify-every-n-gram” feasible and less error-prone, and shows that these wikifier-derived features boost both monolingual and cross-lingual NER.
- It presents broad empirical validation across nine languages (CoNLL + five low-resource), with analyses linking gains to Wikipedia size/interlanguage links, and shows multi-source training gives further improvements.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Using cross-lingual EL as a universal feature generator for NER is a clean, general idea that meaningfully departs from standard practice. The “reverse pipeline” is compelling: disambiguation provides type cues that are truly delexicalized, enabling transfer with no parallel data. The modifications to make wikify-every-n-gram work (candidate generation changes, removing document-level mention embeddings) are simple yet consequential.
- Strong cross-lingual gains under low-resource assumptions: Wikifier features add substantial F1 in direct transfer, e.g., NL 35.06→47.14, DE 55.04→60.97, TR 64.07→65.32. The method outperforms Täckström’s bitext-cluster transfer (which uses parallel corpora) on NL/DE and surpasses Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages while assuming strictly fewer resources (no parallel, no native-speaker interaction). This demonstrates the proposed features are highly effective delexicalized signals.
- Generality and scalability: The approach only requires multilingual Wikipedia; no sentence/word alignments, no POS taggers, and no language-specific rules. The same model applies to both Latin and non-Latin scripts (and the study correctly highlights when to drop lexical/gazetteer features and rely on the wikifier).
- Careful analyses: The ablations that remove Freebase lookups and randomly drop interlanguage links convincingly explain performance degradation in truly low-resource setups and quantify the dependency on Wikipedia coverage. The train/test matrix and multi-source training results, though modest, show the method is not tied to English-only training and gains from more sources.
- Practical signal beyond direct transfer: Wikifier features also improve strong monolingual baselines across all nine languages, indicating utility beyond the cross-lingual use case.

Weaknesses
- Dependency on Freebase and English Wikipedia categories: The method leans heavily on Freebase types and category metadata; Freebase is deprecated and categories are noisy and inconsistent cross-lingually. While the paper shows performance drops when Freebase is removed, it does not explore Wikidata as a modern, better-covered alternative. This threatens reproducibility and long-term utility.
- Limited modern baselines: The comparisons omit contemporary cross-lingual NER methods using multilingual contextual encoders (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R, multilingual adapters, or alignment-based fine-tuning). Even if the focus is on resource-lean settings, a lightweight mBERT zero-shot baseline (no parallel, no target labels) would sharpen the empirical claim and situate the contribution relative to current practice.
- Overlinking and computational concerns: Wikifying every n-gram is aggressive. The paper offers minimal analysis of precision/recall trade-offs, linking error types, NIL handling, and the effect of the “top-2 candidates” heuristic and linker confidence. There is no runtime or memory profile; practitioners may worry about throughput at web scale.
- Evaluation choices and potential confounds: The re-mapping/removal of MISC/TIME/TTL tags across datasets may bias results or complicate comparability to prior work (especially for CoNLL). More detail on how these decisions impact scores per entity type would increase confidence in the conclusions.
- Incomplete feature ablations: The paper attributes gains to both Freebase types and Wikipedia categories plus ranker features, but does not disentangle their individual contributions (e.g., top-1 vs top-2, categories vs types, with/without ranker features). A compact ablation could clarify which signals are primary.

Questions to authors
- Can the approach be ported from Freebase to Wikidata Q-IDs and property-based types? Do you expect similar or better gains, especially for low-resource languages?
- How is NIL handled in features? Do you threshold the ranker score to suppress low-confidence links, and what is the effect of using only top-1 vs top-2 candidates?
- What is the runtime cost of wikifying every n-gram (n ≤ 4) for typical document lengths? Any practical pruning strategies (e.g., stoplists, POS filters) explored beyond the candidate-generation tweak?
- How are Wikipedia categories normalized/filtered to reduce noise? Do you use any hierarchy or frequency-based pruning?
- In multi-source training, how do you balance datasets across languages (sampling/weighting)? Did you observe negative transfer for any pairs?
- Could a joint or multi-task NER–EL model (with the same resource assumptions) further reduce overlinking and improve boundary detection?

Overall assessment
This paper introduces a simple, elegant idea—turn cross-lingual entity linking into universal, delexicalized features—and backs it with broad experiments and informative analyses. While some engineering choices (Freebase reliance, limited modern baselines) and runtime/error analyses are underdeveloped, the conceptual contribution is clear and impactful, and the results are strong under lean-resource assumptions. I favor acceptance for its novelty and potential to influence how we think about cross-lingual transfer with readily available knowledge sources.
