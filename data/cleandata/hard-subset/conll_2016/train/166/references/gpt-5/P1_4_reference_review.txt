Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to create language-independent features. Instead of treating wikification as a downstream step after NER, the authors ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the input to English Wikipedia and use the resulting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as delexicalized features within an Ratinov and Roth (2009)-style NER model. Two practical modifications to Tsai and Roth (2016)’s cross-lingual wikifier make this feasible for dense, all-ngrams grounding. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data) and is evaluated both monolingually and in direct transfer across nine languages, including five low-resource ones with non-Latin scripts.

As I see it, the key contributions are:
1) A simple, general, and effective way to convert cross-lingual wikification outputs into language-independent NER features by grounding all n-grams and using Freebase types/Wiki categories (primary contribution).
2) Extensive empirical evaluation across nine languages (including five low-resource/non-Latin), showing consistent gains in both monolingual and direct-transfer settings, and analyses that relate performance to Wikipedia size and cross-lingual linkage (solid experimental contribution).
3) Evidence that multi-source training is feasible and can yield further gains, and that this approach outperforms prior direct-transfer and weakly supervised baselines that assume more resources (notable comparative result).

Strengths
- Clear, impactful idea: using wikification upstream for NER. The paper convincingly flips the conventional pipeline by demonstrating that disambiguation signals (Freebase types, categories, ranker confidence) provide robust, delexicalized features. The “wikifier only” rows already preserve strong cross-lingual signal, especially where lexical overlap is low (e.g., non-Latin scripts).

- Strong empirical evidence with ablations and analyses. The study spans CoNLL (en/de/es/nl) and five low-resource languages (tr/tl/yo/bn/ta), showing:
  • Monolingual gains from +Wikifier on top of base+gazetteers for all nine languages.
  • Direct transfer improvements that are especially large on Dutch/German/Turkish, and that outperform Täckström et al. (2012) (even with their bitext clusters) and Zhang et al. (2016), despite using fewer resources.
  • Careful ablations (removing Freebase queries; reducing interlanguage links) tie performance to Wikipedia size and cross-lingual linkage density, providing insight into where the method is likely to succeed or struggle.

- Minimal resource assumptions and broad applicability. The method relies only on multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text, no native speaker annotation), yet scales across diverse scripts and languages. The wikifier modifications (restricting candidate generation for n-grams, removing document-level mention-embedding features) make all-ngrams grounding practical and less error-prone.

- Sensible multi-source transfer exploration. The train/test matrix and the multi-source experiments show that training on multiple high-resource languages further improves cross-lingual performance and that English is often best but not universally (e.g., Spanish best for Yoruba), offering guidance for practical deployment.

- Reproducibility-minded design choices. Many features and components are standard (R&R 2009 framework), and the paper provides concrete implementation details (n ≤ 4, top-2 candidates, feature contexts, ranker confidence as a linker). The ablation protocols (interlanguage link drop) are simple and interpretable.

Weaknesses
- Heavy dependence on Wikipedia and Freebase coverage. The method’s effectiveness correlates strongly with the size and English intersection of the target-language Wikipedia, and with Freebase availability. Results degrade (e.g., Yoruba) when intersections are limited, and the “W.-FB query” ablation shows reliance on Freebase types. This raises questions about robustness for truly low-resource settings where both Wikipedia and Freebase coverage are sparse or absent. Given Freebase’s deprecation, a path to Wikidata types or other KBs should be discussed.

- Evaluation design compromises complicate comparability. For low-resource datasets, TIME/TTL tags are dropped and MISC is reassigned to LOC using Freebase types, potentially biasing both training and evaluation toward resources the method exploits. For CoNLL, MISC is ignored to match low-resource settings. These choices may inflate relative gains for approaches leveraging Wiki/KB signals and reduce comparability to standard NER benchmarks.

- Limited methodological novelty relative to broader literature. The core NER framework is a decade-old perceptron system with feature engineering; the main novelty lies in using upstream wikifier features at scale. The paper does not compare against modern cross-lingual embedding-based or neural NER baselines, nor combine with orthogonal techniques (e.g., projection, multilingual embeddings) despite mentioning them. This makes it harder to assess the method’s competitiveness in current settings.

- Computational and scalability concerns underexplored. Grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) with candidate generation and ranking is potentially expensive. The paper does not report runtime, memory, or throughput, nor discuss pruning strategies or latency implications, which are critical for practical use on long documents or large corpora.

- Limited error analysis and per-entity-type breakdown. While aggregate F1’s are reported, the paper lacks detailed error breakdowns (e.g., boundary vs type errors, per-entity performance) and qualitative analysis to guide further improvements (e.g., how often top-2 candidate types mislead; NIL handling; common failure modes in non-Latin scripts).

Questions to authors
- Can you report runtime/throughput and memory usage for wikifying all n-grams (per document and per language), and any pruning thresholds? How does cost scale with document length and with candidate set sizes?

- How do you map Freebase types and Wikipedia categories to PER/LOC/ORG? Is there a curated mapping list? How sensitive are results to this mapping?

- What is the NIL handling strategy? Do you include explicit NIL features or thresholds using ranker confidence? Any empirical analysis of linker rejection thresholds and their impact on precision/recall?

- Could you substitute Freebase with Wikidata (or another KB) and report whether results are similar, especially for low-resource languages with weak Freebase coverage?

- In direct transfer, do you use target-language gazetteers at test time? If so, can you provide a baseline without target gazetteers to isolate the incremental benefit of wikifier features? How does this affect comparisons to prior “resource-light” baselines?

- Please provide per-entity-type F1 and an error analysis (boundary vs type). Do wikifier features disproportionately help typing vs boundary detection?

Additional comments (minor)
- Clarify tokenization and casing across non-Latin scripts and whether capitalization features are disabled there.
- Consider reporting statistical significance for main improvements.
- Releasing code, resources, and the language-to-type mapping would substantially improve reproducibility.

Overall: The paper presents a clear, practically useful idea with solid empirical validation across many languages. Despite some dependencies and evaluation caveats, it advances cross-lingual NER under minimal resource assumptions. I lean toward acceptance.
