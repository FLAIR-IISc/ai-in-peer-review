Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer NER approach that relies on cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features. A standard NER model (averaged perceptron with BIO decoding) is trained on annotated data in one or more source languages and applied to target languages without target-side supervision. The key idea is to wikify every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target text using a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai & Roth, 2016), and use the linked English Wikipedia page’s Freebase types and categories as features, along with Wikipedia-derived gazetteers. The authors evaluate on four CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, showing gains from wikifier features in monolingual and cross-lingual settings, and analyze performance as a function of Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that cross-lingual wikification-derived semantic types (Freebase and Wikipedia categories) are strong delexicalized features for direct-transfer NER, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines and a non-direct-transfer weakly supervised system on several languages.

2) A practical, resource-light pipeline that requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no bitext or human-in-the-loop), including modifications to wikify all n-grams and ablations quantifying dependencies on interlanguage links and Freebase coverage.

3) Broad, multilingual evaluation (9 languages, including non-Latin scripts) with analyses on multi-source training and conditions under which features help.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with strong practical value: The approach is conceptually simple and widely applicable—ground target text to English Wikipedia and use KB-derived types as language-agnostic signals. It needs only Wikipedia dumps and a wikifier, which makes it attractive for low-resource settings where parallel data or native speakers are not available.

- Solid empirical gains and fair comparisons: The wikifier features consistently improve both monolingual and cross-lingual NER across 9 languages. In direct transfer, the method substantially outperforms Täckström et al.’s cross-lingual clustering baseline and Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages, despite using fewer resources. The reported average and language-wise improvements are meaningful (e.g., +10 F1 on Dutch/German/Turkish with wikifier features).

- Thorough ablations and analysis: The “wikifier-only” condition isolates the contribution of the KB features; the degradations from removing Freebase queries or interlanguage links convincingly explain weaker performance on very small Wikipedias. The analysis that performance scales with Wikipedia size is informative. The study of multi-source training shows small but consistent gains and identifies best training languages per target.

- Applicability to non-Latin scripts: The study includes Bengali and Tamil, with a thoughtful discussion of why lexical and gazetteer features fail and wikifier-only performs best, highlighting the method’s delexicalized strength when scripts diverge.

- Reusability and transparency: The model builds on known components (Ratinov & Roth 2009; Tsai & Roth 2016) and details the key feature groups and wikifier modifications, supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Freebase and English-centric KBs: Freebase has been deprecated; reliance on Freebase types and English Wikipedia categories may limit longevity and coverage for many languages (especially those with sparse interlanguage links). While the paper analyzes this dependence, it does not provide an alternative (e.g., Wikidata properties) or quantify how type noise affects NER decisions.

- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior Wikipedia/EL-based NER: Using Wikipedia categories/types as features for NER is not new (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa 2007; Sil & Yates 2013, though monolingual). The novelty here is their cross-lingual application via wikification-at-scale. However, the core NER architecture is a decade-old feature-based perceptron, and the paper does not explore joint NER+EL or more discriminative learning that could better exploit candidate sets and confidence.

- Incomplete comparison to stronger modern baselines: The paper compares to Täckström (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) but not to more recent cross-lingual NER approaches (e.g., multilingual embeddings, projection with better aligners, or multilingual pretrained models). Even acknowledging orthogonality, including a neural delexicalized baseline or a projection-plus-wikifier combination would strengthen claims.

- Use of language-specific gazetteers at test time muddies “direct transfer”: Gazetteers are built per target language from Wikipedia titles. While unsupervised, they provide target-language lexical knowledge and can be strong cues. The paper should more explicitly separate results that use only source-trained parameters and cross-lingual features from those that additionally use target-language gazetteers, to clarify what “direct transfer” entails.

- Label set and evaluation choices: For low-resource languages, TIME/TTL are removed and MISC is remapped or dropped; in CoNLL languages, MISC is ignored in some cross-lingual evaluations. These choices complicate comparability. The paper does not provide per-type scores or sensitivity to these mappings, nor an error analysis of boundary errors vs type errors.

- Scalability and runtime not addressed: Wikifying every n-gram up to 4 per document can be computationally heavy. There is no discussion of runtime/throughput, caching, NIL detection precision, or how overlapping n-grams are reconciled at feature time, which matters for real-world applicability.

Questions to authors
- Direct transfer protocol: In the cross-lingual experiments, do you include target-language gazetteers at test time? If yes, can you report results without target-language gazetteers to isolate the impact of purely cross-lingual features?

- Wikifier details: What is the runtime cost of wikifying every n-gram (n ≤ 4) per document (tokens/sec)? How do you resolve overlapping n-grams when multiple candidates fire types for the same token? Do you threshold the “linker” confidence, and how sensitive are results to k (top-2) and the confidence threshold?

- KB choice: Have you tried replacing Freebase with Wikidata properties/types? If so, how do results change, especially for languages with poor Freebase coverage?

- Tokenization and scripts: How is tokenization handled for non-Latin scripts, and how sensitive is performance to tokenization quality? Are there language-specific heuristics (e.g., segmentation) that could bias results?

- Error analysis: Can you provide per-entity-type F1 and a brief error categorization (boundary vs type, NIL errors, systematic confusions)? This would clarify where wikifier features help most.

Additional comments
- The idea is timely and practically valuable; migrating from Freebase to Wikidata and exploring joint or neural models could substantially strengthen both novelty and robustness. Combining with projection or multilingual embeddings seems straightforward and could push performance further, especially for very small Wikipedias.
