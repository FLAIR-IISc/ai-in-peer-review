- Paper proposes direct-transfer cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features and an averaged perceptron BIO tagger trained on source-language data.  
- Core idea: ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia titles and use Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features, plus per-language gazetteers harvested from Wikipedia.  

Main contributions:
- Shows cross-lingual wikification yields strong delexicalized features that substantially improve direct-transfer NER over baselines on CoNLL languages and is competitive on several low-resource languages.  
- Presents practical feature design: top-2 candidate types, ranker confidence, and scalable n-gram-wide wikification modifications.  
- Empirically links Wikipedia size/interlanguage link coverage to NER transfer quality and studies modest gains from multi-source training.  

Strengths:
- Clear, broadly applicable idea demonstrating that Wikipedia/Freebase typing signals are powerful and complementary to gazetteers.  
- Requires minimal supervision (English NER training set and multilingual Wikipedia) and no parallel data, making it appealing for many languages.  
- Thorough experimental setup across 9 languages with meaningful ablations and correlation analyses between Wikipedia resources and performance.  
- Provides practical guidance on when to prefer wikifier-only features versus adding lexical/gazetteer features, and shows scalability with multi-source training.  
- Keeps NER modeling simple and reproducible by using a standard averaged perceptron BIO tagger.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to prior work using Wikipedia categories/types and joint NER-EL linking signals; novelty is mainly practical/engineering.  
- Reliance on deprecated Freebase and interlanguage links harms generality and future reproducibility; Wikidata would be more appropriate.  
- Lacks comparisons to contemporary neural zero-shot cross-lingual baselines (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), making competitiveness unclear.  
- Possible evaluation confounds in low-resource datasets due to label-space alterations (TIME/TTL removal, MISC retagging) that may affect fairness of comparisons.  
- Scalability and noise concerns for wikifying all n-grams: no runtime/memory reporting or systematic analysis of overlinking noise and trade-offs.  
- Weak NER modeling for non-Latin languages (Bengali, Tamil) with no character/subword/transliteration features, limiting effectiveness in those settings.  

Questions for authors:
- Please compare to modern zero-shot neural baselines (mBERT/XLM-R) and evaluate whether wikifier features help these encoders.  
- Will you replace Freebase with Wikidata and do the reported trends hold with Wikidata? Will you release code, gazetteers, and feature extraction tools?  
- For REFLEX/LORELEI experiments, did Zhang et al. (2016) use the same label normalization; provide side-by-side mapping or sensitivity analysis.  
- What are the average numbers of n-grams wikified per sentence, runtime per document, and are there pruning strategies that retain gains at lower cost?  
- Ablations: what is the effect of top-1 vs top-2 wikifier candidates, and how effectively does ranker-confidence suppress noisy links in terms of precision/recall?
