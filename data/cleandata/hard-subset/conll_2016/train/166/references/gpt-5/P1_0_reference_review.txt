Summary
This paper proposes a direct-transfer approach to cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) that leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features. Each n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language document is grounded to the intersection of the target language and English Wikipedia using a cross-lingual wikifier, and features derived from the top candidate(s) (Freebase types, Wikipedia categories, and ranker scores) are fed into a strong NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) alongside non-lexical features and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no bitext, no supervision in the target language), and is evaluated across CoNLL languages (EN/DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), in monolingual and direct-transfer setups. The authors analyze performance as a function of Wikipedia size and explore multi-source training.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective way to repurpose cross-lingual wikification signals (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) as delexicalized features for cross-lingual NER, avoiding reliance on parallel data and enabling direct transfer to many languages.
2) A systematic empirical evaluation showing that wikifier features are helpful both monolingually and in direct transfer, with detailed analysis of how Wikipedia size and interlanguage link coverage drive performance.
3) Practical engineering of a wikify-all-ngrams pipeline (including modifications to candidate generation/ranking) and a demonstration that multi-source training further helps.

Strengths
- Clear, resource-lean cross-lingual story: The method relies only on multilingual Wikipedia and an off-the-shelf cross-lingual wikifier. No parallel data, alignment, or target-language annotation is needed. This makes the approach widely applicable and attractive for truly low-resource settings.

- Strong empirical signal for direct transfer: The wikifier features provide large gains in direct transfer across multiple languages, especially those that share Latin script with English. The improvements over a base delexicalized model are substantial (e.g., in direct transfer, +wikifier notably boosts Dutch and German), indicating the utility of the semantic typing signal.

- Thorough ablations and analysis: The paper goes beyond raw scores to show that reducing interlanguage links and removing Freebase queries degrades performance, thus causally tying gains to the coverage/quality of Wikipedia/Wikidata-style knowledge. This makes the findings more interpretable and actionable (e.g., invest in better cross-lingual linking to help NER).

- Monolingual gains and multi-source training: Wikifier features help even when training and testing within the same language, suggesting they complement lexical cues. Multi-source training provides additional, albeit modest, improvements, reinforcing the model’s generality.

- Sensible engineering choices for wikify-all-ngrams: Modifications to candidate generation (avoiding token-based candidates for n>1) and ranker features are well-motivated for high-recall wikification at scale, a non-trivial aspect of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on Freebase and Wikipedia coverage: The approach’s benefits hinge on interlanguage links and type coverage; performance collapses in languages with small Wikipedias and poor KB coverage (e.g., YO, BN). The paper acknowledges this but the practical consequence is stark: in some low-resource cases, the method underperforms alternatives that rely on minimal supervision/parallelism. Moreover, Freebase is deprecated; a modern alternative (Wikidata) should be considered and evaluated.

- Inconsistencies/clarity in comparative results: The paper claims to significantly outperform comparable direct-transfer methods on Spanish, Dutch, and German. However, some numbers in Table 2 appear to show the system trailing bitext-cluster baselines on Spanish, while beating them on Dutch/German. Similarly, the claim of outperforming Zhang et al. (2016) “on all five” low-resource languages conflicts with the table entries for Yoruba and Bengali. These discrepancies need reconciliation.

- Fairness/strength of baselines and recency: The main comparisons are to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016); many contemporary neural cross-lingual NER approaches (e.g., multilingual embeddings, projection with neural models, multilingual transformers) are not considered. While orthogonal, their omission leaves uncertainty about the competitiveness of the method vs. recent baselines.

- Computational cost and scalability not reported: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 across entire documents can be expensive and may introduce many spurious candidates. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or memory usage, nor the effect of n or top-K on speed/accuracy. This hinders assessment of deployability.

- Limited error analysis on boundary detection vs. typing: The paper argues the typing signal helps both boundary detection and classification, but there is little breakdown of where gains arise (e.g., boundary F1 vs type accuracy, entity-length strata, error categories). Given the heavy use of semantic types, it would be useful to quantify boundary vs. type contributions.

Questions to authors
- Please reconcile the discrepancies between the narrative and Table 2 for (a) Spanish vs. Täckström’s bitext clusters, and (b) Yoruba/Bengali vs. Zhang et al. (2016). Are we comparing the same settings, and are the reported numbers directly comparable?

- How costly is wikifying all n-grams in practice (per document and per dataset)? Can you report indexing size, average runtime, and memory? How do performance and speed trade off with the n value and top-K candidates?

- Freebase is no longer maintained. Have you tried Wikidata (or DBpedia) for types/categories, and if so, how do results change? If not, do you anticipate similar coverage patterns and gains?

- Can you provide a breakdown of improvements on boundary detection vs. type classification? For example, do wikifier features mainly improve recall of multi-token entities or reduce type confusions?

- How sensitive are results to the choice of top-2 candidates for features? Would using confidence-weighted features or thresholding improve robustness in small-Wikipedia settings?

- For low-resource scripts (BN/TA), you find “Wikifier only” is best in direct transfer. Could you clarify the training regime (which features are included/excluded) and whether adding character-level or script-agnostic features could help?

Overall, this paper presents a simple, well-motivated idea with broad applicability and strong empirical support in many settings. Addressing comparison clarity, modern KBs, and computational considerations would significantly strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance if these concerns are addressed.
