Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a document to English Wikipedia titles, and then derives language-independent features (e.g., Freebase types, Wikipedia categories) for NER. A standard perceptron-based NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) is augmented with these wikifier features and multilingual gazetteers built from Wikipedia. The system requires only Wikipedia dumps (no parallel data), and is evaluated for monolingual and direct-transfer settings across 9 languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts), including low-resource languages. The paper also modifies an existing cross-lingual wikifier to be suitable for dense n-gram linking and provides analyses on the impact of Wikipedia size and multi-source training.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification yields strong, truly delexicalized features for NER transfer, significantly improving zero-shot cross-lingual NER over standard delexicalized baselines and even over methods using bitext-induced clusters. This is the primary technical and empirical contribution.
2) Practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier to handle dense n-gram linking and the integration of wikifier ranker features as a “linker” confidence signal, enabling scalable feature extraction for NER.
3) Broad empirical study across 9 languages (including non-Latin scripts) with analyses of Wikipedia size/interlanguage link coverage and multi-source training, showing when and why wikifier features help.

Strengths
- Clear cross-lingual gains with minimal resources: In direct transfer, adding wikifier features boosts F1 markedly (e.g., NL: 25.2 → 47.1, DE: 41.8 → 61.0, ES: 30.5 → 48.4, TR: 50.5 → 65.3), outperforming Täckström et al.’s parallel cluster baseline. The approach uses only Wikipedia, no parallel data, which is compelling for zero-shot settings.
- Broad and careful evaluation: Results are reported for CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages spanning scripts. The paper also evaluates monolingual settings and multi-source training, and provides sensible guidance on feature selection by script (Latin vs. non-Latin).
- Insightful analysis of resource effects: The experiments that ablate Freebase queries and randomly remove interlanguage links convincingly show why gains diminish for truly low-resource Wikipedias (e.g., Yoruba). This connects performance to concrete resource characteristics.
- Orthogonality to other paradigms: The method is complementary to projection or cross-lingual clustering/embeddings and can plausibly be combined. The paper situates this well in related work and shows it can also improve monolingual models.
- Simple, implementable idea: The approach is conceptually simple (use types/categories as features) and compatible with standard NER architectures.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Freebase and English interlanguage coverage: The approach depends strongly on Freebase typing and interlanguage links to English; performance drops substantially when these are limited (as shown in Table 3), which constrains utility for truly low-resource languages. Freebase is deprecated; Wikidata would be a more sustainable alternative. The paper briefly acknowledges coverage issues but does not propose a concrete remedy.
- Comparability and label-space harmonization: For low-resource datasets, the paper removes tags (TIME, TTL) and remaps/removes MISC using heuristics. This may affect comparability across methods and could bias results. More detail is needed on how comparisons to Zhang et al. (2016) handle these mismatches and whether all systems are evaluated with identical tag sets and preprocessing.
- Baseline breadth: While comparisons to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are useful, stronger modern cross-lingual baselines (e.g., multilingual embeddings or alignment-based delexicalization) are not included. Even a simple multilingual embedding feature baseline would sharpen the empirical case.
- Scalability and efficiency not quantified: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 can be expensive and noisy. The paper does not report runtime, throughput, or coverage statistics, nor the precision/recall trade-off from the linker confidence features. Practical deployment concerns remain unaddressed.
- Reproducibility details: It is unclear whether code, trained wikifier models, or the constructed multilingual gazetteers will be released; certain implementation details (tokenization for non-Latin scripts, feature templates and hyperparameters, confidence thresholds) are only briefly described.

Questions to authors
1) Can you quantify the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (time per document, candidate counts, average link rate) and the effect of the ranker confidence on NER precision/recall?
2) How exactly were tokenization and sentence segmentation handled in non-Latin scripts? Do tokenization errors affect wikifier coverage or downstream NER?
3) For the low-resource comparisons, did Zhang et al. (2016) evaluate on the same pruned label set? Please clarify any preprocessing differences to ensure fair comparison.
4) Can you separate the contributions of Freebase types vs Wikipedia categories in the final features (beyond the “remove Freebase query” ablation), e.g., an ablation that uses only categories?
5) Do results change if you replace Freebase with Wikidata (instance-of, subclass-of)? This would address sustainability and possibly improve coverage in low-resource languages.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider replacing or augmenting Freebase with Wikidata and leveraging graph relations (P31/P279) to improve type coverage, especially for low-resource languages.
- Add a multilingual embedding baseline (or combine embeddings with wikifier features) to strengthen comparisons and show orthogonality.
- Report per-entity-type F1 and an error analysis (boundary vs type errors) to better understand where wikifier features help most.
- Provide statistical significance tests for the main improvements and release code/resources where possible.

Overall assessment: The paper presents a simple, effective, and broadly applicable idea with strong empirical gains in zero-shot transfer. The dependency on Freebase/interlanguage coverage and missing efficiency/reproducibility details are notable weaknesses but appear addressable. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors clarify comparability, add efficiency/ablation details, and discuss a path beyond Freebase (e.g., Wikidata).
