Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that inverts the traditional NER→EL pipeline: it performs aggressive cross-lingual wikification first (grounding every n-gram up to length 4 to English Wikipedia), then uses the resulting English-side Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as language-independent features for NER. The system requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text, alignments, or target-language annotation), and is trained on one or multiple source languages, then directly applied to new languages.

Main contributions as I see them:
- A conceptually novel use of cross-lingual wikification to create delexicalized, language-agnostic features for NER, enabling direct transfer to many languages without parallel data. The “wikify-all-ngrams” design is a bold departure from mention-only EL.
- Broad empirical validation: monolingual gains across 9 languages; strong direct-transfer results on CoNLL languages; and evaluations on five low-resource languages, with analyses on Wikipedia size effects and multi-source training.
- Practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier to support exhaustive n-gram linking and integration into NER, plus ablations that relate performance to Freebase availability and interlanguage link density.

Strengths
- Novel direction with clear potential impact: Reversing the NER→EL pipeline and using cross-lingual wikification as the primary delexicalized signal is an elegant, language-agnostic idea that sidesteps reliance on parallel corpora and POS taggers. This could generalize to other sequence labeling tasks and offers a compelling path for low-resource settings.
- Strong cross-lingual performance without bitext: The approach matches or substantially outperforms prior direct-transfer baselines (e.g., Täckström et al.) and a projection/expectation-based method (Zhang et al.) while assuming fewer resources. The reported gains on DE/NL/ES are especially notable (e.g., +10 F1 over base transfer in several cases), and Turkish shows large improvements despite no parallel data.
- Scalability and practicality: Requiring only Wikipedia dumps makes the method widely deployable. The multi-source training experiments show added benefit from combining annotated sources, and monolingual results indicate the features are useful even when target-language annotation exists.
- Thoughtful analyses: The paper connects performance to Wikipedia size and interlanguage link density via controlled ablations (removing Freebase queries; downsampling interlanguage links), providing actionable guidance (e.g., wikifier-only features can be best for non-Latin scripts; include all features for Latin scripts).
- Methodological clarity on feature design: The integration of top-k candidate types, category/type contexts, and ranker confidence as features is well-motivated and likely useful beyond this specific setup.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Freebase and category noise: The approach leans heavily on Freebase types and English Wikipedia categories. Freebase is deprecated; migration to Wikidata properties and item classes is non-trivial and may affect type coverage/consistency. The paper lacks experiments or a discussion on this transition and on category/type mapping noise and its impact on NER label correctness.
- Aggressive n-gram wikification may inject substantial noise: Linking all n-grams up to 4 tokens is bold but risky. There is no quantitative analysis of wikifier precision/recall under this exhaustive regime, nor ablations on n, top-k candidates, or the ranker confidence threshold. A deeper error analysis would help justify that the NER model learns to exploit signal while ignoring noise.
- Comparisons are dated/limited: While the paper situates itself well against Täckström et al. and Wikipedia-derived data generation, it omits baselines using multilingual word embeddings or simple cross-lingual character/subword models that don’t require parallel data (even basic ones). Even if strong contextual encoders are out of scope, lightweight multilingual embedding baselines would strengthen claims of superiority.
- Evaluation clarity and potential inconsistencies: There appear to be mismatches between the narrative and Table 2 for the low-resource comparisons (e.g., Yoruba/Bengali numbers vs the claim of outperforming Zhang et al. on all five languages). The handling of MISC→LOC mapping and tag removal is pragmatic but may confound cross-paper comparability; statistical significance is not reported. The construction of target-language gazetteers from Wikipedia blurs the “resource-free” story; a clearer protocol (time-stamps, leakage control) is needed.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: Feature templates, training hyperparameters, and the precise wikifier integration/code availability are not fully specified, which could impede replication.

Questions to authors
- How robust is the approach without Freebase? Can you port the pipeline to Wikidata (e.g., using instance-of/subclass-of) and report preliminary results? Any observed differences in type coverage/noise?
- What is the precision/recall of the wikifier when applied to all n-grams? Please provide ablations on n (1–4), top-k candidates (1 vs 2+), and the effect of the ranker confidence threshold/NIL prediction on NER.
- How are Wikipedia-derived gazetteers time-stamped to avoid leakage? Do they include entities that appear in the test sets, and if so, how do you justify that as “no supervision” in the target language?
- Can you clarify the apparent inconsistencies between Table 2 and the narrative for the low-resource baselines (e.g., Yoruba/Bengali vs Zhang et al.) and provide significance testing?
- For non-Latin scripts where “wikifier only” does best, have you tried simple transliteration or multilingual subword embeddings to reduce the dominance of English lexical features during training?

Overall, despite some methodological and reporting gaps, the core idea—using cross-lingual wikification as a universal, language-agnostic feature layer for NER—is fresh and impactful. I am inclined to recommend acceptance for its novelty and demonstrated potential across diverse languages.
