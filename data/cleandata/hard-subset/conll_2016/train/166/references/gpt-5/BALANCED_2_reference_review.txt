Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification as upstream, language-independent features. The model trains on one or more source languages and directly transfers to target languages without target-side annotation or parallel data. Key features come from grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia and extracting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories from the top candidates; these features are combined with a standard feature-based NER model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) plus gazetteers mined from Wikipedia. The authors evaluate on CoNLL (EN/DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), provide ablations on Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage, and explore multi-source training.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification provides effective delexicalized features for direct transfer NER across diverse languages and scripts, improving over strong feature baselines and prior direct transfer methods.
2) Empirical analysis linking performance to Wikipedia/English intersection size and Freebase availability, clarifying when and why wikifier features help, especially for non-Latin scripts.
3) Practical multi-source transfer findings and guidance on feature selection by script, plus showing monolingual gains from the same features.

Strengths
- Clear, resource-lean cross-lingual signal: The paper shows large, consistent gains from wikifier features in direct transfer (e.g., +13–18 F1 on NL/DE/TR over base+gazetteers), without parallel data or language-specific heuristics. This is a compelling, generalizable idea that flips the conventional NER→EL pipeline and uses EL to aid NER.
- Breadth of evaluation and careful ablations: Experiments span 9 languages across different scripts, with both monolingual and cross-lingual settings. The ablation in Table 3 convincingly connects performance to Freebase availability and interlanguage link density, explaining weaker results in very low-resource settings. This diagnostic clarity is valuable and actionable.
- Orthogonality and complementarity to prior transfer methods: Results compare favorably to Täckström et al. (bitext clusters) on CoNLL languages, and to an expectation-learning approach in low-resource settings, highlighting that wikifier-derived features are a strong alternative axis to parallel-projection or multilingual clusters. The method is compatible with those approaches.
- Practical modeling choices and insights: The modifications to the wikifier for dense n-gram linking and the empirical guidance on when to include lexical/gazetteer features (Latin vs. non-Latin scripts) are sensible and grounded in results. Multi-source training yields small but consistent gains and shows the method’s robustness.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to modern multilingual baselines: The paper lacks comparisons to multilingual contextual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) for zero-shot cross-lingual NER, which are now standard baselines. Without these, it is hard to judge competitiveness under current standards. At minimum, show zero-shot transfer results from a strong mPLM and, ideally, combine your wikifier features with mPLM representations.
- Dependency on Freebase and Wikipedia categories raises reproducibility and coverage concerns: Freebase is deprecated and has uneven coverage across languages (as the authors also note). Wikipedia categories are noisy and inconsistent cross-lingually. The approach should be reworked with Wikidata types (and possibly properties) and evaluated to ensure future-proofing and broader coverage.
- Evaluation design issues on labels and significance: The mapping/removal of MISC, TTL, TIME creates non-standard label sets across datasets and may bias results. Please report per-entity performance under a consistent label inventory and provide statistical significance tests. Also clarify the handling of tokenization and annotation differences in REFLEX/LORELEI.
- Limited analysis of wikification noise and runtime: Linking every n-gram up to length 4 can induce substantial noise and computational overhead. The paper would benefit from precision/recall of the wikifier under dense linking, NIL handling strategy, confidence thresholding effects, and a runtime/memory profile. It is unclear how robust the model is to false positives in dense, noisy contexts.
- Baseline/model choices are dated: The NER backbone is a perceptron with greedy decoding; while adequate historically, more competitive sequence models (CRF, BiLSTM-CRF, transformers) should be reported. Some improvements may reflect strong gazetteers rather than the novelty of wikifier features; a clearer isolation of contributions with stronger backbones would strengthen the case.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide zero-shot cross-lingual results with mBERT/XLM-R (fine-tuned on EN) on the same test sets, and/or combine your wikifier features with a transformer backbone? How do results compare and what is the additive benefit?
- Can you replace Freebase with Wikidata for types, and report the impact, especially for low-resource languages where Freebase coverage was absent?
- What is the precision/recall of the cross-lingual wikifier in the dense n-gram setting? How do different top-k and confidence thresholds affect NER performance? How many tokens receive NIL and what features fire in those cases?
- Please report statistical significance for key gains and provide per-entity-type F1. Also clarify the exact label mapping protocol across datasets and whether alternative mappings change conclusions.
- How are language mixtures weighted in multi-source training? Did you try temperature-based sampling or balanced sampling across languages?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Release code, trained wikifier models, and gazetteer generation scripts to aid reproducibility.
- Consider leveraging Wikidata relations (instance-of, subclass-of) and graph-based features; these may be more consistent across languages than categories.
- A small qualitative error analysis (boundary vs. type errors; cases where incorrect EL still helps NER) would make the findings more insightful.
- Report computational cost of dense wikification and possible pruning strategies (e.g., stopword lists per language, POS-based filtering, sliding-window heuristics).

Overall, the paper presents a simple, broadly applicable idea with thorough classical evaluations and useful analyses. To meet current standards, it needs stronger baselines with multilingual transformers, an updated knowledge source (Wikidata), and deeper analyses of wikification noise and label consistency.
