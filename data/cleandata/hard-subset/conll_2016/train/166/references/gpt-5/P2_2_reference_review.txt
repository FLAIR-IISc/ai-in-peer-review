Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a direct-transfer cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in any target language to English Wikipedia, and then uses the predicted Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features in a standard NER model (Ratinov & Roth, averaged perceptron).
- It demonstrates broad empirical gains from these wikifier-derived features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings across 9 languages (CoNLL: EN/ES/NL/DE; low-resource: TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines (Täckström et al., 2012) and a non-direct-transfer low-resource approach (Zhang et al., 2016).
- It provides analyses showing the dependence of feature quality on Wikipedia size and interlanguage links, and shows small but consistent benefits from multi-source training.

Strengths
- Clear, practical contribution with minimal resource requirements: The approach needs only multilingual Wikipedia (no bitext, alignments, or target-language annotations) and scales to many languages. This meets a key CoNLL criterion of potential impact on multilingual NLP users with limited resources.
- Strong empirical evidence and careful ablations: The paper evaluates across nine languages and two scenarios (monolingual and direct transfer). Wikifier features consistently help: e.g., monolingual average F1 improves from 72.91 (+Gazetteers) to 74.80 (+Wikifier), and direct transfer improves markedly (e.g., NL 35.06→47.14, DE 55.04→60.97, TR 64.07→65.32). Ablations removing Freebase queries and interlanguage links convincingly show why low-resource languages see smaller gains (Table 3).
- Orthogonality to other methods: The approach is complementary to projection and multilingual clustering/embedding methods. The paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and shows substantial improvements without using bitext, which strengthens the case for combining with other cross-lingual signals in future work.
- Insightful analysis of when and why it works: The discussion on script effects and feature firing is persuasive (e.g., for BN/TA, “Wikifier only” is strongest because English lexical features never fire in non-Latin scripts). The Wikipedia-size analysis links resource size to performance in a quantifiable way.
- Multi-source transfer results: Training on multiple annotated source languages yields further (albeit modest) gains and suggests easy extensibility when multiple sources exist.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Freebase and English Wikipedia categories: Freebase has been deprecated; this threatens reproducibility and long-term utility. Wikidata is the natural replacement, but the paper does not discuss portability. The strong drops when removing the Freebase query step (e.g., ES 40.04→34.69) indicate sensitivity to this component.
- Model and baselines are dated relative to current NER standards: The core NER model is a 2009 perceptron with hand-crafted features. There is no comparison to modern neural cross-lingual methods (e.g., multilingual contextual encoders, projection via multilingual representations), which weakens claims of state of the art in today’s landscape, even if the idea is orthogonal. Showing complementarity with multilingual encoders would strengthen the contribution.
- Fairness and comparability issues in label processing: The paper maps/removes labels across datasets (e.g., converting some MISC to LOC via Freebase type, dropping TTL/TIME, ignoring MISC in CoNLL for some experiments). While practical, this complicates comparability to prior work and may introduce biases favorable to the proposed features. A clearer justification and controlled comparison (e.g., with and without relabeling) are needed.
- Computational and engineering cost not analyzed: Wikifying every n-gram up to length 4 is potentially expensive and noisy. The paper mentions two modifications but provides no runtime, memory, or scalability analysis, nor precision/recall of the wikifier when used at this dense granularity. Practical deployment guidance is limited.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis: Beyond the single German example, there is little error breakdown by entity type or language. For low-resource and non-Latin scripts, more diagnosis (boundary vs. type errors, failure modes of wikifier) would help readers understand limitations and avenues for improvement.

Questions to authors
- Reproducibility: Can you release code, gazetteers, and the exact cross-lingual wikifier configurations? Given Freebase deprecation, have you tested replacing Freebase types with Wikidata properties/classes, and if so, how does performance change?
- Efficiency: What is the runtime and memory footprint of “wikify every n-gram” per sentence/document across languages? How does inference time compare to the base model without wikifier features?
- Label harmonization: How sensitive are results to the decisions to drop TTL/TIME and to remap or ignore MISC? Can you report results under a standardized tagset and provide a controlled comparison to prior work under the same evaluation?
- Feature ablations: Can you report “Base + Wikifier” (without gazetteers) and “Gazetteers + Wikifier” to isolate the incremental benefit of the wikifier over each component? Also provide per-entity-type F1 to show where wikifier features help most.
- Wikifier training and supervision: Does the wikifier require supervised training beyond Wikipedia dumps (e.g., link annotations)? How is tokenization handled for non-Latin scripts, and does segmentation quality affect wikifier accuracy?

Overall assessment
- Novelty: Moderate. Using cross-lingual EL signals as delexicalized NER features is a neat twist on EL-before-NER; while category/type features have been used monolingually, the cross-lingual grounding makes it broadly applicable.
- Soundness: Generally sound, with sensible ablations; more clarity on label harmonization and computational costs is needed.
- Significance: Good practical significance for low-resource settings without bitext, especially where Wikipedia coverage is non-trivial.
- Clarity: Clear and well organized; could benefit from more implementation details and expanded error analysis.

If the authors address reproducibility (Wikidata replacement), add stronger contemporary baselines or demonstrate complementarity with multilingual encoders, and clarify label handling and efficiency, this would be a solid and impactful contribution.
