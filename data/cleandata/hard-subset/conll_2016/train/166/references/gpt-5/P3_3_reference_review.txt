Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer cross-lingual NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to derive delexicalized, language-independent features. Concretely:
1) It introduces wikifier-derived features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories of top candidate pages) by grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target text to the English Wikipedia, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel data).
2) It provides broad empirical evidence across 9 languages (CoNLL: EN/DE/ES/NL; LRL: TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), showing gains in both monolingual and direct transfer settings, and comparisons to strong direct-transfer baselines and a recent weakly supervised LRL approach.
3) It offers analysis connecting performance to the size of the target-language Wikipedia-English intersection, ablations on Freebase/link coverage, and a small multi-source training study.

Strengths
1) Simple, generally applicable mechanism with strong empirical gains. The use of cross-lingual wikifier features as delexicalized signals is compelling: it consistently improves monolingual models and yields large boosts in direct transfer (e.g., +10 F1 on DE/NL/TR). The approach is orthogonal to projection and multilingual clustering and could be combined with them.

2) Broad and careful evaluation across scripts and resource levels. Evaluating on both CoNLL and five LRLs, including two non-Latin scripts, shows when the method succeeds and where it degrades. The “wikifier only” setting is an informative lower bound on the cross-lingual signal.

3) Useful ablations linking performance to resource characteristics. The experiments removing Freebase querying and randomly dropping interlanguage links quantify the method’s sensitivity to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage and convincingly explain why LRL gains are smaller.

4) Minimal supervision assumptions for LRLs. Outperforming Zhang et al. (2016) on all five LRLs without parallel text or native-speaker interaction is notable. The feature-level integration of wikifier outputs for both boundary detection and typing is a practical advantage over approaches that only use EL for type refinement.

5) Multi-source training results. Although gains are modest, demonstrating that training on multiple annotated languages (and that best training sources vary by target) is useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty is incremental. Using Wikipedia categories/types for NER features is well-documented (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa, Sil & Yates), and joint NER-EL or EL-informed NER has a long history. The key contribution here is enabling direct transfer via a cross-lingual wikifier and applying features to all n-grams; this is valuable but methodologically modest. A stronger positioning against prior cross-lingual EL-informed NER (and why n-gram-wide wikification is crucial) would help.

2) Reliance on Freebase and English intersection undermines the “language independent” claim for truly low-resource settings. Freebase is deprecated and has poor coverage for YO/BN/TA, which directly hurts performance. The obvious modern substitute is Wikidata QIDs and types; the paper should either adopt it or quantify how much of the gap persists with Wikidata. As shown, the approach degrades sharply with small interlanguage link sets.

3) Evaluation comparability and label manipulation. The paper removes MISC in some evaluations and maps MISC to LOC using Freebase types when transferring to LRLs, and later ignores MISC for CoNLL languages in some cross-lingual experiments. This compromises comparability to published CoNLL results (e.g., Täckström) and introduces method-specific bias (mapping informed by the same knowledge source used at test time). Standard 4-type CoNLL scores should be reported where comparisons are made, and the impact of these label adaptations should be isolated.

4) Model strength and baselines. The base model is a greedy BIO perceptron (Ratinov & Roth, 2009). By the time of this work, CRF and neural (BiLSTM-CRF) transfer methods with cross-lingual embeddings existed. The paper does not compare to strong neural direct-transfer baselines or to CRF structured decoding, which likely would reduce boundary errors and interact differently with wikifier features. This limits conclusions about the absolute efficacy of the proposed features.

5) Practicality and noise. Wikifying all n-grams up to 4 is computationally heavy and likely introduces substantial noise; the paper does not report runtime, candidate counts, NIL rates, or thresholds used to reject low-confidence links. Using the top-2 candidates’ types risks propagating ambiguity; there is limited analysis of boundary vs. typing error trade-offs or of precision/recall changes attributable to wikifier-induced false positives.

Questions to authors
- CoNLL evaluation: Did you compute standard 4-class (PER/LOC/ORG/MISC) F1 for the EN→DE/ES/NL direct-transfer comparisons to Täckström? If not, please report both versions and clarify which setting each baseline uses.
- Label adaptation: How sensitive are LRL results to the MISC→LOC mapping heuristic? Can you report numbers without relabeling to quantify the benefit/harm?
- Wikifier details: What fraction of tokens/n-grams receive at least one type? What NIL threshold or ranker confidence cutoff is used? How sensitive are results to using top-1 vs top-2 vs top-k candidates?
- Efficiency: What is the average runtime per document for n-gram wikification and feature extraction? Any pruning strategies beyond skipping stopwords?
- Knowledge base: Can your pipeline switch from Freebase to Wikidata without parallel text? If yes, please provide a small-scale result (especially for YO/BN/TA).

Additional comments
- Consider a BiLSTM-CRF baseline with multilingual embeddings (e.g., MUSE or joint subword models) to contextualize the gains.
- Provide per-entity-type scores and statistical significance tests.
- Release code, feature templates, and the Wikipedia snapshot version to facilitate reproducibility.
- A brief error analysis (e.g., overlinking common words, boundary errors from n-gram mismatches) would strengthen the paper.

Overall: Accept borderline to weak accept. The method is conceptually simple, broadly applicable, and empirically useful, but the novelty and evaluation rigor (comparability, baselines, and practical details) could be strengthened.
