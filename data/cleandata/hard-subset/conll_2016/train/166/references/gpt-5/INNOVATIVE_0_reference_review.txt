Summary and main contributions
- Conceptual shift: The paper inverts the traditional NER→EL pipeline by using cross-lingual wikification as an upstream feature generator for NER. By grounding every n-gram (n≤4) to English Wikipedia and harvesting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories, it builds language-independent features that enable direct transfer without parallel data.
- Practical cross-lingual NER framework: A simple, scalable model (perceptron-based NER with standard/gazetteer features) augmented with modified cross-lingual wikification. It only requires multilingual Wikipedia, no alignments or native-speaker involvement, and supports multi-source training.
- Broad, informative evaluation and analysis: Extensive experiments on 9 languages (CoNLL + 5 low-resource) showing consistent gains in monolingual and cross-lingual settings, outperforming strong direct-transfer baselines and a system with additional supervision. Careful ablations reveal how performance scales with Wikipedia/English intersection size and Freebase availability.

Strengths
- Clear, novel idea with significant impact potential: Treating cross-lingual wikification as a primary feature source for NER is simple yet bold. It turns a widely available resource (Wikipedia) into language-agnostic supervision and cleanly avoids the usual dependence on parallel corpora or dictionaries. This is a compelling new direction for low-resource NER and cross-lingual transfer.
- Strong empirical results under realistic constraints: In direct transfer, the method substantially improves over a well-known baseline (Täckström) and even beats a system that leverages parallel text and native-speaker interaction (Zhang et al. 2016) on multiple low-resource languages. The finding that wikifier features alone can be the best option for some non-Latin scripts is actionable guidance for practitioners.
- Careful, convincing analysis of where the gains come from: The ablation that dials down Freebase queries and randomly removes interlanguage links convincingly correlates performance with Wikipedia/English intersection density. The analysis on feature interactions (lexical vs wikifier) across scripts clarifies when to trust which features.
- Generality and extensibility: The approach works across nine diverse languages, supports multi-source training (showing consistent, if modest, gains), and also improves monolingual NER. It is orthogonal to projection- and embedding-based transfer and can plausibly be combined with them.
- Minimal assumptions and easy replication: The method requires only Wikipedia dumps and a cross-lingual wikifier, and the modifications to wikify all n-grams are clearly described. This makes it accessible to the community and to genuinely low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage and quality: Performance (especially “wikifier only”) drops sharply for languages with small Wikipedias and weak interlanguage links. The reliance on Freebase types raises sustainability/replicability concerns (Freebase deprecation); a discussion or preliminary substitution with Wikidata would strengthen the story. The model’s core signal is brittle where coverage is sparse.
- Evaluation caveats and label surgery: For low-resource languages, the authors remove certain tags (TIME/TTL) and map MISC to LOC using wikifier-derived types. While pragmatic, these interventions may confound comparability and inflate or deflate class-specific performance. A sensitivity analysis to these choices, or reporting per-type results, would help.
- Potential ambiguity and computational overhead of “wikify all n-grams”: Although the authors mitigate some failure modes (e.g., candidate generation changes), disambiguating every n-gram risks noise and cost. There is limited reporting on runtime, coverage, candidate set sizes, and precision/recall of wikifier features—factors that matter for deployment and for understanding where errors enter.
- Limited baselines beyond older direct-transfer: The comparisons are mainly to Täckström (with and without bitext-induced clusters) and Zhang et al. (2016). While the approach is orthogonal to projection and cross-lingual embeddings, including a stronger non-parallel embedding baseline (e.g., bilingual/multilingual word or subword embeddings without bitext, or type-supervised gazetteer-only systems) would contextualize the absolute gains.
- Model architecture is dated: The use of a perceptron tagger with greedy decoding and hand-crafted features is fine for isolating the contribution of wikifier features, but a modern sequence model (e.g., CRF layer, or contextual features) might better exploit the proposed signals and offer a fairer comparison to contemporary methods. This is a minor concern given the conceptual contribution.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Freebase dependency: Can the approach be transitioned to Wikidata properties/types and Wikipedia categories? Any preliminary results or expected differences?
- Wikifier statistics: What are precision/recall/coverage of the wikifier features per language (e.g., proportion of tokens/n-grams with at least one non-NIL type)? How often do top-2 candidates disagree in type?
- Runtime and scalability: What is the throughput for wikifying all n-grams and feature extraction per document? Any pruning strategies that reduce cost without harming accuracy?
- Label adjustments: How sensitive are results to the choices of removing TIME/TTL and remapping MISC to LOC? Can you report per-entity-type F1 where applicable?
- Multi-source training: Did you weight sources or perform any domain adaptation across languages? Any insight into why Spanish helps Yoruba most?

Overall, this is a bold and impactful idea with solid empirical evidence. Despite some limitations around coverage and resources (Freebase), the conceptual novelty and practical utility in low-resource settings justify acceptance.
