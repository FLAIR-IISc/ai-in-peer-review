Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features. It grounds every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a document to (English) Wikipedia entries via a cross-lingual wikifier and uses the associated Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as features in a perceptron-based NER model. No parallel data or target-language annotation is required.
- The method is evaluated in monolingual and direct-transfer settings on CoNLL languages (EN, ES, DE, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA). It shows consistent gains from wikifier features, strong improvements over direct-transfer baselines (including Täckström et al. 2012) on CoNLL, and improvements over a non-direct-transfer system (Zhang et al. 2016) on low-resource languages.
- Analyses quantify how Wikipedia size and interlanguage coverage impact performance, and show multi-source training gives small further gains.

My view of the main contributions (strongest first)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides effective, language-independent features for NER in a direct-transfer setup, yielding large gains without parallel data. The idea is simple but executed convincingly with broad empirical coverage.
2) Analysis connecting performance to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage, including ablations (removing Freebase queries, reducing interlanguage links) that explain failures in low-resource settings.
3) Practical adaptations of a cross-lingual wikifier to densely ground all n-grams and integrate ranking/linker confidence into NER.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful insight with broad applicability: Turning Wikipedia/Freebase typing into delexicalized features is a practical and general strategy for NER transfer. The method sidesteps the need for parallel corpora or target-language supervision and is complementary to many existing approaches (e.g., clustering, embeddings, projection).
- Strong empirical improvements in direct transfer on CoNLL languages: The +Wikifier setting substantially outperforms a strong delexicalized baseline and compares favorably to Täckström et al. (bitext clusters), despite using fewer resources. The gains (often >10 F1 on NL/DE/TR) are large enough to be practically meaningful.
- Thoughtful ablations and error analyses: The paper isolates the contribution of wikifier features (“wikifier only”) and quantifies the sensitivity to Freebase/interlanguage link coverage (Table 3), directly explaining the lower gains on languages with small Wikipedias. This is a rare and valuable diagnostic in cross-lingual work.
- Coverage across nine languages and both scripts: Evaluations include Latin and non-Latin scripts (BN, TA), where the paper correctly observes that lexical/gazetteer features crumble and “wikifier only” can be best. This breadth strengthens external validity.
- Practicality and extensibility: The approach integrates with a classic, efficient NER model; multi-source training further improves results slightly, suggesting easy extensibility. The features are orthogonal to projection-based or multilingual-embedding methods.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Freebase and interlanguage links raises durability/coverage concerns: The features rely heavily on Freebase types and interlanguage mappings to English. Freebase has been deprecated; coverage is poor for several target languages (YO, BN, TA). While the paper analyzes this, the approach’s long-term viability could be improved by discussing/using Wikidata or alternative KBs. This limits reproducibility and future applicability as-is.
- Limited comparison to strong contemporary cross-lingual embedding approaches: Beyond Täckström (clusters) and Zhang et al. (expectation learning), there is no comparison to methods using cross-lingual word embeddings or multilingual contextual models (even static bilingual embeddings available without parallel data via dictionaries or mapping). This makes it hard to position the gains versus other delexicalized signals.
- Evaluation protocol mismatches across datasets: For low-resource languages, TIME/TTL are removed; for CoNLL languages, MISC is ignored and some MISC are re-labeled as LOC using Freebase. These label-set adaptations are pragmatic but could bias results and complicate comparability across systems/datasets. A clearer, standardized evaluation protocol (and results under unmodified settings) would strengthen claims.
- Incomplete methodological detail for reproducibility: The feature inventory (exact category/type sets used), number of perceptron epochs, decoding specifics, handling of NIL cases, candidate thresholds, and wikifier confidence/linker integration are under-specified. The modifications to the wikifier (e.g., n-gram candidate generation, removal of mention-embedding features) need more detail and, ideally, code release.
- Efficiency and scalability are not reported: Grounding every n-gram up to length 4 is potentially expensive. There is no runtime/throughput analysis, which matters for applying this to real corpora and low-resource languages where resources may be scarce.

Questions to the authors
- Can the approach be ported from Freebase to Wikidata or another KB? What would be required, and do you expect similar gains? Do you have preliminary results?
- What fraction of tokens/n-grams receive non-NIL links per language, and how does this correlate with F1? Reporting coverage and average candidate set sizes would clarify where the signal comes from.
- How are NIL/low-confidence cases handled in features? Do you threshold the ranker scores, and how sensitive are results to k (you use top-2 candidates)?
- Can you provide more implementation details (epochs, regularization, exact feature templates, decoding, and the precise list of categories/types used)? Will code and gazetteers be released?
- For the low-resource languages, can you report results without re-mapping/removing tags (i.e., under the native label sets) to assess impact of label harmonization?

Overall evaluation
Novelty is moderate but meaningful: using cross-lingual wikification as features for NER is a natural idea, but the paper is the first (to my knowledge) to push dense n-gram grounding and show broad direct-transfer gains without parallel data, plus thoughtful analyses. The empirical evidence is strong and the paper is clear, with practical value for many languages. Addressing durability (replace Freebase), adding baselines with cross-lingual embeddings, clarifying evaluation protocol, and improving reproducibility would further strengthen the work. I lean toward accept.
