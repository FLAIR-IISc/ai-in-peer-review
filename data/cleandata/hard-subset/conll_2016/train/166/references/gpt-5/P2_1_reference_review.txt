Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in text to English Wikipedia. It then uses the resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as language-independent features in a standard NER model (averaged perceptron with BIO decoding). Two practical tweaks to the wikifier make exhaustive n-gram linking feasible.
- It evaluates both monolingual and direct-transfer (train on source, test on target) settings across nine languages (EN, NL, DE, ES, TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), including five low-resource, non-Latin-script languages. Analyses show the utility of wikifier features, their dependence on Wikipedia size/interlanguage links, and the effect of multi-source training.
- It claims to outperform prior direct-transfer baselines on CoNLL languages and a weakly supervised expectation-based method on low-resource languages, without requiring parallel corpora or target-language annotation.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and resource efficiency: Using a cross-lingual wikifier to derive language-independent features is a clear, modular idea that avoids parallel corpora and can be applied to many languages with only Wikipedia. This directly addresses the “no target annotation” scenario and is orthogonal to projection and cross-lingual clustering techniques.
- Broad and diverse evaluation: The study spans 9 languages, including non-Latin scripts and genuinely low-resource Wikipedias. This breadth increases the ecological validity of findings and shows the method’s generality. The consistent monolingual gains when adding wikifier features bolster the claim that the features carry real NER signal.
- Informative analysis: The ablations that remove Freebase support or randomly drop interlanguage links convincingly diagnose why low-resource languages benefit less: weaker intersections with English and lack of Freebase coverage. The “wikifier-only” vs “all-features” comparisons provide actionable guidance for practitioners (e.g., prefer wikifier-only when scripts differ).
- Multi-source transfer: Although modest, the gains from combining multiple source languages indicate the method’s compatibility with multi-source training, which is valuable in practical scenarios where multiple annotated datasets exist.
- Practical wikifier adjustments: The two modifications to candidate generation/ranking are motivated and mitigate severe false positives when wikifying all n-grams, increasing the approach’s robustness.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to strong modern baselines: The paper does not compare to contemporary zero-shot cross-lingual NER with multilingual contextual encoders (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R) or aligned multilingual word embeddings. Given the wide adoption and strong performance of these baselines, the significance of the proposed approach is difficult to judge without such comparisons. Even a simple CRF over mBERT representations would be an informative baseline.
- Heavy dependence on Freebase and English Wikipedia: Many features hinge on Freebase types, yet Freebase has been deprecated; sustainability and reproducibility are concerns. Results and ablations show sharp drops when Freebase or interlanguage links are missing, which is common in truly low-resource settings. A migration plan to Wikidata or an evaluation using Wikidata properties/types would strengthen the paper.
- Feature calibration issues in cross-script transfer: In several non-Latin settings, “wikifier-only” outperforms “all features,” indicating the model overweights English-specific lexical/gazetteer features and suppresses the delexicalized signal. Yoruba in particular suffers a dramatic drop when adding lexical/gazetteer features. This suggests a lack of appropriate feature normalization or domain adaptation; the paper acknowledges the phenomenon but does not propose a mitigation.
- Evaluation clarity and comparability: Some comparisons are difficult to verify. The claim that the method “significantly outperforms” Täckström et al. on all CoNLL targets appears inconsistent with the table (Spanish seems lower unless columns are mapped differently). The direct-transfer setting appears to use target-language gazetteers and Wikipedia-derived resources at test time; this is reasonable in “unsupervised transfer” but should be made explicit and matched in baselines. For low-resource languages, modifying labels (removing TIME/TTL, mapping some MISC to LOC via Freebase) risks introducing bias and complicates comparisons; clearer justification and aligned baselines are needed.
- Practicality and efficiency: Wikifying every n-gram is potentially expensive. The paper lacks runtime, memory, and throughput measurements, which are important for adoption. Details on tokenization and preprocessing for non-Latin scripts are also missing; these can materially affect results.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you add or report comparisons against zero-shot multilingual encoders (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R) with a simple NER head, and/or against CRF with aligned multilingual embeddings? This is critical to establish significance today.
- Resources at test time: In direct transfer, do you use target-language gazetteers and the target side of Wikipedia for feature extraction? If so, please (i) make this explicit, and (ii) provide ablations without target-side gazetteers to isolate the contribution of the cross-lingual wikifier.
- Freebase dependency: Since Freebase is deprecated, can you replicate the feature pipeline with Wikidata types/properties? How do results change?
- Calibration: Can you mitigate the “all-features hurts” effect (e.g., by feature-group reweighting, instance weighting, or domain-adversarial training) so that delexicalized wikifier features are not overshadowed in cross-script transfer?
- Efficiency and preprocessing: What is the end-to-end runtime for wikifying all n-grams per document, and the memory footprint of indices? How are tokenization and casing handled for non-Latin scripts?

Overall assessment
- Soundness: The method is technically sound and well-motivated; analyses support the central claims.
- Novelty: Moderate. Using Wikipedia/KB-derived types for NER is known; the cross-lingual wikifier-as-features for direct transfer and exhaustive n-gram wikification are the main novel twists.
- Significance: Potentially useful, especially where modern multilingual encoders are unavailable. However, without comparisons to current strong baselines, impact is hard to judge.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Generally clear, but some evaluation details and comparative claims need clarification; code/resource availability is not specified.

I would lean toward borderline accept if modern baselines and resource-dependency clarifications are added; otherwise, the contribution risks being incremental relative to current practice.
