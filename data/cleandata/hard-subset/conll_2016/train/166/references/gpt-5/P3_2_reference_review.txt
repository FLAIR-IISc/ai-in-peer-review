Summary and key contributions
This paper proposes a direct-transfer approach to cross-lingual NER that leverages cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features. A perceptron-based BIO tagger (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) is trained on annotated data in one or more source languages and applied to target languages with no labeled data. The core idea is to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target text to English Wikipedia titles using a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai & Roth, 2016), and to use the associated Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features. Gazetteers are also harvested per language from Wikipedia titles.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides strong, truly delexicalized features for NER transfer, yielding large gains over a base model and over prior direct-transfer methods on CoNLL languages, and competitive performance on five low-resource languages.
2) Practical cross-lingual feature design: using top-2 candidate types and ranker confidence from the wikifier, and simple modifications to enable n-gram-wide wikification at scale.
3) Empirical analysis linking Wikipedia size/interlanguage link coverage to NER transfer quality; and a study of multi-source training that modestly improves transfer.

Strengths
- Clear, orthogonal idea with broad applicability: The paper convincingly shows that entity typing signals from Wikipedia/Freebase, accessed via cross-lingual wikification, are powerful delexicalized features, complementary to gazetteers and superior to purely lexical features in transfer. The gains on Dutch, German, and Turkish are particularly notable (>10 F1 when adding wikifier features in direct transfer).
- Minimal supervision and frugal resource requirements: The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia and an English NER training set; no sentence- or word-aligned parallel data. This is appealing for scaling to many languages and for low-resource settings.
- Thorough experimental design within the chosen paradigm: The authors evaluate monolingual vs. direct transfer across 9 languages (Latin and non-Latin scripts), compare to strong prior transfer baselines (Täckström et al., 2012) and a weakly supervised cross-lingual method (Zhang et al., 2016), and include ablations that isolate the importance of Freebase queries and interlanguage link coverage. The observed correlation between Wikipedia size/intersection and F1 is both intuitive and useful.
- Practical insights and analyses: The paper quantifies when to prefer “wikifier-only” features (non-Latin, low overlap) versus adding lexical and gazetteer features (Latin scripts). The multi-source training matrix and multi-language training results, while modest, show the approach scales with more annotated source languages.
- Simplicity and reproducibility of the NER side: Using a strong, well-understood baseline model (averaged perceptron with BIO) keeps the focus on the cross-lingual features.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior use of Wikipedia for NER and EL: Prior work has used Wikipedia categories/types as NER features (Kazama & Torisawa, 2007), and joint NER-EL models (Sil & Yates, 2013) indicate that linking signals help NER. The main novelty here is leveraging cross-lingual wikification at scale and pushing it to all n-grams. While practical and useful, the conceptual advance may feel incremental without stronger methodological contributions or broader comparisons.
- Dependence on Freebase and interlanguage links hurts generality and sustainability: Freebase is deprecated and coverage is variable (and absent for several low-resource languages in the experiments). The paper shows performance can degrade substantially without Freebase (Table 3). Wikidata would be a more appropriate modern replacement; without it, reproducibility and forward-compatibility are questionable.
- Missing comparisons to contemporary neural cross-lingual baselines: Given the current landscape, zero-shot transfer with multilingual encoders (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), multilingual character/subword models, and bilingual/multilingual embedding methods are strong baselines for cross-lingual NER. The paper’s primary comparisons are to 2012 and 2016 methods; the absence of neural baselines makes it hard to judge the competitiveness of the approach today.
- Potential evaluation confounds in low-resource settings: The authors alter the label space (remove TIME/TTL, remove/retag MISC as LOC via Freebase typing) for REFLEX/LORELEI data. It is unclear whether the comparison to Zhang et al. (2016) uses exactly the same label normalization. Differences in tag mappings can non-trivially affect F1 and should be justified and standardized for fair comparison.
- Scalability/efficiency and precision concerns for “n-gram wikify everything”: Wikifying all n-grams up to 4 per sentence can be computationally heavy, and the paper does not report runtime or memory. The modified candidate generation alleviates some false positives, but there is no systematic analysis of how noise from overlinking affects precision/recall, nor of how the top-2 candidate inclusion trades off robustness vs. noise.
- Limited modeling on the NER side for non-Latin languages: The direct transfer performance for Bengali and Tamil is very low; while the paper concludes wikifier-only is best there, the model lacks character/subword features, transliteration, or script-aware features that are known to help cross-script transfer. This weakens claims about the general utility of the approach in low-resource, non-Latin settings.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to strong contemporary zero-shot cross-lingual NER baselines (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R fine-tuned on English and evaluated zero-shot), and/or to multilingual subword/character models? How does your method fare when used as features on top of such encoders?
- Resources: Do you plan to replace Freebase with Wikidata (instance-of, subclass-of, and sitelinks)? If so, do the trends in Table 3 hold with Wikidata? Can you release code, gazetteers, and feature extraction pipelines for reproducibility?
- Label normalization: For the REFLEX/LORELEI experiments, did Zhang et al. (2016) use the same label removal/retagging? Please provide a side-by-side description and, if different, a sensitivity analysis showing the impact on F1.
- Efficiency: What is the average number of n-grams wikified per sentence and the runtime per document? Are there pruning strategies (e.g., TF-IDF thresholds, stoplists beyond stopwords) that retain most of the gains at lower cost?
- Feature ablations: What is the effect of using only top-1 vs. top-2 wikifier candidates? How often does the ranker-confidence “linker” feature actually suppress noisy links, and what is the resulting precision/recall trade-off?

Overall, this is a well-executed and practically valuable application of cross-lingual wikification to NER transfer, with solid empirical evidence and insightful analyses. The paper would be substantially strengthened by replacing Freebase with Wikidata, adding modern neural baselines, clarifying label normalization in low-resource evaluation, and providing efficiency and ablation details.
