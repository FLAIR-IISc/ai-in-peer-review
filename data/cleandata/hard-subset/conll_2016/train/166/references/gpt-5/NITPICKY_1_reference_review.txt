Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a direct-transfer cross-lingual NER method that relies on cross-lingual wikification to produce language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) by grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia. A simple averaged-perceptron BIO tagger (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) is augmented with (i) base lexical/non-lexical features, (ii) Wikipedia-derived gazetteers per language, and (iii) novel wikifier features.
- Empirical results show consistent gains from wikifier features in both monolingual and cross-lingual settings on CoNLL (EN/DE/ES/NL) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA). In direct transfer (train on EN), the method outperforms Täckström et al. (2012), and on low-resource languages it outperforms Zhang et al. (2016) despite using fewer resources.
- The paper analyzes the dependence of performance on Wikipedia size, ablates Freebase vs. interlanguage links, and explores multi-source training.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and general idea: Using cross-lingual wikification to produce delexicalized features is intuitive, language-agnostic, and does not require parallel data. The approach is orthogonal to projection/bitext methods and can be combined with them.
- Strong empirical signal from wikifier features: The “Wikifier only” setting is surprisingly competitive monolingually and in direct transfer, especially for non-Latin scripts where lexical overlap is minimal (BN/TA). Adding wikifier features consistently improves over base+gazetteers in 9/9 monolingual languages and yields large direct-transfer gains (e.g., NL/DE/TR +10 F1).
- Useful analysis tying performance to resource size: The controlled removal of interlanguage links and Freebase queries (Table 3) convincingly shows the dependency of the signal on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage, explaining weaker gains on truly low-resource languages.
- Multi-source training evidence: Though gains are modest, the paper demonstrates that aggregating annotated data from multiple sources can help, aligning with prior observations on cross-lingual transfer.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and comparability issues:
  - Use of Freebase types is central, yet Freebase has been shut down and mappings are incomplete; the paper does not specify the snapshot/version, fallback to Wikidata, or how to reproduce results post-Freebase. This jeopardizes the claimed “only Wikipedia dump required.”
  - Gazetter construction and wikifier training rely on Wikipedia snapshots; no versions/hyperparameters are reported (e.g., dump dates, filter rules), nor is code/data promised.
  - Tokenization/normalization for non-Latin scripts (BN/TA) is not described; NER performance is sensitive to segmentation.
  - No statistical significance testing is reported, despite many improvements being ~1–2 F1 (e.g., monolingual +Wikifier over +Gazetteers). The multi-source gains are small and could be noise.
- Potentially unfair comparisons and evaluation inconsistencies:
  - It appears MISC is ignored in some evaluations (stated for Figure 2), but it is unclear whether the main CoNLL direct-transfer comparisons (Table 2) use the same label set as Täckström et al. (2012). If MISC is dropped for your scores but not for theirs, the comparison is not apples-to-apples.
  - Direct transfer includes target-language gazetteers (from target Wikipedia) and cross-lingual wikifier features. Prior direct transfer baselines often restrict resources to source-side training and cross-lingual mappings; the paper should clearly justify and harmonize resource assumptions in comparisons.
- Methodological opacity and ad hoc choices:
  - “Ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4)” is an aggressive design; justification for n=4, candidate cap (top-2), and use of ranker features from only the top candidate is not provided. Sensitivity analyses are missing (e.g., n from 2–6, top-k).
  - The modified candidate generation (not querying individual tokens for n>1) is ad hoc; impact on disambiguation/recall is not measured. Likewise, removing mention-embedding features from the ranker may harm disambiguation; no intrinsic wikification accuracy is reported.
  - The use of a tag-context feature over a 1000-word history with greedy decoding is unusual; details on its implementation and its cross-lingual robustness are lacking.
- Label-set and data handling concerns:
  - Converting IOB1 to BIO is fine, but details (scheme chosen, conversion errors) matter for CoNLL comparability.
  - For low-resource sets, MISC tags are mapped/removed using Freebase types; this introduces supervision from external KBs into the training labels and deviates from standard evaluation protocols. The paper should provide per-language label distributions before/after mapping and ensure test labels remain unaltered.
- Outdated core NER backbone and missing stronger baselines:
  - The base model is a 2009 perceptron CRF-like system without neural features; more recent multilingual baselines (e.g., cross-lingual word embeddings, multilingual encoders) are not compared. While the idea is orthogonal, the field would expect comparisons to stronger baselines to validate the contribution’s relevance.

Questions to authors
- Resource assumptions and fairness: In Table 2 direct-transfer experiments, which resources are used at test time per language (target gazetteers? cross-lingual wikifier trained with target-side Wikipedia)? Do Täckström baselines use comparable target-side resources? Are MISC tags included or excluded in your reported CoNLL results?
- Freebase dependency: Which Freebase snapshot/API was used? How many mentions per language receive Freebase types vs. only Wikipedia categories? Can the method work with Wikidata-only, and what is the expected delta?
- Wikifier details and intrinsic quality: What are the candidate set sizes, ranking features, and achieved P/R@k for wikification under your modified setup (n-gram saturation, no token-based candidate generation for n>1)? How many tokens/n-grams end up with NIL? How often are top-2 candidates used?
- Sensitivity and efficiency: Why n=4 and top-2? What is the runtime cost of wikifying every n-gram on CoNLL test sets and on LORELEI languages? Any pruning to avoid combinatorial explosion?
- Preprocessing: How are tokenization, casing, and sentence segmentation handled for non-Latin scripts? Are there language-specific normalizers? How do these choices affect gazetteer matching?

Additional comments (minor)
- Please report per-entity-type F1, and include standard deviations or significance tests.
- Clarify Wikipedia dump dates and provide counts for category/type coverage per language.
- Some claims (e.g., “state of the art English NER” for Ratinov & Roth, 2009) are outdated; please temper wording.
- Ensure figure/table references are self-contained; Figure 2/Table 4 are referenced but not fully shown in the excerpt; verify formatting and completeness.
