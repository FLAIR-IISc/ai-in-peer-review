Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. A model trained on annotated data in one or more source languages is applied directly to many target languages without parallel data or target-language annotation. The key idea is to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia titles via a cross-lingual wikifier and use associated Freebase types, Wikipedia categories, and ranker signals as features. The method is evaluated on CoNLL (EN, ES, DE, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), with analyses of Wikipedia size, feature ablations, and multi-source training.

Main contributions (as assessed, strongest first):
1) Introduction of cross-lingual wikifier-derived features (Freebase types, categories, confidence signals) as delexicalized features for NER, enabling direct transfer without parallel data; demonstrated to substantially improve cross-lingual NER.
2) Comprehensive empirical evaluation across high- and low-resource languages with detailed ablations (wikifier-only, base, +gazetteers, +wikifier), analysis of Wikipedia size effects, and multi-source training, establishing robustness and limitations.
3) Evidence that these wikifier features also benefit monolingual NER and that training on multiple source languages provides modest additional gains.

Strengths
- Clear, principled feature design for language-independent transfer: Using cross-lingual wikification to ground arbitrary n-grams to English Wikipedia and extract types/categories is technically sound and directly addresses delexicalization. Modifications to the wikifier for dense n-gram linking (candidate generation and context features) are justified and documented.

- Strong cross-lingual performance without parallel data or target annotation: On CoNLL target languages, adding wikifier features yields sizable gains over a direct-transfer baseline and surpasses a prior strong bitext-cluster approach. On five low-resource languages, the method outperforms an approach that assumes parallel data and native-speaker interaction, while requiring only Wikipedia.

- Thorough analyses that explain when and why the method works: The correlation between Wikipedia/interlanguage link size and performance is shown via controlled ablations (removing Freebase query step and interlanguage links). The finding that wikifier-only is preferred for non-Latin scripts (when lexical features don’t transfer) is practical and evidenced by results.

- Broad applicability and minimal resource assumptions: The approach only requires multilingual Wikipedia and a cross-lingual wikifier, avoiding sentence- or word-aligned corpora or language-specific heuristics. It scales to many languages and supports multi-source training.

- Monolingual benefits and multi-source transfer: Wikifier features improve monolingual NER across nine languages, indicating complementary signals to standard lexical/gazetteer features. Training with additional source languages generally helps or at least does not harm, suggesting reusable annotation value.

Weaknesses
- Heavy dependence on external KB coverage and Freebase: The method’s core features rely on Freebase types and Wikipedia categories. The paper shows significant performance drops when Freebase queries fail or interlanguage links are removed, especially for low-resource languages. This dependence raises concerns about robustness in languages or domains with sparse KB coverage and about future-proofing given Freebase’s deprecation.

- Limited comparison to stronger contemporary baselines: The main cross-lingual baselines are delexicalized feature systems and bitext clusters. There is no comparison to multilingual embedding-based or neural transfer methods (e.g., multilingual word/character embeddings, cross-lingual contextual models), which could offer alternative delexicalized signals. This makes it harder to assess novelty and competitiveness relative to current techniques.

- Non-standard label adjustments for low-resource evaluation: For REFLEX/LORELEI data, MISC tags are removed or relabeled via Freebase type grounding, and TIME/TTL tags are dropped. These modifications may bias evaluation in favor of the proposed method (which itself uses Freebase types) and reduce comparability to existing NER results on these corpora. More careful justification and control experiments are needed.

- Potential noise and computational cost of dense n-gram wikification: Grounding every n-gram up to length 4 risks substantial ambiguity and false positives (acknowledged and partially mitigated by candidate generation changes). The paper does not provide runtime/efficiency metrics or error rates for wikification at this granularity, leaving questions about scalability in large-scale or real-time settings.

- Use of target-language gazetteers in “direct transfer”: Some improvements stem from gazetteers extracted from target-language Wikipedia titles. While consistent with the paper’s resource assumptions (Wikipedia-only), this is not a purely source-trained model and may limit the generality of claims about transfer without target-language resources.

Questions to Authors
- Resource assumptions: In the direct transfer setting, do results that include “+Gazetteers” always use target-language gazetteers at test time? If so, can you report transfer performance without target-language gazetteers to isolate the wikifier’s contribution?

- Robustness and efficiency: What is the runtime of the wikification step when applied to all n-grams (n ≤ 4) per document across languages? Can you provide statistics on the proportion of n-grams linked, NIL rates, and average candidate counts?

- Freebase reliance: Given Freebase’s deprecation, can the method seamlessly replace Freebase with Wikidata or other KBs? Do you have preliminary results quantifying performance differences?

- Label mapping for low-resource languages: Can you provide an ablation that evaluates without relabeling/removing tags (or with a standardized mapping) to ensure comparability and to quantify the effect of these preprocessing choices?

- Error analysis: Which entity types benefit most from wikifier features in cross-lingual transfer (PER/LOC/ORG)? Are boundary detection or type assignment the primary gains?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound in its central idea and feature engineering, and provides substantial empirical evidence. The primary limitations concern reliance on external KB coverage, comparators to stronger modern baselines, and some evaluation choices.
