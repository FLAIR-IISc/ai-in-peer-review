Summary
This paper tackles cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) in a direct-transfer setting by leveraging cross-lingual wikification. The core idea is to link every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the target-language text to English Wikipedia titles using a cross-lingual wikifier and use the resulting Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features in a standard NER model. The authors modify an existing wikifier to support dense n-gram linking and integrate the wikifier’s ranker features. They evaluate on CoNLL languages (EN, DE, ES, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), showing that wikifier-derived features consistently improve monolingual models and substantially boost direct transfer, with analyses on the effect of Wikipedia size, interlanguage link coverage, and multi-source training.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification provides strong, truly delexicalized features (types/categories) that substantially improve direct-transfer NER without parallel data or language-specific heuristics, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines.
2) A practical integration of dense cross-lingual wikification into NER (modifying candidate generation and ranking for all n-grams) and a thorough empirical study across nine languages, including low-resource and non-Latin scripts, with ablations quantifying dependence on Wikipedia size/interlanguage links.
3) Evidence that the same wikifier features also help monolingual NER and that multi-source training yields further gains, indicating broad utility and composability.

Strengths
- Clear, practical, and general approach: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, avoids parallel corpora and language-specific rules, and integrates with a standard NER architecture. This makes it broadly applicable and easy to combine with other techniques.
- Strong empirical gains in direct transfer: Adding wikifier features yields large improvements across multiple target languages, notably beating the Täckström direct-transfer baselines and performing better than Zhang et al.’s expectation learning on low-resource languages without needing parallel text or native speaker interaction. The effect sizes (e.g., +10 F1 on DE/NL/TR) are meaningful.
- Solid ablations and analyses: The paper quantifies how performance degrades with reduced interlanguage links and removal of Freebase queries, convincingly tying performance to Wikipedia coverage. It also explores script effects, a “wikifier-only” setting, and multi-source training benefits, yielding actionable guidance (e.g., favor delexicalized features for non-Latin scripts).
- Simplicity and reproducibility: The feature sets and data sources are clearly described; the use of a known base model and an existing wikifier (with modifications) facilitates replication. Gazetteer construction from Wikipedia titles is straightforward and well motivated.
- Broad scope of evaluation: Experiments cover both monolingual and cross-lingual regimes, multiple language families/scripts, and multiple training-source configurations.

Weaknesses
- Dependency on Freebase and Wikipedia size: The approach leans heavily on Freebase types and English-centered interlanguage links. Freebase is deprecated, and some target languages (YO, BN, TA) have minimal coverage, leading to low absolute F1 and modest gains. The paper would benefit from replacing Freebase with Wikidata/DBpedia and reporting coverage statistics and results with those.
- Limited comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines: While comparisons to Täckström (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016) are relevant, the paper lacks baselines using multilingual embeddings or modern multilingual transformers (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R) in direct-transfer or zero-shot settings. Without these, it’s hard to assess the competitiveness of the approach today.
- Scalability and precision concerns with “wikify all n-grams”: Linking every n-gram could be computationally expensive and risks introducing noise from spurious links. The paper does not report throughput, runtime, or the fraction of tokens/phrases successfully linked (coverage), nor a detailed error analysis of boundary vs. typing errors induced by mislinking.
- Label mapping for low-resource sets: The process of modifying MISC to LOC using Freebase types and removing TIME/TTL introduces potential biases. It is unclear whether this remapping is consistently applied across baselines, and whether it inadvertently advantages wikifier-based features that drive the relabeling.
- Under-specification of implementation details: Gazetteer construction specifics (redirect handling, disambiguation page filtering, normalization), stopword handling across languages, and hyperparameters for n-gram generation and ranking are not fully documented, which may affect reproducibility and performance.

Questions to authors
- Knowledge base choice: Freebase is deprecated. Can your pipeline use Wikidata QIDs and their types/properties in place of Freebase? If so, what changes are needed, and how do results compare?
- Coverage and efficiency: What proportion of tokens/n-grams receive non-NIL links and usable types/categories per language? What is the runtime throughput for “wikify all n-grams” on typical documents, and how does it scale with Wikipedia size?
- Error analysis: How do wikifier features affect boundary detection vs. type assignment? Which entity types benefit most? Can you quantify the impact of disambiguation errors on NER precision/recall?
- Label remapping: When converting MISC to LOC using Freebase types and removing TIME/TTL, is this applied to all systems equally? Could this create circularity that favors wikifier-based features? Please clarify and, if possible, report results without such remapping.
- Multi-source training: How did you balance training data across languages (sampling/weights)? Did you observe domain or annotation guideline mismatches, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Stopword handling and n-gram pruning: How are stopwords defined per language? Beyond disabling token-based candidate generation for n>1, do you apply additional pruning to reduce spurious links?

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace Freebase with Wikidata/DBpedia and report updated results and coverage statistics; release code/resources for reproducibility.
- Add comparisons to strong zero-shot baselines (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuned on EN and directly applied to target languages), and consider combining your wikifier features with such models.
- Provide runtime/coverage metrics and a targeted error analysis to assess the trade-offs of dense n-gram wikification.
- Clarify and control the label remapping procedure to avoid potential evaluation biases, and report sensitivity analyses.
- Explore robustness techniques for truly low-coverage Wikipedias (e.g., back-off to multilingual embeddings, self-training, or joint NER–EL models that better handle ambiguity).

Overall assessment
The paper offers a practical and broadly applicable way to inject cross-lingual knowledge into NER and presents convincing gains in direct transfer. However, its reliance on Freebase and lack of comparisons to stronger modern baselines limit its impact in a contemporary setting. With an updated knowledge base, stronger baselines, and additional analysis, this work could be a solid contribution.
