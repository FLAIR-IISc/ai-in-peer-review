Summary and claimed contributions
- Introduces a cross-lingual NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) and trains a delexicalized NER model in a source language to directly transfer to many target languages.
- Proposes to wikify every n-gram (n ≤ 4), use top-2 candidates’ types/categories plus ranker confidence as features, and shows gains in both monolingual and direct-transfer settings; also explores multi-source training.
- Provides analyses linking gains to Wikipedia size and ablations that degrade the interlanguage link graph to simulate low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with broad applicability: Cross-lingual wikification provides delexicalized, language-independent features, enabling direct transfer without parallel data. This is orthogonal to projection/bitext methods and can be combined.
- Empirical gains across settings: The +Wikifier features consistently improve over base and gazetteer features in monolingual experiments on 9 languages, and substantially improve direct transfer, especially for Latin-script targets. The ablation that reduces interlanguage links convincingly ties performance to Wikipedia coverage.
- Sensible engineering choices: Using top-k candidates and including ranker confidence as a “linker” feature mitigates disambiguation errors; constructing gazetteers per language from Wikipedia titles is practical and scalable.
- Broader language coverage: Experiments span CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages with diverse scripts, offering a more comprehensive picture than many prior works. The method fares better than a projection-based expectation model on all five LR languages despite assuming fewer resources.
- Multi-source transfer: Although gains are modest, the paper shows that adding training data from multiple source languages usually helps and rarely hurts.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: Using Wikipedia categories/Freebase types as features for NER (Kazama & Torisawa, 2007) and coupling EL with NER (Sil & Yates, 2013) are established. The primary novelty is cross-lingual grounding and dense “wikify-all n-grams.” The paper should more clearly delineate what is genuinely new versus re-applied, and quantify the incremental gain attributable to cross-lingual grounding as opposed to monolingual wikifier features.
- Reproducibility and underspecified methodology: Key details are missing: n-gram candidate pruning, stopword lists per language, NIL handling, exact feature templates, number of epochs/hyperparameters for the perceptron, feature pruning/hashing, thresholds for ranker confidence, and efficiency considerations. The two modifications to Tsai & Roth (2016) are described qualitatively but without quantitative impact on linking accuracy. No run-time or memory costs for “wikify every n-gram (n≤4)” are reported; feature explosion and overlapping n-gram handling are not addressed.
- Evaluation inconsistencies and label mapping: The paper removes TIME/TTL (in LR sets), maps some CoNLL MISC to LOC using Freebase and removes the rest, and later “ignores all MISC” in some settings (train/test matrix) but not others (Table 2). It is unclear whether all comparisons use the same label sets, and whether baselines (e.g., Zhang et al., 2016; Täckström et al., 2012) were evaluated under identical label-removal policies. This undermines comparability.
- Heavy dependence on Freebase (deprecated) and Wikipedia coverage: The approach relies on Freebase types (often unavailable for LR languages) and English interlanguage links. The paper shows sharp drops without Freebase or with reduced interlanguage links. This casts doubt on robustness “to all languages in Wikipedia” and threatens long-term reproducibility since Freebase has been sunset; no results with Wikidata are provided.
- Presentation/formatting issues that impede clarity: Table 2’s “Direct Transfer” section lacks explicit column headers; there are nine numbers although direct transfer should exclude English; the order must be inferred and is ambiguous. Footnotes are misnumbered (“1” for setting n=4). Figure 1’s content is cluttered (e.g., stray Freebase properties), with unclear mapping from example tokens to types. The text alternates between “FreeBase” and “Freebase.” “Table 4” is referenced but not shown; “Figure 2” is referenced but not reproduced here with legible values.

Questions to authors
- Novelty: Beyond applying existing wikifier-derived features cross-lingually, what component is technically new? Can you isolate the gain from cross-lingual grounding vs. monolingual wikifier features via a controlled experiment?
- Wikifier details: What is the precision/recall of your modified wikifier when run in “wikify all n-grams” mode by language? How do you prune candidate spans (e.g., stoplists per language, POS filters, punctuation rules)? Why top-2 candidates; did you ablate k?
- Feature control and efficiency: How many features per token are induced on average with n ≤ 4? What are training and inference times by language? Do you de-duplicate overlapping n-gram features? Any feature selection or L1 pruning?
- Label mapping: Please precisely describe the label-space normalization across all experiments (which tags are removed, remapped, or ignored) and ensure all baselines are evaluated under the same mapping. How sensitive are your results to the MISC→LOC mapping heuristic?
- Resources and sustainability: Given Freebase is deprecated, can you replicate with Wikidata properties/classes? Do results change materially? Will you release code, gazetteers, and the exact feature extraction pipeline?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is appealing and the empirical signal is promising, but (i) novelty over prior NER+Wikipedia/EL work is limited, (ii) the approach’s reliance on Freebase/interlanguage links raises robustness and reproducibility concerns, and (iii) numerous methodological and presentation issues (evaluation inconsistencies, underspecified wikifier/feature pipeline, incomplete/ambiguous tables, no runtime/sig tests) prevent a confident assessment. With clarified evaluation, stronger ablations, and a Freebase-independent implementation (e.g., Wikidata), this work could be substantially strengthened.
