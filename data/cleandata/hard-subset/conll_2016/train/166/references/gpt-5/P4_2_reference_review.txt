Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a document to English Wikipedia titles, and uses the resulting Freebase types, Wikipedia categories, and wikifier ranker features as language-independent signals for NER. The base NER model is an averaged perceptron with BIO decoding (Ratinov and Roth, 2009), augmented with multilingual Wikipedia-derived gazetteers and the proposed wikifier features. The method is trained on one or more source languages and directly transferred to target languages without target-side annotation or parallel data. Experiments cover CoNLL languages (EN, ES, NL, DE) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), with ablations quantifying dependence on Freebase and interlanguage links, and an analysis of multi-source training.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories of linked candidates) are effective delexicalized signals for direct-transfer NER, yielding substantial gains over strong direct-transfer baselines across nine languages and multiple scripts, without requiring parallel data or target supervision.

2) An engineering integration of cross-lingual wikification into NER, including modifications to wikify all n-grams and careful ablations showing how feature quality scales with Wikipedia size and Freebase coverage.

3) Evidence that the same features improve monolingual NER and that multi-source training offers further, albeit modest, gains.

Strengths
- Strong cross-lingual performance without parallel data: Adding wikifier features to a direct-transfer model trained on English improves F1 substantially on CoNLL languages (e.g., ES 48.41 vs. 30.50 with base features; DE 60.97 vs. 41.81; NL 47.14 vs. 25.24) and on TR (+1.25 over base+gazetteers). The method outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) with bitext-induced clusters on NL/DE and surpasses Zhang et al. (2016) on all five low-resource languages while using fewer resources (no parallel data, no native annotator interaction).

- Careful, informative ablations: The “Wikifier only” condition shows the intrinsic cross-lingual signal, especially in non-Latin scripts where lexical features fail (BN/TN). Ablations that remove Freebase querying and interlanguage links (Table 3) quantify why low-resource languages with small Wikipedias underperform and directly tie performance to knowledge base coverage. This causal analysis strengthens technical soundness.

- Resource efficiency and scalability: The approach requires only a multilingual Wikipedia dump (no sentence/word alignments, no target labels) and a cross-lingual wikifier, making it applicable to many languages. The same features also improve monolingual NER (e.g., average monolingual F1 gains from +Gazetteers to +Wikifier: 72.91 to 74.80).

- Breadth of evaluation and practical guidance: Experiments span nine languages and both Latin and non-Latin scripts, with consistent gains, multi-source transfer analysis (Figure 2, Table 4), and actionable guidance (e.g., for non-Latin scripts, de-emphasize lexical features and rely more on wikifier-derived signals).

Weaknesses
- Dependency on Freebase and English categories limits robustness and reproducibility: Performance degrades markedly when Freebase queries are removed, and low-resource languages suffer due to poor Freebase coverage. Freebase is deprecated; the paper does not validate alternatives (e.g., Wikidata) or quantify the feasibility of replacing Freebase. This undermines long-term applicability.

- Potential noise and efficiency concerns from wikifying all n-grams: Linking every n-gram up to length 4 risks substantial over-generation and spurious matches despite the candidate-generation tweaks (e.g., “in Germany”). The paper lacks quantitative measures of wikifier precision/coverage under this aggressive regime and omits runtime/complexity analysis, leaving scalability and error propagation unaddressed.

- Comparisons and baselines could be stronger or better aligned: The main SOTA comparison (Täckström with bitext clusters) uses extra resources; more recent cross-lingual baselines based on multilingual embeddings or neural sequence models are absent. The NER backbone is an older perceptron without CRF/neural decoding; it is unclear whether gains persist when compared against stronger modern baselines.

- Label harmonization and evaluation choices may affect comparability: For low-resource languages, MISC is removed or converted to LOC using Freebase types; CoNLL MISC is ignored in some analyses. These choices could bias results and comparability across systems/datasets. The paper does not report per-type performance or significance testing to assess robustness of gains.

- Limited analysis of errors and NIL handling: The method uses top-2 wiki candidates’ types as features regardless of linker confidence; while a confidence feature is included, there is no analysis of thresholding, NIL rejection, or the trade-off between disambiguation accuracy and NER performance.

Questions to authors
- Coverage/accuracy: What fraction of tokens and n-grams receive at least one non-NIL link per language? What is the top-1 linking accuracy on a standard EL set for the wikifier in the “all n-grams” regime?

- Efficiency: What are the runtime and memory costs of wikifying all n-grams on CoNLL-scale documents? How does decoding time compare across feature settings?

- Freebase dependency: Have you tried replacing Freebase with Wikidata properties/types? If so, how do results change; if not, what do you expect given Table 3?

- NIL and confidence: Do you apply any threshold to suppress low-confidence links, or weight features by linker confidence? Would stricter NILing improve precision in low-resource settings?

- Gazetteers and leakage: How are Wikipedia-derived gazetteers time-sliced to avoid test leakage, especially for CoNLL? Are redirects/disambiguation pages handled specially?

- Multi-source training: How are training instances balanced across languages (sampling/weights)? Do you observe negative transfer for any language pairs?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound in its empirical methodology and ablations, and offers a moderately novel, practical cross-lingual signal that yields consistent gains. The main limitations are reliance on Freebase/English Wikipedia and incomplete analysis of efficiency and linking noise.
