Summary and main contributions
- Conceptual shift: The paper inverts the classic NER→EL pipeline by using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. Every n-gram is grounded (to English Wikipedia), yielding delexicalized signals that transfer across languages without bitext.
- Empirical validation across 9 languages: The approach improves both monolingual and direct-transfer NER, with sizeable gains over prior direct-transfer baselines on CoNLL (ES/NL/DE) and on five low-resource languages, despite requiring only multilingual Wikipedia.
- Analysis and practical guidance: The paper quantifies the dependence of performance on Wikipedia size/interlanguage links, shows the impact of removing Freebase support, and demonstrates modest benefits from multi-source training.

Strengths
- Novel and bold idea: Treating EL as a feature generator for NER (and doing it cross-lingually by grounding all n-grams) is a clear conceptual contribution. It provides a genuinely delexicalized signal that can generalize to unseen languages, and it’s orthogonal to parallel-projection and multilingual representation methods.
- Minimal resource assumptions, broad applicability: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text, gold POS, or target-language supervision). The modified wikifier to handle all n-grams is pragmatic and makes the idea usable across all Wikipedia languages.
- Strong empirical evidence: 
  - Consistent gains from wikifier features in both monolingual and direct transfer settings.
  - Outperforms Täckström et al.’s direct-transfer baselines on ES/NL/DE and exceeds Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages while using fewer resources and no native speaker interaction.
  - Ablations that remove Freebase queries and interlanguage links convincingly explain why low-resource Wikipedias yield weaker signals.
- Useful insights and recommendations: Clear takeaways on when to include lexical/gazetteer features (Latin scripts) vs relying mainly on wikifier features (non-Latin scripts), and evidence that multi-source training provides incremental gains.
- Potentially general: The idea of using cross-lingual EL-derived types/categories as portable features could extend beyond NER to other sequence labeling tasks.

Weaknesses
- English- and resource-centrism of the signal: The approach relies heavily on English Wikipedia and Freebase types. For languages with sparse interlanguage links or missing Freebase coverage (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), performance degrades substantially. The paper acknowledges this via ablations but does not propose mitigation (e.g., leveraging Wikidata, using description text, relation graph features, or back-off strategies).
- Noisy “wikify all n-grams” regime: Linking every n-gram introduces large amounts of candidate noise. The paper removes some ranking features but does not detail NIL/thresholding behavior, precision of top-1/2 linking under dense linking, or how confidence is calibrated to prevent spurious type features from harming boundary detection. A deeper analysis of error propagation from EL to NER is missing.
- Evaluation protocol deviations: The modifications to label sets (removing MISC, mapping MISC→LOC via Freebase, dropping TTL/TIME) complicate comparability. The paper should report results under both the standard CoNLL evaluation and the modified setup, plus per-entity-type scores to assess whether gains are uniform or concentrated.
- Limited baseline breadth and contemporaneity: While comparisons to Täckström (clusters) and Zhang (expectation learning) are fair, the study lacks comparisons to stronger multilingual sequence models or cross-lingual representation baselines (e.g., multilingual BiLSTM-CRF, cross-lingual embeddings, or multilingual transformers if contemporary), or at least to self-training/cluster-augmented direct transfer. Even within the feature-based paradigm, more ablations (e.g., using only EL ranker confidence, or only categories vs only Freebase types) would clarify which signals matter most.
- Practicality and efficiency not discussed: The cost of wikifying all n-grams (n≤4) can be substantial. The paper does not report throughput, candidate set sizes, or memory overhead, nor strategies to prune n-grams or cache results. This matters for scaling to large corpora.

Questions to authors
- NIL/confidence handling: How do you decide when to accept/reject the top candidate? Is there a threshold or learned linker? What is the precision/recall of top-1 linking in the “all n-grams” regime, and how does it correlate with NER errors?
- Signal composition: How are Wikipedia categories mapped to NER labels? Do you filter categories or use type hierarchies to reduce noise? Relative contributions of Freebase vs categories?
- Top-k sensitivity: Why use top-2 candidates? Did you try k=1 or k=3/5, and what is the trade-off between recall and noise?
- Efficiency: What is the average number of n-grams per sentence/doc, average number of candidates per n-gram, and end-to-end runtime compared to a standard NER pipeline?
- Low-resource mitigation: Have you tried replacing Freebase with Wikidata, or using English page descriptions/infobox properties, graph neighbors, or cross-lingual embeddings to compensate for small local Wikipedias?
- Evaluation variants: Can you report CoNLL-standard scores without label-set modifications, and per-entity-type results to assess where wikifier features help most?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the modified wikifier, feature extraction code, and gazetteers?

Overall recommendation
- Leaning accept. The paper introduces a clear and impactful idea—using cross-lingual wikification as delexicalized features for NER—and backs it with broad empirical evidence and useful analysis. Some evaluation and engineering gaps exist, especially for low-resource settings and dense linking noise, but they are addressable and do not diminish the conceptual contribution.
