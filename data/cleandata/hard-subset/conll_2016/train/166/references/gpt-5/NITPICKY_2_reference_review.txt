Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes a direct-transfer cross-lingual NER approach that relies on cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The model trains on annotated data in one/more source languages and is applied to target languages without target-side annotations.
- Core idea: ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language document to English Wikipedia using a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai & Roth, 2016), then use the top candidates’ Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features, alongside standard NER features and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers.
- Experiments span CoNLL (EN, DE, ES, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA). The method improves monolingual models modestly and provides sizable gains in direct transfer, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines and a weakly supervised expectation-learning method on low-resource languages.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, general featureization of cross-lingual wikification for NER (grounding all n-grams and leveraging candidate types/categories) that yields strong delexicalized signals for direct transfer.
2) An extensive empirical analysis across 9 languages showing when wikifier features help (correlated with Wikipedia size and Interlanguage link coverage), including ablations and multi-source training experiments.
3) A simple, resource-lean pipeline (no parallel bitext or target-side annotation) that compares favorably to several older direct-transfer baselines and a weakly supervised method.

Strengths
- Clear empirical utility of wikifier features: Across both monolingual and direct-transfer settings, adding wikifier features consistently improves F1 over base+gazetteers, often by large margins in transfer (e.g., +10 points on NL/DE/TR). The “wikifier only” results provide informative bounds and highlight delexicalized transfer.
- Broad multilingual coverage and analysis: Results on 9 languages (including low-resource and non-Latin scripts) are rare in this line of work. The ablations linking performance drops to reduced interlanguage links and to removal of Freebase evidence are convincing diagnostics of the feature source.
- Orthogonality to other transfer signals: The approach is compatible with multi-source training and (in principle) with clustering or projection. The multi-source matrix and combined training experiments are useful and show small but generally positive gains.
- Methodological simplicity and practicality: Using only Wikipedia dumps and interlanguage links (no word/sentence alignments, no native speaker) is an attractive deployment story, particularly for rapidly adding languages with small or no labeled NER data.
- Insightful error/source-of-signal analysis: The study of why Yoruba (and other low-resource) fares worse via the quality/coverage of Wikipedia/Freebase is helpful and honest.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to modern baselines: The paper does not compare against contemporary multilingual neural models (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R direct transfer, or with lightweight lexicon features). Given their dominance for cross-lingual NER, the absence makes it hard to assess current relevance. Even if the goal is feature-centric direct transfer, a back-off comparison to a simple mBERT zero-shot baseline is necessary.
- Heavy dependence on Freebase and English-centric resources: Freebase is deprecated, coverage is uneven, and the approach’s reliance on Freebase types is central. The paper shows performance craters when Freebase/Interlanguage links are reduced; this questions robustness and long-term maintainability (e.g., to Wikidata-only setups). A drop-in replacement and empirical comparison (e.g., Wikidata) are missing.
- Evaluation inconsistencies and label manipulation: The paper removes/changes tags (e.g., dropping MISC or remapping to LOC based on Freebase typing). This risks unfair or incomparable evaluation across datasets and to cited baselines. For CoNLL, ignoring MISC in some experiments (Figure 2) but not others can distort conclusions. A clear, consistent protocol with parallel numbers under standard CoNLL scoring is needed.
- Insufficient methodological detail and reproducibility gaps: Key training details are underspecified (perceptron epochs, learning rate/decay, feature templates, dev sets/early stopping, tokenization for each language, stopword lists per language, n-gram generation and overlap handling, runtime/memory). Wikifier modifications are only sketched (e.g., top-2 candidates—why 2?; confidence thresholds?). No code/data release is mentioned.
- Potential leakage/heuristic risks and lack of linking evaluation: Grounding every n-gram is aggressive; although one mitigation (no token-wise candidate generation for n>1) is used, the paper does not quantify linking precision/recall, feature coverage, or boundary vs. type errors. Without an EL evaluation or a per-entity-type NER analysis, it’s hard to assess when the features help vs. hurt.

Questions to authors
- Modern baselines: How does your method compare to mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot transfer on the same splits? Can your wikifier features be combined with a transformer encoder (e.g., as additional token features)? Please provide numbers.
- Resource substitution: Can Freebase be replaced by Wikidata in your pipeline? What is the empirical impact on both high- and low-resource languages?
- Evaluation protocol: Please report CoNLL-style scores without altering tag sets (i.e., with MISC) alongside your modified scoring, and clarify which numbers are used in each table/figure. For low-resource sets, provide an appendix mapping of all tag transformations and justify comparability with prior work.
- Reproducibility: Specify training hyperparameters, dev set usage, tokenization/normalization per language, stopword lists, and exact Wikipedia dump versions. Will you release code, gazetteers, and the modified wikifier?
- Design choices: Why choose top-2 candidates and n up to 4? Did you explore top-k sensitivities, n-gram window sizes, or confidence thresholds? How are overlapping n-grams’ features combined, and what is the runtime/memory overhead?

Additional comments (minor)
- Formatting/typos: Inconsistent “FreeBase/Freebase,” malformed footnotes (e.g., “We set n to 4…”), stray dataset IDs inline (e.g., “LDC2014E115 3LDC2015E13”), and table formatting issues hinder readability. Please standardize.
- POS features: You claim POS didn’t help; please report ablations quantitatively.
- Fairness to prior baselines: Clarify whether Täckström et al.’s numbers include MISC and whether your evaluation matches their setup.

Overall, the idea is practical and the multilingual analysis is useful. However, the lack of comparison to current neural transfer baselines, dependence on deprecated resources, and evaluation inconsistencies are substantial concerns that need to be addressed.
