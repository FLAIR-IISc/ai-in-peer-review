Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a direct-transfer NER approach that derives language-independent features by wikifying every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in the input and using the associated English Wikipedia categories and Freebase types as features. The NER backbone is an averaged perceptron (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) with additional Wikipedia-derived gazetteers.
- It presents empirical results on CoNLL languages (EN, DE, ES, NL) and five low-resource languages (TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), showing gains over a direct-transfer baseline and selected prior work, and analyzes the dependence of performance on Wikipedia size and interlanguage links.
- It shows small benefits from multi-source training (training on multiple annotated languages) and provides ablations on components of the wikifier (e.g., Freebase query, interlanguage link removal).

Strengths
- Clear, established backbone and well-defined feature additions. Building on a strong, well-understood NER model and adding delexicalized, cross-lingual features provides a transparent methodology. The feature-engineering choices (gazetteers, wikifier features) are straightforward to interpret and ablate.
- Solid improvements in direct transfer on CoNLL languages. The +Wikifier setting substantially improves over Base/+Gazetteers on NL/DE/ES (e.g., +10 F1 on NL, +5–10 on DE/ES), and compares favorably to Täckström et al.’s cross-lingual clustering, suggesting that the proposed features carry meaningful cross-lingual signal without parallel corpora.
- Useful analysis on the role of Wikipedia resources. The experiments that (i) remove Freebase queries and (ii) downsample interlanguage links to English quantify the degradation as resources shrink, helping to explain the weaker results on truly low-resource languages. This is a welcome, data-driven diagnostic.
- Evidence that the features can help monolingual models. The monolingual gains (+Wikifier over Base+Gaz) across all nine languages indicate the features are not only a transfer “crutch,” but also add value when sufficient target-language annotations exist.
- Multi-source training exploration. Although gains are modest, the finding that adding annotated sources rarely hurts and sometimes helps is practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient comparison to contemporary strong baselines. The paper compares mainly to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016). It lacks comparisons to widely used cross-lingual NER baselines based on multilingual word embeddings and, crucially, multilingual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with cross-lingual embeddings, mBERT/XLM-R zero-shot transfer). Without these, it is hard to assess competitiveness against established modern methods.
- Heavy dependence on Freebase and Wikipedia limits applicability and reproducibility. Freebase is discontinued, and the approach leans on Freebase types as a primary signal (Table 3 shows large drops without it). Reproducibility with current resources (e.g., Wikidata) is not addressed, nor are runtime/engineering details for wikifying all n-grams at scale. The claim that the method “works on all languages in Wikipedia” is weakened by sharp performance drops as Wikipedia shrinks.
- Ad hoc label manipulation and potential evaluation bias. When training on CoNLL and testing on low-resource languages, the paper remaps MISC to LOC based on Freebase typing and drops other MISC/TTL/TIME labels. This conflates ground-truth label space with model-driven external knowledge and may advantage methods that use Freebase/Wikipedia. A cleaner setup would harmonize label sets without relying on the same resources used as features, and report per-type performance.
- Limited methodological detail on wikifier feature integration and noise control. The paper states that “ranker features … could serve as a linker” and uses top-2 candidates per n-gram, but does not specify thresholds, feature weighting, or how overlapping n-grams interact during BIO decoding. Given the very aggressive overgeneration (every n-gram up to length 4), a clearer description of how noise is mitigated is essential.
- Scope of analysis is still narrow. Ablations cover Freebase queries and interlanguage links, but there is no analysis by entity type, error typology (boundary vs. type), effect of n-gram length, or per-language/tokenization specifics for non-Latin scripts. No statistical significance testing is reported. The multi-source gains, while suggestive, are small and not quantified for significance.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Why are there no comparisons to multilingual encoders (mBERT, XLM-R) or to cross-lingual embedding-based NER transfer? If compute is a concern, can you at least include published zero-shot numbers on CoNLL/low-resource languages to contextualize your approach?
- Resource replacement: Since Freebase is deprecated, can your pipeline be reproduced with Wikidata (types and sitelinks)? If so, what changes are required and how does performance compare?
- Feature integration: How exactly are top-2 candidate types incorporated? Are there confidence thresholds or learned gating to suppress low-confidence links? How are overlapping n-gram links combined for a token? Please detail the “linker” behavior and any pruning.
- Label mapping: How sensitive are results to the MISC→LOC remapping procedure? Can you report results with a fixed, harmonized label set that does not use Freebase for relabeling?
- Efficiency: What is the runtime to wikify every n-gram on a CoNLL-sized test set, and the memory/compute requirements? Any batching/indexing optimizations?
- Tokenization/scripts: What tokenization was used for BN/TA (non-Latin), and how does segmentation quality affect wikifier coverage and NER performance? Any normalization applied?
- Per-type results and error analysis: Can you report PER/LOC/ORG F1 and boundary vs. type errors to show where wikifier features help most?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea of leveraging cross-lingual wikification as delexicalized features is sensible and yields gains over older direct-transfer baselines, with some thoughtful analyses. However, the lack of comparisons to established modern cross-lingual NER methods, reliance on deprecated resources, ad hoc label remapping, and limited methodological/detailing reduce confidence in the generality and rigor of the claims. Strengthening baselines, clarifying reproducibility with current resources, and tightening evaluation would significantly improve the paper.
