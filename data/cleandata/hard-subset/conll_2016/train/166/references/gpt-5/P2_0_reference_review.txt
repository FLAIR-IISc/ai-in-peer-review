Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes a direct-transfer NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories) by grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to entries in English Wikipedia. The model builds on a strong English NER system (Ratinov and Roth, 2009) and augments it with (i) multilingual gazetteers derived from Wikipedia and (ii) novel wikifier-derived features. Two practical modifications to a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016) make dense n-gram wikification feasible.
- Empirically, the approach delivers substantial gains in direct transfer without parallel data, outperforming Täckström et al. (2012) on CoNLL (ES/NL/DE) and Zhang et al. (2016) on five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA). Monolingual experiments also show consistent improvements from the wikifier features.
- The paper includes informative analyses: ablations (wikifier-only, base, +gazetteers, +wikifier), sensitivity to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage (simulated link removal and Freebase lookup ablation), and multi-source training showing small but consistent gains.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and orthogonal idea with strong practical value. Using cross-lingual wikification as delexicalized features is elegant and requires only a multilingual Wikipedia dump—no parallel corpora or target-language annotation—making it broadly applicable. The modifications to wikify every n-gram are sensible to limit false positives (e.g., avoid token-based candidate generation for n > 1).
- Strong empirical results in the direct-transfer setting. The method yields large, consistent gains over a well-known direct-transfer baseline (Täckström) on CoNLL and beats a competitive low-resource approach (Zhang et al.) that assumes parallel documents and native-speaker effort. The improvements are substantial (e.g., +10 F1 in several settings) and robust across both Latin and non-Latin scripts, with thoughtful caveats.
- Thorough and insightful analysis. The ablations are cleanly organized and make the contribution of each feature group easy to assess. The controlled experiments reducing interlanguage links and removing Freebase queries convincingly explain performance degradation in low-resource settings—tying outcomes to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage rather than model brittleness. The guidance on when to include lexical/gazetteer features (Latin vs. non-Latin scripts) is useful and actionable.
- Multi-source training and language-pair matrix. Demonstrating that additional source languages often help (and rarely hurt) adds credibility and practicality. The training/test matrix provides concrete guidance on which source languages transfer best, and validates the intuition that higher-resource sources are generally better.
- Monolingual utility. Showing that wikifier features also improve monolingual NER suggests the features capture useful semantics beyond cross-lingual alignment.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on Freebase and Wikipedia categories. The approach heavily depends on Freebase types (which are now deprecated) and category structure, both of which are noisy and uneven across languages. The ablation shows strong drops without Freebase, calling into question long-term sustainability. Discussion of alternative type systems (e.g., Wikidata properties) or a migration plan would strengthen the contribution.
- Efficiency and scalability are not evaluated. Wikifying all n-grams up to length 4 can be computationally expensive, especially for longer documents or large-scale processing. There is no reporting of runtime, memory, or throughput, nor pragmatic guidance (e.g., pruning strategies, caching, indexing) to make the approach scalable in production.
- Reproducibility and implementation detail gaps. The paper builds on an external wikifier and modifies it, but does not provide enough detail to reproduce the candidate generation/ranking changes or exact feature templates, nor state whether code/resources will be released. Details on stopword handling, NIL decisions, and feature thresholds are not fully specified. This may hinder replication.
- Evaluation comparability and label mapping. For the low-resource data, labels (e.g., MISC removal, LOC remapping via Freebase, TTL/TIME removal) are altered. While likely necessary, these choices complicate fair comparisons. It is also unclear whether CoNLL cross-lingual numbers include MISC (as in standard CoNLL) or exclude it in some settings (as stated for Figure 2). Clearer alignment with prior evaluation protocols is needed.
- Baseline coverage and modern alternatives. The paper compares to Täckström (bitext word clusters) and Zhang (expectation learning), but omits more recent cross-lingual representation baselines (e.g., multilingual embeddings or joint EL+NER approaches) that do not require parallel data. Even if orthogonal, a comparison or discussion would help contextualize the gains in today’s landscape.

Questions to Authors
- Efficiency: What are the runtime and memory costs of wikifying all n-grams up to 4? How does cost scale with document length? Are there pruning heuristics or caching strategies? Any empirical speed/accuracy trade-offs?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, gazetteers, and the scripts for label remapping? Can you provide the full feature template list (including ranker confidence features), stopword lists, and NIL-handling rules?
- Feature ablations: What is the relative contribution of Freebase types vs. Wikipedia categories vs. ranker confidence? You partially ablate Freebase; could you also report categories-only and top-1 vs. top-2 candidate features?
- Evaluation protocol: For CoNLL cross-lingual results in Table 2, do scores include MISC (as in standard CoNLL) or exclude it? For low-resource comparisons to Zhang et al., are preprocessing and label sets strictly aligned?
- Training details: How were hyperparameters tuned (dev sets or cross-validation)? In multi-source training, how did you balance languages (sampling, weighting)? Did you try down-weighting lexical features to avoid swamping wikifier signals in non-Latin scripts?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and empirically solid paper with a simple, general idea that yields strong direct-transfer results. Addressing sustainability (post-Freebase), scalability, and reproducibility would notably strengthen it. I lean positive.
