Summary
The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features. Specifically, an external wikifier grounds every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a document to English Wikipedia titles; the resulting Wikipedia categories and Freebase types (from the top candidates) are used as features in an averaged-perceptron NER model alongside standard features and Wikipedia-derived gazetteers. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text or target-language annotations) and is evaluated in both monolingual and direct-transfer settings across nine languages (EN, ES, NL, DE, TR, TL, YO, BN, TA). The method improves monolingual performance and provides substantial gains in direct transfer, outperforming prior direct-transfer baselines and an expectation-based weakly supervised method on low-resource languages. Analysis shows that performance correlates with the size of the Wikipedia–English intersection and that multi-source training further helps.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A practical, language-agnostic featureization for NER via cross-lingual wikification that supports direct transfer without target-language supervision and yields sizable improvements over strong baselines.
2) A broad, carefully controlled empirical study (9 languages) with ablations isolating the impact of wikifier features, gazetteers, Freebase access, English-intersection size, and multi-source training.
3) Evidence that wikification-based features also improve monolingual NER, demonstrating complementary value beyond transfer.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical gains in direct transfer with minimal resources: Training on English and testing directly on ES/NL/DE with wikifier features yields large improvements over the base and gazetteer-only models and surpasses a prior state-of-the-art direct transfer approach using parallel-induced clusters (Täckström et al., 2012). On five low-resource languages, the method outperforms an expectation-learning approach that assumes parallel data and native-speaker interaction (Zhang et al., 2016), despite requiring less supervision. These results substantiate the central claim that wikification provides powerful delexicalized signals.

2) Clear, informative ablations and analysis: The paper isolates the contribution of wikifier features (“wikifier only”), shows additive effects over base and gazetteer features in both monolingual and transfer scenarios, and ties performance to Wikipedia/Freebase coverage via controlled interventions (removing Freebase queries and downsampling interlanguage links). This analysis convincingly explains performance differences across languages and scripts.

3) Methodological simplicity and generality: The approach is simple to implement on top of a standard NER model, requires only Wikipedia dumps, and does not depend on target-language NLP tools (e.g., POS tagging) or parallel corpora. Modifications to the wikifier for dense n-gram linking are explained and sensible (e.g., restricting candidate generation for multi-word n-grams).

4) Breadth of evaluation: Experiments cover four CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages with different scripts, plus multi-source training. The monolingual improvements indicate the features are broadly useful and not only for transfer.

5) Practical insights: The paper provides actionable guidance (e.g., when to rely more on wikifier-only features in non-Latin-script settings) and shows that adding non-target training languages tends to help.

Weaknesses
1) Dependence on Wikipedia/Freebase coverage limits robustness: The method’s effectiveness degrades with small Wikipedia size and poor Freebase coverage, especially for non-Latin scripts (BN, TA, YO). While this is acknowledged and analyzed, it remains a core limitation; performance heavily depends on interlanguage links and Freebase availability. The approach may struggle in truly low-resource settings with sparse or noisy Wikipedia editions.

2) Missing or weaker baselines relative to modern transfer methods: The primary comparisons are to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016). Given the timeframe cited, comparisons to cross-lingual word embedding methods, multilingual neural NER (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF with multilingual embeddings), or recent projection-based neural approaches would strengthen claims of state of the art. The base model (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) is strong but dated; showing that wikifier features help a neural baseline would improve generality.

3) Potential methodological confounds in low-resource evaluation: To handle label set mismatches, the training data’s MISC tags are remapped to LOC when the wikifier indicates a location type and otherwise removed. This uses the same type source that powers test-time features, potentially biasing the transfer setting toward the proposed features. A control experiment without wikifier-driven relabeling, or an alternative standardized mapping, would clarify any inflation due to this preprocessing.

4) Incomplete reporting of implementation and efficiency details: The paper does not provide runtime or resource costs of wikifying every n-gram, nor tokenization/language preprocessing specifics per language. The accuracy of the wikifier by language (linking and disambiguation quality) is not reported, making it hard to attribute errors between the wikifier and the NER model or to estimate scalability.

5) Limited discussion of error modes and boundary detection: While the paper shows that types are strong signals, it would benefit from analysis of boundary errors introduced by n-gram overgeneration and disambiguation mistakes, especially in morphologically rich languages and for organization vs. location confusions.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report wikifier performance (e.g., linking accuracy) per language and its correlation with NER F1? This would help quantify how much headroom remains on the wikification side.
- What are the computational costs of wikifying every n-gram (n ≤ 4) per document, and are there heuristics to prune candidates without harming NER?
- How are tokenization and sentence segmentation handled across languages, especially for non-Latin scripts? Are there language-specific tokenizers?
- How are Wikipedia categories and Freebase types mapped to PER/LOC/ORG? Is there any manual mapping or heuristics that could introduce label leakage?
- For the low-resource setting, can you provide results without the wikifier-driven relabeling of MISC in the English training data to quantify any effect of this preprocessing?
- Have you tried integrating wikifier features into a neural NER model (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) or combining with cross-lingual embeddings to test complementarity?
