Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. By grounding every n-gram (n ≤ 4) to English Wikipedia, it extracts Freebase types and Wikipedia categories as features that transfer across languages without parallel data.
- It demonstrates that these wikifier-derived features improve both monolingual NER and, more importantly, direct transfer performance from English to multiple target languages (CoNLL: ES, NL, DE; low-resource: TR, TL, YO, BN, TA), outperforming prior direct transfer baselines and competing approaches that rely on additional resources.
- The paper analyzes the role of Wikipedia size and interlanguage links on feature quality via ablations and shows modest but consistent gains from multi-source training.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains in direct transfer using only multilingual Wikipedia: Adding wikifier features increases F1 substantially over base and gazetteer features in cross-lingual transfer (e.g., DE: 41.81 → 60.97; NL: 25.24 → 47.14; ES: 30.50 → 48.41). These results exceed the Täckström et al. (2012) direct transfer baseline and even their bitext-cluster model, showing strong utility of the proposed delexicalized features.
- Demonstrated robustness and generality: The method requires no parallel text, gold POS tags, or language-specific heuristics. It leverages only multilingual Wikipedia and an existing cross-lingual wikifier, making it broadly applicable. The same features also improve monolingual NER across 9 languages (+Wikifier increases average F1 from 72.91 to 74.80).
- Solid analysis connecting performance to resource availability: The ablation study in Table 3 quantifies performance degradation when removing Freebase queries or interlanguage links (e.g., Spanish drops from 40.04 to 29.44 with 90% link removal), explaining weaker gains for low-resource Wikipedias and providing a convincing causal explanation.
- Practical insights and multi-source training: The paper offers concrete guidance (e.g., prefer wikifier-only when scripts diverge; include all features for Latin scripts) and shows that adding training data from multiple languages provides further gains without harming performance.
- Methodological novelty within direct transfer: Using cross-lingual wikification to produce mention-level semantic typing (Freebase types, categories) as universally applicable features is a distinctive and well-motivated contribution relative to prior Wikipedia-based NER (mostly monolingual) and prior direct-transfer work (largely clustering/embeddings).

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Freebase and English intersection: Freebase is deprecated, and the approach relies heavily on Freebase types obtained via the English interlanguage link. For low-resource languages where Freebase coverage is poor, performance drops sharply, as shown in the ablations. The paper does not evaluate replacements (e.g., Wikidata) or quantify how category-only features perform without Freebase.
- Label-set and evaluation inconsistencies across datasets: The paper modifies labels (e.g., removing TIME/TTL; converting some MISC to LOC via Freebase typing; ignoring MISC during CoNLL evaluation). While practically understandable, these choices complicate comparability and may bias cross-corpus results. A more controlled mapping or parallel reporting under standard label sets would strengthen claims.
- Clarity on configuration selection and fairness across languages: For Bengali and Tamil, the paper notes that including lexical features can hurt because they don’t transfer; yet the main “+Wikifier” transfer results still include lexical features for BN (yielding 2.99 vs 27.85 for wikifier-only). Reporting best-per-language configurations or a consistent “script-aware” protocol would provide a fairer comparison and likely stronger results.
- Limited error analysis and feature ablations: The paper shows aggregate F1 gains but does not disentangle improvements in boundary detection vs. type assignment. It would help to quantify how much of the gain comes from improved typing (expected from Freebase/categories) versus better mention detection, and to ablate top-1 vs top-2 candidate usage, confidence thresholds, or ranker features.
- Reproducibility and efficiency details: The approach wikifies all n-grams up to length 4, which could be computationally heavy; the paper does not provide runtime, indexing, or resource usage. Tokenization procedures for non-Latin scripts, handling of casing and normalization, and hyperparameters for the wikifier features are only partially specified.

Questions to Authors
- Can the approach be transitioned from Freebase to Wikidata (or another KB) without loss? Have you tested performance using only Wikipedia categories, or categories plus Wikidata properties?
- What is the exact feature set derived from the wikifier (e.g., number of categories/types retained per candidate, normalization, deduplication)? How sensitive are results to using top-1 vs top-2 candidates or to a confidence threshold that rejects low-confidence links?
- How are tokenization and casing handled across languages, especially for non-Latin scripts? Are there language-specific tokenizers, and could tokenization differences explain some low-resource outcomes?
- For low-resource languages, would reporting the best-performing configuration per script (e.g., wikifier-only for non-Latin) change the comparison to Zhang et al. (2016)? The table seems to show inferior numbers for YO and BN relative to Zhang et al.; please clarify the mapping of their results to columns and ensure comparability.
- In multi-source training, are training instances balanced across languages or weighted? Did you observe shifts in label priors or domain effects, and if so, how were they mitigated?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound, and presents a practical, resource-light cross-lingual NER strategy with strong empirical gains and informative analyses. The primary weaknesses concern reliance on Freebase, cross-dataset label handling, and limited fine-grained error analysis.
