Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground every n-gram (n ≤ 4) in a target-language document to English Wikipedia titles, extracting language-independent features (Freebase types and Wikipedia categories). The model builds on a strong English NER system (Ratinov & Roth, 2009) with added gazetteers collected from multilingual Wikipedia and wikifier-derived features, and transfers directly to new languages without requiring parallel text. The authors evaluate on CoNLL (EN/NL/DE/ES) and five low-resource languages (TR/TL/YO/BN/TA), analyze the impact of Wikipedia size on performance, and test multi-source training.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that cross-lingual wikification can supply effective delexicalized features for NER, enabling direct transfer to many languages without parallel data, with consistent gains over strong baselines.
2) Empirical analysis across 9 languages, including low-resource settings, showing how Wikipedia size and interlanguage links affect the utility of wikifier features, and that multi-source training generally helps.
3) Practical adjustments to a cross-lingual wikifier to enable dense n-gram coverage and incorporation of ranker confidence as features.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality of delexicalized features: Using cross-lingual entity linking to obtain language-agnostic type/category signals is a clean idea that directly addresses the brittleness of lexical features in transfer. The ablations (“wikifier only,” base, +gazetteers, +wikifier) consistently show non-trivial gains from wikifier features across all languages, and in non-Latin scripts the wikifier-only setting is most robust.
- Breadth of evaluation and analyses: The paper covers CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages with different scripts. It also provides instructive analyses: (i) how removing Freebase queries and interlanguage links degrades performance (Table 3), and (ii) the effect of using additional source languages for training. This adds credibility and diagnostic insight beyond headline numbers.
- Strong baselines for the direct-transfer era: Comparisons against Täckström et al. (2012) direct transfer and bitext-cluster baselines as well as Zhang et al. (2016) (low-resource) show the proposed approach is competitive or better, especially in settings without parallel text or native speaker intervention.
- Sensible feature engineering and implementation choices: The paper reuses a well-known NER system and carefully designs gazetteer features and wikifier integration (e.g., avoiding token-based candidate generation for multi-word n-grams, using ranker confidence), which makes the approach replicable in principle and modular.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to contemporary multilingual models: The paper does not compare against modern cross-lingual NER baselines based on multilingual transformers (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R, InfoXLM, LaBSE), which now dominate direct transfer and zero-shot NER. Without these comparisons (and/or showing complementarity), it is hard to assess the approach’s relevance in 2025.
- Reliance on Freebase and Wikipedia categories: Freebase has been deprecated; coverage is uneven (notably absent for YO/BN/TA), and categories/types can be noisy. The method’s dependence on interlanguage links and Freebase IDs limits robustness for genuinely low-resource languages. A transition to Wikidata and/or type systems with better multilingual coverage is needed.
- Evaluation design concerns: The ad hoc tag-set adaptations (e.g., mapping MISC to LOC if the wikifier suggests a location; removing TIME/TTL) risk injecting label noise and confounds aligned with the method’s own signals. This complicates comparability and may inflate observed gains. Evaluation on standardized multilingual NER corpora (e.g., WikiAnn) and keeping tag mappings independent of the wikifier would strengthen claims.
- Scalability and efficiency not addressed: Wikifying all n-grams up to length 4 can be computationally expensive. The paper lacks runtime/throughput measurements, memory profile, and discussion of caching/indexing strategies required for scaling to realistic corpora.
- Reproducibility and rigor: It is unclear whether code, trained models, and the modified wikifier will be released. Some details are underspecified (hyperparameters, exact wikifier settings for each language, handling NILs, feature sparsity control). No statistical significance testing is reported; some gains are modest and should be validated.

Questions to authors
- How does the approach compare to fine-tuning mBERT/XLM-R on English and evaluating zero-shot on the same target languages? Are the wikifier features complementary if fused with transformer representations?
- Can you replace Freebase types with Wikidata classes/properties? If so, how does performance change in low-resource languages lacking Freebase coverage?
- What is the computational cost of wikifying all n-grams (preprocessing time per document, average candidate set sizes, memory)? Any pruning or caching?
- How sensitive are results to the top-k candidate cutoff and to using only the top candidate vs. top-2?
- Can you re-run the low-resource experiments without using wikifier-derived signals to modify tag sets (e.g., MISC→LOC), and/or on a standardized multilingual dataset, to rule out confounds?

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is solid and well-executed within a classic feature-based paradigm, with informative analyses. However, the paper lacks comparisons to modern multilingual transformer baselines and relies on deprecated resources (Freebase), raising concerns about current relevance and robustness. I encourage the authors to (i) integrate Wikidata, (ii) benchmark against and combine with mBERT/XLM-R, (iii) provide efficiency metrics and significance tests, and (iv) evaluate on standardized multilingual datasets with clean label policies. These additions could make a strong resubmission.
