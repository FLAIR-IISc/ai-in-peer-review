- Summary: Proposes cross-lingual NER that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to map text to English Wikipedia, leveraging FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features, showing strong gains on high- and low-resource languages without parallel text.

- Contribution: Introduces a novel method to generate language-independent features for cross-lingual NER via cross-lingual wikification.

- Contribution: Provides comprehensive evaluation demonstrating significant improvements over prior direct-transfer methods across multiple languages.

- Contribution: Analyzes the effect of Wikipedia size and script types on feature quality and applicability.

- Strength: Novel feature-engineering insight that FreeBase types from wikification provide strong NER signals even with imperfect disambiguation.

- Strength: Thorough experimental evaluation across high- and low-resource languages with clear baselines and analyses.

- Strength: Practical approach requiring only a multilingual Wikipedia dump, outperforming methods that need parallel text or native speakers for low-resource languages.

- Weakness: Lacks comparison with modern transformer-based cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., multilingual BERT/XLM-R), which are current state-of-the-art.

- Weakness: Insufficient systematic analysis of how wikifier errors propagate to NER performance and specific error impacts.

- Weakness: Limited justification and ablation for modifications to the wikifier candidate generation; no evidence showing those changes improve NER.

- Question: How does the approach compare empirically to transformer-based cross-lingual NER models like mBERT or XLM-R?

- Question: Can the authors provide detailed error analysis demonstrating how incorrect FreeBase type assignments affect NER outcomes?

- Question: For languages with very small Wikipedias (e.g., Yoruba), what mitigation strategies (other knowledge sources, synthetic data) do the authors propose?

- Overall assessment: Valuable, well-executed contribution with compelling practical results—especially for low-resource languages—but needs comparisons to modern transformers and deeper analysis of wikifier limitations to be stronger.
