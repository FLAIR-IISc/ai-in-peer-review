- Summary: Proposes cross-lingual NER using a cross-lingual wikifier to map words to English Wikipedia and use FreeBase types/Wikipedia categories as language-independent features, showing improvements on CoNLL and five low-resource languages.

- Contribution 1: Shows wikification yields strong language-independent features usable for direct transfer without sentence- or word-aligned parallel corpora.

- Contribution 2: Provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages, including non-Latin scripts and low-resource settings.

- Contribution 3: Analyzes how Wikipedia size and interlanguage links affect feature quality and NER performance.

- Strength 1: Novel application—demonstrates that even imperfect wikification provides useful FreeBase-type signals for NER, especially in low-resource scenarios.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical evaluation—consistent improvements over baselines and validation in monolingual experiments.

- Strength 3: Insightful limitation analysis—experiments clearly link Wikipedia size/interlanguage links to feature quality and performance.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty—similar ideas (using Wikipedia categories for NER) exist (e.g., Kazama & Torisawa 2007), and the paper doesn’t clearly distinguish its advances beyond multilinguality.

- Weakness 2: Mischaracterizes "no parallel data"—relying on interlanguage links is effectively using parallel data, which undermines the claim.

- Weakness 3: Scalability concerns—doesn’t address applicability or solutions for languages with negligible Wikipedia presence.

- Weakness 4: Missing computational analysis—no discussion of runtime/cost implications of wikifying all n-grams (n ≤ 4), which may be expensive.

- Question 1: How does this method compare to recent multilingual pretrained models (e.g., multilingual BERT) as baselines?

- Question 2: Please clarify the exact nature of parallel data used and the role of interlanguage links in the method.

- Question 3: Would incorporating other multilingual KBs or resources improve performance for very small-Wikipedia languages, and was this considered?
