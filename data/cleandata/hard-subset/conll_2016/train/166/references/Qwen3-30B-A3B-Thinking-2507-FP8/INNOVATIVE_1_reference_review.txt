

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to cross-lingual NER by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia, then extracting language-independent features from FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories. The key contributions in order of strength are:

1. **Novel use of wikification as a source of language-independent features for NER:** Instead of using wikification as a downstream task (after NER), the authors repurpose it as a feature extraction mechanism, demonstrating that FreeBase types provide strong signals for NER across languages.

2. **Demonstration of effectiveness on non-Latin script languages:** The approach shows significant promise for languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) where traditional approaches struggle, without requiring language-specific processing.

3. **Simple, broadly applicable direct transfer method:** The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, eliminating the need for parallel data, gold POS tags, or native speaker interaction.

## Strengths

**1. Significant novelty in approach:** This paper presents a genuinely novel perspective on cross-lingual NER. The core idea of using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features for NER, rather than using it as a downstream task, is bold and different from existing approaches. This represents a meaningful conceptual shift that could inspire new directions in cross-lingual NLP.

**2. Strong empirical results across diverse languages:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines on both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages, including those with non-Latin scripts. The fact that the approach works well for Bengali and Tamil (languages with entirely non-Latin scripts) is particularly impressive and shows the approach's generalizability.

**3. Practical simplicity and broad applicability:** The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, which are widely available and easily accessible. The authors correctly note that this makes the approach broadly applicable without requiring parallel data or human interaction with the target language, which is a major advantage over other approaches.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited theoretical justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, the paper lacks a strong theoretical explanation for why FreeBase types work as language-independent features. The authors observe that these features work well but don't provide a clear rationale for why Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types are so effective across languages, which would strengthen the contribution.

**2. Incomplete exploration of limitations for low-resource languages:** The paper acknowledges that Wikipedia size impacts performance but doesn't deeply analyze how to overcome these limitations for languages with very small Wikipedia. For example, the paper shows that Bengali and Tamil benefit from wikifier-only features, but doesn't explore whether this is a fundamental limitation of the approach or could be mitigated with additional techniques.

**3. Limited comparison to recent work:** While the paper compares to Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), it doesn't engage with more recent approaches to cross-lingual NER that might have made progress in similar directions. This weakens the paper's position in the current state of the art.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to cross-lingual NER with significant potential to influence the field. While there are methodological limitations and gaps in theoretical justification, the core idea is bold, the empirical results are strong across diverse languages, and the approach is simple and broadly applicable. The novelty of the core concept outweighs the methodological imperfections.

The paper's most significant contribution is the conceptual shift from using wikification as a downstream task to using it as a source of language-independent features. This is a fresh perspective that could inspire new research directions in cross-lingual NLP. The fact that it works well for non-Latin script languages is particularly noteworthy and demonstrates the approach's robustness.

The authors should address the theoretical justification for why FreeBase types work as features and provide more detailed analysis of the limitations for very low-resource languages in the final version. However, these are not sufficient to warrant rejection given the paper's strong novelty and empirical results.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation for why wikifier-only features perform better than combined features for Bengali and Tamil? Is this related to the non-Latin script issue, or are there other factors at play?

2. How might the approach be adapted for languages with extremely small Wikipedia (e.g., Yoruba with only 31K articles)? What would be the minimum Wikipedia size required for the approach to work effectively?

3. The paper mentions that FreeBase doesn't contain entities for Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. How might this limitation be addressed in future work, especially for languages where this is a significant problem?

This paper represents an important step forward in cross-lingual NER with a novel perspective that could have significant impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
