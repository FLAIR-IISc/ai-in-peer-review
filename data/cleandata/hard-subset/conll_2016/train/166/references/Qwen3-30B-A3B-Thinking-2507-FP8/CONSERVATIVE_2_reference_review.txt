

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words to English Wikipedia, extracting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features. The authors claim their method outperforms direct transfer baselines on CoNLL languages and shows promise for low-resource languages without requiring parallel text.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstration that wikifier features can serve as language-independent features for NER
2. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages including non-Latin scripts
3. Analysis of Wikipedia size impact on feature quality

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a thorough evaluation across 9 languages, including challenging non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), which is valuable for cross-lingual NLP research.
2. The analysis of Wikipedia size impact on feature quality (Section 4.4) is insightful and addresses a key limitation of the approach.
3. The method requires minimal resources (just multilingual Wikipedia) and avoids the need for parallel text or human annotation, which is practically appealing.

## Weaknesses

**1. Overstated novelty and lack of rigorous comparison with established baselines (primary weakness)**  
The paper claims "language-independent features" but fails to properly demonstrate this. The features' quality is directly tied to target language Wikipedia size (Table 3), meaning the approach isn't truly language-independent as claimed. More critically, the paper fails to compare against stronger, more recent baselines. The comparison with Täckström (2012) is outdated, and the Zhang et al. (2016) comparison is with a method that requires human interaction (which isn't a direct transfer baseline). The authors claim "significant improvements" without statistical significance testing or comparison to state-of-the-art methods like those using multilingual BERT or XLM-R.

**2. Lack of methodological rigor in validation (primary weakness)**  
The paper lacks essential validation steps expected in NLP research. There are no statistical significance tests for the claimed improvements (e.g., "improves upon base features for all 9 languages" without p-values). The ablation studies are superficial—no analysis of which specific FreeBase types or Wikipedia categories contribute most to performance, nor comparison between FreeBase types versus Wikipedia categories. This makes it impossible to determine whether the features are genuinely novel or merely a recombination of known techniques.

**3. Questionable claims about language independence (primary weakness)**  
The authors claim these are "language-independent features," but the experiments in Table 2 show the opposite: for non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil), the wikifier features alone outperform all other approaches because lexical features fail entirely. This isn't language independence—it's language dependence on Wikipedia size. The paper admits this in Section 4.4 but doesn't properly address how this limitation affects the generalizability of the approach.

**4. Underdeveloped analysis of feature utility**  
The paper shows wikifier features improve performance but doesn't explain *why* they work. For instance, Figure 1 suggests FreeBase types are strong signals, but the paper doesn't analyze which types are most informative (e.g., "person" vs. "location" types) or how imperfect disambiguation affects performance. This lack of deeper analysis weakens the claim that these are "strong signals" for NER.

**5. Limited evaluation on low-resource languages**  
The evaluation on low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) is limited to just five languages, with particularly poor results for Yoruba (33.63 F1 in monolingual experiments). The paper claims "significant improvement" but doesn't establish whether these results are statistically meaningful or merely reflect the limitations of Wikipedia size for these languages. The comparison with Zhang et al. (2016) is misleading because their method requires human interaction, which isn't comparable to a pure direct transfer approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why do you claim these are "language-independent features" when the quality of these features directly depends on target language Wikipedia size (Table 3)?
2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for your reported improvements over baselines?
3. How would your approach perform on languages with Wikipedia size <50,000 articles (e.g., many African languages)?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting approach that leverages Wikipedia for cross-lingual NER, but it fails to meet the methodological rigor expected for a CoNLL submission. The claims of "language-independent features" are contradicted by the data, the comparison with baselines is inadequate, and the validation lacks statistical rigor. While the idea has potential, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish its novelty or superiority over existing methods. I recommend rejection unless the authors can provide stronger validation and more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art baselines.
