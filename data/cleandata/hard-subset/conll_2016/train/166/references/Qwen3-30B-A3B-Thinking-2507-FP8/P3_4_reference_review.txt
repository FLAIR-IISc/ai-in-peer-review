

# Peer Review: "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a method to use cross-lingual wikification for language-independent NER features, (2) a model that outperforms direct transfer baselines on CoNLL languages, and (3) a model that works on low-resource languages with limited Wikipedia coverage. The approach is evaluated across 9 languages, including 5 low-resource ones.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling theoretical foundation**: The paper makes a strong case that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories provide robust language-independent features for NER, demonstrated effectively in Figure 1. The insight that even imperfect disambiguation can yield useful signals is novel and well-justified through careful analysis.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides thorough experiments across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages. The analysis of how Wikipedia size impacts performance (Section 4.4) is particularly valuable, showing that the quality of wikifier features directly correlates with Wikipedia coverage. This provides important practical insights for the field.

3. **Strong baseline comparisons**: The comparison against Täckström (2012) is well-executed, showing significant improvements in all languages. The comparison against Zhang et al. (2016) for low-resource languages is also compelling, demonstrating that the approach outperforms methods requiring native speaker interaction and parallel text.

## Weaknesses

1. **Mischaracterization of "no parallel data" requirement**: The paper claims "no sentence-aligned or word-aligned parallel text is needed," but the approach actually depends on interlanguage links in Wikipedia, which are a form of parallel data (though not sentence-aligned). This is a significant limitation that undermines the paper's primary claim about the approach's resource requirements. The quality of the wikifier features depends entirely on the quality and coverage of these interlanguage links.

2. **Insufficient analysis of language-independence**: The paper assumes FreeBase types provide language-independent features, but doesn't provide evidence that these types map consistently across languages. For example, "person" in FreeBase might correspond to different entity types in different linguistic contexts. Without this analysis, the claim of "language-independent" features is questionable.

3. **Limited handling of low-resource languages**: The paper acknowledges poor performance on low-resource languages (Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) but doesn't provide meaningful solutions. The claim that "wikifier features still give significant improvement" is misleading when the absolute F1 scores are very low (e.g., 6.72 for Yoruba in Table 2). The paper should have discussed practical limitations and alternative approaches for languages with minimal Wikipedia coverage.

4. **Lack of comparison with modern multilingual models**: The paper doesn't compare against contemporary multilingual approaches like multilingual BERT or XLM-R, which have become state-of-the-art for cross-lingual tasks. This omission makes the paper's claims about "outperforming state-of-the-art" questionable, as the authors don't compare against the most relevant recent work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the issue that the interlanguage links used by your wikifier are themselves a form of parallel data? Could you clarify the relationship between your approach and other methods that use Wikipedia for cross-lingual tasks?

2. The paper claims "language-independent features," but doesn't provide evidence that FreeBase types maintain consistent meaning across languages. Could you provide analysis of how FreeBase types map to entity types in different languages?

3. For low-resource languages like Yoruba (with only 31K Wikipedia articles), your approach yields very low F1 scores (6.72). How would you modify the approach to be more effective for such languages, or acknowledge the fundamental limitations of the approach?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper with strong empirical results and valuable insights about the relationship between Wikipedia coverage and cross-lingual NER performance. The core idea of using wikification for language-independent features is compelling and well-supported. However, the paper's claims about the "no parallel data" requirement and the language-independence of the features are overstated, and the lack of comparison against modern multilingual models weakens the significance of the claimed improvements. With more careful analysis of these limitations and a more precise characterization of the approach's requirements, this could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes a useful incremental contribution to cross-lingual NER but overstates its novelty and limitations.
