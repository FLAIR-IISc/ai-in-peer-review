

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams (n≤4) to English Wikipedia, leveraging FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features. The authors claim their method requires only multilingual Wikipedia (no parallel text) and outperforms direct transfer baselines on CoNLL languages and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating that cross-lingual wikification features can serve as effective language-independent features for NER
2. Showing that wikifier features significantly improve cross-lingual NER performance without requiring parallel data
3. Providing an analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality across languages

## Strengths
The paper presents a reasonable approach that appears to work well for many languages. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality (Table 3) is insightful, and the comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) shows competitive results. The use of non-Latin script languages as test cases is valuable for low-resource language research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting**: The paper exhibits numerous inconsistencies that undermine professionalism. "Wikifier" is used interchangeably with "wikification" without clear distinction. "Wikipedia" is sometimes capitalized and sometimes not (e.g., "Wikipedia" vs. "wikipedia"). Language labels are inconsistent (CoNLL vs. CoNLL2002/2003, EN vs. English). Table 2 has inconsistent formatting with "APPROACH" header listing language codes while the legend uses full names. These minor but pervasive inconsistencies suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Insufficient Methodological Detail**: The authors claim to modify the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier system for n-gram grounding but provide minimal implementation details. They state they "modify the system in the following two ways" but don't explain how these modifications were implemented or why they were necessary. For example, they mention "for n > 1, we generate title candidates only according to the whole string," but don't provide examples of the problematic behavior they're fixing. This lack of detail makes the method difficult to reproduce.

3. **Ad-Hoc Data Processing Without Justification**: The paper describes converting MISC tags to LOC tags for low-resource languages, but provides no justification for this specific conversion. The rationale ("many MISC-tagged entities in the LDC2014E115 have LOC tags") is unclear, and the process seems arbitrary. The paper doesn't explain how this affects the evaluation or provide metrics on how many entities were converted, making it hard to assess the validity of the results.

4. **Missing Evaluation Details**: The paper states they use "phrase level F1" but doesn't clarify how they handle overlapping mentions or the conversion from IOB1 to BIO labeling scheme. For non-Latin script languages like Bengali and Tamil, the paper doesn't explain how they handle the conversion from Latin script-based labels to the target language's script, which could significantly impact evaluation metrics.

5. **Inconsistent Treatment of Low-Resource Languages**: The paper claims Bengali and Tamil use "entirely non-Latin scripts" but then lists them as "Latin Script" in Table 2's header, creating confusion. The paper also states that for Bengali and Tamil, "no lexical feature in English will fire at test time" but doesn't explain why this would be the case for the wikifier features, which are supposed to be language-independent.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide a concrete example of how your n-gram modification prevents incorrect linking (e.g., "in Germany" being linked to "Germany")?
2. Why was the MISC-to-LOC conversion specifically chosen for low-resource languages, and how many entities were affected?
3. How exactly did you handle the IOB1 to BIO conversion for non-Latin script languages, and what impact did this have on the results?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach with promising results, the numerous minor methodological inconsistencies and formatting issues demonstrate a lack of attention to detail that would undermine the paper's credibility in a peer-reviewed conference. The inconsistencies in terminology, data processing, and evaluation metrics make it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when such fundamental aspects of the paper are not handled with precision.

The paper has potential but requires significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The authors should provide more methodological detail, clarify their data processing decisions, and ensure consistent terminology and formatting throughout the paper.
