- Proposes cross-lingual NER using wikification to map n-grams to English Wikipedia and extract language-independent features (Freebase types, Wikipedia categories).
- Main contribution: feature generation via cross-lingual wikification without parallel text or language-specific resources.
- Empirical contribution: comprehensive evaluation showing wikifier features significantly improve cross-lingual NER across high- and low-resource languages and analysis linking performance to Wikipedia size.

- Strength: Practical, minimal-resource approach that outperforms prior methods on five low-resource languages without target-language training or native-speaker interaction.
- Strength: Broad evaluation across CoNLL and five low-resource languages, including analysis of Wikipedia size effects on feature quality.
- Strength: Significant performance gains over strong baselines (large F1 improvements on German, Dutch, Spanish and multiple low-resource languages).

- Weakness: Lacks comparison with modern multilingual transformer-based methods (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT), leaving competitiveness unclear.
- Weakness: Claim of “language-independent” features is under-analyzed given reliance on English-centric Wikipedia/Freebase that may introduce cultural or typological biases.
- Weakness: Limited discussion and solutions for very low-resource languages with tiny or no Wikipedia coverage (e.g., Yoruba, indigenous languages).

- Question: How would the approach handle languages with no Wikipedia coverage and could other multilingual resources be integrated?
- Question: How do you mitigate biases from relying on English Wikipedia/Freebase for features across diverse languages and entity types?
- Question: What are the computational/runtime requirements of the wikifier at scale and how does runtime scale with target-language Wikipedia size?

- Additional: Paper is well-organized and clearly written with valuable practical contributions.
- Additional: Revision should address missing comparisons to transformer-based baselines and discuss scalability/robustness for hundreds of languages and poorly maintained Wikipedias.
