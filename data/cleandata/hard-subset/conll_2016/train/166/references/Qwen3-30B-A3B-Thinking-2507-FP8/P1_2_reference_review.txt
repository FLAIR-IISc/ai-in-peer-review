

# Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The core idea is that by grounding words and phrases in any language to the English Wikipedia, the corresponding Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types can serve as features that are language-agnostic. The method only requires multilingual Wikipedia, without needing parallel text or sentence alignment. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art direct transfer methods on CoNLL languages (English, German, Spanish, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).

## Main Contributions
1. Development of a cross-lingual NER model that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to generate language-independent features without requiring parallel text or language-specific heuristics.
2. Demonstration that these wikifier features significantly improve NER performance in cross-lingual settings, especially for low-resource languages where lexical features are less effective.
3. Comprehensive analysis showing that the quality of wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size for the target language, and that training on multiple source languages further improves results.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to cross-lingual NER without parallel data**: The paper's key contribution is its method for generating language-independent features without requiring parallel text, which is a significant limitation of many existing cross-lingual NLP approaches. This makes the method applicable to languages with minimal resources. The authors demonstrate this by achieving strong results on low-resource languages like Yoruba and Tamil where other methods fail.

2. **Strong empirical validation across diverse languages**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across both high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages. The results are compelling, with the proposed method significantly outperforming previous direct transfer approaches (e.g., achieving 60.97 F1 on German vs. 45.6 for Täckström's baseline). The improvements are particularly notable on low-resource languages, where their method outperforms Zhang et al.'s (2016) approach that requires "one-hour interaction with a native speaker" by up to 20 points.

3. **Insightful analysis of feature quality**: The paper provides valuable analysis of why wikifier features work well, particularly their relationship with Wikipedia size. The experiments in Section 4.4 clearly demonstrate that smaller Wikipedia sizes lead to worse feature quality, explaining why the method performs better on CoNLL languages than on low-resource ones. This analysis helps understand the limitations and potential of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the application to cross-lingual NER is novel, the core idea of using Wikipedia categories for NER isn't entirely new. Kazama and Torisawa (2007) used Wikipedia categories for monolingual NER, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how much novelty comes from the cross-lingual aspect versus the use of wikification as a means to obtain these features. The paper's contribution would be stronger if it more clearly articulated this distinction.

2. **Insufficient evaluation of the wikifier**: The paper relies on a pre-existing wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016) but doesn't thoroughly evaluate its performance or quantify how errors in the wikifier affect NER results. The paper mentions that disambiguation isn't perfect (Figure 1), but doesn't provide metrics on how often the wikifier fails or how these failures impact the final NER performance. This is a significant omission given the central role of the wikifier.

3. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), but doesn't compare with more recent cross-lingual NER methods (e.g., those based on multilingual BERT or other transformer models). Given the rapid advancement in cross-lingual NLP, this comparison is essential to establish the paper's relevance and significance.

4. **Modest monolingual improvements**: In the monolingual experiments (Table 2), the addition of wikifier features only improves performance by 1-2 points (e.g., from 88.92 to 89.47 for English). This seems modest compared to the significant improvements in the cross-lingual setting. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the improvement is larger in the cross-lingual setting, which weakens the claim that wikifier features are generally valuable.

5. **Limited discussion of practical limitations**: While the paper notes that Wikipedia size affects performance, it doesn't discuss practical implications for languages with very small Wikipedia coverage. For example, how would the method perform on a language with fewer than 10,000 Wikipedia articles? The paper states that the method can be applied to "all languages in Wikipedia," but doesn't address the practical challenges of using it for languages with minimal Wikipedia presence.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses n=4 for n-grams but doesn't explain why this specific value was chosen. Could you provide more details about the ablation study on n-gram size and why n=4 was optimal?

2. The paper states that wikifier features are most helpful for languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali and Tamil), but doesn't discuss how the method would perform on languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Chinese or Japanese). Could you comment on the potential limitations for such languages?

3. The paper mentions that "the only requirement is a multilingual Wikipedia dump," but doesn't quantify how small the Wikipedia needs to be for the method to still be effective. Could you provide more specific examples of languages with minimal Wikipedia coverage where the method would fail, and what the minimum Wikipedia size requirements might be?

This paper presents a useful approach for cross-lingual NER that could be valuable for languages with limited resources. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the empirical validation across diverse languages and the thorough analysis of feature quality make it a strong contribution to the field. With some additional evaluation of the wikifier and comparison with more recent methods, this could be a very impactful paper.
