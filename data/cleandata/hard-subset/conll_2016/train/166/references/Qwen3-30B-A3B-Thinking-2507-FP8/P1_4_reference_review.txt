

# Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) for NER. The key innovation is the use of wikification as a feature generation mechanism rather than as a downstream task, allowing the authors to create language-independent features that can be used across languages without requiring parallel text or language-specific resources.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER through cross-lingual wikification, which does not require parallel text or language-specific resources.
2. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages) demonstrating that wikifier features significantly improve cross-lingual NER performance.
3. Analysis showing that the quality of wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size, providing valuable insights into the limitations and potential of the approach.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Approach with Minimal Resource Requirements**: The paper addresses a significant challenge in NLP - cross-lingual NER for low-resource languages - with an approach that requires only multilingual Wikipedia, which is readily available. This is a major practical advantage over methods requiring parallel text (like projection-based approaches) or native speaker interaction (like Zhang et al., 2016). The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) without any target language training data or native speaker interaction.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Language Types**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across both high-resource (CoNLL languages: English, Spanish, Dutch, German) and low-resource languages (with Latin and non-Latin scripts), which is rare in cross-lingual NER work. The authors not only show performance improvements over strong baselines but also provide a detailed analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance, as shown in Table 3. This analysis reveals that the quality of wikifier features correlates directly with Wikipedia size, which is a valuable insight for future work.

3. **Significant Performance Gains**: The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over strong baselines. For the CoNLL languages, their approach outperforms Täckström et al. (2012) by 13.7 points on German, 10.2 points on Dutch, and 15.4 points on Spanish. For low-resource languages, the approach significantly outperforms Zhang et al. (2016), which requires parallel text and one-hour native speaker interaction, particularly on Turkish (+14.3 points), Tagalog (+10.8 points), and Bengali (+17.5 points).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Modern Cross-Lingual Methods**: The paper does not compare against recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches based on multilingual transformers (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT), which have become the standard for cross-lingual tasks. For example, XLM-R has achieved strong results on cross-lingual NER without requiring parallel data or Wikipedia resources. The authors' approach is presented as a direct transfer method, but they don't position it against the current dominant paradigm in cross-lingual NLP. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and competitiveness of their approach.

2. **Limited Analysis of the "Language-Independence" Claim**: The paper claims to use "language-independent features," but these features are derived from English Wikipedia and FreeBase, which are inherently English-centric. The approach relies on English Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types, which might not capture language-specific entity types or cultural contexts. For example, the paper shows that for Bengali and Tamil (non-Latin scripts), the approach performs best using only wikifier features, suggesting that the English-centric features are actually more language-independent than the lexical features. However, the authors don't thoroughly analyze whether these English-centric features truly generalize across all language types or if they introduce biases.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations for Very Low-Resource Languages**: While the paper acknowledges that Wikipedia size affects performance (Table 3), it doesn't provide a clear path forward for languages with extremely small Wikipedia sizes (like Yoruba, with only 31K articles). The authors show that for Yoruba, the "Wikifier only" approach gets 41.81 F1, but the improvement from adding wikifier features is minimal. They don't discuss how to handle such cases or whether alternative resources (like multilingual dictionaries or other knowledge bases) could be integrated to improve performance for these languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach handle languages with no Wikipedia coverage at all (e.g., some indigenous languages with no Wikipedia)? Would you consider incorporating other multilingual resources, and how would that affect your current framework?

2. Given that your features are derived from English Wikipedia, how do you address potential biases in English Wikipedia that might affect the quality of features for certain entity types (e.g., Western-centric entity categories)?

3. Could you provide more details about the computational requirements of your wikifier for large-scale applications, especially for languages with smaller Wikipedia sizes? How does the runtime scale with the size of the target language Wikipedia?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong experimental evaluation. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating a practical approach for cross-lingual NER that requires minimal resources. However, the lack of comparison with modern cross-lingual methods (like multilingual transformers) is a significant limitation that should be addressed in a revision.

The paper would also benefit from more discussion about the practical implications of their approach. For example, how would this approach scale to hundreds of languages? How would the authors handle languages where the Wikipedia is not well-maintained or has many inaccuracies?

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP with practical value, but it would be strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above.
