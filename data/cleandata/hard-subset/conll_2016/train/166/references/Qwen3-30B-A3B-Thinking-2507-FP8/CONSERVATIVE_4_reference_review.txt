

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The authors claim their method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, no parallel text or native speaker interaction, and significantly outperforms existing direct transfer methods on CoNLL languages and low-resource languages.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating that wikifier features (FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) provide useful language-independent signals for cross-lingual NER
2. Providing a thorough evaluation across multiple languages (including low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts)
3. Showing that Wikipedia size directly impacts the quality of wikifier features

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides extensive experiments across 9 languages (4 CoNLL languages and 5 low-resource languages), which is a significant strength. The analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance (Section 4.4) is particularly valuable and demonstrates thoughtful empirical investigation.

2. **Strong Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare against multiple relevant baselines, including Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), and show consistent improvements across languages. The comparison with Zhang et al. (2016) is especially compelling as their method requires native speaker interaction and parallel text, while the proposed method requires only Wikipedia.

3. **Clear Analysis of Feature Importance**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of how different feature combinations affect performance (Table 2), including the critical observation that wikifier features are particularly valuable for non-Latin script languages where lexical features fail.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Approach**: The fundamental idea of using Wikipedia categories as features for NER is not new. Kazama and Torisawa (2007) already demonstrated this approach for monolingual NER, and the paper acknowledges this but fails to adequately differentiate their contribution. The paper's "innovation" is merely extending an existing idea to cross-lingual settings without demonstrating substantial methodological improvements over prior work. The authors don't provide evidence that their cross-lingual wikifier modifications (Section 3.3) are necessary or superior to simply applying existing wikification to n-grams.

2. **Insufficient Evidence for Claims**: The paper claims "significant improvements" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance gains, which is a standard requirement in NLP. The paper also doesn't adequately address why their approach would be better than other existing methods that use Wikipedia for cross-lingual NER without parallel text, such as Nothman et al. (2012), which the authors acknowledge but don't properly compare against.

3. **Misrepresentation of Requirements**: The paper claims their method "requires no sentence-aligned or word-aligned parallel text," but they do rely on the English Wikipedia as a parallel resource (the same content in different languages). This is not a genuine advantage over methods that use parallel data, as the English Wikipedia is essentially a massive parallel corpus. The paper fails to address this limitation.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: While the paper shows that wikifier features improve performance, it doesn't provide ablation studies to show why these features work better than other language-independent features (e.g., cross-lingual word embeddings). Without this, it's unclear whether the authors' specific feature choice is truly beneficial or just coincidentally effective.

5. **Overstated Claims about Low-Resource Languages**: The paper claims their approach "significantly outperforms the state of the art" on low-resource languages, but the performance on Bengali and Tamil (Table 2) is still quite poor (below 7% F1). The paper doesn't adequately address why their method struggles with these languages despite claiming to work "for all languages in Wikipedia."

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper acknowledges Kazama and Torisawa (2007) as prior work using Wikipedia categories for monolingual NER. Can you provide a clear explanation of how your work differs substantially from theirs, beyond simply extending it to cross-lingual settings?

2. The paper claims to require "no parallel text," yet relies on the English Wikipedia as a parallel resource. How do you address the fact that the English Wikipedia is itself a form of parallel text (with multilingual content), and how does this compare to methods that use explicitly aligned parallel data?

3. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance improvements. Can you provide this testing to support your claims of "significant improvements"?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed evaluation of a cross-lingual NER approach using wikifier features. The empirical analysis is thorough, and the paper demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across multiple languages. However, the core methodological contribution is not novel, and the paper makes claims that are not sufficiently supported by evidence or properly contextualized within existing literature. The paper would be significantly stronger with a clearer differentiation from prior work, statistical significance testing, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding non-Latin script languages.

While the evaluation is strong, the lack of methodological novelty and insufficient evidence for key claims prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper would benefit from more substantial methodological innovation or a more rigorous comparison with prior work that uses similar approaches to Wikipedia-based features. As it stands, the paper appears to present a minor extension of existing techniques rather than a significant contribution to the field.
