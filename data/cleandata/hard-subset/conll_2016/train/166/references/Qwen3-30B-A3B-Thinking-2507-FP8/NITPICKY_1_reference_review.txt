

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The approach trains on English data and transfers to multiple languages without requiring parallel data. The authors claim significant improvements over direct transfer methods on CoNLL languages and low-resource languages.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification.
2. Comprehensive evaluation across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages, demonstrating robust performance.
3. Analysis showing the relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality, explaining performance variations across languages.

## Strengths
1. The core idea of using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER is well-motivated and novel. The paper provides a clear example in Figure 1 showing how FreeBase types provide valuable signals even with imperfect disambiguation.

2. The evaluation is thorough, covering both CoNLL languages (English, German, Spanish, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), demonstrating the model's versatility.

3. The analysis in Section 4.4 on the relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality is insightful and provides valuable practical guidance for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Reference Omission**: The paper cites "Zhang et al. (2016)" multiple times (in sections 4.3 and 4.4) but fails to include this reference in the bibliography. This is a fundamental error in academic writing that undermines the paper's credibility and makes verification of claims impossible. The authors must provide this reference or remove the citation.

2. **Missing Tables and Figures**: The paper references Table 1 (in Section 3.1) and Figure 2 (in Section 4.5), but neither is included in the provided text. Table 1 is crucial for understanding the feature set, and Figure 2 is essential for interpreting the training language analysis. These omissions make it impossible to properly evaluate the paper's methodology and results.

3. **Terminology Inconsistency**: The paper uses "FreeBase types" and "Wikipedia categories" interchangeably throughout, despite these being distinct concepts. FreeBase is a knowledge base with its own type system, while Wikipedia categories are a different form of organization. This confusion weakens the technical precision of the paper.

4. **Lack of Justification for Key Parameters**: The paper states "We set n to 4 in all our experiments" in Section 3.3 but provides no explanation for why this specific value was chosen. The authors should justify this choice or present ablation studies showing the impact of different n-gram sizes.

5. **Ambiguous Feature Explanation**: The "previous tags features" mentioned in the monolingual experiments ("Wikifier only" row) are never clearly defined. The paper states "This is intended to show the predictive power of wikifier features alone. Without using any lexical features, it gets good scores..." but it's unclear how these "previous tags features" differ from the wikifier features or why they're included in this baseline.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why was n=4 specifically chosen for the n-gram size? What experiments were conducted to justify this choice, and how do different values affect performance?

2. Can you provide the missing Table 1 that summarizes the features used in the model? This is critical for understanding the feature set.

3. The paper states "We use the system proposed in Tsai and Roth (2016)" but doesn't explain the modifications made to this system. Could you provide more details on the specific modifications required for the n-gram grounding?

4. The paper refers to "FreeBase types" and "Wikipedia categories" as if they were interchangeable, but they are distinct concepts. Could you clarify the difference between these two in the paper?

## Overall Assessment

Despite the paper's promising approach and comprehensive evaluation, the critical errors (missing reference, missing tables, terminology confusion) significantly undermine its credibility. The reference omission alone is a serious flaw that would prevent acceptance at a top-tier conference like CoNLL. The missing Table 1 and Figure 2 make it impossible to properly evaluate the methodology and results. The terminology confusion between FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories further damages the paper's technical precision.

While the core idea is interesting, the paper's presentation lacks the rigor expected in a conference paper. The authors should address these fundamental issues before considering submission to a major conference. The minor flaws identified are not just cosmetic but affect the paper's scientific validity and reproducibility.

**Recommendation: Reject**
