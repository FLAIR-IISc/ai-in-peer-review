

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to extract language-independent features from English Wikipedia. The key insight is that grounding words and phrases to English Wikipedia entries (and thus obtaining FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) provides valuable entity information regardless of the input language. The model is trained on annotated data in source languages (e.g., English) and applied to target languages without requiring target-language annotations.

The main contributions as I see them, ordered by strength:

1. **Demonstration of cross-lingual wikifier features as effective language-independent features for NER**: The paper shows that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories extracted via cross-lingual wikification provide strong signals for NER across languages, significantly outperforming direct transfer baselines.

2. **Analysis of feature effectiveness across language types**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of how feature performance varies between Latin-script languages (where lexical features work well) and non-Latin-script languages (where wikifier features become dominant).

3. **Validation of the approach on diverse language sets**: The method is evaluated on CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), demonstrating broad applicability.

## Strengths

1. **Novel feature extraction approach**: The paper introduces a simple yet effective method for obtaining language-independent features through cross-lingual wikification. This approach is distinct from prior work that either requires parallel data (projection) or language-specific resources. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these features outperform existing direct transfer methods across multiple languages.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across language types**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of performance across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages, including non-Latin script languages. The findings that wikifier features dominate for non-Latin script languages (where lexical features fail) represent an important practical insight for cross-lingual NER.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The method consistently outperforms strong baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) across all languages tested. The improvements are statistically significant, with gains of 10+ points on Dutch, German, and Turkish compared to base features.

4. **Validation of cross-lingual applicability**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed features work not only for direct transfer (training on English, testing on other languages) but also improve monolingual models. This dual applicability significantly broadens the contribution's scope.

5. **Insightful analysis of Wikipedia size impact**: The ablation study (Table 3) clearly demonstrates that the quality of wikifier features depends on Wikipedia size and interlanguage links. This provides valuable practical guidance for applying the method to new languages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for feature effectiveness**: While the paper demonstrates that wikifier features work well, it doesn't explain *why* FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories serve as good signals for NER across languages. The connection between Wikipedia categories and standard NER types (PER, LOC, ORG) isn't sufficiently justified.

2. **Insufficient analysis of wikifier limitations**: The paper assumes the wikifier works well but doesn't provide evidence of its accuracy or discuss how errors in wikification might propagate to NER. For example, how do false wikification links affect the NER performance?

3. **Limited comparison with recent Wikipedia-based approaches**: The paper mentions Nothman et al. (2012) and Al-Rfou et al. (2015) in related work but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with these approaches, despite their relevance to the problem. This weakens the novelty claim.

4. **No discussion of computational cost**: The paper modifies the wikifier to process all n-grams (not just named entities), which is computationally expensive. The authors don't address the practical implications of this for large-scale applications.

5. **Incomplete analysis of non-Latin script languages**: While the paper shows good results for non-Latin script languages, it doesn't thoroughly investigate *why* the approach works better for these languages than for Latin-script languages, beyond the script difference.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that wikifier features work well for non-Latin script languages where lexical features fail, but doesn't explain why Wikipedia categories provide better signals than lexical features for these languages. Could the authors provide more evidence or theoretical reasoning for this phenomenon?

2. The paper states that "the FreeBase types still provide valuable information" even with imperfect disambiguation (Figure 1), but doesn't quantify how much error in wikification affects NER performance. Could the authors provide error analysis showing the correlation between wikifier accuracy and NER performance?

3. The authors mention that for non-Latin script languages, the "wikifier features are the strongest signal." However, the paper doesn't explain why English gazetteers don't work for these languages. Is it solely due to script differences, or are there other factors related to cultural context or entity naming conventions?

This paper presents a well-executed and valuable contribution to cross-lingual NER. The core idea of using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features is sound and demonstrated to be effective through thorough evaluation. While the weaknesses noted are important for a complete picture, they don't undermine the paper's core contribution, which is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated across multiple languages. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP.
