

# Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that uses a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words to English Wikipedia, leveraging FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as language-independent features. The authors claim their approach outperforms direct transfer methods on both high-resource languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features for NER without requiring parallel text
2. Demonstrating that wikifier features work particularly well for non-Latin script languages
3. Showing that training on multiple source languages improves cross-lingual NER performance

## Strengths
1. The core idea of using wikifier features for cross-lingual NER is conceptually sound and potentially valuable.
2. The evaluation on diverse languages, including non-Latin scripts, provides comprehensive coverage.
3. The analysis showing the relationship between Wikipedia size and feature quality is insightful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper claims to "modify the system in the following two ways" but fails to provide sufficient technical detail about the modifications to Tsai and Roth (2016). Specifically, the paper states "for the n-grams where n > 1, we generate title candidates only according to the whole string, not individual tokens" but doesn't explain how this modification affects the model's performance or implementation. The paper also claims "it makes no sense to include all of them as features" but provides no evidence to support this claim. Without these details, the method is impossible to reproduce.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout (e.g., "wikifier" vs. "wikification," "FreeBase types" vs. "Wikipedia categories"). Table 2 contains multiple inconsistencies: the "Wiki size" column lists numbers without units (5.1M for English), the "En. intersection" column is ambiguous, and the language codes (EN, NL, DE, ES) are inconsistent with the standard ISO codes (e.g., "TR" for Turkish). The "Monolingual Experiments" section labels the row "Wikifier only" but doesn't clarify that it uses "previous tags features also" as stated in the table caption, creating confusion.

3. **Missing Critical Information**: The paper states "we set n to 4 in all our experiments" without explaining why n=4 is optimal or how this choice was determined. The paper also fails to provide essential training details (learning rate, batch size, number of epochs), making the results impossible to verify or reproduce. The description of how non-Latin scripts are handled is particularly lacking, with claims about Bengali and Tamil having "entirely non-Latin script" but no explanation of how the model processes these scripts.

4. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims to "outperform the state of the art" but fails to clearly define what the state-of-the-art methods are. The comparison with Zhang et al. (2016) is misleading because their method is not a direct transfer approach, and the paper doesn't explain why this comparison is valid. The claim that "the proposed direct transfer model outperforms the state of the art, which assumes parallel text" is unsubstantiated.

5. **Inconsistent Evaluation Metrics**: The paper states "the evaluation metric for all experiments is phrase level F1" but doesn't explain why phrase-level F1 is appropriate for cross-lingual NER, especially when comparing to prior work that likely uses token-level F1. This inconsistency undermines the validity of the comparisons.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please provide a clear explanation of how your modifications to the Tsai and Roth (2016) system specifically improve performance for cross-lingual NER, with sufficient technical detail to allow reproduction.

2. How do you handle non-Latin scripts during the wikification process for languages like Bengali and Tamil? Please provide specific details about the character encoding, tokenization, and handling of non-Latin characters.

3. Why did you choose n=4 for the n-grams? Was this the optimal value, and how did you determine it?

4. The paper claims to use "the same set of test documents as used in Zhang et al. (2016)" but doesn't provide a reference to Zhang et al. (2016) in the text. Please provide the full citation.

## Final Recommendation

Despite the interesting core idea, the paper contains numerous methodological ambiguities, inconsistent terminology, and missing critical details that significantly undermine its credibility and reproducibility. The lack of technical depth in describing the implementation, combined with formatting inconsistencies and unsubstantiated claims, makes it impossible to properly evaluate the contribution. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper due to these substantial minor flaws that collectively prevent a fair evaluation of the work.

The paper's main contribution is potentially valuable, but the presentation and technical details are insufficient to justify acceptance. I recommend rejection.
