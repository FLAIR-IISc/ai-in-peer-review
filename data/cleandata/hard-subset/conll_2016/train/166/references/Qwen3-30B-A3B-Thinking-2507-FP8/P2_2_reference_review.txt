

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia entries. The key contribution is demonstrating that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories obtained through cross-lingual wikification provide strong language-independent features for NER. The authors show that their approach works across multiple languages (including non-Latin scripts) with minimal requirements (only multilingual Wikipedia, no parallel data), outperforming direct transfer baselines on both high-resource CoNLL languages and low-resource languages.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel use of cross-lingual wikification as language-independent features for NER**: The paper's core contribution is the insight that disambiguating words to English Wikipedia entries provides valuable language-independent features for NER, which can be used across all languages in Wikipedia without requiring language-specific features or parallel data.

2. **Demonstration of effectiveness across diverse languages**: The authors provide comprehensive evaluation across 9 languages (4 CoNLL languages and 5 low-resource languages), showing consistent improvements over strong baselines, including languages with non-Latin scripts.

3. **Analysis of Wikipedia size impact on performance**: The paper provides valuable insights showing that the quality of wikifier features directly correlates with Wikipedia size for the target language, explaining performance differences across languages.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and practical approach**: The paper presents a genuinely novel way to create language-independent features for cross-lingual NER. Unlike previous methods requiring parallel data or language-specific heuristics, this approach only needs multilingual Wikipedia, making it widely applicable. The demonstration that FreeBase types work as strong NER signals even with imperfect disambiguation is particularly compelling.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach across a wide range of languages (both high and low-resource), providing comprehensive comparisons against multiple strong baselines. The results consistently show significant improvements (10+ F1 points on several languages), which strongly support the paper's claims.

3. **Insightful analysis of Wikipedia size impact**: Section 4.4 provides a particularly strong analysis showing how Wikipedia size directly affects feature quality. This explains why the approach works better for some languages than others and provides actionable insights for practitioners.

4. **Demonstrated applicability to non-Latin scripts**: The paper successfully demonstrates the approach for Bengali and Tamil (non-Latin scripts), which is a significant practical contribution since many cross-lingual NLP methods fail for such languages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), but omits comparisons with more recent advances in cross-lingual NER, particularly multilingual pre-trained models (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT). Given that these models have become the new state-of-the-art for many cross-lingual tasks, this comparison is essential for properly positioning the work.

2. **Limited theoretical justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical explanation for why FreeBase types work so well as language-independent features. A more thorough analysis of the relationship between Wikipedia categories and NER types would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Insufficient details on wikifier modifications**: The paper mentions modifying the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier to handle n-grams, but provides minimal details about these modifications. More information about how they handled n-gram processing (e.g., how they avoided linking phrases to single words) would improve reproducibility.

4. **Limited error analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of where the model fails, particularly for low-resource languages. Understanding error patterns would help identify the limitations of the approach and suggest directions for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., XLM-R) for cross-lingual NER? Given that these models have become the new state-of-the-art for many cross-lingual tasks, it's important to understand where your method fits in the current landscape.

2. Could you provide more details about the modifications you made to the Tsai and Roth wikifier for handling n-grams? Specifically, how did you determine the optimal n for n-grams, and what challenges did you face with multi-word phrases?

3. For the non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil), how did you handle tokenization? Since these languages use different scripts, the tokenization process might significantly impact the results, yet the paper doesn't discuss this.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel approach to cross-lingual NER that has significant practical value. The core contribution of using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated. The evaluation across diverse languages, including non-Latin scripts, is comprehensive and compelling. However, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and limited theoretical justification represent important weaknesses that should be addressed.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the comparison with recent approaches and provide more details about the wikifier modifications. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP with practical implications for low-resource language processing.
