

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that uses cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features. The approach grounds words in any language to English Wikipedia entries, then extracts FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for NER. The authors claim their method outperforms direct transfer baselines on CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil) without requiring parallel data or language-specific resources.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating that Wikipedia-based features can serve as effective language-independent features for cross-lingual NER
2. Providing a comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages, including non-Latin script languages
3. Showing that the approach outperforms state-of-the-art direct transfer methods without requiring parallel text or native speaker interaction

## Strengths
1. **Comprehensive multi-language evaluation**: The paper evaluates on both high-resource CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages, including non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil). This breadth of evaluation is valuable for establishing the approach's generalizability. The results show that the method performs well even on languages with limited Wikipedia resources, which is a significant practical contribution.

2. **Clear analysis of Wikipedia size impact**: The paper provides a thoughtful analysis of how Wikipedia size affects performance, particularly through Table 3. The demonstration that reducing interlanguage links significantly degrades performance for languages like Spanish and German (from 40.04 to 29.44 F1) provides strong evidence that the Wikipedia features are indeed language-independent.

3. **Practical implementation**: The approach only requires a multilingual Wikipedia dump, making it highly accessible for practical applications. This is a significant advantage over methods requiring parallel corpora or native speaker interaction, as shown in the comparison with Zhang et al. (2016).

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient novelty validation**: The paper's core contribution—using cross-lingual wikification features for NER—is not sufficiently distinguished from prior work. Kazama and Torisawa (2007) already used Wikipedia categories for monolingual NER, and the paper fails to clearly articulate how their cross-lingual approach differs in a meaningful way from this earlier work. The related work section doesn't adequately address this gap, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed method.

2. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports F1 improvements across multiple languages but fails to provide statistical significance tests for these improvements. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it's impossible to determine whether the reported improvements (e.g., +10 points on Dutch, German) are statistically meaningful or could be due to random variation in the test data.

3. **Inadequate comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against Täckström et al. (2012) and Zhang et al. (2016), both of which are now somewhat dated (2012 and 2016, respectively). The field of cross-lingual NER has advanced significantly since then, with more recent approaches like multilingual BERT and XLM-R. The paper fails to compare against these modern baselines, which weakens the claim that their method is state-of-the-art.

4. **Limited analysis of feature effectiveness**: While the paper claims wikifier features are "language-independent," it doesn't provide concrete examples of how these features function across different languages. The analysis in Section 4.4 is superficial, showing only that Wikipedia size affects performance, but not how the specific features (FreeBase types, Wikipedia categories) contribute to the NER task in different linguistic contexts.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide statistical significance tests for your reported improvements over baselines, particularly for the low-resource language experiments?
2. How does your approach specifically differ from Kazama and Torisawa (2007), which also used Wikipedia categories for NER? What makes your cross-lingual approach a significant advancement over their monolingual work?
3. Given the recent advances in multilingual language models (e.g., XLM-R, mBERT), how would your approach compare to these state-of-the-art methods that don't require language-specific resources?

## Final Recommendation
While the paper presents a practical approach that works well across multiple languages, the lack of sufficient novelty validation, statistical rigor, and comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods significantly weakens the contribution. The evaluation is comprehensive, but the claims of innovation are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. I recommend rejection unless the authors can provide stronger evidence of novelty and address the statistical significance concerns.
