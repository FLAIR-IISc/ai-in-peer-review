

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to cross-lingual NER by using a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia, thereby generating language-independent features for NER. The key insight is reversing the traditional pipeline (where NER is performed first, then wikification), instead using wikification to inform NER. By grounding n-grams to English Wikipedia, the authors extract FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features that work across languages. The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, no parallel text, and outperforms existing direct transfer methods on CoNLL languages and shows promising results on low-resource languages.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Fundamental Reversal of NER-Wikification Pipeline**: The core contribution is recognizing that wikification can provide valuable signals for NER, rather than being a downstream task of NER. This represents a conceptual shift in how we approach cross-lingual NER.

2. **Language-Independent Feature Generation**: The paper demonstrates that FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories from cross-lingual wikification provide strong language-independent features for NER, eliminating the need for language-specific features or parallel data.

3. **Practical Implementation with Minimal Requirements**: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, making it widely applicable to any language with Wikipedia coverage, and it works without requiring parallel text or sentence alignment.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty**: The paper's core idea—using wikification to inform NER rather than the reverse—is genuinely novel and bold. It represents a fundamental shift in how we think about the relationship between NER and entity linking, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors correctly identify this as a key distinction from previous work.

2. **Simplicity and Broad Applicability**: The approach is remarkably simple—only requiring multilingual Wikipedia—yet demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple languages, including those with non-Latin scripts. This simplicity makes it highly practical and widely applicable, which is a major strength.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines. The analysis of why wikifier features work better for some languages (e.g., why they're particularly effective for non-Latin script languages) adds significant insight.

4. **Theoretical Insight**: The paper provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the approach works—language-independent features from Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types provide strong signals for NER even with imperfect disambiguation. This insight is valuable beyond just the specific application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: While the paper compares with Zhang et al. (2016), it doesn't compare with more recent cross-lingual NER approaches that might have emerged since their work. A comparison with more recent SOTA would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Discussion on Wikipedia Size Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that Wikipedia size affects performance (Table 3), but doesn't explore how to handle languages with no Wikipedia or extremely small Wikipedia (like Yoruba). This is a significant limitation that should be addressed.

3. **Limited Exploration of the Approach's Boundaries**: The paper doesn't explore the limits of the approach—such as how it would perform on languages with no Wikipedia coverage or for entities that don't have Wikipedia entries. This would help readers understand the scope of the method.

4. **Potential Overestimation of "Language Independence"**: The paper claims the features are language-independent, but the results show significant variation based on Wikipedia size, suggesting the features are more language-dependent than claimed. A more nuanced discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you extend this approach to languages with no Wikipedia or very small Wikipedia (e.g., languages with only a few hundred articles)? Would you consider incorporating other knowledge sources?

2. The paper shows that for non-Latin script languages, wikifier features alone perform best. Could you elaborate on why lexical features perform so poorly for these languages, and whether this suggests a fundamental limitation of the approach?

3. The paper mentions that "other information from Wikipedia which we do not use, such as words from documents and relations between titles, would require additional research." Could you provide a brief roadmap of how these additional sources might be incorporated?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to cross-lingual NER that reverses the traditional pipeline and leverages Wikipedia in a fundamentally different way. The simplicity of the approach, combined with its strong empirical results across multiple languages, makes it a significant contribution to the field. While the paper has some limitations regarding Wikipedia size constraints and comparison with recent work, these don't undermine the core novelty and value of the approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe the paper's core contribution is sufficiently strong to warrant acceptance. The conceptual shift from "NER first, then wikification" to "wikification first, then NER" is bold and valuable, and the empirical results validate its practical utility.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above. The paper's novelty and potential impact on cross-lingual NLP outweigh its methodological limitations.
