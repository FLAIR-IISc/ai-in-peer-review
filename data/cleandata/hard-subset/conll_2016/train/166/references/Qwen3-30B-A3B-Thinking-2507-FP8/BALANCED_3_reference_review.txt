

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types) for NER. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel approach to extracting language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification, without requiring parallel data or language-specific resources
2. Comprehensive evaluation across both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), demonstrating significant improvements over direct transfer baselines
3. Analysis of how Wikipedia size impacts the quality of wikifier features, providing practical insights for real-world implementation

## Strengths

1. **Novel and practical use of cross-lingual wikification**: The paper makes a compelling argument that cross-lingual wikification can provide language-independent features for NER, which is a significant departure from traditional approaches that rely on lexical features or parallel data. The authors convincingly demonstrate through Figure 1 that even with imperfect disambiguation, Wikipedia categories and FreeBase types provide strong signals for NER. This approach is elegant in its simplicity, requiring only multilingual Wikipedia dumps as a resource.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation across diverse languages**: The paper evaluates on both high-resource CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (including non-Latin scripts), providing a more thorough assessment than many cross-lingual NER papers. The results are particularly impressive for low-resource languages where the approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods that require parallel data or native speaker interaction.

3. **Insightful analysis of Wikipedia size impact**: Table 3 provides valuable analysis showing how Wikipedia size directly affects the quality of wikifier features. This is an important practical consideration that goes beyond typical benchmarking and offers actionable insights for practitioners about which languages might benefit most from the approach.

4. **Demonstrated effectiveness on non-Latin scripts**: The paper's success with Bengali and Tamil (non-Latin scripts) is particularly noteworthy, as these pose significant challenges for most cross-lingual NER approaches. The authors show that wikifier features are especially valuable for these languages, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper primarily compares with older methods (Täckström et al., 2012 and Zhang et al., 2016), while more recent neural cross-lingual approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT-based models) have achieved substantial improvements. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the current significance of the proposed approach.

2. **Insufficient analysis of wikifier limitations**: While the paper demonstrates the value of wikifier features, it doesn't thoroughly analyze when and why the wikifier fails. For example, it lacks specific examples of mislinked words that negatively impact NER performance, and how these errors propagate through the model. A deeper analysis of the wikifier's limitations would strengthen the paper's claims.

3. **Lack of systematic feature ablation studies**: The paper shows that adding wikifier features improves results, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of which specific features (FreeBase types vs. Wikipedia categories, coarse vs. fine-grained types) contribute most to the performance gain. This would help understand what aspects of the wikifier are most valuable.

4. **Methodological details of wikifier adaptation**: The paper mentions modifying the Tsai and Roth (2016) wikifier but doesn't provide sufficient detail about these modifications. It's unclear how significant these changes are or whether they could be applied to other wikification systems. The paper also doesn't discuss how the choice of n-gram size (up to 4) affects performance, which could be important for efficiency.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach compare to recent neural cross-lingual NER models (e.g., those using multilingual BERT) that have become dominant in the field? Have you considered adapting your method to work with these more recent architectures?

2. In Table 2, the authors show that for Bengali and Tamil, the wikifier-only approach performs better than using base features. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, especially since these languages use non-Latin scripts? Is there something specific about the wikifier features that makes them particularly well-suited for these languages?

3. The paper mentions that for low-resource languages like Bengali and Tamil, FreeBase doesn't contain entities, so they remove the FreeBase query step. Could you elaborate on how this affects the quality of the features, and whether there are alternative ways to obtain language-independent features for these languages?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and practical approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification, with strong empirical results across diverse languages. The main contributions are significant, particularly for low-resource languages. However, the lack of comparison with recent neural approaches and insufficient analysis of the wikifier's limitations represent substantial weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would be significantly strengthened by incorporating these elements and providing a more comprehensive comparison with the current state of the art.

While the work is promising and addresses an important problem, the current version doesn't fully establish its significance in the context of recent advances in the field. With the suggested improvements, this could be a valuable contribution to the cross-lingual NLP community.
