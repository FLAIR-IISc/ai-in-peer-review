

# Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features from Wikipedia. The core idea is to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia entries (via a cross-lingual wikifier), then use the resulting FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features for training NER models. The approach requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, no parallel text or target language annotations. The authors evaluate on CoNLL languages (English, German, Spanish, Dutch) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), showing significant improvements over direct transfer baselines.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel approach to cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories), demonstrating that these features are valuable even with imperfect disambiguation.
2. A practical modification to the wikifier to process all n-grams (not just named entities), enabling extraction of features from every word in the text.
3. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (including low-resource ones with non-Latin scripts), showing consistent improvements over strong baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Strong practical motivation and simplicity:** The paper addresses a critical problem in NLP (low-resource language NER) with a simple yet effective approach that requires minimal resources (only multilingual Wikipedia). The demonstration that the approach works particularly well for non-Latin script languages (Bengali, Tamil) is highly valuable, as these languages often suffer from poor NER performance due to lack of resources. The "Wikifier only" results for Bengali and Tamil (41.81 and 27.85 F1) are stronger than the "Base Features" results (2.30 and 1.74), providing compelling evidence of the approach's value.

2. **Thorough evaluation:** The authors conduct extensive experiments across multiple languages (CoNLL and low-resource), comparing against multiple baselines (Täckström, Zhang et al.). The evaluation includes both monolingual and cross-lingual settings, providing strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness. The analysis of training on multiple languages (EN+ES+NL+DE) is particularly compelling, showing a 1+ point F1 improvement over English-only training.

3. **Clear demonstration of language-independent features:** The paper convincingly shows that wikifier features are language-independent and can outperform lexical features for languages with non-Latin scripts. The authors' insight that "for languages with non-Latin scripts, no lexical feature in English will fire at test time" is a key contribution to understanding cross-lingual NER challenges.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of the approach's limitations:** The paper shows that the approach performs worse for languages with smaller Wikipedia coverage (Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why this happens or how to mitigate it. The "Quality of Wikifier Features" section is too brief to be convincing. The authors should discuss how to handle languages with minimal Wikipedia coverage, as this is a critical limitation for real-world applications. The paper claims "the overall performance is not as good as the CoNLL experiments" for low-resource languages but doesn't explore why.

2. **Insufficient differentiation from prior work:** While the paper claims novelty in using wikifier features for NER, it doesn't clearly distinguish its approach from Kazama and Torisawa (2007), who used Wikipedia category features for NER, or Sil and Yates (2013), who created a joint model for NER and entity linking. The authors should better explain how their approach differs in a way that constitutes a meaningful contribution, particularly regarding the processing of all n-grams rather than just named entities.

3. **Lack of analysis on wikifier accuracy impact:** The paper states that "even though disambiguation is not perfect, the FreeBase types still provide valuable information," but doesn't quantify how much the wikifier's errors affect NER performance. This is a critical gap, as the approach's effectiveness depends on the quality of the wikification. The authors should provide error analysis showing the correlation between wikifier accuracy and NER performance.

4. **No discussion of computational cost:** The paper doesn't address the computational cost of processing all n-grams for wikification, which could be significant for larger texts. This is an important practical consideration for real-world applications that the authors should discuss, especially given the paper's focus on low-resource languages where computational resources may be limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of your model scale with the size of the target language's Wikipedia? Could you provide more detailed analysis on the correlation between Wikipedia size and NER performance, particularly for the low-resource languages?

2. The paper mentions that FreeBase doesn't contain entities in Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil. How would the approach be affected if FreeBase were expanded to support these languages, and what would be the practical challenges of implementing such an expansion?

3. Could you provide more details on the specific FreeBase types that contribute most to the NER performance? This would help understand which aspects of the semantic information are most useful for cross-lingual NER.

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a simple yet effective approach to cross-lingual NER that requires minimal resources. The core contribution of using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features is valuable, especially for low-resource languages. While there are some limitations in the analysis of the approach's weaknesses and comparison to prior work, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is well-suited for CONLL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly the need for more thorough analysis of the approach's limitations and better differentiation from prior work.
