

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that uses cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features from Wikipedia. The core idea is to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia entries, leveraging FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories as features that work across languages without requiring parallel text. The authors demonstrate their method on CoNLL languages (English, German, Spanish, Dutch) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), showing that their approach significantly outperforms direct transfer baselines.

The main contributions I identify are:
1. A novel application of cross-lingual wikification as a source of language-independent features for NER that requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps (no parallel text needed)
2. Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple language types, including non-Latin scripts, demonstrating that wikifier features provide meaningful signals even when lexical features fail
3. An analysis showing that the quality of wikifier features directly correlates with Wikipedia size in the target language, providing valuable insights into the approach's limitations and applicability

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Practicality**: The paper presents a straightforward, well-motivated approach that leverages existing wikification technology to solve a significant problem in cross-lingual NER. The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, making it practical and accessible for real-world applications. The evaluation on low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil) demonstrates the approach's utility where most other methods fail.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation across multiple dimensions: monolingual NER, direct transfer across languages, Wikipedia size impact, and multiple training languages. The comparison with strong baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) is convincing, with consistent improvements across languages.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Feature Quality**: The analysis in Section 4.4 (quality of wikifier features) is particularly valuable. By showing how Wikipedia size affects performance and demonstrating the impact of removing FreeBase features or interlanguage links, the authors provide concrete evidence for when and why their approach works best.

4. **Demonstrated Utility for Non-Latin Scripts**: The paper shows that for languages with non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), the wikifier features alone outperform lexical features, which is a significant contribution given the challenges of cross-lingual NER for these languages.

5. **Clear Methodology**: The paper clearly explains the modifications to the existing wikifier system to handle n-gram processing and the feature extraction pipeline, making it reproducible and understandable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: While the application of wikification to NER is useful, the core idea is not entirely novel. Kazama and Torisawa (2007) already used Wikipedia category features for monolingual NER, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its technical innovation from prior work. The paper's contribution is more about demonstrating the utility of this approach in cross-lingual settings rather than introducing a fundamentally new method.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Wikifier Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that disambiguation is "not perfect" but doesn't provide any analysis of failure modes or their impact on NER performance. For example, how does the system handle proper names not present in Wikipedia? How does it handle ambiguous terms that wikifiers might incorrectly disambiguate?

3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of wikifying every n-gram in a document, which could be significant for large-scale applications. The authors mention modifying the wikifier system but don't discuss the time or resource overhead.

4. **Low Absolute Performance on Some Languages**: For some low-resource languages (e.g., Yoruba, Bengali), the direct transfer results are quite low (e.g., 6.72 F1 on Yoruba in direct transfer), which might not be practically useful. The paper acknowledges the Wikipedia size limitation but doesn't propose solutions to overcome this constraint.

5. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed error analysis to understand where the system fails (e.g., specific entity types or language-specific challenges), which would help identify the approach's limitations and potential improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that wikifier features work particularly well for non-Latin scripts, but doesn't explain why. Could the authors provide more insight into why these features are more effective for non-Latin scripts than lexical features?

2. The paper claims "no sentence-aligned or word-aligned parallel text is needed," but does rely on English Wikipedia as a reference. Could the authors clarify whether they consider English Wikipedia as part of the "parallel text" requirement or not?

3. The authors convert MISC tags to LOC for some entities in low-resource languages. Could they explain their criteria for this conversion and whether they tested alternative approaches (e.g., keeping MISC tags or using a different mapping)?

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a practical approach to cross-lingual NER that demonstrates significant improvements over baselines. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the comprehensive evaluation and clear analysis of feature quality provide valuable insights for the field. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the limitations of the wikifier and computational considerations.
