

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER model that uses cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The model is trained on annotated data in source languages (primarily English) and directly applied to target languages without requiring parallel text or language-specific resources. The authors demonstrate that grounding words to English Wikipedia provides strong signals for NER across multiple languages.

Main contributions as I see them:
1. A novel method to generate language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification, which provides strong performance improvements across multiple languages.
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing significant improvements over strong baselines on both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil).
3. Insightful analysis demonstrating that the quality of wikifier features directly correlates with Wikipedia size in the target language.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-executed core idea**: The paper's central contribution—using cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features—is both novel and well-supported by evidence. Figure 1 effectively demonstrates how FreeBase types provide strong NER signals even with imperfect disambiguation. The authors convincingly show that these features are useful for both cross-lingual transfer and monolingual NER, which significantly strengthens the contribution.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, covering both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages. The comparison to strong baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) is well-executed and shows consistent improvements across languages. The authors' analysis of feature quality based on Wikipedia size (Section 4.4) adds significant value beyond simple performance reporting.

3. **Practical simplicity**: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps as input, making it highly practical for real-world deployment. The authors' demonstration that training on multiple languages (EN+ES+NL+DE) further improves results (Table 4) is a valuable practical insight.

4. **Insightful analysis of language differences**: The paper provides important insights about language-specific performance differences, showing that Latin-script languages benefit from combined lexical and wikifier features, while non-Latin languages rely primarily on wikifier features. This has clear implications for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to recent Wikipedia-based approaches**: The paper mentions related work using Wikipedia (Nothman et al., 2012; Al-Rfou et al., 2015), but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with these approaches. For example, how does their method compare to the silver-standard data generation approach in Nothman et al. (2012)? This weakens the novelty claim and makes it harder to assess the method's relative contribution.

2. **Underdeveloped handling of non-Latin scripts**: The paper acknowledges that the approach performs poorly for non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), but doesn't propose any solutions for this limitation. For languages with entirely non-Latin scripts, the authors' approach is severely limited, which is a significant drawback for real-world applicability given the global language landscape.

3. **Lack of wikifier robustness analysis**: The paper relies on a cross-lingual wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016), but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of its performance, especially for low-resource languages. Without understanding the wikifier's limitations, it's difficult to fully assess the contribution. For example, how does the wikifier perform on Yoruba (with only 31K articles) compared to Spanish (with 1.3M articles)?

4. **Limited discussion of scalability**: While the authors claim their method "can be applied to all languages in Wikipedia," they don't address how the method scales to languages with extremely small Wikipedia sizes (like Yoruba with 31K articles). The paper shows that performance drops significantly for these languages, but doesn't explore potential solutions.

5. **Insufficient detail on wikifier modifications**: The authors mention modifying the Tsai and Roth wikifier, but provide minimal details about these modifications (Section 3.3). More information about how they handle n-grams and the ranking model would strengthen the reproducibility of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their method "requires no parallel text or language-specific resources," but Wikipedia is a language-specific resource. Can they clarify what they mean by "language-specific resources" in this context?

2. For languages with small Wikipedia sizes (like Yoruba), the authors show that their method doesn't perform as well. Have they explored ways to augment the Wikipedia data for these languages (e.g., using other multilingual resources)?

3. In Section 4.4, the authors show that removing FreeBase queries significantly degrades performance for high-resource languages. How does this impact the method's applicability to languages where FreeBase doesn't contain entities (like Bengali, Tamil, and Yoruba)?

4. The paper demonstrates that Spanish is the best training language for Yoruba (Figure 2). What characteristics of Spanish make it particularly suitable for training a model to recognize Yoruba entities?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper with a strong core contribution that demonstrates a novel and practical approach to cross-lingual NER. The empirical results are compelling, and the analysis of language-specific performance differences adds significant value. However, the paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent Wikipedia-based approaches and a more detailed discussion of the limitations for non-Latin script languages.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the limitations regarding non-Latin scripts and the lack of robustness analysis for the wikifier are significant. Addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above)
