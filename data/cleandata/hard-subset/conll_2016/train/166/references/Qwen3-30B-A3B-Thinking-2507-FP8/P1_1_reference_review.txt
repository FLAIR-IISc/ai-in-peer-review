

# Peer Review: Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground n-grams to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The authors claim this method requires only multilingual Wikipedia, not parallel text or language-specific heuristics. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel method for generating language-independent features for cross-lingual NER using cross-lingual wikification without requiring parallel text or language-specific rules.
2. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple languages (including CoNLL languages and five low-resource languages with non-Latin scripts), demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines.
3. Insightful analysis of how Wikipedia size impacts feature quality, providing practical guidance for when the method is most effective.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Feature Engineering Approach**: The paper's core contribution—using cross-lingual wikification to generate language-independent features—is elegant and addresses a key challenge in cross-lingual NER. By grounding words to English Wikipedia and extracting FreeBase types, the authors create features that work across languages without requiring parallel data. This is particularly valuable for low-resource languages where parallel data is scarce. The approach is simple yet effective, as evidenced by the consistent performance gains across all languages tested.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The evaluation spans both high-resource (CoNLL languages) and low-resource languages (including Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, and Tamil with non-Latin scripts), providing strong evidence of the method's broad applicability. The paper shows significant improvements over strong baselines (Täckström et al. and Zhang et al.) on all languages, with particularly impressive results on low-resource languages where Wikipedia size is limited. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods in both direct transfer and monolingual settings is thorough.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Feature Quality**: The paper provides a valuable analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality. By experimenting with removing FreeBase support and reducing interlanguage links, the authors demonstrate a clear relationship between Wikipedia size and performance. This analysis helps practitioners understand the method's limitations and when it's most likely to succeed, which is crucial for real-world application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete Analysis of Wikifier Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges that wikifier performance depends on Wikipedia size, it fails to thoroughly analyze how the wikifier's accuracy directly impacts NER results. For instance, the paper doesn't quantify how much NER performance degrades when the wikifier makes incorrect disambiguations. This is a significant gap, as the quality of the wikifier is the foundation of the proposed approach. Without understanding this relationship, it's difficult to assess the method's reliability in real-world scenarios.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with Täckström et al. (2012) is problematic. Täckström's method uses word clusters induced from parallel data, which is a fundamentally different approach. The paper claims their method is "orthogonal" and could be combined, but they don't actually test this combination. Instead, they compare against a baseline that doesn't use parallel data (Täckström baseline), making the comparison unfair. A more compelling comparison would include the Täckström bitext clusters result (which uses parallel data) and show whether combining both approaches yields even better results.

3. **Monolingual Performance Gap**: The monolingual experiments show that the authors' model doesn't reach state-of-the-art performance (even with wikifier features added). This is a significant limitation, as the paper claims their features are valuable for monolingual NER too. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a clear explanation for why their features underperform compared to the best monolingual models, which undermines their claim about the general usefulness of their features.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the accuracy of the cross-lingual wikifier specifically affect NER performance? For example, if the wikifier has a 10% error rate in disambiguation, how much would that degrade the NER performance? This would help assess the robustness of your approach.

2. The paper states your method is orthogonal to Täckström's word clustering approach, but doesn't test a combined model. Have you considered combining both approaches? If not, what would be the main technical challenges in doing so?

3. In the monolingual experiments, your model doesn't reach state-of-the-art performance. What are the main limitations of your feature set that prevent achieving better results, and how might these be addressed in future work?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual NER that requires minimal resources (just multilingual Wikipedia). The comprehensive evaluation across diverse languages and the insightful analysis of Wikipedia size effects are significant contributions. While the baseline comparison could be strengthened and the wikifier's impact on performance needs further analysis, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above. The method has clear practical value for low-resource language NER where parallel data is scarce.
