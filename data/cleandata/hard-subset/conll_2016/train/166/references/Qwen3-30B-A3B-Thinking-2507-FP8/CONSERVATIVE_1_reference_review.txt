

# Review of "Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Wikification"

## Summary
This paper proposes a cross-lingual NER approach that leverages a cross-lingual wikifier to ground words and phrases to English Wikipedia, extracting language-independent features (FreeBase types and Wikipedia categories) for NER. The authors claim their method requires no parallel text or language-specific features, only multilingual Wikipedia. They evaluate on CoNLL languages (English, Spanish, Dutch, German) and five low-resource languages (Turkish, Tagalog, Yoruba, Bengali, Tamil), reporting improvements over direct transfer baselines.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The application of cross-lingual wikification to extract language-independent features for NER (though not entirely novel)
2. Demonstration of consistent improvements over direct transfer baselines across multiple languages
3. Analysis of how Wikipedia size affects feature quality, particularly for low-resource languages

## Strengths
1. **Comprehensive multi-language evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across both high-resource CoNLL languages and low-resource languages, including non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil). This broad evaluation is valuable for understanding the method's generalizability.

2. **Insightful analysis of feature quality**: The paper provides a clear explanation of why wikifier features work better for some languages than others (based on Wikipedia size), which is an important contribution to understanding cross-lingual NER limitations.

3. **Practical implementation**: The method requires only multilingual Wikipedia dumps, making it straightforward to implement without specialized resources like parallel text or language-specific tools. This accessibility is a strength for practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient comparison with relevant baselines**: The core idea of using Wikipedia categories for NER is not new (Kazama and Torisawa, 2007), and the paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from existing work. They compare with Täckström (2012) but don't compare with more recent relevant work like Zhang et al. (2016), which they cite as a baseline for low-resource languages. The paper also doesn't compare with silver-standard data generation approaches (Nothman et al., 2012), which would be a more direct comparison.

2. **Questionable novelty of the "cross-lingual wikifier"**: The paper uses a pre-existing wikifier (Tsai and Roth, 2016) with minimal modifications (only changing candidate generation for n-grams). The paper doesn't make significant technical contributions to the wikification system itself, yet presents it as a key innovation. This is a major weakness in a paper claiming novelty.

3. **Inadequate justification for the language-independent claim**: The features are derived from English Wikipedia, so they're not truly language-independent. The paper acknowledges that for non-Latin scripts (Bengali, Tamil), the approach relies solely on wikifier features, which undermines the claim of language-independence. The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation.

4. **Insufficient validation of the core hypothesis**: The paper claims that wikifier features provide "language-independent signals" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these features are fundamentally better than other approaches. For example, they don't show that the same features would work as well if extracted from a different language's Wikipedia (e.g., German Wikipedia for German NER).

5. **Overstated performance claims**: The paper claims "significant improvement" over state-of-the-art, but the improvements are marginal (e.g., +10 points on Dutch, German, Turkish) compared to baselines. More importantly, the paper doesn't adequately address why these improvements are meaningful in the broader context of NER research.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do the authors justify that their approach is novel when Kazama and Torisawa (2007) already used Wikipedia categories for monolingual NER, and Sil and Yates (2013) used a joint NER-entity linking model?

2. Why didn't the authors compare their approach with silver-standard data generation methods (Nothman et al., 2012), which also use Wikipedia but generate training data rather than features?

3. Given that the method relies on English Wikipedia, how can they claim these are "language-independent features" when the features are inherently tied to English's Wikipedia structure?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a practical approach to cross-lingual NER with some modest improvements over existing methods. However, the claimed novelty is questionable, the comparisons with relevant baselines are insufficient, and the methodological contributions are minimal. While the evaluation across multiple languages is valuable, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify the novelty of the core approach. I recommend rejection due to lack of significant methodological innovation and insufficient validation against relevant baselines. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its novelty claims and provide more thorough comparisons to be acceptable for publication.
