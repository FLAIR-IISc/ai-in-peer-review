## Review of "A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing"

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on hand-crafted features. The core contribution lies in demonstrating strong performance on CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks *without* the extensive feature engineering typically required for joint parsing, achieving competitive results with significantly faster runtime. While the approach builds upon existing transition-based parsing frameworks, the successful application of stack LSTMs to learn state representations for joint parsing is a notable advancement. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that provide insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing within this framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, surpassing previous joint models. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Features:** The model’s ability to achieve strong performance without relying on manually designed features is a significant advantage, simplifying the development process and potentially improving generalization.
3. **Efficient Inference:** The greedy inference algorithm results in a linear runtime, making the parser practical for real-world applications. The reported speedup compared to other systems is substantial.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The ablation studies and multilingual experiments provide a thorough understanding of the model’s strengths and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** The underlying transition system is based on existing work (Henderson et al., 2008, 2013). The primary novelty lies in the application of stack LSTMs, which, while effective, isn’t a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While competitive, the model doesn’t surpass the *best* performing systems which *do* utilize extensive feature engineering and/or optimal decoding. The paper acknowledges this, but it limits the impact of the work.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on multilingual data, particularly Chinese, relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings. This suggests a potential limitation in the model’s ability to generalize to languages with limited resources.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the oracle conversion of semantic parses to transitions is not perfect. Could you quantify the impact of this imperfect conversion on the reported results?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific choices made during hyperparameter tuning, particularly regarding the LSTM hidden state dimension and dropout rate? Were any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques employed?
3.  The paper states that the system does not incorporate morphological features. Have you explored the potential benefits of character-based word embeddings as an alternative?
