- Introduces a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state.  
- Uses learned representations to avoid expensive, handcrafted features.  
- Employs a greedy inference algorithm with linear runtime complexity, making it computationally efficient.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and is competitive with pipeline systems.  
- Demonstrates adaptability across multiple languages in multilingual evaluations.  
- Open-source implementation provided, improving reproducibility and practical utility.  
- Strength: high efficiency and fast end-to-end runtime compared to many pipeline systems.  
- Strength: elimination of handcrafted features reduces reliance on linguistic feature engineering.  
- Strength: strong empirical results, outperforming previous joint parsers.  
- Weakness: joint model shows overfitting on in-domain data (WSJ) and is less robust out-of-domain (Brown corpus).  
- Weakness: lacks comparison/integration with state-of-the-art SRL pipeline methods using global optimization and extra features.  
- Weakness: performance degrades in low-resource settings (e.g., Japanese), indicating dependence on large training data.  
- Questions: how to handle low-resource languages (transfer learning, char embeddings)?  
- Questions: can overfitting be reduced via regularization or domain adaptation?  
- Questions: how does performance change when adding linguistic/morphological features for rich morphology languages?  
- Comment: paper is well-written but could expand discussion of limitations and future directions.
