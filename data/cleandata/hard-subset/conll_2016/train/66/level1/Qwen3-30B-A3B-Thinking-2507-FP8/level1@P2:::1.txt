

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that learns representations of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel joint parsing approach using stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithm state, (2) a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, and (3) state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008-9 shared tasks.

As a reviewer, I see the primary contribution as the effective application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing, which demonstrates how neural networks can replace expert-crafted features. The second most significant contribution is the demonstration that joint parsing can be performed efficiently without expensive features. The linear-time inference is a practical contribution but not the most novel aspect.

## Strengths

1. **Significant technical contribution in representation learning**: The paper makes a compelling case for using stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state, obviating the need for expensive expert-crafted features. This represents a meaningful advance over previous joint parsing models, which relied on features derived from complete syntactic parses. The authors provide strong empirical evidence that their approach achieves better performance than previous joint models without these features, demonstrating the power of this representation learning technique.

2. **Strong empirical validation on standard benchmarks**: The paper provides comprehensive results on the CoNLL 2008-9 shared tasks, showing consistent improvements over previous joint parsing models. The ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) effectively demonstrate the value of jointly learning syntax and semantics. The results are presented clearly with appropriate metrics (LAS, semantic F1, Macro F1), making it easy to assess the model's performance.

3. **Practical efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model achieves near state-of-the-art performance while being significantly faster than competing systems (177.6 seconds vs. 439.9 seconds for Lei et al. 2015 on the same dataset). This practical advantage is particularly valuable for real-world applications where speed is critical, and it's well-supported by their runtime measurements.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of integration with state-of-the-art feature-based systems**: The authors acknowledge that their model doesn't match the performance of top systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015) that use expert-crafted features from full syntactic parses. However, they don't provide a thorough analysis of how their approach could be combined with these features to achieve even better results. The claim that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model" is too vague and doesn't address how future work could integrate these approaches.

2. **Limited novelty in parsing algorithm**: While the representation learning aspect is novel, the transition system itself is largely based on Henderson et al. (2013), with only minor modifications. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions from previous work in the parsing algorithm space, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach.

3. **Insufficient analysis of limitations**: The paper notes that the joint model overfits to WSJ in out-of-domain evaluation, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this occurs or how to address it. Without this analysis, it's unclear whether the model's limitations are inherent to the approach or could be mitigated with additional techniques. The authors also don't discuss when their model fails compared to other systems, which would help readers understand the model's strengths and weaknesses.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models but falls behind state-of-the-art systems that use expert-crafted features. How could their approach be integrated with these features to achieve even better results, and what specific features would be most beneficial?

2. The paper mentions that their Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance. How would the model perform on low-resource languages without pretrained embeddings, and what strategies could be employed to improve performance in such scenarios?

3. The authors note that their joint model overfits to WSJ in out-of-domain evaluation. Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the WSJ domain that cause this overfitting, and what techniques might help mitigate this issue?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating how stack LSTMs can effectively replace expert-crafted features in joint syntactic-semantic parsing, achieving state-of-the-art performance for joint models with significant computational advantages. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the paper would be strengthened by addressing the limitations in integration with state-of-the-art feature-based systems and providing a deeper analysis of the model's weaknesses. The work is solid and represents a meaningful advance in the field, but the current version doesn't fully explore the potential of the approach for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
