

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithm state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) demonstrating that neural networks can learn effective representations for joint parsing without relying on expensive syntactic features like dependency paths; (2) introducing a linear-time greedy inference algorithm that achieves competitive performance; and (3) showing that joint parsing can match pipeline approaches in accuracy while being significantly faster.

Strengths: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks, outperforming all previous joint parsing models. The linear time complexity (177.6±18 seconds on a single core for CoNLL 2009 English test data) is a major practical advantage, being 2.5x faster than comparable pipeline systems. The elimination of expert-crafted features represents a significant methodological contribution, showing neural networks can learn linguistic representations without linguistic feature engineering.

Weaknesses: The model does not match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), which use optimal decoding or additional data. The Joint model shows signs of overfitting to the WSJ training domain, as evidenced by the Hybrid model outperforming it on out-of-domain Brown corpus data. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the model would perform on languages with limited training data, as Japanese results (with only 4,393 training examples) show clear dependence on dataset size.

Questions to Authors: How would the model perform if pretrained embeddings were not available for non-English languages? Could the overfitting to WSJ be mitigated by incorporating additional data sources or regularization techniques? What is the computational cost of training compared to other joint models, and how does it scale to larger datasets?
