

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. Demonstrating that a fully end-to-end neural model using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state (not just top elements) can outperform previous joint parsing models without relying on expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses.

2. Providing a computationally efficient approach (linear time complexity) with a runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is significantly faster than competing systems.

3. Establishing that greedy inference with a learned state representation can achieve competitive performance in joint parsing, challenging the assumption that beam search or optimal decoding is necessary for high performance.

## Strengths

1. **Novel representation learning approach for joint parsing**: The paper makes a significant methodological contribution by demonstrating that a single neural network can learn to represent the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) without relying on hand-crafted features. This is a meaningful advance over previous work (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008) that used carefully engineered features to capture parser state. The stack LSTM approach allows the model to implicitly learn relevant state information, potentially avoiding the feature engineering bottleneck.

2. **Computational efficiency and practical utility**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their model achieves linear time complexity with a runtime that is 2.5 times faster than state-of-the-art pipeline approaches (Lei et al., 2015). This is a significant practical contribution, as many high-performing models are computationally expensive. The authors' claim that this will be "useful in practical settings" is well-supported by their runtime measurements.

3. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among *joint* parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets, outperforming previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009) and even matching the performance of top pipeline systems. The results are well-documented across multiple languages, with the model adapting well to different languages (though Chinese benefits from pretrained embeddings).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to top-performing pipeline systems**: The authors claim to outperform "all previous joint parsing models" but don't adequately address how their model compares to the highest-performing pipeline systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) that use expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses. Their model falls behind these systems by 1-3 points in semantic F1 scores, yet they don't provide a thorough analysis of why their approach can't match these results (e.g., whether it's solely due to the lack of features or fundamental limitations of their greedy approach).

2. **Insufficient analysis of the greedy inference limitation**: The paper claims that their greedy approach "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" but doesn't provide sufficient justification for why greedy inference is sufficient for this task. The authors acknowledge that their model doesn't match systems that use optimal decoding (Täckström et al., 2015), yet they don't explore whether a small beam search could yield marginal improvements with minimal computational cost. This weakens their claim that greedy inference is sufficient.

3. **Limited understanding of the model's limitations**: The paper doesn't provide enough analysis of when the model fails or why it underperforms compared to top systems. For example, they note that their joint model overfits to the WSJ domain (outperforming Hybrid on in-domain data but not out-of-domain), but they don't explore what specific aspects of their architecture cause this overfitting or how to mitigate it. The lack of error analysis or case studies limits the paper's contribution to the field.

4. **Overstated claims about "no expert-crafted features"**: The paper claims to use "no expert-crafted features at all," but this is misleading. They use pretrained embeddings (which are derived from large corpora) and rely on the architecture of the stack LSTM to implicitly learn features. The distinction between "pretrained" and "expert-crafted" is blurry, and the paper doesn't sufficiently acknowledge this nuance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models but falls behind pipeline systems that use expert-crafted features. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why your model can't match these pipeline systems, and what specific features would be needed to close this gap?

2. The paper notes that their model overfits to the WSJ domain (performing better on in-domain than out-of-domain data). Could you elaborate on how this overfitting manifests in the model's behavior, and whether you've explored techniques like domain adaptation or data augmentation to address this issue?

3. Given that your model is greedy, what would be the computational cost of using a small beam search (e.g., beam size 5) to potentially improve performance, and how would this affect the model's runtime advantage over pipeline systems?
