

# Peer Review

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that uses stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer) without relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim their model achieves the best performance among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks while maintaining linear time complexity. As I see it, the paper's primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that neural network representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features in joint parsing, (2) introducing a greedy inference algorithm with linear runtime that avoids the complexity of beam search, and (3) providing a practical, fast parser implementation with end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data.

**Strengths**

1. **Practical performance and efficiency**: The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of their approach by demonstrating significantly faster runtime (2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015) while achieving competitive accuracy. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where parsing speed is critical. The authors clearly articulate the trade-off between speed and accuracy, showing that their model achieves near-top performance with substantially better efficiency.

2. **Novel representation learning approach**: The paper's most significant contribution is the demonstration that stack LSTMs can effectively learn representations of the entire parser state, eliminating the need for complex hand-crafted features. This insight is valuable beyond the specific parsing task, as it shows how neural network architectures can automatically discover useful representations that would otherwise require significant linguistic expertise.

3. **Clear architecture and evaluation**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the parser's architecture, transitions, and training procedure. The experimental setup is transparent, with appropriate baselines and comparisons to relevant prior work. The results on CoNLL 2008-9 are convincing, showing that the model outperforms all previous joint parsing approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art systems**: The paper compares primarily with older joint parsing models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009) rather than the most recent state-of-the-art systems. The authors acknowledge that their model "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" but fail to demonstrate how their approach could be integrated with these features. This omission weakens their claim about the significance of their contribution.

2. **Overfitting concerns**: The CoNLL 2009 results show that the Joint model underperforms the Hybrid model on out-of-domain data (Brown corpus), suggesting overfitting to the WSJ training data. The authors don't address this issue or provide analysis of why the Joint model might be overfitting, which is a significant weakness given that robustness to out-of-domain data is crucial for practical parsing systems.

3. **Lack of analysis of learned representations**: While the authors claim their model learns a representation of the entire algorithm state, they provide no evidence of what this representation actually captures. There is no ablation study showing the impact of different components, no analysis of the learned representations (e.g., via visualization or probing tasks), and no comparison of how the stack LSTM representation differs from previous approaches. This makes it difficult to understand the true nature of their contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The paper states that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model." Could you elaborate on which specific features or techniques from the top systems (e.g., Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) could potentially be integrated with your model, and how this might affect performance?

2. The Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data. What specific changes would you recommend to address this overfitting issue, and how might these changes affect the model's runtime performance?

3. The authors claim the stack LSTM learns representations of the entire parser state. Could you provide evidence (e.g., via visualization or probing tasks) that the learned representations actually capture meaningful linguistic information beyond what could be achieved with simpler representations?
