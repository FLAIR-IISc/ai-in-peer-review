1. End-to-end transition-based parser using stack LSTMs represents entire parser state and outperforms previous joint parsing models without expert-crafted features.

2. Computationally efficient (linear time) with runtime 177.6±18s on CoNLL 2009 English single-core, significantly faster than competing systems.

3. Greedy inference with a learned state representation achieves competitive joint parsing performance, challenging the need for beam search or optimal decoding.

4. Novel representation learning: a single neural model learns the full parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) without hand-crafted features.

5. Practical efficiency: linear complexity and ~2.5× faster than a state-of-the-art pipeline (Lei et al., 2015), supporting real-world utility.

6. Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art among joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and good cross-lingual adaptability (Chinese benefits from pretrained embeddings).

7. Insufficient comparison to top pipeline systems: model lags by 1–3 semantic F1 points and lacks analysis explaining this gap.

8. Weak analysis of greedy inference limits: paper doesn't justify why greedy decoding suffices or test small beams for potential gains.

9. Limited error analysis: lacks detailed examination of failure cases, sources of overfitting, and mitigation strategies.

10. Overstated claim about “no expert-crafted features”: use of pretrained embeddings and architectural biases implicitly encode expert knowledge, which isn't acknowledged.

11. Request for analysis on why the model can't match top pipeline systems and which specific features would be needed to close the gap.

12. Request for details on WSJ domain overfitting, its manifestations, and whether domain adaptation or augmentation was tried.

13. Request for estimated computational cost and runtime impact of using a small beam (e.g., beam size 5) and effect on the model’s runtime advantage.
