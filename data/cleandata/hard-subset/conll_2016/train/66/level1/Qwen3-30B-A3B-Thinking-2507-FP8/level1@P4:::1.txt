

Summary: This paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, enabling feature-free learning. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks among joint parsing models, with linear-time greedy inference and significantly faster runtime than pipeline approaches. The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength) are: 1) the use of stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state (not just top items), eliminating the need for expert-crafted features; 2) demonstrating that greedy inference can achieve state-of-the-art results for joint parsing without beam search; and 3) the model's efficiency (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core).

Strengths:
1. Technical innovation in using stack LSTMs for the entire parser state representation is well-executed and clearly motivated. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach eliminates the need for expert-crafted features while achieving strong performance, which is a significant contribution to the field of joint parsing. The authors provide clear evidence of this through their results, showing their model outperforms previous joint models (including Henderson et al., 2008, 2013) on the CoNLL 2008-9 English tasks.

2. The model's efficiency is a strong practical contribution. The paper demonstrates a 2.5x speed improvement over pipeline models (Lei et al., 2015) while achieving comparable performance, making it suitable for real-world applications. This is particularly valuable given the computational demands of previous joint parsing approaches.

3. The model's strong multilingual performance (on par with top systems in most cases) demonstrates good generalizability across languages, with the exception of Chinese without pretrained embeddings. This suggests the approach is robust across different linguistic structures.

4. The paper provides a clear, well-structured description of the parsing algorithm and transition system, making it easy to understand how the joint parsing works. The transition definitions and constraints are logically presented with appropriate formal notation.

5. The authors thoroughly evaluate their model against relevant baselines and provide detailed ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid), demonstrating the benefits of joint learning and the specific contributions of each component.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to outperform the best expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014), which use full syntactic parse features and optimal decoding. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently address how their model could be integrated with these approaches to achieve even better results, which would strengthen the contribution.

2. The paper doesn't adequately address how the model handles non-projective syntactic structures despite mentioning pseudo-projectivization as a preprocessing step. The authors note that "the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect" due to crossing arcs, but they don't provide details on how the model specifically handles these cases or their impact on performance.

3. The multilingual evaluation is limited in scope and depth. The paper mentions Japanese as a "small-data case" but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how the model's performance scales with training data size, which is a significant factor for neural models. The evaluation on non-English languages is not as thorough as the English evaluation.

4. The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison of the model's performance with respect to the amount of training data required. It would be valuable to understand whether the model achieves comparable results with less training data than previous systems.

5. The authors claim "linear time, including feature extraction" but don't provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity of their feature extraction process. Given that the paper emphasizes speed as a contribution, a more thorough complexity analysis would strengthen this claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. The authors state that their model doesn't use "expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses," but they don't discuss how their model could potentially incorporate these features (e.g., by adding them to the input representation) to bridge the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems.

2. The paper mentions that "the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect" for CoNLL 2009 due to crossing arcs. Could the authors provide more details about how this affects the model's performance, and whether the model can handle non-projective structures directly without pseudo-projectivization?

3. The authors note that their Chinese parser "relies on pretrained word embeddings for its superior performance." Could they provide more details about the specific impact of pretrained embeddings on performance, and whether their model could achieve similar results with alternative embedding approaches for low-resource languages?
