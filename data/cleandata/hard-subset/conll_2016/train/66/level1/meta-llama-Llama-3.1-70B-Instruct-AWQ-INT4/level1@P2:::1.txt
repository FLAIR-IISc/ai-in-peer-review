**Summary and Main Contributions**

The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state.
2. A greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction.
3. State-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to joint parsing**: The paper presents a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. This approach is different from previous approaches that use expert-crafted features or pipeline syntax and semantics.
2. **State-of-the-art performance**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.
3. **Efficient inference algorithm**: The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction, making it efficient for practical applications.
4. **Open-source implementation**: The authors plan to release an open-source implementation of the parser, making it available for other researchers to use and build upon.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparison to other approaches**: The paper only compares the parser to a few other approaches, and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art models.
2. **Dependence on pre-trained word embeddings**: The parser relies on pre-trained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.
3. **Limited evaluation metrics**: The paper only reports a limited set of evaluation metrics, and does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the parser's performance.
4. **No analysis of errors**: The paper does not provide an analysis of the parser's errors, which could help to identify areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the parser handle out-of-vocabulary words, and what is the impact on performance?

3. How does the parser's performance vary across different languages and domains?
4. Can you provide an analysis of the parser's errors, and identify areas for improvement?
