**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The main contributions of this work are:

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state, rather than relying on expert-crafted features.
2. **Stack LSTM for Joint Parsing**: The paper introduces a stack LSTM architecture that enables the parser to learn a representation of the algorithm state, including the syntactic and semantic stacks, buffer, and history of actions.
3. **Greedy Inference Algorithm**: The paper presents a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction, which makes the parser efficient and practical for use in real-world applications.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, surpassing previous joint models and achieving comparable results to top-performing systems that use expert-crafted features and more complex architectures.
2. **Efficient and Practical**: The parser's greedy inference algorithm and linear time complexity make it efficient and practical for use in real-world applications, as demonstrated by its end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core.
3. **Adaptability to Multilingual Settings**: The paper demonstrates the parser's adaptability to multilingual settings, achieving comparable results to top-performing systems on the CoNLL 2009 non-English datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison to Other Joint Models**: The paper's comparison to other joint models is limited, and it would be beneficial to include more comparisons to other state-of-the-art joint parsing models.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Word Embeddings**: The paper's results rely heavily on pretrained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.
3. **No Morphological Features**: The paper does not incorporate morphological features, which may be beneficial for highly inflective languages like Czech.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting to the WSJ domain, as evidenced by the Hybrid model's better performance on out-of-domain data?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure, including the number of epochs, batch size, and learning rate schedule?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the parser to incorporate morphological features, which may be beneficial for highly inflective languages?
