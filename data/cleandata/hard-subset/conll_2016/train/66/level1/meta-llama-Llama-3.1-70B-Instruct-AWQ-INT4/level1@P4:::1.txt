**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies. The parser uses a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state and employs a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity. The parser is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieves the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using a stack LSTM.
2. **Greedy Inference Algorithm**: The paper presents a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, making it efficient for practical applications.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficient Algorithm**: The greedy inference algorithm has linear time complexity, making it efficient for practical applications.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.
3. **Novel Approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using a stack LSTM.
4. **Fast Runtime**: The parser achieves an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core.
5. **Multilingual Performance**: The parser adapts easily to non-English languages and achieves competitive performance on the CoNLL 2009 multilingual datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison**: The paper only compares the parser's performance to a limited set of previous joint models and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art models.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The parser's performance relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.
3. **Limited Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary Words**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of how the parser handles out-of-vocabulary words, which may affect its performance in certain scenarios.
4. **No Morphological Features**: The parser does not incorporate morphological features, which may be beneficial for highly inflective languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the parser handle out-of-vocabulary words, and what are the implications for its performance in certain scenarios?
2. Can the parser be extended to incorporate morphological features, and how would this affect its performance?
3. How does the parser's performance compare to other state-of-the-art models that use different approaches to joint parsing?
4. Can the parser be applied to other languages or domains, and what are the challenges and opportunities in doing so?
