This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state without relying on hand-crafted features. The core innovation lies in applying stack LSTMs to the entire parser state, coupled with a greedy inference algorithm for linear time complexity. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results among joint syntactic-semantic parsing models.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs in capturing the entire parsing state for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This eliminates the need for manually designed features, a significant simplification and potential generalization benefit.
2. **Strong Empirical Results on CoNLL Datasets:** The paper presents strong empirical evidence, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks for joint parsing, particularly noteworthy given the absence of hand-crafted features and use of greedy decoding.
3. **Linear-Time Greedy Inference:** The use of a greedy inference algorithm, facilitated by the learned state representation, provides a significant speed advantage over more complex decoding schemes, making the system practical for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in State Representation:** The use of stack LSTMs to represent the *entire* parser state, as opposed to just the stack top, is a novel approach. It addresses a limitation of previous work relying on local features and allows the model to capture more contextual information. The authors clearly articulate this difference from Henderson et al. (2008) and subsequent work.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves competitive performance on the challenging CoNLL datasets, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The comparison to both joint and pipeline models provides valuable context. The authors are careful to acknowledge where their performance falls short of the absolute state-of-the-art (feature-rich, optimal decoding systems).
3. **Efficiency:** The linear-time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a significant strength. The reported runtime (177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English) is impressive and highlights the potential for real-time applications.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The explanation of the transitions and the state representation is particularly well done. The inclusion of Figure 1 and Table 1 aids comprehension.
5. **Open-Source Release:** The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation is a positive aspect that will foster reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, it lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes. Understanding the failure modes would provide valuable insights for future improvements. What types of dependencies are consistently missed or incorrectly parsed?
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance, especially in the multilingual setting, relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings. The impact of embedding quality and potential for cross-lingual transfer learning could be explored further.
3. **Greedy Decoding Limitations:** Although the greedy decoding is efficient, it may sub-optimally explore the search space. A discussion of the potential trade-off between speed and accuracy would be valuable. Is there a systematic way to quantify the errors introduced by the greedy approach?
4. **Comparison to More Recent Neural SRL Systems:** While the authors compare to earlier state-of-the-art systems, they don't deeply compare against more recent and directly comparable neural SRL models that also avoid hand-crafted features. A more comprehensive comparison would better position this work within the current landscape of neural SRL research.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study, systematically removing components (e.g., semantic stack LSTM, specific transitions) would better isolate the contributions of each part of the model and justify the design choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of syntactic and semantic dependencies where the model struggles?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality and size of the pretrained word embeddings, especially for low-resource languages?
3.  Have you experimented with any beam search or other decoding strategies to assess the trade-off between speed and accuracy? If so, what were the results?
4.  How does the performance compare to recent neural SRL models that also aim to minimize hand-crafted features, such as those based on transformers or other advanced architectures?
5.  What is the computational cost associated with the stack LSTMs compared to simpler state representations?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper mentions pseudo-projectivization, but a brief explanation of how this impacts the transitions would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the technique.
*   The notation in equation (6) is a bit dense and could benefit from a clearer explanation of each term.
* The description of the M-SELF transition could be more detailed with an example.
