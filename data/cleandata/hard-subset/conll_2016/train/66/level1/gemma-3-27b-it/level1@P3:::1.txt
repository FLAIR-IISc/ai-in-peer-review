This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state without relying on hand-crafted features. The core contribution lies in demonstrating strong performance on CoNLL 2008-09 shared tasks *without* the typically relied-upon expensive, syntactically-informed features common in high-performing SRL systems. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the efficient, linear-time greedy inference algorithm. Finally, the public release of the implementation is a beneficial addition to the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets *for joint models*. Crucially, they achieve this without the feature engineering typical of top-performing, pipeline-based systems. This is a significant accomplishment, indicating the power of their representation learning approach. The comparison to previous joint models (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) is clear and favorable.

2. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to the entire parser state, rather than just the top of the stack, is a genuinely innovative adaptation. This allows the model to consider broader contextual information during decision-making, addressing a key limitation of prior work focused on local features. The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind this design choice and its potential benefits.  The fact that they effectively eliminate the need for hand-crafted features highlights the effectiveness of this approach.

3. **Efficient Inference:** The linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a practical advantage. In contrast to computationally expensive optimal decoding methods, this parser offers a compelling speed-performance trade-off, making it suitable for real-world applications. The reported runtime (177.6 ± 18 seconds for CoNLL 2009) supports this claim.

4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a detailed experimental setup, including ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) that help to isolate the contributions of different components of their model. The multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 demonstrate the model's generalizability beyond English.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of State-of-the-Art Comparison:** While the authors acknowledge that their system doesn’t match the best *overall* performance (pipeline systems with expert features and reranking), the comparison feels somewhat downplayed.  The statement that many advances in those systems are "orthogonal" is a bit dismissive; a more nuanced discussion of why the proposed method isn't competitive with the absolute best (and where future work might bridge that gap) would be valuable. A more detailed comparison to Zhao et al. (2009), beyond simply stating it used carefully designed language-specific features, would be helpful.  What specific features were most impactful?

2. **Greedy Inference Limitations:** While the linear time complexity is a strength, the greedy inference algorithm may inherently limit performance. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by comparing to systems using optimal decoding. A discussion of potential strategies for mitigating the limitations of greedy decoding (e.g., limited beam search) would be beneficial. Is the speed gain truly worth the potential loss in accuracy?

3. **Dependency on Pretrained Embeddings:** The multilingual results highlight a reliance on pretrained word embeddings, particularly for Chinese. The performance on Japanese, where data is limited, further underscores this point. A more thorough analysis of the impact of embedding quality and potential for cross-lingual transfer learning would strengthen the work. The effect of using different embedding initialization schemes deserves mention.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific language-specific features employed by Zhao et al. (2009) that contribute most to their superior performance?  What steps would be required to incorporate similar features into your model (and what would be the expected impact on runtime)?
2.  Have you experimented with any limited-width beam search strategies during inference? If so, what was the impact on performance and runtime?
3.  What is the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the input POS tags and lemmas? Do errors in these annotations significantly degrade performance?
4. Regarding the 1.5% of CoNLL 2009 sentences with incorrectly encoded transitions, what is the impact of these errors on the reported results, and how might they be addressed in future work?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 could benefit from clearer labeling of the syntactic and semantic dependencies.
*   The reference to "pseudo-projectivization" is somewhat abrupt. A brief explanation of this technique would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with dependency parsing.
*   The notation in equation (6) is a little dense; clarifying the purpose of each term would improve readability.
