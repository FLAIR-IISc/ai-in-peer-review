- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies.  
- Uses stack LSTMs over the entire parser state to learn representations without hand-crafted features.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 joint tasks without syntactically-informed engineered features.  
- Provides an efficient, linear-time greedy inference algorithm with reported fast runtimes.  
- Publicly releases the implementation.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results for joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009, competitive with prior joint work.  
- Novel application of stack LSTMs to the whole parser state, reducing need for manual features.  
- Practical, linear-time greedy inference offering a good speed-performance trade-off.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation including ablations and multilingual tests.

Weaknesses:
- Comparison to overall state-of-the-art (pipeline systems with expert features/reranking) is limited and somewhat downplayed.  
- Greedy inference may limit ultimate accuracy; trade-offs versus beam/optimal decoding are not fully explored.  
- Reliance on pretrained embeddings affects multilingual performance (notably Chinese and Japanese); embedding initialization impact is underanalyzed.

Questions for authors:
- Which specific language-specific features in Zhao et al. (2009) most contributed to their superior performance, and how would adding them affect runtime?  
- Did you try limited-width beam search during inference, and what were its effects on accuracy and speed?  
- How sensitive is the model to errors in input POS tags and lemmas?  
- For the 1.5% of CoNLL 2009 sentences with incorrectly encoded transitions, how do these errors affect results and how can they be fixed?
