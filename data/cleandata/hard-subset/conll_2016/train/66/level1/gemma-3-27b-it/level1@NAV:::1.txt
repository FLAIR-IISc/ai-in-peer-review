## Review of "A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing"

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of utilizing stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parsing state for *joint* syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, achieving competitive results *without* relying on extensive feature engineering. The greedy inference algorithm, while not novel in itself, is efficiently implemented and contributes to the practical applicability of the model. A secondary, but important, contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation on the CoNLL datasets, providing a strong baseline for future research in joint parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves competitive and, in some cases, state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL datasets, exceeding previous joint parsing models. This validates the approach and demonstrates its practical utility.
2. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Features:** By successfully employing stack LSTMs, the authors demonstrate the feasibility of building a high-performing joint parser without the need for extensive feature engineering, a significant advantage in terms of development effort and potential for generalization.
3. **Efficient Implementation:** The linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm makes the parser computationally efficient, enhancing its potential for real-world applications.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough evaluation across different datasets (including multilingual data) provides a robust assessment of the model’s performance and adaptability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** While the application of stack LSTMs is effective, the underlying transition-based parsing framework is well-established. The core algorithmic innovation is relatively incremental, building upon existing work (Henderson et al., 2008).
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper acknowledges that its performance doesn't match state-of-the-art systems utilizing more complex features and optimal decoding. While this is honest, it limits the claim of truly groundbreaking results. A more detailed comparison, including ablation studies, could better clarify the contribution of the stack LSTMs.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The multilingual results demonstrate a reliance on pretrained embeddings. The performance of the Chinese parser, in particular, highlights this dependence. The impact of embedding quality isn't fully explored.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide an ablation study to quantify the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., the individual stack LSTMs, the composition functions) to the overall performance?
2. What is the impact of different embedding initialization strategies on the performance of the multilingual models?
3.  The paper mentions that the semantic representations benefit from syntactic information, and vice versa. Can you elaborate on specific cases where the joint learning is crucial for obtaining accurate semantic dependencies?
