This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, utilizing stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state and employing a greedy inference algorithm. The core contribution is demonstrating strong performance on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks *without* relying on traditional, expert-crafted features typically used in high-performance SRL systems. A secondary contribution is the speed of the greedy inference algorithm, achieving linear time complexity and a practical runtime. A third, more minor contribution, is the introduction of a new semantic transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies in the CoNLL 2009 corpus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among *joint* syntactic and semantic parsers on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. While acknowledging it doesn't beat the best pipeline systems, exceeding prior joint models is a significant achievement, especially given the avoidance of complex feature engineering. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the stack LSTM approach for representing parser state and learning features automatically. The results table (Table 2 and 3) clearly support this claim.
2. **Efficiency of Greedy Inference:** The linear time complexity and reported runtime (177.6s on CoNLL 2009 English) are compelling, particularly in comparison to more accurate but computationally expensive pipeline approaches. This speed makes the model potentially practical for real-world applications where latency is critical. The comparison to Lei et al. (2015) further highlights this advantage.
3. **Novel State Representation:** The use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state, rather than relying on local stack top features, is a significant design choice. This allows the model to capture more contextual information and learn more robust representations. The authors convincingly argue this is especially valuable for greedy models where feature design is more challenging.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and experimental setup. The explanation of the transitions and stack LSTMs is relatively easy to follow, and the ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) provide insightful analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, the individual components (transition-based parsing, stack LSTMs, greedy inference) are not novel in themselves. The paper acknowledges this, but the contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.  The reliance on existing work (Henderson et al., Dyer et al.) is substantial.
2. **Performance Gap to Pipeline Systems:** The paper consistently acknowledges that its performance falls short of the best pipeline systems. While understandable given the design choices, this gap is substantial enough to question whether the simplicity and speed trade-off is worthwhile in all scenarios. More discussion of *where* the model struggles compared to pipeline systems would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors the model makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific semantic roles or syntactic constructions that are particularly challenging?
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, it lacks specifics. Describing the search space and rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters would enhance reproducibility and provide a better understanding of the model’s sensitivity to these parameters.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of errors your model makes compared to the best pipeline systems?
2. What were the specific hyperparameter settings used for the stack LSTMs (e.g., number of layers, hidden state size, dropout rate)?  What search space was explored during hyperparameter tuning?
3.  The paper mentions that the M-SELF transition handles self-dependencies. Could you elaborate on how frequently this transition is used during parsing and its impact on overall performance?
4. Given that the model's performance on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset varies significantly by language, could you comment on which languages benefited the most from the joint parsing approach and which were less improved?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference to the GitHub repository for Lei et al. (2015) is helpful for reproducibility.
*  The inclusion of example transition sequences in the supplementary material is a good practice.
* The discussion of related work is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from including more recent advancements in SRL, particularly those utilizing pre-trained language models.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The strong empirical results, combined with the efficiency of the model, make it a worthwhile contribution. However, the incremental nature of the work and the performance gap to pipeline systems prevent it from being considered a truly exceptional submission.
