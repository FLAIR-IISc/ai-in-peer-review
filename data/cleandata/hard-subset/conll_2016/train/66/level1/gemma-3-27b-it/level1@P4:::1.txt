This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state without relying on hand-crafted features. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a greedy, entirely-data-driven approach, using stack LSTMs, can achieve competitive results on standard CoNLL shared tasks for joint parsing, outperforming previous joint models and approaching the performance of more complex, feature-engineered pipeline systems. A secondary contribution lies in the successful application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing, extending the benefits observed in purely syntactic parsing to the more complex joint task. The third, though less significant, contribution is the creation and release of a fast, open-source implementation of the system, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among *joint* parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. The results are convincingly presented in tables and compared against relevant prior work, showcasing a clear improvement over previous joint approaches. The comparison demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach despite its relative simplicity.
2. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state for joint syntactic and semantic parsing is a significant and innovative aspect of the work. This allows the model to learn representations without relying on extensive feature engineering, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) effectively demonstrate the value of the joint approach and the LSTM representations.
3. **Efficiency:** The greedy inference algorithm combined with the use of stack LSTMs results in a remarkably fast parsing system. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set is a significant advantage, making the model practical for real-world applications. This speed, combined with competitive performance, is a strong point.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The transition system is clearly described, and the architecture of the stack LSTMs is illustrated effectively. The connections to previous work are appropriately discussed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Parsing Technique:** While the application of stack LSTMs is novel in the context of *joint* parsing, the underlying transition system (arc-eager) and the general approach of transition-based dependency parsing are well-established. The core parsing mechanism itself is not a breakthrough, and the primary novelty resides in the representation learning component.
2. **Performance Gap Compared to Pipeline Systems:** The paper acknowledges that its performance is still below that of state-of-the-art pipeline systems with carefully engineered features and optimal decoding. While the authors rightly point out the trade-off between speed and accuracy, this gap is a limitation. The discussion of why these systems aren't directly comparable feels slightly defensive.
3. **Lack of Deep Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on overall performance metrics. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors the model makes and the reasons behind them would be valuable. Understanding the model’s weaknesses could inform future improvements.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is somewhat brief. While the authors mention tuning on the development data, they don't provide sufficient detail about the search space or the methodology used.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors your system makes? Are there specific linguistic phenomena or syntactic/semantic constructions that consistently pose challenges for the model?
2. The paper mentions the possibility of incorporating morphological features. Have you experimented with character-based word embeddings as a way to capture morphological information, and if so, what were the results?
3. What is the impact of the pseudo-projectivization step on performance? Would the model perform significantly worse without it, and are there alternative approaches to handle non-projective dependencies?
4. The semantic self-dependencies account for 6.68% of arcs. Could you discuss the model’s performance on sentences *without* these self-dependencies, and how the introduction of the M-SELF transition impacts overall performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Lei et al. (2015)'s runtime seems to need verification; the linked GitHub repository does not appear to provide runtime measurements directly comparable to those reported in the paper.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural SRL, beyond those explicitly compared in the results section.
*   A brief discussion of the computational resources required for training the model would be helpful.
