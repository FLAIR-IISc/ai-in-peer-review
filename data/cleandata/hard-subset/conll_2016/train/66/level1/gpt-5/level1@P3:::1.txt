Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a transition-based joint parser for syntactic and semantic dependencies that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithm state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), enabling fully greedy, linear-time inference. The transition system is adapted from Henderson et al. (2013), with modifications: (i) copying rather than moving from the buffer in syntax actions to keep items visible to semantic actions, (ii) a new M-SELF action to handle semantic self-arcs, and (iii) predicate sense disambiguation handled via an M-PRED action with a composition function. The model obtains strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results.

I see the primary contributions as:
1) A neural representation for the full joint transition system state via stack LSTMs, replacing expert-crafted feature templates and enabling fast greedy decoding. The empirical gains over prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; 2013) stem largely from this representation learning.
2) A practical, carefully engineered transition system for joint syntactic-semantic parsing, including the interleaving strategy and new M-SELF transition, with an oracle for deriving training sequences and constraints to ensure validity.
3) An extensive empirical study comparing joint vs hybrid (pipeline-like), semantics-only, and syntax-only variants across CoNLL 2008/2009 English and multiple languages, with speed comparisons.

Strengths
- Strong performance among joint models with substantial speed advantages. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model surpasses previous joint parsers (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013). It achieves comparable semantic/JM performance to state-of-the-art pipelines while running significantly faster (e.g., ~2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015 under the authors’ setup).
- Clear demonstration that learned state representations obviate many hand-crafted features. The paper shows that stack LSTMs scale to joint syntax–semantics, not only syntax (Dyer et al., 2015), and yield consistent improvements over earlier locally-scoped, feature-driven joint models. The ablations (Semantics-only vs Hybrid vs Joint) substantiate the benefit of including syntactic information for SRL and vice versa, particularly in-domain.
- Sound engineering of the transition system and constraints for practicality. The interleaving scheme keeps semantics and syntax synchronized without beam search; the M-SELF transition addresses NomBank self-arguments; the copying tweak to arc-eager allows semantics to “see” items otherwise consumed by syntactic actions. The resulting system remains linear-time with feature extraction amortized by recurrent computation.
- Multilingual applicability with competitive results. Without language-specific features or morphology, the joint system is generally on par with the top CoNLL 2009 systems in several languages, and benefits from pretrained embeddings, indicating reasonable cross-lingual robustness.
- Transparent comparisons and positioning w.r.t. pipelines. The paper is candid that top SRL performance still comes from pipelines with global inference and rich features (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015), and frames the contribution as orthogonal and complementary.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond applying stack LSTMs to an existing joint transition system. The core transition framework and interleaving follow Henderson et al. (2013), with relatively minor additions (copying, M-SELF, sense composition). The technical novelty is mainly the representation learning and greedy inference extension; a stronger paper would include a deeper analysis or more substantial innovations in joint modeling or search.
- Greedy training and decoding without exploration or global objectives likely induce exposure bias and suboptimal search. There is no use of dynamic oracles, imitation learning, or beam search; the oracle itself is imperfect (1.5% of EN 2009 sentences cannot be encoded exactly). This likely contributes to the Joint model’s worse out-of-domain SRL than Hybrid, suggesting overfitting to in-domain transition distributions. An ablation with a small beam or dynamic oracle training would clarify the trade-offs.
- Constrained interleaving may bias arc construction and limit modeling capacity. The policy “resolve left-side syntactic arcs before left-side semantic arcs” is plausible but untested versus alternatives (e.g., different interleavings, joint action spaces, or learned schedulers). It may induce order effects that hurt certain phenomena (e.g., rightward arguments, crossing arcs). No analysis quantifies how M-SWAP or the fixed schedule impacts recoverability of complex SRL structures, especially in languages with freer word order.
- Incomplete ablations and analysis of design choices. The paper relies on pretrained “structured skip-gram” embeddings and concatenated POS/lemma vectors, pseudo-projectivization, and the copying change to arc-eager, but lacks ablations for their individual contributions. Similarly, the effect of the M-SELF action and sense composition function is not isolated. The semantics-only baselines are informative, but more fine-grained ablations would better attribute gains.
- Fairness and scope of comparisons. While pipeline systems are acknowledged as stronger, the comparisons conflate differences in modeling (global vs greedy), resources (additional annotations), and features (morphology for non-English). The speed comparison is to one publicly available system; broader runtime comparisons or normalized hardware details would strengthen the claim of practicality. For multilingual settings, omitting morphology disadvantages the method in highly inflected languages; this should be controlled or at least better contextualized.

Questions to authors
- Training: Did you try dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling, or small-beam decoding/training? How do they affect in/out-of-domain performance and speed?
- Interleaving: Did you explore alternative interleaving policies or learned scheduling between syntactic and semantic actions? Any empirical differences?
- Oracle and invalid encodings: How are the 1.5% of unencodable sentences handled during training? Do they cap the achievable SRL F1, and could alternative transition sets reduce this gap?
- Copy vs move: What is the quantitative effect of copying (vs standard arc-eager move) on accuracy and speed? Any issues with duplicated structure representations?
- Ablations: Can you report the impact of pretrained embeddings, POS/lemma inputs, pseudo-projectivization, M-SELF, and the predicate sense composition separately?
- Predicate sense defaulting: The “01” default is 91.22% by type; what is the token-level accuracy on unseen predicates at test time?

Additional comments
- An error analysis (e.g., argument types, long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs) would make the strengths/weaknesses more concrete.
- Character-level or morpheme-level embeddings could mitigate the lack of explicit morphology in multilingual experiments without feature engineering.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid engineering paper that convincingly demonstrates that learned state representations with stack LSTMs yield the best performance among joint syntax–semantics parsers, with attractive speed. The novelty is moderate, but the contribution is practical and timely. With stronger analysis/ablations and modest training/search improvements, this could be a strong accept; as is, I lean toward accept for its empirical value and clear, generalizable design.
