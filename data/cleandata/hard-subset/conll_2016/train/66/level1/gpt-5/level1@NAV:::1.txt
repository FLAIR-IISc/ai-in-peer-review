Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to encode the full parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). Inference is greedy and linear-time. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the system outperforms prior joint models and is competitive multilingual; it is substantially faster than strong pipeline SRL systems.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical advance: a full-state neural representation for joint syn-sem parsing enables a purely greedy, feature-light system to surpass prior joint models and approach pipeline performance, with strong speed gains.
2) Algorithmic engineering: a coherent joint transition system with interleaving, including M-SELF for nominal self-arguments and MSWAP for non-projectivity, realized with recursive composition functions for arcs and predicate senses.
3) Experimental evidence: clear ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) that diagnose the utility and trade-offs of joint modeling.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results for joint parsing: On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model consistently outperforms earlier joint systems (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008/2013; Gesmundo et al., 2009). This directly validates the main claim about full-state representations.
- Efficiency with competitive accuracy: End-to-end runtime of ~178s on CoNLL 2009 English is ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) while delivering comparable semantic F1 to top non-joint baselines, making it attractive for practical use.
- Clean, feature-light architecture: The model dispenses with expensive global syntactic features and handcrafted design, relying on learned composition and stack LSTMs. This reduces engineering burden and likely improves portability.
- Thoughtful ablations and multilingual evaluation: The syntax/semantics-only and hybrid variants elucidate where joint learning helps (WSJ) and where it may overfit (Brown). Multilingual results are generally competitive and highlight the role of pretrained embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior stack-LSTM parsing: The core modeling idea (stack LSTMs encoding parser state) builds heavily on Dyer et al. (2015); the paper’s novelty rests more on application to joint parsing and transition tweaks than on new modeling principles.
- Greedy inference and exposure bias: No beam, global training, or dynamic oracles are explored; observed OOD drop (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) suggests overfitting and error propagation that stronger search/training could mitigate.
- Oracle/transition limitations: Imperfect semantic oracle (1.5% of sentences) and constraints (e.g., repeated-action bans) may bias learning and evaluation; the impact is not quantified.
- Comparisons to SOTA pipelines are incomplete: While acknowledging better pipeline SRL with global inference and extra annotations, the paper lacks significance testing and deeper analysis of remaining error gaps.
- Predicate identification in 2008 handled by a separate classifier, weakening the “jointness” and complicating attribution of gains.

Questions to authors
- How much performance is lost due to the imperfect semantic oracle? Can you quantify its impact or repair rate?
- Did you try beam search or dynamic-oracle training? What is the accuracy–speed trade-off?
- How sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings and POS/lemma quality? Any robustness analysis?
- Can you provide an error analysis indicating where joint modeling helps or hurts (e.g., specific roles, long-distance arcs, non-projectivity)?
