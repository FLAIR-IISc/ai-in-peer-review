- Paper proposes a transition-based joint parser building syntactic dependencies and SRL graphs simultaneously using stack LSTMs encoding full parser state.
- Model performs greedy, linear-time inference with no hand-crafted features and minor transition-system adaptations (M-SELF, buffer copy).
- Empirically outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive with feature-rich pipelines; shows reasonable multilingual speed and performance.
- Contribution: Demonstrates that learned continuous state representations enable accurate joint syntax–semantics parsing with greedy decoding.
- Contribution: Proposes a practical joint transition system with a shared buffer, copy operations, M-SELF for NomBank, and interleaving constraints.
- Contribution: Provides extensive evaluation (English and multilingual) with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and runtime measurements.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains over earlier joint models, validating stack LSTMs over locally scoped features.
- Strength: Simple, fast, and practical—greedy linear-time parsing runs ~2.5× faster than a strong SRL pipeline and uses ≤512 MB memory.
- Strength: Clear ablation studies that show joint modeling benefits and sensible out-of-domain behavior.
- Strength: Minimal feature engineering, relying on pretrained and learned embeddings and composition functions instead of hand-crafted syntactic path features.
- Strength: Thoughtful transition-system design addressing synchronized parsing and NomBank specifics.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—stack LSTMs and much of the transition system follow prior work; new elements are incremental.
- Weakness: Uses teacher-forced training and greedy decoding only; lacks comparisons to small beams, dynamic oracles, or exploration-based training.
- Weakness: Incomplete error analysis—missing role-wise SRL breakdowns, crossing-arc coverage, rare label effects, and significance testing.
- Weakness: Predicate identification and sense-handling choices are underexplored; impact of predicate-ID errors and the “01” default sense not quantified.
- Weakness: Multilingual evaluation limited—underperforms on low-resource/morphologically rich languages and lacks character/morphological modeling.
- Question: How sensitive are results to beam size or to dynamic-oracle / exploration-based training?
- Question: What is the ablation impact of M-SELF and buffer-copy vs buffer-move on accuracy and speed?
- Question: For CoNLL’08, what are results with gold predicate IDs and how much do predicate-ID errors explain performance gaps?
- Question: For CoNLL’09, how often is the “01” default sense used at test time and what's its token-level accuracy?
- Question: Are improvements over prior joint models statistically significant on main test sets?
- Question: Were alternative interleaving schedules examined and how do they affect accuracy?
- Additional comment: Include qualitative error analysis (e.g., non-projective semantic arcs, long-distance arguments, per-role F1).
- Additional comment: Run CoNLL’08 with the same ablation variants used for CoNLL’09 for consistency.
- Additional comment: For reproducibility, report random seeds, variance, and ranges over multiple runs.
