Summary
The paper presents a transition-based joint parser that simultaneously predicts syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs. The method builds on the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but replaces expert-crafted features and local feature scopes with learned, continuous representations of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. The parser uses greedy inference with linear-time complexity. A new semantic transition (M-SELF) is introduced to handle self-dependencies in CoNLL 2009. Experiments on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English and multilingual) show the model outperforms prior joint parsers and is competitive with strong pipeline systems in accuracy while being substantially faster.

Main contributions (as assessed)
- A joint transition-based architecture that learns compact vector representations of the full algorithmic state (stacks, buffer, action history) via stack LSTMs and recursive composition, enabling feature-free, greedy, linear-time joint parsing of syntax and semantics.
- Introduction of a semantic transition (M-SELF) to model self-dependencies in SRL graphs, enabling complete coverage of CoNLL 2009 phenomena.
- Empirical evidence: best performance among prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English; competitive multilingual results; strong speed/accuracy trade-off, with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) clarifying the contribution of joint learning.

Strengths
- Technically sound integration of stack LSTMs into a synchronized joint parsing framework. The modeling of full parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, shared buffer, and action history) with recursive composition functions is well-specified, differentiable end-to-end, and eliminates hand-engineered features. The interleaving protocol and constraints are clearly defined and ensure validity and termination. Complexity remains linear.
- Strong empirical validation against relevant baselines. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model surpasses previous joint parsers (e.g., Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.). The ablations (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid) are informative: they confirm the benefit of syntax for SRL and show mutual gains in-domain for joint training. Syntactic LAS is competitive with comparable transition-based neural parsers.
- Practical efficiency. The model achieves end-to-end test-time parsing substantially faster than a recent high-performing pipeline (≈2.5× speedup under stated conditions), while maintaining competitive accuracy. The linear-time, feature-free nature and modest memory footprint are compelling for deployment.
- Coverage of CoNLL-specific phenomena via M-SELF and M-SWAP. The added M-SELF transition and use of SWAP enable modeling of self-edges and crossing arcs in semantic graphs, increasing representational adequacy for the target tasks.
- Reasonable clarity and reproducibility. The transition inventory, state encoding, training objective, hyperparameters, and use of pretrained embeddings are described in sufficient detail to enable reimplementation. Promised open-source code further supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. The transition system is largely inherited from Henderson et al. (2013), and stack LSTMs for transition-based parsing were introduced in Dyer et al. (2015). The core novelty is the application of stack LSTMs to the joint setting and the M-SELF transition, which, while useful, is incremental rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- Greedy decoding without exploration or global training. The model trains with static oracles and uses fully greedy inference. There is no dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, or beam search. This can exacerbate exposure bias and error propagation; the paper does not quantify the trade-off (e.g., accuracy vs. beam width) or explore simple mitigations. The domain overfitting observed (Joint vs. Hybrid on Brown) suggests this may matter.
- Imperfect oracle conversion for semantic transitions. The authors note that ≈1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded with the transition set, causing mismatches between gold graphs and training sequences. The impact on training signal, upper bound accuracy, and error patterns is not quantified, which weakens the technical rigor of the evaluation.
- Comparative evaluation gaps. While the paper contrasts with previous joint models and some pipelines, it does not integrate or directly compare (under controlled settings) to recent strong neural SRL systems with global inference, nor provide significance testing. The claim that advances are “orthogonal” is plausible but untested; ablations that add a small beam or simple global constraints would strengthen the case.
- Handling of unseen predicate senses is simplistic. Defaulting to “01” for unseen predicates leverages frequency bias (91.22% by type), but the effect on predicate sense disambiguation metrics is not isolated. There is no analysis of performance specifically on unseen predicates, which is important for assessing generalization.

Questions to Authors
- Can you quantify the effect of the imperfect semantic oracle on training and evaluation? For example, report the upper-bound joint accuracy achievable with your transition set, and the model’s performance excluding the affected sentences.
- Did you try dynamic oracles, exploration-based training, or small-beam decoding? What is the accuracy/runtime trade-off versus greedy decoding?
- How sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings and their training corpora? Please provide ablations without pretraining and with different embedding sizes.
- For predicate sense disambiguation, what is the accuracy on unseen predicates, and how much does the “01” default contribute? Could a backoff sense classifier improve this case?
- Have you conducted statistical significance tests across runs with different random seeds for the main English results?
- For multilingual experiments, what POS/morphological features were used (if any)? Could character-based embeddings compensate for missing morphology, especially for Czech/Japanese?

Overall, the work is technically sound, clearly presented, and provides a useful, efficient baseline that advances the state of joint parsing over prior joint models. The novelty is moderate but justified by breadth and speed, and the empirical analysis is generally solid though it would benefit from addressing the points above.
