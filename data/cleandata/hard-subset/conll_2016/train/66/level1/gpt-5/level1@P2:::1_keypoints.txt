- Greedy, transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to represent full parser state (syntax stack, semantic stack, buffer, action history).
- Transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013), adds an M-SELF action to handle semantic self-dependencies.
- Model eschews hand-crafted features and achieves linear-time inference including feature computation.
- Reports strong in-domain CoNLL 2008/2009 English results, competitive multilingual performance, and fast runtime.
- Contribution: unified, feature-lean, greedy joint parsing model that learns full parser-state representations and attains best-reported performance among prior joint models on CoNLL English.
- Contribution: carefully designed joint transition system with M-SELF and recursive composition functions to encode growing syntactic/semantic structures.
- Contribution: empirical study with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and multilingual experiments clarifying when joint modeling helps.
- Strength: empirical gains over prior joint models, validating learned state representations can replace heavy feature engineering.
- Strength: simplicity and speed — greedy linear-time stack-LSTM design yields practical runtime (~178s on CoNLL 2009 English, ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline under same settings).
- Strength: eliminates hand-crafted features for both syntax and semantics, showing learned representations capture needed information.
- Strength: transition system, training details, and implementation choices are well-specified and replicable; M-SELF is a precise extension for self-arguments.
- Strength: broad evaluation across in-domain, OOD, and multilingual datasets, and comparisons to joint and pipeline systems.
- Weakness: incremental novelty — combines existing transition system and stack-LSTMs; main novelty is integration and M-SELF rather than new modeling paradigms.
- Weakness: greedy decoding with local cross-entropy training and no exploration (no dynamic oracles, beam search, or global objectives), risking error propagation and OOD sensitivity.
- Weakness: does not match top pipeline SRL systems that use rich syntactic features, global inference, or extra supervision; claims are limited to “best among joint models.”
- Weakness: transition-oracle mismatch and coverage issues (semantic oracle imperfect; some sentences not encodable), unclear impact on training/evaluation.
- Weakness: limited error analysis and ablations (e.g., by role type, long-distance arcs, effect of action-history, M-SELF, composition choices); limited exploration of pretrained/char/morphological embeddings for multilinguality.
- Question: report absolute improvements over Henderson et al. (2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) per metric with confidence intervals or significance tests?
- Question: how often does the oracle mismatch occur in training, and how does it affect learning and errors on crossing-arc constructions?
- Question: were beam search, dynamic oracles, or scheduled sampling tried, and what effects did they have on in-domain vs OOD performance and runtime?
- Question: how sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings (dim, domain, OOV rate); any experiments with character/morphological embeddings for rich-morphology languages?
- Question: for CoNLL 2008 predicate identification, how integrated is the biLSTM classifier with the joint parser (shared embeddings or joint training), and would joint training reduce pipeline errors?
- Question: was the runtime comparison with Lei et al. (2015) controlled for preprocessing and hardware; can you provide token/sec numbers and run variance?
