- Empirical contribution: full-state neural representation for joint syntactic-semantic parsing enables a greedy, feature-light parser that surpasses prior joint models and approaches pipeline performance with large speed gains.
- Algorithmic engineering: coherent joint transition system with interleaving, M-SELF for nominal self-arguments, MSWAP for non-projectivity, and recursive composition for arcs and predicate senses.
- Experimental contribution: clear ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) that diagnose the utility and trade-offs of joint modeling.

- Strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, consistently outperforming earlier joint systems.
- Efficiency: end-to-end runtime (~178s) ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline while delivering competitive semantic F1.
- Clean, feature-light architecture that removes handcrafted global syntactic features and relies on learned composition and stack LSTMs.
- Thoughtful ablations and multilingual evaluation that show where joint learning helps (WSJ) and may overfit (Brown).

- Limited novelty beyond prior stack-LSTM parsing; main novelty is application and transition tweaks rather than new modeling principles.
- Greedy inference risks exposure bias and error propagation; no beam, global training, or dynamic-oracle explored, and OOD drop (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) suggests overfitting.
- Oracle/transition limitations: imperfect semantic oracle (affecting ~1.5% of sentences) and restrictive action constraints may bias learning and evaluation; impact is unquantified.
- Incomplete comparisons to state-of-the-art pipeline SRL: no significance testing or deep analysis of remaining error gaps.
- Predicate identification for CoNLL-2008 done by a separate classifier, weakening the claimed jointness and complicating attribution of gains.
