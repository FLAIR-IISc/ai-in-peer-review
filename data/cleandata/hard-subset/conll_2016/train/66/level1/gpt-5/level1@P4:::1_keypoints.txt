- Presents a transition-based joint parser using stack LSTMs to predict syntactic dependencies and SRL graphs with greedy, linear-time inference and a new M-SELF transition for self-dependencies.
- Main contribution: joint transition architecture that encodes full parser state with stack LSTMs and recursive composition for feature-free, greedy joint parsing.
- Main contribution: introduction of M-SELF to handle self-dependencies and use of M-SWAP to allow crossing semantic arcs for CoNLL 2009 coverage.
- Main contribution: empirical results showing best performance among prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).
- Strength: technically sound integration of stack LSTMs into a synchronized joint parsing framework with clear interleaving protocol and linear complexity.
- Strength: strong empirical validation against relevant baselines and ablations showing mutual gains from joint training in-domain.
- Strength: practical efficiency with substantial test-time speedups (≈2.5×) versus a recent high-performing pipeline while maintaining competitive accuracy.
- Strength: coverage of CoNLL-specific phenomena via M-SELF and M-SWAP increases representational adequacy for target tasks.
- Strength: reasonable clarity and reproducibility with detailed transition inventory, training setup, hyperparameters, and promised open-source code.
- Weakness: limited novelty—transition system largely from Henderson et al. (2013) and stack LSTMs from Dyer et al. (2015); contributions are incremental.
- Weakness: greedy decoding without dynamic oracle, exploration, or beam search risks exposure bias and error propagation; no exploration of mitigations or accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Weakness: imperfect oracle conversion for semantic transitions (~1.5% of English sentences not perfectly encoded) is unquantified and may affect training/evaluation.
- Weakness: comparative evaluation gaps—no controlled comparisons to recent global-inference neural SRL systems and no statistical significance testing.
- Weakness: simplistic handling of unseen predicate senses (defaulting to “01”) without analysis of its effect on disambiguation or generalization.
- Question: quantify impact of imperfect semantic oracle—report upper-bound joint accuracy and performance excluding affected sentences.
- Question: were dynamic oracles, exploration-based training, or beam decoding tried, and what are the accuracy/runtime trade-offs versus greedy decoding?
- Question: how sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings (ablation without pretraining and with different embedding sizes)?
- Question: provide accuracy on unseen predicates and quantify how much the “01” default contributes; could a backoff classifier help?
- Question: have you run statistical significance tests across multiple seeds for main English results?
- Question: for multilingual experiments, what POS/morphological features were used, and could character-based embeddings compensate for missing morphology?
