Summary
The paper presents a transition-based joint parser that builds syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs simultaneously using stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history). The model uses greedy, linear-time inference with no hand-crafted features, and introduces minor transition-system adaptations (e.g., M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments, copying from buffer to support synchronized parsing). On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, it surpasses prior joint models and is competitive with feature-rich pipelines, and it shows reasonable multilingual performance and speed.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that learned continuous state representations (stack LSTMs) enable accurate joint syntactic–semantic dependency parsing with greedy, linear-time decoding, outperforming prior joint systems on CoNLL’08/’09 English.
2) A practical joint transition system design for synchronized syntax–semantics with a shared buffer and copy operations, including the new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments, and interleaving constraints to ensure both structures are built.
3) Extensive evaluation (English and multilingual) with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and measured speed, showing competitive accuracy without expert-crafted features.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results among joint models: The joint parser consistently improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, validating that global state representations via stack LSTMs are more effective than locally scoped features in earlier joint architectures.
- Simplicity, speed, and practicality: Greedy linear-time inference with learned features achieves 177.6±18s on the CoNLL’09 English test set on a single core, about 2.5× faster than a strong SRL pipeline baseline (Lei et al., 2015). End-to-end speed and low memory footprint (≤512 MB) are compelling for real-world use.
- Clear ablations clarifying the benefit of joint modeling: On CoNLL’09 English, the Semantics-only model underperforms the Hybrid and Joint variants, and Joint surpasses Hybrid in-domain, showing mutual benefits of syntactic–semantic coupling (with sensible out-of-domain behavior).
- Minimal feature engineering: The system relies on learned representations (pretrained + learned embeddings) and recursive composition functions, avoiding expensive syntactic path features that often dominate SRL pipelines. This demonstrates that much of the advantage of hand-designed features can be captured by learned state representations during incremental parsing.
- Thoughtful system design: The interleaving constraints, buffer copy (vs move) for arc-eager, and M-SELF transition address practical issues in synchronized parsing and NomBank-style annotations; these design choices are carefully motivated and effective.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The key technical idea—stack LSTMs encoding parser state—was introduced for syntactic dependency parsing by Dyer et al. (2015). The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013). The new elements (M-SELF, buffer-copy variant, interleaving policy) are useful but incremental. The main contribution is empirical transfer of stack LSTMs to joint parsing rather than a fundamentally new model.
- Greedy training/inference without exploration or beam contrasts: The model uses teacher-forced training and greedy decoding only. There is no comparison to small beam sizes, dynamic oracles, or reinforcement/DAgger-style training, which could affect error propagation in joint parsing. Given the known benefits of exploration for transition-based models, this omission limits the methodological depth.
- Incomplete analysis: While overall metrics are strong, the paper lacks detailed error analysis (e.g., role-wise SRL gains/losses, crossing arcs coverage, effect on rare predicates/labels), statistical significance testing, and oracle/upper-bound analyses (especially given the 1.5% of sentences where the semantic transition oracle is imperfect). It is also unclear how much the M-SELF transition specifically helps.
- Predicate identification and sense handling choices are underexplored: For CoNLL’08, the predicate identification component achieves 91.43% F1, but the impact of these errors on the final joint scores is not quantified; nor is there an ablation with gold predicate IDs. For CoNLL’09, unseen predicates default to sense “01,” which is often correct by type, but a token-level analysis (and alternatives) would better support this heuristic’s fairness and generality.
- Multilingual evaluation could be strengthened: The model is on par with top systems in many languages but underperforms in settings like small-data Japanese. No morphological features or character-based modeling are incorporated, which likely limits performance on morphologically rich languages; this is mentioned as future work but weakens the multilingual claim.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are results to beam size? Does a small beam (e.g., 4–8) or dynamic-oracle training reduce error propagation and improve SRL without large runtime penalties?
- Can you quantify the impact of the M-SELF transition (ablation) and the buffer-copy vs move decision on accuracy and speed?
- For CoNLL’08, what is the performance with gold predicate identification, and how much do predicate-ID errors contribute to overall gap vs pipelines?
- For CoNLL’09, how often is the “01” default sense used at test time, and what is its accuracy by token, not just type? Did you try back-off to distributional neighbors or lemma clusters?
- Are improvements over prior joint models statistically significant on the main test sets? Please include significance tests.
- Did you examine alternative interleaving schedules (e.g., allowing semantic actions earlier or later) and their effect on accuracy?

Additional comments
- Please include qualitative error analysis (e.g., improvements on non-projective semantic arcs via M-SWAP, long-distance arguments, nominal predicates) and per-role F1.
- Reporting results on CoNLL’08 with the same ablation variants used for CoNLL’09 would make the joint-benefit story more consistent.
- Reproducibility: Given sensitivity in neural parsers, include random seed ranges and variance over multiple runs.
