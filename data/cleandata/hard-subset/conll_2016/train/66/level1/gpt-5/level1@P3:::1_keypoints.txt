- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic–semantic parser using stack LSTMs for full state representation, enabling fully greedy, linear-time inference.
- Adapts Henderson et al. (2013) with three main changes: copying instead of moving buffer items, a new M-SELF action for semantic self-arcs, and M-PRED for predicate sense disambiguation with composition.
- Claims: (1) neural state representation via stack LSTMs replacing hand-crafted features for fast greedy decoding, (2) a practical engineered joint transition system with oracle and constraints, (3) extensive empirical comparison across joint/hybrid/semantics-only/syntax-only variants and multilingual tests.
- Strength: Achieves strong joint-model performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is substantially faster than prior joint and some pipeline systems.
- Strength: Demonstrates that learned stack-LSTM state representations reduce reliance on hand-crafted features and improve over earlier feature-driven joint models.
- Strength: Careful engineering of transition system (interleaving, M-SELF, copying tweak) yields a practical, linear-time joint parser.
- Strength: Competitive multilingual results without language-specific features, benefiting from pretrained embeddings.
- Strength: Transparent positioning relative to stronger pipeline systems and clear presentation of trade-offs.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty beyond applying stack LSTMs to an existing transition framework; main advance is representation learning and greedy inference.
- Weakness: Greedy training/decoding without exploration or global objectives likely causes exposure bias and may hurt out-of-domain SRL; no dynamic oracles, imitation learning, or beam experiments.
- Weakness: Fixed interleaving policy may bias arc construction and limit modeling capacity; alternatives or learned schedules are unexplored.
- Weakness: Incomplete ablations — effects of pretrained embeddings, POS/lemma inputs, pseudo-projectivization, copying change, M-SELF, and sense composition are not isolated.
- Weakness: Comparisons conflate modeling, resource, and feature differences; multilingual experiments omit morphology which may disadvantage performance in inflected languages.
- Question: Did you try dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling, or beam decoding/training, and how do they affect in/out-of-domain performance and speed?
- Question: Were alternative interleaving policies or learned schedulers explored, and what were the empirical differences?
- Question: How are the 1.5% of sentences that cannot be encoded by the oracle handled during training, and do they cap SRL F1?
- Question: What is the quantitative effect of copying vs standard arc-eager move on accuracy and speed, and are there issues with duplicated representations?
- Question: Can you provide ablations isolating pretrained embeddings, POS/lemma inputs, pseudo-projectivization, M-SELF, and predicate sense composition?
- Question: For predicate sense defaulting, what is the token-level accuracy on unseen predicates at test time?
- Additional comment: An error analysis by argument type, long-distance dependencies, and crossing arcs would clarify strengths and weaknesses.
- Additional comment: Character- or morpheme-level embeddings could address missing morphology in multilingual experiments without feature engineering.
- Overall: Solid engineering contribution showing practical gains from learned state representations with moderate novelty; leans toward accept pending stronger analysis/ablations and modest search/training improvements.
