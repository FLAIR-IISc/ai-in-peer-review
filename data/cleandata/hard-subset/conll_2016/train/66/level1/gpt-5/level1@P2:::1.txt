Summary
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint parser for syntactic and semantic dependencies that uses stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013), with interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions, and introduces an additional M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies. The approach eschews hand-crafted features, achieves linear-time inference (including feature computation), and reports strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, with competitive multilingual performance and fast runtime.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A unified, feature-lean, greedy joint parsing model that learns full parser-state representations with stack LSTMs, yielding the best reported performance among prior joint syntax–semantics models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English while running very fast.
2) A carefully designed transition system for joint parsing, including a new M-SELF transition and interleaving constraints, with recursive composition functions to represent growing syntactic and semantic structures within a single vector space.
3) An empirical study with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarifies when and how joint modeling helps, plus multilingual experiments indicating reasonable cross-lingual adaptability.

Strengths
- Empirical advances over prior joint models: The model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. This directly validates the core idea that learning full algorithm-state representations can replace extensive feature engineering in joint parsing. The ablations show that joint training beats semantics-only in-domain, confirming the utility of syntax for SRL.
- Simplicity and speed: The greedy, linear-time setup with stack LSTMs yields an end-to-end runtime of ~178s on CoNLL 2009 English on a single core, reported to be ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under identical settings. This makes the approach attractive for practical, large-scale use and low-latency settings.
- Feature-free joint modeling: Eliminating hand-crafted features for both syntax and semantics is non-trivial; the results show that learned state representations can capture information that pipelines normally access through expensive global syntactic features.
- Well-specified model and training details: The transition system and constraints are clearly laid out; stack-LSTM usage, composition functions for syntactic/semantic arcs and predicate senses, optimization choices (SGD with dropout), and pretrained embeddings are described with sufficient detail to reimplement. The addition of M-SELF is a precise and useful extension for handling NomBank-style self-arguments.
- Broad evaluation: The paper evaluates on CoNLL 2008/2009 English (with in-domain and OOD splits), includes multilingual results, and situates performance relative to both contemporaneous joint models and stronger pipeline SRL systems, making clear where the approach stands.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The transition system is inherited from Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack-LSTM machinery from Dyer et al. (2015). The primary novelty is combining these for joint parsing and removing hand-crafted features, plus M-SELF. While valuable, the contribution is more engineering/integration than fundamentally new modeling or inference.
- Greedy decoding without exploration training: The model uses oracle sequences and local cross-entropy training with greedy test-time inference. No dynamic oracles, cost-sensitive training, beam search, or global objectives are explored. The observed OOD drop where Hybrid > Joint suggests susceptibility to error propagation/overfitting that stronger training could mitigate.
- Not state-of-the-art vs. best pipelines: Although the paper is transparent about this, the approach falls short of top SRL systems that leverage rich syntactic features, global inference, reranking, or extra supervision (e.g., FrameNet). The central claim is “best among joint models,” which is narrower in impact; the paper would be stronger with clearer quantification of the margin over prior joint systems and statistical significance testing.
- Transition-oracle mismatch and coverage: The semantic oracle is imperfect (1.5% of English sentences in CoNLL 2009 cannot be encoded exactly), and multiple crossing arcs remain challenging despite M-SWAP. The paper does not quantify how much this mismatch affects training/evaluation or whether errors concentrate in specific constructions (e.g., heavy modifiers, control/raising).
- Limited analysis and ablation depth: While syntax-only/semantics-only/hybrid/joint variants are informative, the paper lacks fine-grained error analysis (e.g., by semantic role, predicate type, long-distance arcs) and ablations on key components (e.g., removal of action-history stack, effect of M-SELF, composition function choices). Sensitivity to pretrained embeddings is only briefly mentioned for Chinese; character/morphological features are not explored, limiting multilingual insight.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report absolute improvements over Henderson et al. (2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) per metric (LAS, SRL F1, Macro F1) with confidence intervals or significance tests? How consistent are gains across predicate types and argument labels?
- How often does the oracle mismatch (unreachable gold transitions) actually occur in training instances, and what is the impact on learning? Do you observe systematic errors on sentences with many crossing arcs?
- Did you experiment with beam search, dynamic oracles, or scheduled sampling? If so, what was the effect on in-domain vs. OOD SRL performance and runtime?
- How sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings (dimensionality, domain, OOV rate)? Have you tried character-level or morphological embeddings, particularly for morphologically rich languages?
- For CoNLL 2008 predicate identification, how tightly integrated is the biLSTM classifier with the joint parser (e.g., joint training or sharing embeddings)? Would joint training help reduce pipeline errors?
- Runtime comparison: Was the speed comparison with Lei et al. (2015) controlled for preprocessing (tagging, lemmatization) and hardware? Can you provide token/sec numbers and variance across runs?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and demonstrates a practical and fast joint parser that advances the state of the art among joint models. While the novelty is incremental and results trail the strongest pipelines, the work is timely, well-executed, and likely to be useful to the community.
