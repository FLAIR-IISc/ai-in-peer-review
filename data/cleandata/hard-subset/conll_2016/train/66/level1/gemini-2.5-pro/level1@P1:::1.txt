**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependency graphs. The model adapts the recently proposed stack LSTM architecture to learn a dense representation of the entire parser state, which consists of separate syntactic and semantic stacks, an input buffer, and a history of actions. This approach allows the parser to make greedy, sequential decisions without relying on any hand-crafted features. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating strong performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture for joint syntactic-semantic parsing.** The primary contribution is the successful extension of the stack LSTM framework to the more complex joint parsing task. By maintaining separate stack LSTMs for the syntactic and semantic structures, the model provides an elegant and principled way to encode the complex parser state, which is a significant advance over previous joint models that used more limited, local feature representations.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence for the viability of feature-free joint modeling.** The paper shows that a greedy model based purely on learned representations can outperform all previous joint parsing systems on standard benchmarks, including those that used more complex search algorithms like beam search. This result strongly supports the hypothesis that representation learning can replace complex feature engineering and search for challenging structured prediction tasks.
3.  **The development of a fast, linear-time joint parser.** By using a purely greedy inference procedure, the parser achieves linear-time complexity, making it significantly faster than many contemporary state-of-the-art systems. This efficiency makes the model highly practical for downstream applications that require rich linguistic analysis.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model Design.** The adaptation of stack LSTMs to the joint parsing setting is clean and well-motivated. The use of four separate LSTMs to summarize the syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history provides a unified method for capturing the entire parser state. This design obviates the need for the kind of complex, manually-designed indicator features that were characteristic of previous transition-based systems, representing a clear step forward in model design for this task.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance for Joint Models.** The paper presents compelling results, establishing a new state of the art among joint models on both the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. Surpassing previous work like Henderson et al. (2013) and Titov et al. (2009) by a clear margin is a significant achievement and validates the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The performance is also competitive with many of the top-performing pipelined systems from the original shared tasks, which is impressive for a greedy, feature-free approach.
3.  **Insightful Ablation and Comparative Analysis.** The experiments on CoNLL 2009, which compare Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint versions of the model, are particularly valuable. This controlled comparison clearly demonstrates the benefits of the joint approach on in-domain data. The finding that the Hybrid model (using gold syntax for training, predicted for testing) performs better on out-of-domain data is an interesting result that sheds light on the generalization challenges of joint models and provides an important direction for future work.
4.  **High Efficiency and Practicality.** The model's linear-time complexity is a major practical advantage. The authors substantiate this with a direct speed comparison to a strong contemporary system, showing a ~2.5x speedup. This, combined with the promise of an open-source implementation, makes the work a valuable contribution to the community, providing a tool that is not only accurate but also practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty of the Core Technique.** The paper's core technical component, the stack LSTM, was introduced in prior work by Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing. This work is therefore an application and adaptation of an existing method to a new, albeit more complex, task. While the adaptation is executed well and yields strong results, the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, as the extension largely involves duplicating the stack mechanism for the semantic component.
2.  **Performance Gap with Top Pipelined Systems.** While the model is state-of-the-art for *joint* systems, the authors are upfront that it does not match the performance of the top overall SRL systems of the time (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015). The performance gap (e.g., ~2 F1 points on CoNLL 2009 semantic F1) suggests that the rich, global features extracted from a complete syntactic parse in a pipeline setting, combined with global inference, still provide a powerful signal that this greedy, transition-based model does not fully capture.
3.  **Limitations of the Transition System.** The paper notes that the oracle conversion from gold parses to transition sequences is imperfect for 1.5% of sentences. This is an inherent limitation of the chosen transition system that places an upper bound on the model's performance. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis of this limitation, for example, by characterizing the types of structures that cannot be generated and discussing whether this is a more significant issue for this joint system than for prior work.
4.  **Clarity on Hyperparameter Tuning.** The authors state that some hyperparameters were "set intuitively" and that further tuning would likely lead to improvements. While understandable, this lack of a systematic tuning process makes the results harder to contextualize. It is unclear if the model is particularly sensitive to these choices, or how much performance might be gained from a more thorough search. A brief sensitivity analysis on key parameters, such as the LSTM hidden dimensions, would increase the reproducibility and robustness of the findings.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your analysis of the Joint vs. Hybrid models on in-domain vs. out-of-domain data is very interesting. You suggest the Joint model overfits to the training domain (WSJ). Could you elaborate on the potential source of this overfitting? For instance, do you suspect the model learns spurious correlations between specific syntactic constructions and semantic roles that are unique to WSJ? Have you considered alternative regularization strategies beyond dropout that might help the Joint model generalize better?
2.  For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is handled by a separate bidirectional LSTM classifier. Was there a specific reason not to integrate this step more tightly into the main parser, for example by adding a transition that identifies a word as a predicate? This would seem to create a more fully "joint" system.
3.  You introduce a new `M-SELF` transition to handle self-dependencies from NomBank. This seems like a useful addition. Could you provide a quantitative analysis of its impact? For example, how does the model perform specifically on these self-dependencies, and what is the effect on overall semantic F1 if this transition is removed from the model?
