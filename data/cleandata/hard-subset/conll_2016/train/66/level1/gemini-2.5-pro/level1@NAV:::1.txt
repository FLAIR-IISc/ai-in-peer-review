**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependency structures. The core innovation is the use of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state—including syntactic and semantic stacks, an input buffer, and an action history—thereby eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it establishes a new state of the art among joint models while being highly efficient. The primary contribution is the successful extension of the Stack LSTM parsing paradigm to the complex joint syntactic-semantic task, demonstrating that representation learning can effectively replace feature engineering in this setting. A secondary contribution is the thorough empirical analysis, including insightful ablation studies, which validates the model's effectiveness and efficiency compared to previous work.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper’s main idea—a feature-free joint parser based on learning a representation of the complete parser state—is elegant and impactful. This approach successfully extends recent advances in neural parsing to a more complex, joint structured prediction task. The strong results, outperforming all previous joint models on standard benchmarks, provide compelling evidence for the validity of this method.
2.  The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors benchmark their model against a wide array of relevant systems on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008/2009, multilingual), providing a clear picture of its capabilities. The ablation studies (e.g., Syntax-only vs. Joint) are particularly effective at isolating and quantifying the benefits of the joint learning framework.
3.  The model's efficiency is a significant practical advantage. Its greedy, linear-time inference makes it substantially faster than competing models that use beam search or global optimization, positioning it as an attractive and strong baseline for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses**
1.  While the model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint systems, its performance still falls short of top-tier pipelined systems that leverage rich syntactic features and global inference. This performance gap suggests that the learned representations, while powerful, may not yet fully capture the crucial long-distance structural information that explicit features (e.g., predicate-argument paths) provide.
2.  The novelty of the method is an incremental, though successful, extension of existing work. The model directly adapts the Stack LSTM framework from Dyer et al. (2015) and a transition system from Henderson et al. (2013) to the joint setting. While the engineering and empirical results are strong, the core conceptual advance is somewhat limited.
3.  The analysis of out-of-domain performance is underdeveloped. The paper notes that a pipelined variant (Hybrid) outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus due to overfitting. This interesting finding warrants deeper investigation into why the joint training process is more sensitive to domain shift.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on why the Joint model appears more susceptible to overfitting on out-of-domain data compared to the Hybrid pipeline model? Does the joint training learn domain-specific syntactic-semantic correlations that do not generalize well?
