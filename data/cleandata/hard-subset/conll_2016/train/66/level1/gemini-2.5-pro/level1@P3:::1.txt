**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents a novel transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependency structures. The core of the model is its use of four Stack Long Short-Term Memory networks (Stack LSTMs) to learn a continuous vector representation of the entire parser state: the syntactic stack, the semantic stack, the buffer of upcoming words, and the history of actions taken. This approach allows the model to make greedy, local decisions without relying on any hand-crafted, expert features. The parser is based on the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013), extended with a new transition to handle self-dependencies in semantic graphs. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all previous joint models. It achieves this performance while maintaining linear-time complexity, making it significantly faster than many state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions of this work**
1.  The primary contribution is the successful application of Stack LSTMs to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. While Stack LSTMs were previously introduced for syntactic parsing (Dyer et al., 2015), this work demonstrates their effectiveness in representing a more intricate state involving two separate but interacting stacks (syntactic and semantic). This provides strong evidence that learned, continuous representations can effectively replace complex, hand-engineered features for joint modeling tasks.
2.  The paper presents a new state-of-the-art result for *jointly learned* models on the widely-used CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. By outperforming prior joint systems like Henderson et al. (2013), the work establishes a new performance baseline for this class of models.
3.  The work provides a fast, accurate, and feature-free joint parser. The linear-time complexity and demonstrated speed are significant practical contributions, offering a strong alternative to slower, more complex pipelined or globally-optimized systems.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Effective State Representation:** The key strength of this paper is its elegant approach to modeling the parser state. By using Stack LSTMs to summarize the syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history, the model obviates the need for traditional feature engineering, which is notoriously difficult and intuition-driven in complex, joint transition systems. The strong results, particularly the significant improvement over the Henderson et al. (2013) system on which the algorithm is based, directly validate the power of this learned representation. This is a significant step forward for incremental parsing models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Analysis:** The paper achieves impressive results, establishing a new state-of-the-art among joint parsing models on standard benchmarks. The authors are commendably transparent about where their model stands relative to the overall state-of-the-art (i.e., behind feature-rich pipelines and globally optimized models), which contextualizes their contribution appropriately. The inclusion of several variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides a thorough ablation study that yields valuable insights, such as the mutual benefit of joint modeling on in-domain data and the tendency for it to overfit on out-of-domain data.
3.  **High Efficiency:** The greedy, linear-time nature of the parser is a major practical advantage. The reported speedup of nearly 2.5x over a recent state-of-the-art system (Lei et al., 2015) is substantial. In an era of increasingly complex and computationally expensive models, a system that pushes performance boundaries while remaining highly efficient is a welcome and important contribution. This makes the model a viable candidate for practical applications where both speed and accuracy are required.
4.  **Clear and Reproducible Methodology:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is described in sufficient detail. The transition system is clearly defined (Table 1), the neural architecture is well-illustrated (Figure 3), and the training procedure is standard. The authors' commitment to releasing an open-source implementation further strengthens the work and will be a valuable resource for the community.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Performance Gap with Overall State-of-the-Art:** While the model is the best among joint systems, it still lags behind top-performing pipelined systems from the original shared tasks (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009) and more recent state-of-the-art SRL systems (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015; Lei et al., 2015). The primary reason for this is likely the purely greedy inference. Top systems often benefit from global features, beam search, or reranking to resolve local ambiguities that a greedy model cannot. While the authors acknowledge this, the gap remains a significant limitation of the proposed approach in terms of pushing the absolute performance ceiling.
2.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The paper's novelty lies almost entirely in the application of Stack LSTMs as a scoring function. The underlying transition system is largely adopted from Henderson et al. (2013), with the single addition of the M-SELF transition. While the application is novel and effective, the work does not propose a fundamentally new parsing algorithm or explore alternative ways to synchronize the syntactic and semantic processes. This narrows the scope of the contribution to a (very effective) new modeling technique for an existing algorithm.
3.  **Incomplete Hyperparameter Optimization:** The authors state that "Other hyperparameters have been set intuitively; careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." This statement weakens the empirical claims. Without a systematic hyperparameter search, it is difficult to know if the reported results represent the model's true potential or if the current configuration is brittle. For example, the dimensions of the various embeddings and hidden layers were tuned, but other crucial parameters (learning rate schedule, optimizer choice) seem to be fixed based on prior work. This introduces uncertainty into the comparisons with other systems.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The primary weakness of the model appears to be its greedy nature. Have you experimented with incorporating beam search into your decoder? While this would increase the runtime, it might significantly close the performance gap with the global SOTA models and would provide a clearer picture of the accuracy vs. speed trade-off for this architecture.
2.  You introduce a new M-SELF transition to handle nominal predicates. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of its impact? For instance, what is the performance on the subset of semantic dependencies that are self-loops with and without this transition? An ablation study removing M-SELF would quantify the importance of this addition to the transition system.
3.  The result that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus is very interesting, suggesting the Joint model overfits to the WSJ training data. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this is the case? Could it be that the tightly coupled representations learned during joint training are too domain-specific, whereas the Hybrid model's pipeline approach provides a more robust syntactic analysis that generalizes better?
