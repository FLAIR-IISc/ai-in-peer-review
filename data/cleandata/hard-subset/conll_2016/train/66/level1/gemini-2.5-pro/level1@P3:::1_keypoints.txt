- Proposes a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic and semantic dependencies using four Stack LSTMs to represent syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history.  
- Makes greedy, local decisions without hand-crafted features and adds an M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies.  
- Evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009 English benchmarks, outperforming previous joint models while retaining linear-time complexity and substantial speedups.  
- Primary contribution: successful application of Stack LSTMs to model a complex joint parser state, replacing hand-engineered features with learned continuous representations.  
- Sets a new state-of-the-art among jointly learned models on standard CoNLL benchmarks.  
- Provides a fast, accurate, feature-free joint parser with practical linear-time performance.  
- Strength: novel and effective state representation that improves over Henderson et al. (2013) and validates learned representations for incremental parsing.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance with transparent comparisons and useful ablation variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid).  
- Strength: high efficiency—greedy, linear-time parser with ~2.5x speedup over recent strong baselines.  
- Strength: clear, reproducible methodology and commitment to open-source release.  
- Weakness: still lags behind overall state-of-the-art pipelined and globally optimized systems, likely due to purely greedy inference.  
- Weakness: limited algorithmic novelty—main innovation is applying Stack LSTMs to an existing transition system with a single added transition.  
- Weakness: incomplete hyperparameter optimization and lack of systematic tuning weakens empirical claims.  
- Q to authors: Have you tried beam search or other global decoding to trade off runtime for improved accuracy?  
- Q to authors: Please provide an ablation quantifying the impact of the M-SELF transition on self-loop semantic dependency performance.  
- Q to authors: Why does the Hybrid model generalize better out-of-domain than the Joint model—do tightly coupled joint representations overfit domain-specific patterns?
