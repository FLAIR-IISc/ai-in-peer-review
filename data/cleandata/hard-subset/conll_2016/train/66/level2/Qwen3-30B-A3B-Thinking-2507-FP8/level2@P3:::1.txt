

Summary: The paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that learns representations of the entire algorithm state using stack LSTMs. It achieves competitive performance on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks while using a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity. The main contributions are: (1) a joint parsing model that learns representations of the entire algorithm state without expert-crafted features, (2) a greedy inference approach with linear time complexity, and (3) competitive performance on CoNLL shared tasks compared to previous joint parsing models.

Strengths: The work demonstrates a significant computational advantage with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015). This makes the approach practical for real-world applications where speed is critical. The paper provides a thorough comparison to previous joint parsing models, showing that the proposed approach outperforms all previous joint models on CoNLL 2008-9 English tasks. The work also successfully addresses the challenge of joint parsing without relying on expensive features derived from full syntactic parses, demonstrating that neural representations can effectively capture necessary information during parsing.

Weaknesses: The paper does not sufficiently clarify the novelty of applying stack LSTMs to joint parsing compared to Dyer et al. (2015), which introduced stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing. The distinction between the syntactic parsing work and the joint parsing approach needs to be more clearly articulated to establish the contribution. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems that use expert-crafted features is limited, with the paper acknowledging that performance is inferior to these systems but not providing a detailed analysis of why or how the joint model could be integrated with these features. The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of greedy inference, particularly how it compares to beam search in terms of performance trade-offs, which is a significant aspect of the approach.

Questions to Authors: How does the model's representation of the entire algorithm state specifically differ from the representations used in Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing? What would be the computational cost of implementing beam search instead of greedy inference, and how would that affect performance? How might the model be integrated with expert-crafted features used by state-of-the-art systems without losing the computational efficiency advantage?

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of joint parsing, particularly in demonstrating that neural representations can effectively replace expensive feature extraction. The computational efficiency is a significant practical advantage. However, the paper would benefit from more precise articulation of the novelty compared to prior work, a deeper analysis of the trade-offs between greedy inference and more complex search strategies, and a clearer path for integrating with complementary techniques that could further improve performance. The presentation of the joint parsing approach is technically sound, but the connection to prior work needs strengthening to better establish the contribution. The model's performance on multilingual data is promising, but the paper could provide more insight into why Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings while other languages do not. The work would be strengthened by addressing these points, particularly the novelty claim, to better position the contribution within the broader literature.
