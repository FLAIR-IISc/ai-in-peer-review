

Summary: This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire algorithm state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The main contributions are: (1) a novel application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state for joint parsing; (2) demonstration that greedy inference can achieve competitive performance on joint parsing tasks; and (3) an efficient linear-time parsing algorithm that outperforms previous joint models on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing how stack LSTMs can effectively replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing, which is an important insight for the field. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks, which is a notable achievement given the dominance of pipeline approaches in the literature.

2. The linear time complexity of the parser (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) provides a practical advantage over more complex systems, making it suitable for real-world applications where efficiency matters. This efficiency is well-documented and compared to relevant baselines.

3. The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the transition-based parsing algorithm and the stack LSTM architecture, making it easy to understand the technical contributions. The implementation details and experimental setup are well-specified, enhancing reproducibility.

4. The authors provide a thorough comparison to related work, including both joint models and pipeline approaches, which helps contextualize their contribution within the broader field of syntactic-semantic parsing.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address why their joint model outperforms pipeline approaches that use full syntactic parses for semantic analysis, which are still the dominant approach in the field. While they acknowledge that pipeline systems using expert-crafted features achieve higher performance, they don't provide a clear analysis of why their joint approach is competitive despite not having access to the same features.

2. The ablation studies are limited, particularly regarding the specific contribution of the stack LSTM representation. The paper compares their model to Henderson et al. (2008) and variants but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the stack LSTM representation is the key factor driving performance gains, rather than other aspects of their architecture.

3. The multilingual results are somewhat underwhelming, particularly for Chinese where performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings. The paper doesn't clearly explain why this dependency exists or how it might be addressed in future work, which limits the generalizability of their approach.

4. The paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems but doesn't explore how their approach could be integrated with those systems. This is a missed opportunity to demonstrate how their efficient representation learning could complement existing high-performance pipelines.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that their joint model outperforms pipeline approaches on CoNLL 2008-9, but pipeline systems like Zhao et al. (2009) achieve higher performance. Could you clarify why your joint model is competitive with these pipeline systems despite not having access to the same features?

2. The authors mention that their model relies on pretrained embeddings for Chinese performance. Could you provide more details on how the embeddings are incorporated and whether you've explored alternatives to pretrained embeddings for low-resource languages?

3. The paper claims that their model "learns a representation of the entire algorithm state" but doesn't clearly demonstrate how this representation differs from and improves upon the representation used in Henderson et al. (2008). Could you provide more specific evidence of the benefits of this approach?

4. The paper states that their model is "not as accurate as the top systems" but doesn't provide a clear analysis of where the model fails. Could you elaborate on the specific types of errors that are most common in your model compared to the top systems?
