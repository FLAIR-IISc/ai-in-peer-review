

Summary: This paper introduces a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state, enabling greedy inference with linear time complexity. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics without expert features, (2) state-of-the-art performance among joint models on CoNLL 2008–9 English tasks, and (3) a highly efficient implementation (177.6s on single-core for CoNLL 2009 test set).

Strengths:  
1. **Strong empirical validation**: The parser achieves SOTA joint parsing performance on CoNLL 2008–9 English, outperforming prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al. 2008, Gesmundo et al. 2009) by 1.5–2.3% in semantic F1. The results are statistically significant and rigorously compared to relevant baselines.  
2. **Compelling efficiency**: The 2.5× speed advantage over pipeline systems (e.g., 177.6s vs. 439.9s for Lei et al.) is well-documented with end-to-end runtime measurements, making it practically valuable for real-world deployment.  
3. **Clear novelty**: The shift from local state representations (e.g., top-of-stack features) to full-state stack LSTMs for joint parsing is well-motivated and effectively demonstrated, addressing a key limitation in prior work.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Limited comparison to SOTA SRL systems**: The paper acknowledges falling behind pipeline-based state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Zhao et al. 2009, Lei et al. 2015) but does not quantify the gap or analyze why their greedy approach underperforms on specific tasks (e.g., out-of-domain Brown corpus).  
2. **Missing ablation on stack LSTM**: The contribution hinges on stack LSTMs, yet no ablation study compares the full-state representation against a local-feature baseline (e.g., using only top-of-stack embeddings), weakening the novelty claim.  
3. **Greedy inference limitations**: While runtime efficiency is praised, the paper does not explore trade-offs between greedy inference (used here) and beam search (used in top pipelines), nor justify why the performance drop is acceptable for the speed gain.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How would incorporating expert-crafted features (e.g., syntactic paths) from pipeline systems affect your model’s performance on the out-of-domain Brown test set?  
2. Could you provide an ablation study showing the impact of the stack LSTM’s full-state representation versus a local-feature variant?  
3. Given that your model underperforms on the Brown corpus (indicating potential overfitting), what strategies would you propose to improve generalization?
