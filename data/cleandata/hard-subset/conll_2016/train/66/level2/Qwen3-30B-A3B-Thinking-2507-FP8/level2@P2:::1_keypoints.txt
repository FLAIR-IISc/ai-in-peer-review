- Summary: Proposes a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to model the full parser state, claiming feature-free learning and top joint-model performance with linear-time inference (177.6s on CoNLL 2009).

- Contribution 1: Applies stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic parser state (stacks, buffer, action history), removing hand-crafted features.

- Contribution 2: Shows empirical superiority over prior joint syntax–semantics models on CoNLL 2008–9 English.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates practical linear-time runtime (177.6s) markedly faster than pipeline systems.

- Strength 1: Novel architecture—using stack LSTMs for the full state is a clear technical advance and is well-motivated and implemented.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical validation—ablations show joint learning outperforms hybrid and semantics-only variants in-domain.

- Strength 3: Practical runtime advantage—the greedy, linear-time design yields substantially faster inference than pipelines.

- Weakness 1: Misleading “best performance” claim—comparison is only to other joint models and omits stronger pipeline state-of-the-art systems.

- Weakness 2: Inaccurate “no expert features” claim—the model relies on pretrained word embeddings, which are effectively expert features.

- Weakness 3: Weak multilingual evaluation—Chinese and Japanese results depend on embeddings and small-data issues, undermining claims of easy adaptability.

- Weakness 4: Missing ablation—no test showing whether stack LSTMs are necessary versus simpler top-stack or feature-based representations.

- Question 1: Why omit direct comparison to Zhao et al. (2009) and how does the joint model fare against that pipeline state of the art?

- Question 2: How do the authors justify “no expert-crafted features” given pretrained embeddings, and can comparable results be achieved with random embeddings?
