- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithm state.  
- Uses greedy inference with linear time complexity and achieves competitive results on CoNLL 2008-9 English tasks.  
- Main contributions: end-to-end state representation without expert-crafted features, linear-time greedy inference, and strong CoNLL task performance.  
- Strength: significant speed advantage — parses CoNLL 2009 English test in ~177.6±18s on a single core, ~2.5× faster than a prior pipeline.  
- Strength: thorough comparison showing it outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008-9 English.  
- Strength: demonstrates neural representations can replace expensive features from full syntactic parses.  
- Weakness: novelty unclear relative to Dyer et al. (2015) which introduced stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing; distinction needs clearer articulation.  
- Weakness: limited comparison and analysis versus state-of-the-art systems that use expert-crafted features; lacks ways to integrate such features.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of greedy inference limitations and performance trade-offs compared to beam search.  
- Question: how does the model’s entire-algorithm-state representation concretely differ from Dyer et al. (2015)?  
- Question: what is the computational cost and expected performance impact of replacing greedy inference with beam search?  
- Question: how could the model be integrated with expert-crafted features without losing its computational efficiency?  
- Suggestion: strengthen novelty claims and connections to prior work, analyze greedy vs. beam trade-offs, and explain multilingual results such as Chinese reliance on pretrained embeddings.
