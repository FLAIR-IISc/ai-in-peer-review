- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs that achieves state- or near state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008/2009 with greedy, linear-time inference.  
- Demonstrates that stack LSTMs can replace expensive, hand-crafted features for high-performance joint parsing.  
- Shows multilingual adaptability with results on CoNLL 2009 beyond English (secondary contribution).  

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results outperforming previous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and competitive with pipeline approaches.  
- Greedy inference yields guaranteed linear time complexity, improving scalability.  
- Eliminates reliance on expert-crafted features, simplifying development and improving generalization.  
- Paper is clearly written with helpful figures and tables explaining method and experiments.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited direct comparison to absolute state-of-the-art SRL systems that use full parses and complex reranking.  
- Evaluation is concentrated on CoNLL datasets, limiting evidence of broader generalizability.  
- Ablation study lacks depth on why configurations perform differently and on causes of joint-model overfitting (esp. out-of-domain).  
- Insufficient error analysis to identify common failure modes and guide improvements.  

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide a detailed error analysis comparing joint-model errors to those from stronger pipeline systems?  
- What is the computational cost of stack LSTMs specifically, versus simpler feature-based representations?  
- Were any data augmentation strategies tried to mitigate poor performance for low-resource languages like Japanese?  
- How sensitive are results to stack LSTM initialization and would alternative initialization strategies affect performance?  

Additional comments:
- Related work section should be expanded to include more recent neural SRL and joint-model advances.  
- Clarify hyperparameter tuning procedures; structured skipgram embedding usage is a helpful detail.
