This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on expert-crafted features. The core contribution is a demonstration that strong joint parsing performance can be achieved with a simple, greedy algorithm and learned representations, competitive with more complex and feature-engineered systems. A second contribution is the efficient, linear-time inference of the proposed model. Finally, the results on CoNLL 2008/2009 demonstrate competitive performance, and in some cases, state-of-the-art results among joint learners, especially considering the computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, surpassing previous joint parsing models. This is a significant achievement, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. The comparison against a variety of baselines, including both joint and pipelined models, provides a solid understanding of the model’s capabilities.
2. **Computational Efficiency:** The linear-time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a major strength. This makes the model practical for real-world applications where speed is critical. The reported runtime (177.6s on CoNLL 2009) is notably faster than many state-of-the-art systems.
3. **Novel Representation Learning:** The use of stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser state is a key innovation. The paper convincingly argues that this approach allows the model to circumvent the need for expensive, manually-designed features, a common bottleneck in traditional parsing systems. The fact that the model performs well *without* these features is compelling.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model, algorithm, and experimental setup. The explanations of the transitions and stack LSTMs are particularly helpful. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm in the supplementary material is also beneficial.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strongest Baselines:** While the paper compares against a good range of models, the performance is not directly compared to the very strongest, feature-rich, pipeline-based systems and systems utilizing optimal decoding. The authors acknowledge this but framing the performance as "comparable" to these systems may be slightly misleading, as the best performing systems still achieve higher scores. A more detailed breakdown of the performance gap would be helpful.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study on Stack LSTM Components:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of stack LSTMs overall, but it would be valuable to see an ablation study examining the contribution of individual components (e.g., the different stack LSTMs for S, M, and B). This would help to better understand which aspects of the architecture are most critical for performance.
3. **Sense Disambiguation Handling of OOV Predicates:** The handling of out-of-vocabulary predicates (defaulting to sense "01") is a pragmatic solution but could potentially limit performance. A more sophisticated approach to OOV predicate sense disambiguation might be explored. The impact of this decision on the overall results is not clearly quantified.
4. **Multilingual Results – Limited Discussion:** While the multilingual results are reported, the discussion is brief. Providing more insight into the performance across different languages, and any observations about language-specific challenges or adaptations, would strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison to the strongest baseline systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015, Zhao et al., 2009), quantifying the performance gap and discussing the trade-offs between your model's efficiency and their accuracy?
2.  Would you consider performing an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of individual stack LSTMs (S, M, B, A) to the overall performance?
3.  What is the distribution of OOV predicates in the test sets, and what percentage of the time does defaulting to sense "01" result in a correct prediction?
4.  For the multilingual experiments, could you elaborate on any language-specific observations or adaptations that were made?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "pseudo-projectivization" is used without explanation. A brief definition or reference would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the technique.
*   The reference to "structured skipgram embeddings" could benefit from a slightly more descriptive explanation for readers who are not experts in word embeddings.
*  Minor: In Table 3, "Macro F1" is not defined in the caption.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The proposed model is both effective and efficient, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
