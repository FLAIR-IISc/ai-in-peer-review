This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on expert-crafted features. The primary contribution is a competitive joint parsing model achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, while maintaining linear time complexity through greedy inference. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs in bypassing the need for expensive, hand-engineered features in joint parsing, previously considered crucial for high performance. The multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 offer a further, though less substantial, contribution, showing adaptability to languages beyond English.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser demonstrably outperforms previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. The comparison to previous state-of-the-art systems, including those relying on pipeline approaches and extensive feature engineering, is compelling. The results highlight the efficacy of the proposed stack LSTM approach for joint modeling.
2. **Linear Time Complexity:** The use of a greedy inference algorithm guarantees linear time complexity, a significant advantage for practical applications. This is clearly articulated and justified, contrasting with the computational expense of optimal decoding or pipeline-based approaches. This is a valuable aspect, especially when considering scalability.
3. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Features:** The elimination of expert-crafted features is a noteworthy accomplishment, simplifying the development process and reducing reliance on linguistic expertise. This simplifies the model and enhances its potential for generalization. The successful replacement of these features with learned representations demonstrates the power of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the transition system, stack LSTMs, and training procedure. The figures and tables are helpful in understanding the model and experimental results.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Comparison to Current State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares to previous CoNLL shared task submissions, it acknowledges that its performance does not match the absolute state-of-the-art SRL systems which utilize features from full syntactic parses and complex reranking schemes. A more direct comparison, perhaps including a reproduction or analysis of a leading system, would strengthen the claims. The paper mentions these systems but doesn't sufficiently contextualize the gap.
2. **Over-reliance on CoNLL Datasets:** The evaluation is primarily focused on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, demonstrating performance on more diverse datasets or tasks could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model's generalizability.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study Details:** The ablation study (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) is valuable, but the discussion of *why* certain configurations perform as they do could be more detailed. Specifically, the observed overfitting of the Joint model on the out-of-domain data warrants further investigation and explanation. A more granular analysis of the learned representations could provide insights.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying common error types and discussing potential improvements based on these observations would enhance the impact of the work.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the joint model, particularly those that differentiate it from the stronger, pipeline-based systems?
2. What is the computational cost of the stack LSTMs compared to simpler feature representations? While the overall runtime is reported, understanding the contribution of the LSTMs to this runtime would be useful.
3. The paper notes limitations with Japanese performance due to data scarcity. Were any data augmentation techniques explored to mitigate this issue?
4. How sensitive is the model to the initialization of the stack LSTMs? Would different initialization strategies significantly impact performance?

Additional Comments:

The discussion of related work is relatively brief. Expanding this section to include more recent advances in neural SRL and joint modeling would provide a more complete context for the contributions of this paper. The reference to “structured skipgram” embeddings is helpful. Consider clarifying the hyperparameter tuning process in more detail.
