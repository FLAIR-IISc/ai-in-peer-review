This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state without relying on expert-crafted features. The system achieves strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models.

**Contributions:**

1. **Strongest:** The achievement of state-of-the-art results among joint syntactic-semantic parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks is a significant contribution. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its competitive edge against prior work in the field.
2. **Significant:** The successful application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntactic and semantic dependencies, *without* manual feature engineering, is a noteworthy contribution. This is particularly valuable as it reduces the reliance on costly and time-consuming feature design, offering a more data-driven and potentially more generalizable approach.
3. **Moderate:** The demonstration of linear-time complexity for the greedy inference algorithm is valuable, specifically regarding practical applications. While not a novel theoretical result (building on Nivre’s analysis), highlighting this property alongside strong performance is beneficial.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates strong performance on standard datasets, convincingly showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to previous joint parsing models and pipeline systems provides a clear benchmark.
2. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs:** Utilizing stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser state represents a significant innovation. This allows the model to capture complex relationships without hand-engineered features, a common bottleneck in NLP.
3. **Efficient Inference:** The linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm makes the parser practical for real-world applications where speed is crucial. This is a valuable advantage over more complex, but slower, parsing approaches.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, experimental setup, and results. The use of examples and figures aids in understanding the technical details.
5. **Open-Source Release:** The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation is a positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strongest Baselines:** While the paper compares favorably to previous *joint* models, it acknowledges falling short of the best-performing *pipeline* systems that utilize expert-crafted features and optimal decoding. The discussion on why these systems are not directly comparable feels somewhat dismissive; a more detailed analysis of the performance gap and potential integration strategies would be beneficial.
2. **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The choice of greedy inference, while providing efficiency, may limit the model's ability to find the globally optimal parse. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough exploration of the impact of greedy decoding on performance would be valuable. Even a brief analysis of error cases stemming from greedy choices could strengthen the paper.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but provides limited details on the process. More specifics on the tuning strategy (e.g., grid search, random search) and the criteria used for selection would enhance reproducibility.
4. **Multilingual Results:** While multilingual results are presented, the analysis is relatively brief. A more in-depth discussion of the model's performance across different languages, and potential adaptations for low-resource languages, could strengthen the paper.
5. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of individual components, such as the different stack LSTMs or the composition functions. This makes it difficult to assess the relative importance of each element in the overall system.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of using optimal decoding (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015) within your framework, considering the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency?
2. What types of error cases are most frequently encountered due to the use of greedy inference, and could you provide examples?
3. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used for selecting the optimal values?
4. How might the model be adapted to better handle low-resource languages, and have you considered techniques like cross-lingual transfer learning?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "structured skipgram embeddings" is helpful, and the inclusion of a link to the GitHub repository of the system is appreciated.
*   The use of Figure 1 to illustrate an example parse is effective.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the runtime performance of the system compared to other relevant approaches.
