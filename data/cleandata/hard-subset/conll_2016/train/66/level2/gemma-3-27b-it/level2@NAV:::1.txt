## Review of "A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependencies"

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state without relying on hand-crafted features. The core idea – utilizing stack LSTMs to effectively capture parser state for joint parsing – is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in NLP: avoiding error propagation in pipelined systems.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating that strong parsing performance can be achieved through a purely learned, greedy approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing, achieving state-of-the-art results amongst models employing similar joint learning strategies on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets.  A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons with both pipelined and other joint models, along with an analysis of ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only). Finally, the efficient linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm makes the model potentially practical for real-world applications.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The experimental results are compelling, particularly the performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, outperforming previous joint models. The authors clearly articulate the advantages of their approach and place their work within the context of existing literature. The analysis of different model configurations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information. The reported runtime is also a notable positive aspect.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems which *do* utilize expert-crafted features and optimal decoding is somewhat dismissive, framing them as "orthogonal" without exploring potential integration. A more thorough discussion of these avenues for improvement would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the model's performance on multilingual data, while comparable to other systems, could benefit from more in-depth analysis of language-specific challenges. The impact of the pseudo-projectivization step could also be more clearly discussed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  While you acknowledge the strengths of systems utilizing expert-crafted features, could you elaborate on potential strategies to incorporate such features into your framework without sacrificing the benefits of end-to-end learning?
2.  The paper mentions limitations regarding unseen predicates. Could you discuss potential methods for addressing this issue beyond defaulting to sense “01”?
3.  Could you provide more details on the specific parameters of the Stack LSTMs (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit size) and the rationale behind their selection?
