- Transition-based parser jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs, avoiding expert-crafted features.  
- Core contribution: simple greedy algorithm + learned representations achieves strong joint parsing performance competitive with more complex, feature-engineered systems.  
- Second contribution: efficient, linear-time inference for the model.  
- Empirical results: competitive and sometimes state-of-the-art on CoNLL 2008/2009 among joint learners, with notable computational efficiency.  

- Strength: Strong empirical results, outperforming previous joint models and compared against a variety of baselines.  
- Strength: Computational efficiency via linear-time greedy inference (reported 177.6s on CoNLL 2009).  
- Strength: Novel use of stack LSTMs to learn parser-state representations, obviating manual feature engineering.  
- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful transition and stack LSTM explanations and example run.  

- Weakness: Limited comparison to the strongest, feature-rich pipeline and optimal-decoding baselines; performance gap not fully quantified.  
- Weakness: No ablation study isolating the contributions of individual stack LSTM components (e.g., S, M, B, A).  
- Weakness: OOV predicate handling defaults to sense "01," which may limit performance and whose impact is not quantified.  
- Weakness: Multilingual results are reported but discussed only briefly, lacking language-specific analysis or adaptations.  

- Question: Please provide a detailed, quantitative comparison to the strongest baselines (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2009) discussing accuracy vs. efficiency trade-offs.  
- Question: Can you perform an ablation study to measure the contribution of each stack LSTM (S, M, B, A)?  
- Question: What is the OOV predicate distribution in test sets and how often does defaulting to sense "01" produce a correct prediction?  
- Question: Can you elaborate on language-specific observations or adaptations in the multilingual experiments?
