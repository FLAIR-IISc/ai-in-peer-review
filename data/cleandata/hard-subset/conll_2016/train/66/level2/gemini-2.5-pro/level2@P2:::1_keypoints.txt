- Presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency structures using Stack LSTMs to represent the full parser state, evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009 with strong results.

- Contribution: Novel application of representation learning (Stack LSTMs) to joint syntactic-semantic parsing, showing learned parser-state embeddings suffice for complex structured prediction.

- Contribution: Develops a fast, linear-time greedy joint parser that is competitive with top systems and substantially faster than comparable state-of-the-art models.

- Contribution: Provides a comprehensive empirical analysis and ablation study across multiple datasets and model variants (Joint, Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid).

- Strength: Successfully scales Stack LSTMs to the more complex joint parsing problem, outperforming prior joint models that relied on beam search and hand-crafted features.

- Strength: Rigorous, insightful experimental evaluation including multilingual benchmarks and useful comparisons between model variants revealing trade-offs like Hybrid outperforming Joint out-of-domain.

- Strength: High efficiency and practical relevance—greedy linear-time parsing with reported speeds ≈2.5× faster than a recent state-of-the-art and open-source release.

- Strength: Clear presentation with well-explained transition system, architecture diagrams, and transparent experimental details.

- Weakness: Still trails state-of-the-art pipelined systems by a non-trivial margin (e.g., ~2–3 semantic F1 points), raising questions about practical benefits over strong pipelines.

- Weakness: Limited technical novelty beyond combining existing components (Stack LSTMs and interleaved transition system) with little discussion of specific extension challenges or design motivations.

- Weakness: Transition system has an imperfect oracle for ~1.5% of sentences with crossing semantic arcs, placing an upper bound on achievable performance that is not fully analyzed.

- Question: Can the authors elaborate on why joint training may be more susceptible to domain-specific overfitting than the pipelined approach, given Hybrid beats Joint out-of-domain?

- Question: Instead of defaulting unseen predicates to sense "01," could the learned contextual representation be used to predict senses dynamically via similarity to seen predicates?

- Question: Can the authors estimate the oracle performance (F1) obtainable from the oracle transition sequence versus gold parses to contextualize the ceiling imposed by the transition-based approach?
