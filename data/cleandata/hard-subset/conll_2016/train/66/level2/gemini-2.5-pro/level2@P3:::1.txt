**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency structures. The model's primary innovation is its use of stack Long Short-Term Memories (LSTMs) to learn a single vector representation that summarizes the entire state of the parsing algorithm—encompassing a syntactic stack, a semantic stack, a buffer of upcoming words, and a history of actions. This learned representation is then used by a classifier to make parsing decisions, entirely obviating the need for manually engineered features. The system operates in linear time. On the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared task benchmarks, the model is shown to outperform all previously published joint parsing systems.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Architecture for Joint Parsing:** The main contribution is the successful extension of the stack LSTM framework to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The four-stack architecture provides an elegant and effective method for learning a holistic representation of the parser state, demonstrating that complex structural dependencies can be captured without explicit feature engineering.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance for Joint Models:** The work establishes a new state-of-the-art on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 tasks among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics. The comprehensive experiments, including insightful ablation studies, convincingly demonstrate the superiority of this approach over previous joint models that relied on local features or more complex search algorithms.
3.  **An Efficient and Practical Parser:** By using a greedy inference algorithm, the parser achieves linear-time complexity. The reported parsing speed is significantly faster than contemporary high-performance systems, making this a practical and valuable tool for downstream applications that require both syntactic and semantic analysis at scale.

**Strengths**

1.  **Model Elegance and Novelty:** The proposed model is a clear and principled application of representation learning to a complex structured prediction problem. Moving away from hand-crafted features towards a learned representation of the complete parser state is a significant conceptual advance. The design is clean and powerful.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a strong and convincing evaluation. It reports results on multiple standard datasets (CoNLL 2008, 2009 English, and 2009 multilingual), compares against a wide range of relevant systems, and includes well-designed ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid). This rigorous evaluation solidly supports the paper's claims.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The transition system, model architecture, and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail to allow for replication. The commitment to release an open-source implementation is a valuable contribution to the community.
4.  **Honest Positioning of the Work:** The paper does a commendable job of situating its contributions within the broader literature. It clearly acknowledges that its performance does not match state-of-the-art systems that use pipelined architectures, global features, optimal decoding, or additional training data, correctly identifying those as orthogonal research directions. This provides a balanced and accurate picture of the work's impact.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Representations:** While the model's performance is impressive, the paper provides little insight into the nature of the learned state representations. An analysis or visualization of what the stack LSTMs capture would significantly strengthen the work. For example, are there particular syntactic configurations that the model learns are predictive of semantic roles? A more detailed error analysis could also explain *why* the joint model overfits on out-of-domain data compared to the pipelined hybrid model, which would be a valuable finding. This would be a nice-to-have addition.
2.  **Incomplete Handling of Semantic Graph Structures:** The paper notes that the transition system, even with an M-SWAP action, cannot perfectly encode all gold-standard semantic graphs, affecting 1.5% of sentences. This seems to be an inherent limitation of mapping complex, non-projective graphs to a sequential, stack-based transition system. It would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion of this limitation. For instance, what types of graph structures are problematic, and what is the performance impact on those structures? Clarifying this would provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities and limitations. This revision seems essential for clarity.
3.  **Predicate Identification as a Separate Step:** For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is handled by a separate bidirectional LSTM classifier before parsing begins. While this is a reasonable design choice, it means this component is not integrated into the joint learning framework. This slightly weakens the "fully joint" aspect of the model for this specific task, as errors from the predicate identifier are passed to the parser. A brief justification for this design choice versus, for instance, incorporating predicate identification as another parser action, would be helpful. This is a minor point that could be addressed with a short clarification.

**Questions to Authors**

*   The hybrid model surpasses the joint model on the out-of-domain test set, which is attributed to the joint model overfitting. Could this indicate that the syntactic and semantic representations learned jointly are too tightly coupled to the training domain? It would be interesting to hear any thoughts on potential regularization strategies or architectural modifications that might enhance the joint model's robustness and generalization to new domains.
*   Regarding the imperfect oracle for semantic graphs, could the authors provide a brief characterization of the semantic structures that are not correctly encoded by the transition sequence? Understanding whether this limitation affects specific predicate types or argument structures would be valuable context for the results.
