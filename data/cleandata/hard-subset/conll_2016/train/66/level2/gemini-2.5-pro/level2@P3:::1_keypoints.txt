- Paper introduces a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent parser state, eliminating manual features and running in linear time.  
- Main contribution: extends stack LSTM framework to joint parsing with a four-stack architecture that learns a holistic parser-state representation.  
- Contribution: achieves state-of-the-art results among joint syntax–semantics models on CoNLL 2008/2009, supported by comprehensive experiments and ablations.  
- Contribution: presents an efficient, practical parser with greedy inference and faster parsing speed than many comparators.  
- Strength: model is elegant and novel, shifting from hand-engineered features to learned representation of complete parser state.  
- Strength: provides thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, baselines, and ablation studies.  
- Strength: paper is clear, reproducible, and promises open-source implementation.  
- Strength: paper honestly situates its results relative to pipelined and globally optimized systems, acknowledging orthogonal improvements.  
- Weakness: limited analysis/visualization of learned stack LSTM representations and lacking detailed error analysis, especially regarding out-of-domain overfitting.  
- Weakness: transition system cannot perfectly encode all gold semantic graphs (affecting ~1.5% of sentences); more discussion on problematic graph types and impact is needed.  
- Weakness: predicate identification is handled separately for CoNLL-2008, weakening the fully joint claim and passing errors from the predicate classifier to the parser.  
- Question: could joint syntactic and semantic coupling cause domain overfitting, and what regularization or architectural changes might improve out-of-domain robustness?  
- Question: can the authors characterize the semantic structures the imperfect oracle fails to encode (e.g., specific predicate types or argument structures) and its effect on performance?
