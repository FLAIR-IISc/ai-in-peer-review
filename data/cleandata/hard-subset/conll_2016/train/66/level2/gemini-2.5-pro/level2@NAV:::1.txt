**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based model for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The core idea is to use stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (stacks and buffer), which allows the model to make parsing decisions without relying on any hand-crafted features. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it demonstrates strong performance.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An effective application of stack LSTMs to the joint syntactic-semantic parsing task. This demonstrates that learned representations can successfully replace complex feature engineering in this challenging, structured prediction problem.
2.  State-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on standard English benchmarks. The comprehensive experiments, including ablation studies, convincingly validate the benefits of the joint, representation-learning approach over previous methods.
3.  A highly efficient, linear-time parser. The system's speed makes it a practical tool for downstream NLP applications, contrasting sharply with slower, more complex pipeline models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The paper establishes a new state-of-the-art for joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. The results are clearly presented and compared against a strong and relevant set of baselines. The inclusion of multilingual results further underscores the model's robustness and adaptability.
2.  **Elegant and Principled Model:** By replacing an extensive, manually engineered feature set with a learned representation of the parser's state, the model is simpler and more generalizable than previous work. This is a compelling demonstration of the power of representation learning for complex NLP tasks.
3.  **High Practicality and Impact:** The model's greedy nature results in linear-time complexity, making it significantly faster than many top-performing but computationally expensive pipeline systems. This efficiency, combined with the promise of an open-source implementation, gives the work high potential for practical impact and use by the community.
4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes valuable ablation experiments (e.g., syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that provide clear insights into the contributions of the joint learning framework. The authors are also commendably transparent about the model's limitations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance Gap with Pipelined Systems:** While the model is the best among joint systems, it still lags behind the top state-of-the-art pipeline models that leverage rich syntactic features and more complex decoding. This suggests that the learned representations, while powerful, do not yet fully capture all the necessary information that a complete syntactic parse provides for semantic role labeling.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The work effectively combines two existing lines of research: the transition system from Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM architecture from Dyer et al. (2015). While the application and engineering are excellent, the novelty in terms of fundamental model components is somewhat limited.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus, which you note is a sign of overfitting. Have you experimented with other regularization techniques besides dropout that might improve the Joint model's generalization?
2.  The M-SELF transition appears to be a novel addition to the transition system. Could you provide a brief analysis of its impact on performance, perhaps by ablating it?
