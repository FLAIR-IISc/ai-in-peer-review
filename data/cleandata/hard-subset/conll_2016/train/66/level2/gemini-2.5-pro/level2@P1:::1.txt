**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependency graphs. The model's primary novelty lies in its use of stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state, including the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. This learned representation allows the model to make greedy parsing decisions without relying on hand-crafted indicator features. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all previously published joint parsing systems and achieves performance comparable to many strong pipeline systems from that era, while being significantly faster.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the successful application and extension of the stack LSTM architecture to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. By learning a holistic representation of the algorithmic state, the work demonstrates that explicit, hand-engineered features (such as syntactic path features for SRL) can be effectively replaced by learned representations in a joint model.
2.  The paper presents a fast, linear-time joint parser that is highly effective. The strong empirical results, which set a new state of the art for joint models on these standard benchmarks, combined with its computational efficiency, make it a valuable contribution with practical implications.
3.  The work includes a well-designed set of experiments, including an ablation study that separates the model into syntax-only, semantics-only, and a pipelined "hybrid" variant. This analysis provides clear insights into the effects of joint learning and the contribution of syntactic information within this specific neural architecture.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Effective Model Architecture:** The core idea of using stack LSTMs to encode the entire state of a joint parser is a compelling and logical next step from prior work on syntax-only parsing. The model elegantly avoids the need for tedious feature engineering, which has historically been a major component of building high-performance parsers. The strong results, outperforming all previous joint models, clearly validate that this is a powerful and effective approach.
2.  **Thorough and Honest Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on standard, competitive benchmarks. They not only compare against relevant joint models but also contextualize their performance against stronger, feature-engineered pipeline systems. They are commendably transparent about the fact that their model does not achieve the absolute state of the art, and they clearly articulate the different design choices (e.g., greedy search, no expert features) that lead to this gap. This provides a balanced and credible picture of the work's contributions.
3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** A significant strength of this work is its efficiency. By using a greedy inference algorithm and avoiding expensive feature extraction, the parser is very fast, as demonstrated by the runtime comparison with another state-of-the-art system. This makes the model highly practical for downstream applications where both speed and quality are important.
4.  **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model is described clearly, and the motivation is well-argued. The ablation study comparing the joint, hybrid, and single-task models is particularly insightful, especially the discussion of the performance trade-offs between the joint and hybrid models on in-domain versus out-of-domain data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The contribution of this work lies almost entirely in the statistical model used for scoring actions. The underlying transition system itself is largely adopted from Henderson et al. (2013), with one minor addition (M-SELF) to handle a specific data artifact. While applying a new learning technique is a valid contribution, the paper would be stronger if it explored more novel aspects of the joint transition system itself.
2.  **Remaining Gap with Pipelined State-of-the-Art:** While the authors are transparent about this, the performance gap between this model and top-performing pipeline systems is notable. This suggests that the learned representations, while powerful, may not yet fully capture the kind of long-range structural information that hand-crafted features (e.g., syntactic paths between predicates and arguments) provide. A more detailed error analysis comparing this model's outputs to those of a feature-rich system could provide valuable insights into the current limitations of this representation learning approach.
3.  **Predicate Identification as a Pipelined Step:** For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is performed by a separate BiLSTM classifier before the main parser runs. This slightly undermines the "fully joint" narrative of the paper. While this is a common approach, it would be interesting to know if the authors considered integrating predicate identification as another action within the transition system, which would make the model more fully integrated.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The comparison between the `Joint` and `Hybrid` models is very interesting. You find that `Hybrid` performs better out-of-domain, which you attribute to overfitting in the `Joint` model. Could you elaborate on this? Does this point to a more general challenge for joint models, where they might learn spurious correlations specific to the training domain?
2.  You mention that the oracle transition sequence for the semantic graph is imperfect for 1.5% of sentences, even with the M-SWAP action. Could you characterize the graph structures in these sentences? Does this represent a hard ceiling for this class of transition-based models, and could you speculate on what kinds of new transitions might be needed to handle them?
3.  Could you provide a brief qualitative error analysis? For instance, does your model struggle with semantic dependencies that span long syntactic distances, where a feature-rich pipeline model might have an advantage due to access to the complete syntactic tree? Understanding the specific failure modes would be very helpful.
