## Review of "A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependencies"

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on hand-crafted features. The core contribution lies in demonstrating strong performance on CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks *without* the extensive feature engineering typically required for joint parsing, achieving competitive results with significantly faster runtime. A secondary contribution is the successful application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics, showing their effectiveness beyond purely syntactic parsing. Finally, the paper provides a clear and well-documented implementation of a fast, greedy joint parser.

The strengths of this submission are considerable. The experimental results are compelling, consistently outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable performance to pipeline systems on the CoNLL datasets. The linear runtime of the greedy inference algorithm is a significant practical advantage. The ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) provide valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and experimental setup. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as not matching the performance of systems with extensive feature engineering or utilizing optimal decoding.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the performance is strong, the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental. Stack LSTMs were already established in syntactic parsing, and applying them to joint parsing, while effective, doesn’t represent a radical departure. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of *why* this approach works so well – what specific aspects of the stack LSTM architecture are particularly beneficial for joint modeling? Additionally, the comparison to state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited; a more direct comparison to recent neural SRL models (beyond citing their performance) would be valuable. The reliance on pseudo-projectivization is mentioned but its impact on the results isn’t fully explored.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of using stack LSTMs for *joint* syntactic and semantic parsing compared to other recurrent neural network architectures?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the size of the LSTM hidden state dimension? Were other configurations explored?
3.  What is the impact of pseudo-projectivization on the reported results? Would the performance differ significantly on non-projective datasets?



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear presentation and strong experimental results. While the novelty is moderate, the practical benefits and insightful analysis make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance.
