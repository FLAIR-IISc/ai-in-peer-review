Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to encode the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) and performs greedy, linear-time inference. Evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009, it achieves the best reported results among joint models and is substantially faster than strong pipelines.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) Strong empirical results and speed for joint parsing: state-of-the-art among joint models on CoNLL 08/09 English, competitive multilingual results, with end-to-end runtime gains.
2) A practical modeling recipe for joint parsing without hand-crafted features: full-state stack-LSTM representation, interleaved transition system, and a new M-SELF transition for nominal self-arguments enabling greedy decoding.
3) Diagnostic variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) clarifying the value of joint learning vs pipelining.

Strengths
- Empirical support for claims: Clear, standard benchmarks (CoNLL 08/09), competitive English and multilingual results, and fair baselines (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.). The joint model outperforms prior joint systems; variants substantiate design choices (e.g., joint vs hybrid).
- Practicality and efficiency: Linear-time greedy inference with feature extraction integrated; reported single-core runtime (≈178s) is meaningfully faster than a strong pipeline under identical settings, making the approach attractive for deployment.
- Reduction of feature engineering: The model dispenses with expert-designed features and full syntactic trees, yet remains competitive; this has genuine engineering value, especially cross-lingually.
- Clear modeling innovations for joint parsing: Encoding the entire parser state, interleaving constraints, and M-SELF for NomBank self-dependencies are well-motivated and broadly applicable.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior stack-LSTM parsing: The extension from syntax-only to joint parsing is incremental; the main novelty is the transition design (interleaving and M-SELF) rather than fundamentally new learning machinery.
- Incomplete analysis/ablation: No ablations of key components (action history, composition functions, copying vs moving, effect of M-SELF) or beam vs greedy; limited error analysis (e.g., crossing arcs, OOD).
- Reproducibility and significance: Code is promised but not available at review time; no statistical significance tests; oracle conversion for semantics is imperfect without quantifying its impact.
- Generalization and fairness: OOD performance suggests overfitting (Hybrid > Joint on Brown); reliance on pretrained embeddings and lack of morphology/character features disadvantage some languages.

Questions to authors
- How often does the oracle fail to encode gold semantic graphs, and what is the measurable impact on training and test accuracy?
- Did you try small beams or dynamic oracle/DAgger to mitigate greedy errors? If so, what is the speed/accuracy trade-off?
- Can you provide ablations isolating the contribution of action history LSTM, composition functions, and the interleaving policy?
- Does the reported runtime include POS/lemma prediction and predicate identification (for 2008), and how does performance change without pretrained embeddings or with character/morphological features?
