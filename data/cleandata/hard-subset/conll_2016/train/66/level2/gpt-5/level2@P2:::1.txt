Summary and Contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, linear-time transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs. It encodes the entire parser state—syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history—using stack LSTMs, avoiding expert-crafted features.
- It introduces a joint transition system adapted from Henderson et al. with important modifications: interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions on a shared buffer, a new M-SELF transition to handle nominal self-dependencies (NomBank), and learned composition functions for both syntactic and semantic structures.
- Empirically, the model outperforms prior joint syntax–semantics parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive across multiple languages in CoNLL 2009; it is also notably fast.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results among joint models with clear baselines. The joint model surpasses Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and contemporaneous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and provides multilingual results on par with top shared-task systems (except the strongest pipelines). The controlled comparisons (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint) convincingly isolate the benefit of joint modeling and the use of learned state representations.
- Efficiency with linear-time inference and feature extraction. The design achieves end-to-end speed that is substantially faster than a competitive pipeline SRL system under similar settings, making it attractive for practical use without sacrificing much accuracy.
- Novel and clean modeling of joint state with stack LSTMs. Encoding the full algorithmic state (including action history) removes reliance on hand-crafted features while retaining the benefits of transition-based parsing. The architectural clarity (four stack LSTMs and recursive composition functions) makes the contribution easy to reproduce and extend.
- Sound extension of the transition system to SRL specifics. The M-SELF transition to handle NomBank-style self-arguments, and constraints that enable interleaved syntax/semantics while preserving linear complexity, show careful algorithmic design grounded in the properties of semantic graphs (crossing arcs, reentrancies).
- Sensible multilingual evaluation and practical considerations. Pretrained embeddings, small-memory CPU training, and explicit hyperparameters indicate awareness of replicability and portability.

Weaknesses
- Predicate identification in CoNLL 2008 is not integrated into the joint model. The use of an external biLSTM predicate identifier breaks the “joint” formulation in 2008 and may undercut the central thesis that learned state representations can obviate pipelines. Integrating predicate identification into the transition system (e.g., predicting M-PRED vs. none) would make the claim stronger and allow end-to-end learning.
- Limited analysis of the transition oracle and representational gaps. The paper notes that 1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the semantic transitions, but does not quantify the resulting oracle upper bound or training noise. Without reporting an oracle accuracy or the fraction of gold arcs lost due to conversion, it is hard to assess headroom and where errors originate (transition set vs. learning).
- Greedy decoding with static oracle training is under-justified. The model uses greedy inference and gold action supervision but lacks ablations with beam search or globally/dynamically normalized training (e.g., max-margin with dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling). The out-of-domain drop (Joint < Hybrid on Brown) suggests exposure bias; stronger training/decoding could mitigate this.
- Incomplete ablations on representation components. The gains are attributed to “learning the whole state,” but the paper does not isolate the contributions of: pretrained vs. learned embeddings, action-history LSTM, composition functions (gs, gm, gd), M-SELF/MSWAP, or interleaving constraints. Such ablations would bolster the methodological contribution and guide future adopters.
- Comparison fairness and significance. While the paper compares to strong pipelines, some systems exploit additional resources or features. Statistical significance of improvements over prior joint models and of Joint vs. Hybrid/Semantics-only is not reported. Speed is only reported for English; multilingual speed/accuracy trade-offs are not analyzed.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report the oracle upper bound (LAS, SRL F1, Macro F1) induced by your transition sets and constraints, and the proportion of gold arcs lost in CoNLL 2008/2009 due to non-encodable structures? How do these impact training/evaluation?
- How much does each component contribute? Please provide ablations for: removing pretrained embeddings; removing the action-history stack; replacing recursive composition with simple head-dependent concatenation; disabling M-SELF/MSWAP; using beam search.
- For CoNLL 2008, why not integrate predicate identification into the transition process (e.g., a “no-predicate” option vs. M-PRED)? Did you attempt joint training of the predicate identifier with the parser, and if so, what was the effect?
- Are improvements over Henderson et al. (2008/2013) statistically significant? Likewise, are differences among your four variants (Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint) significant on WSJ and Brown?
- What are the main error categories? For SRL: long-distance arguments, adjuncts vs. core roles, crossing arcs, nominal predicates. For syntax: non-projectivity after deprojectivization. Any evidence that M-SWAP/M-SELF specifically reduces certain errors?

Additional Comments
- Please fix minor typos (e.g., “dismabiguated”) and clarify notation (e.g., consistent use of copy vs. move for S-RIGHT/S-SHIFT in Table 1 and text).
- Report the number of epochs or update steps (not just wall time), random seed control, and variance over multiple runs.
- Consider adding character-level or morphological features (especially for Czech/Japanese) and discuss their effect; prior work suggests they help in low-resource/morphologically rich settings.
- Given the out-of-domain drop for Joint, consider globally normalized training or dynamic oracles to mitigate exposure bias; even a small beam or imitation learning could help without large runtime costs.
- Providing multilingual runtime and memory usage, and per-sentence complexity vs. sentence length, would strengthen the practicality claim.
