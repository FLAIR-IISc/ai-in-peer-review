Summary and claimed contributions
- A fast, greedy, transition-based joint parser for syntactic and semantic dependencies that learns dense representations of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, eliminating hand-engineered features. The model interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions and introduces a new M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies.
- Empirical demonstration that the approach achieves the best reported accuracy among joint syntax–semantics models on CoNLL 2008–2009 English, with competitive multilingual performance and strong runtime efficiency (linear-time decoding and feature extraction).
- An analysis via controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) showing how joint modeling compares to pipelining and to semantics without syntax.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful methodological advance for joint parsing: Representing the full algorithmic state with stack LSTMs removes reliance on expert-crafted features and expensive syntactic path features while retaining accuracy. This is an elegant unification of transition systems and representation learning, addressing a long-standing barrier for joint models.
- Strong, well-scoped empirical results on standard benchmarks: The joint model outperforms prior joint systems (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008/2013; Titov et al., 2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and is competitive across languages. The controlled comparisons (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) convincingly support the claim that joint modeling helps in-domain and that syntax benefits SRL.
- Practicality and efficiency: Greedy inference with linear-time complexity and entirely on-the-fly feature computation yields an end-to-end runtime of ~178s on CoNLL 2009 English test (single core), substantially faster than a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015). This speed/accuracy balance is valuable for real-world deployment.
- Thoughtful transition system engineering: The interleaving scheme over a shared buffer is simple and effective; the inclusion of M-SWAP for crossing arcs and the novel M-SELF transition for NomBank-style self-dependencies improves coverage of semantic phenomena within the same framework.
- Solid multilingual generalization: With minimal language-specific engineering and only pretrained embeddings, the model performs on par with top systems for most CoNLL 2009 languages, indicating robustness beyond English.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond prior stack LSTM parsing work: The core technical idea—using stack LSTMs to summarize parser state—is adapted from Dyer et al. (2015). The main new elements are the extension to semantic parsing and joint interleaving plus a few additional transitions (notably M-SELF). While valuable, the methodological novelty is incremental relative to the prior stack-LSTM parser.
- Greedy decoding without exploration or dynamic oracle training: The model uses static oracles and greedy inference, which are known to suffer from error propagation in transition systems. There is no comparison to beam search, dynamic oracle training, or learning-to-search alternatives; this could further improve accuracy with small runtime costs, and would help position the speed/accuracy trade-off more fully.
- Incomplete ablations: The paper shows component variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) but lacks finer-grained ablations. For example, the effects of pretrained embeddings, action-history stack A, composition functions, interleaving constraints, or M-SELF are not disentangled. This makes it hard to identify which design choices contribute most to gains.
- Oracle limitations and upper bounds not quantified: The authors note that semantic oracle conversion is imperfect (e.g., 1.5% of English sentences in CoNLL 2009 cannot be encoded correctly). The impact on training signal and evaluation ceilings is not analyzed. A measurement of oracle F1/LAS upper bounds for the transition system would clarify the headroom and potential bias.
- Limited error analysis and robustness study: There is little qualitative analysis (e.g., predicate types, long-distance arguments, crossing arcs, nominal vs verbal predicates) or robustness to OOVs and low-resource languages (beyond a brief note on Japanese). The observation that joint overfits to WSJ (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) is important, but the paper stops short of diagnosing causes or proposing remedies (e.g., regularization, domain-adaptive embeddings, multi-task objectives).

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is the joint model to the interleaving protocol (switching from syntax to semantics after S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT and back after M-SHIFT)? Have you tried alternative schedules (e.g., semantic transitions first, or alternating fixed ratios) and what is the performance/latency impact?
- What is the effect of the M-SELF transition on CoNLL 2009 English SRL F1? An ablation removing M-SELF would help quantify its contribution.
- Can you report oracle upper bounds (LAS, SRL F1) induced by your transition system on CoNLL 2008/2009, and how often oracle paths are infeasible due to crossing arcs or other constraints?
- Did you experiment with dynamic oracle training, scheduled sampling, or small beams? If so, what was the trade-off between accuracy and runtime?
- For multilingual experiments, were pretrained embeddings trained on comparable corpora across languages? How sensitive are results to embedding quality, and do character-level or morphological features yield gains in morphologically rich languages?

Additional comments
- Consider including statistical significance testing for key comparisons on test sets.
- A small error analysis by dependency length, argument type, and predicate POS would strengthen insights and guide future improvements.
- The runtime comparison is appreciated; adding accuracy-vs-latency curves (e.g., greedy vs small beam) would help practitioners choose settings.
- Releasing code, pretrained models, and scripts for data preprocessing (e.g., pseudo-projectivization) will greatly enhance reproducibility.
