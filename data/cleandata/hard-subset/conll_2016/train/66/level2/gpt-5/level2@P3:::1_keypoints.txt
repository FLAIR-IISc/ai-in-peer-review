- Joint transition-based parser using stack LSTMs to jointly predict syntactic dependency trees and SRL graphs with greedy linear-time inference; strong English and competitive multilingual results.

- Contribution: learned full algorithmic state via stack LSTMs, removing hand-crafted features and achieving a strong accuracy–speed trade-off.

- Contribution: engineering extensions—M-SELF for NomBank self-dependencies and a copy modification to arc-eager to support interleaving syntax and semantics.

- Contribution: systematic empirical comparison of Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint variants, showing joint training benefits and efficiency of greedy parsing.

- Strength: principled integration of representation learning (stack LSTMs) that improves over prior joint systems using the same transition schema.

- Strength: strong efficiency—greedy, linear-time parsing yields ~2.5× speedup over a competitive pipeline under identical hardware.

- Strength: careful experimental design with informative system variants that isolate syntax/semantics contributions.

- Strength: broad applicability and transparent reporting of limitations (dependence on pretrained embeddings for Chinese, small-data issues in Japanese, oracle imperfections).

- Strength: well-specified transition system, composition functions, and training details that support reproducibility.

- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—combines existing ingredients (stack LSTMs, Henderson-style transitions) with a modest engineering addition (M-SELF).

- Weakness: oracle and training limitations (~1.5% of English sentences not perfectly encoded) are noted but not fully analyzed or mitigated.

- Weakness: incomplete ablations (e.g., action-history LSTM, composition functions, pretraining) and no statistical significance testing reported.

- Weakness: fairness of comparisons unclear—use of pretrained embeddings and separate predicate-identification model may confound results versus prior systems.

- Weakness: limited error-profile and generalization analysis (e.g., crossing arcs, long-distance roles, domain shift), and evidence of in-domain overfitting.

- Question: How much of the gain is due to pretrained embeddings vs. stack-LSTM state summarization—please provide ablations.

- Question: What is the empirical impact of the imperfect semantic oracle on training loss and test performance; results on oracle-perfect sentences?

- Question: Are the improvements over prior joint models statistically significant on CoNLL 2008/2009 (WSJ and Brown)?

- Question: For Chinese, how large is the gap without pretrained embeddings—please report numbers without pretraining.

- Question: How essential is the arc-eager “copy” modification—provide ablation reverting to move semantics.

- Question: What is the failure rate and typical error mode for M-SELF arcs; do they introduce spurious self-arguments?

- Suggestion (essential): add significance testing and key ablations (pretraining effect; action-history LSTM; shallow vs. compositional encoders) and include a brief error analysis on crossings, long-distance roles, and domain shift.

- Suggestion (desirable): quantify oracle imperfection’s impact or report controlled results on oracle-perfect subsets; consider dynamic-oracle training if feasible.

- Suggestion (desirable): provide clearer apples-to-apples comparisons (e.g., prior joint systems augmented with embeddings) and discuss resource parity.

- Suggestion (desirable): include concrete Chinese numbers without pretraining and analyze predicate-identification error propagation for CoNLL 2008.

- Overall: solid, well-executed application with competitive accuracy and efficiency; novelty is incremental but empirical and engineering contributions are convincing pending added ablations, significance tests, and targeted analysis.
