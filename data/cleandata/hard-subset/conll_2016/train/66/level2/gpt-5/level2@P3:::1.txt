Summary and contributions
The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs using stack LSTMs to learn a continuous representation of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history). Greedy inference runs in linear time including feature extraction. On CoNLL 2008–2009 English, the work reports the best performance among joint systems, with multilingual results comparable to top systems from CoNLL 2009. Key contributions as assessed here:
1) A joint transition system with learned representations of the full algorithmic state via stack LSTMs, eliminating hand-crafted features and achieving strong accuracy–speed trade-offs (Sections 2–3, 5).
2) An engineering extension to the joint transition set (M-SELF) to handle NomBank-style self-dependencies and a modification of arc-eager to copy rather than move buffer items to accommodate interleaving of syntax and semantics (Section 2.2).
3) A systematic empirical comparison across joint, hybrid, and single-task variants, showing the impact of joint training and the efficiency advantages of greedy, end-to-end parsing (Section 4.2–5).

Strengths
1) Clear, effective integration of representation learning into joint parsing. The use of stack LSTMs to summarize S/M/B/A provides a principled way to replace large, expensive feature sets with learned state representations. Evidence: consistent improvements over prior joint systems using the same transition schema (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; Henderson et al., 2013; Gesmundo et al., 2009) on CoNLL 2008–2009 (Section 5).
2) Strong efficiency with competitive accuracy. The greedy, linear-time procedure yields an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 s on CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, ~2.5× faster than a competitive pipeline SRL system (Lei et al., 2015) under identical hardware (Section 5). This is a practical contribution for real-world use.
3) Careful experimental design with informative variants. The comparison among Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint variants isolates the contribution of syntax to SRL and vice versa, demonstrating that joint training benefits in-domain performance without degrading syntax (Section 4.2–5).
4) Broad applicability and transparency about limitations. Multilingual results are competitive in most languages; the paper openly notes reliance on pretrained embeddings for Chinese and the small-data limitation in Japanese (Section 5). The oracle imperfection for semantic transitions is also acknowledged (Sections 4, 5), showing awareness of modeling constraints.
5) Well-specified transition system and training details. The interleaving constraints, stack operations, composition functions (gs, gm, gd), and optimization settings are described concretely (Sections 2–4), supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty relative to prior work. The central technical ingredients—stack LSTMs for parser state (Dyer et al., 2015) and a Henderson-style synchronized joint transition system (Henderson et al., 2013)—are combined rather than fundamentally extended. The new M-SELF action is a necessary engineering addition for NomBank self-arguments but modest in scope (Section 2.2). This does not diminish utility, but it tempers novelty.
2) Oracle and training limitations not fully explored. The paper notes that ~1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the semantic transition sequence due to multiple crossing arcs (Section 4). The impact of this imperfection on learning and evaluation is not quantified, and no dynamic-oracle training or alternative transition design is explored to mitigate it.
3) Incomplete ablations and significance testing. While system variants are presented, there is no ablation of key architectural components (e.g., removal of action-history LSTM, effect of composition functions vs. shallow representations, contribution of pretrained embeddings vs. learned-only). Statistical significance of reported gains over prior joint systems is not reported, making it harder to assess robustness.
4) Fairness and clarity of comparisons. The joint system uses pretrained “structured skip-gram” embeddings (Section 3.5), while prior joint baselines may not, potentially conflating architectural and data advantages. For CoNLL 2008, predicate identification is handled by a separate biLSTM (91.43% F1; Section 4.1), but the propagation of its errors into joint metrics is not analyzed. For CoNLL 2009, comparisons against state-of-the-art pipelines include systems with richer resources; the paper notes orthogonality, but a clearer apples-to-apples comparison set would strengthen claims.
5) Limited analysis of error profiles and generalization. The joint model overfits in-domain relative to Hybrid on Brown (Section 5), yet there is minimal qualitative or quantitative error analysis (e.g., long-distance SRL arcs, crossing structures handled via M-SWAP, predicate sense errors, domain shift). This limits actionable insights for future improvement.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How much of the gain over Henderson et al. (2013) is attributable to pretrained embeddings vs. the stack-LSTM state summarization? Please provide ablations removing pretraining and/or replacing stack LSTMs with local features.
- What is the empirical impact of the imperfect semantic oracle (Section 4) on training loss and test performance? Can results be reported on the subset of sentences with perfect oracle conversion to bound the effect?
- Are the improvements over prior joint models statistically significant on CoNLL 2008/2009 (WSJ and Brown)? Please provide significance tests.
- For Chinese, the text notes strong dependence on pretrained embeddings. Could the numbers without pretraining be included to quantify this gap?
- How essential is the “copy” modification to arc-eager (Section 2.2)? An ablation reverting to move semantics for S-RIGHT/S-SHIFT, coupled with alternative interleaving, would clarify the design choice.
- What is the failure rate and typical error mode for M-SELF arcs? Do they introduce spurious self-arguments in practice?

Suggestions for improvement (feasible within revision time)
- Essential: Add significance testing and key ablations (effect of pretraining; ablation of action-history LSTM; shallow vs. compositional structure encoders). Include a brief error analysis focusing on crossing arcs, long-distance roles, and domain shift.
- Desirable: Quantify the oracle imperfection’s impact; if dynamic-oracle training is impractical, at least report controlled results on oracle-perfect subsets.
- Desirable: Provide clearer apples-to-apples comparisons (e.g., prior joint systems augmented with embeddings if available) and discuss resource parity.
- Desirable: Include concrete numbers for Chinese without pretraining and for predicate-identification error propagation on CoNLL 2008.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed application of stack-LSTM state representations to joint dependency parsing and SRL, yielding competitive accuracy with strong efficiency and clean engineering. Novelty is incremental but the empirical contribution and practical value are convincing. With added ablations, significance testing, and targeted analysis, the paper would meet a high bar for acceptance.
