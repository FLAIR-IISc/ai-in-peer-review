- Summary: Proposes a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs encoding full parser state, enabling greedy linear-time inference with strong CoNLL 2008/2009 results and faster runtime than pipelines.

Contributions:
- Strong empirical joint parsing performance and runtime gains: state-of-the-art among joint models on CoNLL 08/09 English and competitive multilingual results with end-to-end speed improvements.
- Practical, feature-free modeling recipe: full-state stack-LSTM representation, interleaved transition system, and a new M-SELF transition for nominal self-arguments enabling greedy decoding.
- Diagnostic variants: syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid models that clarify joint learning benefits versus pipelining.

Strengths:
- Empirical validation: Clear benchmarks and fair baselines show the joint model outperforms prior joint systems and design choices are substantiated by variants.
- Practicality and efficiency: Linear-time greedy inference with integrated feature extraction yields meaningful single-core runtime improvements, attractive for deployment.
- Reduced feature engineering: Eliminates expert-designed features and full trees while remaining competitive, offering engineering value across languages.
- Clear modeling innovations: Encoding full parser state, interleaving constraints, and M-SELF are well-motivated and broadly applicable.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty over prior stack-LSTM parsing: Extension to joint parsing is incremental, with novelty mainly in transition design rather than new learning methods.
- Incomplete analysis and ablations: Missing ablations for action history, composition functions, copying vs moving, M-SELF, and beam vs greedy comparisons; limited error analysis.
- Reproducibility and statistical significance concerns: Code not available at review time, no significance testing, and oracle semantic conversion issues not quantified.
- Generalization and fairness issues: OOD performance suggests overfitting (Hybrid > Joint on Brown); reliance on pretrained embeddings and lack of character/morphological features may hurt some languages.

Questions to authors:
- How often does the oracle fail to encode gold semantic graphs, and what is its measurable impact on training/test accuracy?
- Did you try small beams or dynamic oracle/DAgger to reduce greedy errors, and what speed/accuracy trade-offs were observed?
- Can you provide ablations isolating contributions of the action-history LSTM, composition functions, and interleaving policy?
- Does reported runtime include POS/lemma prediction and predicate identification, and how does performance change without pretrained embeddings or with character/morphological features?
