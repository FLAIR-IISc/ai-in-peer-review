**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies. The parser uses a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The greedy inference algorithm has linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The parser is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008-2009 English shared tasks and achieves the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based parser.
2. **Stack LSTM for Joint Parsing**: The paper introduces a stack LSTM architecture that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state, enabling the parser to make informed decisions about transitions.
3. **Linear Time Complexity**: The paper achieves linear time complexity for the greedy inference algorithm, including feature extraction, making it efficient for practical applications.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The paper reports improved performance on the CoNLL 2008-2009 English shared tasks, surpassing previous joint models.
2. **Efficient Inference**: The paper achieves linear time complexity for the greedy inference algorithm, making it efficient for practical applications.
3. **No Expert-Crafted Features**: The paper does not rely on expert-crafted features of the full syntactic parse, making it a more flexible and generalizable approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison**: The paper does not compare its approach to other state-of-the-art models that use expert-crafted features or more complex architectures.
2. **Overfitting**: The paper notes that the joint model overfits to the WSJ training data, which may limit its generalizability to other domains.
3. **Limited Multilingual Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the joint model on a limited set of non-English languages, which may not be representative of its performance on other languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the stack LSTM architecture handle cases where the syntactic and semantic dependencies are not synchronized, such as in cases of semantic self-dependencies?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored and the criteria used for selection?
3. How does the joint model perform on other languages with different morphological and syntactic properties, such as Arabic or Russian?
