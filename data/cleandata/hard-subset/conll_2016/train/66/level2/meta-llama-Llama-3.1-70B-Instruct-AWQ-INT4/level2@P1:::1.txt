**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and report the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to joint parsing that uses a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state, allowing for the simultaneous prediction of syntactic and semantic dependencies.
2. **Greedy Inference Algorithm**: The authors develop a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction, making their model efficient and scalable.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors report state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach to Joint Parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to joint parsing that uses a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state, allowing for the simultaneous prediction of syntactic and semantic dependencies.
2. **Efficient Inference Algorithm**: The authors develop a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction, making their model efficient and scalable.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors report state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, which may not be representative of other languages or tasks.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Other Joint Models**: The authors do not compare their model to other joint models that use different approaches, such as graph-based or pipeline-based models.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Word Embeddings**: The authors rely on pretrained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to incorporate morphological features, which are important for highly inflective languages?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the stack LSTM and the composition functions used in the model?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate their model on other languages or tasks, such as the CoNLL 2009 multilingual shared task?
