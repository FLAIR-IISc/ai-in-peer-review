- Paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs and greedy incremental decoding, removing hand-crafted features.
- Achieves competitive results on CoNLL 2008/2009 and offers a fast runtime compared to pipeline and complex-decoding systems.
- Demonstrates that learned representations can replace extensive feature engineering for joint parsing.
- Provides a strong empirical baseline on standard benchmarks for future work.
- Reports significantly faster runtime (e.g., 177.6s on CoNLL 2009), highlighting practical applicability.
- Includes ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) that clarify component contributions.
- Commits to open-sourcing the code to aid reproducibility and follow-up research.
- Core technique (Stack LSTMs) is not novel; main contribution is application rather than new algorithmic development.
- Heavy reliance on pretrained word embeddings, with multilingual performance tied to embedding quality.
- Lacks detailed error analysis to pinpoint specific failure modes (semantic relation types, long-distance dependencies).
- Does not fully quantify the impact of non-projectivity conversion and oracle errors; use of pseudo-projectivization is unclear.
- Needs clearer articulation of what combination/configuration of techniques is innovative.
- Requests more analysis on sensitivity to embedding source and low-resource language performance.
- Asks for breakdown of differences versus top pipeline systems on specific error types.
- Questions rationale and performance impact of pseudo-projectivization given arc-eager’s capabilities.
- Seeks elaboration on hyperparameter choices and whether systematic optimization was performed.
- Suggests expanding related work to include recent neural SRL approaches (transformers, GNNs).
- Recommends clearer visual representation and detailed example of the joint parsing transition sequence.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-written paper with valuable contributions and fast, competitive results; recommends acceptance with minor revisions.
