## Review

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state, eschewing traditional expert-crafted features. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that effective joint parsing *can* be achieved without relying on the expensive, full syntactic parse information typically used in pipelined approaches. The authors achieve competitive results on CoNLL 2008 & 2009, and demonstrate reasonable multilingual performance. While not SOTA, the speed and simplicity of the approach are noteworthy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Feature-Free Joint Parsing:** The paper convincingly shows that strong joint parsing performance is achievable without hand-engineered features derived from complete parse trees, relying instead on learned representations from Stack LSTMs. This is a significant step towards more streamlined and potentially more adaptable joint models.
2. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs:** The successful adaptation of Stack LSTMs to a joint parsing task expands the applicability of this architecture beyond purely syntactic dependency parsing.
3. **Competitive Performance with a Simple, Greedy Algorithm:** Achieving performance comparable to more complex, pipeline-based systems with a fast, greedy algorithm is a valuable result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The most significant strength is the departure from feature engineering. The paper’s central idea – learning a state representation – is a bold one, and directly addresses a bottleneck in traditional joint parsing. While Stack LSTMs aren’t new, *applying them to learn the entire parser state* without explicit features is a noteworthy contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model outperforms prior joint parsing models (Henderson et al.) and achieves competitive, though not state-of-the-art, performance on established benchmarks (CoNLL 2008 & 2009). The results clearly support the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Speed and Efficiency:** The reported runtime (177.6 seconds) is a clear advantage, particularly compared to systems relying on complex feature sets and/or optimal decoding. This makes the model potentially more practical for real-world applications.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the technical details in a reasonably accessible manner. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the experimental setup is adequately described.
5. **Multilingual Results:** While not a primary focus, the reported multilingual results demonstrate some degree of generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The paper largely focuses on comparing against *previous joint models*. The comparison to strong, pipeline-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009) acknowledges a performance gap but doesn’t deeply analyze *why* the proposed model lags behind. A more detailed error analysis, contrasting the types of errors made by the proposed system and the pipeline baselines, would be valuable.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** While the authors experiment with semantics-only and syntax-only models, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of different LSTM configurations, embedding sizes, or composition functions would strengthen the analysis and provide deeper insights into the model’s behavior.
3. **Simplified Predicate Sense Disambiguation:** The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to sense "01" feels like a rather simplistic solution. While acknowledged, this could introduce bias and potentially limit performance on datasets with different predicate distributions.
4. **Greedy Decoding Limitations:** The use of a greedy decoding algorithm is explicitly stated as a trade-off for speed. However, the paper doesn’t fully explore the potential performance gains from exploring more sophisticated decoding strategies, even if it means sacrificing some speed.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Propagation:** Joint models are often proposed to avoid error propagation. The paper doesn’t explicitly analyze whether this is actually happening in practice – is the joint model more robust to syntactic errors than a pipeline?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, comparing the types of errors made by your model and the strong pipeline baselines (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009)?
2. What is the impact of the "01" sense defaulting strategy on the overall performance, particularly on datasets with different predicate distributions?
3. Have you explored any strategies to mitigate the limitations of the greedy decoding algorithm, such as limited-horizon beam search or re-ranking?
4. How does the performance of your model change when using larger or smaller LSTM hidden state dimensions?
5. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings?




**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to "structured skipgram" embeddings could benefit from a brief explanation of what these are.
* The discussion of related work feels a bit cursory. A more comprehensive review of recent advances in neural SRL would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong empirical results and clear presentation, makes it a worthwhile submission. I lean towards **Accept**. The potential of the approach outweighs the current limitations, and I believe the authors' response to the questions raised will further strengthen the paper. The willingness to eschew complex feature engineering in favor of learning representations is a refreshing and promising direction.
