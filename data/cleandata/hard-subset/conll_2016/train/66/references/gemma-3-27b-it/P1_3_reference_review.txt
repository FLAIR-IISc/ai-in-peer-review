## Peer Review for CoNLL Paper: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to represent the parser's state. The core idea is to move away from hand-crafted features and complex decoding schemes (like beam search) prevalent in prior joint parsing models, opting instead for a greedy, representation-learning approach. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable results to pipeline systems, while maintaining a significantly faster runtime.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Greedy Joint Parsing:** The paper convincingly shows that a purely greedy, representation-learning approach can achieve competitive results in joint syntactic-semantic parsing. This is a valuable contribution, challenging the assumption that optimal decoding and extensive feature engineering are essential for strong performance.
2. **Successful Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The paper effectively extends the successful application of Stack LSTMs from purely syntactic parsing to the more complex task of joint syntactic-semantic parsing. This showcases the power of these representations for capturing relevant information about parser state.
3. **Strong Empirical Results and Baseline:** The results on standard CoNLL datasets provide a strong empirical baseline for future work in joint parsing, particularly for models focused on efficiency and reduced feature engineering.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanations of the transition-based algorithm and the Stack LSTM architecture are clear and sufficient for understanding the core ideas.
2. **Significant Performance Improvement:** The reported performance gains over previous joint models are substantial, particularly considering the simplicity of the proposed approach. Achieving comparable results to complex pipeline systems with a purely greedy approach is a notable achievement.
3. **Computational Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set is impressive and highlights the practical value of this approach.  Faster parsing is crucial for real-world applications.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, including ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that effectively demonstrate the benefits of the joint approach. The multilingual results also strengthen the generalizability of the method.
5. **Open-Source Commitment:** The authors’ promise to release an open-source implementation is a valuable contribution to the research community, facilitating further research and development.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** The core transition system is based on Henderson et al. (2013). While the application of Stack LSTMs is significant, the algorithmic innovation is relatively limited.  The primary advance lies in the representation learning, not the parsing algorithm itself.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art is Uneven:** The comparison to state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge the superiority of feature-rich pipeline systems, the direct comparison is to systems that use different strategies (e.g., global reranking, external datasets). A more direct comparison, perhaps incorporating some of those advancements into a baseline, would strengthen the claims. The justification for not including these features (computational cost) is valid, but should be explicitly acknowledged as a trade-off.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the model makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Where does the greedy approach specifically falter? What kinds of dependencies are most often mispredicted?
4. **Dependency on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings. The extent to which the model could perform with randomly initialized embeddings or embeddings trained from scratch isn't fully explored. This raises questions about the true extent of the representation learning capability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Algorithmic Innovation:** Given that the core transition system is derived from previous work, could you elaborate on any considerations given to exploring alternative transition systems alongside the one presented?  What were the reasons for choosing this specific system?
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples of typical errors made by the model, and discuss potential strategies for addressing them?
3. **Embedding Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the pretrained embeddings? Could you provide results with randomly initialized embeddings or embeddings trained specifically on the target dataset?
4. **Greedy Decoding Limitations:** The paper highlights the benefits of greedy decoding, but also implicitly acknowledges its limitations. Can you discuss specific scenarios where the greedy approach struggles and potential future directions for incorporating more sophisticated decoding strategies (while maintaining efficiency)?
5. **Multilingual Analysis:** The multilingual results show varying levels of performance. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance differences across languages, and identify any language-specific challenges?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Table 1 is a bit dense and could benefit from further clarification.
*   The reference to Lei et al. (2015) runtime should be verified. The stated runtime seems low.
*   The discussion of morphological features and character-based embeddings is a good point for future work.




Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a compelling and efficient approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
