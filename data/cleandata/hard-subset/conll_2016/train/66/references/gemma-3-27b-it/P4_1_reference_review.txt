This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, synchronized algorithm and leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state. The key idea is to avoid hand-crafted features and instead learn representations directly from the parser’s state, utilizing a greedy inference approach.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating that strong performance can be achieved in joint syntactic-semantic parsing *without* relying on expert-crafted features, which are often expensive to extract. The use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, rather than just the top of the stack, is a significant factor in achieving this. A secondary contribution is the implementation and evaluation of a fast, greedy joint parser that achieves competitive results on the CoNLL shared tasks, offering a practical alternative to more complex and slower systems. Finally, the detailed analysis comparing the model to existing approaches and variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models and achieving comparable results to pipeline systems. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach and its potential for practical applications. The multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 are also encouraging.
2. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Features:** The successful elimination of expert-crafted features is a significant advancement. It simplifies the model development process and reduces the reliance on linguistic expertise, making the system more adaptable to different languages and domains. This also addresses a key bottleneck in traditional joint parsing approaches.
3. **Efficient Inference:** The greedy inference approach results in a fast parser (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English), making it suitable for real-time applications where speed is critical. This is a notable advantage over more accurate but slower models that rely on optimal decoding or reranking.
4. **Thorough Ablation Study:** The comparison of different variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) provides a clear understanding of the benefits of joint parsing and the interactions between syntactic and semantic information. This analysis strengthens the claim that the proposed model effectively leverages both syntactic and semantic cues.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** The core algorithmic framework builds heavily on Henderson et al. (2013) and Nivre (2008). While the application of stack LSTMs is a valuable addition, the fundamental parsing algorithm itself is not novel. The paper acknowledges this, but it’s a key consideration when evaluating the contribution.
2. **Performance Gap Compared to State-of-the-Art:** While competitive, the model’s performance still lags behind state-of-the-art systems that leverage expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, and additional datasets. The paper acknowledges this and correctly identifies areas for future improvement, but the gap is substantial. The presented results are quite old, and there hasn't been much progress since.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The model relies on pretrained word embeddings, and the performance on the Chinese dataset is particularly sensitive to their availability. This suggests a potential limitation in the model’s ability to generalize to languages with limited resources or different embedding spaces.
4. **Simplified Predicate Sense Disambiguation:** The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to sense "01" is a pragmatic but potentially suboptimal solution. While acknowledged, this approach may limit the model's accuracy when encountering novel predicates.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the specific choices made regarding the architecture of the stack LSTMs (e.g., number of layers, hidden state size)? What impact did different configurations have on performance?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for incorporating morphological features or character-based word embeddings. Have any preliminary experiments been conducted to explore these avenues?
3.  The authors note a performance dip in the joint model for out-of-domain (Brown corpus) data. Could they provide more analysis on the types of errors that occur in this scenario and discuss potential strategies to improve generalization?
4.  Given the substantial gap between the presented model and current state-of-the-art systems, what specific advancements (beyond those mentioned in the paper) do the authors believe would be most impactful in bridging this gap?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work in neural network-based dependency parsing, particularly recent advancements in graph neural networks and transformers.
*   The supplementary material should include a more comprehensive example run of the algorithm, illustrating the transitions and stack LSTM states at each step.
*   The description of the experimental setup could be more precise, including details on the specific hyperparameter tuning process and the hardware used for training and evaluation.
