This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser built on a transition-based framework, leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate strong performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that effective joint syntactic-semantic parsing *can* be achieved without extensive feature engineering, relying instead on learning representations of the parsing state via Stack LSTMs and a greedy decoding approach. While transition-based parsing and joint models are not new, the complete elimination of hand-crafted features and the successful application of Stack LSTMs to this task represent a significant shift. A secondary contribution is the efficient implementation – the runtime is notably fast. Finally, the multilingual results, while not a central focus, demonstrate reasonable generalization capability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is its rejection of the conventional wisdom regarding feature engineering in joint parsing. The authors boldly claim, and demonstrate to a significant degree, that learned representations alone can achieve competitive performance. This is a potentially paradigm-shifting result.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser consistently outperforms previous joint models on standard benchmarks. The fact that it approaches the performance of more complex, feature-rich pipeline systems is particularly impressive, given the simplicity of the approach.
3. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime is significantly faster than many competing systems, making this a practical solution for real-world applications. This is a valuable characteristic, especially considering the computational demands of many modern NLP models.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and the experimental setup. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm (referenced in the text) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the model struggles. Understanding the types of errors the model makes would provide valuable insights for future improvement and would help contextualize the performance gains. It’s stated performance doesn’t match the best systems, but *why* isn't explored.
2. **Greedy Decoding Caveats:** The reliance on greedy decoding is a potential limitation. While it contributes to the efficiency of the model, it may sacrifice accuracy compared to beam search or other more sophisticated decoding algorithms. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation of the trade-off between speed and accuracy would be beneficial.
3. **Over-reliance on CoNLL Datasets:** The evaluation is almost entirely focused on the CoNLL datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, they may not fully represent the diversity of real-world text. Testing the model on additional datasets or in different languages would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Sense Disambiguation Handling:** The handling of out-of-vocabulary predicates with a default sense ("01") is a pragmatic workaround, but it introduces a potential bias and could limit performance on datasets with different sense distributions. A more robust sense disambiguation mechanism should be considered.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors the model makes most frequently (e.g., incorrect predicate identification, wrong sense disambiguation, incorrect argument labeling)?
2. Have you experimented with beam search or other more sophisticated decoding algorithms, and if so, what was the impact on performance and runtime?
3. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the size of the LSTM hidden states and the dimensionality of the embeddings?
4. What is the computational cost of training this model compared to other joint parsing models that rely on hand-crafted features?

**Additional Comments:**

The reference to "Fig. 5 in the supplementary material" is a bit awkward. Perhaps include a simplified illustration within the main paper. The claim about the asymptotic runtime complexity being linear needs more justification—Nivre (2009) should be cited directly, or the argument expanded upon.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing. While there are some weaknesses, the potential impact of this work is significant. I recommend **acceptance** despite the areas for improvement, as the novelty and strong empirical results outweigh the limitations. The paper pushes the field forward by demonstrating that high-performance joint parsing can be achieved without the heavy reliance on hand-engineered features that has historically characterized this area.
