## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser with Stack LSTMs"

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state, dispensing with expert-crafted features and employing greedy inference. The authors claim to outperform previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 & 2009 (English) and achieve comparable multilingual results on CoNLL 2009. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration that a surprisingly effective joint parser can be constructed *without* relying on the extensive feature engineering that has historically dominated the field. The secondary contribution is a reasonably thorough exploration of this approach, including ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid models) and analysis of performance across datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of Feature Engineering:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn representations obviating the need for manually designed features. This represents a shift towards more data-driven approaches in joint parsing, a domain traditionally reliant on linguistic expertise.  The reported performance, while not SOTA, is competitive considering this constraint.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The authors evaluate on established CoNLL datasets, employing standard evaluation metrics and comparing against relevant baselines, including previous joint models and strong pipeline systems. The inclusion of a multilingual evaluation further strengthens the assessment.
3. **Ablation Studies:** The systematic exploration of different model configurations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing and the benefit of joint modeling.  These studies provide evidence that the joint approach is, indeed, providing value beyond simply performing the tasks separately.
4. **Speed:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set is impressive, suggesting practical applicability. This speed advantage, while not the primary focus, is a positive attribute.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of Stack LSTMs and greedy joint parsing is presented as novel, both components are established techniques. Stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) were already demonstrated to be effective for syntactic parsing, and greedy parsing, while less common, is not a fundamentally new concept. The paper's innovation lies more in *applying* these techniques to the joint parsing problem than in developing fundamentally new algorithms.
2. **Performance Gap from SOTA:** Despite outperforming prior *joint* models, the parser's performance falls short of state-of-the-art systems that utilize expert-crafted features and optimal decoding strategies (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this, but the gap is significant enough to raise concerns about the practical impact of this approach *relative* to existing methods.  Simply stating that future work could bridge the gap isn’t sufficient; the scale of the gap needs consideration.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser struggles—specific linguistic phenomena, syntactic structures, or semantic relations—would be valuable for guiding future improvements. The authors claim overfitting in the multilingual setting, but do not delve into which languages or data characteristics contribute to this.
4. **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The choice of greedy decoding is motivated by efficiency, but it is a known limitation in parsing. While the authors acknowledge this, the potential impact on parsing accuracy is not sufficiently discussed. A comparison against a beam search approach (even with limited beam width) would be informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific linguistic phenomena or syntactic/semantic structures where your parser consistently underperforms?
2.  Have you experimented with beam search decoding? If so, what was the impact on performance and runtime? If not, why not?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for incorporating morphological features or character-based embeddings. Can you elaborate on how these would be integrated into your architecture and what potential benefits you anticipate?
4. While you show the speed improvement over Lei et al. (2015), can you provide a more comprehensive runtime comparison with a wider range of systems, including those that rely on more extensive feature engineering?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.
*   The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for readability.
*   The reference to "structured skipgram embeddings" lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the technique. Briefly explain what these embeddings represent and why they are beneficial.



Overall, this paper presents a competent and well-executed study. While the core contributions are incremental rather than groundbreaking, the demonstration of a competitive joint parser without relying on expert-crafted features is valuable. However, the performance gap from SOTA systems and the lack of detailed error analysis raise concerns that need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
