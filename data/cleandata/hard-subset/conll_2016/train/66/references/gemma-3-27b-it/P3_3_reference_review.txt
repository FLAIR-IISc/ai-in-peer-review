## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser built upon the transition-based framework of Henderson et al. (2008), but significantly updated with the use of Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008 & 2009 English shared tasks and competitive multilingual performance on CoNLL 2009, while maintaining a relatively fast parsing speed. 

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The core contribution is a successful demonstration of how Stack LSTMs can effectively learn state representations in a joint syntactic-semantic parsing framework, obviating the need for extensive feature engineering. This is particularly notable given the complexity of joint modeling.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on established benchmarks (CoNLL 2008/2009) provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Speed and Efficiency:** The reported runtime (177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English) is a valuable asset, positioning the model as a practical solution for real-world applications.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The elimination of hand-crafted features in a joint parsing model is a significant step forward.  Joint models often suffer from the feature engineering bottleneck, and this work demonstrates a compelling alternative leveraging representation learning.  The use of Stack LSTMs to capture the entire parser state, rather than just the top of the stack, is a clever design choice.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English and multilingual) and includes ablations (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) allowing for insightful analysis of the contributions of syntactic and semantic information. Comparing against a wide range of existing models (pipeline and joint) demonstrates a strong understanding of the state-of-the-art.
3. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm in the supplementary material is a helpful addition.  The detailed description of the transitions and their effects on the stacks and buffer provides sufficient clarity for replication.
4. **Practicality and Speed:**  The reported speed is a major advantage over many existing systems, making this a more viable option for deployment in real-time applications.  The promise of an open-source implementation further increases its potential impact.
5. **Addressing Limitations:** The authors are upfront about the limitations of their approach, acknowledging that it doesn't yet match the performance of the very best expert-crafted feature-based systems and outlining potential directions for future work.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not Revolutionary:** While the results are strong, the improvement over previous *joint* models is incremental, not dramatic. The authors rightly acknowledge that the best systems are still pipeline-based. The paper doesn’t fully address *why* the performance gap remains—what specific linguistic phenomena or features are still best captured by pipeline approaches?
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would be beneficial.  What types of errors does the model consistently make?  Are there specific semantic roles or syntactic structures that are particularly challenging? Understanding the error patterns could guide future improvements.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn't detail the process extensively. The sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters isn't discussed, and it’s unclear how robust the reported results are to different settings.  The choice of dropout rate and LSTM hidden size could heavily influence performance.
4. **Limited Discussion of the Greedy Inference:**  The choice of greedy inference is a key difference from earlier work (Henderson et al., 2008). However, the paper doesn’t delve into the potential drawbacks of this approach.  Could a beam search, despite its computational cost, yield significantly better results by mitigating the effects of early errors?  A brief discussion of the trade-offs would be valuable.
5. **Sense Disambiguation Handling:** The handling of unseen predicates (defaulting to "01" sense) is a pragmatic workaround but introduces a potential bias.  A more sophisticated approach to handling out-of-vocabulary predicates would be desirable.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the specific linguistic phenomena where your model still lags behind pipeline-based systems? Are there particular types of semantic arguments or syntactic structures where the model consistently struggles?
2.  What was the range of hyperparameters explored during tuning, and how sensitive were the results to changes in these parameters? Could you share some ablation studies evaluating the impact of different LSTM hidden dimensions or dropout rates?
3.  Have you experimented with beam search decoding, even on a smaller scale, to assess its potential impact on performance? If so, what were the results? If not, could you explain the practical considerations that prevented you from doing so?
4.  How does the model’s performance vary across different syntactic structures (e.g., projectivity, depth of the parse tree)?
5.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by defaulting to sense "01" for unseen predicates?  Have you considered alternative strategies for handling out-of-vocabulary predicates?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent in a few places.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the model's parameters (e.g., embedding dimensions, LSTM hidden sizes).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The use of Stack LSTMs to eliminate the need for hand-crafted features is a significant achievement, and the strong empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical, and I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions raised above.
