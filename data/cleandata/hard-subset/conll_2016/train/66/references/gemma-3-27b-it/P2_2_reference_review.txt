## Review for CoNLL Paper: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state during a greedy, incremental parsing process. The core idea is to eliminate the need for hand-crafted features traditionally used in joint parsing by learning representations from the entire parser state – stacks and buffer – using Stack LSTMs. The authors demonstrate improved performance over previous joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, and comparable performance to stronger pipeline-based systems, while achieving significantly faster runtimes.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The paper demonstrates that Stack LSTMs can effectively learn representations sufficient for high-performing joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing *without* relying on traditional, manually engineered features. This is a strong contribution, showing the power of representation learning in a complex NLP task.
2. **Competitive Performance with a Greedy Approach:** Achieving competitive results using a purely greedy inference approach is noteworthy.  Greedy methods are often simpler and faster, but typically sacrifice accuracy compared to more complex search algorithms. This work shows that with good representation learning, a greedy approach can be surprisingly effective.
3. **Strong Baseline & Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of experiments comparing different model configurations (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides a valuable ablation study, clearly demonstrating the benefits of joint parsing and the contribution of each component.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional pipeline approaches and motivates the use of representation learning to overcome those limitations. The connection to previous work (Henderson et al., 2008) is well-established, and the paper builds logically upon that foundation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results on the CoNLL datasets are compelling. The model consistently outperforms previous joint models, and achieves competitive performance against strong pipeline-based baselines, especially considering the speed advantage. The multilingual results also provide evidence of the model’s generalizability.
3. **Efficient Implementation:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 test set is a significant advantage, making the system potentially useful for real-world applications where speed is critical.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper details the experimental setup clearly, including hyperparameter settings and training procedures.  The inclusion of ablation studies and comparison to multiple baselines strengthens the validity of the findings.
5. **Open-Source Availability:** The promise of releasing an open-source implementation is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the *application* to joint parsing is effective, the core method – Stack LSTMs – is not novel.  Dyer et al. (2015) introduced Stack LSTMs, and the paper doesn’t present significant architectural innovations *to* the Stack LSTM itself. The novelty lies more in *how* it's applied, rather than *what* is applied.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art is Limited:** The comparison to the very best performing systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015) is largely framed around limitations – they use expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. While acknowledged, the paper doesn’t deeply engage with *how* to bridge the gap to these top performers. Further exploration of integrating these orthogonal advancements would strengthen the work.
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:**  The results, particularly for multilingual performance, highlight a reliance on high-quality pre-trained word embeddings. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t extensively explore the impact of different embedding strategies or methods for handling out-of-vocabulary words beyond the simple random initialization.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific dependency types, long-range dependencies, rare predicate senses) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Bridging the Gap to SOTA:** You acknowledge the gap in performance compared to systems using expert-crafted features and optimal decoding.  What are your initial thoughts on how to integrate these advancements into your model? Specifically, could you foresee benefits from incorporating some limited form of syntactic features, even if learned, or exploring more sophisticated decoding algorithms?
2.  **Embedding Sensitivity:** How sensitive are your results to the quality and size of the pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding initialization strategies or training methods?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide some preliminary error analysis, identifying common error patterns in your model’s predictions? This could involve analyzing specific dependency types or predicate senses where the model struggles.
4. **Transition Constraints:** While you mention constraints on transitions to ensure valid parses, were any other constraints considered during development and discarded? If so, could you briefly explain why they were not adopted?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The inclusion of example transition sequences (mentioned in §A and the supplementary material) is helpful for understanding the parsing process.
* The organization of the paper is logical and the figures are clear and informative.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a compelling approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.  While the core method (Stack LSTMs) isn’t entirely novel, the application to joint parsing and the achieved performance with a greedy algorithm are significant contributions. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and promises to be a valuable resource for the research community.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
