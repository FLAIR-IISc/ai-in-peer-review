This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state, eschewing expert-crafted features and employing greedy inference. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline systems, particularly regarding speed.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective State Representation with Stack LSTMs:** The central contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of using stack LSTMs to represent the entire state of a transition-based joint parser, replacing traditional feature engineering. This allows the model to learn representations without relying on manually designed features, a significant step toward more data-driven parsing.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on standard datasets (CoNLL 2008/2009), demonstrating that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance amongst joint parsing models. Specifically, the results on CoNLL 2009 are notable given the comparison to more complex pipeline systems.
3. **Fast Inference:** The greedy inference approach combined with the efficient stack LSTM representation results in a significantly faster parsing speed compared to state-of-the-art systems that employ optimal decoding or rely on pipelines. This speed is a practical advantage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanation of the transition-based procedure and the stack LSTM architecture is clear and well-structured. The inclusion of Figure 1 and Table 1 aids in understanding the technical details.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors appropriately compare their model against relevant baselines, including previous joint models (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) and strong pipeline systems. This contextualizes their contributions effectively.
3. **Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides valuable insights into the contributions of syntactic and semantic information to the overall performance. These experiments clearly demonstrate the benefit of joint modeling.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The emphasis on fast inference and the intention to release an open-source implementation increase the practical impact of the work.
5. **Multilingual Results:** Presenting results on multiple languages (CoNLL 2009 multilingual data) demonstrates the model’s generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** While the application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing is valuable, the underlying transition system (Henderson et al., 2013) isn’t significantly altered. The primary novelty lies in the state representation rather than a fundamentally new parsing algorithm. The paper acknowledges this, but the framing emphasizes novelty where it is less pronounced.
2. **Performance Gap with SOTA:** Despite strong performance among *joint* models, the system doesn’t reach the performance levels of the best pipeline systems that leverage expert-crafted features and/or optimal decoding. This gap, while acknowledged, could be further addressed in the discussion. The justification that these features are "expensive" doesn't fully explain the gap, as efficient feature extraction methods exist.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the model makes could inform future improvements. For example, are there specific linguistic phenomena that consistently cause errors?
4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the process isn't described in sufficient detail. Providing more specifics about the search space and optimization strategy would enhance reproducibility.
5. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on the Chinese dataset is heavily reliant on pretrained word embeddings. A more detailed discussion of how the model handles unseen words or languages without readily available embeddings would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the stack LSTM representation compared to traditional feature engineering approaches? While you highlight speed in inference, a discussion of the training time and memory requirements would be valuable.
2.  What specific linguistic phenomena does the model struggle with, and do you have hypotheses about why these are challenging?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the LSTM hidden state dimension and the dropout rate?
4.  Are there any plans to investigate methods for incorporating morphological features or character-based word embeddings to improve performance on morphologically rich languages or for handling out-of-vocabulary words?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Lei et al. (2015)’s runtime could be clarified – is that the entire pipeline runtime, or just the SRL component?
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each experiment.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The use of stack LSTMs for state representation is effective, and the empirical results are strong. While the core algorithm is not radically new, the application and the demonstrated performance are significant. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through further experimentation and analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
