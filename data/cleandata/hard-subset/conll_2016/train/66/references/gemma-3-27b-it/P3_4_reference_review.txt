## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that leverages Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state, eschewing hand-crafted features. The model employs a greedy, incremental parsing approach, updating syntactic and semantic stacks in an interleaved manner.  The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results against pipeline systems, while maintaining a significantly faster runtime.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Feature Learning for Joint Parsing:** The successful application of Stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics *without* relying on traditional, engineered features is a key contribution. This shows the power of representation learning to capture the necessary information for this complex task, potentially simplifying the development process and reducing reliance on linguistic expertise.
2. **Competitive Performance with a Greedy Approach:** Achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance using a purely greedy decoding strategy is notable.  Most high-performing systems utilize more computationally expensive search algorithms. This suggests a practical benefit for real-world applications where speed is critical.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Baseline:** The thorough evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, 2009 English and multilingual) and comparison to a wide range of existing systems provides a solid benchmark and demonstrates the model’s generalizability.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser consistently outperforms previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL datasets.  The results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The performance is particularly impressive given the absence of hand-crafted features.
2. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 is a significant advantage. Many competitive systems take substantially longer, making this approach potentially more suitable for applications requiring low latency.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a thorough description of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. This allows for reproducibility and facilitates further research. The inclusion of a transition example and the discussion of constraints are helpful.
4. **Well-Positioned within the Literature:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of prior work in joint parsing and clearly articulate how their contribution builds upon and differs from existing approaches. The comparison to both pipeline and joint models is insightful.
5. **Ablation Studies & Analysis:** The inclusion of the Semantics-only, Syntax-only, and Hybrid models allow for a better understanding of the benefit of joint training and the contribution of each parsing component.  The analysis of the results (e.g., the out-of-domain performance of the Hybrid model) is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Algorithm:** While the application of Stack LSTMs *to this specific joint parsing task* is valuable, the underlying algorithmic framework (arc-eager parsing with interleaved transitions) is largely based on Henderson et al. (2013). The innovation primarily lies in the representation learning component. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the algorithmic contributions versus the representation learning contributions.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art is Not Fully Convincing:** While the paper acknowledges that it doesn’t match the performance of the *very* best systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015), it downplays the gap.  These systems leverage features and/or decoding strategies that are acknowledged as expensive but demonstrably effective.  A more thorough discussion of *why* these systems outperform the proposed model, beyond simply stating they are orthogonal, is needed. It's not enough to say "we expect future work to integrate them"; a discussion of the challenges of doing so would be helpful.
3. **Reliance on Pretrained Embeddings:** The model's performance, particularly in the multilingual setting, depends heavily on pretrained embeddings. While this is a common practice, it introduces a dependency on external resources.  A more detailed analysis of the impact of different embedding strategies (e.g., training embeddings from scratch) would be valuable.
4. **Potential for Overfitting:** The performance gains on the in-domain test set, coupled with the drop in performance on the out-of-domain test set (Brown corpus), suggest some potential for overfitting.  While the authors note this, a more detailed analysis of the overfitting mechanisms and potential regularization strategies (beyond dropout) would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect. Could you quantify the impact of this imperfect conversion on your reported results? Specifically, how many sentences have transition sequences that incorrectly encode the gold-standard parse?
2.  You state that integrating features from top-performing pipeline systems is a direction for future work.  What are the *specific* challenges in integrating those features (e.g., how would you efficiently represent and incorporate path features within the Stack LSTM framework)?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors your system makes?  Are there specific syntactic or semantic dependencies that are consistently mispredicted?  This could help guide future improvements.
4.  Given the success of Stack LSTMs, have you considered experimenting with other more recent neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) for state representation learning?  What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of using Transformers in this context?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure illustrating the Stack LSTM architecture (Figure 3) is helpful, but could benefit from more detailed annotations explaining the flow of information.
*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but could be more tightly focused on the most relevant contributions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn effective representations for this task is a significant achievement, and the resulting parser achieves competitive performance with a fast runtime. While the core algorithmic framework isn't entirely novel, the combination of representation learning and a greedy approach is compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
