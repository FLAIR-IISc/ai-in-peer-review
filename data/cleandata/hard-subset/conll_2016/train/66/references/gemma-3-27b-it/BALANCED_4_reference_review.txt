This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state, eschewing hand-crafted features and employing greedy inference. The system demonstrates strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable results to pipeline-based state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs for Joint Parsing:** The central contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of Stack LSTMs for encoding the entire parser state in a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing framework. This allows the model to achieve competitive performance *without* relying on extensive feature engineering or full parse trees, a significant advancement.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art or competitive performance among joint models, and comparable performance to strong pipeline baselines. This showcases the practical utility of the proposed approach.
3. **Efficient Greedy Inference:** The use of greedy inference provides a significant speed advantage over systems relying on optimal decoding or reranking. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data highlights the model’s potential for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The transitions and model architecture are explained with sufficient detail.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental results are comprehensive, including comparisons to a wide range of baseline systems and ablation studies (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid). This strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Novelty in Representation Learning:** The application of Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser state in a joint setting is novel and provides a compelling alternative to traditional feature-based approaches. The design choice to avoid explicit feature engineering is commendable.
4. **Practical Efficiency:** The speed of the greedy inference algorithm is a significant advantage, making the model suitable for applications where real-time performance is critical.
5. **Multilingual Performance:** The demonstration of comparable performance on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets suggests the model’s generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains over Strong Pipelines:** While the model achieves competitive performance, it doesn't consistently outperform the strongest pipeline-based systems that *do* utilize carefully engineered features and optimal decoding. The paper acknowledges this, but a deeper analysis of the gap and potential avenues for bridging it would be valuable. Specifically, a discussion on *why* those features are still helpful, even with the learned representations, is missing.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on the Chinese dataset is strongly linked to the quality of the pretrained word embeddings. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different embedding initialization strategies and training data would be beneficial.
3. **Simplification of Predicate Sense Disambiguation:** The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to sense "01" is a pragmatic solution but introduces a potential source of error. A more sophisticated approach to handling unseen predicates, perhaps leveraging subword information or contextual similarity, could improve performance.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are presented clearly, a more detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the model’s weaknesses and potential areas for improvement. Understanding the types of errors the model makes could guide future development efforts.
5. **Lack of Discussion on Transition System Limitations:** The paper briefly mentions the oracle error rate in the transition sequence, but doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations imposed by the chosen transition system and how these might impact performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that expert-crafted features are expensive to extract. Can you quantify this cost (e.g., in terms of runtime or engineering effort) compared to the computational cost of training and running the Stack LSTMs?
2. What is the sensitivity of the model's performance to the hyperparameters, particularly the LSTM hidden state dimension and the dropout rate?
3. The results on CoNLL 2009 suggest a trade-off between performance in-domain (WSJ) and out-of-domain (Brown). Can you elaborate on the reasons for this trade-off and potential strategies for mitigating it?
4. You mention the limitations of the transition system. Could exploring alternative, potentially more expressive, transition systems improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 is very helpful for understanding the task.
*   The distinction between copying and moving items from the buffer is important and clearly explained.
*   The reference to publicly releasing the code is a significant positive aspect.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The proposed model demonstrates competitive performance, efficiency, and a novel approach to representation learning. While there are areas for improvement, the paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and offers a compelling alternative to traditional feature-based methods. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should be encouraged to address the questions raised to further strengthen their contribution.
