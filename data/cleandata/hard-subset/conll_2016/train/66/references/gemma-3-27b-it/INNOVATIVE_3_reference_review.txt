This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to represent the parser's state, eschewing traditional hand-crafted features and employing a purely greedy decoding strategy. The core contribution is a demonstration that strong joint parsing performance *can* be achieved without explicit, expertly designed features, relying instead on learned representations of the parsing process itself. A secondary, and notable, contribution is the speed of the system, offering a compelling alternative to slower, feature-rich pipeline approaches. Finally, the multilingual results, while not groundbreaking, show a degree of generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** This is the strongest aspect of the paper. The decision to move away from feature engineering and rely entirely on learned state representations, coupled with a greedy decoding scheme, is a bold and interesting direction. It challenges the established paradigm in joint parsing where extensive feature design is considered crucial. The successful performance achieved is a significant statement on the power of representation learning in this domain.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser consistently outperforms previous joint models, including the foundational work of Henderson et al., on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. While it doesn’t surpass the best pipeline systems, the gap isn't enormous, and the trade-off with speed is appealing.
3. **Speed and Efficiency:** The reported runtime (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009) is significantly faster than many existing systems. This practical advantage is important and makes the model potentially valuable for real-world applications where latency is a concern.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the technical details of the model and experimental setup. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm (referenced in the text) is helpful for understanding the process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of *Why* it Works:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the approach works, it offers limited insight into *why*. A more detailed ablation study investigating the contributions of different components (e.g., the different Stack LSTMs, the composition functions) would strengthen the analysis. Simply stating that the entire parser state representation is beneficial isn't sufficient.
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to state-of-the-art systems feels somewhat incomplete. The authors acknowledge that their system doesn't match the performance of the best expert-crafted feature-based systems, but the discussion of these systems feels cursory. A more detailed comparative analysis – even if it highlights the limitations of their approach – would be valuable. Specifically, while they mention orthogonal advances, a discussion of *attempting* to integrate some (even superficially) would demonstrate a stronger understanding of the field and areas for future work.
3. **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The choice of greedy decoding is presented as a deliberate design decision. However, it’s also a potential limitation. The paper does not sufficiently explore the impact of this choice on performance, or compare it to beam search (even if it results in a speed trade-off). It's possible that beam search could recover some of the performance gap with the stronger baseline systems.
4. **Predicate Sense Disambiguation Handling:** The handling of unseen predicates at test time (defaulting to sense "01") is a pragmatic solution but feels like a potential weakness. A more robust mechanism for handling unseen predicates would be desirable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study examining the contribution of each Stack LSTM (S, M, B, A) to the overall performance of the model?
2. Have you experimented with integrating any of the features or techniques used in the state-of-the-art pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009), even in a limited fashion? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3. Have you explored beam search decoding as an alternative to the greedy approach? If so, what was the impact on performance and runtime? If not, why not?
4. What is the error analysis regarding the cases where the model performs poorly? Are there specific linguistic phenomena or sentence structures that consistently cause problems?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to a “structured skipgram” embedding doesn’t seem to appear in the bibliography. Please double check and add if necessary. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning seems brief. A more detailed description of the tuning process would be helpful.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a compelling and potentially impactful paper. The novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results outweigh the limitations. The paper introduces a significant new direction for joint parsing research and I lean towards **Accept**. The authors should address the questions and concerns raised in the review during the rebuttal period to further strengthen the submission.
