- Presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using Stack LSTMs to learn entire parser-state representations without hand-crafted features.  
- Builds on Henderson et al. (2008) with transition-based incremental parsing and fully greedy inference, achieving strong speed.  
- Shows state-of-the-art results among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English) and competitive multilingual performance on CoNLL 2009.  
- Demonstrates that learned state representations reduce reliance on feature engineering for joint parsing.  
- Achieves competitive empirical performance while outperforming prior joint models despite no hand-crafted features.  
- Uses greedy inference with learned representations to produce a very fast parsing system suitable for real-time applications.  
- Clearly motivates joint parsing and effectively relates the approach to prior work, highlighting differences.  
- Provides thorough experiments across datasets with useful ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid).  
- Commits to releasing open-source code, supporting reproducibility.  
- Adapts Stack LSTMs effectively to the joint parsing task and maintains a buffer for syntax–semantics interaction.  
- Core transition system and greedy inference lack novelty; main innovation is representation learning.  
- Does not match top-performing, feature-rich non-greedy systems; more analysis of failure cases is needed.  
- Lacks a comprehensive error analysis to identify common syntactic or semantic mistakes.  
- Hyperparameter tuning details are vague; more transparency on tuning strategy is required.  
- Heavy reliance on pretrained embeddings, especially for multilingual results, raises concerns about OOV and embedding quality.  
- Requests a detailed error analysis specifying frequent error types and patterns across structures and roles.  
- Asks for sensitivity analysis to hyperparameters (LSTM size, dropout) and a performance table for configurations.  
- Suggests exploring simple automatic features (e.g., word-shape) to assess potential gains despite the feature-free design.  
- Inquires about scalability with sentence length and potential limitations of Stack LSTMs on long sentences.  
- Asks for clarification on the impact of the M-SELF transition and its effect on nominal predicate handling.  
- Notes the paper is well-written, Figure 1 is helpful, and runtime comparisons (e.g., Lei et al.) are valuable.  
- Recommends adding a table summarizing key hyperparameters and their values.  
- Overall: a solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical and efficiency contributions; recommends acceptance with minor revisions for deeper error analysis and hyperparameter details.
