The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state, eschewing hand-crafted features and employing a greedy inference approach. The primary claimed contribution is achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 while maintaining speed and simplicity. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of applying stack LSTMs to joint parsing, extending their previous success in syntactic parsing. Finally, they introduce a novel transition, M-SELF, to handle self-dependencies within the semantic graph. 

From my assessment, the core contribution is the demonstration of a reasonably performing joint parser *without* extensive feature engineering, achieved through the use of stack LSTMs. The M-SELF transition is a necessary addition for the specific corpus, but hardly a groundbreaking innovation. The speed of the parser is a practical benefit, but not a scientific contribution in itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Simplicity and Speed:** The model’s reliance on learned representations and greedy decoding leads to a demonstrably faster parsing speed compared to more complex, feature-rich pipeline systems. This is a valuable practical advantage, particularly for applications requiring real-time processing. The reported 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 is impressive.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves results comparable to strong baseline systems on the CoNLL datasets, indicating the effectiveness of the stack LSTM approach for capturing relevant information from the parsing state. While not exceeding state-of-the-art, achieving parity without hand-crafted features is notable.
3. **Clear Presentation of Algorithm:** The explanation of the transition-based parsing procedure, including the interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions, is well-structured and easy to follow. The inclusion of Table 1 is helpful for understanding the action space.
4. **Novel Transition for Self-Dependencies:** The introduction of the M-SELF transition addresses a specific characteristic of the CoNLL 2009 data and demonstrates a practical solution to a real-world parsing challenge.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Significant Novelty:** While the combination of stack LSTMs and greedy decoding for joint parsing is a reasonable approach, the individual components are not novel. Stack LSTMs have been previously applied to syntactic parsing, and greedy decoding is a well-established technique. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new algorithmic insight.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to state-of-the-art systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015) is largely dismissive. The authors acknowledge they don’t match performance, attributing it to feature engineering and optimal decoding, but don't sufficiently investigate *why* their approach falls short beyond these generalizations. A more detailed ablation study, perhaps comparing different LSTM configurations or embedding sizes, would strengthen the analysis. The reliance on a single, older speed comparison (Lei et al., 2015) feels selective.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Limited Tuning:** The authors admit that hyperparameters were not exhaustively tuned. The description of hyperparameter selection is vague (“tuned on the dev data”). The choice of dropout rate and LSTM hidden dimension could significantly impact performance, and a more rigorous search is necessary.
4. **Overstated Claims of Bypassing Features:** The claim that the model "bypasses those expensive features" (referring to Gildea & Jurafsky's path features) is too strong. The stack LSTM *learns* representations that may implicitly capture similar information, but this is not equivalent to directly utilizing those engineered features. The paper lacks evidence demonstrating the model's ability to encode equivalent information without explicit feature design.
5. **Inconsistent Notation/Detail:** Table 1 describes the stacks as containing *both* symbolic and vector representations. However, the text doesn't clearly describe how these are combined within the LSTM. Is the symbolic representation used for indexing, or is it embedded as well? This lack of clarity hinders reproducibility. Also, the explanation of "copying" vs. "moving" items from the buffer feels convoluted and could be simplified.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study comparing the performance of your model with different LSTM configurations (e.g., number of layers, hidden dimension size) and embedding sizes?
2.  Please clarify how the symbolic and vector representations are combined within the stack LSTMs. Provide a more explicit description of the input format for the LSTM.
3.  The paper states that the system doesn't match the performance of systems using "expert-crafted features." Can you quantify the impact of adding a small number of carefully selected, high-performing features to your model? Would this close the performance gap?
4. The claim of speed is prominent. How does the speed compare to other neural network-based, greedy parsers, not just pipeline systems?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to a GitHub repository is good practice, but confirming its availability during the review process would be beneficial. The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are unnecessarily verbose. Consider streamlining the language for greater clarity. The discussion of related work is acceptable, but not particularly comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a functional and reasonably efficient joint parser, it lacks substantial novelty and falls short of demonstrating a significant advancement in the field. The experimental analysis is adequate but could be strengthened by more thorough ablation studies and a more nuanced comparison to state-of-the-art systems. Due to the lack of groundbreaking contributions and the presence of several weaknesses, I recommend **rejection**. While the work may be valuable for practitioners seeking a fast, feature-engineered-free parser, it doesn't meet the standards for publication at a highly selective venue like CoNLL.
