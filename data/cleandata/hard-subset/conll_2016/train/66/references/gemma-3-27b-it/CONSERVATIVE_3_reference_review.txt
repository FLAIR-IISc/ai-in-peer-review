This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state, abandoning traditional feature engineering and beam search in favor of a greedy approach. The primary claimed contribution is achieving state-of-the-art performance among *joint* parsing models on CoNLL 2008/2009, while maintaining fast parsing speed. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of learning representations of the entire parser state, rather than relying on hand-crafted features or local stack information. A final, less substantial claim, is showing comparable multilingual performance on CoNLL 2009.

In my assessment, the primary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of a competitive joint parsing system *without* extensive feature engineering, especially given the speed advantage. The use of stack LSTMs to represent the parser state is a reasonable application of existing technology, but not inherently novel. The experimental results are the most compelling aspect of this work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves strong results on the CoNLL shared tasks, outperforming previous *joint* models. While it doesn't surpass the best pipeline systems, the performance gap is narrowing, and this represents a solid advancement in joint parsing.  The reported LAS and F1 scores are clearly presented and provide a quantifiable measure of success.
2. **Speed Advantage:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 English test data is a significant benefit.  Many high-performance systems are computationally expensive. The authors rightly emphasize this as a practical advantage.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the transition-based procedure and the stack LSTM architecture are clear and easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 1 and the example transition sequence (mentioned, though not included) aids understanding.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide results for CoNLL 2008 and 2009, and also experiment with variations of the model (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid) providing ablation studies to understand the contributions of each component. The multilingual results, while not a primary focus, add further evidence of the model’s generalizability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core innovation – using stack LSTMs for state representation – is an application of an existing technique. The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new algorithmic approach or a novel neural network architecture. The claimed novelty of representing the *entire* parser state feels incremental, as previous work already explored LSTM-based state representation, even if more limited in scope.
2. **Comparison to Pipeline Systems:** While acknowledging the superiority of pipeline systems with hand-crafted features, the paper doesn’t adequately address *why* these features remain beneficial. A deeper analysis of what information is lost by avoiding explicit feature engineering would strengthen the work. Simply stating that "recent advances in representation learning can bypass those expensive features" is insufficient. Is it truly bypassing or merely approximating these features?
3. **Greedy Inference:** The decision to use greedy inference is presented as a simplifying choice, but its impact on performance is not fully explored. Beam search, though computationally more expensive, often leads to better results. A comparison of greedy vs. beam search, even on a small scale, would be valuable. The authors don’t convincingly justify the trade-off.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error patterns and discussing the limitations of the model would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future work. What types of semantic dependencies are particularly challenging for the system?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the features used in the state-of-the-art pipeline systems that outperform your model? Specifically, which features contribute most to the performance gain?
2. Have you experimented with beam search decoding, even with a small beam size, and if so, how did it affect performance compared to the greedy approach?
3. Could you provide an example of a transition sequence (as referenced in the text) demonstrating the interleaved syntactic and semantic transitions?
4. What is the impact of the pseudo-projectivization step on performance? Do you observe a significant difference in performance when processing truly non-projective trees?
5. The paper mentions the potential for incorporating morphological features or character-based word embeddings. Have you explored these options, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to "Figure 5 in the supplementary material" feels out of place in the main text. Either include the figure or remove the reference. The statement regarding the oracle conversion of semantic parses having a 1.5% error rate is concerning and should be explored further. While the authors acknowledge it, the impact on the reported results is not quantified.
