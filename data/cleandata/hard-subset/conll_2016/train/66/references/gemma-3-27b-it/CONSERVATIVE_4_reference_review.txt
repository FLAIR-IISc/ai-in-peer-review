This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state, eschewing traditional, hand-crafted features. The core contribution appears to be demonstrating competitive performance on standard datasets (CoNLL 2008/2009) *without* relying on the extensive feature engineering typically employed in state-of-the-art systems. A secondary, though practical, contribution is the system’s speed.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance Without Feature Engineering:** The reported results are encouraging. Achieving performance comparable to pipeline systems known for their complex feature sets, while relying solely on learned representations, is a noteworthy achievement. This suggests the Stack LSTM approach effectively captures necessary information from the parsing state.
2. **Practical Efficiency:** The reported runtime (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English) is a significant advantage. Many high-performing systems are computationally expensive, limiting their practical applicability. This fast, joint model addresses a key need for real-time or large-scale parsing.
3. **Clear Presentation of Methodology:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the transition-based parsing algorithm and the application of Stack LSTMs. The diagrams and table detailing transitions are helpful for understanding the technical details.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English & multilingual) and the ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provide a reasonable assessment of the model's capabilities and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing is a reasonable extension of prior work (Dyer et al., 2015), the core algorithmic approach (incremental structure building based on Henderson et al., 2008) is well-established. The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new parsing paradigm. The primary innovation lies in replacing hand-crafted features with learned representations, a trend already present in other areas of NLP.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The authors acknowledge that their system does not match the performance of state-of-the-art systems relying on expert-crafted features and optimal decoding (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015). While they attribute this to differences in methodology, a more direct comparison—perhaps by incorporating *some* of those features into their model—would strengthen the claim of competitiveness. Simply stating these advances are “orthogonal” isn’t sufficient justification.
3. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Analysis:** The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are useful, but lack depth. It would be helpful to understand *why* the Semantics-only model underperforms and *what* specific syntactic information is most beneficial to the joint model. A more granular analysis of the learned representations could offer insights.
4. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The multilingual performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings, particularly for Chinese. This raises concerns about the model's ability to generalize to languages with limited resources for pretraining. The impact of embedding quality on performance isn't adequately addressed.
5. **Greedy Decoding:** The reliance on greedy inference is a significant limitation. While contributing to speed, it almost certainly sacrifices accuracy compared to beam search or other more sophisticated decoding strategies. The authors do not sufficiently justify this trade-off.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations? What specific features or linguistic patterns do the Stack LSTMs appear to be capturing?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality and size of the pretrained embeddings, particularly for low-resource languages?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating even a small set of carefully selected hand-crafted features into your model? If so, what was the impact on performance?
4.  What is the computational cost of exploring beam search during inference? Is it feasible to integrate beam search without significantly sacrificing the model's speed advantage?
5.  The paper mentions inconsistencies during the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions. Could you elaborate on the nature of these inconsistencies and their potential impact on the reported results?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to Lei et al. (2015) runtime is helpful but should also include information on the hardware used. Please clarify if the stated runtime of 177.6 seconds was measured on the same hardware as the baseline systems.
