## Review

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, greedy approach, leveraging Stack LSTMs to represent parser state without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors claim to outperform previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks and achieve comparable multilingual performance.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of a competitive joint parser without extensive feature engineering:** The core contribution lies in showing that a reasonably performing joint syntactic-semantic parser can be built *without* the intricate, manually designed features that traditionally dominate this area. This is particularly relevant given the cost of developing and maintaining such features.
2. **Effective application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing:** The paper successfully adapts Stack LSTMs, previously demonstrated for syntactic parsing, to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.
3. **Fast, Greedy Inference:** The implementation provides a fast parsing speed, highlighting a potential benefit for practical applications where speed is critical.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The system achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results compared to established joint parsing models on the standard CoNLL datasets.  While not state-of-the-art against systems utilizing extensive features and reranking, the performance is noteworthy given the minimalist feature set. The tables clearly present a comparison with relevant baselines, aiding in assessment.
2. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a comprehensive description of the parsing algorithm, transition definitions, and the neural network architecture. The inclusion of Table 1 detailing the transitions is particularly helpful. The explanation of how the stacks and buffer are managed within the greedy parsing process is well-articulated.
3. **Practicality and Efficiency:**  The reported runtime (177.6 seconds) is significantly faster than many feature-rich pipeline systems, making the approach appealing for applications where speed is important. The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation further enhances the paper’s value.
4. **Thoughtful Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of the Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid models provides a useful analysis of the contribution of each component to the overall performance, supporting the claim that both syntactic and semantic information are beneficial, and providing insight into the interaction between the two.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas – transition-based parsing, Stack LSTMs – are not novel in themselves. The primary contribution is the *combination* of these established techniques in the context of joint parsing.  The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the existing landscape and a more precise articulation of what differentiates this work beyond simply applying Stack LSTMs to a different task.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strongest Baselines:** While the authors compare to previous joint models, they acknowledge that their performance doesn’t match the *best* performing systems, which rely on carefully engineered features, optimal decoding, and/or external datasets. The justification for this omission (orthogonality of advancements) is weak. A more direct comparison, even with an attempt to integrate some of those advancements, would strengthen the paper significantly. Simply stating they expect future work to integrate those features feels like an admission of a significant gap.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of errors the system makes would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.  For example, are there specific semantic roles or syntactic structures that consistently cause problems?
4. **Over-reliance on Established Methods:** While the use of established methods provides a degree of robustness, it also limits the potential for groundbreaking advancements. The claim that linguistic theory offers less guidance in feature design feels somewhat dismissive, given the successes of feature-rich systems. A more balanced view acknowledging the benefits of linguistic insights would be more appropriate.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What specific modifications or extensions would be required to incorporate features similar to those used in the top-performing systems (e.g., path features, global reranking) into your model?  Provide a rough estimate of the computational cost of adding such features.
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of errors your system makes and potential reasons for these errors?  Include examples.
3.  The paper states the system relies on pseudo-projectivization. How sensitive is the performance of the system to the quality of this pseudo-projectivization step, and what impact does it have on parsing accuracy?
4. The transition sequence in section 2.1 states that the algorithm switches to semantic transitions until an M-SHIFT is taken. Is it possible for the algorithm to stall if no M-SHIFT transition is ever available? If so, what is the method to resolve this?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are helpful but could benefit from clearer labeling and more detailed explanations of the components.
*   Consider providing more details about the hyperparameter tuning process.
*   The reference to Lewis et al. (2015) feels slightly out of place, given the different task.  If it's included, a clearer explanation of its relevance would be helpful.
