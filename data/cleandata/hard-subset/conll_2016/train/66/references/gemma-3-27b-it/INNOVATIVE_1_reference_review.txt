This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state, eschewing hand-crafted features and employing a purely greedy inference approach. The primary contribution is a demonstration that strong joint parsing performance can be achieved *without* relying on expensive, globally-aware features traditionally central to state-of-the-art systems, and *without* computationally expensive search algorithms. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is a clean, relatively simple implementation of a joint parser based on a well-established algorithmic framework (Henderson et al.’s incremental structure building). Finally, the multilingual evaluation, though not a focus, provides some evidence of the method’s adaptability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Approach:** The core idea – to perform competitive joint parsing solely on the basis of learned state representations and a greedy decoding strategy – is genuinely exciting. The field has become dominated by feature engineering and complex decoding schemes, and this work offers a compelling alternative. The shift away from explicit, hand-crafted features is particularly significant, and represents a substantial conceptual simplification.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009, exceeding prior joint models and approaching the performance of pipeline systems, are impressive. This is not merely a theoretical contribution; it demonstrates practical viability. Achieving comparable performance with significantly less engineering effort is a substantial win.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The inclusion of the example transition sequence in the supplementary material is helpful for understanding the algorithm. The architecture diagrams are informative.
4. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 is very attractive, especially compared to more complex systems. This practical speed advantage could be crucial for real-world applications.
5. **Open Source Commitment:** The authors’ commitment to releasing an open-source implementation further enhances the value of this work and will facilitate its adoption and extension by the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis & Ablation:** While the authors present results for variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid), a more thorough ablation study is needed.  What specifically is being *learned* by the Stack LSTMs? What components are most crucial to performance? A deeper analysis of where the model struggles (error types) would be insightful.
2. **Comparison to Current SOTA is Weak:** The authors acknowledge that their performance lags behind the most recent state-of-the-art systems (Lei et al., 2015, Täckström et al., 2015, FitzGerald et al., 2015) but largely dismiss this as due to the use of features or datasets orthogonal to their work. While true, this framing downplays the gap. A more nuanced discussion of how their approach *could* be integrated with these advances (beyond simply stating it’s possible) would strengthen the paper.
3. **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The reliance on greedy decoding is a potential drawback. While it contributes to the system’s speed, it may be sacrificing accuracy. A brief investigation into the impact of greedy decoding on performance, perhaps by comparing to a limited-beam search, would be beneficial. The paper touches on this with the observation of overfitting in the multilingual setting, which may be related to greedy decoding.
4. **Sense Disambiguation Handling:** The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to sense "01" is a pragmatic solution but feels somewhat ad-hoc. It's not clear how robust this approach is, and it could introduce bias. More discussion of this limitation is warranted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations within the Stack LSTMs? Are there any discernible patterns or linguistic properties encoded in these representations?
2.  You mention potential integration with systems leveraging external datasets. Can you elaborate on how you envision doing so, specifically addressing the challenges of incorporating such data into your current framework?
3. What is the impact of greedy decoding on the types of errors the system makes? Are there particular error patterns that are exacerbated by this approach?
4. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of initialization and hyperparameters for the Stack LSTMs? Did you conduct a thorough hyperparameter search?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to the runtime comparison with Lei et al. (2015) is excellent – a clear demonstration of the practical benefits of the approach.
* The discussion of related work is reasonable but could be expanded to include more recent advances in neural SRL.



**Overall:**

Despite the weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper presents a truly novel and promising approach to joint parsing. The authors have demonstrated that strong performance can be achieved without the complex feature engineering and decoding schemes that have traditionally dominated the field. While further work is needed to address the limitations, the boldness of the idea and the encouraging empirical results make this a valuable contribution. The potential for a simpler, faster, and more adaptable joint parsing system is significant.
