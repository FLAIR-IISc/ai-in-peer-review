## Review

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state, eschewing traditional, hand-crafted features. The authors claim to outperform previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks and achieve comparable multilingual results. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration of a fully neural joint parser that achieves competitive performance *without* relying on the intricate feature engineering historically dominant in this area. A secondary contribution is a streamlined, greedy parsing approach, offering a speed advantage over more computationally intensive methods like those employing optimal decoding. The focus on a simple, end-to-end system is potentially valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves results comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, previous state-of-the-art systems on the CoNLL datasets. While not surpassing the very best pipeline systems, the performance gap is justifiable given the lack of hand-crafted features and the speed advantage. This demonstrates the viability of a purely representation learning approach to joint parsing.
2. **Simplicity and Speed:** The adoption of a greedy parsing approach combined with the Stack LSTM architecture results in a significantly faster system than many of its competitors.  A runtime of 177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 is a practical advantage, particularly for applications requiring real-time processing.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the transition system and the neural network architecture. The inclusion of Figure 3 illustrating the Stack LSTM setup is helpful.
4. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors provide ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) which offer valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing within their framework. The multilingual evaluation, even if brief, adds to the overall robustness of the evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to *joint* parsing is presented as novel, the individual components (Stack LSTMs, arc-eager parsing) are well-established. The core innovation lies in the *combination* of these, but the degree of novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The claim of bypassing "expensive features" is overstated; the authors merely replace them with learned representations, which require substantial training data and computational resources.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparisons to pipeline systems are somewhat superficial. The authors acknowledge these systems outperform theirs, but the discussion lacks a detailed analysis of *why*. A more direct comparison, perhaps with an attempt to incorporate some of the features used in those pipelines into their neural framework, would be valuable. The statement that advancements in those systems are “orthogonal” feels dismissive; feature engineering and optimal decoding are fundamental to achieving high performance in this task.
3. **Data Dependency & Generalizability Concerns:** The results on the multilingual datasets, particularly Japanese, suggest the model’s performance is highly sensitive to data availability. The authors acknowledge this, but the implications for the generalizability of the approach are not thoroughly discussed. Further investigation into data augmentation or transfer learning techniques would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** There’s no in-depth error analysis presented. Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., specific dependency types, rare predicates) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Simply stating that performance is comparable to other systems isn’t sufficient.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the model, including details about hardware, training time, and memory requirements?
2.  You mention the potential for incorporating morphological features. Have you experimented with character-based word embeddings as an alternative, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes, and how these errors differ from those made by strong pipeline-based systems?
4.  The paper states the greedy approach is key to speed. How much performance is lost, if any, by using greedy decoding rather than a beam search or other optimal decoding algorithm?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions and the 1.5% error rate should be further explored. Does this impact the reported results?
*   The justification for choosing the specific hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden state dimension, dropout rate) could be strengthened.
*   The reference to “structured skipgram” embeddings seems a bit out of place and could be expanded or simplified.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. While the innovation is incremental, the demonstration of a competitive, fully neural system with a focus on speed is valuable. However, the weaknesses related to limited novelty, insufficient comparison to strong baselines, and lack of error analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. Addressing the questions and concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper.
