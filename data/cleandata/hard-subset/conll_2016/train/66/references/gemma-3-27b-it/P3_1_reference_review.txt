## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure building approach. The key innovation lies in learning representations of the *entire* parser state using Stack LSTMs, eschewing hand-crafted features traditionally relied upon in such models. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming prior joint models and achieving competitive results against pipeline systems, all while maintaining a significantly faster runtime.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective joint parsing *without* hand-crafted features:** The paper convincingly shows that strong performance in joint syntactic-semantic parsing can be achieved purely through representation learning, eliminating the need for complex, expert-designed features. This is a significant step towards more generalizable and adaptable parsing models.
2. **Successful application of Stack LSTMs to a joint parsing task:** While Stack LSTMs have shown promise in syntactic parsing, this work extends their utility to the more complex realm of joint syntactic-semantic analysis, showcasing their ability to capture and represent the interactions between these two levels of linguistic structure.
3. **Efficient and competitive performance:** The model's speed, outperforming slower pipeline-based systems, highlights its practicality for real-world applications.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL datasets.  The performance gains, while not surpassing the *best* systems (which often use pipeline approaches and substantial external resources), are impressive given the simplicity of the model and its lack of hand-engineered features. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are well-designed and offer valuable insight into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:**  The authors effectively articulate the limitations of traditional pipeline approaches and the motivation for their feature-learning paradigm. The rationale for using Stack LSTMs, given their ability to summarize the entire parser state, is sound.
3. **Thorough Description of the Model:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the transition system, the LSTM architecture, and the training procedure. The inclusion of examples and references to previous work helps to clarify the technical details. The description of the transition constraints is particularly valuable as it explains how the parser remains in a valid state.
4. **Practicality and Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 test data is a significant advantage, making the model attractive for applications where speed is critical. The promise of an open-source implementation further enhances its accessibility.
5. **Multilingual Performance:** The comparable performance on multiple languages demonstrates the model's potential for cross-lingual transfer learning.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Methodology:** The core algorithmic framework (transition-based, incremental parsing) is based on prior work (Henderson et al., 2008, 2013). The primary novelty lies in the application of Stack LSTMs and the elimination of hand-crafted features.  While valuable, this is an incremental rather than a breakthrough advance. The paper acknowledges this, but it’s important to contextualize the contribution accurately.
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines is Uneven:** The paper acknowledges that the best-performing systems rely on expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, and/or additional datasets. The comparison focuses more on *previous joint models*, potentially downplaying the gap between this work and the current state-of-the-art. A more direct comparison, potentially through replication or integration of key components from those systems, would strengthen the evaluation.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper presents performance numbers but lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific types of semantic relations, long-range dependencies) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
4. **Over-reliance on Pretrained Embeddings:**  The performance on the multilingual task, particularly for Chinese, is attributed to pre-trained embeddings. This raises questions about the model’s inherent ability to learn cross-lingual representations and its reliance on high-quality external resources.
5. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but provides limited details about the process. A more thorough discussion of the tuning strategy and its impact on performance would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors the model makes (e.g., incorrect predicate identification, wrong argument labeling, issues with specific semantic roles)?
2.  **Ablation Study on Stack LSTM Architecture:**  Have you experimented with different configurations of the Stack LSTMs (e.g., number of layers, hidden state size)? How sensitive is the performance to these parameters?
3. **Integration of External Resources:** Have you considered integrating any external knowledge sources (e.g., WordNet, FrameNet) into the model, even if it means moving away from the purely feature-learning approach?  Could such integration further improve performance?
4. **Decoding Strategy:**  While the paper focuses on greedy decoding, have you explored the potential benefits of using beam search or other decoding strategies? Would this trade-off speed for accuracy?
5. **Impact of Pseudo-Projectivization:** The paper mentions pseudo-projectivization.  How much does this preprocessing step impact the final performance? Have you explored approaches to handle non-projective dependencies directly?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*  The reference list is comprehensive.
*  The use of figures to illustrate the parsing process is helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic parsing.  The demonstration of strong performance without hand-crafted features is particularly noteworthy.  While the core methodology builds on existing work, the application of Stack LSTMs and the efficient implementation make this a worthwhile contribution.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  Addressing the questions posed to the authors would further strengthen the paper.
