## Review

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs and a greedy inference approach. The authors claim to outperform prior joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 & 2009, achieving comparable results to pipeline systems, while maintaining a fast runtime.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Demonstration of effective joint parsing without hand-crafted features:** The primary contribution lies in showing that strong performance in joint syntactic-semantic parsing can be achieved solely through learned representations, specifically via stack LSTMs, bypassing the need for costly, expertly designed features common in pipeline systems.
2.  **Empirical evaluation on standard datasets:** The thorough experimentation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets provides a valuable empirical baseline for future work in joint parsing, especially for greedy, representation-learning based approaches.
3.  **Fast, end-to-end system:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English is a positive aspect, potentially enabling practical applications where speed is critical.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges of traditional pipeline approaches and motivates the need for joint models, particularly emphasizing the feature engineering bottleneck.
2.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is generally well-defined, utilizing standard datasets and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of several variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) provides valuable insights into the contributions of syntactic information.
3.  **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance compared to other joint models and, notably, pipeline systems, despite avoiding hand-crafted features. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems, acknowledging their reliance on features and optimal decoding, is honest and balanced.
4.  **Detailed Technical Description:** The description of the transition system, stack LSTMs, and composition functions is sufficiently detailed, allowing for potential reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the *application* of stack LSTMs to joint parsing is a reasonable approach, the individual components – stack LSTMs, arc-eager parsing, and greedy inference – are not novel. The paper doesn't present a significant algorithmic or architectural innovation; it's more of an effective combination of existing techniques. The claim of bypassing "expensive features" is somewhat overstated, as learning representations *is* computationally expensive, just shifted from feature engineering to model training.
2.  **Overstated Claims & Lack of Granularity in Performance Analysis:** The statement that the model "surpasses" several other joint models feels slightly strong, as the improvements are not always substantial. A more granular analysis of *where* the model excels and fails would be beneficial. For instance, a detailed error analysis would provide insights into the types of dependencies the model struggles with.
3.  **Inconsistent/Missing Detail Regarding Implementation:** While the paper mentions publicly releasing the code, crucial details like hyperparameter tuning strategies are glossed over ("tuned on the dev data from the set of values...") without specifics. The lack of details on implementation choices (e.g., specific LSTM cell type, optimization algorithm beyond "stochastic gradient descent") hinders reproducibility.  The reference to GitHub for runtime comparisons is concerning; details should be included in the paper itself.
4.  **The Semantic Self-Dependency Transition Feels Ad-hoc:** The introduction of the `M-SELF(r)` transition feels like a patch to address a specific quirk of the CoNLL 2009 data.  While necessary, it lacks a strong theoretical justification and raises questions about generalization to other datasets. Why wasn’t this identified in prior work?
5.  **Minor Formatting/Presentation Issues:** The frequent use of symbols (e.g., `gs`, `gm`) without sufficient initial explanation can be confusing. Table 1 is excessively long and dense, making it difficult to quickly grasp the transition definitions. The text references "Figure 1" which is not included in the submitted text.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically for the LSTM dimensions, dropout rate, and action representation size?
2.  What is the computational cost of training the stack LSTMs compared to the cost of manually engineering the features used in the pipeline systems you compare against? A more quantitative comparison of feature engineering effort versus training cost would strengthen your argument.
3.  Can you provide an error analysis to identify the types of errors the model makes most frequently? Are there particular dependency types or syntactic structures that the model consistently struggles with?
4.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical motivation behind the `M-SELF(r)` transition? Is it a general phenomenon that could be relevant to other datasets, or is it specific to the CoNLL 2009 corpus?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visual diagram illustrating the flow of information through the stack LSTMs and the interaction between syntactic and semantic transitions.
*   Please include Figure 1 in the submission.
*   The paper would be improved by comparing the model's performance against strong baseline models that utilize simpler representation learning techniques (e.g., bag-of-words embeddings) to better isolate the benefit of the stack LSTMs.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a solid empirical evaluation of a reasonable approach to joint parsing, the lack of significant novelty and the minor methodological and presentation issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The results are positive, but the contributions, as I see them, are incremental. **I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.** The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, provide more detailed implementation details, and strengthen the claims of novelty.
