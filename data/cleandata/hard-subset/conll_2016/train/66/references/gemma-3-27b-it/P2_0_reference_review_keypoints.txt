- Paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using a synchronized transition-based algorithm with Stack LSTMs and greedy inference.  
- Main contribution: applying Stack LSTMs to learn whole-parser-state representations, reducing hand-crafted features.  
- Demonstrates competitive empirical performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and comparable accuracy to state-of-the-art while being much faster.  
- Reports strong runtime efficiency (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009), highlighting practical applicability.  
- Clear motivation: addresses cascading errors and expensive feature engineering in pipeline approaches.  
- Thorough comparison to prior joint models and strong pipeline baselines, consistently outperforming previous joint systems.  
- Comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets and multilingual settings, including ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid).  
- Authors plan to release open-source implementation and note practical limitations and future directions (e.g., morphological features, external data).  
- Paper is well written, organized, and includes helpful examples and transition system description.  
- Limited novelty in the core transition algorithm, which relies heavily on prior work (Henderson et al., 2013); main novelty is the Stack LSTM application.  
- Model still lags behind top-performing systems that use extensive feature engineering and optimal decoding; lacks deeper analysis of the performance gap.  
- Little analysis of what Stack LSTM representations capture; qualitative/interpretive analysis is missing.  
- Heavy reliance on pretrained embeddings for multilingual (notably Chinese) performance raises concerns for low-resource languages.  
- Questions for authors: analyze specific features causing the performance gap, visualize/interpret Stack LSTM representations, assess sensitivity to pretrained embeddings, and quantify oracle transition conversion impact.  
- Minor presentation suggestions: make equation notation more consistent and briefly explain pseudo-projectivization.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing listed weaknesses.
