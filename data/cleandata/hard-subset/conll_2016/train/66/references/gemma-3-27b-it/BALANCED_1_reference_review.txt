This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, greedy approach, leveraging Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state. The key innovation lies in representing the *entire* algorithmic state, rather than relying on hand-crafted features or only the top of the stack, and employing entirely greedy inference. The paper demonstrates strong performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English) shared tasks, comparable to or exceeding previous joint parsing models, and delivers competitive multilingual results on CoNLL 2009.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective State Representation with Stack LSTMs:** The successful application of Stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state – syntactic, semantic, and buffer – without relying on manually engineered features is a significant contribution. This allows the model to learn relevant representations automatically, a valuable approach, especially for greedy parsing where intuition-driven feature engineering is less effective.
2. **Strong Baseline for Greedy Joint Parsing:** The paper establishes a strong baseline for greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The performance achieved, particularly on CoNLL 2009, is noteworthy and highlights the potential of this approach. While not SOTA, it demonstrates a viable alternative to pipeline or optimal decoding methods.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to a wide range of existing methods (joint, pipeline, and state-of-the-art SRL systems) across multiple datasets (English, multilingual CoNLL 2009), provides a solid foundation for assessing the strengths and limitations of the proposed model. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of Stack LSTMs and a greedy, state-focused parsing approach is a novel contribution to the field of joint parsing. While Stack LSTMs have been used in syntactic parsing, applying them to *jointly* model syntax and semantics in this manner is a clear advance.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves competitive performance on benchmark datasets. While it doesn't surpass the best-performing systems that rely on extensive feature engineering and optimal decoding, the results are strong, particularly considering the simplicity and speed of the greedy inference.
3. **Efficiency:** The parser’s speed (177.6 ± 18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data) is a significant advantage, making it potentially useful in practical applications where real-time performance is crucial. This is explicitly highlighted in the paper, and is a strong selling point.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a detailed description of the model, experimental setup, and results. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm in the appendix (referenced in the text) is particularly helpful.
5. **Ablation studies:** The ablation study is thorough and clearly illustrates the impact of jointly learning syntax and semantics, and demonstrates the usefulness of each component.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gap to SOTA:** While competitive, the parser’s performance lags behind state-of-the-art systems that utilize more sophisticated features and decoding strategies. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the reasons for the gap and potential avenues for closing it.
2. **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The reliance on greedy decoding, while contributing to the parser’s speed, may limit its ability to capture long-range dependencies and make globally optimal decisions. This is hinted at in the discussion of the Hybrid model’s performance on the out-of-domain data.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The model relies on pretrained word embeddings, and the multilingual performance is noted to be affected by the quality of these embeddings. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different embedding sources would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors the parser makes and guide future improvements.  Knowing *where* the model struggles would be immensely helpful.
5. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, it lacks detail regarding the process. More specifics on the search space and criteria would strengthen the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of incorporating additional features, such as morphological features or character-based word embeddings, particularly for languages with rich morphology?
2. Have you experimented with different decoding strategies (e.g., beam search) and, if so, what was the impact on performance and efficiency?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the common types of errors made by the parser and their potential causes?
4. What was the specific search space and methodology used for hyperparameter tuning, and why were the chosen hyperparameters selected?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to Lei et al. (2015)’s runtime requires an update, as that paper’s runtime may have been reported under different conditions or with a different hardware setup.
* It would be beneficial to explicitly state the computational resources used for training and evaluation in the experimental setup section.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The proposed model is novel, competitive, and efficient. While the performance gap to state-of-the-art systems is a concern, the paper provides a strong foundation for future research. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically incorporating a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the greedy decoding approach and including a more detailed error analysis. The authors have provided a useful, well-documented implementation, and this will be valuable to the community.
