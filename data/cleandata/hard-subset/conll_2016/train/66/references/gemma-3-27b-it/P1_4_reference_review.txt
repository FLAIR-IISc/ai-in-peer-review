## Peer Review for CoNLL Paper: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure building approach. The key innovation lies in learning representations for the *entire* parser state using Stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features, and employing entirely greedy inference. The authors demonstrate strong performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English) shared tasks, comparable to (and in some cases surpassing) previous joint models and competitive with pipeline-based systems, while also boasting a significantly faster runtime.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Joint Parsing with Learned State Representations:** The paper convincingly shows that complex, hand-engineered features are *not* necessary for achieving strong performance in joint syntactic-semantic parsing. Utilizing Stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state provides a powerful and surprisingly effective alternative. This challenges the prevailing paradigm in high-performance SRL relying heavily on expert features.
2. **Competitive Performance with a Greedy Algorithm:** Achieving competitive results with a purely greedy inference algorithm is notable, as optimal decoding (like beam search) is often a critical component in state-of-the-art parsing systems. This emphasizes the strength of the learned representations and the efficiency of the approach.
3. **Fast and Practical System:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 is a significant advantage, positioning this system as a viable option for practical applications where speed is paramount.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents clear and compelling experimental results on standard datasets, demonstrably outperforming previous joint parsing models and remaining competitive with pipeline systems.  The consistent improvement over the baseline (Henderson et al., 2008) strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The motivation for avoiding hand-crafted features and exploring greedy inference is well articulated, grounding the work in the practical challenges of NLP system development and maintenance.  The paper clearly connects the method to existing work in incremental parsing.
3. **Thorough Ablation Study:** The inclusion of “Semantics-only”, “Syntax-only”, and “Hybrid” models provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information and the benefits of truly joint modeling. This shows a good understanding of the components and contributions.
4. **Practicality and Reproducibility:** The promise of an open-source implementation and the detailed description of the experimental setup enhance the practicality and reproducibility of the work. The speed comparison with another publicly available system is also a valuable addition.
5. **Multilingual Results:** Presenting results on multiple languages (CoNLL 2009) highlights the adaptability of the proposed model and increases the generalizability of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While competitive with older systems, the paper acknowledges that its performance does not match the *current* state-of-the-art SRL systems that leverage full syntactic parses, optimal decoding, and external datasets.  A more direct comparison, perhaps with results from a recent shared task, would strengthen the claims of contribution.  Simply stating they are "orthogonal" is insufficient; a discussion of *how* they could be integrated would be helpful.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific SRL argument types, complex syntactic structures) would provide valuable insights for future improvement and help to contextualize the results.
3. **Simplification of Predicate Sense Disambiguation:** The handling of out-of-vocabulary predicates by defaulting to “01” sense is a pragmatic solution, but potentially introduces bias and limits performance.  A more sophisticated approach to unseen predicate sense disambiguation would be beneficial.
4. **Overreliance on Existing Infrastructure:** The system builds heavily on the existing transition system of Henderson et al. (2013). While leveraging prior work is positive, it leaves open the question of whether the gains are due primarily to the Stack LSTMs or refinements of the underlying transition system.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison with *current* state-of-the-art SRL systems, quantifying the performance gap and outlining potential avenues for bridging it (e.g., integrating external knowledge, using more sophisticated decoding)?
2.  Can you provide some examples of failure cases, and categorize them to help understand the limitations of your model? Specifically, what types of semantic arguments or syntactic structures are most challenging for your system?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding types or initialization strategies?
4.  You mention that the arc-eager algorithm is used, however, oracle conversion isn’t perfect.  How much does this imperfect oracle affect the reported results?
5. The paper highlights the speed of inference. Is the training time competitive compared to similar approaches?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The presentation is clear and well-organized.
*   Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the joint parse structure.
*   The reference to the open-source implementation is crucial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic parsing. The successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn parser state representations, coupled with a fast greedy inference algorithm, demonstrates a promising direction for future research. While some weaknesses remain, the strengths of the work, particularly the strong empirical results and practical considerations, outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
