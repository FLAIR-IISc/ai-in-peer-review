This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s algorithmic state, eschewing expert-crafted features and utilizing greedy inference. The core contribution is demonstrating strong performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 tasks *without* relying on traditional, expensive features derived from full syntactic parses. A secondary contribution lies in the adaptation of the transition-based framework, including the addition of a new transition to handle self-dependencies. Finally, the paper offers a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation, including comparisons to both joint and pipeline systems, and presents results on multiple languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive, and in some cases state-of-the-art, results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, particularly given the absence of hand-crafted features. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the stack LSTM approach for capturing relevant information during parsing. The comparison against a range of existing systems (both joint and pipeline) provides a valuable benchmark.
2. **Novel Application of Stack LSTMs:**  Applying stack LSTMs to the *entire* parser state, rather than just top-of-stack elements, is a clever innovation. This allows the model to capture broader contextual information, which is crucial for accurate joint parsing. The authors correctly identify this as a key differentiator from prior work.
3. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data is a significant advantage, particularly when contrasted with slower pipeline approaches. This makes the parser practical for real-world applications where speed is critical. The authors rightly emphasize the value of this aspect.
4. **Clear and Thorough Explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, the transition system, and the model architecture. The inclusion of Table 1 detailing the transitions is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Parsing Algorithm:** While the application of stack LSTMs is novel, the underlying transition-based parsing algorithm itself is heavily based on Henderson et al. (2013). The paper doesn't significantly advance the algorithmic framework beyond the original work; the main contribution is the *representation learning* component. This is acknowledged to some extent, but could be more clearly articulated.
2. **Performance Gap with SOTA:** The parser, despite its strong showing, does not surpass the very best performing systems which *do* utilize expert-crafted features and/or optimal decoding. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a limitation. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of why this performance gap exists and potential avenues for closing it (beyond simply integrating the features they explicitly mention).  Is the greedy inference the primary bottleneck, or is there something about the LSTM representation that limits its capacity?
3. **Ablation Study Could Be More Detailed:** The ablation study (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides some insight, but feels somewhat superficial. Specifically, the performance difference between Joint and Hybrid on the out-of-domain data (Brown corpus) is interesting but not fully explained. More detailed analysis of the error patterns in each configuration would be helpful. What specific types of errors are more common in the Semantics-only model?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis beyond the high-level observations in the ablation study. Understanding the types of errors the model makes would provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the greedy inference approach and how it might contribute to the performance gap observed compared to systems with optimal decoding?
2. The paper mentions that the “oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect.” How significant is this issue, and how might it affect the evaluation results? Could a more accurate oracle conversion process potentially improve performance?
3. What specific types of errors are most frequently made by the Semantics-only model?  Is it struggling with ambiguity, long-distance dependencies, or something else?
4. You mention exploring character-based word embeddings for morphologically rich languages. Have you conducted any preliminary experiments in this direction, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The discussion of related work is good, but could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural dependency parsing.
*  The paper would benefit from a visualization of the stack LSTM architecture and how it interacts with the transition system.
* The reference to Lei et al.’s runtime is helpful, but clarifying the hardware used for both systems would make the comparison more precise.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution. The authors successfully demonstrate the effectiveness of stack LSTMs for joint parsing without relying on hand-crafted features. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more detailed analysis of the performance gap and error patterns, would significantly strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the questions and suggestions above.
