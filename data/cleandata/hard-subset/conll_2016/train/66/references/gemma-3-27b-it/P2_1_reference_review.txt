## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that leverages stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's algorithmic state, moving away from hand-crafted features. The parser employs a greedy, incremental approach based on the work of Henderson et al. (2008), but demonstrates improved performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, and comparable multilingual performance on CoNLL 2009. A key claimed benefit is faster runtime compared to pipeline approaches and state-of-the-art systems relying on more complex decoding or feature engineering.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective joint parsing without extensive feature engineering:** The paper successfully shows that a strong joint parsing system can be built using purely learned representations and a greedy decoding approach, challenging the prevailing reliance on manually designed features and complex decoding schemes.
2. **Empirical Results on Standard Benchmarks:** The parser achieves competitive results on established datasets (CoNLL 2008/2009), providing a solid empirical foundation for the proposed approach and offering a valuable baseline for future research.
3. **Fast Runtime:** The reported runtime is significantly faster than several strong baselines, highlighting the practical benefits of the proposed greedy approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional pipeline approaches and motivates the use of representation learning to overcome the challenges of feature engineering in joint parsing.  The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008) is well-defined, and the differences are explicitly stated.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:**  The parser demonstrably outperforms previous joint models on the CoNLL datasets, indicating a meaningful improvement over the state-of-the-art in that specific area. While acknowledging it doesn’t match *all* state-of-the-art systems (those leveraging more resources or pipelines), the results are competitive and compelling, especially considering the simplicity of the approach.
3. **Practical Significance: Speed:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 is a significant advantage, making the system potentially useful in real-world applications where speed is critical. This is a strong selling point and separates it from many higher-performing but computationally expensive alternatives.
4. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of experiments with "Semantics-only," "Syntax-only," and "Hybrid" models provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing, demonstrating the benefits of the joint approach and helping understand the contribution of each component.
5. **Open-Source Commitment:**  The promise of an open-source release is commendable and will facilitate further research and reproducibility.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Technique:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing is a reasonable approach, the core technique itself (Stack LSTMs) is not novel. The primary contribution lies in the successful *application* of existing techniques to this specific problem, rather than the development of fundamentally new algorithms.  The paper needs to more clearly articulate what *specifically* about the combination and configuration of these techniques is innovative.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The parser relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings, and the results on multilingual data suggest performance is strongly tied to the availability of good pretrained embeddings for each language.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of different embedding sources and the potential for learning embeddings from scratch.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the parser struggles (e.g., specific types of semantic relations, long-distance dependencies) would provide valuable insights for future improvement. What kinds of errors does it make that the strong baseline systems avoid?
4. **Handling of Non-Projectivity & Oracle Errors:** The paper acknowledges issues with the non-projectivity conversion and oracle errors, yet doesn’t fully explore their impact.  A more quantitative assessment of these errors would be beneficial. It seems odd to rely on pseudo-projectivization when the underlying transition system should be capable of handling non-projective trees.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the performance differences between your system and the top-performing pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009) on specific error types?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality and source of the pretrained word embeddings, especially for low-resource languages?
3.  Given that the arc-eager algorithm *can* handle non-projective trees, why is pseudo-projectivization used, and what is the impact of this step on performance?
4. Can you elaborate on the choices made for the hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden state dimension, dropout rate)? Was there any systematic hyperparameter optimization performed?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural SRL, particularly those leveraging transformers or graph neural networks.
* The paper could benefit from a clearer visual representation of the joint parsing process, perhaps with a more detailed example transition sequence.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. While the core techniques are not entirely novel, the successful application of these techniques to achieve competitive performance and fast runtime is significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should focus on providing a more nuanced discussion of the novelty of their approach, performing a more detailed error analysis, and elaborating on the impact of key design choices.
