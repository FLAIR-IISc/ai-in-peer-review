## Peer Review for CoNLL Paper: "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that leverages stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. The model employs a greedy, incremental, transition-based approach and demonstrates strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline systems. The authors emphasize the speed of their system and plan to release an open-source implementation.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The successful adaptation of stack LSTMs to *jointly* model syntactic and semantic dependencies is the most significant contribution. This demonstrates the power of representation learning to obviate the need for extensive feature engineering in a complex parsing task.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL shared tasks, establishing the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparative analysis against various systems is thorough and insightful.
3. **Fast and Practical System:** The reported runtime (177.6s for CoNLL 2009) is a notable advantage, particularly when contrasted with slower, feature-rich pipeline systems, making it a viable option for practical applications.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Feature Representation:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in joint parsing – the reliance on costly, expertly-designed features.  By employing stack LSTMs to learn state representations, the authors provide a compelling alternative that simplifies the development process and potentially improves generalization. This is particularly important for greedy models where feature design is less guided by linguistic theory.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering both the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. The inclusion of experiments on a semantics-only and syntax-only variant, as well as a hybrid approach, provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information. The comparisons to a diverse range of systems (joint, pipeline, and state-of-the-art SRL) strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow.  The detailed description of the transition system and the stack LSTM architecture is particularly helpful. The inclusion of example run in the supplementary material is a great benefit.
4. **Practical Impact:** The reported speed and the promise of an open-source release increase the practical impact of the research.  A fast, accurate, and readily available joint parser will be a valuable resource for the NLP community.
5. **Demonstrates Scalability:** The multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 indicate the potential for adapting the model to different languages with minimal modifications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding the types of errors the model makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific semantic relations that are consistently mispredicted? What syntactic structures pose the biggest challenge?
2. **Reliance on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The model’s performance is dependent on pre-trained word embeddings. While this is a common practice, the paper doesn’t fully explore the impact of different embedding strategies or the potential for learning embeddings jointly with the parsing model.
3. **Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary Words:** The strategy for handling out-of-vocabulary words (random initialization) feels simplistic. It's unclear how this impacts performance, especially on languages with rich morphology or limited pre-trained resources.  More sophisticated approaches (e.g., character-level embeddings) could be considered.
4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art is Caveated:** The authors acknowledge that their system doesn’t match the performance of the very best systems, which utilize expert-crafted features and optimal decoding. While this is true, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy, speed, and complexity would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns in both syntactic and semantic parsing?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding strategies or considered learning embeddings jointly with the parser?
3.  What strategies did you consider for handling out-of-vocabulary words beyond random initialization, and why did you choose the approach implemented in the final system?
4.   The model relies on the arc-eager algorithm for syntactic parsing. Have you investigated other syntactic parsing algorithms and their potential impact on joint parsing performance?
5.  You mention that the model can benefit from integrating advancements in other systems. Could you be more specific about which advances you consider most promising for future work?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Figure 1" appears in the introduction but there is no Figure 1 in the text provided.
*   The discussion of M-SELF transition is somewhat unclear and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.  The proposed model is innovative, effective, and practical. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the questions and comments raised.
