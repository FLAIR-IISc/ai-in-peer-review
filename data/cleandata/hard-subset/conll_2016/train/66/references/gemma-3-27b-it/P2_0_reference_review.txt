## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, synchronized algorithm. The core contribution lies in replacing traditional, hand-crafted features with representations learned by Stack LSTMs applied to the entire parser state, and employing a purely greedy inference approach. The authors demonstrate that their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English data and achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art systems, while being significantly faster.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state in a joint syntactic-semantic setting is the strongest contribution. It demonstrates the power of representation learning to reduce reliance on expert-crafted features in complex NLP tasks.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving competitive performance with a purely greedy, feature-free model on standard benchmarks is a significant accomplishment. This suggests the approach is robust and scalable.
3. **Speed and Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 is a noteworthy advantage, potentially making this model suitable for real-world applications where speed is critical.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for the work – overcoming the limitations of cascading errors and expensive feature engineering in traditional pipeline approaches. The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008, and subsequent developments) is well established.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors meticulously compare their model to a broad range of previous joint parsing models and strong pipeline baselines.  The consistent outperformance of previous joint models highlights the improvement achieved by the proposed approach. The inclusion of speed comparisons against another published system (Lei et al., 2015) is particularly valuable.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:**  The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, multilingual CoNLL 2009) and demonstrating the model's adaptability. The ablation study (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) offers insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing.
4. **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge limitations and point to avenues for future improvement, such as incorporating morphological features or leveraging external datasets. The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation further enhances the paper's impact.
5. **Well-written and Structured:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow.  The inclusion of examples (Figure 1 & 2) and a description of the transition system (Table 1) are helpful for understanding the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** While the application of Stack LSTMs is novel and effective, the underlying transition-based parsing algorithm itself relies heavily on Henderson et al. (2013). The core algorithmic contribution is the application of LSTMs, rather than a novel parsing paradigm.
2. **Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:**  While competitive, the model still falls short of the very best performing systems on CoNLL 2009, which leverage extensive feature engineering and/or optimal decoding.  The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper analysis of *why* this gap exists (beyond simply stating feature engineering is the difference) would be beneficial.  What specific features are most crucial for achieving top performance?
3. **Limited Analysis of Stack LSTM Representations:** The paper demonstrates that Stack LSTMs *work* well, but offers little insight into *what* the LSTMs are learning. A qualitative analysis of the learned representations or an investigation of their sensitivity to different input features could provide valuable understanding and motivate future research.
4. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on multilingual data, especially Chinese, relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings. This raises questions about the model's ability to generalize to languages with limited resources. A more thorough analysis of the impact of pretrained embeddings would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance gap between your model and the state-of-the-art systems, identifying specific features or techniques that contribute most to the performance difference?
2.  Have you explored techniques for visualizing or interpreting the Stack LSTM representations? If so, what insights have you gained about what the model is learning?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and availability of pretrained word embeddings, particularly for low-resource languages?
4.  The paper mentions limitations regarding the oracle transition conversion. Can you quantify the impact of this on the evaluation results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used in the equations (e.g., gs, gm, gd) could be made more consistent and descriptive.
*   The reference to “pseudo-projectivization” could benefit from a brief explanation of its purpose and potential impact on the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.  The effective application of Stack LSTMs to learn representations for the entire parser state is a compelling innovation, and the experimental results are convincing. While the core algorithm builds upon existing work, the substantial performance gains and speed advantages make this a valuable contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
