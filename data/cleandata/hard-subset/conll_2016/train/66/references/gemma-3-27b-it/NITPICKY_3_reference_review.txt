The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state, eschewing expert-crafted features and employing a greedy inference approach. The primary claimed contribution is achieving competitive performance with pipeline systems on CoNLL shared tasks *without* relying on manually engineered features or optimal decoding. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs for jointly modeling syntactic and semantic structure in a greedy parsing framework. A tertiary claim is the speed of the implemented system.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty & Justification of Design Choices:** While the use of stack LSTMs is mentioned as a key component, its application to joint syntactic-semantic parsing within a *greedy* framework, particularly *without* expert features, feels more like a natural extension of existing work (Dyer et al., 2015, Henderson et al.) than a groundbreaking advance. The authors don’t convincingly demonstrate *why* a greedy approach with learned representations is superior, or even competitive, to the established pipeline approaches that leverage full parses. The justification for entirely abandoning expert features is weak; the paper acknowledges these features are powerful but dismisses their computational cost without a rigorous cost-benefit analysis in the context of modern hardware.

2. **Evaluation Rigor & Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to state-of-the-art systems is superficial.  The authors acknowledge falling short of systems using expert-crafted features and optimal decoding, but the dismissal as "orthogonal" feels dismissive. A more detailed breakdown of *where* the model falters compared to these systems (specific error types, dependency lengths, etc.) is needed.  The comparison to Lei et al. (2015) is limited to runtime; performance differences aren't adequately contextualized. Furthermore, the provided runtime is for the English CoNLL 2009 dataset – runtime for other languages is not reported, making cross-lingual comparisons problematic.

3. **Implementation & Hyperparameter Details:** The level of detail regarding hyperparameter tuning is insufficient. Phrases like "tuned on the dev data from the set of values {0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 1.0}" are vague and don't reveal the optimization process. The "intuitive" setting of other hyperparameters is concerning. Reproducibility is hampered by the lack of precise details. The mention of a 1.5% error rate in the transition sequence conversion due to non-projectivity, and the resulting impact on evaluation, is glossed over.  This requires a more thorough discussion of its effect on results.

4. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper contains several instances of awkward phrasing and redundancy. For example, the repeated emphasis on "no expert-crafted features" becomes repetitive. Table 1 is overly dense and difficult to parse. The supplementary material is referenced but is not part of this review process, making verification of claims regarding example run sequences impossible.

5. **Limited Analysis of Semantic Transitions:** The introduction of the `M-SELF` transition to handle self-dependencies is a positive addition, but there's a lack of analysis on its impact. How does the inclusion of this transition affect overall performance? Are self-dependencies handled better than in prior systems?



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for joint parsing – avoiding error propagation in pipelines. The argument for reducing reliance on expensive feature engineering is reasonable.

2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of standard CoNLL datasets and evaluation metrics provides a well-defined testing ground and allows for comparison with existing work.

3. **Competitive Performance:**  The model achieves competitive, if not state-of-the-art, performance on the CoNLL datasets. The performance on multilingual data is a positive aspect.

4. **Open-Source Commitment:** The promise of an open-source implementation is valuable for the research community.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis comparing your system to state-of-the-art pipeline systems? Specifically, what types of errors does your system make more frequently?
2.  What was the exact procedure for hyperparameter tuning? Please provide more specific details on the search space and optimization strategy for all hyperparameters.
3.  How significantly does the 1.5% error rate in the transition sequence conversion (due to non-projectivity) affect the reported results? Can you quantify this impact?
4.  What is the runtime of the system on the non-English CoNLL 2009 datasets?
5.  What is the rationale behind choosing a greedy decoding approach over beam search, given that beam search is commonly used and often improves performance?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review, particularly regarding recent advancements in neural SRL models.
* The supplementary material should be essential to the review process to adequately assess claims.
* The writing style could be improved for clarity and conciseness.



**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the work presents a reasonable approach and achieves competitive results, the incremental novelty, insufficient detail regarding implementation and evaluation, and lack of rigorous analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above to strengthen the contribution and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of their proposed system.
