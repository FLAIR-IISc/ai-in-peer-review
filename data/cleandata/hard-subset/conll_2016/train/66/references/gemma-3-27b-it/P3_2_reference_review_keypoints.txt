- Paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using Stack LSTMs to learn entire parser-state representations, avoiding hand-crafted features.  
- Uses a greedy, transition-based inference strategy and reports fast runtime and strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009.

- Contribution: shows Stack LSTMs can effectively replace hand-engineered features for joint parsing state representation.  
- Contribution: achieves competitive empirical performance on CoNLL datasets while being significantly faster than prior joint models.  
- Contribution: demonstrates that greedy parsing with learned state representations yields an efficient joint parser.

- Strength: clear motivation for joint modeling and moving away from feature engineering.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation against strong baselines, including multilingual CoNLL 2009 results.  
- Strength: impressive parsing speed (177.6s on CoNLL 2009 test) demonstrating practical efficiency.  
- Strength: technically sound presentation with a helpful architecture diagram.  
- Strength: authors commit to open-source release for reproducibility.

- Weakness: limited novelty in core transition algorithm—main novelty is state representation learning rather than a new parsing framework.  
- Weakness: comparisons to absolute SOTA are not fully comprehensive; need clearer analysis of the performance gap and its causes.  
- Weakness: ablation studies not granular enough (e.g., impact of LSTM size or simpler representations).  
- Weakness: simplistic handling of unseen predicates (defaulting to sense "01") without exploring richer alternatives.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed error analysis to identify common failure modes.

- Question: provide quantitative evidence that representing the entire state is superior to using only top stack items.  
- Question: explain why performance lags behind top SOTA systems using full parses or global reranking and how to bridge the gap.  
- Question: have alternative methods (e.g., character-level embeddings) been tried for unseen predicate senses?  
- Question: give concrete error examples where greedy inference causes suboptimal parses.

- Additional comment: writing is generally clear but some sections (e.g., 3.3) could be simplified.  
- Additional comment: Figure 1 is useful but should be larger and better labeled.

- Overall: strong, practically useful paper showing learned state representations enable fast, competitive joint parsing; merits acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).
