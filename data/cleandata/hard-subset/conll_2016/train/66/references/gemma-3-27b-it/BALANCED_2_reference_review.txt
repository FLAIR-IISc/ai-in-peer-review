This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based approach and leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline systems.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The core contribution lies in successfully adapting stack LSTMs to model the state of a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser *without* relying on hand-crafted features. This demonstrates the power of representation learning in capturing necessary information for accurate parsing.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art results among joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, and remains competitive with pipeline approaches, despite its simplicity and speed.
3. **Efficient Greedy Inference:** The use of greedy inference makes the parser significantly faster than many competitive systems, opening possibilities for practical applications where speed is critical.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Representation Learning:**  The paper effectively demonstrates that a learned, holistic representation of the parser state (using Stack LSTMs) can obviate the need for extensive feature engineering, a significant advance in the field. This addresses a long-standing challenge in parsing, particularly for greedy approaches.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, CoNLL 2009 multilingual) and compare their system against a diverse set of baselines, including previous joint models and pipeline systems.  The ablation study (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed model and the experimental setup.  The inclusion of a transition example and illustrations (Figures 1 & 3) greatly enhance understanding.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The fast runtime of the parser (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009) makes it attractive for real-world applications where low latency is important. The promise of an open-source implementation further enhances its accessibility and impact.
5. **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that their system does not match the performance of state-of-the-art systems relying on expert-crafted features and optimal decoding, framing their work as a stepping stone towards further improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper presents comprehensive results, a more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the parser would be valuable. Understanding *where* the system struggles could provide direction for future work.  For example, are there specific syntactic structures or semantic relations that consistently cause errors?
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance of the multilingual model is highlighted as being reliant on pretrained word embeddings, particularly for Chinese. This suggests a potential limitation in the model's ability to generalize to languages with limited resources or different embedding characteristics.  A discussion of how the model might be adapted to address this limitation would be beneficial.
3. **Greedy Inference Limitations:** While the greedy inference is a strength in terms of speed, it also introduces the possibility of suboptimal solutions.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more quantitative assessment of the impact of greedy inference on performance would be useful.  Exploring methods to mitigate the limitations of greedy inference (e.g., limited beam search) could be a valuable direction for future research.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural SRL Models:** The paper primarily compares to systems from 2009-2015.  A comparison to more recent, fully neural SRL models (e.g., those based on transformers) would provide a more current benchmark for evaluating the model’s performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the parser? Specifically, are there particular syntactic structures or semantic relations where the model consistently underperforms?
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality and characteristics of the pretrained word embeddings, especially for low-resource languages?
3. Have you experimented with any techniques to mitigate the limitations of greedy inference, such as limited beam search or re-ranking? If so, what were the results?
4. How would the performance of your system compare to recent state-of-the-art neural SRL models (e.g., transformer-based models)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in Equation 6 is slightly unclear. Consider clarifying the meaning of the summation and the role of the terms within the exponent.
*   The reference to "pseudo-projectivization" could benefit from a brief explanation of its purpose and impact on the results.
*   It would be helpful to indicate the computational resources used for training and evaluation (e.g., GPU type, memory).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The authors effectively demonstrate the power of representation learning and present a practical, efficient, and competitive parsing system. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments outlined above.
