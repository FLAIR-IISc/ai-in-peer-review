## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:** This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure building approach. The key innovation lies in using Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the *entire* parser state (stacks and buffer) without relying on hand-crafted features, coupled with a fully greedy inference procedure. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable results to pipeline systems, while also boasting a significantly faster runtime.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:**  The paper successfully demonstrates the power of Stack LSTMs in a joint parsing setting. This is a significant application of recent advances in representation learning, showing their ability to obviate the need for complex feature engineering in a traditionally feature-heavy task.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on established benchmarks (CoNLL 2008 & 2009) for joint parsing, positioning it as a viable alternative to pipeline approaches.
3. **Fast and Practical System:** The reported runtime is notably faster than existing strong systems, making it a potentially useful tool for real-world applications.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of Stack LSTMs for full state representation and greedy inference represents a distinct departure from previous joint parsing models.  While Stack LSTMs have been used for syntactic parsing, applying them effectively to a *joint* task, and demonstrating the elimination of hand-crafted features, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 are compelling.  The parser consistently outperforms previous joint models.  The comparison to pipeline systems, highlighting speed advantages, is particularly convincing. The multilingual results, even if comparable, demonstrate some degree of generalizability.
3. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the technical details of the approach. The inclusion of an example transition sequence (mentioned in §A) is helpful for understanding the algorithm.  The justification for design choices (e.g., copying buffer elements vs. moving them) is well-articulated.
4. **Practical Significance:** The fast runtime of the parser, combined with its competitive performance, suggests it has genuine practical value. The promise of an open-source release further enhances this.
5. **Thorough Ablation Study:** The experimentation with "Syntax-only", "Semantics-only", and "Hybrid" models provides valuable insight into the contribution of each component and demonstrates the benefits of joint modeling.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strongest Baselines:** While the paper compares to several relevant systems, it acknowledges that its performance doesn’t match the very best systems relying on optimal decoding, expert-crafted features, or external data. The authors frame these as “orthogonal” advancements, but a more detailed discussion of *how* these could be integrated into their framework would be beneficial.  Simply stating they expect future work to combine them feels insufficient.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific dependency types, long-distance dependencies, ambiguous cases) would be valuable for guiding future improvements. This is especially important given the lack of traditional feature engineering – knowing what information is *missing* from the learned representations is crucial.
3. **Greedy Inference Limitation:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of greedy inference. While it contributes to speed, it potentially sacrifices accuracy.  A discussion of the trade-off between speed and accuracy, and perhaps some preliminary exploration of limited-beam search as a possible compromise, would strengthen the paper.  The impact of greedy search on performance, in terms of lost accuracy, isn't quantified.
4. **Dependency on Pretrained Embeddings:** The multilingual results highlight the importance of pretrained embeddings (especially for Chinese). This suggests the model may not generalize well to languages without readily available high-quality embeddings. A discussion of this limitation would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of how advancements like optimal decoding, richer feature sets, and external datasets could be incorporated into your framework? What are the primary challenges in combining these with the Stack LSTM-based approach?
2.  Can you provide some examples of error cases where the model consistently fails, and discuss what information might be helpful to improve performance in those scenarios?
3.  Have you experimented with any limited-beam search strategies? If so, what was the impact on performance and runtime? If not, do you have any insights into the potential trade-offs between speed and accuracy with a beam search approach?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality and availability of pretrained word embeddings, particularly for low-resource languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The mention of code availability is a significant positive.
*   The references are comprehensive and demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.
* The supplementary material should be thoroughly examined, especially the example transition sequence and details on hyperparameter tuning.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and effective approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The combination of Stack LSTMs and greedy inference is a compelling contribution, and the empirical results are impressive. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding comparison to the absolute best-performing systems and lack of error analysis, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above.
