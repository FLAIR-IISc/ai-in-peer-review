## Review

This paper presents a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, eschewing hand-crafted features in favor of learning representations of the parser’s entire state using Stack LSTMs and employing a fully greedy inference procedure. The primary contribution is the demonstration that strong joint parsing performance *can* be achieved without relying on traditional, expensive, and often linguistically motivated features typically extracted from complete syntactic parses. This is a bold move, and the reported results—outperforming prior joint models and achieving competitive performance against pipelined state-of-the-art systems—are genuinely impressive, given the simplicity of the approach. A secondary, but still valuable, contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, and multilingual CoNLL 2009), demonstrating the model’s generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Shift:** The core idea of replacing expert-crafted features with learned state representations, combined with a fully greedy approach, represents a significant departure from the dominant paradigm in joint parsing. This is a conceptually clean and potentially highly impactful approach, especially considering the increasing focus on end-to-end neural models. The authors correctly identify a key bottleneck in pipelined systems – feature engineering – and offer a compelling alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported performance gains over previous joint models, including those from Henderson et al., are substantial. Achieving competitive results against strong pipelined systems *without* those carefully engineered features is a strong validation of the approach. The multilingual results, while not a primary focus, further bolster the claim of generalizability.
3. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 English test data is significantly faster than many state-of-the-art pipelined systems. This speed advantage could be crucial for practical applications where real-time performance is essential.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The description of the transition system and the Stack LSTM architecture are easy to follow, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors compare their results to other systems, a deeper analysis of the types of errors made by their model is lacking. Understanding *where* the model struggles would be valuable for guiding future research. Are there specific linguistic phenomena or edge cases where the greedy approach falters?
2. **Ablation Study Scope:**  The ablation study (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) is helpful, but could be expanded.  It would be insightful to investigate the impact of different Stack LSTM configurations (e.g., hidden layer size, number of layers) on performance. Also, the impact of pre-trained embeddings vs. learned embeddings isn't thoroughly investigated.
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The results on the multilingual data highlight a dependence on good pre-trained embeddings, particularly for Chinese. This raises concerns about the portability of the model to low-resource languages where high-quality pre-trained embeddings may not be available.  The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a significant limitation.
4. **Greedy Inference Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of greedy inference. While computationally efficient, greedy search isn't guaranteed to find the optimal parse. A discussion of the potential trade-offs between speed and accuracy, and perhaps an exploration of limited-horizon search strategies, would strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error types and potential causes?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality and quantity of pre-trained embeddings, particularly for languages other than English?
3. Have you explored any strategies to mitigate the limitations of greedy inference, such as limited-horizon search or beam search with a small beam size? If so, what were the results?
4. Can you clarify the specific advantage of copying elements in the buffer during syntactic transitions rather than moving them, and how this impacts the performance of semantic operations?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to "structured skipgram" embeddings is helpful for reproducibility. The authors should ensure that their code and data are readily available upon publication to facilitate further research. A brief discussion of the computational cost of training the Stack LSTMs would also be useful.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The authors have successfully demonstrated that competitive joint parsing performance can be achieved without relying on traditional, hand-crafted features. The clear writing, thorough evaluation, and efficiency of the proposed method are all commendable. I recommend **acceptance**. The boldness of the approach and the promise of a simpler, more efficient paradigm for joint parsing outweigh the limitations.
