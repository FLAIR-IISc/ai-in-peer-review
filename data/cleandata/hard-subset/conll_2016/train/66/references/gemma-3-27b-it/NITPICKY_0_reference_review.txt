## Review

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parsing state, eschewing expert-crafted features and employing a greedy inference approach. The authors claim to outperform prior joint models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009, achieving competitive results with pipeline systems, albeit at a speed advantage.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  A demonstration of the viability of a fully representation-learning-based approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing, eliminating the need for hand-engineered features.
2.  A practical, fast implementation of a joint parser, offering a performance trade-off appealing for real-time applications.
3.  A thorough experimental evaluation on standard CoNLL datasets, establishing a strong baseline for future work in this area.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elimination of Feature Engineering:** The most compelling aspect of this work is its success in achieving competitive performance *without* relying on the extensive feature engineering typically required in joint parsing models. This simplifies development and potentially improves generalization. The authors correctly identify the cost associated with complex features like paths between predicate and argument.
2.  **Speed Advantage:** The greedy inference approach and the efficient implementation result in a significantly faster parsing speed compared to state-of-the-art systems reliant on optimal decoding or reranking. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English is a significant benefit for practical applications.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, multilingual CoNLL 2009), with clear comparisons to existing systems. The inclusion of ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides valuable insight into the contributions of different components.
4.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ approach. The inclusion of Figure 1 illustrating an example parse is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental but Not Truly End-to-End:** While the model avoids *explicit* feature engineering, the reliance on pretrained word embeddings (and POS tags) represents a form of pre-existing knowledge injection. This is not inherently problematic, but it should be acknowledged as a dependency, not a completely clean slate. The authors mention this but downplay its significance.
2.  **Suboptimal Performance Compared to SOTA:** The system, while competitive, does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on any of the datasets. The authors acknowledge this and attribute it to differences in features and decoding strategies. However, the performance gap is not insignificant, and it raises questions about the limitations of the chosen approach. The differences are dismissed as ‘orthogonal’ advancements, but this feels like a convenient explanation.
3.  **Ambiguous Transition Definitions & Implementation Details:** Table 1 is difficult to parse. The notation used to represent dependencies (e.g., `gs(u,v, l)`) is unclear without constant reference to the surrounding text. The description of the buffer modification in the semantic transitions is confusing ("copies it to the top of S"). Further, the authors state they *copy* items to the buffer but then also state they *move* items. This inconsistency is concerning. The implementation details in section 4.1, while detailed, are somewhat disorganized. The justification for specific hyperparameter choices is lacking.
4.  **Constraints on Transitions - Potential for Improvement:** The constraints imposed on transitions, while necessary to ensure valid parses, might be overly restrictive, potentially limiting the model’s ability to explore more complex parse structures. The reliance on pseudo-projectivization adds complexity and introduces a potential source of error.  The authors note a small percentage of cases where the oracle transition sequence is incorrect due to this.
5.  **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the model makes would be crucial for guiding future improvements. Are there specific linguistic phenomena that the model struggles with? A qualitative examination of failure cases is missing.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the choice of hyperparameters, particularly those that were not tuned on the development set?
2.  Can you elaborate on the impact of the transition constraints on the model’s performance? Have you explored alternative constraint strategies?
3.  What specific types of errors does the model make most frequently? A qualitative error analysis would be highly valuable.
4.  The description of buffer handling in the semantic transitions is unclear. Please clarify exactly how items are moved and copied between the buffer and stacks, providing a concrete example if possible.
5.  While you acknowledge the use of pretrained embeddings, how would the performance change if the model was trained from scratch with randomly initialized embeddings?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper frequently references previous work (Henderson et al., 2008, 2013) which seems somewhat self-referential. While building on prior work is expected, a more balanced discussion of related literature would be beneficial.
*   The formatting of mathematical equations is inconsistent.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its strengths, this paper suffers from a number of minor flaws—ambiguous explanations, lack of detailed error analysis, and suboptimal performance. While the elimination of feature engineering and the speed advantage are commendable, the paper does not present a sufficiently compelling case for a significant advance in the field. **I lean towards rejection.** A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, and including a more in-depth error analysis, would be necessary for reconsideration.
