## Review of “A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser”

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental approach. The key innovation lies in employing Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the *entire* parser state, eschewing traditional, hand-crafted features. The system utilizes a greedy inference strategy and demonstrates strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable results to pipeline-based state-of-the-art systems, albeit with a significant speed advantage.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective State Representation Learning for Joint Parsing:** The paper convincingly shows that Stack LSTMs can effectively capture the relevant information from the parser's state for joint syntactic and semantic parsing, eliminating the need for carefully engineered features. This is a valuable contribution as feature engineering is often a bottleneck in NLP.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving competitive performance on the established CoNLL datasets, while significantly faster than existing systems, validates the approach and its practical utility.
3. **Efficient Greedy Parsing:** The use of a greedy inference strategy, coupled with the learned state representation, results in a highly efficient parser, addressing a common drawback of joint models – computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of pipeline-based systems and the potential benefits of joint modeling. The motivation for moving away from hand-crafted features and towards learned representations is well-explained.
2. **Strong Baselines and Thorough Evaluation:** The comparison against a comprehensive set of baselines, including previous joint models and state-of-the-art pipeline systems, provides a strong validation of the proposed approach. The inclusion of multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 further strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Speed and Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for parsing the CoNLL 2009 test data is impressive, highlighting the practical advantages of the greedy inference and efficient state representation. This is a crucial benefit, especially for real-world applications.
4. **Technical Soundness:** The explanation of the transition system and the use of Stack LSTMs are clear and well-presented. The architecture diagram (Figure 3) is helpful in understanding the model’s components.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing an open-source implementation fosters reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithms:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing is valuable, the underlying transition system is largely based on existing work (Henderson et al., 2013). The core algorithmic innovation is arguably the state representation learning, not the parsing framework itself. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Comparison to SOTA is not Fully Comprehensive:** The paper acknowledges that performance doesn’t match the *very best* systems, but downplays this somewhat. A more detailed comparison to the strongest SOTA models (e.g., those leveraging global reranking and extra datasets) and discussion of *why* the gap exists would be beneficial. Specifically, it's unclear how much the gap is due to feature engineering differences vs. the greedy decoding vs. other factors.
3. **Ablation Study Could Be More Granular:** While ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are presented, a more fine-grained analysis of the Stack LSTM’s contribution would be helpful. For example, how does performance degrade with smaller LSTM hidden state dimensions or with simpler representation methods? This would help to understand the capacity and importance of the learned representations.
4. **Handling of Unknown Predicates is Simplistic:** The strategy for handling unseen predicates (defaulting to sense "01") is a reasonable fallback, but it’s a rather simplistic approach. The impact of this strategy on overall performance, especially for languages with richer lexical ambiguity, should be discussed.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would provide valuable insights into the model’s limitations and potential areas for improvement. Identifying common error patterns (e.g., specific semantic argument types that are frequently mislabeled) would be highly informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You state the core innovation is learning representations of the entire parser state. Could you provide more quantitative evidence (e.g., ablation studies) demonstrating the benefit of representing the *entire* state versus, say, just the top items on the stack?
2.  The paper compares against a wide range of baselines, but doesn't thoroughly address *why* the system doesn't reach the performance of the absolute state-of-the-art systems that employ features from full syntactic parses and/or global reranking. Could you elaborate on this gap and potential avenues for bridging it?
3.  The approach to handling unseen predicates seems rather simplistic. Have you explored alternative methods, such as utilizing character-level embeddings to derive sense representations for unseen predicates?
4.  Could you provide some examples of error cases where the greedy inference strategy demonstrably leads to suboptimal parses?  



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections (e.g., Section 3.3) could benefit from further simplification.
*   Figure 1 (example parse) is helpful but could be larger and more clearly labeled.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The demonstrated ability to learn effective state representations and achieve competitive performance with a fast, greedy parser is impressive. While the core algorithmic innovation may not be revolutionary, the practical benefits and thorough evaluation make this work worthy of acceptance.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, especially providing a more nuanced discussion of the performance gap with SOTA and expanding the ablation studies, would further strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
