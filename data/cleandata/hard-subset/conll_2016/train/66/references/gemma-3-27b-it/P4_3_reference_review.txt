This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state, and employing a greedy inference approach. The key contribution is demonstrating that strong performance in joint parsing can be achieved without relying on hand-crafted features or complex decoding schemes, relying instead on learned representations and a simpler, faster inference procedure. A secondary contribution lies in the effective application of stack LSTMs to this joint task, extending their success from purely syntactic parsing. Finally, the paper provides a solid empirical evaluation on multiple CoNLL shared task datasets, establishing competitive performance compared to prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, surpassing previous joint parsing models, including those employing more complex features and decoding schemes. This is demonstrated through clear tables and comparisons with established baselines.
2. **Novelty in Feature Representation:** The use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features, represents a significant shift in approach. This simplifies model development and potentially allows for better generalization. The paper convincingly argues that the stack LSTM captures relevant information despite the absence of explicit, linguistically motivated features.
3. **Efficiency:** The greedy inference approach leads to a significantly faster parsing time (177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English) compared to more complex systems, making it practical for real-world applications. This speed advantage is clearly highlighted in the results section.
4. **Multilingual Performance:** The model demonstrates reasonable performance on non-English datasets from CoNLL 2009, indicating a degree of language independence and adaptability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains Over Strong Pipelines:** While competitive, the model’s performance still falls short of the very best systems that leverage hand-crafted features and more sophisticated decoding (e.g., reranking). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't deeply analyze *why* the gap remains, beyond stating that these advances are orthogonal. A more detailed comparison of feature importance or error analysis might be beneficial.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The reported performance, particularly for Chinese, relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings. The robustness of the model without these embeddings is questioned, and the paper acknowledges a performance drop in their absence. This suggests the model isn’t fully learning representations from scratch.
3. **Simplifying Assumptions in Transition System:** The copying of buffer elements during syntactic transitions, while simplifying implementation, might introduce inefficiencies or limit the model's ability to capture certain linguistic phenomena. The rationale is explained, but its impact on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study on LSTM Architecture:** The paper mentions several hyperparameters, but lacks a focused ablation study on the impact of different LSTM configurations (e.g., hidden size, number of layers) on the final performance. This would help demonstrate the sensitivity of the model to architectural choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions that the system’s performance doesn’t match the top expert-crafted feature-based systems. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis to identify specific linguistic phenomena where the proposed model struggles compared to these stronger baselines?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and size of the pre-trained word embeddings? Can you elaborate on the strategies explored to mitigate the dependence on pre-trained embeddings, such as character-based embeddings?
3. The paper states that the copying of buffer elements during syntactic transitions simplifies implementation. Have you explored alternative implementations without copying, and what was the impact on performance and computational cost?
4. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the constraints on transitions and how they contribute to preventing invalid parser states? Are there scenarios where these constraints might be overly restrictive or hinder performance?
5. Given the performance gain from the hybrid model on out-of-domain data, do you believe incorporating a separate syntactic parsing step as a pre-processing stage would be a beneficial addition to the model, even if it introduces a pipeline structure?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.
*   Figure 1 could be improved by highlighting the transitions being applied.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is generally consistent, but could be clarified in a few places.
*   The reference to the 439.9 second runtime of Lei et al. (2015) is helpful context.
