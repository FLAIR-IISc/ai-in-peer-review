The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging Stack LSTMs to represent the parser's state, eschewing traditional hand-crafted features. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that a purely representation-learning-based approach can achieve competitive performance on standard CoNLL datasets for joint parsing, particularly within a greedy inference framework. A secondary contribution is the introduction of a new transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies commonly found in datasets like CoNLL 2009. Finally, the paper provides a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, including multilingual data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Significant Performance Gains:** While the paper demonstrates competitive performance, it *doesn’t surpass* the state-of-the-art systems that *do* utilize expertly crafted features and optimal decoding. The authors acknowledge this, but framing the work as "bypassing" expensive features feels overstated. The performance gains over Henderson et al. (2008) are incremental, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a paradigm shift. The claim of achieving “comparable” results to state-of-the-art systems is misleading when substantial gaps remain. A more honest framing would position this as a viable *alternative* approach, not a replacement.

2. **Insufficient Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology – Stack LSTMs – is not novel; Dyer et al. (2015) introduced it. The application to joint parsing is a natural extension, not a breakthrough. The combination of existing techniques (arc-eager parsing, Stack LSTMs) lacks a genuinely innovative component. The contribution feels more like a solid engineering effort than a fundamental research advance.

3. **Incomplete Ablation Study & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The ablation study (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) is superficial. The discussion lacks depth regarding *why* certain configurations perform as they do. For example, the statement that “syntax and semantics benefit each other” in the training domain is insufficient. Further, the reliance on intuitively set hyperparameters, with only a limited range explored for dropout and action representation size, raises concerns about the robustness of the results. There is no discussion of the sensitivity of performance to these parameters.

4. **Implementation Details and Reproducibility Concerns:** While the authors promise an open-source release, the description of the implementation (e.g., “two-layer LSTMs, with hidden state dimension 100”) is vague. Critical details – such as the optimization algorithm beyond “stochastic gradient descent,” specific initialization schemes, and regularization techniques beyond dropout – are missing. This hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the potential impact of implementation choices. The reference to a specific implementation of Lei et al. (2015) for runtime comparison feels like a weak justification and begs the question of whether a fairer comparison with other state-of-the-art systems is possible.

5. **Minor but Persistent Formatting and Grammatical Errors:** The paper contains a surprising number of minor errors. For example, "gs(u,v, l)" appears without clear definition prior to its usage. Additionally, the inconsistent use of italics and boldface, and occasional awkward phrasing detract from the paper’s professionalism. While individually minor, the cumulative effect is noticeable.




**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Presentation of the Algorithm:** The transition-based parsing framework and the integration of Stack LSTMs are described in a clear and understandable manner. The example transition sequence in the supplementary material, referenced in the text, is a helpful illustration.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, multilingual data) provides a relatively thorough assessment of the model's performance.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The reported runtime (177.6±18 seconds) is a noteworthy advantage, particularly for applications where speed is critical. The promise of an open-source release further enhances the practical value of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter search process, including the rationale for the chosen ranges and the impact of different settings on performance?
2. The ablation study is limited. Could you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the contribution of each component of the model, perhaps by systematically removing or replacing different elements?
3. The paper claims to "bypass" expensive features. Can you quantify the computational cost of extracting these features compared to the cost of training and running your model, providing a more concrete justification for this claim?
4. Can you clarify the optimization algorithm used for training? Was momentum or other adaptive learning rate methods employed?
5. Will the open-source implementation include clear documentation and instructions for reproducing the reported results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include "inter alia," others do not).
*   The discussion of related work could be more critical, focusing on the limitations of previous approaches and how this work addresses those limitations.
*   The introduction could benefit from a more focused statement of the research problem and the specific hypotheses being tested.




**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents a solid engineering effort and a reasonably thorough evaluation, the lack of significant novelty and performance gains, combined with the methodological weaknesses and minor presentation flaws, do not warrant acceptance to a competitive conference like CoNLL. The work feels incremental and doesn’t offer a substantial contribution to the field. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
