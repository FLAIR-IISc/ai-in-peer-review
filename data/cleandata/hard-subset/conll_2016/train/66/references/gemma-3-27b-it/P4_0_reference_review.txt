This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features and employing greedy inference. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline-based systems.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs in learning representations of the parser's state for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This allows for competitive performance *without* relying on traditionally important, but expensive-to-extract, syntactic features.
2. **Competitive Joint Parsing Performance with Greedy Inference:**  The model achieves strong performance on standard benchmarks utilizing a simple, efficient, greedy inference approach. This is a significant achievement, as many high-performing systems rely on more complex and computationally intensive decoding strategies.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, and multilingual CoNLL 2009) and includes ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) which provides insight into the contribution of the joint modeling approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Representation Learning:** The use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, rather than just top-of-stack items, is a novel application in the context of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. This sidesteps the need for extensive feature engineering.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser outperforms previous joint parsing models and achieves comparable, and sometimes better, results than pipeline approaches, especially considering its efficiency.  The results on CoNLL 2009 are particularly noteworthy, showcasing competitive performance against more complex systems.
3. **Efficiency:** The greedy inference and relatively straightforward model architecture result in a fast parsing system (177.6 ± 18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English). This practical efficiency is a significant advantage.
4. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:**  The paper provides a clear description of the transition-based algorithm, the stack LSTM architecture, and the training procedure.  The inclusion of a detailed explanation of the transitions and their modifications to the stacks and buffer is helpful.
5. **Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid models allows a detailed analysis of the joint model's contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains Over Strong Baselines:** While the performance is competitive, the improvement over existing pipeline systems, especially those employing sophisticated features and reranking, is not substantial. The authors acknowledge this and attribute it to the absence of those features in their model.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on multilingual data, particularly Chinese, relies heavily on pre-trained embeddings. This suggests a sensitivity to embedding quality and might limit generalization to low-resource languages without suitable embeddings.
3. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Neural SRL Models:** The comparison to related work does not include a thorough evaluation against very recent, state-of-the-art neural SRL models that *also* leverage end-to-end learning and complex architectures. More up-to-date comparisons would strengthen the positioning of the work.
4. **Simplification of Semantic Self-Dependencies:** The handling of semantic self-dependencies with a dedicated transition feels somewhat ad-hoc. A deeper investigation into how the model learns to represent and resolve these dependencies could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the LSTM hidden state dimension and dropout rate?  Were more extensive hyperparameter search experiments conducted beyond what is reported?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for overfitting on the WSJ domain (CoNLL 2009). Could you provide more details on the observed overfitting, such as error analysis or differences in performance on specific types of dependencies?
3.  Given that the greedy inference is a key design choice, did you experiment with beam search or other decoding strategies to assess the potential performance gains? If so, what were the results?
4.  How does the model handle long-range dependencies, particularly in semantic parsing, given the limitations of the stack LSTM's memory?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figure illustrating the parsing process (Figure 1) could be more clearly labeled and explained.
* While the paper mentions future work, a more concrete roadmap for addressing the identified limitations would be beneficial.
* The supplementary material should contain more detailed examples of transition sequences to enhance understanding of the parsing process.
