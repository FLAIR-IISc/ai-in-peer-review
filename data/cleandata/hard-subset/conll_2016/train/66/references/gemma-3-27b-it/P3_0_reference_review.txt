## Review for CoNLL Paper: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure building approach, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The key innovation lies in learning representations for the *entire* parser state using Stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features, and employing entirely greedy inference.  The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance compared to previous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English) and competitive multilingual results on CoNLL 2009.  A significant practical benefit highlighted is the system’s speed.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective State Representation Learning:** The successful application of Stack LSTMs to capture the complete parser state for joint syntactic and semantic parsing is a valuable contribution. This shows a pathway towards reducing reliance on feature engineering, a significant bottleneck in traditional NLP systems.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves competitive performance against established joint models on standard benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  Outperforming prior joint models *without* hand-crafted features is noteworthy.
3. **Efficient Greedy Inference:** The choice of greedy inference, combined with the learned state representation, leads to a remarkably fast parsing system. This is crucial for practical applications where speed is paramount.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for joint parsing, the limitations of pipeline approaches, and the advantages of their proposed method.  The connection to previous work (Henderson et al.) is well-explained, and the differences are highlighted effectively.
2. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The thorough experimentation on multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008/2009 English & multilingual) and comparisons to a variety of baselines, including both joint and pipeline systems, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed parser. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are particularly insightful.
3. **Practical Significance of Speed:**  The reported runtime (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009) is a significant advantage, particularly when compared to slower pipeline systems.  This makes the parser a viable option for real-time applications.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to releasing an open-source implementation, which is crucial for enabling the community to build upon their work.
5. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The adaptation of Stack LSTMs to handle the joint parsing task is well-explained and the architecture is logically presented. The choice to maintain the buffer allows for interaction between syntax and semantics during the parsing process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing is effective, the underlying transition system (Henderson et al., 2013) and the greedy inference are not novel in themselves. The primary innovation is the *representation* learning, which, while impactful, feels less groundbreaking than a fundamentally new parsing algorithm.
2. **Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:** The parser does not match the performance of the top-performing, feature-rich, and often non-greedy systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015). While the authors acknowledge this and attribute it to the lack of those features, it’s a crucial distinction. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of *where* the system falls short and *why*, potentially identifying specific error patterns.
3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a comprehensive error analysis.  Understanding the types of errors the system makes could reveal opportunities for improvement and guide future research.  For example, are there specific semantic roles or syntactic structures that are consistently misparsed?
4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While hyperparameters are mentioned, the details of the tuning process are somewhat vague ("tuned on the dev data").  A more detailed description of the tuning strategy would strengthen the reproducibility and understanding of the results.  
5. **Reliance on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance, particularly for the multilingual setting, relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings.  The impact of embedding quality and potential issues with out-of-vocabulary words should be discussed in more detail.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? Specifically, what types of errors (syntactic, semantic, sense disambiguation) are most frequent, and are there any patterns related to specific sentence structures or semantic roles?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the LSTM hidden state dimension and the dropout rate? Could you provide a table summarizing the performance with different configurations?
3. **Feature Importance:** Although you intentionally avoided hand-crafted features, have you explored adding even simple, automatically generated features (e.g., word shape features) to see if they provide any performance gains?
4. **Scalability:** How does the performance scale with sentence length? Is there a point at which the Stack LSTM struggles to maintain sufficient contextual information?
5. **Semantic Self-Dependencies:** The introduction of `M-SELF` is clever. Can you elaborate on the impact of this transition on overall performance and whether it consistently improves results on instances involving nominal predicates?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Figure 1 is helpful for illustrating the joint parse structure.
*   The comparison to Lei et al. (2015) in terms of runtime is a valuable addition.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters and their values.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic parsing.  While the core algorithmic components are not entirely novel, the successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn effective state representations and the resulting performance and efficiency gains are significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, a more in-depth error analysis and a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process would substantially strengthen the paper.
