## Peer Review for "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that leverages Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving comparable results to strong pipeline-based systems, while being significantly faster. The core approach is a greedy, incremental parsing procedure guided by these learned state representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Joint Parsing with Learned Representations:** The paper convincingly shows that strong joint syntactic and semantic parsing can be achieved *without* the extensive feature engineering traditionally relied upon. This is significant as it lowers the barrier to entry for developing joint models and opens avenues for easier adaptation to new languages.
2. **Practical Speed & Scalability:**  The greedy inference and efficient LSTM-based state representation lead to a significantly faster parsing system compared to state-of-the-art pipeline approaches, highlighting its potential for practical applications.
3. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:**  The thorough evaluation on the CoNLL datasets, including comparisons to a range of previous systems, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art (or very near it) performance among joint models on established benchmark datasets.  The consistently positive results across multiple datasets (including multilingual data) demonstrate the robustness of the method.  The numerical results are clearly presented and readily comparable to previous work.
2. **Novelty in Feature Representation:** Utilizing Stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state, rather than focusing solely on the stack top, is a key innovation. This holistic approach appears to be crucial for capturing the relevant contextual information for joint prediction. The argument for bypassing expensive, hand-crafted features is compelling and well-supported by the results.
3. **Practicality & Efficiency:** The emphasis on greedy inference and the resulting fast parsing speed are important practical advantages.  This makes the model more suitable for real-time applications or processing large volumes of text. The reported runtime is a significant improvement over other competitive systems.
4. **Clear & Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and the experimental setup. The use of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1 and Figure 3) is effective in illustrating the parsing process and the model architecture.
5. **Open-Source Commitment:** The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation is highly commendable and will facilitate further research and development in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall performance numbers, it lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For instance, are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., long-distance dependencies, complex semantic roles) that consistently lead to errors?
2. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines is Limited:** While the comparisons to previous joint models are solid, the comparison to state-of-the-art *pipeline* systems is somewhat limited.  The authors acknowledge that their performance doesn't match the best pipeline systems, but a more in-depth analysis of the trade-offs between speed, accuracy, and feature engineering would be beneficial.  Specifically, it’s unclear if the performance gap is closing or widening with current advancements in pipeline SRL.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t provide enough detail about the process. How sensitive is the model's performance to changes in hyperparameters?  A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter space would strengthen the validity of the results.  The lack of ablation studies (e.g., removing layers from the LSTMs) would also be valuable.
4. **Reliance on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained embeddings, particularly for the multilingual setting, is a potential limitation. While effective, it introduces an external dependency and might not generalize well to languages with limited pre-trained resources. The effect of the pre-trained embeddings should be quantified.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide some examples of common errors made by the model and discuss potential reasons for these errors?  Specifically, are there certain types of dependencies or semantic roles that are consistently mispredicted?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What search strategies were used, and how sensitive is the performance to different hyperparameter settings?
3. **Ablation Study:** Have you conducted any ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., Stack LSTMs, learned embeddings)?  
4. **Computational Cost/Scalability:**  While the runtime is reported, what is the memory footprint of the model? How does performance scale with sentence length?  
5. **Multilingual Data Requirements:** How does the performance vary with the size of the training data for different languages? What is the minimum amount of training data required to achieve reasonable performance on a new language?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper refers to Figure 5 in the supplementary material, but that figure wasn't available at the time of review.
*  It would be interesting to see a discussion of the limitations of the greedy inference approach, and whether exploring more sophisticated decoding strategies (e.g., beam search) could further improve performance.
* The mention of morphological features in the discussion of future work is pertinent, but expanding on this idea with concrete examples of how they could be incorporated would be helpful.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the submission. I recommend acceptance.
