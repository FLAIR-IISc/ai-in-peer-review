This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure-building approach, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on hand-crafted features. The primary contributions of this work are: (1) the application of stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parsing state, enabling a feature-rich model without manual feature engineering; (2) a demonstration of competitive performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models and achieving comparable results to pipeline systems; and (3) a fast, greedy inference procedure with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Stack LSTMs:** The core strength of the paper lies in the effective utilization of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state. This approach allows the model to learn relevant information without the need for explicitly designed features, a significant advantage in complex NLP tasks like joint parsing. The use of stack LSTMs addresses a key limitation of previous work, which often relied heavily on feature engineering.

2. **Competitive Performance:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, particularly when compared to other *joint* models. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and validate its ability to capture syntactic and semantic dependencies. While acknowledging that pipeline systems with handcrafted features still achieve superior results, the work establishes a strong baseline for joint parsing.

3. **Efficiency:** The greedy inference procedure results in a notably fast parsing time (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English). This efficiency is a significant practical benefit, making the parser suitable for real-time applications or scenarios where rapid parsing is crucial. This is explicitly highlighted by the authors and provides a tangible advantage over more complex and slower systems.

4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to various baseline models (including ablations) and state-of-the-art systems. The authors also present multilingual results, demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed approach. The inclusion of Syntax-only and Semantics-only experiments offers valuable insight into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** The foundation of the parser—the incremental structure-building approach and the transition system—is largely based on Henderson et al. (2008, 2013). While the application of stack LSTMs is novel, the core algorithmic contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking.  The paper relies heavily on existing work in terms of the parsing algorithm itself.

2. **Performance Gap Compared to Strongest Systems:** Despite achieving competitive results, the parser's performance still lags behind the top-performing systems that leverage carefully engineered features and optimal decoding strategies. The authors acknowledge this but it indicates a potential area for future improvement. The comparison to the state-of-the-art demonstrates that there is still room to close the performance gap.

3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance of the multilingual model, particularly the Chinese parser, relies heavily on the quality of pretrained word embeddings. This suggests a potential limitation in the model's ability to generalize to languages where high-quality pretrained embeddings are not readily available. While acknowledged, this dependence is a potential bottleneck for wider applicability.

4. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Study on Stack LSTM Parameters:** While the paper mentions tuning dropout rates and action representations, a more in-depth ablation study on the stack LSTM architecture (e.g., hidden state dimensions, number of layers) would be beneficial. This would provide further insight into the impact of different design choices on the model's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that the system copies items to the stack rather than moving them, allowing access for semantic operations. Could you elaborate on the specific design choices that led to this decision and whether alternative approaches (e.g., maintaining separate copies) were considered?

2.  Could you provide more details about the limitations of the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions, particularly the 1.5% of cases where it incorrectly encodes the gold standard? Understanding the nature of these errors would be helpful in assessing the overall robustness of the system.

3. The "Hybrid" model shows improved performance on the out-of-domain Brown corpus. What specific characteristics of the training data (WSJ) might lead to the overfitting observed in the "Joint" model, and how could this be mitigated?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.
* The inclusion of a runtime comparison with other systems is valuable.
* The code availability is a strong positive aspect.
* It would be interesting to see an analysis of the types of errors made by the parser and how they differ from those made by pipeline systems.
