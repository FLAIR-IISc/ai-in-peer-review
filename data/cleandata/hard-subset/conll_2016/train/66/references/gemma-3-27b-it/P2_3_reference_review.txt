## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, greedy approach. The core innovation lies in utilizing Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state (syntactic and semantic stacks and buffer) *without* relying on hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results compared to pipeline systems, while also being significantly faster.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Joint Parsing Without Expert Features:** The paper convincingly shows that strong performance in joint syntactic-semantic parsing *can* be achieved without relying on the typically extensive feature engineering traditionally used in this area. This is a significant result, simplifying model development and potentially improving generalization.
2. **Successful Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** This work extends the successful application of Stack LSTMs, previously shown for syntactic parsing, to the more complex task of joint parsing. It demonstrates their ability to capture sufficient information from the parsing state for accurate prediction.
3. **Efficient and Competitive Performance:** The model’s speed (177.6s on CoNLL 2009) is a noteworthy contribution, making it practical for real-world applications where efficiency is crucial.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over previous joint parsing models on standard datasets. The performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 is compelling, and the comparisons to both joint and pipeline systems are well-presented.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional pipeline approaches and the potential benefits of a joint model that avoids cascading errors. The rationale for using Stack LSTMs to represent the parsing state is also well-explained.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct comprehensive experiments, including ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) that provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components of the model. The multilingual evaluation adds further strength.
4. **Practicality & Open-Source Commitment:** The reported runtime is impressive, and the commitment to releasing an open-source implementation enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.
5. **Clear and Accessible Writing:** The paper is well-written and relatively easy to follow, even for those not deeply familiar with transition-based parsing. The inclusion of an example run (referenced in the paper) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core Algorithm:** While the application of Stack LSTMs to joint parsing is a valuable extension, the underlying transition system (Henderson et al., 2013) is not novel. The primary innovation lies in the representation learning approach *applied to* this existing framework.  This needs to be explicitly acknowledged and contextualized.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art is Limited:** While the comparisons to previous joint models are strong, the comparison to *current* state-of-the-art systems (which often rely on those expensive features the authors aim to avoid) is less direct. The authors acknowledge this, but it leaves open the question of how much performance is sacrificed for the benefits of feature engineering and more complex decoding. The discussion focuses primarily on speed differences.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:**  The performance on multilingual data, particularly Chinese, is heavily reliant on pre-trained embeddings. This suggests that the model's ability to generalize to new languages might be limited without sufficient pre-training resources.
4. **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The use of greedy decoding, while contributing to the efficiency of the system, may inherently limit the achievable accuracy compared to models employing more sophisticated decoding strategies (e.g., beam search).  The trade-off between speed and accuracy isn't fully explored.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying the types of errors the model makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Novelty Clarification:** Could you elaborate on the specific contributions of this work beyond applying Stack LSTMs to the existing transition system? How significant is the shift from hand-crafted features to learned representations in terms of reducing development effort and improving portability?
2. **Decoding Strategy:** Have you experimented with beam search or other decoding strategies, even if it impacted runtime?  What is the potential accuracy gain from moving away from greedy decoding?
3. **Feature Ablation:** Beyond the presented ablation studies, could you provide a more detailed analysis of which components of the Stack LSTM representation (e.g., the individual stack LSTMs, the composition functions) are most critical for performance?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a brief error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors made by the system? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that the model struggles with?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Lei et al. (2015) runtime should include a link to the code, as mentioned in the text.
*   The explanation of the interleaved syntactic and semantic transitions is clear, but a visual diagram might further enhance understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic parsing. While the core algorithmic innovation is incremental, the successful application of Stack LSTMs to learn effective representations without hand-crafted features, coupled with the model’s speed and competitive performance, makes it a worthwhile contribution.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
