This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based, incremental structure building approach, utilizing Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state. The primary contribution is a competitive joint parsing model that eschews expert-crafted features, relying instead on learned representations from a greedy parsing procedure. A second contribution lies in demonstrating that the Stack LSTM architecture effectively captures the necessary information for joint parsing without requiring extensive feature engineering. Finally, the system's speed (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data) is a practical benefit, though not a novel contribution in itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models and reaching comparable levels to pipeline-based systems that rely on expert-crafted features. This provides clear evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are well-presented in tables, allowing for easy comparison with existing systems.
2. **Novelty in Feature Representation:** The use of Stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state, eliminating the need for hand-engineered features, is a significant step forward. This approach simplifies the modeling process and potentially improves generalization. The authors effectively highlight the challenges of feature engineering in greedy models and position their work as a solution.
3. **Efficiency:** The reported runtime of the parser is impressive, significantly faster than many state-of-the-art systems that rely on more complex inference procedures. This makes the model potentially suitable for real-time applications or large-scale data processing.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The description of the transition-based procedure, Stack LSTMs, and the statistical model is sufficiently detailed to allow for replication. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm in the supplementary material is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Algorithm:** The transition system itself is largely based on existing work (Henderson et al., 2013), with the primary innovation being the representation learning aspect. While the results are strong, the algorithmic contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking.  The authors acknowledge this, but the paper might overstate the novelty of the overall approach.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper demonstrates competitiveness with earlier systems, the comparison to the most advanced state-of-the-art systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015) reveals a performance gap.  The authors attribute this to the absence of expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, and external datasets; however, the extent to which these factors contribute to the gap is not thoroughly analyzed. A more detailed analysis of where the model falls short compared to these systems would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Stack LSTM Components:** The paper describes the use of four Stack LSTMs (one for each stack). However, it lacks an ablation study to evaluate the individual contribution of each Stack LSTM. It is unclear if all four LSTMs are equally important or if removing one or more could maintain acceptable performance with reduced complexity.
4. **Simplified Predicate Sense Disambiguation:** The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to the “01” sense is a pragmatic solution but may negatively impact performance, particularly on datasets with a diverse range of predicates. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore alternative strategies for handling unseen predicates.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis comparing your model's predictions to those of the state-of-the-art systems mentioned in the paper? Specifically, what types of errors does your model make that the stronger systems avoid?
2.  Would you consider an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each individual Stack LSTM?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with the Stack LSTMs compared to other representation learning techniques (e.g., simple word embeddings)? Is the performance gain justified by the increased computational demands?
4.  You mention that the system is optimized for speed. How does this optimization impact the overall accuracy? Is there a trade-off between speed and performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference to "pseudo-projectivization" could be clarified for readers unfamiliar with the technique.
*  The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for readability.
*  The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the greedy inference approach.
