### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The core idea is to apply the Stack LSTM architecture (Dyer et al., 2015) to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) without relying on any hand-crafted features. This learned representation is then used by a classifier to make a sequence of greedy decisions to construct both parse structures simultaneously. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating strong performance relative to prior joint models and significant speed advantages over state-of-the-art pipeline systems.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel application of Stack LSTMs to the joint syntactic-semantic parsing task.** This work successfully extends the featureless, state-summarizing paradigm from syntax-only parsing to a more complex, multi-output problem. It demonstrates that learning a representation of the full algorithmic state is sufficient to coordinate the generation of two distinct but related structures, obviating the need for expensive, manually designed features.
2.  **Strong empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of a greedy, featureless joint model.** The paper shows that this approach outperforms all previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. This is a significant result, suggesting that the benefits of joint modeling can be realized without the complexity of beam search or intricate feature engineering.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis including multiple model variants and efficiency comparisons.** The authors provide valuable ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that isolate the contributions of the joint components and offer insights into the model's behavior, such as its overfitting tendency on out-of-domain data. The reported speed-up over comparable state-of-the-art systems highlights the practical utility of the proposed method.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model Architecture:** The paper's primary strength lies in its clean and powerful modeling approach. By replacing complex, hand-engineered feature templates—which are particularly difficult to design in a joint setting—with learned representations of the parser's data structures, the model offers a simpler and more generalizable framework. This is a compelling demonstration of the power of representation learning to capture the complex interactions between syntax and semantics during an incremental parse.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance and Strong Baselines:** The results are convincing. The model sets a new state-of-the-art among joint parsing systems on these standard benchmarks, significantly outperforming its direct predecessor (Henderson et al., 2013). The comparisons are fair and well-contextualized, acknowledging that the model does not surpass heavily-engineered pipeline systems but explaining that many of their advances are orthogonal. This honesty strengthens the paper's claims.
3.  **High Practicality and Efficiency:** The greedy nature of the parser, combined with the efficiency of the neural architecture, results in a very fast system. The reported runtime (177.6s on the CoNLL 2009 test set) is nearly 2.5 times faster than a comparable state-of-the-art system. This makes the model highly attractive for practical applications where parsing speed is a critical concern. The authors' commitment to releasing the code further enhances the work's potential impact.
4.  **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The transition system is clearly defined in Table 1, the model architecture is well-illustrated in Figure 3, and the experimental setup is detailed. The inclusion of model variants like "Hybrid" and "Semantics-only" provides valuable analysis and strengthens the conclusion that joint modeling is beneficial, particularly for in-domain data.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty of the Core Technique:** The main weakness is that the core technical machinery, the Stack LSTM, is not new and is taken directly from Dyer et al. (2015). The transition system is also a modification of Henderson et al. (2013). While the application to the joint task is novel and the results are strong, the work can be seen as a successful combination and application of existing methods rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new parsing or learning paradigm. This slightly limits the paper's conceptual contribution.
2.  **Performance Gap with Top Pipeline Systems:** The authors are transparent about this, but the model still falls short of the overall state-of-the-art on these tasks, which is held by pipeline systems with global features, optimal decoding, or external data. This raises the question of whether this greedy, joint approach is fundamentally limited. While the speed is a major advantage, the paper doesn't fully explore whether the performance gap could be closed by incorporating a limited beam search or other techniques that might bridge the gap without sacrificing all of the efficiency gains.
3.  **Limited Hyperparameter Exploration:** The paper states that many hyperparameters "have been set intuitively" and that "careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." While some parameters were tuned, this lack of a more systematic exploration of the model's architectural choices (e.g., hidden state dimensions, number of LSTM layers) slightly weakens the claims. A more rigorous tuning process or sensitivity analysis would provide greater confidence that the reported results represent the full potential of this architecture.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  You introduce a new `M-SELF` transition to handle nominal predicates, which you note account for 6.68% of semantic arcs. This seems like a crucial, task-specific addition. Could you provide an ablation study showing the performance drop (particularly in semantic F1) if this transition is removed? This would quantify its importance.
2.  The result that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data is interesting, and you attribute it to overfitting. Could you elaborate on why the joint training might be more susceptible to overfitting in this way? Have you considered other regularization techniques besides dropout (e.g., L2 regularization on the composition matrices) to see if this gap can be closed, allowing the joint model to generalize better?
3.  The greedy inference is a core aspect of your model's speed. Have you performed any error analysis to characterize the types of mistakes this greedy approach makes compared to higher-performing pipeline or beam-search systems? For example, does it struggle more with long-distance dependencies or complex, multi-argument predicates where a global view might be more beneficial?
