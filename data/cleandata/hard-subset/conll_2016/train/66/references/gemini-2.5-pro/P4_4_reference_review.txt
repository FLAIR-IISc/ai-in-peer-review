### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model adapts the synchronized, incremental parsing framework of Henderson et al. (2013) but makes two key modifications: first, it replaces hand-crafted features with learned representations of the entire parser state using Stack LSTMs; second, it employs a purely greedy inference strategy instead of beam search. The parser's state, comprising a syntactic stack, a semantic stack, an input buffer, and an action history, is encoded into a fixed-size vector representation by four separate Stack LSTMs. This representation is then used by a classifier to select the next parsing action. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks. Results show that it outperforms previous joint parsing models and is competitive with top-performing systems from the original shared tasks, while being substantially faster due to its greedy nature.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel application of Stack LSTMs to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.** The primary contribution is the successful extension of the Stack LSTM architecture, previously used for syntactic parsing, to a joint model. This involves managing separate syntactic and semantic data structures and learning a unified, continuous representation of the complete parser state to guide both types of parsing decisions simultaneously, thereby obviating the need for complex, manually engineered features.
2.  **An effective and efficient greedy model for joint parsing.** The paper demonstrates that a fully greedy model, guided by rich learned state representations, can outperform previous joint models that relied on more complex search algorithms like beam search. The resulting system is linear-time and computationally efficient, making it practical for downstream applications.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis with insightful ablations.** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on standard CoNLL benchmarks, including multilingual data. The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid model variants provides a clear and controlled analysis of the benefits and trade-offs of the joint approach versus pipelined alternatives within the same modeling framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty.** The core idea of learning a holistic representation of the parser state for a joint task using multiple Stack LSTMs is a novel and logical extension of prior work. The architecture is technically sound, effectively integrating separate syntactic and semantic operations under a unified decision-making process. By successfully replacing an extensive set of discrete, expert-crafted features with learned representations, the work represents a significant advance for transition-based joint parsing.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. It significantly surpasses the performance of the Henderson et al. systems, from which its algorithmic framework is derived. This provides strong evidence for the superiority of learning representations of the entire parser state over using features from a more limited local context. Furthermore, its performance is comparable to many of the top-performing, feature-rich pipeline systems from the original shared tasks.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis.** The paper's evaluation is thorough. The ablation studies comparing the joint model to syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid versions are particularly valuable. They empirically confirm the utility of syntactic information for semantic parsing (Joint vs. Semantics-only) and offer interesting insights, such as the observation that the pipelined Hybrid model generalizes better to out-of-domain data than the Joint model, suggesting the latter may be more prone to overfitting. The inclusion of multilingual results demonstrates the model's adaptability, and the runtime comparison highlights its practical efficiency.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility.** The paper is clearly written and provides a detailed description of the transition system, model architecture, and experimental setup. The diagrams and tables are informative and support the text well. The authors' commitment to releasing an open-source implementation upon publication significantly enhances the paper's potential impact and facilitates reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Contemporary State-of-the-Art.** The model's performance, while strong compared to older systems, lags behind contemporary state-of-the-art SRL systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this gap but attribute it primarily to orthogonal factors like global inference and expert features derived from full syntactic parses. However, the paper would be stronger with a more in-depth analysis of *why* the learned representations are insufficient to close this gap. It remains unclear whether the primary limitation is the greedy search strategy or an inability of the LSTM-based state representation to capture the same crucial non-local information that full-parse features provide.
2.  **Uncertainty Regarding Oracle Training Data.** The authors note that the oracle used to convert gold-standard parses into training sequences is imperfect for 1.5% of English sentences due to complex, crossing semantic arcs. This introduces ambiguity into the training process. The paper does not specify how these problematic cases are handled (e.g., are they discarded, or is the model trained on a flawed action sequence?). Training on an imperfect oracle could place an artificial ceiling on the model's performance and potentially teach it to avoid certain valid, complex structures. This is a potentially significant technical detail that impacts the soundness of the training procedure.
3.  **Hyperparameter Selection Seems Ad-hoc.** The paper states that many hyperparameters were "set intuitively," with only a few (dropout, action embedding size) being tuned on development data. While common in practice, this lack of a more systematic hyperparameter search process weakens the empirical claims. It is difficult to ascertain whether the reported results are representative of the model's full potential or simply a product of a particular "intuitive" configuration. A more rigorous tuning process, even if briefly described, would lend greater confidence to the results and their analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify how the 1.5% of sentences with imperfect oracle transition sequences were handled during training? Were these sentences excluded from the training set, or was the model trained using the imperfect sequences generated by the oracle? How might this issue affect the model's ability to learn to produce complex semantic graphs with multiple crossing arcs?
2.  The Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus, which you suggest is a sign of the Joint model overfitting. Could you elaborate on why joint training might be more susceptible to overfitting in this context? Does the model learn spurious correlations between specific syntactic and semantic constructions in the WSJ training data that do not generalize?
3.  The performance gap between your greedy model and globally optimized models is notable. Have you considered experiments with beam search? Given the reported speed of your greedy parser, a small-beam search might be computationally feasible and would provide a direct way to test whether the primary performance bottleneck is the search algorithm or the expressiveness of the learned state representations.
