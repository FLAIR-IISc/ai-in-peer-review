### Summary of the Paper
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model's core innovation is its use of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state—encompassing the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. This approach allows the parser to make informed decisions without relying on any hand-crafted, high-level syntactic features (like tree paths), which have been central to previous high-performing systems. The model is based on the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but replaces its feature-based classifier and beam search with a fully neural, greedy decision-making process. The authors demonstrate that their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieves competitive results on multilingual data.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Successful Neural Extension for Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is the successful adaptation and extension of the Stack LSTM architecture (Dyer et al., 2015) from syntactic parsing to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This demonstrates that learning a holistic representation of the algorithmic state is a viable and powerful strategy for joint modeling, effectively obviating the need for feature engineering.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Greedy Joint Parsing:** The work establishes a new state-of-the-art for greedy, joint parsing on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. By outperforming its direct intellectual predecessors (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013), the paper provides strong evidence for the superiority of learned state representations over locally-scoped, expert-crafted features within this algorithmic framework.

3.  **Insightful Ablation Studies:** The paper includes a well-designed set of experiments (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that rigorously evaluate the interactions between the syntactic and semantic components. The finding that the "Hybrid" pipeline model outperforms the "Joint" model on out-of-domain data is particularly insightful, highlighting potential overfitting challenges in joint learning.

### Strengths
1.  **Model Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed model is conceptually elegant. It replaces the complex and often brittle process of feature engineering with a principled, end-to-end learned representation. The move to a purely greedy inference procedure, enabled by the powerful state representations, results in a very fast and algorithmically simple system, which is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper's claims are well-supported by strong results on standard benchmarks. The model consistently outperforms previous joint models, which is the most relevant comparison. The authors are intellectually honest about their model's limitations, clearly stating that it does not surpass the top-performing pipeline systems that use global features or reranking, and framing these as orthogonal contributions. This transparency strengthens the paper.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is comprehensive. It covers two different shared tasks (CoNLL 2008 and 2009), includes both in-domain and out-of-domain test sets, and extends to multiple languages. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and the dynamics of joint learning. The inclusion of a runtime comparison is also a welcome and practical addition.

4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the transition system, the role of the four Stack LSTMs, and the composition functions used to build structural representations. The diagrams effectively illustrate the model architecture and data flow.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core technical machinery—the Stack LSTM—is taken directly from Dyer et al. (2015). The transition system is largely adopted from Henderson et al. (2013). While the combination and application to the joint task are novel and effective, the work is more of a highly successful application and extension of existing methods rather than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.

2.  **Incomplete Joint Modeling for CoNLL 2008:** For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is handled by a separate, pipelined BiLSTM classifier before the main joint parser is run. This slightly undermines the "fully joint" narrative, as it introduces a potential source of cascading errors that joint modeling is meant to avoid. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the impact of this design choice or explored integrating predicate identification into the transition system (e.g., via an `IDENTIFY-PRED` action).

3.  **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that many hyperparameters were "set intuitively" and that further tuning is expected to yield improvements. While understandable given computational constraints, this raises concerns about the robustness and reproducibility of the results. A more systematic exploration of key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding sizes, LSTM dimensions, dropout rate), even on the development set, would increase confidence in the model's reported performance.

4.  **Comparison to Contemporary Models:** While the comparison to previous joint and pipeline systems is excellent, the discussion of related work could be strengthened by more deeply engaging with other contemporary neural approaches, even if they address slightly different tasks (e.g., constituent-based SRL). For example, a deeper discussion of the relative merits of this incremental, structured approach versus end-to-end BiLSTM models (like Zhou and Xu, 2015) would provide valuable context.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The finding that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data is very interesting. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Does it suggest that the joint model learns domain-specific correlations between syntax and semantics that are brittle and do not generalize, whereas the pipeline approach is more robust in this regard?

2.  Regarding the CoNLL 2008 task, what was the rationale for using a separate predicate identification module? Have you considered integrating this step into the transition system, and what challenges do you foresee in doing so? What is the performance of the full system when using gold predicates, to better isolate the parsing performance from predicate identification errors?

3.  The paper introduces a new `M-SELF` transition to handle nominal predicates in NomBank. Could you provide a brief quantitative analysis of its impact? For example, how does the model perform on these specific self-dependencies, and what is the overall system performance if this transition is omitted?
