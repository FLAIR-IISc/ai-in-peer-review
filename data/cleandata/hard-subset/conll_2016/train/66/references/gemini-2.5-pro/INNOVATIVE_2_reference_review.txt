### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a greedy, incremental parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model uses a transition-based framework, but its primary novelty lies in its use of stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the parser's *entire* algorithmic state—the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. This approach completely eschews the complex, hand-crafted features that have dominated previous work on this task. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that their system outperforms all prior joint models and is competitive with strong pipeline systems, despite being entirely greedy and feature-free.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A new paradigm for joint parsing:** The most significant contribution is the successful demonstration that end-to-end representation learning can replace feature engineering for a complex, structured prediction task like joint parsing. By encoding the entire parser state into a vector, the model moves beyond the locally-scoped features of prior work, representing a bold and conceptually elegant new direction for the field.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of a simple, greedy approach:** The paper shows that a purely greedy model, when empowered with rich, learned representations, can achieve state-of-the-art performance among joint systems. This challenges the prevailing assumption that high performance on such tasks necessitates beam search or other complex inference methods. The model's impressive speed is a highly valuable practical outcome of this design choice.
3.  **A thorough comparative analysis:** The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants provides a clear and insightful ablation study. This analysis convincingly isolates the benefits of the joint modeling approach and offers interesting findings regarding its behavior on out-of-domain data.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** This work is a prime example of the kind of bold, forward-thinking research that pushes the field forward. Instead of incrementally improving upon existing feature-based paradigms, the authors propose a fundamentally different approach. The idea of learning a holistic representation of a parser's algorithmic state is powerful and elegant. It successfully applies recent advances in representation learning (stack LSTMs) to a challenging joint task, showing a clear path away from the brittle and labor-intensive process of feature engineering. This conceptual leap is the paper's greatest strength.
2.  **Impressive Results that Validate the Approach:** For a novel method that intentionally constrains itself (greedy inference, no expert features), the results are excellent. Outperforming all previous joint models on the CoNLL 2008/2009 tasks is a major achievement. The fact that it is competitive with many complex, feature-rich pipeline systems is remarkable and strongly supports the paper's central claims. It proves that what was lost by discarding engineered features was more than compensated for by what was gained from a more global, learned state representation.
3.  **Methodological Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is very well-written and the proposed system is described clearly. The transition system is a sensible adaptation of existing work, and the integration of the stack LSTMs is straightforward and well-motivated. The ablation studies are particularly well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the authors' conclusions about the benefits of joint learning.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The system's speed is a significant advantage. By demonstrating high accuracy with a linear-time greedy model, the authors present a system that is not just a research artifact but a potentially useful tool for downstream applications where both speed and comprehensive linguistic analysis are required.

### Weaknesses

While my overall assessment is highly positive, I have identified a few areas that could be stronger. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the importance of the core contribution.

1.  **The novelty is primarily in application, not technique:** The core neural component, the stack LSTM, was introduced in prior work (Dyer et al., 2015). The main contribution here is the clever and effective *application* of this tool to the more complex joint parsing task. While this is a very significant contribution in its own right, the paper does not propose a fundamentally new neural architecture or learning algorithm.
2.  **Limited analysis of the learned representations:** The paper convincingly shows that the learned state representation works, but it offers little insight into *why* it works. A qualitative error analysis comparing the model's outputs to those of a feature-based system could have been illuminating. For instance, what kinds of long-distance or complex dependencies does this global state representation help capture that were difficult for previous models? This would have strengthened the paper's conclusions.
3.  **Uncertainty around hyperparameter tuning:** The authors frankly state that many hyperparameters were "set intuitively." While I appreciate the honesty and understand the practical constraints, this leaves open the question of how much performance is dependent on this specific configuration. More systematic tuning could potentially lead to even better results and would increase confidence in the model's robustness.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The finding that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data is intriguing. You suggest this is due to overfitting. Does this imply a fundamental weakness in this style of joint modeling for domain adaptation, or could this be addressed with different training or regularization strategies? For example, would techniques like dropout on the connections between syntactic and semantic components help?
2.  The `M-SELF` transition is a nice, clean solution to handle nominal predicates. Was this a common source of error for previous transition-based systems? Could you provide a brief sense of the performance impact of adding this specific transition?
