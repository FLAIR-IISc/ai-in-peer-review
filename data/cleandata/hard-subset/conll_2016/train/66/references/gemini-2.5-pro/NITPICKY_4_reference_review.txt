### **Review**

#### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model's primary contribution is the application of stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history), thereby eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that this feature-agnostic, greedy model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, while being significantly faster than more complex pipeline models. The authors also introduce a minor modification to the transition system, `M-SELF`, to handle nominal predicates.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance Among Joint Models:** The proposed model establishes a new state of the art for joint syntactic-semantic parsers on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. It convincingly outperforms its direct predecessors (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013), demonstrating the effectiveness of learning representations for the entire parser state versus relying on more local features.

2.  **Effective Elimination of Feature Engineering:** The paper successfully shows that modern representation learning techniques, specifically the stack LSTM, can replace the complex and expensive feature engineering common in high-performance parsing systems. This is a valuable finding, particularly for greedy models where designing comprehensive feature sets can be challenging.

3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The greedy inference mechanism makes the parser very fast. The reported end-to-end runtime is impressive and represents a significant practical advantage over slower, more complex systems that rely on pipelines, global features, or exact inference. This makes the system potentially very useful for downstream applications.

4.  **Insightful Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of `Syntax-only`, `Semantics-only`, and `Hybrid` models provides a well-controlled analysis of the system's components. The finding that the `Joint` model overfits on the out-of-domain data compared to the `Hybrid` model is an interesting and honestly reported result that sheds light on the trade-offs of joint learning.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Precision:** The description of the core algorithm contains several ambiguities and omissions that hinder reproducibility and rigorous assessment.
    *   The description of the `S-RIGHT` transition is confusing. It states `u` is popped, a new structure rooted at `u` is pushed, and *then* `v` is copied to the top of the stack. This sequence of operations is non-standard for arc-eager parsers and its precise effect on the stack contents is unclear.
    *   There is an unexplained asymmetry between the `S-LEFT` and `M-LEFT` transitions. `S-LEFT` replaces the buffer front, while `M-LEFT` is stated to leave the semantic stack `M` "unchanged," which seems inconsistent and is not justified.
    *   Footnote 1 mentions a deviation from Henderson et al. (2013) regarding the initial position of the `root` symbol, stating "This works better," but provides no empirical evidence or justification for this claim.
    *   Important implementation details are missing. For instance, the dimensions of the weight matrices (`Zs`, `Zm`, `Zd`) and bias vectors (`es`, `em`, `ed`) for the composition functions are not specified.

2.  **Questionable Experimental Protocol:** The training and hyperparameter tuning procedures lack the rigor expected for a top-tier conference.
    *   The authors state, "Other hyperparameters have been set intuitively; careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." This admission undermines the strength of the reported results, as it suggests the model's full potential was not explored in a systematic way.
    *   The hyperparameter search space for dropout includes the value `1.0`. A dropout rate of 1.0 would zero out the entire layer, which makes no sense and suggests a typographical error in the paper.
    *   The training stopping criterion is described as "when the dev performance does not improve for approximately 6–7 hours of elapsed time." This is an imprecise, non-reproducible heuristic. Standard practice is to specify a fixed number of epochs of non-improvement.
    *   Claims of "significantly outperforms" are made without any statistical significance testing.

3.  **Inherent Algorithmic Limitations:** The chosen transition system has a critical flaw that is relegated to a footnote.
    *   Footnote 5 reveals that for 1.5% of English sentences, the oracle transition sequence cannot correctly reconstruct the gold-standard parse. This represents a hard ceiling on the model's maximum possible performance and is a significant limitation of the chosen framework. This point is important enough to warrant discussion in the main body of the paper, as it affects the interpretation of all reported scores.

4.  **Minor Presentation and Formatting Issues:** Several minor flaws detract from the paper's overall quality.
    *   There is a clear typo in the equation for `yt` in Section 3.2: `[st;mt;bt,at]` should be `[st;mt;bt;at]`.
    *   Figure 3 is not self-contained; its caption states that its meaning depends on a figure in the supplementary material, which is poor practice.
    *   Table 1 uses notation like `gs(u,v, l)` before it is defined in Section 3.3. The `(v, v)` notation for items on the stack/buffer is also not explicitly defined.
    *   The use of "inter alia" in the introduction's citation block is overly colloquial for a formal research paper.

#### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact sequence of push/pop operations for the `S-RIGHT` transition? Does the stack grow by one or two elements after this action? An explicit example of the stack contents before and after would be helpful.

2.  The reported dropout search range includes `1.0`. Is this a typo for another value, such as `0.0`?

3.  Could you justify the use of an imprecise, time-based stopping criterion for training instead of a standard, epoch-based early stopping criterion?

4.  Regarding the runtime measurement of 177.6±18 seconds, what is the source of this variance? Is it from different random initializations of the model?

5.  What is the motivation for the asymmetry between `S-LEFT` (which modifies the buffer) and `M-LEFT` (which does not)? Does this reflect a linguistic intuition or a practical implementation choice?
