### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser. The model adapts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but replaces hand-crafted features with learned representations of the complete parser state (stacks and buffer) using four stack LSTMs, following Dyer et al. (2015). The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating superior performance over previous joint models and achieving competitive results with top-performing systems, while being significantly faster.

### Contributions
1.  **Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parsing:** The primary contribution is the successful application of the stack LSTM architecture to the more complex task of joint parsing. While the underlying neural architecture and transition system are adapted from prior work (Dyer et al., 2015; Henderson et al., 2013), this work demonstrates its effectiveness in learning representations for a multi-stack parser state without reliance on traditional, manually engineered feature sets.
2.  **Ablation Study and Performance Analysis:** The paper provides a well-designed set of experiments isolating the contributions of syntactic and semantic components (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint). This analysis offers clear insights into the benefits and drawbacks of joint modeling in this framework, such as the observation that joint training helps in-domain but may overfit for out-of-domain data.
3.  **A Novel Semantic Transition:** The introduction of the `M-SELF(r)` transition to handle self-dependencies in the CoNLL 2009 corpus is a small but necessary methodological extension to the base transition system.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. Achieving performance comparable to top-performing, feature-rich pipeline systems with a purely greedy, featureless model is a significant achievement and highlights the power of the learned state representations.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The parser's speed (177.6s on the CoNLL 2009 English test set) is a major practical advantage over more complex, slower pipeline models that rely on global inference or expensive feature extraction. This makes the system a viable candidate for real-world applications.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 tasks, including in-domain and out-of-domain test sets for English, as well as a multilingual evaluation. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provide valuable controlled comparisons.
4.  **Simplicity of the Approach:** The model's core idea is elegant. By replacing complex feature engineering with a principled method for learning representations of the parser's algorithmic state, it points towards a more general and less labor-intensive direction for designing structured prediction models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Methodological Imprecision:** Several key details of the algorithm and training procedure are vague, hindering reproducibility and a full understanding of the model's mechanics.
    *   The training stopping criterion is described as "when the dev performance does not improve for approximately 6–7 hours of elapsed time." This is not a reproducible scientific standard. An epoch-based patience criterion should have been used and reported.
    *   The hyperparameter tuning process is concerning. The paper states, "Other hyperparameters have been set intuitively; careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." This admission suggests the reported results may not be fully optimized and that the experimental process was not entirely rigorous.
    *   The constraint preventing infinite loops is described as "Repetitive M-SWAP transitions are disallowed." This is ambiguous. Does "repetitive" mean two consecutive swaps, or swapping the same two items back and forth at any point? The exact mechanism must be specified.
2.  **Clarity of the Transition System:** The description of the modified arc-eager system is difficult to follow.
    *   Footnotes 2 and 3 attempt to explain why buffer items are copied rather than moved, but the explanation is muddled and conflates the `S-SHIFT` and `S-RIGHT` actions. A clearer, step-by-step motivation for this crucial deviation from the standard algorithm is needed.
    *   The description of the `M-LEFT(r)` transition in Section 2.2 states "M remains unchanged," but the corresponding entry in Table 1, `(u, u),M`, is non-standard notation and confusing. It is unclear if `u` (the top of the stack) is popped and then pushed back on, or if the notation simply means the stack before the operation. This should be clarified.
    *   The interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions is a core component, but the description is brief. This control flow seems critical, and its design choices warrant more justification than is provided.
3.  **Limitations of the Oracle:** Footnote 5 acknowledges that the transition system cannot perfectly reconstruct the gold parse for 1.5% of English sentences. This is a non-trivial failure rate for an oracle. The paper fails to discuss the implications of this. How are these sentences handled during training? Are they discarded? Does the model train on imperfect transition sequences for these cases? This omission leaves a significant gap in the experimental methodology.
4.  **Unsupported Minor Claims:** The paper contains small but unsupported assertions. For instance, Footnote 1 claims that initializing the buffer with `root` at the end "works better for the arc-eager algorithm." This claim is presented without any citation or empirical evidence from the authors' own experiments. Such statements detract from the paper's overall scientific rigor.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the exact stopping criterion used for training? Was it a fixed number of epochs, or an early stopping mechanism based on a set number of validation steps/epochs without improvement?
2.  Regarding the 1.5% of sentences for which the oracle fails to generate a correct transition sequence: how were these instances handled during training? Were they excluded, or was the model trained on the imperfect sequences produced by the oracle?
3.  Can you provide a more precise definition of the constraint used to disallow "repetitive M-SWAP transitions"?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the control flow that alternates between syntactic and semantic transitions? Was this heuristic tuned, or were other interleaving strategies explored?
