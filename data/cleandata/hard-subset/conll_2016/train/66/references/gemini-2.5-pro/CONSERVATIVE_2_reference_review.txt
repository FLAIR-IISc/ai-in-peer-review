**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a new transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model is based on the incremental algorithm of Henderson et al. (2013) but makes two primary modifications: it uses Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) rather than relying on hand-crafted features, and it employs a purely greedy inference strategy. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms previous joint parsing models. However, it does not achieve state-of-the-art performance, lagging behind established, feature-rich pipeline systems. The main claimed advantages are the model's simplicity (no feature engineering) and its high speed.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the successful application of the Stack LSTM architecture, previously used for syntactic parsing, to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This demonstrates the viability of learning holistic representations of the parser state for this joint task.
2.  A secondary contribution is the development of a fast, greedy, feature-free joint parser that achieves competitive results, particularly when compared to other joint models. The paper includes a valuable set of ablation studies (e.g., syntax-only, semantics-only) that help isolate the effects of the joint modeling within this specific neural framework.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Baseline Improvement:** The proposed model shows a clear and significant performance improvement over the line of work it is directly built upon (Henderson et al., 2008; 2013). This provides convincing evidence that learning a representation of the entire parser state is more effective than using the more limited, feature-based representations of those prior works. This is a solid, incremental contribution.
2.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," and "Hybrid" variants provides a well-controlled experimental setup. These ablations offer useful insights, for instance, confirming that syntax is beneficial for semantics in this framework, and highlighting a potential overfitting problem for the joint model on out-of-domain data, where the pipelined "Hybrid" model performs better.
3.  **Efficiency:** The reported parsing speed is impressive. A fast and reasonably accurate parser is of significant practical value. By eschewing beam search and expensive feature extraction, the authors present a compelling case for the model's utility in real-world applications where speed is a critical constraint.
4.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains its methodology. The approach of replacing complex feature engineering with a learnable representation has an appealing simplicity and elegance, and the multilingual results suggest good portability.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Failure to Surpass State-of-the-Art:** The most significant weakness is that the proposed model does not outperform established state-of-the-art systems on the benchmark tasks. While it surpasses prior *joint* models, it falls short of the top-performing pipeline systems that rely on well-understood, heavily engineered features and more complex inference or reranking. The argument that advances in those systems are "orthogonal" is insufficient; the burden remains on a new method to demonstrate a path to superior performance, not just comparable performance with a different architecture. Without this, the work primarily stands as an engineering contribution (a faster system) rather than a breakthrough in parsing accuracy.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core technical innovation, the Stack LSTM, is adopted directly from Dyer et al. (2015). The contribution here is an application of an existing method to a new, albeit related, problem. While this is a valid and useful contribution, the conceptual novelty is limited. The extension from a single-stack syntactic parser to a multi-stack joint parser is a relatively straightforward architectural adaptation.
3.  **Unjustified Use of Greedy Inference:** The paper emphasizes its use of "entirely greedy inference." While this choice is key to the model's speed, it is presented without sufficient justification or analysis. Standard practice in high-performance transition-based parsing involves beam search. The paper provides no experiments to show how performance is affected by a small beam (e.g., k=5 or 8). It is unclear if the performance gap with SOTA systems is due to the model's representations or this simplified search strategy. A rigorous analysis would compare greedy decoding against beam search to understand this trade-off.
4.  **Insufficient Hyperparameter Optimization:** The authors admit that "hyperparameters have been set intuitively" and that tuning is "expected to yield improvements." For a paper whose contribution rests entirely on empirical results, this is a methodological concern. It raises questions about the robustness of the current results and whether the model's full potential has been realized or properly evaluated. A more systematic approach to hyperparameter tuning is expected for a rigorous empirical study.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide results or analysis on the effect of incorporating a small beam search (e.g., k=4, 8) into your decoder? How does this affect the trade-off between speed and accuracy, and does it help close the performance gap with the state-of-the-art pipeline models?
2.  Your results show that the pipelined "Hybrid" model outperforms the "Joint" model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus. This suggests the joint model is overfitting to the training domain. Do you have any analysis as to why this might be the case, and how this common issue in joint modeling might be mitigated within your framework?
3.  You state that advances in SOTA systems (e.g., global features, optimal decoding) are orthogonal to your model. However, it is not obvious how one would integrate global features from a full parse tree into a greedy, incremental, transition-based model. Could you elaborate on how you envision such an integration might work?
