### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependency structures. The model's primary novelty lies in its use of stack LSTMs to learn a dense, continuous representation of the entire parser state—encompassing separate syntactic and semantic stacks, an input buffer, and an action history—obviating the need for any hand-crafted, feature engineering. The parser adapts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) and extends the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015) to this more complex joint task.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and effective application of stack LSTMs for joint syntactic-semantic parsing.** The paper successfully demonstrates how to extend a powerful representation learning technique from a single-task (syntax) to a multi-task (joint syntax-semantics) setting, learning a holistic representation of a complex algorithmic state.
2.  **State-of-the-art results for joint parsing models.** The proposed model is shown to significantly outperform all previous joint parsing systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. This provides strong empirical evidence for the power of this feature-free, representation-learning approach.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis and a highly efficient system.** The paper includes valuable ablation studies (e.g., Syntax-only, Hybrid) that probe the interactions between the syntactic and semantic components. Furthermore, the greedy nature of the parser makes it extremely fast, presenting a strong practical contribution for real-world applications.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Model Elegance and Simplicity:** The core strength of this work is its clean and elegant model. By replacing complex, manually-designed feature templates with learned representations of the entire parser state, the authors present a conceptually simple yet powerful system. This approach bypasses the notoriously difficult and time-consuming process of feature engineering that characterized previous state-of-the-art parsers, particularly for joint tasks where feature interactions can be complex. The model is end-to-end differentiable and relies on a few well-understood components (LSTMs, composition functions).

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper provides a robust evaluation on two highly competitive shared tasks (CoNLL 2008 and 2009). The key result—outperforming all prior *joint* models—is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the merit of the proposed approach over previous methods that used more limited state representations and feature sets. The fact that this greedy, feature-free system is competitive with many strong, feature-rich pipelined systems from the same era further underscores the effectiveness of the learned representations.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Contextualization:** The authors do an exemplary job of situating their work within the existing literature. They are transparent about the systems their model does *not* outperform and provide clear reasons why (e.g., use of global inference, reranking, or additional training data). The ablation experiments (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint) are particularly insightful. The comparison between the Joint and Hybrid models on in-domain vs. out-of-domain data, for instance, provides a nuanced look at the benefits and potential overfitting risks of joint learning.

4.  **High Practical Utility:** The parser's speed is a major practical advantage. As reported, parsing the entire CoNLL 2009 test set in under three minutes on a single CPU core is impressive and makes the system highly suitable for downstream applications where parsing is a bottleneck. The commitment to release an open-source implementation further strengthens the paper's potential impact on the community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Technical Novelty:** While the application is novel and the results are strong, the core technical components are not new. The work is a direct and logical extension of the stack LSTM parser from Dyer et al. (2015) to the joint parsing framework of Henderson et al. (2013). The primary innovation is in the combination and successful application of these existing ideas to a new, more complex task, rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new modeling technique. This does not diminish the quality of the work but positions it as a strong piece of engineering and empirical research rather than a groundbreaking theoretical advance.

2.  **Gap to Overall State-of-the-Art:** The paper is honest about this, but it remains a limitation that the system does not surpass the top-performing systems of the time, which relied on heavily engineered features and/or global inference/reranking. This suggests that while learned representations can replace a significant amount of feature engineering, they do not (at least in this greedy formulation) fully capture the complex dependencies that global models or very specific features can. This leaves open the question of how much performance is being left on the table by the greedy search strategy.

3.  **Vagueness in Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper states that some hyperparameters were "set intuitively" while others were tuned from a small set. Specifically, key architectural choices like the LSTM hidden dimension, the parser state dimension, and the composition function dimension were all set to 100. Given the sensitivity of neural models to such choices, this lack of rigorous tuning is a minor weakness. It is unclear whether performance could be further improved with a more systematic search, or if the model's capacity is constrained by these intuitive choices.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The comparison between the `Joint` and `Hybrid` models on the out-of-domain Brown test set is very interesting. You attribute the `Hybrid` model's superior performance here to the `Joint` model overfitting to the WSJ training data. Could you elaborate on this? Do you have any analysis (e.g., error analysis) that points to specific types of syntactic-semantic correlations present in WSJ but not Brown that the joint model might be incorrectly learning?

2.  In Section 4, you note that the oracle conversion from a gold semantic graph to a transition sequence is imperfect for 1.5% of English sentences due to crossing arcs. How does this "oracle error" affect training? Is the model being trained on slightly incorrect action sequences for these sentences, and could this be placing a ceiling on the model's performance for sentences with complex, non-projective semantic structures?

3.  Could you provide more rationale for the choice of setting the LSTM hidden state dimension, parser state dimension (`yt`), and composition function dimension all to 100? Was any preliminary experimentation done to guide this "intuitive" choice? For example, is it possible that the composition functions are a bottleneck if their output dimension is the same as the full LSTM summary vectors?
