### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. Its main novelty lies in using stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history) without relying on any hand-crafted indicator features. The model builds upon the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015), adapting them to the joint task. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks and achieves competitive speed and accuracy compared to many top-performing pipelined systems from that era.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **Successful Application of Learned State Representations to Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a neural architecture (stack LSTMs) can effectively learn a holistic representation of a complex, multi-stack parser state for a joint structured prediction task. This successfully replaces the extensive and costly feature engineering common in prior work, particularly the reliance on features extracted from a complete syntactic tree.
2.  **A Fast and Effective Greedy Joint Parser:** The paper presents a complete, high-performing system that is fully greedy and featureless. The engineering of the interleaved transition system, including a novel `M-SELF` action for nominal predicates, combined with the neural model, results in a parser that is significantly faster than contemporary state-of-the-art systems while achieving comparable accuracy.
3.  **Insightful Ablation and Multilingual Analysis:** The experiments on the CoNLL 2009 dataset, which include syntax-only, semantics-only, and a hybrid pipeline variant, provide a clear and controlled analysis of the benefits (and potential pitfalls, like overfitting) of joint modeling within this framework. The strong multilingual results further validate the generality and data-driven nature of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Simplicity:** The proposed model represents a significant step forward in simplifying the parsing pipeline. By replacing complex, hand-engineered feature sets with learned, continuous representations, the model is more general and less reliant on linguistic expertise or costly feature extraction. The architecture is a clean and logical extension of recent work (Dyer et al., 2015) to a more complex, joint task, and its success is a powerful argument for this paradigm.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Efficiency:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art results among joint models and is competitive with strong pipelined systems on both the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 tasks. Crucially, it does so while being remarkably fast due to its greedy nature. The authors are transparent about not outperforming all systems (e.g., those with global inference or extra data), but the performance-to-speed tradeoff is excellent, making the system highly practical.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare against the correct baselines (especially Henderson et al., 2013, which uses a similar transition system but with features and beam search). The inclusion of multiple variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint) on the CoNLL 2009 data is a major strength, as it allows for a nuanced discussion of the interactions between syntax and semantics. The finding that the Hybrid model performs better out-of-domain is an interesting and honestly reported result that points to the complexities of joint learning.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly explaining the transition system, the model architecture, and the experimental setup. The promise to release the code is a welcome contribution to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Novelty of Components:** While the *application* is novel and the results are strong, the core technical components are adapted directly from prior work. The transition system is a modification of Henderson et al. (2013), and the neural architecture is a multi-stack version of Dyer et al. (2015)'s stack LSTM parser. The main contribution is in the successful combination and extension of these existing ideas to the joint parsing domain, rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new parsing algorithm or neural architecture.
2.  **Limitations of Greedy Inference:** The decision to use entirely greedy inference is framed as a benefit for speed, but it is also a significant modeling constraint. The paper does not explore the impact of this choice by, for instance, implementing a beam search. This makes it difficult to disentangle whether the performance gap with top-tier systems is due to limitations in the learned representation or simply due to search errors that a beam could mitigate. The comparison with Henderson et al. (2008), who used beam search, is therefore not entirely apples-to-apples; the current work's superior performance is impressive but could potentially be even higher.
3.  **Under-explored Domain Adaptation Issue:** The result showing the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus is intriguing but not deeply analyzed. The authors suggest overfitting, which is likely correct, but the paper would be stronger with a brief error analysis. What specific phenomena in the WSJ training data lead the joint model astray on out-of-domain text? This is a missed opportunity for deeper insight into the challenges of joint learning.
4.  **Impact of Oracle Conversion Imperfections:** The paper notes in a footnote that the oracle transition sequence for gold parses is imperfect for 1.5% of English sentences. This is a known issue for transition-based systems facing non-local dependencies, but it places a hard ceiling on the model's maximum possible performance. While brief, this point deserves more prominent placement and perhaps a short discussion of its potential impact on training and evaluation, as the model is being trained on data that is sometimes known to be unachievable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The comparison between the greedy inference of this work and the beam search of Henderson et al. (2008) is central. Have you experimented with adding a beam search to your stack LSTM model? This would provide a clearer picture of the power of your learned representations by controlling for the search algorithm and would likely close some of the performance gap to the global, feature-rich models.
2.  Regarding the out-of-domain performance where the Hybrid model beats the Joint model: could you provide any qualitative error analysis? For example, does the Joint model tend to produce syntactically plausible but semantically incorrect attachments on the Brown corpus, suggesting the syntactic signal is overpowering the semantic one in unfamiliar contexts?
3.  The new `M-SELF` transition is a sensible addition to handle self-dependencies in NomBank. Could you quantify its contribution? For example, what is the recall on this specific arc type, and how does the overall semantic F1 score change if this transition is removed from the model?
