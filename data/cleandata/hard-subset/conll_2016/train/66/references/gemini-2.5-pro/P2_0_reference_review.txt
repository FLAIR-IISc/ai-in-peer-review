### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The core idea is to apply the stack LSTM architecture, previously used for syntactic parsing, to learn a dense vector representation of the entire state of a more complex joint parsing algorithm. This learned representation replaces the need for hand-crafted, high-dimensional sparse features traditionally used in such systems. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating strong performance.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **Successful Application of Representation Learning to a Complex Joint Task:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that the stack LSTM model can be successfully extended from a single-stack syntactic parser to a multi-stack (syntactic, semantic, buffer, actions) joint parser. This shows the robustness and scalability of learning representations of entire algorithmic states for complex structured prediction.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Among Joint Models:** The proposed system establishes a new state-of-the-art among strictly joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks. It achieves this with a simple greedy inference procedure and without any task-specific feature engineering, highlighting the power of the learned representations.
3.  **A Fast and Practical System:** The paper provides a compelling argument for the model's practical utility by showing it is significantly faster than competing high-performance systems. The greedy nature of the parser leads to linear-time complexity, making it suitable for real-world applications.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model:** The paper's main strength lies in its clean and well-motivated approach. Instead of designing intricate features to capture interactions between syntax and semantics, the model learns to represent the entire parser state and lets a neural classifier make decisions. This is a powerful demonstration of the "representation learning" paradigm and provides a strong baseline for future neural joint models. The extension from the original stack LSTM to a four-stack system is a non-trivial and successful engineering feat.

2.  **Strong and Well-Positioned Empirical Results:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on standard, competitive benchmarks. They are careful and honest in their claims, showing superiority over previous *joint* systems (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013) while acknowledging the performance gap to the overall state-of-the-art, which often relies on pipelines, complex features, and slower inference. This positioning is appropriate and credible. The multilingual results further demonstrate the model's general applicability.

3.  **Excellent Ablation Study and Analysis:** The experiments on the CoNLL 2009 data, which compare the `Joint` model to `Syntax-only`, `Semantics-only`, and `Hybrid` variants, are particularly insightful. This analysis provides clear evidence for several key conclusions: (i) syntax is beneficial for semantics (Joint/Hybrid > Semantics-only), (ii) the joint objective does not harm syntactic parsing performance, and (iii) the joint model may be prone to overfitting on in-domain data, as shown by the `Hybrid` model's superior performance on the out-of-domain Brown corpus. This is a level of detailed analysis that strengthens the paper significantly.

4.  **High Practical Value:** The reported speed is a major advantage. A 2.5x speedup over a comparable state-of-the-art system makes this parser a highly attractive option for downstream tasks that require fast and accurate linguistic analysis. The commitment to releasing an open-source implementation further enhances the potential impact and reproducibility of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While the application is novel and significant, the core technical components—the stack LSTM and the recursive composition functions—are directly adopted from Dyer et al. (2015). The paper is more of a strong application paper that successfully extends an existing technique to a more complex problem, rather than one that introduces a fundamentally new model or algorithm. The novelty lies in the system architecture and the empirical results, not the underlying neural machinery.

2.  **Predicate Identification is Pipelined:** For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is performed by a separate bidirectional LSTM classifier before the main parser runs. This slightly undermines the "joint model" narrative, as it re-introduces a pipeline step for a key part of the semantic analysis. A truly end-to-end joint model would integrate predicate identification into the transition system itself, for example by having an action that decides whether the token on the buffer is a predicate.

3.  **Significant Performance Gap with Overall State-of-the-Art:** The paper is transparent about this, but the gap remains a notable weakness. The semantic F1 scores are several points behind systems that use global features, reranking, or exact inference (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015). This raises the question of whether the purely greedy, local approach is hitting a performance ceiling. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on whether this gap is fundamental to the greedy approach or could be closed by incorporating a limited beam search, which has proven effective for similar transition-based parsers.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the pipelined predicate identification for CoNLL 2008: Have you considered integrating predicate identification directly into the transition system? For instance, could the `M-PRED(p)` action be modified to first decide *if* the current word is a predicate before choosing its sense? What are the anticipated challenges of such an approach?

2.  The result where the `Hybrid` model outperforms the `Joint` model on out-of-domain data is very interesting. Your analysis suggests the `Joint` model overfits to the WSJ training data. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible that the syntactic parser, when trained alone, learns more general representations that provide a more robust signal for the semantics-only parser, whereas the joint training forces the syntactic representations to become too specialized for the WSJ semantic annotations?

3.  Your model's speed is a key advantage. Did you experiment with beam search? It would be valuable to quantify the trade-off between speed and accuracy for this model. For example, how much does a small beam (e.g., k=5) improve LAS and Semantic F1, and what is the corresponding slowdown? This would help clarify how much of the performance gap to SOTA is due to greedy search versus the model's representations.
