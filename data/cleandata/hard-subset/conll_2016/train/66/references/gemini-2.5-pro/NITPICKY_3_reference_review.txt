### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model extends the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015) to a joint parsing setting, largely following the synchronized transition algorithm of Henderson et al. (2013). The primary novelty lies in using learned representations of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) to make greedy parsing decisions, thereby obviating the need for expert-crafted features derived from a complete syntactic parse.

As I see them, the contributions are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that the stack LSTM architecture can be effectively adapted to the task of joint syntactic-semantic parsing, outperforming previous joint models that rely on more local features or beam search.
2.  A thorough set of ablation experiments (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that effectively isolates the contribution of the joint modeling from the underlying neural architecture, including an interesting finding regarding performance on out-of-domain data.
3.  The introduction of a new transition, `M-SELF`, to handle the self-dependencies present in the CoNLL 2009 dataset, a minor but necessary modification to the transition system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance on Joint Models:** The proposed model establishes a new state of the art among *joint* parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 tasks. It convincingly outperforms the models it is most directly based on (Henderson et al., 2008; 2013), providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of learning a representation of the entire parser state.
2.  **Well-Designed Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid models provides valuable insight. The comparison between the Joint and Hybrid models, particularly the performance reversal on in-domain vs. out-of-domain data, is an interesting result that highlights a potential overfitting issue and warrants further investigation. This analysis strengthens the paper significantly.
3.  **Efficiency:** The reported parsing speed (177.6±18 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 test set) is a significant practical advantage over more complex, feature-rich pipeline systems or models requiring expensive inference. The claim of practicality is well-supported.
4.  **Clarity of Model Description:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written. The description of the transition system, the stack LSTM architecture, and the composition functions is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core contribution is the combination of two existing lines of work: the joint transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM of Dyer et al. (2015). While this is a valuable engineering effort with strong results, the conceptual advance is somewhat incremental. The model is a direct, albeit non-trivial, application of a known technique to a related problem.
2.  **Unjustified Hyperparameter Selection:** The statement "Other hyperparameters have been set intuitively; careful tuning is expected to yield improvements" is a significant methodological concern. Rigorous research requires a more principled approach to hyperparameter selection than intuition. This admission undermines the claim that the presented results are optimal for this architecture and weakens the reproducibility of the work. For example, were the LSTM dimensions, embedding sizes, or composition function dimensions tuned at all? A grid search or Bayesian optimization, even over a small range, would be more convincing.
3.  **In-elegance and Limitations of the Transition System:**
    *   The modification to the standard arc-eager algorithm, where S-SHIFT and S-RIGHT *copy* an item from the buffer rather than *moving* it, feels like an artificial constraint imposed by the interleaved syntactic/semantic decision process. This is explained in footnote 2 and reiterated in footnote 3, but it complicates the algorithm and deviates from a well-understood parsing strategy. The implications of having the same item exist on both the buffer and the syntactic stack are not discussed.
    *   Footnote 5 notes that the oracle transition sequence fails to perfectly encode the gold parse for 1.5% of English sentences. This is a fundamental limitation of the chosen transition system. It places a theoretical ceiling on performance and means the model is trained on imperfect or unobtainable action sequences for a subset of the data. This deserves more than a passing mention in a footnote.
4.  **Minor Clarity and Formatting Issues:**
    *   In Table 1, the notation for composed structures (e.g., `gs(u,v, l)`) is used before the composition functions themselves are defined in Section 3.3. This forces the reader to jump ahead to understand the table.
    *   The citation style is slightly informal and inconsistent. The use of "inter alia" in the first section is unusual for a formal publication.
    *   Figure 3 is quite dense. While it conveys the general architecture, the specific state depicted is difficult to parse without cross-referencing the supplementary material, limiting its effectiveness as a standalone figure.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the "intuitive" setting of hyperparameters: Could you please provide more detail on this process? Were any preliminary experiments run to guide this intuition? Given that results can be highly sensitive to these settings, how confident are you that the reported performance is representative of the model's full potential and not just a fortunate configuration?
2.  The modification of the arc-eager S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT actions to copy instead of move items from the buffer is a key deviation. Did you explore alternative transition systems that would not require an item to be simultaneously available to both the syntactic stack and the semantic action sequence? For instance, could all semantic actions pertaining to a word be performed *before* it is moved from the buffer? What are the algorithmic trade-offs?
3.  The 1.5% oracle failure rate seems non-trivial. How are these sentences handled during training? Are they simply excluded, or is the model trained on the imperfect transition sequence? Could this be a contributing factor to the performance gap between your greedy model and the top-performing systems that use global inference over the entire structure?
