### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a greedy, transition-based model for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The core innovation is the use of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state, including syntactic and semantic stacks, the input buffer, and the history of actions. This allows the model to make parsing decisions without any hand-crafted, expert features. The proposed system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it is shown to outperform previous joint parsing models and achieve performance competitive with contemporaneous pipeline systems, while being significantly faster.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A successful, feature-free model for joint parsing:** The paper effectively extends the Stack LSTM parsing framework of Dyer et al. (2015) to the more complex task of joint syntactic-semantic analysis. It demonstrates that representation learning can automatically capture the necessary information from the entire parser state, obviating the need for expensive, manually-designed features that were central to previous systems.
2.  **Strong empirical validation and analysis:** The work provides a thorough evaluation on standard benchmarks, establishing a new state of the art among joint models at the time. The ablation studies (e.g., `Syntax-only`, `Hybrid`) offer valuable insights into the benefits and trade-offs of the joint approach, such as the interesting result that a pipelined `Hybrid` model generalizes better to out-of-domain data.
3.  **A fast and practical parsing system:** By relying on greedy inference, the parser is highly efficient. The reported speed is a significant practical advantage over more complex, slower models, making it a valuable contribution for real-world applications.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Method:** The approach of representing the complete algorithmic state with Stack LSTMs is a clean and powerful idea. It moves away from the brittle and labor-intensive process of feature engineering that characterized earlier transition-based parsers. This is a significant conceptual step forward, showing how to apply modern representation learning techniques to a complex, structured prediction task involving multiple interacting components (syntax and semantics).
2.  **Impressive Performance Gains over Baselines:** The model consistently and significantly outperforms its most direct predecessor, the feature-based joint parser of Henderson et al. (2013), across all metrics. This provides compelling evidence for the superiority of learning representations of the entire parser state over using features from a more localized context (e.g., top of the stacks). Achieving results comparable to the top pipeline systems of the era without any syntactic path features is a major accomplishment.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experiments:** The inclusion of `Syntax-only`, `Semantics-only`, and `Hybrid` model variants is a major strength. This controlled comparison allows for a clear analysis of the interactions between the syntactic and semantic components. It confirms that joint training helps semantics (Joint > Semantics-only) without hurting syntax much, and reveals an interesting domain adaptation behavior where the `Hybrid` model is more robust out-of-domain.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The transition system, model architecture, and training procedure are described with sufficient detail. The authors' commitment to releasing an open-source implementation is commendable and greatly increases the value and impact of the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty over Prior Work:** The core technical mechanism, the Stack LSTM, is directly adopted from Dyer et al. (2015). The primary novelty lies in the *application* of this technique to a joint task, which involved designing an interleaved transition system and using separate stacks. While this is a non-trivial engineering and modeling effort, the fundamental neural architecture is not new. The paper could be more explicit in positioning its contribution as a successful extension and application of an existing method.
2.  **Significant Gap to the State-of-the-Art:** While the authors are transparent about this, the model's performance is substantially below that of top-performing systems, particularly more recent ones that use global inference, richer features, or reranking. The authors argue these advances are orthogonal, but this also highlights the limitations of a purely greedy approach. The results suggest that while representation learning is powerful, it may not be a complete substitute for more powerful search or global modeling techniques.
3.  **Inability to Handle Certain Semantic Structures:** The paper notes that the transition system cannot perfectly oracle-decode 1.5% of English sentences due to complex crossing dependencies. This is a fundamental limitation of the chosen transition-based framework. It means the model is trained on imperfect data and is structurally incapable of producing the correct output for a subset of the test data. The impact of this oracle limitation on the final reported scores is not analyzed, making it hard to assess how much performance is lost due to the model versus the framework itself.
4.  **Oversimplified Handling of Unseen Predicates:** The strategy for handling predicates not seen during training is to default to the "01" sense. While this may be a reasonable heuristic, its actual impact is not evaluated. It is unclear what percentage of test set predicate *tokens* (as opposed to types) are unseen and must rely on this fallback, or how model performance degrades on sentences containing them. This is a potentially important source of error that is not explored.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The `Hybrid` model outperforms the `Joint` model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus, which you attribute to overfitting in the joint model. Could you elaborate on this hypothesis? Why might a joint model be more susceptible to overfitting to the source domain (WSJ) than a pipeline model where syntax is fixed? Does this finding suggest a potential general weakness for joint learning in domain adaptation scenarios?
2.  Regarding the imperfect semantic oracle, what is the oracle F1 score of your transition system on the development set? Providing this upper bound would help contextualize the reported results and better disentangle errors made by the statistical model from the inherent representational limitations of the transition system.
3.  The performance on Japanese is notably lower than on other languages, which you attribute to the small dataset size. However, the model also performs well on German, which has a similarly sized training set in the CoNLL 2009 data. Could other linguistic factors, such as a higher degree of non-projectivity or different argument-structure patterns, be contributing to the lower performance for Japanese? Has there been any error analysis to this effect?
