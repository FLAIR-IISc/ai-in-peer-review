### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model is a significant evolution of the synchronized approach of Henderson et al. (2013), replacing hand-crafted features with learned representations of the entire parser state—syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history—using stack LSTMs. The core contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature-Free Joint Parser:** The primary contribution is the successful application of stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) to the complex state of a joint syntactic-semantic parser. By composing vector summaries of all data structures, the model learns to make parsing decisions without any expert-designed feature templates, demonstrating that rich representations can supplant feature engineering in this joint task.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance for Joint Models:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that their approach outperforms all previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. The model is also shown to be competitive with many top-performing, feature-rich pipeline systems of that era, while being substantially faster.
3.  **Rigorous Ablation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a well-designed set of experiments with "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," and "Hybrid" variants of their model. This analysis provides valuable insights into the bidirectional influence of syntax and semantics in their framework and reveals interesting phenomena, such as the joint model's tendency to overfit to the training domain compared to a pipeline variant.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model:** The architectural design is a clean and logical extension of prior work. It takes the algorithmic framework of Henderson et al. (2013) and the representation learning technique of Dyer et al. (2015) and combines them into a cohesive and powerful system. This move away from manually specified features on stack/buffer configurations towards a holistic, learned state representation is a significant conceptual and practical simplification.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Honest Comparison:** The results are compelling. Outperforming prior joint models like Henderson et al. (2013) clearly shows the benefit of the stack LSTM representation. The authors are also commendably transparent about their system's performance relative to the absolute state-of-the-art, correctly identifying that orthogonal advances (e.g., global inference, extra training data, sophisticated feature engineering) are responsible for the remaining performance gap. This contextualizes their contribution appropriately.

3.  **Excellent Analysis via Ablation Studies:** The experiments on the CoNLL 2009 dataset, which compare the full joint model to syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid pipeline versions, are a major strength. This controlled comparison allows for clear conclusions: syntax is crucial for semantic performance (Joint/Hybrid vs. Semantics-only), and joint training offers benefits on in-domain data but can be brittle on out-of-domain data (Joint vs. Hybrid). This is a valuable scientific contribution beyond simply presenting a new high-performing model.

4.  **Efficiency:** The reported parsing speed is a significant practical advantage. By relying on greedy inference and cheap learned representations, the system is much faster than pipeline models that require expensive feature extraction from a complete syntactic tree. This makes the model a strong candidate for practical applications where both speed and quality are important.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** While the application of stack LSTMs to this problem is new and effective, the core neural machinery is directly inherited from Dyer et al. (2015). The contribution lies more in the successful engineering and integration of these components for a new, more complex task rather than in proposing a fundamentally new learning algorithm or neural architecture. The paper could be strengthened by a deeper discussion of the challenges posed by the joint task and how the stack LSTM architecture was adapted or found to be sufficient.

2.  **Pipelined Component for Predicate Identification:** For the CoNLL 2008 task, predicate identification is performed by a separate, standalone BiLSTM classifier before the main parser runs. This slightly contradicts the "joint model" narrative, as it introduces a pipeline step. While this is a pragmatic and effective solution, it leaves an open question about whether this initial decision could be more tightly integrated into the joint transition system, which would make the model more truly "end-to-end."

3.  **Strictly Greedy Inference:** The paper highlights greedy inference as a key design choice that ensures speed. However, this is also a significant limitation. No experiments are presented to explore the trade-off between speed and accuracy using a simple beam search. Given the power of the learned representations, it is highly likely that even a small beam could correct local errors and yield a substantial boost in accuracy for a relatively small cost in speed. Without this analysis, it is difficult to know if the performance gap with top systems is due to the model's representational capacity or simply the limitations of the greedy search.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the CoNLL 2008 setup: Can you elaborate on the decision to use a separate BiLSTM for predicate identification? Did you consider integrating this decision into the transition system, for instance by adding transitions that propose a word as a predicate? What would be the main challenges of such an approach (e.g., action space explosion, sparse rewards)?

2.  The comparison between the `Joint` and `Hybrid` models on out-of-domain data is very interesting. Your conclusion is that the joint model overfits. Do you have any more specific hypotheses as to *why*? For example, is it possible that the syntactic representations learned in the joint model become biased towards resolving ambiguities that are helpful for SRL on WSJ text, but this bias is detrimental when parsing a different domain like Brown?

3.  Could you provide results or discussion on the impact of a small beam search (e.g., beam size 2, 4, or 8) on both performance and speed? This would provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities and help disentangle the effects of the representation from the effects of the search algorithm. It seems this would be a crucial experiment for understanding the model's full potential.
