### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model's core novelty lies in its use of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state—encompassing separate syntactic and semantic stacks, an input buffer, and an action history. This approach completely eschews the need for hand-crafted, feature-based representations of the parser state, which were central to previous models. The proposed model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it establishes a new state of the art for joint parsing models and achieves performance competitive with many top-performing pipeline systems, while being significantly faster.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A successful feature-free approach to joint parsing:** The paper provides a compelling demonstration that learned representations of the complete parser state, via Stack LSTMs, are powerful enough to replace the complex, manually engineered features common in previous high-performing joint and pipelined systems. This is a significant finding for incremental parsing.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance for joint models:** The model significantly outperforms prior joint parsing systems (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; 2013) on standard benchmarks, validating the effectiveness of the proposed representation learning approach.
3.  **A fast and effective parsing system:** By relying on purely greedy inference, the parser is linear in time and very fast in practice. Achieving results comparable to much slower, feature-rich systems makes this work a valuable contribution for practical applications.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model:** The paper’s main strength is its clean and effective architecture. Instead of relying on an intricate set of hand-designed features to capture relevant information from the parser's history, the model learns to encode the entire state of its constituent data structures (stacks, buffer) into fixed-size vectors. This is a well-motivated application of recent advances in representation learning and provides a strong argument for moving away from feature engineering in structured prediction tasks.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The model sets a new performance bar for joint parsing systems on the CoNLL 2008/2009 tasks. The authors are commendably transparent about where their model stands relative to the overall state-of-the-art, correctly noting that it does not surpass top pipeline systems that use global features or inference. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are particularly insightful, providing a clear analysis of the interactions between syntactic and semantic parsing within this framework.
3.  **High Efficiency:** The model's speed is a significant practical advantage. The reported runtime is impressive and makes the system a viable component for downstream NLP applications that require large-scale processing. The direct comparison to the runtime of Lei et al. (2015) effectively highlights this strength.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The transition system, model architecture, and experimental setup are described with clarity and precision. The promise to release an open-source implementation is a welcome contribution to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Performance Gap with Top Pipeline Systems:** While the authors are upfront about this, it remains a key limitation. The model does not outperform state-of-the-art systems that use features from a complete syntactic parse (e.g., path features) and/or global inference. This suggests that the information captured by the greedy, incremental state representation, while powerful, may not be a full substitute for global structural information, particularly for complex semantic dependencies. This raises questions about the upper bound of performance for this class of greedy models.
2.  **Incremental Methodological Contribution:** The core technical components—the synchronized transition system and the Stack LSTM—are adapted from Henderson et al. (2013) and Dyer et al. (2015), respectively. While the combination and application to this joint task are novel and successful, the work could be viewed as a well-executed but incremental extension of existing methods rather than a fundamental breakthrough in parsing algorithms or neural architectures.
3.  **Inherent Limitations of the Transition System:** The model inherits limitations from its transition-based foundation. It must pseudo-projectivize syntactic trees, and more importantly, it cannot perfectly oracle-decode all semantic graphs due to complex crossing dependencies (affecting 1.5% of English sentences). While the `M-SWAP` and new `M-SELF` actions help, this oracle error places a ceiling on the model's potential performance that is not present in graph-based parsers. The impact of this limitation is not fully quantified.
4.  **"Intuitive" Hyperparameter Selection:** The authors state that many hyperparameters were set "intuitively" and that further tuning would likely yield improvements. While this is often true, the lack of a more systematic hyperparameter search (e.g., grid search on key parameters like hidden dimensions) somewhat weakens the reported results. It is difficult to assess whether the performance gap with other systems is due to fundamental model limitations or suboptimal tuning.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The analysis of out-of-domain performance, where the Hybrid (pipeline) model outperforms the Joint model, is interesting. You suggest this is due to the Joint model overfitting to WSJ. Could you expand on this? Does this imply a fundamental trade-off where joint learning creates representations that are less transferable across domains compared to a more modular pipelined approach? Have you explored any techniques to improve the Joint model's robustness to domain shift?
2.  Regarding the imperfect oracle for semantic transition sequences (affecting 1.5% of sentences), can you provide an "oracle" score on the development set? This would help clarify the model's upper bound and contextualize its performance by showing how much error is attributable to the model versus the inherent limitations of the transition system.
3.  The `M-SELF` transition is a novel addition to handle self-dependencies. How critical is this transition to the model's performance on the CoNLL 2009 data? An ablation study removing this action would be valuable to understand its impact.
