### Review

**Summary of the paper and its contributions**

This paper introduces a novel transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model is built upon the algorithmic framework of Henderson et al. (2013) but makes a radical departure from prior work by completely eliminating hand-crafted features. Instead, it uses four separate Stack LSTMs to learn a single, dense vector representation of the entire parser state—the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. Decisions are made greedily based on this learned representation. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it surpasses all previous joint models and achieves performance competitive with strong, feature-engineered pipelined systems, while being significantly faster.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new paradigm for joint parsing:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating that a holistic, learned representation of a parser's algorithmic state is sufficient for high-performance joint syntactic and semantic parsing. This work compellingly argues that the complex feature engineering that dominated previous systems can be replaced by a principled representation learning approach.
2.  **A fast and simple high-performance parser:** By combining a greedy inference strategy with a feature-free neural model, the authors have produced a parser that is not only conceptually elegant but also highly efficient and practical.
3.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, including crucial ablations (e.g., syntax-only, semantics-only) that convincingly situate the model's performance and highlight the benefits of the joint approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** The paper's primary strength is its bold conceptual leap. It moves the field away from the Sisyphean task of designing and extracting complex, often computationally expensive, features for joint modeling. The core idea—that a Stack LSTM can summarize the entire history of parsing decisions and structures into a compact representation—is powerful and elegant. This is not merely an application of LSTMs to a new task; it is a thoughtful integration of a neural architecture that is perfectly suited to the stack-based data structures of the underlying parsing algorithm. This approach sets a new direction for research in parsing.
2.  **Model Simplicity and Efficiency:** The commitment to a purely greedy, feature-free model is a major strength. This design choice results in a system that is not only asymptotically linear but also extremely fast in practice, as evidenced by the runtime comparison. In an era where many state-of-the-art systems rely on massive models and slow decoding (e.g., beam search, reranking, or exact inference), this work provides a refreshing and compelling case for the power of simple, fast, and strong models. The authors' choice to prioritize this direction over squeezing out the last fraction of an F1 point is commendable.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results for a Novel Approach:** Crucially, the conceptual novelty is backed by strong results. The parser significantly outperforms previous joint models, demonstrating the clear superiority of its representation learning approach over earlier attempts with more limited feature sets. Even more impressively, it is competitive with top-performing *pipelined* systems from the shared tasks, despite being denied access to the global syntactic features those systems rely on. This shows that the information needed for semantic analysis can be effectively captured and propagated through the learned state representation during the incremental parse.
4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants provides valuable insight. These controlled experiments clearly demonstrate that a) the syntactic parser is strong in its own right, b) syntactic information is beneficial for semantics, and c) joint learning offers a tangible benefit over a pipelined approach (at least on in-domain data). This level of analysis strengthens the paper's conclusions significantly.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:** The most apparent weakness is that the model's performance, while strong, does not match the absolute state-of-the-art systems that employ global inference, richer feature sets, or additional annotated data. The authors are commendably transparent about this. However, from my perspective, this is an entirely acceptable—and frankly, expected—trade-off for the substantial gains in speed, simplicity, and the elimination of manual feature engineering. The paper is not claiming to be a new SOTA but to be a new *way* of building parsers, and it makes that case convincingly. This is less a flaw and more a clear positioning of the work's contribution.
2.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** One could argue that the core architectural component (the Stack LSTM) and the transition system are adapted from prior work. However, this view overlooks the main contribution. The novelty lies not in inventing a new RNN cell but in the insightful synthesis of these components to tackle the significantly more complex problem of joint syntax and semantics. Demonstrating that this architecture scales effectively to a joint task with multiple interacting stacks is a non-trivial and important finding.
3.  **Heuristic for Unseen Predicates:** The method for handling unseen predicates (defaulting to the ".01" sense) is a simple and pragmatic heuristic. While it appears to work reasonably well, it feels somewhat disconnected from the sophisticated representation learning used in the rest of the model. This is a very minor point and does not detract from the core contributions, but a more integrated solution could be an area for future work.

**Questions to Authors**

The paper introduces a new `M-SELF` transition to handle nominal predicates that are their own arguments, noting this is a non-trivial phenomenon (6.68% of semantic arcs). Could you provide any analysis on the impact of this new transition? For instance, what is the recall on this specific arc type, and how does a model trained without this transition perform? This would help clarify the contribution of this specific algorithmic modification.
