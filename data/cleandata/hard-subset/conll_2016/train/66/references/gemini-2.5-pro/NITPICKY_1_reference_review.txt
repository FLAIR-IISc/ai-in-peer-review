### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model extends the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015) to represent the complete state of a joint parser—comprising syntactic and semantic stacks, a buffer, and an action history—without reliance on hand-crafted features. The authors evaluate the model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms previous joint models and is competitive with many pipeline systems from the shared tasks, while being significantly faster than more recent state-of-the-art models.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A straightforward but effective application of the stack LSTM architecture to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, including the introduction of a new transition for self-dependencies.
2.  An empirical demonstration that a greedy, feature-free model with learned state representations can surpass older, more complex joint models on standard benchmarks.
3.  A useful set of ablation experiments (e.g., Syntax-only, Hybrid) that help quantify the benefits of the joint approach within this specific modeling framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance Against Relevant Baselines:** The model achieves its primary goal of outperforming previous joint models like Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) by a significant margin on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. This clearly shows the benefit of learning a representation of the entire parser state over more locally-scoped feature models.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The parser's speed is a significant practical advantage. The reported runtime of ~3 minutes on the CoNLL 2009 test set is impressive and makes the system a viable option for downstream applications, a notable strength compared to slower, globally optimized models.

3.  **Informative Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid model variants provides valuable insights. The comparison between the Joint and Hybrid models, in particular, offers a nuanced look at how joint training affects in-domain vs. out-of-domain performance, highlighting a potential overfitting issue.

### Weaknesses

Despite its promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, a lack of methodological rigor, and imprecise writing that collectively undermine its scientific contribution and call its results into question.

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Rigor in Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier conference. The authors explicitly state, "Other hyperparameters have been set intuitively; careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." This admission is a major weakness. Scientific results must be based on a principled and reproducible methodology, not "intuition." Furthermore, the training stopping criterion—"when the dev performance does not improve for approximately 6–7 hours"—is inexact, hardware-dependent, and not reproducible. These issues cast serious doubt on the reliability and optimality of the reported results.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision in Model Description:** The paper's exposition is frequently confusing and imprecise.
    *   The crucial modification of using `copy` instead of `move` for buffer operations is explained across two fragmented footnotes (§2.2, footnotes 2 and 3), rather than being clearly presented in the main text.
    *   The notation in Table 1 (e.g., `gs`, `gm`) is used before it is defined in Section 3.3, forcing the reader to jump ahead. The format `(v, v)` for stack items is never explicitly defined.
    *   Figure 3 is incomprehensible without the supplementary material it references, rendering it useless for a self-contained paper.
    *   The description of the "Hybrid" model in §4.2 appears contradictory. It states that the model trains on predicted syntax, but then claims it "does not offer the entire syntactic tree for feature extraction." A pipelined model by definition uses the complete output of the first stage, so this description is either incorrect or exceptionally poorly explained.

3.  **Key Limitations and Details are Glossed Over:** The authors downplay or ignore important details and limitations of their approach.
    *   The authors acknowledge that their oracle translation from gold parses to transition sequences fails for 1.5% of sentences (Footnote 5). This is a non-trivial error rate inherent to the chosen transition system. The potential impact of this "oracle imperfection" on model training and performance is a significant methodological concern that is mentioned and then immediately dismissed.
    *   The claim of using "no expert-crafted features at all" (§1) is an overstatement. The model explicitly uses predicted POS tags as input, and the predicate identification component for CoNLL 2008 uses both lemmas and POS tags. This kind of imprecise, absolutist claim weakens the paper's credibility.

4.  **Incomplete and Opaque Reporting of Results:** The results section contains unsupported claims and omits key data points, preventing a full assessment.
    *   In §5, the authors claim their "Syntax-only model performs slightly better than Dyer et al. (2015)," but the corresponding LAS score for this model is conspicuously missing from Table 3. A direct claim of superiority must be substantiated with data in the table.
    *   Similarly, the paper states that the Chinese parser's superior performance relies on pretrained embeddings and that without them performance was "on par with the others," but this crucial ablation result is "not shown." Important findings should be presented as data, not anecdotes.

In summary, while the core idea is sound, the execution and presentation are rushed and lack the meticulousness required for publication. The combination of methodological weaknesses, unclear descriptions, and incomplete reporting makes it difficult to trust the results or build upon this work confidently.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the experimental methodology? Specifically, what does it mean for hyperparameters to be set "intuitively"? Can you provide a more standard and reproducible stopping criterion than "6-7 hours of elapsed time"?

2.  Please clarify the mechanics of the "Hybrid" model. Does the semantic model have access to the full, pre-predicted syntactic tree during its training, or only to a partial parse state, as your description seems to imply? If the latter, how is this technically achieved?

3.  Why was the LAS score for your Syntax-only model omitted from Table 3? Please provide this number to substantiate your claim of outperforming Dyer et al. (2015).
