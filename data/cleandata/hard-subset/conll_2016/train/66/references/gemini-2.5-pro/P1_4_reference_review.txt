### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a greedy, transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The core innovation is the use of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state—comprising syntactic and semantic stacks, an input buffer, and an action history—without relying on any hand-crafted features. The model is an extension of the synchronized algorithm of Henderson et al. (2013), but replaces its feature-based classifier with a neural architecture that learns to compose representations of parse fragments. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating strong performance and high efficiency.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **An effective feature-free model for joint parsing:** The primary contribution is successfully extending the Stack LSTM architecture, previously used for syntactic parsing, to the more complex joint syntactic-semantic task. This demonstrates that representation learning can effectively replace intricate feature engineering for joint structured prediction, providing a powerful and elegant modeling paradigm.
2.  **State-of-the-art results for a joint model:** The paper establishes a new state of the art for *joint* models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. It significantly outperforms previous joint systems, including the one whose transition algorithm it is based on (Henderson et al., 2013), validating the superiority of the learned state representation.
3.  **A fast, practical parser with insightful analysis:** The greedy, feature-free approach results in a very efficient system, a significant practical advantage. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid vs. Joint) are well-designed and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between joint and pipelined architectures, particularly concerning in-domain versus out-of-domain performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The paper's core idea of learning a continuous representation of the entire parser state is clean and compelling. It moves away from the brittle and labor-intensive process of feature engineering that characterized previous transition-based parsers. This is a significant conceptual advance and aligns well with the modern direction of NLP research.
2.  **Strong and Well-Supported Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are convincing. The model consistently outperforms prior joint parsing systems across multiple standard datasets. The comparisons are fair and well-contextualized, acknowledging where the model stands relative to both older joint systems and more recent, computationally heavier pipelined systems.
3.  **Excellent Ablation Study:** The comparison between the Joint, Hybrid, Semantics-only, and Syntax-only models in Section 4.2 is a major strength. It provides a controlled analysis that clearly disentangles the contributions of the syntactic and semantic components. The finding that the Hybrid model performs better out-of-domain is particularly interesting and sheds light on the potential for joint models to overfit to domain-specific correlations between syntax and semantics.
4.  **High Efficiency:** The parser's speed is a key practical advantage. By reporting the end-to-end runtime and comparing it against a recent state-of-the-art system, the authors provide concrete evidence that their approach is not just accurate but also highly practical for real-world applications where processing speed is a concern.
5.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, with clear explanations of the transition system, model architecture, and experimental setup. The promise to release an open-source implementation is commendable and will make this strong baseline easily accessible to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** While the statistical model is novel, the underlying transition system is largely adopted from Henderson et al. (2013), with the addition of a single new transition (M-SELF). The contribution is therefore primarily in the application of a known neural architecture (Stack LSTM) to an existing algorithmic framework, rather than a fundamental innovation in parsing algorithms.
2.  **Gap with Global, Feature-Rich Systems:** The authors are transparent that their performance trails state-of-the-art systems that use global inference or heavily engineered features from a full syntactic parse. While the paper's focus on a greedy, feature-free model is valid, it would benefit from a deeper discussion of *why* this gap exists. Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., long-distance dependencies) that the learned representations fail to capture as effectively as explicit path features from a complete tree? This would provide a more nuanced understanding of the limitations of the incremental approach.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper demonstrates *that* the model works well, but offers little insight into *what* the learned state representations capture. An analysis of the learned vectors or attention mechanisms (if applicable) could have provided a deeper understanding of how the model implicitly encodes syntactic information for semantic decisions. For example, does the syntactic stack summary `st` learn to represent concepts like grammatical relations or path features that are explicit in other models? This is a missed opportunity for deeper scientific insight.
4.  **Incomplete Hyperparameter Optimization:** The authors state that some hyperparameters were "set intuitively" and that performance could likely be improved with more careful tuning. While understandable, this slightly weakens the claims about the model's peak performance and makes it harder to assess whether the reported results represent the full potential of the architecture.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The out-of-domain results show the Hybrid model outperforming the Joint model, which you attribute to overfitting. Could you elaborate on this? Does this suggest a fundamental challenge for joint learning in this greedy framework, or could this overfitting be mitigated with other techniques like multi-task learning, domain-adversarial training, or different regularization strategies?
2.  Your novel M-SELF transition is introduced to handle a specific and relatively frequent phenomenon (6.68% of semantic arcs in the English data). Could you provide an analysis or ablation on its impact? How much does this single transition contribute to the overall semantic F1 score?
3.  The model is impressively fast. Could you clarify if the reported runtime of 177.6 seconds includes the predicate identification step for the CoNLL 2008 task, or if it is solely for parsing the CoNLL 2009 data where predicates are given? Also, does this timing include loading the model?
