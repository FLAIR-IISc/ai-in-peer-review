### Reviewer #2

**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based framework. The primary novelty lies in its application of Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), thereby eliminating the need for manually engineered features. The proposed model uses a purely greedy search algorithm. The authors evaluate their system on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating superior performance over previous joint models and achieving competitive results against other top systems from that period, while being significantly faster than many state-of-the-art pipelines.

**Contributions**
As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The successful application of the recently proposed Stack LSTM architecture to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, extending the work of Dyer et al. (2015).
2.  An empirical demonstration that learned representations of the complete algorithmic state can supplant traditional, feature-engineered approaches in a greedy, transition-based joint parser, achieving strong results on standard benchmarks.
3.  A thorough ablation study that isolates the contributions of the syntactic and semantic components within the joint model.

**Strengths**
1.  **Methodological Soundness within its Paradigm:** The paper builds directly upon established work in transition-based parsing (Henderson et al., 2013) and neural parsing (Dyer et al., 2015). The extension is logical, and the implementation appears to be solid. The experimental setup is rigorous, including multiple variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that provide a controlled analysis of the model's components. This is good scientific practice.
2.  **Clear Improvement Over Predecessor Models:** The results clearly show that this approach significantly outperforms the Henderson et al. line of work, which serves as its direct inspiration and primary baseline. This demonstrates a concrete, measurable advance for this specific class of joint, transition-based models.
3.  **Efficiency:** The model's speed is a significant practical advantage. By relying on a greedy search and avoiding expensive feature extraction, the parser is substantially faster than many top-performing systems. The authors provide a direct comparison to a recent state-of-the-art system, substantiating this claim. This makes the system a valuable baseline for future work where speed is a consideration.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core technical machinery—the Stack LSTM—is taken directly from Dyer et al. (2015). The transition system is largely adopted from Henderson et al. (2013). While the combination is new for this specific joint task, the work is more of an application of existing methods than the introduction of a new fundamental technique. The contribution is primarily empirical.
2.  **Comparison Against an Incomplete Set of Baselines:** The authors claim to outperform "all previous joint parsing models," but candidly admit they do not match the performance of top systems that use pipelined architectures with expert-crafted features (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010) or more powerful decoding (Täckström et al., 2015). This framing is problematic. The ultimate goal is to build the best parser, regardless of architecture. By focusing the comparison on a subclass of (joint) models that were already not state-of-the-art, the paper overstates its standing. A convincing argument for a new approach requires demonstrating superiority over the strongest existing methods, not just those that are most methodologically similar. The work presents a faster, simpler model that is less accurate than the state of the art, which is a valid but different contribution than what is implied.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper is missing a deeper analysis of the model's behavior. While the quantitative results are strong, there is no discussion of *why* learning the entire parser state is beneficial. What specific parsing phenomena does this model handle better than models with more local features? Conversely, where does the greedy, feature-free approach fail compared to the feature-rich, globally optimized models? An error analysis would be crucial for understanding the trade-offs involved and would provide more valuable insights than simply reporting scores.
4.  **Insufficient Rigor in Hyperparameter Selection:** The authors state that "hyperparameters have been set intuitively," with only dropout rate and action embedding size being tuned. This is a significant concern for a paper reliant on a complex neural architecture. This lack of rigorous tuning raises questions about the reported results' optimality and reproducibility. It is difficult to assess whether the performance is due to the model's architecture or fortunate "intuitive" choices.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The central premise is that representation learning can replace feature engineering. However, your system does not achieve the performance of the top feature-engineered systems from the CoNLL 2009 era. Could you clarify whether the primary contribution is a new state-of-the-art for joint models, or a demonstration of a speed/accuracy trade-off? If the latter, it would strengthen the paper to frame it as such and provide a more direct analysis of this trade-off against the top pipelined systems.
2.  Could you provide some qualitative examples or a brief error analysis? For instance, are there specific long-distance dependencies or complex SRL structures that your model captures better than Henderson et al. (2013) due to the global state representation provided by the Stack LSTMs?
3.  Regarding the claim that advances in SOTA systems are "orthogonal" and can be integrated: this seems speculative without evidence. For example, many features used in top systems (e.g., path features) rely on a complete syntactic tree, which is unavailable in your greedy, incremental setup. Could you elaborate on how such features could be integrated in a non-trivial way?
