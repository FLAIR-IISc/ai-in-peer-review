### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model's architecture is based on the stack LSTM, extending the work of Dyer et al. (2015) to a joint task. It represents the entire state of the parser—comprising a syntactic stack, a semantic stack, an input buffer, and an action history—using four separate stack LSTMs. The concatenated summary vectors of these LSTMs are fed into a classifier to make a greedy decision about the next parsing action. This approach avoids any hand-crafted, expert features and relies entirely on learned representations. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all previous joint parsing models. While it does not achieve state-of-the-art performance compared to highly-engineered pipelined systems, it is significantly faster, making it a practical alternative.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Architecture for Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is the successful extension of the stack LSTM framework to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The architecture, which maintains and learns representations for two separate output structures (syntactic and semantic stacks) simultaneously while sharing a common buffer, is a clean and effective way to model the joint state without manual feature engineering.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results for a Greedy, Feature-Free Model:** The paper provides a strong empirical result showing that a purely greedy, representation-learning-based parser can surpass previous joint models that relied on more complex search algorithms (like beam search) and/or feature engineering. This reinforces the power of neural representation learning for structured prediction and simplifies the parsing paradigm.
3.  **An Efficient and Practical Joint Parser:** The work contributes a very fast joint parser. The reported speed, which is significantly faster than contemporary state-of-the-art systems, is a major practical contribution, making sophisticated joint analysis more accessible for downstream applications where efficiency is critical.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model Design:** The model is an elegant application of recent advances in neural networks to a classic structured prediction problem. By using stack LSTMs to summarize the entire parser history and state, the model learns the features relevant for parsing decisions directly from data. The use of separate composition functions for syntactic and semantic structures (`gs` and `gm`) is a principled way to handle the different output types within a unified framework.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors rightly compare their model primarily against previous joint models, where they demonstrate clear superiority. The ablation studies on the CoNLL 2009 data (`Syntax-only`, `Semantics-only`, `Hybrid`, `Joint`) are particularly strong. They effectively isolate the contribution of the joint modeling, quantify the impact of syntax on semantics, and uncover a very interesting finding about the `Hybrid` model's superior robustness on out-of-domain data, which suggests interesting avenues for future research on domain adaptation in joint models.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The transition system, model architecture, and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail. The inclusion of figures and tables (e.g., Table 1 defining transitions, Figure 3 showing the architecture) greatly aids understanding. The commitment to release an open-source implementation is a significant strength that will benefit the community.
4.  **Honest and Clear Positioning:** The authors are transparent about the limitations of their model's performance relative to the absolute state-of-the-art. They correctly identify that advances in feature engineering, global inference, and the use of external data are orthogonal to their contribution and are not included. This honest positioning makes the paper's claims more credible and helps the reader understand the exact contribution of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Technical Novelty:** The core technical machinery, the stack LSTM, was introduced by Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing. This paper's main technical step is to apply this machinery to a joint task by, in essence, adding a second (semantic) stack and LSTM. While this is a non-trivial and valuable engineering and modeling contribution, the foundational algorithmic novelty is limited. The work is more of a strong application and extension paper than one introducing a fundamentally new neural architecture or learning paradigm.
2.  **Significant Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:** While the model is fast, the performance gap on the primary evaluation metrics (especially Semantic F1) compared to top-performing systems of the era is substantial. For example, on the CoNLL 2009 English task, the joint model's semantic F1 (84.62) is several points behind systems like Roth and Woodsend (87.9) or Lei et al. (87.4). The authors' argument that their model is simpler and feature-free is valid, but the performance trade-off may be too large for applications where accuracy is the top priority.
3.  **Unexplored Impact of Greedy Search:** The paper frames the use of greedy inference as a simplifying choice that leads to high speed. However, this is also a significant limitation. It is unclear how much of the performance gap is due to the greedy search versus the feature-free representations. An experiment with even a small beam search (e.g., beam size of 4 or 8) would have been invaluable. It would help disentangle the effects of search from representation and clarify whether this architecture could be competitive with the state-of-the-art on accuracy if a stronger search algorithm were used.
4.  **Sub-optimal Predicate Identification/Disambiguation:** The predicate handling feels somewhat disconnected from the main model. For CoNLL 2008, predicate identification is handled by a separate BiLSTM classifier, making it a pipelined component. For unseen predicates at test time, the model simply defaults to the ".01" sense. While this is a reasonable heuristic, it's a missed opportunity for more sophisticated handling within the representation learning framework, which could potentially learn to generalize better to unseen predicates.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The finding that the `Hybrid` model outperforms the `Joint` model on out-of-domain data is fascinating. You attribute this to the joint model overfitting to the WSJ training domain. Could you elaborate on this hypothesis? Does this suggest that for domain adaptation, decoupling the syntactic and semantic models might be a more robust strategy, even when using neural representations?
2.  Regarding the greedy inference: What was the primary motivation for sticking to a purely greedy model instead of exploring a small beam search? Given the speed of your system, it seems plausible that a small beam could significantly close the accuracy gap with top systems while still being much faster than models using complex features and global inference. Was this tested?
3.  The new `M-SELF` transition for NomBank predicates is a nice, targeted addition. Can you provide any analysis on its impact? For instance, what proportion of these self-dependencies in the development set does the model correctly identify using this action?
