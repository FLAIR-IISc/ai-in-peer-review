

# Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that fundamentally changes how parser state is represented and processed. The primary contribution is learning representations for the parser's *entire algorithmic state* rather than just top items on stacks (a departure from Henderson et al. 2008), using no expert-crafted features. A secondary contribution is the use of entirely greedy inference instead of beam search. The third contribution is demonstrating that these innovations lead to state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 joint parsing tasks without using expensive pipeline features.

## Strengths
1. **Conceptual Novelty**: The core idea of learning representations for the *entire* algorithmic state (not just top items) represents a significant conceptual shift from previous work. Most parsers focus on limited state information, while this approach treats the parser's entire state as a learnable entity. This is a bold methodological departure that could inspire new directions in parsing research, not just incremental improvements.

2. **Practical Performance**: The parser achieves SOTA results on CoNLL 2008/2009 without using expensive pipeline features or beam search. The runtime (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) is nearly 2.5x faster than comparable state-of-the-art systems, making it highly practical for real-world applications. This speed-performance combination is compelling and addresses a genuine industry need.

3. **Methodological Simplicity**: The elimination of expert-crafted features and beam search represents a clean, principled approach that reduces complexity while maintaining performance. This aligns with the growing trend in NLP toward end-to-end learning with minimal hand-crafted components, making the model more robust and easier to deploy.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Performance Gaps**: While the system outperforms previous joint models, it falls behind top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014) by a significant margin (1.5-2.5% LAS). Given the authors' claim that their approach "bypasses expensive features," the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why their model can't close this gap without incorporating those features.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the impact of the key innovations. For instance, how much of the performance gain comes from learning the entire state versus the greedy inference? The comparison between "Joint" and "Hybrid" models helps somewhat, but more targeted ablations would strengthen the claims.

3. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims that "linguistic theory and researcher intuitions offer less guidance in feature design" for greedy models, but doesn't provide theoretical or empirical evidence to support this assertion. A deeper analysis of why learning the entire state works better than previous approaches would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper mentions that the model's performance doesn't match top systems that use "orthogonal" advances. Could you elaborate on which specific orthogonal advances (e.g., morphological features, extra datasets) might most significantly boost your model's performance, and whether your architecture could incorporate these without compromising the core novelty?

2. The greedy approach is surprising given that most parsing tasks use beam search. Could you provide more insight into why greedy inference works so well with your state representation? Is it because the state representation captures sufficient context to make each greedy decision accurate?

3. The paper states that the model "adapts easily" to non-English languages, but Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings. How would the model perform without these embeddings, and what would be the minimal dataset size needed for good performance in low-resource languages?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a bold, novel approach to joint parsing that challenges conventional wisdom about parser state representation and inference. While the performance gap with top systems is notable, the conceptual innovation, practical speed, and clean architecture make this a significant contribution to the field. The paper successfully demonstrates that learning the entire algorithmic state is a viable and superior alternative to hand-crafted features, opening a promising new direction for parsing research. Given my reviewing philosophy prioritizing novel concepts over incremental improvements, I recommend acceptance. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in a revision, but the core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance.
