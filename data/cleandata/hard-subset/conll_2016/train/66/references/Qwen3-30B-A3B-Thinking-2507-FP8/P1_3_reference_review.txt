

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire parser state (not just top items on the stack), and (2) using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The main contributions I see are:

1. A novel representation learning approach for joint parsing that captures the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features.
2. A highly efficient parser that achieves competitive performance while being significantly faster than previous approaches (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core).
3. Demonstrating that joint parsing can achieve competitive performance without expensive features derived from full syntactic parses.

## Strengths

1. **Novel representation learning approach**: The paper's most significant contribution is the use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state rather than just local features. This is a meaningful advancement over previous joint parsing models that relied on expert-crafted features. The authors convincingly show that their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, including Henderson et al. (2008) and variants. The key insight that the entire state matters for parsing decisions is well-supported by their results.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper provides comprehensive results on multiple datasets, showing that their joint model significantly outperforms previous joint parsing approaches. Their model achieves performance comparable to top systems at CoNLL 2009, despite not using expert-crafted features. The results are well-organized and presented with appropriate statistical significance.

3. **Computational efficiency**: The parser's runtime (177.6±18 seconds on a single core for CoNLL 2009 English test data) is a major practical advantage. The paper correctly notes that this is almost 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015), making it particularly valuable for real-world applications where speed is critical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent work**: The paper doesn't compare with several recent neural network models for joint parsing that have been published since the CoNLL 2009 shared task (e.g., after 2015). For example, the paper mentions "state-of-the-art SRL systems" but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches that might have been published after the paper's timeframe. This limits the significance of the claimed "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" claim.

2. **Limited analysis of why the model works**: While the paper claims the model discovers "cheap alternatives" to expensive features, it provides no detailed analysis of what these features are or why the model's representation is effective. A deeper analysis of the learned representations or ablation studies would strengthen the contribution significantly. For example, it would be valuable to know which parts of the parser state are most informative for the parsing decisions.

3. **Multilingual results are not as strong as claimed**: The paper states that the model "adapts easily" to multilingual data, but the results in Table 4 show it's only on par with the top systems in most cases. The Chinese parser's performance relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings, suggesting the model might not generalize as well as claimed. The paper should acknowledge this limitation more clearly.

4. **Lack of ablation study for greedy inference**: The paper claims greedy inference is a key contribution, but provides no ablation study showing how much performance is lost by using greedy inference versus beam search. Without this comparison, the claim that greedy inference is sufficient isn't fully validated.

5. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the system doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems but doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, they don't address how their model might perform on longer sentences or more complex syntactic structures, which could be important for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model outperforms all previous joint parsing models, but doesn't compare with more recent neural models (post-2015). How would your model compare with state-of-the-art neural joint parsing models published after the CoNLL 2009 shared task?

2. The paper states that the model discovers "cheap alternatives" to expensive features, but provides no analysis of what these features are or why the model's representation works. Could you provide examples of features that were effectively replaced by your model's learned representations?

3. The Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance. How would the model perform without these embeddings, and what does this say about the model's generalizability to languages without good pretrained embeddings?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and efficient approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing that demonstrates strong empirical results. The representation learning approach using stack LSTMs to capture the entire parser state is a meaningful contribution to the field. The computational efficiency of the model is particularly valuable for practical applications. However, the paper would benefit from comparisons with more recent work, a deeper analysis of the learned representations, and a more thorough discussion of limitations. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above. The paper is well-written, the methodology is sound, and the results are significant. The main issues are the limited comparison with more recent work and the lack of deeper analysis of the model's representation learning capabilities.
