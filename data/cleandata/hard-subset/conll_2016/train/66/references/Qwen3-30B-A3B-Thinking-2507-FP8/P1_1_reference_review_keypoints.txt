- Introduces a joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs that represents the entire parser state, uses greedy inference, and claims SOTA joint results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English while being much faster.

Main contributions:
- Learns representations for the entire parser state, removing the need for expert-crafted features.
- Demonstrates that greedy inference can effectively handle joint syntactic-semantic parsing without beam search.
- Provides a fast, practical system achieving competitive performance without expensive feature engineering.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results, outperforming prior joint models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks.
- Significant speed advantage (~2.5× faster than Lei et al. pipeline), making it attractive for real-world use.
- Simple and elegant architecture: end-to-end neural approach with no hand-crafted features.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks a fair comparison to a pipeline that uses the authors' own syntactic parser as the first stage.
- Novelty is incremental since core techniques (stack LSTMs, parsing algorithm) are adapted from prior work.
- Ablation studies are insufficient to explain why the joint model outperforms hybrids and why it degrades out-of-domain.
- No error analysis provided to identify failure modes and limitations.

Questions for authors:
- How could orthogonal advances (expert features, reranking) be integrated into this framework to boost performance?
- Can you provide results for a pipeline using your syntactic parser as stage one to better compare joint vs. pipeline approaches?
- What aspects of the joint model cause overfitting on out-of-domain data and how can this be mitigated?
