

# Review

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes a new joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser based on an incremental structure building approach. The authors claim two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state rather than just top stack items, and (2) using entirely greedy inference instead of beam search. They assert their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English tasks and achieves competitive multilingual performance. The main contributions I see are: (1) an implementation of a joint parser using stack LSTMs for the entire algorithmic state, (2) a demonstration of its performance on CoNLL shared tasks, and (3) a claim of computational efficiency.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a clear and well-structured description of the transition-based parsing algorithm and its joint syntactic-semantic extensions. The formalization of transitions (Table 1) and the parsing procedure (Section 2.1-2.3) are technically sound.

2. The experimental setup follows standard CoNLL shared task protocols, using appropriate datasets, metrics, and baselines for comparison. The ablation studies (Section 5) provide some useful insights about the contribution of joint parsing versus separate syntax/semantics components.

3. The computational efficiency claim (177.6±18 seconds on single core) is well-supported by the experimental results and provides a practical advantage over some pipeline systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: The paper claims to outperform previous joint models but fails to compare with the actual state-of-the-art systems that achieve the best results on these shared tasks. The authors acknowledge their model "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014), yet they do not provide a compelling argument for why their approach is valuable despite this performance gap. The comparison to "all previous joint parsing models" is insufficient when the top-performing systems use different (and presumably better) approaches.

2. **Questionable novelty of the main claim**: The paper claims to learn representations for "the parser's entire algorithmic state," but the stack LSTM approach used is directly from Dyer et al. (2015), which was developed for syntactic parsing. The paper fails to demonstrate how this approach is meaningfully different or better for *joint* syntactic-semantic parsing than the approaches used in previous joint parsing work. The authors cite Henderson et al. (2008) as the basis for their transition system, but their "entire state" representation is not clearly shown to provide significant advantages over simply using the top of the stack.

3. **Insufficient justification for greedy inference**: The paper claims "entirely greedy inference rather than beam search" as a key innovation but provides no empirical or theoretical justification for why this approach works well for joint parsing. Greedy parsing is known to be suboptimal compared to beam search in many parsing contexts, and the authors fail to address why their approach avoids the typical performance degradation of greedy parsing. Without evidence that greedy inference is actually beneficial in this specific context, this claim appears unsubstantiated.

4. **Inadequate validation of the "no expert features" claim**: The authors claim to "use no expert-crafted features at all," but the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate that their model is truly feature-free. The model uses pretrained embeddings, learned representations for relations, and predicate sense disambiguation, which could be considered forms of feature engineering. The paper also doesn't compare to models that use the same embedding techniques but with more standard feature sets, making it difficult to assess whether the "no expert features" claim is meaningful.

5. **Overstated significance of results**: The paper claims "it outperforms all previous joint parsing models" but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The results in Table 2 and 3 show marginal improvements over previous joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013), but these improvements are not statistically significant or substantial enough to justify the novelty claim. The paper also fails to address whether these marginal gains are practically meaningful.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you reconcile your claim of outperforming "all previous joint parsing models" with your acknowledgment that your system "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014)? What makes your approach valuable despite this performance gap?

2. Can you provide more detailed analysis of why greedy inference works well for your joint parsing task, particularly given that greedy parsing typically underperforms compared to beam search in parsing tasks?

3. The paper claims to "use no expert-crafted features at all," but your model still uses pretrained embeddings, learned relation representations, and predicate sense disambiguation. How do you distinguish these from "expert-crafted features," and why does this distinction matter for the claim of feature-free parsing?

4. The multilingual results (Table 4) show that your Chinese parser "relies on pretrained word embeddings for its superior performance." How would you expect your model to perform on low-resource languages without access to pretrained embeddings, and what steps would you take to address this limitation?
