

# Review for CONLL Submission

## Summary
This paper introduces a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state of the parser (not just the top items on the stack) using stack LSTMs, and employs entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, while achieving significantly faster parsing speeds (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core).

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel representation learning approach for joint parsing that learns representations for the entire parser state (not just top items), eliminating the need for expensive expert-crafted features.
2. A greedy inference approach for joint parsing that achieves competitive performance with significantly improved speed over previous methods.
3. A demonstration that neural network-based approaches can effectively replace expensive features derived from full syntactic parses in joint parsing tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The paper demonstrates clear performance improvements over previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009, which are the standard evaluation benchmarks for this task. The model outperforms Henderson et al. (2008), the most directly related previous work, by a meaningful margin (e.g., 1.3% LAS on CoNLL 2008). This is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Clear and well-motivated technical insight**: The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of previous joint models - they struggle to match pipeline approaches because they rely on expensive features derived from full syntactic parses, which aren't available during greedy parsing. The stack LSTM approach effectively learns these features implicitly, which is a valuable theoretical contribution.

3. **Significant speed advantage**: The runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for the full CoNLL 2009 English test set is approximately 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) (439.9±42 seconds). This makes the approach highly practical for real-world applications where speed is critical, which is an important contribution for the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper claims to outperform "all previous joint parsing models" but doesn't directly compare with state-of-the-art neural approaches like Zhou and Xu (2015) and Lei et al. (2015), which are explicitly mentioned in the paper as achieving better performance. The authors acknowledge these systems but don't provide a direct comparison, which weakens their claim of outperforming all previous joint models. This is a significant oversight given that these are the most relevant recent works.

2. **Lack of sufficient ablation studies**: The paper compares "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," and "Hybrid" models, but doesn't provide enough ablation to isolate the contribution of each key component. For example, it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the stack LSTM representation versus the greedy inference approach. This makes it difficult to assess the relative importance of their key innovations.

3. **Insufficient analysis of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why. They mention that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model," but don't detail what specific features or techniques they're missing. This is a significant gap, especially since they claim to have eliminated the need for those features.

4. **Limited discussion of model limitations**: The paper mentions that the parser has trouble with crossing arcs (due to non-perfect oracle conversion), but doesn't provide quantitative analysis of how this affects performance or potential ways to address it. This is particularly important for a parsing task where crossing dependencies are common.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform all previous joint parsing models but doesn't directly compare with Zhou and Xu (2015) or Lei et al. (2015), which are neural approaches for SRL. Could you clarify why these systems aren't included in your comparison, and whether your approach could be combined with their techniques to achieve even better results?

2. The paper mentions that the model is slower than some pipeline systems but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the accuracy/speed trade-off. Could you elaborate on how your approach compares to other state-of-the-art systems in terms of this trade-off?

3. The paper states that for unseen predicates at test time, the system defaults to the "01" sense, which is correct for 91.22% of predicates by type. Could you provide more details about the distribution of "01" sense across different predicate types and how this affects the overall performance metrics?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic parsing by demonstrating that neural representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features while achieving competitive performance with significantly improved speed. The empirical results are strong on the standard CoNLL benchmarks, and the speed advantage is substantial for practical applications.

However, the paper's claim that it outperforms "all previous joint parsing models" is weakened by the lack of direct comparison with recent neural approaches. The ablation studies also don't sufficiently isolate the contribution of the key innovations. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these issues, particularly the comparison with recent neural approaches and providing more detailed ablation analysis. The paper has clear value to the field and presents a compelling alternative to expensive pipeline approaches for joint parsing.
