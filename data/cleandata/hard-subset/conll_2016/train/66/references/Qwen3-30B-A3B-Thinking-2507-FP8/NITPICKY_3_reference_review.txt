

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state during parsing, rather than just top items on the stack. The parser uses greedy inference instead of beam search and claims to outperform previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. The authors demonstrate that their approach can bypass expensive features derived from full syntactic parses.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel joint parsing approach that learns representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state
2. A greedy parsing approach that avoids beam search while maintaining competitive performance
3. Demonstrating that neural representations can replace expert-crafted features for joint parsing

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of representing the full parser state rather than just top items, which addresses a significant limitation in previous work.
2. The runtime performance (177.6±18 seconds on a single core) is impressive compared to previous systems (439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015), making the approach practical for real-world applications.
3. The multilingual results are strong, with the model performing comparably to top systems across multiple languages, demonstrating good generalization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete Methodology Description**: The paper claims to use a fourth stack LSTM (A) for "the history of actions" but fails to explain how this is integrated into the model. Section 3.2 states "A is never popped from, only pushed to" but provides no details on how the action history is used in the transition decision process. This is a critical omission that prevents proper replication of the model.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in equation (1), the authors use "st;mt;bt,at" for concatenation but use a comma instead of semicolon, which is confusing. The term "Lluí́s" (with an accent on the i) appears with an incorrectly placed accent mark in multiple places. The paper also inconsistently capitalizes "CoNLL 2008 and 2009" versus "CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English."

3. **Missing Critical Details for Unseen Predicates**: Section 3.5 claims the system defaults to "01" sense for unseen predicates but fails to explain how the lemma is predicted for these unseen words. The authors state "our system constructs a predicate from the predicted lemma of the word at that position" but don't describe the lemma prediction method, which is essential for understanding how the system handles unseen predicates.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper claims their model "significantly outperforms" previous work but provides no statistical significance tests for their results. This is a major omission in NLP research, as differences in performance could be due to random variation rather than model improvements.

5. **Inadequate Runtime Comparison**: The paper claims their system is "almost 2.5 times faster" than Lei et al. (2015) but fails to specify the hardware used for their measurements. Without knowing the CPU model, memory, or other hardware specifications, the runtime comparison is meaningless for reproducibility and fair comparison.

## Questions to Authors
1. How exactly is the action history stack (A) incorporated into the transition decision process? Please provide a clear description or diagram showing how the history of actions influences the classifier.

2. For the lemma prediction of unseen predicates in section 3.5, what model or method is used to predict the lemma when the word has not been seen in training? Please provide details of this component.

3. Why were statistical significance tests not performed for the reported results? How would the results change if significance testing were applied?

4. What specific hardware specifications were used for the runtime measurements (CPU model, memory, etc.)? Please provide these details for fair comparison with other systems.

5. The paper states "the transition sequence incorrectly encodes the gold-standard joint parse" for 1.5% of English sentences (footnote 5). What is the nature of these errors, and how do they impact the reported results?
