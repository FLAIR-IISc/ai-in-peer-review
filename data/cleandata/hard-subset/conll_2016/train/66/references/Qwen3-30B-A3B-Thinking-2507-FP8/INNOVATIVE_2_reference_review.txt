

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that represents the entire algorithmic state of the parser using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features or limited state representations. The model uses entirely greedy inference (no beam search) and claims to outperform previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while being significantly faster than competing systems.

## Main Contributions
1. A completely feature-free joint parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state (not just local stack items)
2. A bold rejection of beam search in favor of greedy inference for joint parsing, with demonstrated success
3. A practical, fast parsing system that achieves competitive performance without expensive syntactic features

## Strengths
1. **Fundamental conceptual novelty**: The paper's core contribution is a paradigm shift in how parsing models are designed. By representing the entire parser state rather than just local features, the authors break from the dominant approach of using expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses. This is a significant conceptual advance that challenges conventional wisdom in parsing.

2. **Bold methodological choice**: The decision to use entirely greedy inference (no beam search) is particularly noteworthy. Most state-of-the-art parsers rely on beam search for better accuracy, but the authors demonstrate that greedy inference with their state representation can outperform these more complex approaches. This represents a courageous departure from established practice that could inspire future work.

3. **Practical significance**: The system achieves end-to-end parsing in 177.6 seconds on a single core for the CoNLL 2009 English test set, which is 2.5× faster than the pipeline system of Lei et al. (2015). For practical NLP applications where speed is critical, this represents a substantial advantage over competing approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the full-state representation versus the greedy inference. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from each component, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the state representation.

2. **Limited theoretical justification**: While the paper claims the representation captures "the complete syntactic parse tree" information, it doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why this representation should work better than previous approaches. The paper relies on empirical results rather than deeper linguistic insights.

3. **Comparison to recent work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address how their approach compares to the most recent neural joint parsing work, such as the graph-based methods that have recently dominated parsing benchmarks. This weakens the claim of being "state-of-the-art" in the current landscape.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you explain the significant performance gap between your system and the top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014) that use full syntactic parses? Is this gap primarily due to the lack of syntactic features, or are there other factors?

2. The paper claims that "recent advances in representation learning can bypass those expensive features," but doesn't provide a clear explanation of how the learned representations capture the same information as the hand-crafted syntactic features. Could you elaborate on this mechanism?

3. Given that your system uses greedy inference, how would you handle cases where the greedy choice leads to an incorrect parse that could have been corrected with beam search? What is the failure mode of your system compared to beam-search parsers?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a bold and novel approach to joint parsing that challenges conventional wisdom in the field. The conceptual shift from local to full-state representation, combined with the successful rejection of beam search, represents a significant contribution that could inspire new directions in parsing research. While the methodological execution has some limitations, the novelty and practical significance of the approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.

The paper's core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance, even though it doesn't match the absolute state-of-the-art performance of expert-crafted systems. The field needs more such bold approaches that challenge established paradigms, and this paper delivers on that front. The practical speed advantage also makes the approach highly valuable for real-world applications.
