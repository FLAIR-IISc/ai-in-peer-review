- Introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser state using stack LSTMs.  
- Claims three contributions: whole-state representation, entirely greedy inference (no beam search), and a fast model that outperforms prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009.  
- Empirically outperforms Henderson et al. (2008) and variants across English and other languages, showing a significant empirical contribution.  
- Demonstrates strong practical efficiency (e.g., ~177.6±18s on CoNLL 2009 English, ~2.5× faster than Lei et al. 2015).  
- Clear technical contribution: well-motivated use of stack LSTMs to capture full algorithmic parser state.  
- Limited analysis of greedy inference: no comparison to a beam-search version to quantify accuracy vs. speed trade-offs.  
- Inadequate comparison to prior neural approaches, especially how it differs from or improves on a Dyer et al. (2015)-style neural joint model.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of which expert-crafted features are being replaced and how learned representations capture that information.  
- Insufficient multilingual analysis: minimal explanation for language-specific performance differences and reliance on pretrained embeddings (e.g., Chinese).  
- Requests clarification on why whole-state representation yields gains over limited state representations used previously.  
- Asks which specific expert-crafted features are missing/replaced (e.g., predicate-argument path) and how the model compensates.  
- Asks how greedy inference affects performance and whether a beam-search variant would improve results without large runtime cost.  
- Overall: solid empirical and efficiency contributions, technically sound and well-written, but needs stronger analysis of representation learning and greedy vs. beam-search trade-offs.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen analysis of representation learning and decoding trade-offs.
