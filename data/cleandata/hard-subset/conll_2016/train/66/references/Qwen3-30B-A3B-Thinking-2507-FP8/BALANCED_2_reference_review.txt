

# Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser Using Stack LSTMs"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, avoiding expert-crafted features and using greedy inference instead of beam search. The main contributions are:

1. **Novel representation learning approach for joint parsing**: The key contribution is learning representations for the entire parser state (not just top items on the stack), enabling the parser to avoid expensive expert-crafted features while achieving competitive performance.

2. **Demonstrated speed advantage**: The paper shows that their greedy parser is significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) than previous systems (e.g., 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015).

3. **Empirical validation on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves competitive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models while using no expert-crafted features.

## Strengths

1. **Clear empirical demonstration of performance gains**: The paper provides compelling evidence that learning the entire parser state representation leads to improved performance over previous joint parsing models (Tables 2-3). The joint model significantly outperforms Henderson et al. (2008) and other joint models on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Practical speed advantage**: The parser's speed (177.6±18 seconds on a single core) is a significant practical advantage over previous systems (2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015). This makes the model more applicable for real-world settings where inference speed matters.

3. **Well-structured and clear presentation**: The paper provides a thorough explanation of the transition system, stack LSTM implementation, and composition functions, making it easy for readers to understand and potentially replicate the approach. The architecture is well-motivated and clearly presented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems**: The paper acknowledges that their system doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), but doesn't adequately explain why or how their approach could be combined with these techniques. The paper should provide a more thorough analysis of the trade-offs between their approach and these systems, and how their model could potentially integrate with them.

2. **Lack of comprehensive ablation study**: While the paper includes variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid), it doesn't provide a detailed ablation study to isolate the specific contribution of learning the entire state representation versus using greedy inference. The paper claims both are key contributions, but doesn't demonstrate how much each contributes to the performance gain.

3. **Limited analysis of the learned representations**: The paper demonstrates that the model works well but doesn't provide any analysis of what the learned stack LSTM representations capture. Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand why the model outperforms previous approaches, which is a key claim of the paper.

4. **Dependence on pretrained embeddings for multilingual performance**: The paper notes that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance, but doesn't provide ablation studies showing how much the embeddings contribute versus the model architecture. This weakens the multilingual claim, as the performance gains for Chinese might be primarily due to the embeddings rather than the model itself.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their model outperforms previous joint parsing models but falls behind state-of-the-art SRL systems that use expert-crafted features. Could the authors provide more specific analysis of why their approach doesn't match these systems, and how their model could potentially be combined with those techniques to achieve better performance?

2. The paper mentions that their Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for superior performance. Could the authors provide ablation results showing the performance difference with and without these embeddings, to better isolate the contribution of their model architecture?

3. The paper claims that learning the entire parser state representation is the key contribution. Could the authors provide an analysis of what specific features or patterns the stack LSTM learns that previous models missed, beyond just stating that it outperforms them?

4. The paper uses greedy inference instead of beam search. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of why greedy inference works well here, compared to previous joint parsing models that used beam search? What specific aspects of the parser state representation make greedy inference effective for this task?
