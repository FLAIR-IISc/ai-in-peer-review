

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than just the top items on the stack as in previous work. The model uses entirely greedy inference instead of beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, with multilingual results comparable to top systems at CoNLL 2009, while being significantly faster than previous state-of-the-art systems.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1. A novel approach to joint parsing that eliminates the need for expert-crafted features by learning representations for the entire parser state
2. Demonstrating that greedy inference can effectively handle joint syntactic-semantic parsing without the need for beam search
3. Providing a practical, fast system that achieves competitive performance without expensive feature engineering

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, outperforming all previous joint models including Henderson et al. (2008) and variants. This is significant because joint parsing has been challenging, and previous approaches often required expensive features from full syntactic parses. The authors provide thorough comparisons with relevant prior work.

2. **Practical speed advantage**: The model's runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core is nearly 2.5x faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) (439.9±42 seconds). This speed advantage makes the model highly attractive for real-world applications where processing time is critical, and it's a meaningful contribution to the field.

3. **Simplicity and elegance**: The model's architecture is remarkably simple, using no expert-crafted features and entirely greedy inference. This demonstrates that neural representations can effectively replace traditional feature engineering in joint parsing, which aligns with the trend toward end-to-end learning in NLP. The approach is clean and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to pipeline approaches**: While the authors acknowledge their model doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems, they don't provide a fair comparison to a pipeline approach using their own syntactic parser as the first stage. A pipeline with their syntactic parser would provide a more meaningful baseline than comparing to systems that use entirely different syntactic parsers. This omission weakens the claim that joint parsing provides clear advantages over pipelines.

2. **Limited novelty**: The core technique (stack LSTMs) is directly adapted from Dyer et al. (2015), and the parsing algorithm is based on Henderson et al. (2008). The paper's main contribution is applying these techniques to joint parsing, but the novelty is incremental. The authors don't sufficiently articulate what makes their specific application novel compared to existing work.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper presents several variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid), but the ablation studies don't provide enough insight into why the joint approach works better. For example, the paper notes the joint model outperforms the hybrid on in-domain data but falls behind on out-of-domain data, suggesting overfitting, but doesn't explore why this occurs or how to mitigate it. More detailed analysis of the joint model's behavior would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any error analysis to understand where the model fails and why. This would be valuable for understanding the model's limitations and potential directions for improvement. Without error analysis, it's difficult to assess the model's specific strengths and weaknesses.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that their model doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems, but acknowledges that many of these advances are orthogonal to their model. How do you envision integrating these orthogonal advances (like expert features or reranking) into your framework to further improve performance?

2. The paper claims the model is faster than pipeline approaches, but doesn't compare to a pipeline using the same syntactic parser. Could you provide results for a pipeline where your syntactic parser is used as the first stage, to better understand the trade-off between joint and pipeline approaches?

3. The paper mentions that the model's performance on out-of-domain data (Brown corpus) is better with the hybrid approach than the joint approach, suggesting overfitting. What specific aspects of the joint model might be causing this overfitting, and how could it be addressed?

This is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of joint parsing, with strong empirical results and a practical speed advantage. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly with a more comprehensive comparison to pipeline approaches and more detailed analysis of the model's behavior.
