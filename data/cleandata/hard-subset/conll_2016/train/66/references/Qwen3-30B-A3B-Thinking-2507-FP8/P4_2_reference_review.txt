

Summary and Main Contributions: This paper presents a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features or partial state representations. The authors claim three key contributions: (1) a novel representation learning approach for joint parsing using stack LSTMs to capture the complete parser state; (2) a greedy parsing procedure that avoids beam search while maintaining competitive performance; and (3) empirical demonstration that end-to-end neural models can achieve strong results without expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses.

Strengths:
1. Technical soundness of the representation learning approach: The paper makes a clear technical contribution by extending stack LSTMs from syntactic parsing to joint syntactic-semantic parsing. The authors provide a well-structured implementation of the stack LSTMs for all four components of the parser state (S, M, B, and A), with detailed composition functions for handling both syntactic and semantic structures. The approach is technically rigorous and builds logically on Dyer et al. (2015).

2. Performance gains over previous joint models: The authors demonstrate clear empirical improvements over all previous joint parsing models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013) on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. The results are well-presented with comprehensive tables showing that their joint model outperforms all prior joint approaches on these benchmarks.

3. Significant efficiency advantage: The paper demonstrates a substantial runtime improvement (177.6±18 seconds vs. 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015) on the CoNLL 2009 English test set, making the system practical for real-world applications. This efficiency gain is particularly noteworthy given that the authors use a single core rather than distributed processing.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited novelty compared to prior work: While the application to joint parsing is a reasonable extension, the core idea of using neural networks to replace hand-crafted features in parsing is not novel. Similar approaches have been successfully applied to syntactic parsing (Dyer et al., 2015; Goldberg, 2015) and semantic parsing (Collobert et al., 2011; Zhou and Xu, 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these established lines of work.

2. Performance gap with state-of-the-art systems: The authors acknowledge their model does not match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), which use features derived from full syntactic parses and employ optimal decoding. The paper doesn't adequately address how their approach could be integrated with these more powerful methods to close this gap.

3. Insufficient analysis of the representation learning: The paper demonstrates that the full-state representation works well but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why it works better than previous approaches. There's no detailed examination of which aspects of the algorithmic state are most informative, or how the stack LSTM effectively captures these aspects. This limits the theoretical contribution of the work.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would you integrate your greedy approach with optimal decoding methods (like those used by Täckström et al., 2015) to potentially improve performance while maintaining efficiency?
2. The paper states that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance. Can you elaborate on how these embeddings impact performance compared to other languages, and whether this represents a fundamental limitation of the approach?
3. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of which specific components of the algorithmic state (S, M, B, or A) contribute most to performance, and why?
