

# Review for CONLL

## Summary
This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn representations for the entire algorithmic state of a transition-based parser, rather than just the top items on the stack. The model uses entirely greedy inference without beam search, and claims to outperform previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks while being significantly faster. The authors demonstrate that their approach eliminates the need for expert-crafted features that typically rely on the full syntactic parse tree.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel representation learning approach for joint parsing that captures the entire algorithmic state (not just local stack top items), eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that greedy inference can outperform beam search in joint parsing, with significant runtime advantages.
3. A practical, high-speed parsing system (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) that achieves competitive performance without expensive features.

## Strengths
1. **Significant performance gains**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, outperforming previous joint models by 1-2 LAS points and 0.5-1.5 semantic F1 points. This is a meaningful advancement in the field, particularly since it avoids the expensive feature engineering that has characterized previous high-performing systems.

2. **Clear demonstration of the value of full-state representation**: The authors effectively show that learning representations for the entire parser state (not just top items) is crucial for performance, as evidenced by their ablation study showing that the joint model significantly outperforms the "Semantics-only" and "Syntax-only" variants.

3. **Practical runtime advantage**: The runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on a single core is a substantial improvement over competing approaches (e.g., 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015), making this model highly practical for real-world applications where speed is critical.

4. **Well-structured and clearly explained**: The paper provides a thorough explanation of the transition system, stack LSTM architecture, and composition functions. The example transition sequences and table of transitions make the algorithm accessible to readers unfamiliar with transition-based parsing.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of explanation for why greedy inference outperforms beam search**: The paper claims their greedy approach outperforms previous joint models that used beam search, but fails to explain why this is the case. Previous work (e.g., Dyer et al., 2015) showed beam search generally outperforms greedy inference in syntactic parsing, so the authors need to provide stronger justification for this surprising result.

2. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: While the paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the top expert-crafted feature-based systems, it doesn't sufficiently explore why this is the case or how their approach could be combined with those features. The paper mentions "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model" but doesn't provide a clear path forward for integration.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases and limitations**: The paper presents strong performance results but lacks detailed analysis of where the model fails or its limitations. Without understanding failure cases, it's difficult to assess the model's real-world applicability or potential for improvement.

4. **Insufficient ablation study**: The ablation study comparing "Semantics-only," "Syntax-only," and "Joint" models is too limited. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of why the joint model outperforms the variants, particularly regarding the interaction between syntactic and semantic components.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more specific analysis of why greedy inference outperforms beam search in this context, given that beam search is typically preferred for parsing tasks?

2. The paper mentions that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model" but doesn't elaborate. Could you clarify which specific features or techniques from top systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015) could be integrated with your approach, and what the expected performance gains might be?

3. The multilingual results show that Chinese performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings. Could you provide more details on how the model handles morphological features in languages where they are important, and whether you've explored character-based embeddings as a potential solution?

4. Given that your joint model outperforms the "Hybrid" model on in-domain data but underperforms on out-of-domain data, could you elaborate on the nature of this domain shift and what specific aspects of the joint model might be causing this overfitting?
