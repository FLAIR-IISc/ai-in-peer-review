

# Review for CONLL Submission

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state rather than just local features. The key contributions are: 1) A novel joint parsing approach that learns representations for the entire parser state using stack LSTMs; 2) A greedy parsing strategy that avoids beam search complexity while maintaining competitive performance; 3) Demonstrating that expensive syntactic features (like path features) can be bypassed through neural representation learning.

## Strengths
1. **Novel representation learning approach**: The paper's most significant contribution is using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state rather than just local features. This is a meaningful departure from previous joint parsing work that relied on expert-crafted features. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this approach can bypass expensive features like "path" features that have been standard in high-performance semantic role labeling systems.

2. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results for joint parsing on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks compared to previous joint models. The authors provide thorough comparisons with previous joint models (Henderson et al. 2008, Gesmundo et al. 2009, Titov et al. 2009) and show clear improvements. The results are well-documented with detailed tables and statistical significance.

3. **Computational efficiency**: The parser achieves an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is almost 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) (439.9±42 seconds). This practical efficiency advantage makes the system more viable for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Inadequate comparison with top pipeline systems**: While the paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the best pipeline systems, the comparison is not comprehensive. The authors don't provide a fair comparison with the most recent and best pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al. 2009, Lei et al. 2015) that use expert-crafted features. This weakens the claim that their model "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" since the best systems are pipeline-based, not joint.

2. **Lack of ablation study for greedy inference**: The paper claims that using greedy inference rather than beam search is a key contribution, but provides no ablation study showing how much performance is lost by using greedy inference versus beam search. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the significance of this contribution.

3. **Limited novelty of stack LSTM application**: While the paper presents the stack LSTM approach as novel for joint parsing, the stack LSTM itself was introduced by Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing. The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why the stack LSTM approach is particularly advantageous for joint parsing compared to its use in syntactic parsing.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides strong overall results but lacks a detailed error analysis to understand where the model succeeds or fails. This would help identify specific limitations and potential directions for improvement.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to outperform all previous joint parsing models but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the best pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al. 2009, Lei et al. 2015) that achieve higher performance. Could the authors clarify how their model compares to these systems in terms of both performance metrics and computational efficiency?

2. The paper states that "the performance does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this is the case. Could the authors elaborate on which specific features or techniques they're missing compared to these systems?

3. The paper claims that greedy inference is a key contribution but provides no ablation study comparing greedy versus beam search. Could the authors provide results showing how much performance is lost by using greedy inference versus beam search?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a solid contribution to joint parsing with a novel representation learning approach that avoids expert-crafted features. The empirical results are strong for joint models, and the computational efficiency is a tangible advantage. However, the lack of comprehensive comparison with top pipeline systems and the absence of an ablation study for the greedy approach significantly weaken the paper's claims. The work would be substantially strengthened by addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison with the best pipeline systems and including an ablation study for the greedy inference approach.

The paper would be suitable for acceptance with major revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly the comparison with top pipeline systems and the ablation study for greedy inference.
