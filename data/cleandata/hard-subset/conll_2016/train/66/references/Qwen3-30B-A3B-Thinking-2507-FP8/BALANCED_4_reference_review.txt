

# Review of "Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with a Stack LSTM"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while using greedy inference instead of beam search. As I see them, the main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that learning representations for the entire parser state (not just local features) enables effective joint parsing without expert-crafted features; (2) providing a fast, single-core parser that achieves competitive results (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data); and (3) showing good multilingual generalization on non-English CoNLL 2009 datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Impressive runtime performance**: The authors provide a clear, well-documented runtime analysis showing their parser is significantly faster than state-of-the-art systems (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is almost 2.5 times faster than Lei et al.'s pipeline model). This practical advantage is valuable for real-world applications where speed matters, and the authors make a strong case for the model's utility in practical settings.

2. **Demonstrating neural representations can replace expert features**: The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that neural representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features for joint parsing. The authors convincingly demonstrate their model performs comparably to systems that use these features, suggesting representation learning can obviate the need for manual feature engineering, which is an important insight for the field.

3. **Strong multilingual results**: The model achieves competitive results across multiple languages on the CoNLL 2009 shared task, performing on par with top systems in most cases. This demonstrates good generalization capabilities, particularly when pretrained embeddings are used (as evidenced in the Chinese case where performance improves with pretrained embeddings).

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of ablation study to validate key claim**: The paper's central claim is that learning representations for the entire parser state is the key to improved performance. However, the authors provide no ablation study comparing their full state representation to variants using only local features (e.g., top of stack). Without this, it's difficult to confirm that the full state representation is the main contributor to their results rather than other aspects of their model.

2. **Incomplete comparison to recent neural approaches**: While the paper acknowledges it doesn't match the top expert-crafted feature-based systems, it fails to compare to recent neural approaches that might use similar neural architectures. For instance, they don't compare to Zhou and Xu (2015) or other state-of-the-art neural SRL systems that might use different neural representations but similar architectural principles.

3. **Insufficient discussion of greedy inference limitations**: The paper claims greedy inference is advantageous for speed but doesn't adequately address potential limitations. While they achieve good results, they don't provide evidence that greedy inference is sufficient for high-quality parsing, nor do they compare to beam search variants of their own model to quantify the trade-off between speed and accuracy.

4. **Lack of training detail and hyperparameter tuning**: The paper mentions "careful tuning is expected to yield improvements" but provides no details on the hyperparameter tuning process or what was tested. This makes it difficult to reproduce their results or understand the full potential of their model. The authors should provide more detail on the training process, especially given their claim of achieving state-of-the-art results.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more details on an ablation study comparing your full state representation with variants that use only local features (e.g., top of stack)? This would help validate your key claim about the importance of the full state representation.

2. How does your model compare to recent neural approaches for joint parsing, such as Zhou and Xu (2015) or other systems that use neural architectures for semantic role labeling? A comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What specific values were tested and why were the final choices made? This would improve reproducibility.

4. Have you considered comparing your greedy approach with a beam search variant of your model to quantify the accuracy trade-off? This would provide important context for your speed claims.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that neural representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features for joint parsing, with impressive runtime performance. However, the lack of ablation studies to validate the key claim about the full state representation, incomplete comparison to recent neural approaches, and insufficient detail on training procedures are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these improvements, the paper could make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper shows promise but requires more thorough validation of its central claims.
