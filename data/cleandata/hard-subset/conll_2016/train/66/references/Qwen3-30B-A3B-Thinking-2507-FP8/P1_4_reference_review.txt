

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features or beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks while being significantly faster.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The demonstration that a neural model learning representations for the *entire* parser state (not just top items on stack) can achieve competitive results on joint parsing without expert-crafted features.
2. The empirical evidence that a greedy parsing approach using stack LSTMs can match state-of-the-art performance while offering a substantial speed advantage (2.5x faster than comparable systems).
3. The insight that neural representations can effectively replace expensive, manually designed features used in previous systems.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with significant speed advantage**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their model achieves competitive performance on standard benchmarks (outperforming previous joint models on CoNLL 2008 and matching top CoNLL 2009 systems) while being substantially faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core). This is a major practical contribution that addresses a real-world concern in NLP deployment.

2. **Novel architectural insight**: The authors successfully extend stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) to joint syntactic-semantic parsing, demonstrating that learning representations for the *entire* parser state is more effective than focusing only on the top items. This insight is valuable for the field, as it challenges the conventional wisdom that parser state can be adequately summarized by just the top stack elements.

3. **Clear demonstration of feature learning capability**: The paper effectively shows that their model can learn representations that replace expensive, manually designed features (like semantic paths between predicates and arguments). This is an important contribution to the field, as it demonstrates that neural models can capture linguistic knowledge that previously required human expertise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient novelty claim**: While the authors extend stack LSTMs to joint parsing, they don't clearly distinguish their approach from previous work. Dyer et al. (2015) already demonstrated stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing, and the extension to joint parsing isn't sufficiently novel. The paper doesn't adequately address whether this is a significant advancement over prior work or merely a straightforward extension.

2. **Lack of explanation for performance gap**: The authors acknowledge their model doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015). However, they don't provide a meaningful analysis of *why* their model falls short or what specific features they're missing. This is a significant weakness, as it leaves the reader uncertain whether the limitations are fundamental to their approach or could be addressed with minor modifications.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent work**: The paper compares with systems from 2009-2015 but doesn't address more recent state-of-the-art approaches (e.g., work from 2016-2023 that might have further improved joint parsing performance). This makes it difficult to assess where the authors' model stands in the current landscape of joint parsing.

4. **Limited analysis of failure modes**: The paper provides no detailed analysis of cases where the model fails or where it struggles compared to state-of-the-art systems. Without understanding the limitations of their approach, it's difficult to assess the practical applicability of their method.

5. **Multilingual results lack depth**: The multilingual results (Table 4) show the model adapts easily to other languages but relies heavily on pretrained embeddings for Chinese. The paper doesn't adequately explain why this dependency exists or how it might be mitigated, which is a significant limitation for languages without good pretrained embeddings.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their model outperforms previous joint models, but doesn't address why it doesn't match the performance of expert-crafted feature-based systems. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the specific features or capabilities that their model lacks compared to these top systems?

2. The paper mentions that their model is "comparable to the top systems at CoNLL 2009" but doesn't provide a clear comparison with more recent state-of-the-art approaches (post-2015). Could you update your comparison to include more recent work in the field?

3. The multilingual results show Chinese performance depends on pretrained embeddings. How might this dependency be reduced, and what would be the impact on performance without these embeddings?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical and efficient approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing with clear advantages in speed and simplicity. The empirical results are strong, and the insight about learning representations for the entire parser state is valuable. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with recent work, a clearer explanation of the performance gap with top systems, and a deeper analysis of the model's limitations.

The main contribution is the demonstration that a neural approach can effectively replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing while offering a substantial speed advantage. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the practical value of this work is significant for real-world NLP applications where efficiency matters. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
