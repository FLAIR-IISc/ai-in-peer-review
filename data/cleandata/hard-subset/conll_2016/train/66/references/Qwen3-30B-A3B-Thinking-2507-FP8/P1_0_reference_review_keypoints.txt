Summary: Introduces a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to learn full parser-state representations and greedy inference, yielding better joint-model performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 and faster runtimes while removing hand-crafted features.

Main contribution 1: Shows learning representations for the entire algorithmic state (via stack LSTMs) can replace expert-crafted features and improve joint parsing performance.

Main contribution 2: Demonstrates greedy inference is effective for joint parsing, offering competitive accuracy with much lower computational cost than beam search.

Main contribution 3: Extends stack LSTMs from purely syntactic to joint syntactic–semantic parsing, showing the approach’s versatility.

Strength 1: Strong empirical results across in-domain and out-of-domain CoNLL 2008/2009 English evaluations, outperforming prior joint parsing models.

Strength 2: High computational efficiency (≈2.5× faster than a competing pipeline model) making the approach practical for real applications.

Strength 3: Thorough ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid, Joint) that clarify component contributions.

Strength 4: Clear technical presentation with detailed transition descriptions, architecture, and illustrative tables.

Weakness 1: Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art SRL systems that use neural nets plus expert features, which weakens claims about overall competitiveness.

Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of model limitations, e.g., causes of overfitting to WSJ in out-of-domain tests.

Weakness 3: Weak discussion of hyperparameter selection and tuning; settings described as "intuitive" without systematic search.

Weakness 4: Limited theoretical justification for why learning full algorithmic state is superior to previous feature-based approaches.

Weakness 5: No error analysis showing where the model fails or which sentence types are problematic.

Question 1: Would adding key expert-crafted features (e.g., predicate–argument path) close the performance gap with top feature-based SRL systems?

Question 2: What specific aspects of the WSJ training data lead the Joint model to overfit, and how could that be mitigated?

Question 3: Did you perform systematic hyperparameter search; if not, which hyperparameters should be prioritized for tuning?

Question 4: Which specific top CoNLL 2009 systems is the Joint model being compared to, and on what basis is it considered comparable?

Question 5: How reliant is the Chinese model on pretrained embeddings and what are the implications for performance in low-resource languages?

Overall: Valuable contribution demonstrating state-representation learning for joint parsing, but would be stronger with deeper comparisons, limitation analyses, and hyperparameter/tuning details.
