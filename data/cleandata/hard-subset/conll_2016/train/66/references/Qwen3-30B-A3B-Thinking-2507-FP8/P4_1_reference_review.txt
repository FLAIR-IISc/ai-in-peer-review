

# Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that extends the stack LSTM approach (Dyer et al., 2015) to represent the entire parser's algorithmic state rather than just the top items on the stack. The main contributions are:

1. A novel architecture for joint parsing that learns representations for the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that this approach achieves competitive performance on standard CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks without relying on expensive features derived from full syntactic parses.
3. A practical, fast parsing system with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, making it suitable for real-world applications.

## Strengths
1. **Technical soundness of the stack LSTM approach**: The paper provides a clear and well-justified extension of stack LSTMs to joint parsing. The authors carefully detail the transition system, composition functions, and how the four stack LSTMs (for syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) work together to represent the entire parser state. The approach is technically robust and properly builds on the foundation of Dyer et al. (2015).

2. **Competitive results on standard benchmarks**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. It significantly outperforms previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013), which is a significant contribution to the field. The results are thoroughly evaluated using standard metrics (LAS, semantic F1, Macro F1).

3. **Practical efficiency**: The paper demonstrates a clear advantage in computational efficiency, with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on a single core for the CoNLL 2009 English test set—nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015). This makes the approach particularly valuable for practical applications where speed is critical.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty compared to recent work**: While the authors present their stack LSTM approach as novel, it's a direct extension of Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing to joint parsing. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this work from recent advances in neural joint parsing (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015; Li et al., 2010), making the contribution appear incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Performance gap with state-of-the-art systems**: The paper acknowledges that their system doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), which use features derived from full syntactic parses. This is a significant limitation, as the authors claim their approach "outperforms all previous joint parsing models," but the performance gap with the best systems suggests the approach has limitations that aren't adequately addressed.

3. **Lack of comprehensive ablation studies**: While the paper includes some variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid), it doesn't conduct a thorough ablation study to isolate the specific contribution of the stack LSTM representation versus other components. This makes it difficult to assess what exactly drives the performance gains and whether the approach would work as well with different architectures.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that their system outperforms previous joint parsing models but doesn't match state-of-the-art SRL systems. Can the authors clarify why their approach, which avoids expensive features, can't achieve similar performance to systems that use those features? What specific features are missing in their model that are critical for high performance?

2. The paper mentions that their Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for superior performance, but without them, it's on par with other systems. Can the authors provide more details on how the pretrained embeddings affect performance across languages, and whether their model could be adapted to work well with limited resources?

3. The paper claims their system "uses no expert-crafted features at all," but the experiments include pretrained embeddings and learned features. Can the authors clarify the distinction between "expert-crafted" features and their learned representations?
