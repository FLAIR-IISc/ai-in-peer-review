

# Review

**Summary and Main Contributions:**  
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the *entire* algorithmic state of the parser (not just top items on the stack) using stack LSTMs, with entirely greedy inference. The primary contribution is the insight that learning a holistic representation of the parser's state can eliminate the need for expensive expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that greedy parsing with this approach achieves competitive performance with significantly better speed (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core, 2.5x faster than comparable systems). The third contribution is showing that neural representations can replace traditional syntactic features in joint parsing, opening a new direction for future work.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novel Representation Approach:** The core idea of representing the *entire* parser state is genuinely novel and bold. Previous joint parsing work (Henderson et al., 2008; Titov et al., 2009) only used representations for local stack top items, but this paper demonstrates that capturing the full state leads to better performance without expert features. This represents a significant conceptual shift in how we think about parser state representation.

2. **Practical Speed Advantage:** The 2.5x speed improvement over state-of-the-art systems (Lei et al., 2015) is substantial for real-world applications. The paper clearly establishes this as a practical contribution, especially since many high-performing systems rely on computationally expensive beam search and feature engineering.

3. **Elimination of Expert Features:** The paper successfully demonstrates that neural representations can replace expensive syntactic features traditionally used in semantic role labeling. This aligns with the broader trend in NLP of using neural networks to learn features rather than relying on hand-crafted ones, but does so in a particularly elegant way for joint parsing.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of Clear Causal Evidence:** The paper doesn't convincingly establish that the entire-state representation is the key innovation. It's possible the performance gains are simply due to using stack LSTMs (which Dyer et al., 2015 demonstrated for syntactic parsing), not the holistic state representation. The paper should compare against a baseline that uses stack LSTMs but only for local state features.

2. **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art:** The model still falls behind systems that use expert-crafted features (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014) by a meaningful margin. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how their approach could integrate these features, which they claim are "orthogonal." This limitation needs more discussion, especially regarding potential future integration.

3. **Overfitting to WSJ Domain:** The paper notes that the joint model overfits to WSJ (in-domain) data, with the hybrid model outperforming it on out-of-domain Brown corpus. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, yet the paper provides only superficial analysis of this issue. A deeper exploration of why the joint model overfits would strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison between your system and one that uses stack LSTMs only for local state features (not entire state)? This would help clarify whether the entire-state representation is truly the key innovation.

2. The paper claims that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model." Could you elaborate on specific ways these features could be integrated with your approach without compromising the greedy inference?

3. Given the observed overfitting to WSJ, what techniques could be applied to improve the model's generalization to out-of-domain data while maintaining the speed advantages?

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper presents a bold, novel approach to joint parsing that challenges conventional wisdom about the need for expert-crafted features. While the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems is notable, the conceptual shift toward holistic state representation is significant and could inspire future work. The speed advantage is particularly compelling for practical applications. Given my emphasis on novelty over methodological perfection, I believe the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field despite the performance limitations. The paper is well-written, the claims are supported by experiments, and the speed advantage is substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the causal evidence question and the overfitting issue.
