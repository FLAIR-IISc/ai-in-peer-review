

# Review of "Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state of the parser, rather than just the top items on the stack. The model uses entirely greedy inference instead of beam search. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state for joint syntactic-semantic parsing, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. A greedy parsing algorithm that outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.
3. Demonstrating that neural representations can replace expensive syntactic features while achieving competitive performance with significantly faster runtime.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of Representation Learning Approach**: The paper provides a clear and technically sound extension of stack LSTMs to joint parsing. The architecture (Figure 3) and mathematical formalization (Section 3.2) are well-explained, showing how the entire parser state can be summarized into a fixed-length vector for transition classification. This represents a concrete technical advancement over prior joint parsing work that relied on hand-crafted features.

2. **Empirical Validation Against Relevant Baselines**: The model achieves significant improvements over previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 (outperforming Henderson et al., 2008 and variants) and CoNLL 2009 English (surpassing Gesmundo et al., 2009 and Henderson et al., 2013). The comparison includes both previous joint models and state-of-the-art pipeline approaches, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the method's strengths.

3. **Practical Runtime Advantage**: The parser achieves an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is 2.5x faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015). This practical advantage for real-world applications is a significant contribution, especially given the paper's focus on avoiding expensive features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Novelty**: The core innovation (using neural networks to replace hand-crafted features) is not fundamentally new. Dyer et al. (2015) already demonstrated stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing, and this work is a straightforward extension to joint parsing. The novelty is incremental rather than transformative, as the paper's main contribution is applying an existing technique to a new domain.

2. **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art**: The paper acknowledges that their model does not match the performance of top systems that use expert-crafted features (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015). The authors state that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model," suggesting that their contribution is limited in scope and cannot compete with more comprehensive approaches.

3. **Limited Analysis of Joint Learning Benefits**: While the paper presents "Semantics-only," "Syntax-only," and "Hybrid" variants, there is insufficient analysis of why the joint model outperforms these variants. The paper states that "in the training domain, syntax and semantics benefit each other" but provides minimal evidence for this claim, making it difficult to assess the true value of joint learning.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "no expert-crafted features at all," but relies on pretrained embeddings. How does this differ from "expert-crafted features" as defined in the paper? Could the authors clarify their definition of "expert-crafted features" in the context of their claims?

2. The joint model outperforms the hybrid model in-domain but underperforms out-of-domain. What specific aspects of the joint model might be causing this overfitting to the WSJ domain, and how might this be mitigated?

3. The paper states that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance. How would the authors expect the model to perform on low-resource languages without access to such embeddings, and what alternatives might be explored?

4. Given that the model does not match top systems using expert-crafted features, what specific integration strategies would the authors propose to combine their approach with those feature-based systems to achieve even better results?
