- Novel joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state rather than expert features.  
- Two claimed innovations: whole-state representations and entirely greedy inference (no beam search).  
- Evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009 and multiple languages, claiming competitive accuracy and large speed gains.

- Strength: Demonstrates major practical speed advantage (single-threaded ~177.6s vs ~439.9s) while keeping competitive performance.  
- Strength: Clear, reproducible methodology with detailed description of transition system and four stack LSTMs.  
- Strength: Comprehensive empirical evaluation including syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and multilingual results.

- Weakness: Novelty claim underdeveloped—insufficiently distinguished from Dyer et al. (2015) applying stack LSTMs to parsing.  
- Weakness: Misleading performance framing—outperforms prior joint models but lags top pipeline systems that use expert features.  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of limitations, notably reliance on pretrained embeddings (Chinese) and implications for low-resource languages.  
- Weakness: Missing hyperparameter details (embedding sizes, LSTM dims, etc.), hindering reproducibility and sensitivity assessment.  
- Weakness: Lacks analysis explaining why joint modeling empirically helps (mechanisms of shared representations).

- Question: How does your stack LSTM approach for joint parsing concretely differ from Dyer et al. (2015), and what new challenges arose?  
- Question: Can you provide a nuanced trade-off analysis between your greedy joint approach and higher-accuracy but slower pipeline systems?  
- Question: How would the model perform without pretrained embeddings and what strategies do you recommend for low-resource languages?

- Overall: Practical, efficient, and well-documented contribution with comprehensive results, but needs clearer novelty justification, more nuanced performance claims, and deeper discussion of limitations.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address novelty, performance framing, and limitations.
