

# Review for CONLL Paper

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that represents the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs rather than relying on expert-crafted features or limited state representations. The approach uses entirely greedy inference instead of beam search and demonstrates competitive performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while offering significant speed advantages.

## Main Contributions
1. The core innovation of using stack LSTMs to represent the *entire* parser state (not just the top of the stack) for joint parsing, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstrating that a purely greedy parsing approach can achieve competitive performance on joint parsing tasks, a significant departure from previous work that relied on beam search.
3. The practical demonstration of a much faster parser (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), which is 2.5x faster than state-of-the-art pipeline models.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Core Approach**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the stack LSTM representation of the *entire* parser state, not just the top items. This is a bold, principled approach that eliminates the need for hand-crafted features, which has been a major bottleneck in joint parsing. Previous work (like Henderson et al. 2008) used limited state representations or relied on expensive features, making this a significant conceptual advance.

2. **Practical Speed Advantage**: The runtime efficiency is a major strength. The paper demonstrates that their greedy parser achieves competitive accuracy while being substantially faster than competing approaches (2.5x faster than Lei et al. 2015). This practical advantage could make the approach valuable for real-world applications where speed matters.

3. **Strong Multilingual Generalization**: The model performs comparably to top systems across multiple languages, showing good generalization capabilities. The authors demonstrate that their approach adapts well to different linguistic structures without requiring language-specific modifications.

4. **Theoretical Insight**: The paper makes a compelling argument that neural representation learning can bypass expensive syntactic features traditionally used in SRL. This insight has broader implications for NLP beyond just parsing, suggesting a path toward more unified, end-to-end learning approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art**: While the model outperforms previous joint parsing approaches, it still lags behind the top systems (e.g., Zhao et al. 2009, Lei et al. 2015) that use expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address how their approach might be combined with these techniques to close the gap.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key innovations. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the stack LSTM representation versus the greedy inference approach. A more detailed analysis would strengthen the claim that the entire-state representation is the key innovation.

3. **Limited Analysis of Greedy Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly examine the limitations of greedy inference. For joint parsing tasks where optimal decoding is known to be beneficial, the authors should address why their greedy approach still achieves competitive performance and whether there are specific cases where it fails.

4. **Incomplete Discussion of Performance Gap**: The authors note their model doesn't match top systems but don't provide sufficient analysis of *why* this gap exists. A more detailed comparison of the specific features or techniques that enable the top systems to outperform would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the stack LSTM representation versus the greedy inference approach? How much of the performance gain comes from each component?

2. The paper mentions that the model's performance lags behind systems using "expert-crafted features." Could you identify specific features that would be most valuable to integrate with your approach?

3. Given that your model doesn't use the full syntactic parse for feature extraction, how would your approach compare to systems that use partial syntactic features from the parser's stack (like the Hybrid model in your experiments)?

4. The paper mentions that your model is faster but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the trade-off between speed and accuracy. Could you elaborate on how much accuracy is sacrificed for speed, and whether there are specific use cases where this trade-off is most beneficial?

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to joint parsing that eliminates expert-crafted features and achieves practical speed advantages. While the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems is notable, the core innovation of representing the entire parser state with stack LSTMs is significant and bold. The paper makes a compelling case that neural representation learning can bypass expensive features traditionally used in SRL, which is an important conceptual advance. The speed advantage is also a substantial practical contribution. I believe this paper opens a new direction for joint parsing research that could be highly influential.
