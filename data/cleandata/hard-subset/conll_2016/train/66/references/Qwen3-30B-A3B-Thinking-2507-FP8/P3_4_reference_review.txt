

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the *entire* parser's algorithmic state (not just top items on stacks) using stack LSTMs, and employs entirely greedy inference. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English tasks while being significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core). The primary contributions are: 1) a novel representation of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, 2) a greedy inference approach for joint parsing, and 3) competitive performance without expert-crafted features.

## Strengths

1. **Novel representation learning approach**: The paper makes a strong case for learning representations of the *entire* parser state rather than just top items on stacks. This is a clear conceptual advance over previous work (Henderson et al., 2008, 2013) that relied on hand-crafted features. The stack LSTM approach elegantly captures the full context of the parsing algorithm, which is particularly valuable for greedy models where linguistic intuition offers less guidance for feature design.

2. **Competitive performance with efficiency**: The model achieves strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009, outperforming previous joint parsing systems. The runtime advantage is particularly noteworthy - parsing the entire CoNLL 2009 English test set in 177.6±18 seconds on a single core is nearly 2.5× faster than state-of-the-art systems like Lei et al. (2015). This efficiency makes the approach highly practical for real-world applications.

3. **Clear architecture and implementation**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the parsing algorithm, transitions, and neural architecture. The use of stack LSTMs is appropriately motivated and implemented, with sufficient detail to reproduce the model. The experimental setup is standard and well-motivated, with thorough comparison to relevant baselines.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art**: While the authors acknowledge they don't use expert-crafted features or optimal decoding (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015), they don't properly quantify the performance gap. The paper claims their model "outperforms all previous joint parsing models," yet the strongest competitors (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014) use features derived from full syntactic parses and global reranking - exactly what the authors claim to bypass. Without experiments showing how much performance would improve by adding these elements, the contribution appears overstated.

2. **Lack of ablation study for key components**: The paper presents a "Joint" model, but doesn't properly isolate whether the gains come from the full-state representation, the greedy inference, or both. The "Semantics-only" and "Syntax-only" variants provide limited insight since they discard entire components rather than systematically ablate features. For instance, the paper doesn't compare their full-state representation against a model that uses expert features but still employs a stack LSTM.

3. **Inadequate explanation for performance gains**: The paper claims their model "bypasses expensive features" but doesn't explain *why* this approach works better than previous joint models that used similar features. The authors acknowledge that their performance "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" - yet they don't investigate whether their full-state representation could potentially capture the same information as those features. This is a critical omission.

4. **Questionable generalization of "greedy vs. beam"**: The paper presents greedy inference as a key innovation, but the runtime advantage is primarily due to the single-core implementation rather than the greedy approach itself. The paper doesn't compare their greedy model to a beam-search version of their own model (which would be a fairer comparison), nor does it address whether the greedy approach causes significant performance degradation compared to optimal decoding.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the full-state representation versus the greedy inference? For instance, how would a beam-search version of your model perform?

2. You acknowledge that your model doesn't match the performance of expert-crafted feature systems. How much would adding features like "path between predicate and argument" (Gildea and Jurafsky, 2002) improve your model? Would these features be redundant given your full-state representation?

3. The paper states that "the specific advance we employ is the stack LSTM" - but Dyer et al. (2015) already used stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing. What makes the application to *joint* parsing fundamentally different and more challenging than syntactic parsing alone?

## Conclusion

This is a well-executed paper that makes a valid contribution to the field by demonstrating that full-state representation learning can effectively replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing. The runtime advantage is a significant practical contribution. However, the paper overstates the novelty and significance of the work by not adequately addressing the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems that use different (but complementary) techniques. The authors should temper their claims to better reflect the actual contribution, which is a practical, efficient joint parser that doesn't require expert features - not necessarily a fundamentally superior approach to joint parsing.

The paper would be strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of how their approach complements rather than replaces existing techniques, and by providing ablation studies that isolate the contribution of their key innovations. With these improvements, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid but somewhat incremental advance with an overstatement of its novelty.
