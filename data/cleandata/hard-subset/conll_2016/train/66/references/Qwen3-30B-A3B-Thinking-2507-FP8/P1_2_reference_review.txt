

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, while using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The main contributions of the work are:

1. A novel approach to joint parsing that learns representations for the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that greedy inference can achieve competitive performance on joint parsing tasks, which is a significant departure from previous joint parsing work that relied on beam search.
3. A fast, end-to-end system that achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL shared tasks with an impressive runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution to eliminating feature engineering**: The paper makes a compelling case that representation learning can replace expensive expert-crafted features traditionally used in joint parsing. By learning representations for the entire parser state rather than just the top items on the stack, the authors demonstrate that a simpler model architecture can outperform previous joint models that relied on extensive feature engineering. This is an important insight for the field, as it shows how neural approaches can reduce reliance on manual feature design.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear performance gains**: The authors provide thorough evaluation showing their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. The results in Tables 2 and 3 clearly demonstrate that their joint model surpasses all prior work, including Henderson et al. (2008) and its variants. This is a significant achievement given that previous joint models often struggled to match pipeline systems.

3. **Practical speed advantage**: The runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for the entire CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core is a substantial improvement over competing systems. The authors compare it to Lei et al. (2015), which takes 439.9±42 seconds, showing their model is nearly 2.5 times faster. This speed advantage makes the model more practical for real-world applications where parsing speed matters.

4. **Competitive multilingual results**: The paper demonstrates that the model adapts well to different languages, with results comparable to the top systems on CoNLL 2009 non-English datasets. This shows the model's generalizability and potential for broader application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing is interesting, the paper builds heavily on Henderson et al. (2008) and Dyer et al. (2015). The main contribution appears to be the adaptation of stack LSTMs to joint parsing rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors themselves acknowledge this by stating "it draws from the algorithmic insights of the incremental structure building approach of Henderson et al. (2008)," which suggests the novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why their approach works better. There's no ablation study showing the contribution of each component (e.g., the full state representation vs. just the stack top). The results in Table 2 and 3 show that their joint model outperforms others, but don't explain the specific mechanisms behind this improvement. This weakens the claim that the "entire algorithmic state" representation is the key innovation.

3. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: While the paper acknowledges that their system doesn't match the top expert-crafted feature-based systems, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of when their approach would be preferable. The authors claim their model is "comparable to the top systems" but don't clarify the trade-offs between accuracy, speed, and implementation complexity. The evaluation primarily compares to previous joint models rather than the best-performing systems overall.

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, the paper would be stronger with additional evaluation on other datasets or more thorough analysis of performance across different languages and domains. The multilingual results are promising but limited to CoNLL 2009, which doesn't provide enough evidence of robustness.

5. **Insufficient detail on training and hyperparameters**: The authors mention "careful tuning is expected to yield improvements" but don't provide details about their hyperparameter tuning process or the specific parameters used. This makes it difficult to reproduce their results or understand the full potential of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to use "no expert-crafted features at all," but still relies on pretrained embeddings and learned vector representations. Could you clarify the distinction between "expert-crafted features" and the representations your model uses, and how this differs from other neural models that use pretrained embeddings?

2. The authors state that their model "outperforms all previous joint parsing models," but the results don't show statistically significant differences in most cases. Could you provide statistical significance tests for your main results to better support this claim?

3. The paper mentions that their system "is on par with the top three systems in most cases" for multilingual results. Could you provide more details about the top systems they're comparing to, including their architectures and computational requirements, to better understand the trade-offs between your approach and the state-of-the-art?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the joint parsing literature by demonstrating that a simpler, end-to-end neural approach can achieve strong performance without expert-crafted features. The speed advantage is particularly compelling for practical applications. However, the novelty of the approach is somewhat limited, and the paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of why their method works better and more detailed comparisons to the best-performing systems. With these improvements, this could be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, but with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses outlined in this review.
