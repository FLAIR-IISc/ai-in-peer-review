

# Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a new joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state (rather than just the top items on the stack) and employs greedy inference instead of beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets while being significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core).

## Main Contributions
1. A joint parsing model that claims to learn representations for the entire algorithmic state rather than just top stack items
2. A speed advantage over previous systems (177.6s vs 439.9s for Lei et al.)
3. The claim that greedy inference can be competitive with beam search for joint parsing

## Strengths
1. The runtime speed claim is plausible and could be useful in practical applications, particularly for resource-constrained environments.
2. The open-source implementation promise is valuable for reproducibility and community adoption.
3. The multilingual results are generally competitive across several languages, demonstrating reasonable generalization.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art systems**: The authors acknowledge their model doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) but fail to properly contextualize this gap. They don't compare against recent neural systems that use similar architectures (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015) that have shown strong performance. The paper claims to "outperform all previous joint parsing models" but doesn't demonstrate superiority over the most recent state-of-the-art, which is a critical omission for a paper claiming to advance the field.

2. **Misleading "no expert-crafted features" claim**: The authors claim they "use no expert-crafted features at all," but this is inaccurate. They use pretrained embeddings (which are a form of feature engineering), require careful hyperparameter tuning (a form of expert knowledge), and rely on specific neural architecture choices. The paper fails to clarify what they mean by "expert-crafted features" and how their approach differs from standard neural feature engineering practices.

3. **Insufficient validation of greedy inference**: The paper claims that "entirely greedy inference" is beneficial, but provides no evidence that greedy inference is better than beam search for this task. They acknowledge that optimal decoding (beam search) is generally better for parsing, yet don't compare their greedy model against a beam search version of their own architecture. Without this comparison, the claim that greedy inference is superior is unsubstantiated.

4. **Lack of ablation studies to isolate key innovations**: The authors present "Semantics-only" and "Syntax-only" variants but don't conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of representing the "entire algorithmic state." Without these, it's unclear whether their performance gains stem from this specific innovation or other aspects of their model architecture.

5. **Unjustified claim of superiority over established methods**: The paper claims their model "outperforms all previous joint parsing models," but the comparison is limited to specific prior work (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013) while ignoring more recent state-of-the-art systems. This is misleading and fails to establish the true significance of their contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify what you mean by "no expert-crafted features" and how your approach differs from using pretrained embeddings as features? The paper states "it uses no expert-crafted features at all," but pretrained embeddings are a form of feature engineering.

2. Why did you choose greedy inference over beam search without providing evidence that greedy is better for joint syntactic-semantic parsing? Could you provide a beam search variant of your model for comparison?

3. Can you provide ablation studies showing how much of your performance gain comes from representing the entire algorithmic state versus other aspects of your model?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an incremental extension to existing stack LSTM approaches for joint parsing. While the runtime speed claim is potentially useful, the paper fails to establish its contribution as a meaningful advancement over the current state-of-the-art. The lack of proper comparison to recent neural systems, misleading claims about feature engineering, and insufficient validation of key innovations (greedy inference, full state representation) significantly undermine the paper's claims. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their evaluation and analysis to meet the high standards of the CoNLL conference.
