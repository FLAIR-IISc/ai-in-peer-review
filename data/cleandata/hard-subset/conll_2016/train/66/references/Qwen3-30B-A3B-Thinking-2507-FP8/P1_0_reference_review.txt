

# Peer Review for CONLL

## Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser state (not just top items on the stack) using stack LSTMs, and employs greedy inference rather than beam search. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks while being significantly faster than competing systems. The model eliminates the need for expert-crafted features by learning comprehensive representations of the parser's algorithmic state.

## Main Contributions (Ordered by Strength)
1. **Learning representations for the entire algorithmic state**: The paper demonstrates that learning representations for the full parser state (not just top items) using stack LSTMs can replace expert-crafted features, leading to superior performance on joint parsing tasks.
2. **Demonstrating the effectiveness of greedy inference for joint parsing**: The work shows that greedy inference can achieve competitive performance without the computational cost of beam search, making the model more practical for real-world applications.
3. **Extending stack LSTMs to joint syntactic-semantic parsing**: The authors successfully apply stack LSTMs, previously used for syntactic parsing, to the more complex joint parsing task, showing the technique's versatility.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results**: The paper provides comprehensive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating that the model outperforms all previous joint parsing models. The results are thorough, covering both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation, and the model achieves competitive performance with the top systems at CoNLL 2009.

2. **Computational efficiency**: The model's speed (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) is a significant practical contribution, being nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015). This makes the system more viable for real-world applications.

3. **Thorough ablation studies**: The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid, and Joint models) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component of the model, providing valuable insights into the importance of joint modeling.

4. **Clear technical presentation**: The paper provides detailed descriptions of the transition-based parsing procedure, stack LSTM architecture, and composition functions. The use of tables to illustrate transitions and the architecture makes the technical content accessible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems**: While the paper compares to previous joint parsing models, it does not sufficiently compare to the most recent state-of-the-art SRL systems that use neural networks with expert-crafted features (e.g., Lei et al. 2015, Täckström et al. 2015). The paper states "Our system's performance does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems," which undermines the significance of their claims. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper.

2. **Lack of analysis of model limitations**: The paper mentions that the Joint model overfits to WSJ in the out-of-domain setting but provides no detailed analysis of why this happens or how to address it. A deeper exploration of the model's limitations would make the contribution more valuable.

3. **Insufficient hyperparameter tuning discussion**: The paper states that hyperparameters were "set intuitively" and that "careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." This weakens the reported results, as it suggests the performance might not be optimal. The authors should provide more details about their hyperparameter selection process.

4. **Limited theoretical justification**: While the paper claims that "linguistic theory and researcher intuitions offer less guidance in feature design" for greedy models, it provides no evidence to support this claim or explain why learning the entire algorithmic state is theoretically superior to the previous approaches.

5. **No analysis of error cases**: The paper does not provide any analysis of where the model fails or what types of sentences it handles poorly. Understanding these limitations would be valuable for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "Our system's performance does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems." How much better would the performance be if the authors integrated some of the key features used in those systems (e.g., the "path" between predicate and argument)? Would the performance gap close if these features were added to the model?

2. The paper mentions that the Joint model overfits to WSJ in the out-of-domain setting. Can you provide more details about this overfitting? What specific aspects of the WSJ training data are causing the overfitting, and how could this be addressed?

3. The paper states that "careful tuning is expected to yield improvements." Did you perform a systematic hyperparameter search, and if not, why not? What specific hyperparameters would you recommend tuning for optimal performance?

4. The paper mentions that the model achieves comparable results to the top systems at CoNLL 2009, but doesn't specify which specific systems. Can you clarify which systems you're comparing to and why you believe your model is comparable?

5. The paper uses pretrained embeddings for Chinese but notes that without them, the Chinese parser was "on par with the others." How much does the performance of the Chinese model depend on the quality of the pretrained embeddings, and how might this affect the model's performance in low-resource languages?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of joint parsing by demonstrating that learning the entire algorithmic state representation can eliminate the need for expert-crafted features while maintaining strong performance. While the empirical results are compelling, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art systems, a deeper analysis of the model's limitations, and a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.
