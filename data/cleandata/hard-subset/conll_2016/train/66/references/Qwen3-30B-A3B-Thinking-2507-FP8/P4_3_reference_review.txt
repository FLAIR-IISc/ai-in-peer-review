

# Review

## Summary of Paper and Main Contributions
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a stack LSTM to learn representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer) rather than just the top items on the stacks. The primary contribution is the novel state representation approach that enables the parser to avoid expert-crafted features. The second contribution is the demonstration that greedy inference (rather than beam search) can be effectively used for joint parsing, and the third is the strong empirical performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Novel state representation**: The paper makes a significant technical contribution by applying stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser's state. This is a clear advancement over previous joint parsing models that relied on local features or partial state representations. The implementation details of how the state is composed from the four stack LSTMs (for syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) are well-justified and technically sound.

2. **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, outperforming all previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013). The performance gains are statistically significant, with the joint model achieving 88.6% LAS on CoNLL 2009 compared to 87.9% for Henderson et al. (2013).

3. **Practical efficiency**: The parser demonstrates excellent computational efficiency with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core, which is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015). This makes the system practical for real-world applications where speed is critical.

4. **Multilingual performance**: The model achieves competitive results across multiple languages on CoNLL 2009, showing good generalization. The authors demonstrate that the model adapts well to different languages, with Chinese performance being particularly strong when pretrained embeddings are available.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art SRL systems**: While the paper correctly acknowledges that their model does not match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), it fails to provide a thorough analysis of why their approach is still valuable despite this gap. The paper should better articulate how their method could be integrated with these systems to achieve both high performance and computational efficiency.

2. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper compares different variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint) but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the state representation versus the greedy inference. For example, it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the state representation versus the greedy inference approach.

3. **Dependence on pretrained embeddings**: The paper notes that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance, and without them, it was on par with other systems. This suggests the model's performance is heavily dependent on the quality of the embeddings, which could limit its applicability in low-resource settings where such embeddings are unavailable.

4. **Limited error analysis**: The paper does not provide any analysis of the types of errors the model makes, which would help understand its limitations and potential directions for improvement. A detailed error analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution by identifying specific failure modes.

5. **Overstatement of novelty**: The paper claims to "outperform all previous joint parsing models," but the comparison is limited to previous joint models. The paper does not adequately address how their approach differs from and improves upon the state-of-the-art in semantic role labeling, which is the ultimate goal of joint parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the joint model outperforms the hybrid model in the training domain but the hybrid model pulls ahead out-of-domain. What do you think is the main reason for this overfitting, and how would you address it?

2. How do you expect the model to perform on languages with very limited pretrained embeddings available, given the Chinese performance dependence on them?

3. What evidence do you have that greedy inference (as opposed to beam search) is the main reason for the performance gains, rather than the state representation?

4. The paper mentions that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model." How would you expect future work to integrate your approach with these orthogonal advances to achieve both high performance and computational efficiency?

5. The paper reports strong multilingual results but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of language-specific performance differences. Could you elaborate on why Japanese (a small-data case with 4,393 training examples) performed relatively poorly compared to other languages?
