

# Review for CONLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire parser's algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The model uses entirely greedy inference rather than beam search, and achieves competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks. The main contributions are:

1. A novel approach to joint parsing that learns representations for the entire parser state rather than just top items on stacks, enabling end-to-end learning without expert features.
2. Demonstration that neural representations can effectively replace expensive, expert-crafted features traditionally used in joint parsing, particularly for semantic role labeling.
3. A significantly faster parsing system (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) compared to previous joint models.

## Strengths

1. **Innovative representation learning approach:** The paper makes a compelling case for using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state rather than just top items, which is a significant methodological advance. This approach avoids the need for labor-intensive feature engineering that has traditionally been a hallmark of joint parsing systems. The authors provide strong evidence that this representation learning technique can capture the necessary information for parsing without manual feature design.

2. **Competitive performance with practical advantages:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, matching or exceeding previous joint models while being substantially faster (nearly 2.5x faster than comparable pipeline models). This speed advantage is particularly valuable for real-world applications where parsing latency matters.

3. **Strong multilingual results:** The model demonstrates good generalization across languages (English, German, Spanish, Chinese), with results comparable to top systems on non-English CoNLL 2009 datasets. This indicates the approach has broad applicability, with the notable exception of Chinese where pretrained embeddings significantly boost performance.

4. **Clear theoretical justification:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of why greedy parsing with full-state representations could be advantageous, particularly for joint parsing where linguistic theory offers less guidance for feature design. The comparison to pipeline approaches that rely on expensive features is well-justified.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to recent neural SRL systems:** While the paper acknowledges that state-of-the-art SRL systems outperform their model, it doesn't compare to the most recent neural approaches (e.g., transformer-based models) that have emerged since the CoNLL 2009 shared task. The paper's claim that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model" is not thoroughly substantiated with recent comparisons.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies:** The paper compares "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," and "Hybrid" models, but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of how each component (stack LSTMs, greedy inference, full-state representation) contributes to the overall performance. A more detailed ablation would strengthen the claims about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed representation learning approach.

3. **Lack of error analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but doesn't provide a qualitative analysis of the model's errors. Such an analysis would help identify the specific limitations of the approach and suggest concrete directions for improvement, which would be valuable for the community.

4. **Overreliance on pretrained embeddings for Chinese:** The paper notes that Chinese performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings, but doesn't explore how this affects the model's generalizability or how it could be improved for languages with limited pretrained resources. This is a significant limitation for a model claiming multilingual applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to recent neural SRL systems (e.g., those using transformers) that have been published since the CoNLL 2009 shared task? Could your approach be combined with these newer techniques?

2. The paper suggests the Joint model overfits to WSJ (in-domain) data. What specific aspects of your model might be contributing to this, and what techniques could be applied to improve out-of-domain generalization?

3. You note that Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings. What alternative approaches could be used for languages with limited pretrained resources, and how might your model be adapted to handle these cases?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the exact novelty compared to Dyer et al. (2015), who also used stack LSTMs for parsing. While the paper mentions the use of stack LSTMs for joint parsing, it's not entirely clear how this differs from the syntactic parsing work by Dyer et al.

The evaluation on non-English datasets is promising, but the paper would be stronger with more discussion of why the model performs well on some languages (like Spanish and German) but appears to depend more on pretrained embeddings for Chinese.

The authors are correct that the model doesn't match the top systems that use expert-crafted features, but they should more explicitly acknowledge that their approach is complementary to those systems rather than a direct replacement. A discussion of how their model could be integrated with these existing techniques would strengthen the paper's contribution.
