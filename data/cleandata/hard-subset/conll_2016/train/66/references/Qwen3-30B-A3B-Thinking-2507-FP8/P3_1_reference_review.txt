

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on hand-crafted features. The key contributions are: (1) a novel representation learning approach for joint parsing that captures the full parser state, (2) a practical greedy parsing system that outperforms previous joint models while being significantly faster, and (3) a comprehensive analysis of the interactions between syntactic and semantic parsing components through ablation studies.

## Strengths

1. **Novel representation learning approach**: The paper makes a significant methodological contribution by demonstrating that learning representations for the entire parser state (rather than just the top items on the stack) can effectively replace hand-crafted features. The stack LSTM approach builds upon Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing but extends it to the joint parsing task. The ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) provide strong evidence that the full joint model benefits from the interaction between syntax and semantics.

2. **Practical efficiency**: The parser achieves an impressive speed of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core. This is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015), making it highly suitable for practical applications. The authors convincingly argue that this speed advantage is particularly valuable for real-world systems where parsing latency matters.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English and multilingual), including comparisons to previous joint parsing models and state-of-the-art systems. The results demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and English 2009 tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems**: The paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015). However, the authors don't provide a thorough analysis of *why* their approach doesn't perform as well, or how their method could be integrated with these approaches. This is a significant limitation since the paper's main claim is that their approach "bypasses expensive features," yet they don't show how to combine their representation learning with the best features from existing systems.

2. **Lack of justification for greedy inference**: While the authors claim that their approach uses "entirely greedy inference rather than beam search," they don't adequately justify this choice or analyze its impact on performance. For parsing tasks, greedy inference often leads to suboptimal solutions compared to beam search or optimal decoding. The paper would benefit from a comparison of parse quality between greedy and beam search on a subset of the data, as well as an explanation of why greedy inference is appropriate for this task.

3. **Overstated claim about "entire algorithmic state"**: The authors claim to learn representations for "the parser's entire algorithmic state," but the paper doesn't provide evidence that the additional information captured by their approach is actually being utilized effectively. The stack LSTM representation is still limited to the contents of the stacks and buffer, not the entire algorithmic state. This overstatement undermines the significance of the methodological contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that your system "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" and acknowledges that many advances in those systems are "orthogonal to our model." Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how your model could be integrated with these approaches to achieve better results? Specifically, how might your representation learning be combined with the high-performing features from systems like Lei et al. (2015)?

2. You claim to use "entirely greedy inference" but don't compare it to beam search. Could you provide a comparison of parse quality between greedy and beam search on a small subset of the test data? How does the greedy approach affect the labeled attachment score and semantic F1?

3. The paper mentions that your model "achieves comparable multilingual results to the top systems at CoNLL 2009." However, the Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings, while the Japanese model has limited training data (4,393 examples). Could you clarify how much of the multilingual performance is due to the model architecture versus the availability of pretrained embeddings?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to joint parsing by demonstrating that learning representations for the entire parser state can replace hand-crafted features. The empirical results are solid, and the speed advantage is a significant practical contribution. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems, a justification for the greedy inference approach, and a more accurate characterization of the "entire algorithmic state" claim.

The paper is well-written, follows the CoNLL shared task guidelines carefully, and provides a clear technical contribution to the field. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the literature on joint parsing.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
