

# Review of "A New Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary
The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that extends the stack LSTM approach of Dyer et al. (2015) to joint parsing. The authors claim two key innovations: learning representations for the entire algorithmic state (not just top stack items) and using entirely greedy inference. They report improved performance on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks compared to previous joint models, with faster runtime than some pipeline approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of stack LSTMs to joint syntactic-semantic parsing (a straightforward extension of existing work)
2. Demonstration of competitive performance on CoNLL shared tasks without using expert-crafted features (though not matching state-of-the-art)
3. Efficient runtime characteristics for practical deployment

## Strengths
1. The parsing algorithm and transition system are clearly described, which aids reproducibility.
2. Comprehensive evaluation on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, including multilingual results.
3. The runtime advantage (177.6±18 seconds on single core) is a practical contribution that could benefit real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty and inadequate comparison**: The primary claim of learning "the entire algorithmic state" is not novel. Dyer et al. (2015) already introduced stack LSTMs that summarize the entire stack state for syntactic parsing. The paper fails to provide a clear ablation study demonstrating that the "entire state" representation is the key to the performance gains. The authors acknowledge their system doesn't match the top pipeline systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014), which use expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses. This undermines the claimed significance of their approach.

2. **Insufficient validation against established baselines**: The paper compares only to previous joint parsing models, not to the state-of-the-art pipeline systems that use expert-crafted features. The authors admit their model "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems," which is a significant limitation. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach would outperform these systems if integrated with their feature sets, making the claimed advantage questionable.

3. **Inadequate evidence for the core innovation**: The paper states "it learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state, not just the top items on the stack," but fails to demonstrate why this is necessary or superior to more localized representations. Without ablation studies showing that the "entire state" representation is responsible for the performance gains, the innovation claim remains unsubstantiated. The paper's results could just as well be attributed to using neural networks generally, rather than the specific representation approach.

4. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper cites work up to 2015 but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches that have emerged in the field, such as those using transformer architectures or contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT). Given the rapid advancement in neural NLP, this omission weakens the paper's claim of being competitive with state-of-the-art.

5. **Overstated claims about "bypassing expensive features"**: The authors claim their approach "bypasses those expensive features," but the paper doesn't demonstrate that their method actually achieves comparable performance without these features. The fact that their system doesn't match the performance of systems using these features suggests the "bypass" is not truly effective, and they're simply trading off performance for efficiency.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide ablation studies showing that the "entire algorithmic state" representation is responsible for the performance gains, rather than the neural network architecture itself?

2. How would your system perform if integrated with expert-crafted features from pipeline systems (e.g., syntactic path features), which the authors acknowledge are a mainstay of high-performance SRL?

3. Why did the authors choose not to compare with more recent neural approaches to joint parsing (e.g., those using transformer models or contextual embeddings) that have become standard in the field?

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a straightforward extension of existing work to joint parsing, with limited evidence supporting its claimed innovations. The performance gains are marginal compared to state-of-the-art pipeline systems that use expert-crafted features, and the paper fails to provide adequate validation of its core innovation. While the runtime efficiency is a practical contribution, it doesn't compensate for the lack of methodological novelty and insufficient validation against established baselines. The paper would benefit significantly from a more rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art approaches and a clearer demonstration of why the "entire algorithmic state" representation is necessary.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence supporting the claimed novelty and performance advantages over established methods. The paper presents an incremental extension of prior work without demonstrating sufficient methodological rigor or compelling evidence for its central claims.
