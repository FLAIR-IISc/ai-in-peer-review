- Learns representations for the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) with stack LSTMs, removing expert-crafted features.  
- Demonstrates greedy inference can be competitive for joint syntactic and semantic parsing, unlike prior beam-search approaches.  
- Presents a fast end-to-end system achieving state-of-the-art on CoNLL 2008/2009 English with 177.6±18s runtime on a single core.

- Eliminates feature engineering by learning full parser-state representations, outperforming prior joint models using hand-crafted features.  
- Strong empirical results: outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks.  
- Practical speed advantage (~2.5× faster than Lei et al. 2015) for CoNLL 2009 English parsing.  
- Competitive multilingual performance on CoNLL 2009 non-English datasets, indicating generalizability.

- Novelty is limited: approach mainly adapts Henderson (2008) and Dyer (2015) ideas to joint parsing rather than introducing a fundamentally new method.  
- No ablation studies to isolate contributions of components (e.g., full state representation vs. stack-top).  
- Insufficient comparison to top expert-crafted-feature systems; trade-offs between accuracy, speed, and complexity not fully analyzed.  
- Evaluation restricted to CoNLL 2008/2009; broader datasets, languages, and domains are needed to demonstrate robustness.  
- Lacks detailed training and hyperparameter tuning information, hindering reproducibility.

- Clarify how “no expert-crafted features” differs from using pretrained embeddings and learned representations used by other neural models.  
- Provide statistical significance tests for main results to support claims of outperforming previous joint models.  
- Detail the top multilingual systems used for comparison (architectures and computational requirements) to clarify trade-offs.

- Recommendation: Accept — valuable demonstration of a simpler end-to-end neural joint parser with strong speed and competitive accuracy, but should address analysis, comparisons, and reproducibility concerns.
