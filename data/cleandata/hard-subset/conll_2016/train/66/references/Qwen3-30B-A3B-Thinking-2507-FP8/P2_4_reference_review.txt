

# Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state of a transition-based parser rather than just the top items on the stack. The key contributions are: 1) a stack LSTM-based representation of the complete parsing state that eliminates the need for expert-crafted features; 2) a greedy inference approach that outperforms previous joint models; and 3) a practical system with significantly faster runtime than competing approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with thorough comparison**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous joint parsing models on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons with Henderson et al. (2008), Gesmundo et al. (2009), Titov et al. (2009), and others, showing their model outperforms all previous joint parsing approaches. The results on multilingual data also demonstrate good generalization.

2. **Practical performance advantage**: The parser's runtime (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) (439.9±42 seconds), making it highly suitable for practical applications. This speed advantage is particularly valuable in real-world NLP pipelines where computational efficiency matters.

3. **Clear technical contribution**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the transition system (Table 1), the stack LSTM architecture (Figure 3), and the composition functions (Equations 3-5). The approach of using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state is a significant technical advance that properly justifies the paper's main claim.

4. **Appropriate scope and limitations**: The authors correctly acknowledge that their model doesn't match the performance of systems using expert-crafted features or additional datasets, and they properly identify orthogonal advances that could be integrated in future work. This balanced perspective strengthens the paper's credibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with models up to 2015, but the field has advanced significantly since then. The paper should compare with more recent state-of-the-art systems (e.g., those using transformer-based architectures or more sophisticated neural representations) to properly establish the current standing of the proposed approach. The authors mention "state-of-the-art SRL systems" but don't compare with the most recent models.

2. **Insufficient analysis of the key innovation**: While the paper claims that learning representations for the entire algorithmic state is the key innovation, it lacks an ablation study showing how much of the performance gain comes specifically from this aspect versus other components (e.g., the specific transition set, the greedy inference approach). This weakens the claim of novelty.

3. **Limited error analysis**: The paper provides numerical results but doesn't analyze the specific types of errors the model makes or how they compare to previous models. A qualitative error analysis would help understand the model's strengths and weaknesses and provide insights for future improvements.

4. **Underutilization of the multilingual results**: The multilingual results (Table 4) are presented but not thoroughly analyzed. The authors note that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance, but don't explore how this dependency affects the model's generalizability or what could be done to improve performance in low-resource languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to more recent state-of-the-art joint parsing systems (e.g., those from 2016-2018 that use advanced neural architectures like transformers)?

2. Could you provide an ablation study showing how much of the performance gain comes specifically from learning representations for the entire parser state versus other aspects of your model?

3. What specific types of errors does your model make, and how do they differ from errors made by previous joint parsing models?

4. The paper mentions that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance—could you discuss how this limitation might be addressed for low-resource languages?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a meaningful contribution to joint parsing by demonstrating that neural representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features. The model is well-designed, the results are convincing, and the runtime advantage is significant for practical applications. However, the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art systems and insufficient analysis of the key innovation limit the paper's impact. With minor revisions addressing these weaknesses, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

The paper meets the criteria for acceptance with minor revisions, particularly regarding the comparison with more recent work and the ablation study. The practical contribution of a fast, feature-free joint parser is valuable and well-supported by the evidence provided.
