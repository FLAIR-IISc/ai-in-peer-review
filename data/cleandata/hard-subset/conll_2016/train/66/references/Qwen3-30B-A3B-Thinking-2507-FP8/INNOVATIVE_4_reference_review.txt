

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that represents a significant conceptual shift from previous approaches. The key innovation is learning representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state (not just top items on the stack or recent states) using stack LSTMs, while using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The system outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks and achieves a runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core.

## Main Contributions
1. The novel use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state for joint parsing, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. The demonstration that entirely greedy inference can achieve competitive performance compared to beam search approaches in joint parsing.
3. A practical, fast system that avoids the need for expensive features derived from full syntactic parses.

## Strengths
1. **Conceptual novelty**: The paper's core contribution—representing the entire algorithmic state rather than just top items on the stack—represents a bold conceptual shift that challenges conventional parsing approaches. This is a significant departure from the standard practice of focusing on local features and represents a major advance in representation learning for parsing.

2. **Practical significance**: The system's speed (177.6±18 seconds on a single core) is impressive, being almost 2.5 times faster than state-of-the-art pipeline models. This practical advantage makes the approach immediately useful for real-world applications, which is often overlooked in NLP research.

3. **Generalizability**: The system performs well across multiple languages (English, German, Spanish, Chinese), demonstrating the approach's broad applicability. The multilingual results are comparable to the top systems at CoNLL 2009, showing the approach isn't language-specific.

4. **Theoretical insight**: The paper correctly identifies that for greedy models, linguistic theory offers less guidance in feature design, making representation learning a more natural approach. This insight has broader implications for NLP beyond just parsing.

5. **Simplicity**: The approach's simplicity (no beam search, no expert features) is a strength in itself, demonstrating that complex systems aren't always necessary for good performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Performance gap**: The system doesn't match the performance of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), which use optimal decoding or exploit additional datasets. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide a clear path to bridge this gap.

2. **Limited comparison**: The comparison with state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited, with the paper focusing mainly on older joint parsing models rather than the most recent neural approaches. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claims about novelty and competitiveness.

3. **Lack of deeper analysis**: The paper claims that their approach works better because linguistic theory offers less guidance for greedy models, but doesn't provide empirical evidence or deeper analysis to support this specific claim. A more thorough investigation of why the entire-state representation works better would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Missed opportunity for integration**: The paper mentions that incorporating morphological features or character-based embeddings could improve performance, but doesn't explore this, which is a missed opportunity to further validate and strengthen the approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more detailed analysis on why the entire-state representation leads to better performance, beyond the general claim about linguistic theory offering less guidance for greedy models?

2. How would you integrate the advanced features used in top-performing systems (like Zhao et al., 2009) with your approach to potentially close the performance gap while maintaining the speed advantage?

3. The paper mentions that the model is fast, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other recent fast neural joint parsers. Could you clarify how your runtime compares to the most recent state-of-the-art neural joint parsers?

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant conceptual advance that challenges conventional parsing approaches. The novelty of learning representations for the entire algorithmic state rather than just local features is compelling, and the practical benefits of speed and simplicity are substantial. While the performance gap with the absolute top systems is notable, the paper's contribution is sufficiently novel and valuable to warrant acceptance. The authors have made a bold, conceptually interesting move that could inspire new directions in parsing research, which aligns with the conference's interest in novel approaches.
