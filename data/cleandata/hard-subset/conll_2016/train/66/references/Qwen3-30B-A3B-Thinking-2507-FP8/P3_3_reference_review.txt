

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a stack LSTM to represent the entire algorithmic state of a transition-based parser, rather than just the top elements of the stack. The authors claim two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire parser state (not just top items), and (2) using entirely greedy inference instead of beam search. They report competitive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, with a runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the English test set.

The primary contributions I identify are:
1. A novel application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntactic and semantic parsing through the entire algorithmic state
2. A demonstration that greedy inference with full-state representation can achieve competitive performance with more complex systems
3. A comprehensive evaluation showing the model's effectiveness across multiple languages

## Strengths

1. **Strong methodological foundation**: The paper builds effectively on Dyer et al. (2015) by extending stack LSTMs to joint parsing. The transition system (Table 1) is well-designed, clearly explained, and properly handles the complexities of semantic parsing (e.g., M-SELF for self-dependencies). The implementation details, including the four stack LSTMs and composition functions, are technically sound and well-motivated.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides thorough results across multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, CoNLL 2009 English, and multilingual CoNLL 2009). The comparison to previous work is extensive and well-organized, with careful attention to the different tasks (2008 vs. 2009). The runtime comparison (177.6s vs. 439.9s for Lei et al., 2015) is particularly compelling for practical applications.

3. **Clear presentation of limitations**: The authors honestly acknowledge their model's limitations compared to top systems (e.g., not using expert-crafted features), and correctly identify orthogonal improvements that could be integrated (e.g., exact inference, additional datasets).

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to "use no expert-crafted features at all," which is misleading. While they don't use features derived from full parse trees, they do use word embeddings (pretrained or learned), POS tags, and other linguistic features. The key innovation is the *representation of the entire state*, not the absence of features. This mischaracterization weakens their contribution claim.

2. **Lack of comparative analysis**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain *why* their approach outperforms previous joint models. They show results but don't provide error analysis or ablation studies to determine what aspects of their model (full-state representation vs. greedy inference) are most impactful. The comparison to Henderson et al. (2013) is limited to the same transition system, but their model uses features while the authors' model doesn't.

3. **Insufficient justification for greedy inference**: While they claim greedy inference is "faster" and "simpler," they don't properly justify why greedy inference works well for this task when previous work (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013) used beam search. The paper doesn't compare greedy vs. beam search performance with their own model, which would strengthen their claim.

4. **Multilingual results lack depth**: The multilingual results (Table 4) show the model adapts well to other languages but don't analyze *why* Chinese benefits from pretrained embeddings while other languages don't. The paper notes Chinese relies on pretrained embeddings but doesn't explore the implications for low-resource languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "no expert-crafted features," but the model uses word embeddings, POS tags, and other linguistic features. How do you reconcile this with your claim, and how do these compare to the "expert-crafted features" used by previous systems?

2. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to determine whether the key innovation is the full-state representation or the greedy inference. Could you provide results comparing greedy vs. beam search with your model?

3. The CoNLL 2009 results show Joint overfits to WSJ (in-domain) while Hybrid performs better out-of-domain. What specific aspects of the joint model cause this overfitting, and how might it be mitigated?

## Conclusion

This is a solid contribution that advances joint parsing by leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state. The model is fast and achieves competitive results, making it practically useful. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by more thorough comparative analysis and clarification of their feature usage. The work is valuable for its practical efficiency and clear demonstration of neural approaches to joint parsing, but it doesn't represent a fundamental breakthrough in the field. With some revisions addressing the weaknesses noted, this would be a strong submission.
