

# Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on a transition-based algorithm that learns representations for the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. The main contributions are:

1. A novel representation learning approach that captures the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer) rather than just top items, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. A demonstration that greedy inference can effectively replace beam search in joint parsing while maintaining strong performance.
3. Competitive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, with significantly faster runtime (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data) compared to state-of-the-art pipeline systems.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Innovation in Representation Learning**: The core contribution is the use of stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state (stacks and buffer), rather than just the top items as in previous work. This is a significant technical advance that enables the model to capture contextual information without expert-crafted features. The paper provides a thorough explanation of how the stack LSTMs work, including composition functions for syntactic and semantic structures, and demonstrates how this approach enables the parser to outperform previous joint models.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, outperforming all previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013). The results are comprehensive, including comparisons to pipeline systems (though slightly behind the top pipeline systems that use expert-crafted features). The multilingual results are also strong, with competitive performance across English, German, Spanish, and Chinese.

3. **Practical Efficiency**: The paper demonstrates a significant runtime advantage (nearly 2.5x faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al., 2015) while maintaining competitive accuracy. This practical advantage makes the model suitable for real-world applications where speed is critical, which is a valuable contribution beyond academic performance metrics.

4. **Clear Methodology and Implementation**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the parsing algorithm, transition constraints, and statistical model. The architecture is well-illustrated, and the composition functions for syntactic and semantic structures are clearly defined. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough to allow replication.

5. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison**: The paper includes a thorough comparison with previous joint parsing models and state-of-the-art pipeline systems. The analysis of the Semantics-only, Syntax-only, and Hybrid variants provides valuable insight into the contributions of the joint approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Overall Framework**: While the representation learning approach is novel, the parsing framework (transition-based, synchronized algorithm) is largely borrowed from Henderson et al. (2013). The paper does not sufficiently clarify how this representation learning approach fundamentally differs from previous work or why the algorithmic state representation is necessary for joint parsing.

2. **Performance Gap with Expert-Crafted Feature Systems**: The model's performance is behind the top pipeline systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) that use expert-crafted features. The authors acknowledge this but do not demonstrate how their approach could integrate these features, limiting the practical significance of the contribution.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Representation Benefits**: While the paper demonstrates that the model works well, it lacks a detailed analysis of why learning the entire algorithmic state is beneficial. An ablation study comparing the performance of the full state representation to more limited representations would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., dependence on pretrained embeddings for Chinese, performance on small-data Japanese), but does not thoroughly discuss the model's limitations in handling highly non-projective structures or cases where the transition constraints might fail.

5. **Overlooking Recent Work**: The paper does not discuss more recent neural approaches to joint parsing that might have addressed similar challenges (e.g., Kiperwasser and Goldberg, 2016), suggesting the authors may not be fully aware of the broader context of the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "no expert-crafted features at all," but uses pretrained embeddings (Gigaword) and learned representations for syntactic/semantic labels. How do these differ from "expert-crafted features" in the authors' view?

2. Could the authors provide a runtime comparison with pipeline systems that use similar feature sets (not just the top pipeline systems), to better understand the efficiency advantage?

3. The Joint model outperforms Hybrid on WSJ but underperforms on Brown. Could the authors provide more analysis of why this domain shift occurs and how the Joint model might be improved for out-of-domain data?

4. For predicates unseen in training, the model defaults to "01" sense (91.22% correct by type). How does this affect overall performance, and could the authors provide more details on the predicate sense disambiguation for unseen predicates?

5. The paper states "entirely greedy inference rather than beam search." What are the specific advantages of this choice in terms of model performance and training efficiency compared to beam search?
