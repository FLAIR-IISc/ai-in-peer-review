

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs

## Summary
This paper presents a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser based on the incremental structure building approach of Henderson et al. (2008), with two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire algorithmic state (not just top items on the stack) using stack LSTMs, and (2) using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while being significantly faster.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural representation of the entire parsing state using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that greedy parsing with full-state representations can outperform previous joint parsing models that used beam search.
3. A practical, efficient parser achieving competitive performance without expensive feature engineering.

## Strengths

**1. Novel Representation of Parser State (Primary Strength)**  
The paper's most significant contribution is the use of stack LSTMs to represent the *entire* parsing state, not just the top items on the stack as in previous work. This addresses a fundamental limitation in transition-based parsing where feature engineering has been a major bottleneck. By learning a continuous representation of the full state, the model avoids the need for hand-crafted features that typically require linguistic expertise. This represents a meaningful step toward more automated parsing systems, and the results (outperforming all prior joint models) provide strong empirical validation of this approach.

**2. Demonstrated Efficiency and Practical Utility**  
The paper provides a compelling case for practical utility with the runtime claim of 177.6±18 seconds on a single core for the CoNLL 2009 English test set—nearly 2.5x faster than Lei et al. (2015). This speed advantage is particularly valuable for real-world applications where parsing latency matters. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this efficiency is achieved without sacrificing performance, making the model both theoretically interesting and practically useful.

**3. Comprehensive Comparison to Prior Work**  
The authors provide a thorough comparison to previous joint parsing models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013), showing consistent improvements across both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets. The ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provide valuable insights into the contributions of each component of the model.

## Weaknesses

**1. Inadequate Comparison to State-of-the-Art SRL Systems**  
While the authors correctly note their model doesn't match "top expert-crafted feature-based systems," they don't provide sufficient comparison to these systems. The paper acknowledges that systems like Roth and Woodsend (2014) and Lei et al. (2015) use "orthogonal" advances, but doesn't detail how their model specifically compares to these in terms of performance or why their approach might not benefit from these techniques. A more detailed analysis of where the model falls short and why would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**2. Superficial Analysis of Greedy vs. Beam Search**  
The authors claim greedy inference outperforms beam search, but provide no meaningful analysis of *why* this is the case. In parsing, beam search is typically used to overcome the limitations of greedy decisions. The paper doesn't explore the trade-offs between these approaches in this specific context, nor does it provide evidence that greedy is indeed better for this task. This is a significant omission given that beam search is standard practice in parsing.

**3. Limited Discussion of Multilingual Generalization**  
While the paper reports multilingual results, the analysis of why the model performs well on some languages but not others is superficial. The Chinese results depend on pretrained embeddings, but the paper doesn't analyze how language-specific characteristics (e.g., morphological complexity, word order) affect performance. The Japanese results (with only 4,393 training examples) suggest a data dependency that isn't sufficiently explored. A more systematic analysis of language-specific factors would strengthen the paper's contribution to multilingual parsing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the Joint model outperforms previous joint parsers but falls behind state-of-the-art SRL systems. Could you provide a more detailed comparison showing exactly where your model falls short (e.g., specific metrics on WSJ vs. Brown corpus) and what specific techniques might help close this gap?

2. The authors note that the Joint model overfits to WSJ (in-domain) while the Hybrid model performs better on out-of-domain data. Could you elaborate on why this happens and whether there are specific techniques (beyond just more training data) that could improve generalization without significantly increasing computational cost?

3. The paper mentions that Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings. How would your model perform on languages with limited pretraining resources (e.g., low-resource languages), and what strategies would you recommend for such cases?

## Conclusion

This paper presents a well-executed and significant contribution to joint parsing by demonstrating that learning representations for the entire algorithmic state can outperform previous joint parsing models while being more efficient. The work represents an important step toward reducing feature engineering in parsing systems. While the comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems could be strengthened, and the analysis of greedy inference could be more thorough, the paper makes a compelling case for the value of full-state representations in joint parsing. The demonstrated efficiency and competitive performance make this a valuable contribution to the field, with clear potential for practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
