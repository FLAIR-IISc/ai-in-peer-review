

# Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using a stack LSTM to learn representations of the entire algorithmic state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel joint parsing approach that avoids expensive syntactic features by learning full state representations; (2) a greedy inference procedure that outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks; and (3) a demonstration of the model's efficiency with a runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set.

## Strengths
1. The approach to learning representations of the entire parser state is well-motivated and represents a clear technical contribution to joint parsing. The stack LSTM approach elegantly handles the algorithmic state without relying on hand-crafted features.

2. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, with particularly strong performance compared to the Henderson et al. (2008) model.

3. The runtime efficiency claim is well-supported with specific measurements, and the comparison to Lei et al. (2015) provides a compelling practical advantage for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Numerous minor typographical and formatting errors**: The paper contains multiple instances of "Lluı́s" instead of the correct "Lluís" (in both the main text and references), inconsistent spacing around commas (e.g., "v,u" instead of "v, u"), and sentence fragments (e.g., "Hyperparameters The input representation..."). These are distracting and undermine the paper's professionalism.

2. **Lack of specific performance metrics**: While the paper claims to "outperform all previous joint parsing models," it provides no specific numerical improvements or statistical significance testing. For instance, stating "our joint model significantly outperforms" without specifying by how much or providing p-values is insufficient for a scientific claim.

3. **Inadequate comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper compares to older joint parsing models but fails to address more recent neural network-based approaches to joint parsing (e.g., Transformer-based models), which have become increasingly dominant in the field. This omission makes the contribution seem less novel in the current landscape.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide specific numerical improvements (with statistical significance) over previous joint parsing models rather than general claims of "outperforming"?

2. The paper states that the joint model "falls behind only Zhao et al. (2009)" but doesn't explain why this gap exists despite using a similar pipeline approach. Could you elaborate on this discrepancy?

3. The runtime claim of "177.6±18 seconds" is given but not specified whether this is for the entire test set or per sentence. Could you clarify this?

## Additional Comments
The paper contains several minor technical inconsistencies that require attention:
- In Section 2.2, the description of M-LEFT/M-RIGHT transitions states "do not remove the dependent from the semantic stack and buffer respectively," but Table 1 shows the buffer being modified (not removed).
- In Equation (1), the notation "elementwisemax {0,d+W[st;mt;bt,at]}" is confusing; it should use proper mathematical notation with brackets.
- The phrase "a multilingual results" (in Introduction) should be "multilingual results."

While the paper presents a well-motivated approach with strong empirical results, the numerous minor errors and lack of specific performance metrics significantly detract from its presentation. The failure to address recent neural network-based approaches also weakens the paper's contribution claim. These minor issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility and make it difficult to assess the true novelty and significance of the work.
