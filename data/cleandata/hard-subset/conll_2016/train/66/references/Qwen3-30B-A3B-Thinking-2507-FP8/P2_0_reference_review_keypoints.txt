- Introduces a joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs that represent the entire parser state and uses greedy inference.  
- Claims to outperform previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and to be much faster (≈177.6±18s on CoNLL2009 English test).

- Contribution: learns representations for the full parser state to avoid expert-crafted features.  
- Contribution: uses greedy inference to achieve competitive accuracy with much faster parsing speed.  
- Contribution: shows neural methods can replace expensive features from full syntactic parses in joint parsing.

- Strength: strong empirical improvements over prior joint models on CoNLL benchmarks (e.g., +1.3% LAS on CoNLL 2008).  
- Strength: clear technical insight that stack LSTMs can implicitly learn features missing in greedy parsers.  
- Strength: substantial practical speed advantage (~2.5× faster than a pipeline baseline).

- Weakness: lacks direct comparison to recent neural SRL/joint methods (e.g., Zhou & Xu 2015, Lei et al. 2015), weakening the “outperforms all” claim.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablation studies to separate effects of stack LSTM representations vs. greedy inference.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of why it still trails top expert-feature systems and which specific features/techniques are missing.  
- Weakness: inadequate quantitative analysis of limitations like handling crossing arcs and their impact on performance.

- Question: Why were recent neural approaches (Zhou & Xu 2015, Lei et al. 2015) not included in direct comparisons, and could their techniques be combined?  
- Question: Can the authors provide a detailed accuracy/speed trade-off comparison with other state-of-the-art systems?  
- Question: Please provide more detail on the distribution and effect of defaulting unseen predicates to sense “01” (correct 91.22% by type).

- Overall: Valuable contribution demonstrating neural joint parsing with speed advantages; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to add comparisons and stronger ablation/analysis.
