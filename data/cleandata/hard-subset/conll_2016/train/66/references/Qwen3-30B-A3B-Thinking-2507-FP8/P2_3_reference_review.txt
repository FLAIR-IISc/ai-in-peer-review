

# Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser Using Stack LSTMs"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that represents the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, rather than just the top items on the stack. The key contributions are:

1. A representation learning approach for the entire parser state (not just top items), eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that greedy inference can be effective for joint parsing without beam search.
3. Strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, with competitive performance to top systems and significantly faster runtime.

## Strengths

**1. Clear and meaningful technical contribution:** The paper makes a solid contribution in representing the entire parser state using stack LSTMs rather than just the top items. This addresses a key limitation in previous work where features were limited to the top of the stack. The explanation of how the stack LSTM works is clear and well-justified, building on Dyer et al. (2015) while extending it to joint parsing.

**2. Strong empirical results with appropriate comparisons:** The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. The results are presented with appropriate comparisons to the relevant literature, including both previous joint models and pipeline approaches. The ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) provide valuable insights into the relative contributions of syntax and semantics.

**3. Practical efficiency advantage:** The paper's claim of a 177.6±18 second runtime for parsing the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art pipeline approaches (e.g., 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015). This efficiency makes the approach more viable for real-world applications.

**4. Multilingual evaluation:** The paper demonstrates the model's adaptability to other languages (Chinese, Japanese, German, Spanish), showing it's on par with top systems in most cases. This is important for demonstrating the generalizability of the approach.

## Weaknesses

**1. Incremental contribution with limited novelty:** While the representation learning approach is novel, the overall architecture builds heavily on Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), with the main difference being the use of stack LSTMs for the entire state. The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight how this differs from previous neural approaches to parsing. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

**2. Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art:** The paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match top systems using expert-crafted features or optimal decoding, but doesn't explore why. The authors claim these are "orthogonal" advances, but don't provide a clear path for how to integrate them. A more thorough analysis of the limitations compared to top systems would strengthen the paper.

**3. Lack of feature analysis:** The paper claims their model "discovers cheap alternatives available during a greedy parsing procedure" but provides no evidence or analysis of what features it actually learns. Without this, it's hard to evaluate whether the model is truly learning useful representations or just memorizing patterns.

**4. Limited comparison to recent neural joint parsing work:** The paper compares to previous joint parsing models but doesn't compare to other recent neural joint parsing approaches (e.g., graph-based neural models like Lluís et al., 2013). This omission makes it difficult to assess how much of the improvement is due to the specific representation learning approach versus other neural network advances.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the features learned by your model, and how they compare to the expensive features used in expert-crafted systems? What specific linguistic phenomena does your model capture that were previously only accessible through expensive features?

2. You mention that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model." Which specific advances (e.g., specific feature types, optimal decoding techniques) would you expect to most significantly improve your model, and what would be the technical challenges in integrating them?

3. How does your approach compare to the recent neural joint parsing work of Zhou and Xu (2015), which also uses neural networks for joint parsing without expert features? What are the key differences in performance and architecture?

4. The paper states that your Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for superior performance. Why does the model depend so heavily on these embeddings for Chinese, and how would performance change if character-based embeddings were used instead?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic parsing with clear technical innovation in representing the entire parser state. The empirical results are strong and the runtime advantage is significant for practical applications. However, the contribution is somewhat incremental given the reliance on previous work, and the paper would be strengthened by more thorough comparisons to state-of-the-art neural approaches and a deeper analysis of the features learned by the model.

The paper meets the requirements of the conference with a clear technical contribution, strong empirical results, and practical relevance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above. The work is a valuable addition to the literature on joint parsing, particularly for its efficiency and demonstration that greedy parsing can be effective for joint tasks.
