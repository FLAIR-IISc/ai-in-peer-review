

# Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire parser state (not just top stack items) and (2) using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The system is evaluated on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, claiming to outperform all previous joint parsing models while being significantly faster than pipeline approaches.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. The application of stack LSTMs to represent the *entire* parser state for joint syntactic-semantic parsing, bypassing expensive features previously required for semantic role labeling
2. Demonstrating competitive performance with greedy inference (vs. beam search) for joint parsing
3. Providing a fast, single-threaded implementation (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data) that is nearly 2.5x faster than state-of-the-art pipeline systems

## Strengths

1. **Practical efficiency with strong speed advantage**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their greedy approach achieves significantly faster parsing (177.6±18 seconds vs. 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al.'s pipeline system) while maintaining competitive performance. This is a major practical contribution for real-world applications where parsing speed is critical. The runtime is particularly impressive for a single-threaded implementation.

2. **Clear methodology and implementation**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the transition-based parsing algorithm, including the syntactic and semantic transitions, their constraints, and the stack LSTM architecture. The description of the four stack LSTMs (for S, M, B, and A) and their interactions is thorough and well-structured, making the approach highly reproducible.

3. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The authors provide thorough results across CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, as well as multilingual results for German, Spanish, Chinese, and Japanese. The comparison with multiple variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides valuable insights into the contribution of joint modeling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty justification**: The paper claims novelty in applying stack LSTMs to joint parsing, but doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from Dyer et al. (2015), which already applied stack LSTMs to syntactic parsing. The paper doesn't clearly articulate what specific challenges or differences exist in the joint parsing setting that weren't present in syntactic parsing. The novelty claim is underdeveloped.

2. **Misleading performance claims**: The paper states "it outperforms all previous joint parsing models," but this is misleading. While it outperforms previous *joint* models (like Henderson et al., 2008), it falls behind the top *pipeline* systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015) that use expert-crafted features. The authors acknowledge this gap but don't provide a nuanced analysis of the trade-offs between their approach and these superior-performing pipeline systems.

3. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper notes that Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings (without which it's "on par with the others"), yet fails to discuss this as a significant limitation for low-resource languages. The authors don't explore how this dependency on pretrained embeddings might be addressed or how the model would perform in truly low-resource settings.

4. **Insufficient hyperparameter details**: While the paper mentions tuning dropout rates on dev data, it doesn't provide details on how other key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensions, LSTM hidden states) were selected. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess reproducibility and whether the results are sensitive to parameter choices.

5. **Lack of analysis on why joint modeling works**: The paper demonstrates that joint modeling improves performance in the training domain (WSJ), but doesn't provide a clear explanation for *why* joint modeling helps. A deeper analysis of how the shared representations between syntax and semantics improve performance would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your stack LSTM approach for joint parsing differ from Dyer et al.'s (2015) syntactic parsing work? What specific challenges did you face in applying it to joint parsing that weren't present in the syntactic case?

2. You claim your model "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" but fall behind state-of-the-art pipeline systems. Can you provide a more nuanced analysis of the trade-offs between your approach and these pipeline systems, particularly regarding performance vs. speed?

3. The Chinese results depend heavily on pretrained embeddings. How would you expect the model to perform on languages without pretrained embeddings, and what strategies might address this issue for low-resource languages?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical and efficient approach to joint syntactic-semantic parsing with clear speed advantages. The methodology is well-described and the empirical results are comprehensive. However, the novelty claim is underdeveloped, the performance claims are somewhat misleading, and the limitations of the approach (particularly regarding pretrained embeddings) are not sufficiently discussed. The paper makes a valuable contribution to efficient parsing but doesn't fully establish its novelty or address the limitations of its approach.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about novelty, performance claims, and limitations. The paper has strong practical value, but needs to more clearly articulate its contribution relative to prior work and provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between performance and efficiency.
