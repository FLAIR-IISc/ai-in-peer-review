

# Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using a stack LSTM to represent the entire algorithmic state, rather than just top items on the stack. The authors claim two key innovations: (1) learning representations for the entire parser state, and (2) using entirely greedy inference rather than beam search. The primary contribution is the demonstration that this approach outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks while being significantly faster.

## Strengths

1. The paper clearly demonstrates the benefits of learning representations for the entire parser state rather than just top items on the stack, which is a well-justified contribution to the field.

2. The runtime efficiency (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data) is impressive compared to state-of-the-art pipeline systems (439.9±42 seconds), making the approach practical for real-world applications.

3. The multilingual results are strong, showing the model adapts well across languages with minimal modifications, particularly when pretrained embeddings are used.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and confusing description of the parsing algorithm**: The paper states "B0 contains the input sentence with the first word at the front of B and a special root symbol at the end" (2.1), but then in 2.2 notes "this works better for the arc-eager algorithm (Ballesteros and Nivre, 2013), in contrast to Henderson et al. (2013), who initialized with root at the buffer front." This creates confusion about the exact initialization procedure. Furthermore, the explanation of how the buffer is implemented as a stack (2.1) conflicts with standard parsing terminology, where buffers are typically queues (FIFO), not stacks (LIFO).

2. **Critical technical ambiguities**: The paper claims "M-LEFT and M-RIGHT do not remove the dependent from the semantic stack and buffer respectively, unlike their syntactic equivalents" (2.2), but without Figure 1 (which is referenced but not included in the provided text), it's impossible to verify this claim. The phrase "like all in the example" is particularly confusing without the figure.

3. **Inconsistent citation formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous typographical errors (e.g., "correspodning" in equations 3-5, "Lluı́s" with inconsistent diacritics) and inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "CoNLL 2008" vs. "conll 2008" in references). These are minor but indicate a lack of careful proofreading.

4. **Unclear experimental comparisons**: The paper states it "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" but fails to provide a clear, quantitative comparison to the top systems on CoNLL 2009 (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009). The reference to "Table 3" is made without including the table, making it impossible to assess the claimed performance gains.

5. **Misrepresentation of technical contributions**: The paper claims to "use no expert-crafted features at all," but in Section 3.3, it describes using "pretrained embeddings" and "learned embeddings for syntactic and semantic arc labels." These are not "expert-crafted features" in the traditional sense, but the claim of "no expert-crafted features" is misleading since the pretrained embeddings represent significant prior knowledge.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to use "entirely greedy inference rather than beam search," but the algorithm description in Section 2.2 doesn't explain how the greedy approach avoids the error propagation problems associated with greedy parsing. Can you clarify how the greedy approach specifically avoids these issues?

2. The paper states that "the algorithm also places partial syntactic and semantic parse structures onto the front of the buffer, so it is also implemented as a stack." This is technically incorrect—buffers are typically implemented as queues, not stacks. Please clarify this implementation detail.

3. The paper claims to outperform "all previous joint parsing models," but Table 3 (referenced in Section 5) is not included in the provided text. Please provide the full table to verify the claimed performance gains.

## Final Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting approach to joint parsing with promising runtime improvements, the numerous technical ambiguities, inconsistent descriptions, and minor typographical errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The confusion around the buffer implementation, the unverified claim about semantic transitions, and the misleading "no expert-crafted features" claim are particularly problematic for a paper that purports to make methodological contributions. These issues, while seemingly minor, indicate a lack of attention to detail that would make the work difficult to reproduce or build upon. Given the stringent standards of the CoNLL conference, I recommend rejection.
