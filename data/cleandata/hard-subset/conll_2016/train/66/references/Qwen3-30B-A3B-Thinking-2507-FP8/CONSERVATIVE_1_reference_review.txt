

# Review of "A New Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a new joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, avoiding expert-crafted features and using greedy inference instead of beam search. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009, while being significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. An incremental parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state (not just top items)
2. A greedy inference approach for joint parsing
3. A demonstration of competitive performance without expert-crafted features

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a clear and well-structured description of the parsing algorithm and its transitions. The formalization of the joint parsing procedure and constraints on transitions is thorough and well-justified.

2. The experimental setup is detailed and follows standard CoNLL shared task protocols. The authors provide comprehensive results across multiple languages and tasks, which adds credibility to their claims.

3. The runtime efficiency claim is notable and well-documented, with the parser being significantly faster than several state-of-the-art pipeline approaches (nearly 2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015).

## Weaknesses

1. The paper's primary claim of outperforming previous joint parsing models is misleading. The authors acknowledge their system "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems" (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015). The paper's comparison is limited to previous joint models, not the current state of the art in joint parsing. The authors should either demonstrate competitive performance against the best systems or be more explicit about the limitations of their approach relative to established methods.

2. The paper lacks sufficient evidence for the key innovation claim that learning representations for the "entire algorithmic state" is superior to more localized representations. The authors cite Dyer et al. (2015) as using stack LSTMs for syntactic parsing, but provide no ablation studies demonstrating the specific benefit of their approach over using just the top items (as in Dyer et al.). Without these experiments, it's impossible to validate their claim that the entire state representation is the key to their performance.

3. The paper claims to avoid "expert-crafted features" but fails to demonstrate that this is a meaningful advantage. The authors acknowledge that their system doesn't match the performance of systems that use "arbitrarily-scoped syntactic features" (Gildea and Jurafsky, 2002), which are "a mainstay of high-performance semantic role labeling systems." The paper doesn't explain why these features are unnecessary or why their approach is better, especially when they admit to not using them. This is a significant weakness given the authors' claim that "recent advances in representation learning can bypass those expensive features."

4. The paper provides no evidence that greedy inference is sufficient for joint parsing. Previous work (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013; Täckström et al., 2015) has demonstrated that beam search or other search strategies can significantly improve parsing performance. The paper doesn't compare greedy inference to beam search in their model, nor does it provide justification for why greedy inference would be adequate for this complex task.

5. The paper's multilingual results are presented as "comparable to the top systems" but fail to provide any statistical significance testing for these claims. The results show the model performs well on most languages, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that this is statistically significant compared to the top systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform previous joint parsing models, but doesn't compare to the current state of the art in joint parsing (e.g., the top systems from CoNLL 2009). Can you clarify how your model compares to these systems, and why your approach is superior despite not using their features?

2. The paper states that the "entire algorithmic state" representation is key to their performance, but provides no ablation study demonstrating this. What would be the performance if you used only the top items on the stack (as in Dyer et al., 2015)?

3. The paper claims to avoid expensive features, but acknowledges that systems using these features outperform yours. How do you reconcile this with your claim that your approach "bypasses those expensive features"?

4. Why is greedy inference sufficient for this task, given that previous work has shown that beam search can significantly improve parsing performance? Have you tried comparing greedy inference to beam search in your model?

This paper presents an interesting approach to joint parsing, but falls short of the rigorous validation required to support its claims of outperforming previous work. The authors' assertion that they've "bypassed expensive features" is not substantiated by their results, as their system still lags behind systems that use those features. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to the current state of the art, clearer justification for their key innovations, and ablation studies demonstrating the specific contribution of each component.
