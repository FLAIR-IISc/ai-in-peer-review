

# Review of "A Neural Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state rather than just the top items on the stack. The authors claim three key contributions: (1) a joint parsing model that learns representations for the entire parser state, (2) an entirely greedy inference approach that avoids beam search, and (3) a fast system that outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical contribution**: The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over previous joint parsing models on standard benchmarks. The results show that their model outperforms Henderson et al. (2008) and variants on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets, which is a meaningful advancement in the joint parsing field. The authors provide thorough comparisons with multiple baselines and demonstrate consistent improvements across different languages.

2. **Practical efficiency**: The runtime claim of "177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core" is compelling, especially when compared to "almost 2.5 times faster" than Lei et al. (2015). This computational advantage is a significant practical contribution, as many state-of-the-art systems are computationally expensive, limiting their real-world applicability.

3. **Clear technical contribution**: The use of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state is a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The paper provides a clear explanation of how the stack LSTM architecture captures the full algorithmic state, which is a meaningful contribution to the understanding of how neural networks can be used to model parsing algorithms.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of the greedy inference approach**: The paper claims to use "entirely greedy inference rather than beam search" as a key difference, but provides insufficient analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and speed. The authors acknowledge their system doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems, which often use optimal decoding, but they don't compare their greedy approach to a beam search version of their own model. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the greedy approach is limiting performance or if the state representation is the real key to their success.

2. **Inadequate comparison to prior neural approaches**: The paper mentions Dyer et al. (2015) as a syntactic parser but doesn't clearly explain how their joint model differs from a neural joint model built on Dyer's approach. The authors claim their state representation is the key innovation, but they don't provide sufficient analysis of why their representation works better than previous neural approaches to joint parsing. This weakens the novelty claim.

3. **Lack of analysis on feature replacement**: The paper states it "uses no expert-crafted features at all" and claims to "bypass those expensive features," but doesn't provide a clear analysis of which specific features are being replaced or how the neural model achieves similar performance without them. This is a significant gap, as the paper's main claim is about replacing expert-crafted features with learned representations, yet it doesn't show how the learned representations capture the same information.

4. **Insufficient multilingual analysis**: The paper claims multilingual results are "comparable to the top systems" but provides minimal analysis of why performance varies across languages. The Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance, but the paper doesn't explain why this is necessary or how it would generalize to other languages without similar resources. This weakens the claim of broad applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform Henderson et al. (2008) and variants, but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why the entire-state representation approach works better than the more limited state representations used in previous models. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the specific differences in state representation that led to the performance gains?

2. The paper mentions that their system doesn't match the performance of top expert-crafted feature-based systems, but doesn't analyze which specific features are missing. Could you elaborate on which specific features (e.g., "path" between predicate and argument) are being replaced by the neural model, and how the model compensates for their absence?

3. The paper claims to use "entirely greedy inference" as a key difference, but doesn't compare the performance of a beam search version of their model. How does the greedy approach affect performance, and would a beam search version improve results without significantly increasing runtime?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the joint parsing field with a well-executed empirical study that demonstrates clear improvements over previous joint models. The computational efficiency claim is particularly strong and valuable for practical applications. However, the paper's claims about replacing expert-crafted features are not sufficiently supported by analysis, and the lack of comparison between greedy and beam search approaches weakens the argument for the greedy inference approach.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a clear description of the algorithm and implementation. While the novelty claim could be strengthened with more analysis of the representation learning aspect, the empirical results are compelling. The authors' claim that "recent advances in representation learning can bypass those expensive features" is supported by the results, but the paper would benefit from more detailed explanation of how exactly the neural model achieves this.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to strengthen the analysis of the representation learning aspect and to provide a clearer explanation of the trade-offs between greedy and beam search approaches. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, and the empirical results are convincing, but the theoretical justification for the approach could be more robust.
