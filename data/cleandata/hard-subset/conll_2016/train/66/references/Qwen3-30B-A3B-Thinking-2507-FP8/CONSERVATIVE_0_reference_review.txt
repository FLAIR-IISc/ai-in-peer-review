

# Review of "A New Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser"

## Summary
This paper presents a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state (not just top stack items) and uses entirely greedy inference. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while being significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core). The approach builds on stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) and uses a transition-based parsing algorithm with syntactic and semantic transitions.

## Main Contributions
1. A parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state rather than just top items on stacks
2. A greedy inference approach for joint parsing (contrasting with beam search)
3. A claim of superior performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 compared to previous joint models

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a thorough description of the parsing algorithm, transition system, and stack LSTM architecture, making the method clear and reproducible.
2. The experimental evaluation covers multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008, 2009 English, and multilingual), providing comprehensive results.
3. The runtime speed claim is empirically supported and the speed advantage (2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015) is a valid contribution for practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art systems**: The paper claims to "outperform all previous joint parsing models" but fails to properly compare against the top systems at the time of the CoNLL 2009 shared task (Zhao et al., 2009; Che et al., 2009), which they explicitly admit they fall behind. The paper acknowledges that their performance doesn't match "top expert-crafted feature-based systems" but doesn't adequately explain why their approach is valuable when it falls behind these systems. This weakens their main claim of superiority.

2. **Misleading claim of "no expert-crafted features"**: The paper states it "uses no expert-crafted features at all," but this is inaccurate. The model relies on part-of-speech tags, pretrained embeddings, and a carefully designed transition system that embeds linguistic knowledge. The authors use the stack LSTM framework (Dyer et al., 2015), which itself builds on prior work that did use features. This misrepresentation undermines the novelty claim.

3. **Unjustified greedy inference**: The paper claims their greedy approach outperforms beam search methods, which contradicts the parsing literature where beam search generally provides better accuracy. The authors don't provide sufficient evidence or explanation for why greedy inference works better here, making this claim suspicious without rigorous comparison against beam search versions of their model.

4. **Lack of ablation studies on key components**: The paper presents variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) but doesn't sufficiently isolate the contribution of the "entire algorithmic state" representation versus other components. It's unclear whether the key innovation is the representation learning or the greedy inference.

5. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The authors acknowledge their system doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems but don't adequately discuss why this matters or how their approach might complement rather than replace those systems. The paper's focus on outperforming previous joint models rather than addressing the performance gap with top systems is a significant weakness.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to outperform "all previous joint parsing models" but falls behind Zhao et al. (2009) and other pipeline systems. Can you provide a more detailed comparison showing why your approach is valuable despite not matching the performance of these top systems?

2. You claim to use "no expert-crafted features at all," but your model depends on part-of-speech tags and a carefully designed transition system. How do you distinguish your approach from the use of expert-crafted features in prior work?

3. Your greedy inference approach contradicts the standard parsing literature. Could you provide an ablation study comparing your greedy model to a beam-search version of the same architecture?

4. The paper states "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model," but doesn't elaborate. Can you specify which orthogonal advances could be integrated into your framework to improve performance?

This paper presents an interesting technical contribution to parsing, but its claims are overstated and lack sufficient evidence to support the main claims. The performance claims are not as strong as presented, and the novelty is overstated relative to existing work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by more rigorous comparison with the top systems of the time and a more nuanced discussion of how this approach fits into the broader landscape of joint parsing methods.
