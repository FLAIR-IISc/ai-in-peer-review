- Summary: Joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state with greedy inference; claims stronger accuracy than prior joint models and much faster runtime.
- Contribution 1: Novel approach learning representations for the entire parser state rather than only top stack items.
- Contribution 2: Greedy parsing strategy that avoids beam search yet outperforms previous joint models.
- Contribution 3: Practical, high-speed parser (e.g., 177.6±18 s on CoNLL 2009 English single core).
- Strength 1: Demonstrates neural networks can replace hand-crafted features by modeling full parser state via stack LSTMs.
- Strength 2: Achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing approaches on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.
- Strength 3: Substantial speed advantage over competing systems, beneficial for real-world use.
- Weakness 1: Incomplete comparison to top expert feature–based systems and no clear explanation or integration strategy for those features.
- Weakness 2: Limited analysis of generalization — joint model overfits WSJ in out-of-domain (Brown) tests with no diagnosis or remedies.
- Weakness 3: Insufficient explanation of why structured skip-gram embeddings are superior to standard embeddings for this task.
- Question 1: Provide qualitative analysis showing learned representations capture the same information as expert-crafted syntactic features.
- Question 2: How does the model perform for low-resource languages or without pretrained embeddings, and what is the impact of removing them?
- Question 3: Why does the joint model overfit WSJ in out-of-domain settings and what architectural changes could mitigate this?
- Overall recommendation: Positive — novel and well-executed with strong empirical and speed results, but needs minor revisions to strengthen SOTA comparisons and generalization/embedding analyses.
