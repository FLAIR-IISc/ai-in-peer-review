

# Peer Review: Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing

## Summary
This paper presents a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state (rather than just top stack items) using stack LSTMs, and employs entirely greedy inference. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing approaches on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while being significantly faster than competing systems.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel joint parsing approach that learns representations for the entire parser state (not just top stack items)
2. A greedy parsing strategy that avoids beam search while outperforming previous joint models
3. A practical, high-speed parsing system (177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data on single core)

## Strengths

1. **Novel Representation Learning Approach**: The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively learn representations for the *entire* parser state rather than relying on hand-crafted features. This aligns with the broader trend toward end-to-end learning in NLP and avoids the need for expensive feature engineering that has been a hallmark of previous successful systems. The stack LSTM implementation (Dyer et al., 2015) is particularly well-suited for this task.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing approaches on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. The fact that it outperforms all previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009; Henderson et al., 2013) is a significant achievement, particularly considering it doesn't require full syntactic parse features.

3. **Practical Performance**: The speed advantage (177.6±18 seconds vs. Lei et al.'s 439.9±42 seconds) is substantial and well-documented. For real-world applications where speed is critical, this could be a major advantage over more complex pipeline approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper acknowledges that their system doesn't match top expert-crafted feature-based systems (Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), but fails to provide a clear explanation of *why* their approach works as well as it does without those features. The authors state these advances are "orthogonal" but don't demonstrate how their model could be integrated with these features to achieve even better results. This limits the practical significance of their contribution.

2. **Limited Analysis of Generalization**: The paper notes that the joint model overfits to WSJ in out-of-domain settings (Brown test set), but provides no analysis of *why* this happens or how to address it. Understanding the generalization limitations would be valuable for future work, especially for practical applications where domain shifts are common.

3. **Insufficient Detail on Embedding Approach**: While the paper uses "structured skipgram" embeddings (Ling et al., 2015), it doesn't clearly explain why these are superior to standard word embeddings for their task. A more detailed analysis of how these embeddings contribute to the model's performance would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model learns "cheap alternatives" to expensive syntactic features, but doesn't provide evidence that these learned representations are actually capturing the same information as the expert-crafted features. Could you provide a qualitative analysis showing how the model's internal representations correspond to the linguistic features used by previous systems?

2. The multilingual results section notes that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance. How would the model perform on low-resource languages without such embeddings? What would be the impact on performance if we removed the pretrained embeddings for other languages?

3. The authors state that their joint model overfits to WSJ in out-of-domain settings. Could you provide more insight into why this happens, and what architectural changes might mitigate this issue?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively represent the entire parser state for joint syntactic-semantic parsing. The performance gains over previous joint models are significant, and the speed advantage is practically important. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations in the comparison to state-of-the-art systems and providing deeper analysis of the model's generalization behavior. The authors should clarify how their approach could be integrated with the most successful systems (which use expert-crafted features) rather than framing them as orthogonal.

With minor revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field. The core technical contribution (learning the full parser state) is novel and well-executed, and the empirical results are compelling.
