

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a new joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state rather than just top items on the stack, and uses entirely greedy inference. The authors claim their model outperforms previous joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks while being faster than competing systems.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel joint parsing approach that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state (not just top items on stacks)
2. A greedy parsing strategy that avoids beam search
3. A stack LSTM-based architecture that eliminates the need for expert-crafted features

## Strengths
1. The paper demonstrates a clear performance advantage over previous joint parsing models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008) on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.
2. The runtime efficiency claim (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) is well-supported with concrete numbers.
3. The paper provides a clear description of the novel transition system and its relationship to prior work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical inconsistencies in transition definitions**: The paper claims "M-LEFT and M-RIGHT do not remove the dependent from the semantic stack and buffer respectively, unlike their syntactic equivalents," but Table 1 shows M-LEFT modifying the buffer (Bt+1 = M (gm(v,u, r), v), B) which contradicts this claim. This fundamental inconsistency undermines the paper's core technical contribution.

2. **Inconsistent notation throughout**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for transitions (e.g., M-RIGHT vs. M-RIGHT, M-LEFT vs. M-LEFT, M-SELF vs. M-SELF) and for arrows (e.g., "u `! v" vs. "u r! v" vs. "u r v"). This makes the technical content difficult to follow and appears unprofessional.

3. **Lack of critical implementation details**: The paper claims "a runtime of 177.6±18 seconds" but fails to specify the hardware used for this timing measurement, which is essential for reproducibility. Similarly, the paper states "91.22% of predicates by type" but doesn't explain how this percentage was calculated or how the "01" sense was selected for unseen predicates.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation claims**: The paper states "we fall behind only Johansson and Nugues (2008)" but doesn't provide specific performance metrics (e.g., LAS, F1 scores) to support this claim. The paper also claims "Joint overfits to WSJ" based on out-of-domain performance, but provides no evidence (e.g., training vs. validation curves) to back up this assertion.

5. **Formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains multiple typographical errors including "Lluı́s" (should be "Lluis"), inconsistent Greek symbols in formulas (e.g., "⌧" instead of "τ"), and incorrect accent marks in "disambiguated." These errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the inconsistency between the claim that "M-LEFT and M-RIGHT do not remove the dependent" and the buffer modifications shown in Table 1 for these transitions.
2. What specific hardware was used to measure the 177.6±18 second runtime? Please provide these details for reproducibility.
3. How was the "91.22% correct" statistic for the "01" sense calculated? What was the specific metric used (e.g., precision, recall, F1)?
4. Please provide specific performance metrics (LAS, F1 scores) to support the claim that your model "falls behind only Johansson and Nugues (2008)".

## Additional Comments
The paper's core technical contribution is promising, but the numerous inconsistencies, formatting errors, and missing implementation details significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The authors' claims about performance and advantages are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The technical inconsistencies in the transition definitions are particularly problematic as they directly challenge the validity of the proposed approach. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to address these issues before publication.
