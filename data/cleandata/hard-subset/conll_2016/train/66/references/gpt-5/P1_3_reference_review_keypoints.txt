- Summary: Greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic parser using stack LSTMs to encode full parser state, with a new M-SELF transition and copy-based operations; shows accuracy gains and large speedups on CoNLL 2008/2009.

- Contribution 1: Practical joint syntax–semantics parser that learns full-state representations via stack LSTMs and uses fully greedy inference for strong accuracy and speed.

- Contribution 2: Algorithmic adaptations for joint parsing: interleaved syntactic/semantic transitions, copy-based arc-eager operations, and a new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments.

- Contribution 3: Empirical evidence that learned representations reduce need for hand-crafted joint features, outperforming prior joint models and yielding competitive multilingual results.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical gains and clear ablations; outperforms several prior joint systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 English with well-chosen baselines (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint).

- Strength 2: Clear, efficient engineering and attractive speed–accuracy trade-off; greedy inference yields substantially faster end-to-end runtime with linear-time design and small memory footprint.

- Strength 3: Thoughtful adaptation of stack LSTMs to a complex joint transition system, using separate stacks and recursive composition for mixed fragments.

- Strength 4: Sensible modeling of task idiosyncrasies (M-SELF, copy-based buffer, interleaving constraints) showing careful attention to joint parsing specifics.

- Strength 5: Broad evaluation including multilingual experiments and results with/without pretrained embeddings.

- Weakness 1: Limited methodological novelty—mainly adapts Dyer et al.’s stack LSTMs to an existing transition system with engineering tweaks.

- Weakness 2: Oracle and annotation conversion issues are under-explored; semantic oracle cannot perfectly encode gold graphs and pseudo-projectivization is used for syntax without quantifying impact.

- Weakness 3: Limited ablations and error analysis; unclear contributions of pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, composition functions, M-SELF, interleaving constraint, and copy vs move.

- Weakness 4: Joint model may overfit in-domain and degrade out-of-domain (Brown); robustness, regularization, and variance across runs are not explored.

- Weakness 5: Comparisons to SOTA are not fully controlled; need experiments isolating search vs feature differences (e.g., beam vs greedy with same features).

- Question 1: How are training targets handled for sentences where the semantic oracle cannot encode the gold graph, and what fraction of gold arcs/sentences are affected?

- Question 2: What are ablation results (Macro F1) for removing pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, M-SELF, using move vs copy, and relaxing the interleaving constraint?

- Question 3: Did you try dynamic oracle training or exploration methods (e.g., scheduled sampling) to mitigate exposure bias in greedy decoding, and with what results?

- Question 4: Are reported scores averaged over multiple seeds, what is the variance across runs, and are improvements statistically significant?

- Question 5: Are runtime comparisons (e.g., Lei et al. 2015) measured on identical hardware and settings, and what are tokens/sec for each model?

- Question 6: What is the error profile: which relations/distances benefit most from joint modeling, how often is M-SWAP used, and does it help non-projective languages?
