Summary and main contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a continuous representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). It eschews hand-crafted features and beam search.
- It introduces a small extension to the transition system (M-SELF) to handle self-arguments in CoNLL-2009 and uses M-SWAP to allow crossing semantic arcs. It reports improved results over prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual performance, with strong runtime efficiency.
- It provides ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) to study the impact of joint modeling and syntax availability for SRL.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance over prior joint transition systems: learning a representation of the full algorithmic state with stack LSTMs rather than relying on local, hand-engineered features. This is a sensible and timely application of representation learning to joint parsing and addresses longstanding feature-engineering costs.
- Strong empirical showing against prior joint models (Henderson et al. and successors) and competitive performance with shared-task leaders, while being significantly faster. The reported single-core end-to-end runtime is compelling for practical use.
- Well-motivated transition system design: unified buffer with interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions, inclusion of M-SWAP for non-projective semantics, and the novel M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments are thoughtful choices that align with the data’s structural properties.
- Useful ablations: providing semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid variants helps isolate the benefit of joint modeling and syntax for SRL, and shows that joint training can help in-domain performance.
- Multilingual evaluation: although not state of the art across the board, the parser adapts to several languages without language-specific features, reinforcing claims about reduced feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to Dyer et al. (2015): the main difference is applying stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system with greedy inference. The contribution is primarily an engineering integration rather than a fundamentally new modeling or inference principle. A beam or global training variant, or an analysis of representation learned across S/M/B/A, would strengthen novelty.
- Insufficient clarity and validation of the interleaving policy: the hard constraint to switch to semantic transitions after S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT (until M-SHIFT) is a strong procedural bias (e.g., resolving left-side syntax before left-side semantics). There is no ablation comparing alternative interleavings, nor analysis of how this constraint affects recall of long-distance or right-side arguments. This may also limit theoretical guarantees about linear-time behavior when combined with M-SWAP.
- Oracle and training data issues not addressed experimentally: the oracle mapping is imperfect (1.5% of EN sentences not exactly encodable). The paper does not quantify how those gold arcs are approximated or dropped during training, nor the resulting upper bound on attainable F1. This is crucial for interpreting the margin to pipeline systems and for reproducibility.
- Reproducibility gaps: code is promised “on publication,” but no anonymized artifact is provided. Several training details are underspecified or unusual: extremely aggressive learning rate decay (0.1 per epoch), no mention of batch size, gradient clipping, or early-stopping criteria beyond vague wall-clock time; dropout search includes 1.0 (nonsensical for keep prob); hardware details for speed are incomplete; no random seed control or variance reporting across runs for accuracy (only for runtime). Significance testing is absent.
- Evaluation incompleteness and possible confounds: claims about outperforming prior joint work and being “comparable to top systems” are not backed by statistical tests and rely on pretrained embeddings (e.g., AFP-only for English) that may differ from others’ settings. The handling of unseen predicates by defaulting to “01” is justified by type-level stats; token-level impact, and robustness to lemma errors, are not analyzed. The hybrid setup is under-specified: at test time, does the semantic model consume predicted syntactic action histories/structures, or is it truly syntax-free? The description suggests inconsistency.

Minor issues and presentation
- Typos and notation: “correspodning,” “dismabiguated,” “zeroMarkov order,” mixed delimiters in [st;mt;bt,at], inconsistent hyphenation, stray spaces around dashes, and several places where commas/semicolons are inconsistent.
- Several references to figures/tables/supplementary material without sufficient detail in-text (e.g., exact numbers, example sequences).
- Asymptotic analysis cites linearity via Nivre (2009), but M-SWAP can induce superlinear behavior without a bound on swaps; the paper does not prove a bound nor provide empirical swap counts.

Questions to authors
- Interleaving policy: Why this exact syntax→semantics interleaving? Did you try allowing free interleaving or other schedules? What is the impact on performance and complexity? Provide an ablation.
- Oracle imperfection: How are non-encodable gold graphs handled during training (arc deletion, approximation, relaxed loss)? Quantify the fraction of lost arcs and the resulting ceiling on F1.
- Complexity with M-SWAP: Do you enforce a bound on swaps per token? What is the empirical distribution of M-SWAP counts per sentence, and worst-case behavior?
- Hybrid setup: At test time, does the semantic model rely on any predicted syntactic actions/states? Please clarify the exact inputs available to the semantic model during both training and testing for the hybrid variant.
- Reproducibility: Please provide precise hardware (CPU model, clock), implementation details (optimizer choice, batch size, gradient clipping, early-stopping criterion), random seeds, and report mean±std over multiple runs for accuracy. Also clarify whether 0.1 decay is multiplicative per epoch and justify the dropout search including 1.0.
- Predicate sense defaulting: What is the token-level coverage, and how sensitive is performance to lemma errors in CoNLL predictions?
- Ablations: Please report the effect of removing pretrained embeddings, the action-history LSTM, and the M-SELF transition. Provide significance tests for main comparisons.

Overall, the paper is promising and practically relevant, but I am concerned about limited methodological novelty, unvalidated procedural constraints, and reproducibility/evaluation gaps. Substantial clarifications and additional analyses would be needed for a strong acceptance.
