Summary and main contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn dense representations of the full parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history), replacing hand-crafted feature engineering.
- It introduces a transition system that interleaves syntax and semantics over a shared buffer, adds a self-arc transition (M-SELF) for NomBank-style self-dependencies, and supports non-projective semantic graphs via M-SWAP. It claims superior performance to prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results, with strong speed.
- It provides ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) to analyze the impact of joint learning and shows the approach scales to multiple languages using pretrained embeddings.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and technical novelty in representation: Using four stack LSTMs to summarize the entire algorithmic state (including action history) is a clean and effective way to replace sparse, manually engineered, long-range features that are otherwise needed for SRL. The composition functions for recursive syntactic and semantic fragments are well-integrated with the transition system.
- Practical, fast joint parsing: Fully greedy inference with a compact model yields linear-time decoding and strong wall-clock performance (∼178s on CoNLL 2009 En), substantially faster than competitive pipelines. This is valuable for real-world deployment.
- Careful transition design for SRL idiosyncrasies: The addition of M-SELF for self-arguments and allowing multi-headed dependents (no popping in M-LEFT/RIGHT) match the target SRL graph properties; M-SWAP addresses crossing semantic arcs. The interleaving design (copy on S-SHIFT/RIGHT) preserves buffer visibility needed for semantics.
- Solid empirical coverage and ablations: Results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (in/out of domain) and multilingual data are broad. The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint comparisons support the claim that joint learning helps in-domain semantics without hurting syntax.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters, training details (dropout, SGD schedules), and embeddings are specified; code availability on publication further strengthens impact.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete oracle/transition coverage and its consequences: The authors note that for 1.5% of English CoNLL 2009 sentences the gold joint parse cannot be encoded with the transition system (despite M-SWAP). This caps the upper bound for both training (oracle supervision) and evaluation of joint parsing, but the effect size is not quantified. How often are gold arcs unreachable? What fraction of errors are due to transition limitations vs modeling? Without this analysis, it’s hard to attribute performance gaps.
- Greedy decoding and exposure bias: The fully greedy training and inference likely induce exposure bias and compounding errors, and may explain overfitting to WSJ and the weaker OOD performance (Hybrid > Joint on Brown). No dynamic oracle, exploration-based training, scheduled sampling, or globally normalized (CRF/structured) objective is attempted; nor is beam search compared. Prior work shows these can substantially help transition-based parsing and SRL.
- Limited linguistic features for multilingual: The model ignores morphology/character information, which is known to be critical for inflected languages (e.g., Czech). While the authors acknowledge this, comparisons with systems that do use morphology are thus not strictly apples-to-apples; it would be useful to either add char/morph features or restrict/flag comparisons accordingly. The Japanese performance suggests data scarcity sensitivity; no data augmentation or multilingual transfer is tried.
- Transition scheduling design choices are under-justified: Forcing semantics to trigger only after S-SHIFT/RIGHT, and resolving left-side syntax before left-side semantics, imposes a strong procedural bias. There’s no ablation comparing alternative interleaving schedules, nor analysis of how often this ordering blocks beneficial semantic attachments. Additionally, cycle prevention and termination guarantees for semantic graphs (with M-SWAP and not popping dependents) are not fully formalized beyond disallowing repetitive swaps; are semantic cycles possible?
- Evaluation and analysis gaps: Statistical significance testing across systems is not reported. Error analyses are limited; we lack breakdowns by role type, predicate type (verbal vs nominal), long-distance vs local arguments, or crossing vs non-crossing arcs. Ablations on key modeling choices (pretrained vs learned embeddings, action history LSTM, composition functions, label embeddings) are missing. The default “01” sense for unseen predicates may inflate sense accuracy in-domain; a targeted analysis would clarify its real impact.

Questions to authors
- Oracle limitations: What proportion of gold semantic arcs (and sentences) are unreachable with your transition system? How does training with imperfect supervision affect learning (e.g., measured upper bound vs achieved)? Can you characterize the unreachable patterns (e.g., multiple crossings, specific constructions)?
- Decoding/training variants: Did you try a small beam, dynamic oracle training, scheduled sampling, or globally normalized training (e.g., locally factored CRF objective)? If so, what was the trade-off in speed vs accuracy and OOD robustness?
- Transition scheduling: Have you experimented with alternative interleaving policies (e.g., allowing semantic actions earlier, or revisiting semantic decisions after more syntax is built)? Any impact on WSJ vs Brown?
- Graph well-formedness: Do you enforce acyclicity or other global constraints in the semantic graph? If not, how often do cycles occur in predictions?
- Ablations: What is the contribution of pretrained embeddings vs learned-only? Of action-history LSTM A? Of label embeddings in composition? How much does M-SELF contribute on nominal predicates?
- Resources/comparability: Are your Gigaword embeddings the only external resource? For multilingual, did you restrict to shared-task constraints (e.g., no extra morphology analyzers)? Can you report results without pretrained embeddings for all languages (not only Chinese) to gauge dependence?

Additional comments
- The approach is timely and elegant: it shows that learned state representations can replace expensive syntax-derived features for SRL within a fast joint parser. Addressing the oracle gap, adding a modest global training signal or small beam, and injecting char/morph features would likely close much of the remaining gap to expert-feature pipelines while retaining speed.
- Minor: Please include significance tests, a brief qualitative error analysis, and clarify whether M-SELF edges are handled consistently with the official evaluation.
