Summary
The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire parser state and performs fully greedy inference without hand-crafted features. It adapts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2013), adds a new transition (M-SELF) for nominal self-dependencies, and interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer. The model achieves better results than prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and substantial speed gains, though it trails the very best pipeline systems that use heavy feature engineering and global inference.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, bold joint parsing architecture that learns continuous representations of the full joint state (stacks and buffer) via stack LSTMs, enabling a fully greedy, feature-free parser that is competitive with more complex systems.
- An operational joint transition system and training recipe for dependency SRL and syntax, including a new M-SELF transition for NomBank-style self-arguments and a practical interleaving protocol that keeps semantics effective without full trees.
- A thorough empirical study (English and multilingual) with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarifies the value of jointly modeling syntax and semantics under a greedy, learned-state paradigm, and demonstrates strong speed/accuracy trade-offs.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and novelty: Learning a compact representation of the entire joint parsing state and committing to greedy inference—eschewing expert features and beams—constitutes a clear, bold direction. The result that such a model can match/exceed prior joint parsers is impactful and practically relevant.
- Empirical gains over prior joint models: On both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, the joint model surpasses Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and other joint baselines, suggesting the learned-state approach is superior to locally-scoped, feature-based joint models.
- Speed and practicality: Linear-time greedy decoding with a small memory footprint, and a reported 2.5× speedup over a strong SRL pipeline under identical conditions, make this a compelling option for real-world usage. Open-source release further increases impact.
- Informative ablations: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint comparisons are well-chosen and illuminate how much semantics benefits from syntax, how much syntax suffers (slightly) or not from being joint, and how joint can overfit in-domain vs. hybrid OOD—useful insights for the community.
- Multilingual validation: Results across several CoNLL 2009 languages show the approach is broadly applicable and competitive with top systems, particularly when pretrained embeddings are available.

Weaknesses
- Limited originality beyond integration: The transition inventory is largely inherited from Henderson et al. (2013), and the learning machinery from Dyer et al. (2015). The main novelty is applying stack LSTMs to the joint setting, plus a minor M-SELF action. While valuable, this is more an effective synthesis than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
- Greedy decoding without global training/decoding: The paper establishes that greedy works well, but does not explore whether modestly more powerful search (small beams) or global training (e.g., dynamic oracles, exploration) would close the gap to the best pipelines and mitigate OOD overfitting. Given the simplicity, this is a natural and missing analysis.
- Imperfect oracle/transition mapping for semantics: The conversion from gold semantic graphs to action sequences is not perfect (due to crossing arcs), which may cap upper-bound performance and blur interpretation. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify its impact on training/evaluation or propose mitigation.
- Comparison scope and analysis: While state-of-the-art pipelines are discussed, the paper mostly positions itself as complementary. A stronger head-to-head controlled comparison (e.g., a pipeline that uses the same embeddings and training data) would sharpen conclusions. Error analysis is light; we learn little about where the joint greedy model helps/hurts (by role type, predicate class, long-distance edges).
- Reliance on external annotations and design choices: The model assumes predicted POS and lemmas; CoNLL 2009 predicates are pre-identified; CoNLL 2008 uses a separate BiLSTM classifier for predicate identification. The interleaving policy and the buffer-copy tweak are chosen heuristically; ablations on these design choices would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
- Oracle conversion: What fraction of training/development sentences yield imperfect semantic action sequences per language? Can you quantify the upper bound this imposes and its empirical impact on final F1?
- Search/training: Did you try small beams or dynamic-oracle training? If so, what is the accuracy/runtime trade-off? If not, do you expect beam-2/4 to help OOD SRL without losing the speed advantages?
- Interleaving and buffer-copy design: How critical is the policy “switch to semantics after S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT until M-SHIFT”? Have you tried alternatives (e.g., always alternate, or agenda-based mixing)? Similarly, what is the exact impact of copying vs. moving buffer items on performance?
- Representation ablations: How sensitive are results to the action-history stack, pretrained embeddings, and composition functions (e.g., label embeddings, separate Zs/Zm)? Please provide ablations to isolate which components are essential.
- Predicate sense generalization: For unseen predicates defaulting to “01,” what is the error profile? Would a character/subword encoder mitigate this, especially in morphologically rich languages?

Overall, while the work builds on known components, the choice to go all-in on learned state representations and greedy joint inference—and to show they can supplant expensive, feature-heavy pipelines with competitive accuracy and much better speed—is a meaningful and timely contribution. I lean positive given the conceptual clarity, practical impact, and solid empirical evidence, and I would welcome this paper at CoNLL.
