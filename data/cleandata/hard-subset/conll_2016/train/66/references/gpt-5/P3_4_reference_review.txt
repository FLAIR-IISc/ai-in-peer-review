Summary and main contributions
- A fast, fully greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that represents the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs, rather than hand-crafted features or limited local context. Decisions are made by a simple linear scoring layer over these learned state summaries.
- A concrete transition system and scheduling for synchronized joint parsing, including practical modifications: copying rather than moving buffer items during syntax actions to keep them available to semantics; a new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments; and interleaving rules that constrain when semantic actions can fire.
- Empirical results showing consistent gains over prior joint transition-based models (e.g., Henderson et al. 2008/2013) on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English), competitive multilingual performance, and strong runtime efficiency.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and strong engineering: The paper cleanly marries Henderson-style joint transitions with Dyer-style stack-LSTM state representations. The architecture is simple, end-to-end differentiable, and removes reliance on expensive, pipeline-derived path features, addressing a well-known bottleneck in SRL.
- Solid empirical improvements over prior joint parsers: On both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, the joint model outperforms earlier joint systems (including those using beam search and hand-crafted features). The controlled variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) are informative and demonstrate that joint training helps in-domain.
- Efficiency: The greedy decoder with compact neural features yields substantial runtime improvements over competitive pipelines while maintaining accuracy. This matters for practical deployment and large-scale processing.
- Reasonable multilingual generalization: Without task- or language-specific feature engineering, the model is competitive across several CoNLL 2009 languages, benefiting noticeably from pretrained embeddings in Chinese.
- Sound modeling choices and completeness: The use of four stack LSTMs (S/M/B/A), recursive composition functions for syntactic and semantic attachments (gs, gm) and predicate senses (gd), constraints on legal transitions, and pseudo-projectivization together make a coherent, implementable system. Training details (dropout, SGD, embeddings) are adequate for reproduction, and code release is promised.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The primary technical ingredients (stack LSTMs for transition-based parsing; Henderson-style synchronized transitions) are known. The new elements (copy vs move, interleaving schedule, M-SELF) are practical but incremental. The paper’s value is largely empirical and engineering, rather than a fundamentally new learning or inference approach for joint parsing.
- Greedy decoding without exploration or global normalization: The model is susceptible to exposure bias and label bias. There is no comparison to beam search, globally normalized training, or dynamic oracles, all of which are standard in transition-based parsing and SRL and might reduce error propagation. Given the scheduling constraints between syntax and semantics, the search space may be particularly fragile to early mistakes.
- Imperfect oracle and unquantified impact: The paper acknowledges that the semantic transition oracle cannot encode all gold graphs (1.5% of English sentences), but does not analyze how these constraints affect training bias, test-time errors, or which graph phenomena are lost. This is important, since training on an approximate oracle can mislead the learner.
- Analysis depth: While the variants are useful, ablations are otherwise limited. Missing are (i) per-component ablations (e.g., removing the action-history LSTM, removing predicate-sense composition gd, or replacing recursive composition with bagging), (ii) analyses by dependency length, label, and crossing arcs, and (iii) the effect of pretrained embeddings vs learned-only across languages. The paper also lacks a qualitative error analysis and a discussion of how the interleaving schedule biases which decisions are made with/without sufficient context.
- Comparisons and setup: The strongest English SRL systems cited are stronger but orthogonal; however, an empirical bridge is missing. For example, a pipeline that feeds this model with a strong external syntactic parser (or vice versa) would better quantify the benefit/cost of joint learning here. For multilingual, the omission of morphology or character-level modeling likely depresses certain languages, but this is not explored beyond a note in discussion.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the interleaving schedule? Did you try allowing semantics to fire multiple times before returning to syntax, or learning when to switch? Any performance trade-offs if semantics can operate before a syntactic S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT?
- What is the quantitative impact of the imperfect semantic oracle on training and evaluation? Can you report dev/test performance restricted to sentences/graphs that are encodable vs non-encodable, and characterize the structural phenomena that cause failures?
- Did you try beam search or globally normalized training (e.g., CRF loss) or dynamic oracles? Even a small-beam experiment would clarify whether the learned state summary suffices for greedy inference.
- How much does each component contribute? Please report ablations removing (a) the action-history LSTM, (b) the predicate-sense composition gd, (c) pretrained embeddings, and (d) recursive composition (replacing with head-only features).
- For unseen predicates defaulting to “01”: what is the token-level accuracy of this heuristic, and how much does it affect end-task performance on out-of-domain data?
- Can you provide analyses of SRL F1 by arc length, label type (core vs adjunct), and the presence of crossing arcs, to substantiate the claim that learned state summaries recover information usually provided by path features?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, timely engineering contribution that demonstrates that learned parser-state representations plus greedy inference suffice to surpass prior joint transition-based models and approach competitive pipelines, with clear speed benefits. The methodological novelty is modest but the empirical and practical impact for joint syntax–semantics parsing is real. Strengthening the paper with a minimal exploration of search/training alternatives, a deeper oracle and error analysis, and tighter ablations would significantly improve its scientific value. I lean toward acceptance if space allows for the additional analyses or if code release makes reproduction straightforward.
