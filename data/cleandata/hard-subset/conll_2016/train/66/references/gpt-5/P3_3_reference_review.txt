Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a fast, greedy joint parser for syntactic and semantic dependency parsing built on stack LSTMs. It extends the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. by (i) learning continuous representations of the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, action history) via stack LSTMs and recursive composition functions, rather than relying on handcrafted features; (ii) using purely greedy decoding without beam search; and (iii) adding a small number of transition-system tweaks (e.g., copying buffer items for syntax to keep them available to semantic actions, and a new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments). The model achieves state-of-the-art among prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and strong runtime.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An effective joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that learns representations for the entire joint parsing state with stack LSTMs and greedy inference, outperforming prior joint models and running very fast.
2) A concrete interleaved transition system for joint parsing with practical modifications (buffer-copying in arc-eager; M-SELF) and a simple sense handling strategy.
3) An empirical demonstration that learned state representations can obviate expensive path-based features in joint parsing, with controlled comparisons (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and multilingual results.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over prior joint models with strong speed: The joint system consistently beats Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and is on par with top systems in several non-English languages. The end-to-end runtime is compelling for practical use.
- Clean, unified modeling of the whole algorithmic state: Using four stack LSTMs (S, M, B, A) plus recursive tree composition is principled and elegant. It generalizes Dyer et al.’s approach to the joint setting and eliminates reliance on handcrafted path features, aligning well with current trends in representation learning.
- Sensible transition design for joint parsing: The interleaving protocol and modifications (copying instead of moving in syntactic actions; M-SWAP; M-SELF to support NomBank self-dependencies) are well-motivated and practically important for correctness and coverage.
- Controlled baselines and analysis: The syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid settings help quantify how much joint modeling contributes, and the WSJ vs Brown split reveals domain effects (joint gains in-domain; hybrid slightly better OOD), which is informative.
- Reproducibility: Use of public embeddings, clear hyperparameters, and a promise of open-source code improve transparency and impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core technical advances are a relatively direct combination of Henderson-style synchronized transitions with Dyer-style stack LSTMs and greedy inference. The novel transition (M-SELF) and buffer-copying tweak are incremental. The paper’s main value is empirical and engineering-focused rather than introducing new learning or inference ideas for joint parsing.
- Greedy training/decoding without exploration: The model uses static oracles and locally normalized greedy decisions. There is no dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, global loss, or beam search. Exposure bias and error propagation are likely, especially for semantics. This is consistent with the observed OOD degradation and underperformance vs. best pipelined SRL. Even a small-beam or dynamic-oracle ablation could strengthen the case.
- Imperfect semantic oracle and its impact: The conversion cannot encode some gold graphs (e.g., 1.5% English 2009). The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify how it affects training loss or final accuracy, or whether these cases cluster on particular phenomena (crossing arcs, nominal predicates). This is important given that training inconsistencies can bias the learned policy.
- Baselines and fairness of comparisons: The “Hybrid” baseline does not expose full syntactic trees to the semantic component (only partial structures visible on S/B), so it underestimates the benefit of a strong pipeline. Some comparisons to top pipelined systems include global reranking/DP decoding and rich features; a more apples-to-apples neural pipeline baseline with full predicted trees and comparable representation capacity would be informative. The runtime comparison to Lei et al. could clarify whether both include identical preprocessing (POS/lemma) and hardware details.
- Limited analysis and ablations: The paper lacks deeper error analysis (e.g., long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs, nominal predicates), component ablations (importance of action-history LSTM, buffer LSTM, pretrained embeddings), and sensitivity to hyperparameters. Multilingual gains in Chinese appear heavily driven by pretrained embeddings; the role of morphology is not explored, which is crucial for languages like Czech.

Questions to authors
- Oracle and training consistency: For the sentences whose gold joint parses cannot be encoded exactly, how do you construct training targets? Do you drop arcs, approximate with nearest encodable structures, or alter the sequence? What fraction of semantic arcs are lost, and how does this affect development performance?
- Exposure bias: Did you try dynamic oracle training or scheduled sampling? If not, can you comment on feasibility for your transition set, and whether beam search (even small) helps semantics, especially OOD?
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing the action-history LSTM, the buffer LSTM, or pretrained embeddings? How much does M-SELF contribute on NomBank-heavy subsets?
- Interleaving constraints: By forcing left-side syntactic dependencies before left-side semantic ones and switching only on buffer-modifying actions, do you restrict the reachable set of joint states? Can some gold semantic attachments be delayed too long or be forced early, affecting accuracy?
- Runtime comparison: Does the reported 177.6±18 seconds include predicate identification for CoNLL 2008 and sense disambiguation time? Are both systems evaluated with identical preprocessing and identical single-core constraints?

Additional comments
- Incorporating character-level or morphological features could materially improve non-English performance at little runtime cost.
- A small-beam or globally normalized variant might offer a favorable accuracy–speed trade-off and mitigate overfitting to WSJ.
