Summary and claimed contributions
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire parser state with stack LSTMs. It follows the Henderson et al. transition system with modifications (copy- vs move-based operations, interleaving constraints) and introduces a new M-SELF transition for semantic self-arguments. The model uses learned composition functions to represent partial trees/graphs and chooses actions via a classifier over allowed transitions. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 show improvements over prior joint systems and competitive multilingual results, with strong runtime advantages.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical joint syntax–semantics parser that learns full-state representations via stack LSTMs and performs fully greedy inference, yielding strong accuracy for joint parsing and substantial speedups.
2) Algorithmic adaptations for joint parsing with stack LSTMs: interleaved syntactic/semantic transitions over a shared buffer, copy-based arc-eager operations, and a new M-SELF transition to handle NomBank self-arguments.
3) Empirical evidence that representation learning can obviate expert-crafted joint features: improved results over prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual performance.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical gains over prior joint models with clear ablations. The joint model outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), Gesmundo et al. (2009), and Titov et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. The syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint comparisons are well chosen and illuminate the contribution of joint modeling versus pipelining and the value of syntax to SRL.

2) Clear, efficient engineering geared to practical use. The greedy inference with learned full-state representations offers an attractive speed–accuracy trade-off: reported end-to-end runtime on CoNLL 2009 English is ~178s on a single core, substantially faster than a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under the authors’ setup. The linear-time design and small memory footprint are compelling for deployment.

3) Thoughtful adaptation of stack LSTMs to a non-trivial joint transition system. Using separate stack LSTMs for syntax, semantics, buffer, and action history, together with recursive composition functions for mixed syntactic/semantic fragments, is elegant and arguably the key to making greedy inference competitive without hand-crafted global features.

4) Sensible modeling of task idiosyncrasies. The new M-SELF transition addresses NomBank self-arguments; copy-based buffer access preserves items for both syntactic and semantic actions; constraints on action interleaving ensure completeness. These design choices show careful attention to the specifics of joint dependency parsing.

5) Broad evaluation, including multilingual. Results on multiple languages from CoNLL 2009, with and without pretrained embeddings, demonstrate adaptability and provide useful evidence beyond English.

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty relative to prior stack LSTM parsing. The core contribution largely adapts Dyer et al. (2015) to a known joint transition system (Henderson et al.), with engineering tweaks. While valuable, the conceptual advance may be viewed as incremental. A deeper analysis of why full-state representations help joint parsing, or novel training/inference techniques (e.g., dynamic oracles, global objectives), would strengthen the contribution.

2) Oracle and annotation conversion issues are under-explored. The semantic oracle cannot perfectly encode gold structures (1.5% of EN sentences), and pseudo-projectivization is used for syntax. The impact of this supervision noise on learning and evaluation is not quantified. Does the model learn to compensate, and how sensitive are results to these approximations?

3) Limited ablations and error analysis. It is unclear how much each component contributes: pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, composition functions (gs/gm/gd), M-SELF, interleaving constraint, or copy vs move. An ablation table and qualitative error analysis (e.g., crossing arcs, long-range arguments, out-of-domain degradation) would make the empirical claims more convincing.

4) Joint overfitting and domain robustness. Joint beats hybrid in-domain but lags out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting overfitting to WSJ syntax–semantics correlations. The paper does not explore mitigation (e.g., stronger regularization, domain-invariant features, multi-domain training), nor quantify variance across runs or statistical significance.

5) Comparisons to SOTA are not fully apples-to-apples. While the paper fairly notes that pipelined/global systems exploit orthogonal advances, the main message would benefit from controlled comparisons that isolate the search vs feature differences (e.g., beam vs greedy with the same features; adding limited global constraints) to better position the proposed approach.

Questions to authors
1) Oracle noise: For sentences where the semantic transition sequence cannot encode the gold graph, how are training targets generated? Are arcs dropped, approximated, or otherwise handled? What fraction of gold arcs is lost, and what is the delta in dev performance if those sentences are removed?

2) Ablations: What is the effect on Macro F1 of removing pretrained embeddings, the action-history stack, M-SELF, or using move instead of copy in arc-eager? Similarly, what is the impact of the interleaving constraint (e.g., allowing more flexible switching)?

3) Training strategy: Did you try dynamic oracle training or exploration (e.g., scheduled sampling) to reduce exposure bias in greedy decoding? If so, what was the outcome?

4) Variance and significance: Are reported scores averages over multiple seeds? If not, what is the variance across runs, and are improvements over Henderson et al. statistically significant?

5) Runtime comparison details: Is the Lei et al. (2015) runtime measured with identical hardware, single core, and preprocessing? What is the average tokens/sec for each model to facilitate fair comparison?

6) Error profile: Which semantic relations and argument distances benefit most from the joint model? How often is M-SWAP used, and does it primarily help languages with more non-projectivity?

Overall, this is a solid, practically impactful paper with clear empirical value and a well-engineered adaptation of stack LSTMs to joint dependency parsing. Addressing the ablation/oracle questions and clarifying robustness would strengthen the submission.
