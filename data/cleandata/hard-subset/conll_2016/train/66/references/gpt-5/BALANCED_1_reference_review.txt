Summary
The paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire parser state with stack LSTMs and performs greedy inference without hand-crafted features or beam search. The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013), with interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer, and introduces a new M-SELF transition for semantic self-dependencies. The model is evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual), shows strong improvements over prior joint models, competitive performance with pipeline systems, and substantial speed gains. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) explore the contribution of joint training and syntax.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A fast, feature-lean joint parser that learns representations of the full algorithmic state via stack LSTMs and achieves strong empirical results: it outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and is competitive with pipelines while being substantially faster.

2) An effective joint transition system and training recipe, including an explicit handling of semantic self-dependencies (M-SELF) and ablation variants that clarify the role of syntax in SRL.

3) A thorough empirical study across English and multiple languages, with runtime analysis and comparisons to prominent baselines.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advance over prior joint models: The joint system and its variants consistently outperform Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009), demonstrating that learning the entire parser state (rather than local feature templates) matters. The gains hold on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English; multilingual results are on par with top systems.

- Strong speed-accuracy tradeoff: The end-to-end runtime (≈178s on CoNLL 2009 English test, single core) is ~2.5x faster than a competitive pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) while delivering comparable macro F1, making it practical for large-scale use.

- Thoughtful model design and engineering: The shared-buffer interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions is principled, permits SRL without expensive full-tree features, and preserves linear-time decoding. The M-SELF transition addresses CoNLL 2009 nominal self-arguments, a real dataset quirk.

- Informative ablations: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint variants clarify how syntax helps SRL and how joint training can help (WSJ) or hurt (Brown) relative to pipelining, indicating where the model overfits and where it generalizes.

- Reproducibility orientation: The paper provides training details (embeddings, hyperparameters, optimization) and promises an open-source implementation; this is valuable for community adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior components: The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM machinery is adopted from Dyer et al. (2015). Beyond the full-state encoding, M-SELF, and copy-vs-move adjustments, the algorithmic novelty is incremental. The paper’s main contribution is empirical integration rather than a fundamentally new parsing or learning paradigm.

- Greedy decoding without exploration or global training: The model uses greedy inference and local cross-entropy training without dynamic oracles, global objectives, or beam search. While speed is a plus, the absence of exploration-aware training likely contributes to overfitting (WSJ vs Brown) and may cap performance relative to structured or exact-inference SRL systems.

- Incomplete analysis of oracle/transition limitations: The semantic oracle is imperfect (cannot encode gold for ~1.5% of English sentences), but the impact on training and evaluation is not quantified (e.g., how many arcs lost, sensitivity of SRL F1 to oracle errors, whether errors cluster around crossing arcs handled by M-SWAP).

- Limited diagnostics on what the model learns: There is little error analysis by arc length, predicate type (verbal vs nominal), crossing/non-crossing arcs, or predicate-argument distance. Given that the approach eschews path-based features, it would be useful to understand where the learned representations substitute effectively and where they fail.

- Domain robustness and feature reliance: The hybrid outperforming joint on Brown suggests domain overfitting; yet no domain adaptation techniques (e.g., regularization variants, representation sharing constraints) are explored. The model also relies on pretrained embeddings; English results without pretraining are not reported (only Chinese was mentioned as sensitive), leaving unclear how robust the approach is in low-resource or out-of-domain settings.

Questions to Authors
- Oracle/transition conversion: What fraction of gold semantic arcs are lost due to oracle imperfections (beyond the 1.5% sentence rate), and how much would an ideal oracle improve dev/test F1? Do most failures involve multiple crossing arcs even with M-SWAP?

- Significance and variance: Are the improvements over prior joint models statistically significant? Please report variance over multiple random seeds and, if possible, paired tests.

- Pretraining sensitivity: For English, what is the performance delta with and without pretrained embeddings? How sensitive are results to embedding quality (e.g., domain mismatch)?

- Error analysis: Can you provide a breakdown of SRL F1 by predicate POS (verb vs noun), argument distance, arc crossing, and sentence length? How often is M-SELF used and how much does it improve nominal SRL?

- Copy vs move choice: Did you empirically compare your “copy” modification of SHIFT/RIGHT against moving the buffer item (with a different interleaving strategy)? What is the accuracy/speed tradeoff?

- Overfitting and training strategies: Did you try scheduled sampling, dynamic oracles, or beam search with small widths? Any evidence that exploration-aware training reduces the WSJ-Brown gap?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider dynamic oracle training and/or small-beam decoding to mitigate greedy search errors while preserving most speed benefits.

- Explore character-level or morphological features, especially for morphologically rich languages, and report their impact.

- Provide more detailed runtime breakdowns (syntax vs semantics phases) and memory usage; include comparisons to joint baselines when available.

- Release code and training scripts at camera-ready; include seeds and exact preprocessing to ensure reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper offers a solid, well-engineered integration that delivers strong accuracy-speed tradeoffs and advances the state of joint models, despite modest novelty and limited analysis. The contribution is valuable to the community and suitable for CoNLL.
