- Paper: greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs, interleaved transitions, new M-SELF, recursive composition; competitive accuracy and much faster runtime.

- Contribution 1: Shows whole-state stack-LSTM representations enable fully greedy joint syntax–semantics parsing to outperform prior joint models with strong speed benefits.

- Contribution 2: Proposes a practical joint transition system (including M-SELF) and an interleaving schedule, with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).

- Contribution 3: Empirical study on CoNLL-2008/2009 across languages with runtime analysis, demonstrating good accuracy–speed tradeoffs without hand-crafted features.

- Strength: Clear empirical gains over prior joint models; ablations show syntax helps SRL and results are consistent with literature.

- Strength: Strong speed–accuracy tradeoff — near state-of-the-art semantics while being ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline baseline.

- Strength: Sensible, well-motivated architecture: four stack LSTMs (S, M, B, A) and recursive composition remove need for feature engineering.

- Strength: Practical completeness — handles predicate ID, sense disambiguation, nominal self-arguments, and non-projective syntax via pseudo-projectivization and M-SWAP.

- Strength: Multilingual evaluation shows adaptability beyond English, competitive when pretrained embeddings are available.

- Weakness: Novelty is incremental over Dyer et al. (2015); main novelty is applying stack LSTMs to joint parsing with modest transition additions.

- Weakness: Greedy training/decoding with a static oracle and local loss exposes the model to exposure bias and search errors, possibly limiting semantic F1.

- Weakness: Oracle/transition coverage issues: ~1.5% of EN-2009 sentences can't be encoded; semantic conversion is imperfect and impacts comparability.

- Weakness: Comparability and significance concerns: differing resources/embeddings across baselines and no reported statistical significance tests.

- Weakness: Limited ablation/analysis depth — missing finer-grained component studies and error breakdowns that would clarify drivers of gains.

- Question: Please report statistical significance for main improvements over Henderson et al. and for Joint vs Hybrid/Semantics-only on WSJ and Brown.

- Question: What fraction of arcs/tokens are affected by sentences whose gold graphs cannot be encoded by the oracle? Any empirical upper bound?

- Question: How sensitive is performance to beam size (e.g., small beams 4–8)? Any preliminary beam results?

- Question: Were dynamic oracles or scheduled sampling tried to mitigate exposure bias; if not, comment on feasibility for this transition set?

- Question: For multilingual experiments, were pretrained embeddings and tokenization comparable across languages; can you report results without pretrained embeddings?

- Comment: Clarify theoretical runtime with M-SWAP — worst-case complexity can exceed linear when swaps are frequent.

- Comment: The default "01" for unseen predicate senses is pragmatic; report a small error analysis or alternative backoffs.

- Comment: Add training details (gradient clipping, epochs, early-stopping patience) and clarify whether action embeddings are shared between syntactic/semantic actions.

- Comment: Ensure figures/tables are legible and include error bars where appropriate.

- Comment: Open-source code and scripts for data conversion (pseudo-projectivization, oracle generation) should be provided for reproducibility.

- Overall: Solid, well-executed application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing with a strong efficiency story; moderate novelty but acceptable pending clarifications on comparability, oracle limits, and training/decoding analysis.
