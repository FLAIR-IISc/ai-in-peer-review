Summary and claimed contributions
The paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), enabling fully greedy decoding without expert-crafted features. The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013), with an added M-SELF action to handle semantic self-dependencies and a specific interleaving policy between syntactic and semantic transitions. Experiments on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English and multilingual) show the model surpasses prior joint parsers and is competitive with stronger pipeline systems while being substantially faster.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A greedy, joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns full parser-state representations via stack LSTMs and recursive composition, eliminating hand-engineered features and outperforming prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. This is the primary technical and empirical contribution.

2) A unified transition system and training setup for joint parsing, including a new M-SELF transition for self-arguments and an interleaving policy that shares a buffer across syntax and semantics, with linear-time complexity and strong practical runtime.

3) Empirical study contrasting joint vs. pipeline-like alternatives (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid) showing the benefits of joint learning in-domain and competitive performance out-of-domain and across multiple languages.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance against joint baselines: The joint model consistently improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, indicating that learning full parser-state representations is beneficial beyond locally scoped features. These gains hold in both LAS and SRL metrics and in the joint macro score.

- Competitive with high-performing pipelines at much lower cost: On CoNLL 2009 English, the joint model’s overall scores are on par with the top systems from the shared task (except Zhao et al., 2009), while end-to-end runtime is ~177.6±18 seconds on a single core, about 2.5× faster than Lei et al. (2015) under matched settings. This demonstrates clear practical advantages.

- Technically sound architecture with clear design choices: The model integrates stack LSTMs over four structures (S, M, B, A), recursive composition functions for syntactic/semantic arcs and predicate sense, and an explicit constraints/scheduling scheme. The approach preserves linear-time decoding and provides a consistent learning objective (cross-entropy over allowed actions) with dropout regularization.

- Evidence on joint vs. pipeline factors: By reporting Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid, and Joint, the paper isolates the effect of joint learning. Joint beats Hybrid on WSJ, suggesting mutual reinforcement of syntax and semantics, while Hybrid does better OOD (Brown), identifying overfitting as a likely issue for joint learning.

- Breadth: Multilingual experiments show the approach adapts beyond English and is comparable to top systems on several languages, highlighting generality. The paper also includes predicate identification for CoNLL 2008 with a simple BiLSTM (91.43 F1), completing the end-to-end pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The transition set is largely from Henderson et al. (2013), and the representation learning framework closely follows Dyer et al. (2015). The main novelty is applying full-state stack LSTMs to joint parsing and adding M-SELF. The contribution is more integrative/engineering than conceptually new.

- Greedy decoding without exploration exacerbates exposure bias: Training optimizes next-action log-loss under gold sequences, while test-time is fully greedy with no dynamic oracle, cost-sensitive learning, or beam. The OOD drop (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) suggests overfitting/search error; no experiments test whether beam or exploration training would improve robustness.

- Imperfect oracle for semantic transitions: The paper notes ~1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded due to multiple crossing arcs despite M-SWAP. The impact on training/evaluation is not quantified. It is unclear whether these instances introduce label noise or bias in the learned policy.

- Insufficient ablations and error analysis: The contribution of key components (M-SELF, M-SWAP, interleaving policy, copy vs. move modifications to arc-eager, pretrained embeddings) is not quantified via ablation. Claims such as Chinese benefiting substantially from pretraining are not backed by numbers in the main text. No qualitative/phenomenon-level analysis is provided.

- Comparability details could be clearer: The speed comparison with Lei et al. (2015) mentions “identical settings,” but hardware and software stack details are not fully specified. For CoNLL 2008, the predicate identification module contributes to end-to-end results but its effect on joint performance is not separated. For multilingual settings, lack of morphological features is acknowledged but not explored (e.g., with character-level embeddings).

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- What is the quantitative impact of the imperfect oracle conversion (e.g., training instances affected, delta in dev performance if these cases are excluded)? How are such cases handled during training?

- Provide ablations: (i) without M-SELF; (ii) without M-SWAP; (iii) with standard arc-eager move instead of copy; (iv) without pretrained embeddings (at least for English and Chinese). How do these affect LAS, SRL F1, and Macro F1?

- Have you tried beam search or exploration/dynamic-oracle training? Even small beams or scheduled sampling could address exposure bias; please report any preliminary results or explain constraints.

- Can you quantify the claim about Chinese relying on pretraining (numbers with/without pretrained embeddings)? Similarly, do character-level embeddings reduce the gap on morphologically rich languages?

- Clarify the runtime comparison setup (CPU model, BLAS, compilation flags) and whether SRL-only inference time (excluding syntax) is also measured for the Hybrid baseline to contextualize the practical trade-off.

- Is the open-source implementation available at the time of camera-ready, and does it include scripts for reproducing the reported numbers (including predicate identification for CoNLL 2008)?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and provides solid empirical evidence that full-state neural representations enable fast and competitive joint parsing. Novelty is moderate but meaningful within the joint parsing setting.
