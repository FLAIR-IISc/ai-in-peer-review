Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a fully greedy, transition-based joint parser for syntactic dependency parsing and semantic role labeling (SRL) that learns continuous representations of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. The system interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions over shared data structures, introduces a new semantic transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies, and trains end-to-end without expert-crafted features. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual) show the model outperforms prior joint systems and is competitive with pipeline baselines, while being substantially faster.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A unified, greedy transition system for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing with learned representations of the full algorithmic state via stack LSTMs, obviating hand-engineered features and beam search, yet outperforming prior joint models.
2) An effective synchronization of syntax and semantics over a shared buffer with novel design choices (copy vs move for syntax steps; interleaving constraints) and a new M-SELF transition to cover NomBank self-arguments.
3) Strong empirical results: new SOTA among prior joint approaches on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and compelling speed gains over feature-rich pipelines, plus informative baselines (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) elucidating the contribution of joint modeling.

Strengths
- Clear modeling advance for joint parsing with learned state representations: Using stack LSTMs to summarize the entire state (four stacks: syntactic, semantic, buffer, action history) is a principled way to replace costly, hand-crafted long-distance features (e.g., predicate–argument paths). The design elegantly accommodates the asynchrony of syntax and semantics while preserving access to shared buffer content (via copy instead of move) and enforcing constraints to prevent invalid states.

- Strong results against prior joint systems with a simpler, faster decoder: The model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. It demonstrates that greedy inference with learned state summaries can beat beam-search-based joint systems that rely on manually designed features. The runtime numbers (≈178s single-core on CoNLL 2009 English test) are compelling, especially compared to competitive pipelines (>2× slower).

- Good experimental design and diagnostic baselines: The inclusion of semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid (syntax pipeline → semantic learner) variants is thoughtful. Results show (i) semantics benefits from syntax; (ii) joint learning helps in-domain but may overfit vs hybrid OOD; and (iii) syntax does not degrade when trained jointly. This provides insight into when joint training is beneficial.

- Practical handling of SRL peculiarities within the transition system: The M-SELF action for NomBank self-arguments and M-SWAP for non-projective semantic arcs make the transition set expressive for SRL graphs, with clear constraints to avoid infinite loops and duplicates. This careful engineering improves coverage.

- Reproducibility and reasonable engineering: Pretrained embeddings, sensible hyperparameters, and public code promise facilitate adoption. The memory and compute footprint are low.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Dyer et al. (2015): The core technical contribution heavily reuses the stack LSTM idea from Dyer et al. The main new aspects are adapting the framework to a synchronized joint parsing setting and adding a small number of task-specific transitions/constraints. While practically useful, the conceptual leap beyond Dyer et al. may be seen as incremental.

- Incomplete analysis of modeling choices and capacity: The paper lacks ablations for critical components: action history stack, composition functions (gs, gm, gd), pretrained vs learned embeddings, dropout, and the copy-vs-move modification. It also omits a beam-search variant or globally normalized alternative (e.g., CRF or reinforcement learning/dynamic oracle) to quantify the trade-off between greediness and accuracy.

- Oracle/coverage limitations for semantic transitions undercut claims: The authors note that the oracle conversion to transitions is imperfect (≈1.5% of English sentences are not correctly encodable). The empirical impact on training and evaluation is not quantified (e.g., how many gold arcs lost; does this bias models?). This weakens the “joint” upper bound and merits closer analysis.

- Generalization and overfitting concerns: The hybrid model surpasses joint on the Brown test set, suggesting the joint learner overfits WSJ-specific correlations. There is no discussion of domain adaptation or regularization strategies (e.g., stronger dropout, data augmentation, predicate sense smoothing) to mitigate this.

- Comparisons to strongest SRL systems are limited: While acknowledging orthogonal advances (global features, exact inference, multi-resource training), the paper does not attempt lightweight integrations (e.g., adding inexpensive syntax features, small rerankers, or minimal beam) to position the approach closer to state of the art. Statistical significance testing is not reported.

Questions to authors
- Oracle coverage: For the 1.5% of sentences where the gold joint structure is not exactly representable, what proportion of gold semantic arcs are lost? How does excluding those from supervision affect dev/test scores? Did you consider augmenting the transition set to close this gap?

- Greedy vs beam/global normalization: Did you try small beams or globally normalized objectives (e.g., at the action level)? How much accuracy is left on the table relative to pure greediness, especially OOD?

- Ablations: Which components contribute most? Please report: no action history A; no pretrained embeddings; replacing composition functions with simple sums; removing M-SELF; using move instead of copy for S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT; and without pseudo-projectivization.

- Predicate sense backoff: Defaulting unseen predicates to “01” gives a strong prior—how sensitive are results to this choice? Did you try lemma-only classifiers or character-based sense backoff for rare words?

- Error analysis: What are the major error types in SRL (argument attachment vs labeling, long-distance vs local)? Do syntax errors predominantly harm SRL via headedness or via attachment distance? Any insights for improving OOD performance?

- Multilingual setup: How were OOVs and tokenization handled across languages, especially Chinese? Any language-specific preprocessing beyond embeddings? Could character-level embeddings reduce reliance on external pretrained word vectors?

Additional comments
- Please report statistical significance for main improvements. 
- Clarify whether SRL graph acyclicity or other structural constraints are enforced during decoding. 
- Publishing code and pretrained models will materially increase impact; please include configuration files and random seeds.
