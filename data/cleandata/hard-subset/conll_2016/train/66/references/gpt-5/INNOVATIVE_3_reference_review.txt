Summary and main contributions
- A fast, greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that learns a vector representation of the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) via stack LSTMs, removing the need for expert-crafted, globally-scoped features or beam search.
- A practical transition design for joint parsing that interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer, with a copy-based arc-eager variant and a new M-SELF transition to handle NomBank self-dependencies.
- Strong empirical results: new SOTA among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual performance, and substantially faster end-to-end runtime than feature-rich pipelines, while remaining feature-agnostic.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness: Entire-state neural representation for joint parsing. Moving from local, top-of-stack feature templates to a learned representation of the full joint state is a clear, non-incremental idea. It directly challenges the conventional wisdom that rich handcrafted syntactic features and global decoding are necessary for strong SRL performance.
- Simplicity and speed: Fully greedy inference, no beam, no global decoding, and no hand-engineered features, yet competitive with complex pipelines. The single-core runtime (≈178s on CoNLL-2009 English test) is practically attractive and 2.5× faster than a strong published system.
- Clear empirical win over prior joint models: Consistent improvements over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) demonstrate the value of whole-state representation learning for joint parsing. The syntax-only variant also slightly improves over a strong neural syntactic baseline, indicating the approach does not sacrifice syntactic quality.
- Solid ablation framing via model variants: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint settings are well-chosen to probe where the gains come from, and reveal a nuanced story: syntax helps semantics in-domain, while the hybrid avoids the joint model’s out-of-domain overfitting.
- Thoughtful engineering choices for joint transitions: Copying instead of moving in arc-eager to expose items to semantic actions, and introducing M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments, are small but crucial design innovations that enable faithful joint modeling in a greedy framework.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond adapting stack LSTMs to joint parsing: The core learning and composition mechanisms closely follow Dyer et al. (2015), and the transition inventory largely follows Henderson et al. (2013). The main novelty is the joint, entire-state application and small but important transition tweaks. Readers may view this as an elegant adaptation rather than a fundamentally new model class.
- Sparse analysis/ablations on what matters: The paper lacks ablations on the four-state components (S/M/B/A), composition choices, copy vs move in arc-eager, action history, and pretrained embeddings. It is difficult to attribute gains to architecture vs. representations vs. training. Likewise, the impact of the M-SELF transition is not quantified.
- Incomplete treatment of oracle imperfections and error propagation: The semantic oracle is imperfect (multiple crossings) and 1.5% of English sentences cannot be encoded exactly. There is no analysis of how this upper bound affects learning/testing, nor diagnostics for failure modes. The overfitting observed out-of-domain suggests a need for training with exploration/dynamic oracles, which is not explored.
- Comparison scope and significance: While the comparisons to prior joint models and several strong SRL systems are appreciated, significance testing is not reported, and some newer neural baselines (e.g., with character/morphology, structured decoding, or shallow global constraints) are absent. Multilingual results omit morphology, which handicaps certain languages and complicates fairness.
- Reproducibility and stability: Code is promised but not provided; there is limited reporting on variance over random seeds, sensitivity to hyperparameters, or the contribution of pretrained embeddings—particularly important given the multilingual results.

Questions to authors
- How crucial are each of the four stack summaries (S, M, B, A)? Can you report ablations removing A (action history), using a standard LSTM for B, or limiting attention to top-k elements?
- What is the empirical impact of copy vs. move in S-RIGHT/S-SHIFT? Does reverting to standard arc-eager (move) materially hurt semantics, and by how much?
- How often is M-SELF used in training/test, and what is the F1 delta if it is removed?
- Can you quantify the effect of the imperfect semantic oracle? What fraction of gold arcs are unrecoverable, and how does this translate into an upper bound on F1?
- Did you attempt small beams or dynamic oracles to reduce train-test mismatch or curb overfitting on WSJ while preserving speed?
- How sensitive is performance to pretrained embeddings (including OOV handling) and to dropout? Please report mean/std over multiple seeds.
- For multilingual, can you show whether character/morphological embeddings help, especially for Czech/Japanese? Even a small pilot would clarify extensibility.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. This paper advances a clear, novel direction—learning an entire-state neural representation for joint syntactic–semantic parsing with fully greedy inference—and demonstrates that it can rival far more complex pipelines while being substantially faster. Although some components are adapted from prior work and the analysis could be deeper, the conceptual boldness and strong empirical results justify acceptance and will likely influence future joint modeling and fast parsing research.
