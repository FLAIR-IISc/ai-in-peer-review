Summary and claimed contributions:
The paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that builds both structures incrementally with a single, greedy decision sequence. The model encodes the entire parser state—syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history—using stack LSTMs, and composes partial structures with neural functions. It modifies the arc-eager paradigm to copy (rather than move) buffer items so semantics can access them, interleaves syntactic/semantic transitions, and introduces a new M-SELF transition for semantic self-dependencies. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 show the approach surpasses prior joint models and is competitive with top pipeline systems, while being fast.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) A simple, fast, greedy joint parsing architecture that learns a full-state representation via stack LSTMs and eliminates hand-crafted features, yielding strong performance improvements over previous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009.
2) A transition system and scheduling for joint dependency parsing, including the M-SELF operation and buffer-copy variant enabling semantic access, plus empirical comparisons (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that quantify the benefits of joint learning.
3) Broad empirical validation: competitive results to top systems and favorable runtime; multilingual evaluation across CoNLL 2009 languages.

Strengths:
1) Strong empirical gains over prior joint models with clear baselines. The joint model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and contemporaries on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. The ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid vs joint) provide evidence that joint training helps SRL in-domain and that the approach does not harm syntax.
2) Practical efficiency. The parser runs in linear time with greedy decoding and small memory footprint, and achieves substantially faster end-to-end runtime than a strong publicly available SRL pipeline under comparable conditions. This makes the approach attractive for practical deployments.
3) Clear, technically sound formulation. The transition inventory and constraints are precisely specified; the interleaving schedule, copying vs moving behavior, and handling of crossing arcs via M-SWAP are well motivated. The neural composition functions for syntactic, semantic, and predicate-sense nodes are defined explicitly, and training details (loss, optimization, dropout, embeddings) are provided.
4) Handling of semantic self-dependencies. The addition of M-SELF directly addresses a known quirk of CoNLL 2009 nominal predicates and appears to be a simple and effective extension of the transition set.
5) Multilingual validation. Results across multiple languages are competitive with top shared-task systems, indicating robustness beyond English.

Weaknesses:
1) Limited novelty relative to prior work; primarily an application of stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system. The transition system largely follows Henderson et al.; the main advance is replacing hand-crafted features and beam search with learned full-state representations and greedy decoding. While valuable, this is an incremental methodological step.
2) Greedy inference without global normalization or exploration risks suboptimal search and overfitting. The joint model underperforms a hybrid pipeline out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting overfitting to WSJ. No experiments with beam search, global training, or dynamic oracles are reported to mitigate exposure bias or search errors.
3) Incomplete ablations and analysis. The paper does not quantify the individual contribution of: action-history stack, buffer-copy vs move, M-SELF, pretrained embeddings across all languages, or the interleaving schedule. There is no error analysis to characterize where the joint model helps or hurts (e.g., long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs).
4) Supervision noise from imperfect oracles is acknowledged but not analyzed. For a non-trivial fraction of sentences, the gold semantic structures cannot be perfectly encoded; the impact on training and test performance is not quantified, and no alternative (e.g., approximate/dynamic oracle) is considered.
5) Comparison fairness and reproducibility details. The runtime comparison is to a different system class and may not be strictly apples-to-apples; more standardized efficiency metrics (tokens/sec, identical preprocessing) would strengthen claims. Code is promised but not available at review time; while many hyperparameters are listed, details of pretraining (tokenization, vocabulary handling), random seeds, and variance are limited.

Questions to authors:
- What is the quantitative impact of the M-SELF transition? Please report results with and without M-SELF on CoNLL 2009 English and the prevalence of self-arcs by language.
- Have you tried small beams or globally normalized training (or dynamic oracles)? If so, how do accuracy and runtime trade off compared to greedy?
- Can you provide ablations on key design choices: action-history stack A, copying vs moving buffer items, effect of removing pretrained embeddings, and removing the interleaving constraint?
- How large is the supervision mismatch due to non-encodable semantic graphs (percentage of arcs/states), and what is its measured effect on training loss and final F1?
- For CoNLL 2008, how do predicate identification errors propagate into joint performance (e.g., performance with gold vs predicted predicates)?
- Could you report mean/std over multiple random seeds for main results, and tokens/sec to facilitate fair runtime comparisons?

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a clear, technically sound, and practically useful joint parsing approach that delivers strong improvements over prior joint models with a simple, efficient design. While the conceptual novelty is moderate, the empirical results, clarity, and efficiency make it a solid contribution. Addressing the analysis and ablation gaps and clarifying fairness/reproducibility would further strengthen the work.
