Summary and contributions
- This paper presents a greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that reuses the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2008/2013) but replaces hand-engineered features with stack LSTMs that encode the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history). The model is trained with local classification over allowed transitions and composes partial structures recursively. A new M-SELF transition is introduced to handle NomBank-style self-arguments. The approach is fast and shows strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) Methodological: a fully greedy, feature-free joint parser that learns representations of the entire transition state with stack LSTMs, coupled with recursive composition for both syntactic and semantic structures.
  2) Empirical: consistent improvements over prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual performance; informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) quantifying the interplay of syntax and semantics.
  3) Engineering: a simple, fast system with low memory and strong throughput; introduction of an explicit M-SELF transition for nominal self-arguments.

Strengths
- Clear methodological advance for joint parsing within the transition-based paradigm. Moving from local feature templates to learned state representations (four stack LSTMs + compositional encoders) is well-motivated and technically sound. The paper carefully maps the transition inventory to neural composition functions, enabling the learner to exploit long-range state without manual design. This addresses a central limitation of prior joint transition systems.
- Strong and thorough empirical evaluation for a joint setting. The model surpasses earlier joint work (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and the multilingual results are competitive with the top CoNLL 2009 systems. The inclusion of both in-domain and out-of-domain (Brown) evaluation is valuable. Ablations (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid) directly probe whether syntax helps SRL and vice versa; findings (joint > hybrid in-domain but not OOD) are plausible and informative about overfitting.
- Practicality and speed. The system is simple to run (single-core, ~512MB), substantially faster than a strong pipeline baseline under comparable settings, while maintaining competitive accuracy. This makes the contribution useful for real-time or resource-constrained settings.
- Soundness and completeness of the transition design. The paper carefully adapts arc-eager with copying to keep items available for semantic actions, interleaves syntactic and semantic phases with clear constraints, and augments with M-SWAP and M-SELF for crossing arcs and self-arguments. This yields a coherent, linear-time greedy algorithm with explicit constraints to avoid invalid states.
- Reproducibility intent and reasonable training details. The model specifies embeddings, composition dimensions, dropout, optimization, pseudo-projectivization, and pretrained embeddings. The promise of open-source code further supports impact.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to Dyer et al. (2015). The primary modeling innovation is applying the stack-LSTM state encoding to a joint transition system plus adding M-SELF; the transition backbone and interleaving are largely inherited from Henderson et al. While the empirical results are good, the conceptual step may be viewed as a straightforward extension rather than a fundamentally new joint inference approach.
- Greedy local inference and imperfect oracle introduce avoidable suboptimality. The conversion of gold semantics to transitions is not perfect (notably when multiple crossings exist), and the model trains with local classification over allowed moves. This can bias learning and cap performance. The paper would be stronger with beam search or global learning comparisons, or at least an analysis quantifying the accuracy loss due to (i) oracle imperfections and (ii) greediness.
- Limited analysis of what state representation buys. While the results show gains over prior joint systems, there is little probing of which parts of the learned state (e.g., action history vs. buffer vs. stacks) matter, or how the interleaving constraint affects decisions. An ablation over the four summaries, composition functions, or label embeddings would substantiate the “entire-state” claim.
- Comparisons and fairness caveats. The speed comparison to Lei et al. (2015) is useful but the task setups and feature budgets differ substantially; additionally, several stronger SRL systems leverage global inference and extra resources. A controlled pipeline baseline using the same representations (no global reranking, same embeddings) would better isolate the benefit of joint modeling vs. learned features. Statistical significance tests on main results are not discussed.
- Generalization and error analysis are light. The out-of-domain drop and the hybrid outperforming joint on Brown suggest overfitting to WSJ; more detailed error analyses (e.g., by predicate/argument type, long-distance arcs, crossing arcs, non-projective syntax) would clarify strengths and failure modes, and whether the joint model helps specific phenomena.

Questions to authors
- How much of the end-to-end accuracy gap to state-of-the-art SRL is attributable to greediness versus the absence of global SRL features? Have you tried modest beams or global normalization, and what is the speed/accuracy trade-off?
- What proportion of semantic arcs in CoNLL 2009 cannot be perfectly encoded by your oracle, and what is the measured impact if you drop those sentences or arcs during training?
- Can you provide ablations removing individual stack LSTMs (e.g., action history or buffer) and/or switching off composition functions (flat representations) to quantify their contribution?
- How sensitive is performance to the interleaving constraint (syntax until S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT, then semantics until M-SHIFT)? Have you tried alternative schedules or learned switching?
- For multilingual experiments, did you include morphological features where available or only word/POS embeddings? Any results with character-based embeddings to compensate?

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed application of learned state representations to joint dependency-based syntax and semantics, with strong empirical results and practical speed. While the novelty over prior stack-LSTM parsing work is moderate, the paper meets the criteria of soundness, clarity, and relevance, and offers a useful joint baseline with insightful ablations. Addressing the analysis and inference concerns would further strengthen the contribution.
