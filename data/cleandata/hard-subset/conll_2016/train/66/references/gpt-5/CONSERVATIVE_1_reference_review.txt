Summary and main contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn continuous representations of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history), rather than hand-engineered features or local/top-of-stack views.
- It extends the Henderson et al. (2008/2013) synchronized transition system with a new M-SELF transition to handle nominal self-dependencies, and interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions while sharing the buffer.
- Empirically, the model outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, provides multilingual results comparable to top CoNLL 2009 systems, and is fast due to greedy inference.

Strengths
- Clear, unified model that eliminates expert-crafted features: The stack-LSTM representation of the full algorithmic state is cleanly motivated and removes the need for expensive path features, which are standard in SRL pipelines. This contributes to faster inference and simpler engineering.
- Competitive results against prior joint models: On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model improves over Henderson et al. and related joint baselines. This supports the claim that richer state representations can substitute for hand-crafted features in joint parsing.
- Sensible ablations: The paper includes Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid variants, allowing some insight into the contribution of joint training vs. pipelining and the importance of syntax for SRL. The observation that Joint helps in-domain but may overfit out-of-domain is informative.
- Practical efficiency: The greedy design yields strong speedups (2.5× vs. a published SRL pipeline under stated settings) with modest memory, making this attractive for practical use cases.
- Modest multilingual evidence: Results on multiple CoNLL 2009 languages show the approach adapts reasonably well, particularly with pretrained embeddings, lending some support to generality.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established components: The core transition system follows Henderson et al. (2013) and the representation learning relies on the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). The key novelty is applying full-state stack LSTMs to the joint setting and adding M-SELF, which is incremental rather than conceptually substantial. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis isolating the gains due specifically to full-state representations vs. strong local representations or beam search.
- Incomplete baselines and fairness of comparisons: The “Hybrid” baseline does not expose the semantics model to full-tree syntactic features, unlike standard pipelines; thus it cannot fairly assess whether joint learning beats a strong pipeline with access to complete syntactic structure. The runtime comparison is to one public system; implementation differences and hardware details may confound the speed claim.
- Greedy inference without analysis of search errors: The paper commits to greedy decoding but provides no comparison to beam search or globally normalized/dynamic-oracle training, nor analysis of error propagation. Given well-documented search errors in transition-based parsing/SRL, this is a notable omission. The observed out-of-domain degradation of Joint suggests potential overfitting to in-domain decision sequences.
- Oracle and transition coverage issues: The semantic oracle is not perfect due to multiple crossings; 1.5% of English sentences cannot be correctly encoded. It is unclear how these are handled during training and how much this biases learning/evaluation. No quantitative analysis of the impact of M-SWAP and M-SELF is provided.
- Evaluation gaps and statistical rigor: No significance testing or variance estimates are reported; only runtime has ± values. Hyperparameters are “set intuitively,” and there is no systematic tuning or ablation of pretrained embeddings, label embeddings, composition functions, or the effect of predicate identification accuracy (CoNLL 2008) on end metrics. The multilingual evaluation omits morphology, which is known to be important; the claim of being “on par” is not carefully qualified given these differences in resources.

Questions to authors
- Beam/greedy: How does the joint model perform with a small beam (e.g., 4–16)? Does beam search close the out-of-domain gap with the Hybrid model? Have you tried dynamic-oracle training to mitigate exposure bias?
- Oracle coverage: How exactly are sentences whose gold joint parses cannot be encoded by the transition system handled during training? Are they excluded, approximated, or forced with suboptimal sequences? What fraction across languages, and what is the performance impact?
- Contribution isolation: Can you provide ablations quantifying (i) full-state summaries vs. top-of-stack/local features, (ii) the contribution of M-SELF and M-SWAP, and (iii) the effect of pretrained embeddings? A comparison against a strong pipeline where the semantic model has access to full-tree syntactic features would be helpful.
- Statistical significance and stability: Please report variance across multiple runs with different random seeds and provide significance tests for the main comparisons (e.g., vs. Henderson et al., vs. your ablations, and vs. multilingual baselines).
- Predicate identification (CoNLL 2008): How sensitive are end metrics to the 91.43% predicate identification F1? Can you quantify the impact on semantic F1 if gold predicate identification is used?
- Runtime comparison: Can you clarify the exact hardware, threading, and implementation differences in your speed comparison to Lei et al. (2015)? Have you compared to a tuned, single-core baseline from another system implemented in the same framework to control for overhead?
- Handling morphology: Did you attempt character-level or subword embeddings for morphologically rich languages? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper is a solid incremental contribution demonstrating that full-state stack LSTMs can support fast joint parsing with performance competitive with prior joint models. However, relative to established baselines and state-of-the-art pipelines, the novelty is limited and the empirical validation lacks several critical comparisons and analyses. I would be more enthusiastic with stronger baselines (especially a fair pipeline), significance tests, and a deeper study of search vs. modeling trade-offs.
