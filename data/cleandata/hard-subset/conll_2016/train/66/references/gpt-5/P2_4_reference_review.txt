Summary
This paper presents a greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to encode the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with additions such as an M-SELF transition for nominal self-arguments and an interleaving schedule between syntax and semantics. The model dispenses with hand-crafted features, relies on learned representations (including pretrained “structured skip-gram” embeddings), and trains with cross-entropy over allowable transitions. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual) show the joint model outperforms prior joint systems and is competitive with pipeline SOTA, while being substantially faster; ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) quantify the contribution of joint prediction.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A fast, feature-free, greedy joint dependency parser that learns full parser-state representations via stack LSTMs and achieves the best reported results among prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. This is significant because prior joint models often required beam/global inference or hand-engineered features.
- A practical transition system and neural composition scheme for joint syntax–semantics, including an interleaving schedule that shares the buffer and a new M-SELF action to model nominal self-dependencies; plus ablations demonstrating the impact of syntactic information on SRL in this framework.
- Strong empirical evidence that full-state neural representations can replace expensive, global syntactic features in SRL, yielding competitive accuracy with much lower runtime.

Strengths
- Empirical significance and breadth: The joint model consistently outperforms earlier joint systems (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive with top pipeline systems. Multilingual results are generally on par with leading systems, evidencing portability.
- Technical soundness and clarity: The integration of stack LSTMs over S, M, B, and action history is well-motivated and consistent with prior success in syntactic parsing. Composition functions for syntactic/semantic arcs and predicate senses are clearly specified, and the interleaving protocol is described with constraints and complexity.
- Ablations and analysis: The paper evaluates syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint variants. The finding that Joint > Hybrid in-domain but Hybrid > Joint OOD is informative and suggests where greedy joint training helps or hurts. A runtime comparison shows large practical gains (∼2.5x faster than a strong pipeline).
- Reproducibility details: Hyperparameters, training regimen (dropout, learning rate schedule), embeddings, and preprocessing (pseudo-projectivization) are documented. Memory and runtime footprints are provided. Promised open-source implementation further supports reproducibility.
- Practicality: Greedy, linear-time decoding with small memory makes the approach attractive for real-time or large-scale settings without sacrificing too much accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior stack-LSTM parsing: The main methodological novelty is applying stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system with a reasonable scheduling strategy and a small extension (M-SELF). Much of the machinery (stack LSTMs, composition functions, greedy local decision rule) is adapted from Dyer et al. (2015). The work is impactful but incremental.
- Greedy inference and OOD generalization: The joint model overfits to WSJ relative to Hybrid on Brown, indicating the cost of local decisions. There is no exploration of small-beam or globally normalized variants to mitigate search errors, even though this seems directly applicable and could close the OOD gap.
- Oracle/upper-bound limitations not quantified: The paper notes that the semantic oracle is imperfect (multiple crossing arcs) and that 1.5% of English sentences cannot be correctly encoded. However, there is no report of oracle upper bounds for LAS/SRL given the transition constraints, so it is hard to assess headroom and how much error is due to modeling vs. transition limitations.
- Comparisons and significance: While SOTAs using orthogonal advances are listed, statistical significance testing is absent. Some implementation choices (e.g., pretrained embeddings) are known to materially affect results; except for Chinese, the paper does not quantify their contributions or variance across runs.
- Limited analysis: Error decomposition is minimal (e.g., predicate sense errors vs. argument attachment, role confusions, long-distance or crossing arcs). Multilingual gaps (e.g., lack of morphology) are acknowledged but not probed; character-level or morphological features could be straightforward extensions to test.

Questions to authors
- What is the oracle upper bound (LAS and SRL F1) imposed by your transition system and constraints on the CoNLL 2008/2009 datasets? How often do crossing-arc cases or other constraints prevent exact gold reproduction, and how much accuracy is potentially lost?
- Did you try small-beam decoding or globally normalized training? If so, does it improve Brown performance without hurting speed too much?
- How sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings? For English, what is the delta when removing pretrained vectors? What is the run-to-run variance with different random seeds?
- For CoNLL 2008, could joint training incorporate predicate identification rather than using a separate BiLSTM classifier? If tried, how did it compare?
- How are hyperparameters tuned across languages, and were they re-used or re-tuned per language? Any overfitting risk on dev sets?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed empirical paper with a clear, practical contribution: a fast, feature-free joint parser that advances the state of joint syntax–semantics without complex inference. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the results, speed, and thorough ablations justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with suggestions to quantify oracle bounds, explore lightweight global search, and expand analysis of error types and embedding sensitivity.
