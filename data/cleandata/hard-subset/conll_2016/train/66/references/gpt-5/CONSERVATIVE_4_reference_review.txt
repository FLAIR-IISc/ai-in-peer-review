Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. It removes hand-crafted features and beam search, relying on learned representations of stacks, buffer, and action history.
- It augments the Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) joint transition system with a new M-SELF transition to handle nominal self-arguments and an interleaving strategy to synchronize syntactic and semantic actions; predicate sense disambiguation is integrated as an action.
- Empirically, it reports improvements over prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and faster runtime than a strong pipeline SRL system.

Strengths
- Solid empirical coverage and useful ablations. The paper evaluates on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English and multilingual) with four settings (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, Joint). This allows isolating the contribution of joint modeling and shows expected trends (Semantics-only < Hybrid/Joint; some overfitting of Joint OOD). This is methodologically sound and strengthens the claims.
- Competitive performance vs prior joint models with a simpler, faster architecture. The method consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and variants, while providing a clear runtime advantage (single-core, end-to-end). The speed/accuracy trade-off is a practical strength for pipeline replacements.
- Removal of hand-crafted features without catastrophic loss vs pipelines. While still behind the very best feature-engineered or exact-inference systems, the gap is modest given the simplicity. This is encouraging for applications where feature extraction or heavy decoding is prohibitive.
- Clear, implementable modeling framework. The stack LSTM state encoding, composition functions for syntactic/semantic arcs and predicates, and transition constraints are described sufficiently to reproduce the approach in principle. The new M-SELF transition is a pragmatic addition for NomBank phenomena.
- Multilingual evaluation. Results beyond English demonstrate some portability; the paper also candidly reports where pretrained embeddings are important (Chinese), which is useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established baselines. The transition system is largely inherited from Henderson et al. (2013); stack LSTMs for parsing are from Dyer et al. (2015). The main novelty is applying full-state stack LSTM encoding to joint parsing and adding M-SELF. This feels incremental; there is no new learning objective, decoding algorithm, or stronger global modeling beyond greediness.
- Oracle mismatch and incomplete supervision not adequately analyzed. The paper acknowledges that the oracle conversion is imperfect for semantic graphs (and 1.5% of English 2009 sentences cannot be correctly encoded). It is unclear how these cases are handled during training/testing, what proportion per language is affected, and what the accuracy impact is. This threatens the validity of the reported improvements if the training signal is systematically noisy.
- Greedy inference without alternatives or robustness checks. The method is fully greedy; no beam search, dynamic oracle training, or global objectives are explored. Given well-known error propagation in transition-based SRL and joint parsing, the paper should at least quantify (i) the gap to a small beam, (ii) ensemble or multi-run variance, or (iii) training with exploration. Without this, it is hard to endorse the design as sufficiently validated.
- Comparability and statistical significance. The paper compiles numbers from heterogeneous systems with different resources (e.g., morphological features, extra annotations) and acknowledges gaps but does not provide controlled comparisons (e.g., pipeline vs joint under identical embeddings/features) beyond the “Hybrid” variant. No significance testing or confidence intervals are reported for key differences, while runtime variance is reported. This weakens claims of superiority over prior joint models.
- Sparse analysis. There is little qualitative or quantitative error analysis (by arc type, crossing arcs, predicate sense OOVs, domain shift). The interleaving design choice (resolving left-side syntax before left-side semantics) is not ablated; its effect on accuracy is unknown. The “default to 01” strategy for unseen predicates is asserted rather than evaluated against simple back-offs. These omissions limit understanding of when/why the model works or fails.
- Reproducibility and tuning. Code is promised but not provided at review time; many hyperparameters are “set intuitively,” and careful tuning is deferred. Given the sensitivity of neural parsers, this raises concerns about reproducibility and fairness of comparisons. Multilingual gaps (e.g., lack of morphology) are known but unaddressed.

Questions to authors
- Oracle conversion: Precisely how are sentences/parses handled when the gold joint parse cannot be encoded by the transition system? Are they dropped, approximated, or altered? Please report rates per language and any measured impact on accuracy.
- Greedy vs beam: What is the accuracy and runtime impact of a small beam (e.g., 4–8)? Did you try dynamic oracle training or global objectives? Any variance across multiple random seeds?
- Interleaving scheme: Why enforce resolving left-side syntactic dependencies before left-side semantics? Did you try alternative schedules, and what effect do they have?
- Significance: Are improvements over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) statistically significant on CoNLL 2008/2009 (per-sentence bootstrap or stratified tests)? Please report p-values or CIs.
- Pretrained embeddings: Provide ablations without pretrained embeddings for English and other languages, and quantify their contribution. For predicate sense OOVs, did you compare the “01 default” to type-level frequency back-off or lexeme clustering?
- Error analysis: Can you provide breakdowns by (i) crossing vs non-crossing semantic arcs, (ii) nominal vs verbal predicates, (iii) frequent vs rare roles, and (iv) in-/out-of-domain for SRL?

Overall, the paper is well-executed and practically valuable, but the methodological contribution is incremental and several validation gaps remain. I am cautiously negative without stronger evidence on oracle handling, robustness of the greedy design, and significance of the claimed gains.
