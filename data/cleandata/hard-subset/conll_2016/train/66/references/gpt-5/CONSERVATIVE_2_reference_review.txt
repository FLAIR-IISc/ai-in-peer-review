Summary and contributions
- A transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state and performs fully greedy inference. The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2013) with an additional M-SELF transition for self-dependencies and an interleaving protocol for syntax/semantics.
- Empirical evaluation on CoNLL-2008/2009 English and multilingual data: improvements over prior joint models (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.), speed advantages, and performance competitive with (but generally below) top pipeline systems that leverage expert-crafted features and/or exact decoding.
- Controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) to probe the utility of joint training and syntax for SRL.

Strengths
- Clear, implementable transition system with a principled encoder: Using stack LSTMs to summarize S/M/B/A provides a clean formulation that avoids feature engineering. The interleaved syntax/semantics design is well specified, and the added M-SELF transition addresses a real annotation artifact in CoNLL-2009.
- Solid empirical coverage: Results on CoNLL-2008, CoNLL-2009 (in-domain and out-of-domain), and several non-English languages provide reasonable evidence that the approach is broadly applicable. The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants helps contextualize the benefits and trade-offs of joint modeling.
- Efficiency: The system is fast and simple (greedy, single-pass), and the reported wall-clock comparison to a strong SRL pipeline highlights a practical advantage that is often overlooked in joint parsing work.
- End-to-end modeling of predicate sense disambiguation within the same framework (M-PRED), with a fallback behavior for unseen predicates, yields a self-contained pipeline for 2008 (predicate detection via BiLSTM + joint parser) and 2009 (joint parser only).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The core contributions are largely an integration of existing ideas—stack LSTM encoders (Dyer et al., 2015) with the Henderson et al. transition system—plus a minor transition (M-SELF). The paper does not introduce new learning or inference principles beyond applying the stack LSTM to the joint setting. Given that the strongest baselines are older joint systems, it is unclear how this compares to recent neural SRL pipelines with full syntactic features and/or exact decoding when controlled for resources.
- Greedy inference without analysis or stronger baselines: The choice to go fully greedy is a key claim, yet there is no comparison to beam search or dynamic-oracle training, and no discussion of exposure bias. Given known benefits of non-greedy search in transition-based parsing, stronger evidence (ablation: greedy vs. small beam; static vs. dynamic oracle) is needed to substantiate the claim that greedy suffices here.
- Oracle conversion limitations and search space coverage: The semantic oracle is imperfect (multiple crossing arcs), making some gold graphs unreachable (1.5% of English 2009 sentences). The paper does not quantify how many gold arcs are lost or how this impacts training/test performance and error profiles. This undermines the rigor of the training signal and comparability with systems whose search spaces cover the gold.
- Comparisons and statistical rigor: The paper uses “significantly” without statistical tests. Some comparisons (e.g., speed vs. Lei et al., 2015) might be affected by implementation and resource differences; more controlled experiments are needed. The hybrid model does not expose full-tree syntactic features to the SRL component, making it a weak proxy for a true pipeline baseline; thus conclusions about joint vs. pipeline are limited.
- Generalization and overfitting: The joint model loses to the hybrid out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting overfitting to WSJ. This is precisely where joint models are often claimed to help. The paper lacks analysis (e.g., by arc length/label, OOV rate, predicate type) to diagnose why joint training hurts OOD and whether the stack-LSTM state actually captures long-range or cross-structure dependencies well.
- Reproducibility and reporting: Many hyperparameters are “set intuitively,” early stopping criteria are vaguely described (“6–7 hours”), and important training details (dropout placement, initialization, gradient clipping, random seeds) are missing. The code is promised on publication, which limits review-time verification.

Questions to Authors
- Please quantify the oracle conversion gap: What fraction of arcs (not just sentences) are unreachable in training for English and other languages? How does performance change if those instances/arcs are excluded or approximated?
- Can you report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap) for key improvements over prior joint models and for Joint vs. Hybrid and Semantics-only?
- How much do pretrained embeddings contribute? Provide ablations without pretraining (beyond the anecdote for Chinese), and with/without lemmas. Also report sensitivity to embedding dimensionality.
- What is the impact of using a small beam or dynamic-oracle training? If not implemented, please justify why not, given the known benefits in transition-based parsing.
- For CoNLL-2008, how much does predicate identification accuracy limit end-to-end SRL? Is there an error breakdown showing where the joint model helps/hurts relative to the hybrid?
- Please clarify training details: exact early stopping criterion, dropout application points, gradient clipping, initialization, and number of epochs. Also provide random seed stability (mean/std over multiple runs).
- For the speed comparison with Lei et al. (2015): were both systems run on identical hardware/software stacks? Does your time include POS/lemma preprocessing and predicate detection (for 2008)?

Overall, the work is carefully engineered, efficient, and shows respectable accuracy, but the methodological novelty is modest and the empirical validation lacks stronger controls (beam/dynamic oracle, oracle coverage analysis, statistical testing, and a truly competitive neural pipeline baseline). Strengthening these aspects would substantially improve the paper’s rigor and impact.
