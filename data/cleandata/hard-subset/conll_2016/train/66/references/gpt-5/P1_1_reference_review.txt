Summary and main contributions
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) instead of relying on expert-crafted features or beam search. The model interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions (adapted from arc-eager and Henderson et al.), adds a new M-SELF transition to handle CoNLL-2009 self-dependencies, and composes syntactic and semantic structures via recursive neural composition functions. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 show the method surpasses prior joint models and is competitive with top pipeline systems while being substantially faster.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that learning a continuous representation of the entire joint parsing state with stack LSTMs enables fully greedy joint syntax–semantics parsing to outperform previous joint models and approach pipeline performance, with strong speed benefits.
2) A practical joint transition system (including an M-SELF transition) and interleaving schedule that permits efficient construction of both syntactic trees and semantic graphs, along with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) clarifying the role of joint learning.
3) An empirical study across English and multiple languages on CoNLL-2008/2009 with runtime analysis, showing good accuracy-speed tradeoffs without hand-engineered features.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over prior joint models: On CoNLL-2008 and 2009 (EN), the joint model improves upon Henderson et al. and follow-ups, validating the core claim that whole-state representation learning beats localized, feature-based joint parsers. The ablations (Semantics-only < Hybrid < Joint in-domain) are informative and consistent with the literature that syntax helps SRL.
- Strong speed–accuracy tradeoff: Fully greedy decoding with stack LSTMs yields near state-of-the-art semantic performance while being ~2.5x faster than a strong pipeline baseline (Lei et al., under identical settings). This is compelling for real-time applications and large-scale processing.
- Sensible, well-motivated architecture: The use of four stack LSTMs (S, M, B, A) with recursive composition for syntactic and semantic attachments is elegant, removes the need for global feature engineering (e.g., dependency paths), and tightly couples structure building and representation learning.
- Practicality and completeness: The system handles predicate identification (CoNLL-2008) with a compact BiLSTM classifier, predicate sense disambiguation within the transition system, nominal self-arguments via M-SELF, and non-projective syntax via pseudo-projectivization plus semantic M-SWAP—covering many edge cases needed in practice.
- Multilingual evaluation: Results on multiple CoNLL-2009 languages indicate the approach adapts beyond English and is competitive with top systems, especially when pretrained embeddings are available.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to Dyer et al. (2015): The main technical novelty is applying stack LSTMs to joint syntax–semantics with a few transition additions (e.g., M-SELF) and an interleaving protocol. While the system-level result is valuable, the methodological advances over prior stack-LSTM parsing are modest.
- Greedy training/decoding without exploration: The model uses a static oracle with greedy decoding and local log-loss; no dynamic oracle, beam, or global objective. Exposure bias and search errors likely limit semantic F1 (e.g., Brown set, where Hybrid > Joint suggests overfitting). An analysis or experiment with beam or dynamic oracles would strengthen the claims.
- Oracle/transition coverage issues: The paper acknowledges that 1.5% of sentences (EN-2009) cannot be encoded by the oracle transition sequence due to crossing arcs, and that semantic conversion is imperfect. The impact on training/test accuracy is not quantified; this raises concerns about upper bounds and fairness across systems.
- Comparability and significance: Several comparisons are to systems with different feature sets and resource usage (e.g., pretrained embeddings not uniformly used in older joint models; some pipelines use global reranking and extra annotations). Statistical significance tests are not reported; some gains over strong recent baselines may be within variance.
- Limited analysis/ablation depth: While core ablations are present, finer-grained analyses (e.g., removing action-history A, removing buffer LSTM, no composition functions, effect of pretrained embeddings per language, error breakdown by label/predicate type/nominals) are missing. This limits insight into what components drive semantic gains.

Questions to authors
- Can you report statistical significance for the main improvements over Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and for Joint vs Hybrid/Semantics-only on WSJ and Brown?
- What fraction of arcs/tokens are affected by sentences whose gold graphs cannot be encoded by the oracle? Have you measured an empirical upper bound imposed by oracle limitations?
- How sensitive is performance to beam size? Even a small beam (e.g., 4–8) could help semantics without large runtime cost. Any preliminary results?
- Did you try dynamic oracles or scheduled sampling to mitigate exposure bias? If yes, what was the impact; if not, can you comment on feasibility for this transition set?
- For multilingual experiments, did all languages use comparable pretrained embeddings and tokenization? Can you report results without pretrained embeddings (beyond Chinese) to quantify their contribution?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the theoretical runtime with M-SWAP. While average performance is linear, worst-case complexity can exceed linear when swaps are frequent; a brief discussion would help.
- The defaulting to “01” for unseen predicate senses is pragmatic; a small study on its errors or alternatives (e.g., lemma+POS backoff classifiers) would be useful.
- Training details: consider reporting gradient clipping, number of epochs, and patience for early stopping to enhance reproducibility. Also clarify whether action embeddings are shared between syntactic/semantic actions.
- Some figures/tables are referenced (Table 2–4, Fig. 5 in supplement); ensure they are clearly legible and include error bars where appropriate.
- Open-source code availability is a plus; please include scripts for data conversion (pseudo-projectivization, oracle generation) since these steps are critical.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-executed application of stack LSTMs to joint syntax–semantics with strong empirical results and a compelling efficiency story. The conceptual novelty is moderate, but the paper advances the state of joint parsing and provides a practical alternative to feature-rich pipelines. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying comparability/significance and expanding analysis around oracle limitations and training/decoding choices.
