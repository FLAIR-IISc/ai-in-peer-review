Summary and contributions
This paper presents a transition-based, fully neural joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) and performs greedy decoding without beam search. The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with interleaving syntax/semantics actions, plus a new M-SELF transition for semantic self-dependencies and a copy-based variant of arc-eager to keep items available to both modules. The model is trained with standard log-loss over oracle action sequences and leverages pretrained “structured skip-gram” embeddings. Results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 show the joint model outperforms prior joint parsers and is competitive with top pipeline systems, with notable speed gains.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A fast, fully neural joint syntax–semantics parser that summarizes the entire algorithmic state via stack LSTMs and achieves new SOTA among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, with competitive multilingual results.
2) An empirical analysis demonstrating the trade-off between joint and pipelined training using controlled variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid), showing mutual benefits in-domain and overfitting behavior out-of-domain.
3) Practical design and engineering of a synchronized transition system for joint parsing with greedy inference, including the M-SELF transition and copy-based arc-eager adaptation.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results for joint modeling with substantial speed advantages: The joint model surpasses prior joint parsers (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive with top shared-task systems while running ~2.5× faster than a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under identical hardware. This convincingly demonstrates the practicality of the approach.

2) Clear, well-motivated modeling: Using stack LSTMs to summarize full parser state removes the need for hand-crafted, globally scoped features that pipelines typically rely on. The interleaving constraints and “copy” tweak to arc-eager are carefully reasoned to keep both syntactic and semantic operations coherent over a shared buffer.

3) Useful ablations and variants: The Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid settings provide informative evidence about the importance of syntactic supervision for SRL, the degree of coupling between tasks, and domain sensitivity. The finding that Joint > Hybrid in-domain but not OOD is a nuanced and valuable insight.

4) Broad evaluation and multilingual applicability: Experiments span CoNLL 2008 and 2009, English and multiple non-English languages. The paper clearly notes where pretrained embeddings are especially beneficial (e.g., Chinese), demonstrating portability and practicality.

5) Implementation detail and training protocol transparency: The paper lists hyperparameters, pretrained embedding setup, optimization details, and constraints, which facilitates reproduction once code is available.

Weaknesses
1) Limited conceptual novelty beyond combining known components: The transition system follows Henderson et al.; representation learning via stack LSTMs follows Dyer et al. The main novelty is their integration for joint parsing, plus minor extensions (M-SELF, copy-based arc-eager). While the engineering is solid and results are strong, the methodological advance is incremental.

2) Greedy decoding without exploration or global normalization: The choice of purely greedy inference is consistent with speed goals, but the paper provides no comparison to small-beam decoding, dynamic oracles, or globally normalized training, which might reduce error propagation and improve robustness (particularly OOD). This leaves open whether modest search would significantly improve accuracy at small cost.

3) Oracle and coverage limitations not quantified: The paper notes the oracle for semantic transitions is imperfect due to crossing arcs (and that some CoNLL 2009 sentences cannot be encoded). However, the impact on training (e.g., percentage of sentences/edges affected, performance delta with perfect vs imperfect oracle) is not quantified, making it hard to assess the ceiling and where errors come from.

4) Analysis gaps: While overall scores are reported, the paper lacks finer-grained error analysis (e.g., by arc distance, predicate sense frequency, argument label confusability, crossing vs non-crossing arcs), statistical significance tests, and variance across runs. Given reliance on pretrained embeddings and dropout, reporting multiple seeds and significance would strengthen claims.

5) Reproducibility and fairness of comparisons: Code is promised “on publication,” but not yet available. Some strong baselines (e.g., the exact configurations of top 2009 pipelines) are not replicated in-house; the speed comparison is only against one public pipeline. More thorough, apples-to-apples comparisons (e.g., same hardware, I/O, and pre/post-processing for multiple baselines) would bolster the practical claims.

Questions to authors
1) Oracle coverage: What fraction of sentences and edges in each dataset cannot be represented by your transition sequences (especially for semantics with crossing arcs)? How does excluding/approximating these affect training targets and final performance?

2) Greedy vs beam: Did you try small-beam search (e.g., beam=4 or 8) or dynamic oracles? What is the accuracy/speed trade-off compared to greedy?

3) Sensitivity and variance: How sensitive are results to random seed, dropout, and pretrained embeddings? Please report mean/std over multiple runs and significance vs prior joint parsers.

4) Interleaving policy: Did you explore alternative interleaving strategies (e.g., resolving some semantic actions earlier/later, or joint action spaces)? Any observed impact on error propagation?

5) Predicate sense handling: For unseen predicates defaulting to “01,” what is the token-level frequency and accuracy impact? Do errors concentrate on rare senses, and could a backoff lexicon or character-based lemma help?

6) Multilingual setup: Which languages used morphological features (if any)? Have you tried character-based word embeddings to compensate in morphologically rich languages like Czech?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments with small-beam decoding and/or dynamic oracles; report speed/accuracy trade-offs.
- Quantify oracle limitations and provide targeted error analysis (arc length, label confusion, crossing arcs).
- Report variance across seeds and significance tests; include ablations for each stack LSTM component (e.g., removing action-history LSTM, removing composition functions).
- Explore lightweight morphology or character-level embeddings, especially for highly inflected languages.
- Provide more comprehensive runtime comparisons against multiple strong baselines under matched conditions.

Overall, this is a solid engineering contribution with clear practical value and strong empirical results for joint parsing. While methodological novelty is moderate, the paper advances the state of joint models and is well-executed. I lean toward acceptance if space permits.
