Summary and contributions:
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that represents the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. The system interleaves syntactic (arc-eager) and semantic transitions over shared buffer/stack structures, learns composition functions for partial structures, and predicts actions via a softmax classifier over allowed transitions. It introduces an M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies and evaluates on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual), reporting improvements over prior joint models and competitive runtime.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) A joint transition system that learns continuous representations of the entire joint parser state via stack LSTMs and performs fully greedy inference without expert-crafted features, outperforming previous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.
2) An empirical study with controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) demonstrating the contribution of joint modeling to SRL and showing competitive multilingual performance with strong efficiency.
3) Engineering extensions for joint parsing, including interleaved transition scheduling and an explicit M-SELF operation for NomBank-style self-dependencies, with a practical, fast implementation.

Strengths:
- Technical soundness of the joint transition design and representation learning: The interleaved syntactic/semantic transition system is carefully specified with constraints to avoid invalid states, and the use of stack LSTMs to summarize stacks/buffer/action history is well grounded in prior work. Composition functions for syntactic, semantic, and predicate-sense nodes are clearly defined and differentiable, enabling end-to-end training with backpropagation and dropout.

- Strong empirical improvements over prior joint parsers: On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model consistently surpasses Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and related joint systems, indicating that learning full-state representations reduces the need for hand-designed, globally scoped features traditionally used in SRL.

- Comprehensive experimental variants and analysis: The paper includes syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint configurations, isolating the impact of joint modeling. The reported trend (semantics-only < hybrid < joint in-domain) plausibly supports the claim that syntax and semantics benefit each other when trained jointly, while the out-of-domain behavior highlights overfitting considerations.

- Efficiency and practical relevance: The system achieves linear-time decoding and reports an end-to-end runtime that is substantially faster (≈2.5×) than a strong SRL pipeline under identical hardware settings, making it suitable for real-time or large-scale applications.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The transition set is formalized (including buffer-copy modifications), constraints are explicit, training details and hyperparameters are described, and pretrained embeddings are specified. The promise of open-source code further supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to existing stack-LSTM parsing: The core modeling advance is applying stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) to a known joint transition system with greedy decoding. While effective, the architectural novelty is incremental (state representation and scheduling) rather than introducing new learning or inference paradigms for joint parsing.

- Greedy training and decoding without global objectives or dynamic oracles: The model uses a static oracle with locally normalized decisions and no beam or globally normalized training. This likely contributes to the gap with state-of-the-art SRL systems using global inference/reranking and may partly explain the out-of-domain degradation (Brown) for the joint model. No comparison to beam search or globally normalized training is provided.

- Oracle mismatch for semantic structures and its impact: The paper notes that the oracle conversion is imperfect (e.g., ≈1.5% of English CoNLL 2009 sentences) due to crossing arcs despite M-SWAP. The empirical effect of these mismatches on training and test accuracy is not quantified, leaving uncertainty about ceiling effects and potential biases.

- Limited analysis and ablations: Beyond the syntax/semantics variants, there is little ablation on important components (e.g., effect of the M-SELF action, action-history stack A, pretrained embeddings across languages, label embedding dimensionality). Error analyses by label type, predicate class, or crossing-arc phenomena are not included, making it hard to diagnose where the joint learner helps or fails.

- Statistical rigor and variance reporting: The paper reports single-run scores (with runtime variance) but does not include confidence intervals, significance tests, or multi-seed variance for accuracy metrics. Given close competition with strong baselines and domain shifts, statistical support would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors (optional):
- How sensitive are results to the greedy choice? Did you experiment with small beams or globally normalized training (e.g., CRF or max-margin) and, if so, what was the trade-off between accuracy and speed?
- Can you quantify the impact of oracle mismatches (e.g., by excluding affected sentences or reporting oracle upper bounds) on semantic F1 and macro scores?
- What is the performance variance across random seeds and runs? Are the reported improvements over prior joint models statistically significant?
- How much does the M-SELF transition contribute in practice? An ablation removing M-SELF (or handling self-arcs post hoc) would clarify its necessity.
- In the hybrid model, are semantics trained only with predicted syntax from your syntax-only model, or is there any exposure to gold syntax? How robust is hybrid performance to parser errors (e.g., when varying the syntax-only model’s accuracy)?
- Did you evaluate the effect of dynamic oracles or exploration during training to mitigate exposure bias, especially for out-of-domain data?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and demonstrates solid empirical improvements and efficiency. The main limitation is the incremental novelty over prior stack-LSTM parsing and the lack of global training/decoding or deeper analysis, but the work nonetheless represents a meaningful contribution to joint parsing with neural state representations.
