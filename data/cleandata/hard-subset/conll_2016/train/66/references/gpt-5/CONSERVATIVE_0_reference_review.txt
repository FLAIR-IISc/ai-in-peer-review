Summary and claimed contributions
- A greedy joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire parser state using stack LSTMs, removing the need for hand-crafted features and beam search. Transitions are largely inherited from prior joint parsing work, with a new M-SELF action to handle semantic self-dependencies.
- Empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English) showing improvements over earlier joint models (e.g., Henderson et al. 2008/2013; Gesmundo et al. 2009), competitive multilingual results, and strong speed. Variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) are compared.
- Practical efficiency: a single-core end-to-end runtime that is substantially faster than at least one public pipeline SRL system, with small memory footprint.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over prior joint parsers. The model surpasses Henderson et al.-style joint baselines on CoNLL 2008/2009, suggesting that learned full-state representations provide tangible benefits over localized feature views.
- Simplicity and efficiency. Fully greedy decoding and no hand-crafted features simplify deployment and yield good speed (reported ~178s single-core on CoNLL 2009 English) with low memory usage. This is attractive for practical settings.
- Clear, coherent modeling framework. The paper cleanly integrates stack LSTMs over syntax/semantics/buffer/action history, recursive composition functions for structure building, and a unified decision rule with transition constraints. The proposed M-SELF transition pragmatically addresses NomBank self-arguments.
- Breadth of evaluation. The paper covers CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English), several multilingual datasets, and multiple model variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint), providing a reasonably broad picture of where joint learning helps.
- Transparency about limitations. The authors acknowledge that they do not match feature-engineered pipelines or exact inference models and that the semantic oracle is imperfect for some non-projective cases. This candor is helpful for readers to place the contribution.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to established work. The transition system closely follows Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and the representation mechanism is largely Dyer et al. (2015) applied to a joint setting. Beyond the M-SELF action and the decision to go fully greedy, the technical novelty is modest. A stronger case for novelty would require more analysis of why full-state encoding (vs top-k elements) and interleaving constraints matter.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics. There is no systematic ablation to quantify the contribution of (i) action-history stack vs no history, (ii) full-state stacks vs limited top-of-stack features, (iii) pretraining vs random init, (iv) the copy vs move change in S-RIGHT/SHIFT, (v) the interleaving strategy, (vi) M-SELF, and (vii) dropout rates/hidden sizes. Without these, it is hard to ascribe the observed gains to specific design choices rather than general neural capacity or embeddings.
- Incomplete baseline coverage for pipelines and inference. The “Hybrid” baseline uses the authors’ own syntax-only parser at training time and exposes only partial structures at test time, which is not representative of strong pipeline SRL systems that leverage full parses and global features. A more rigorous comparison should include a pipeline with a competitive off-the-shelf syntactic parser and state-of-the-art SRL models, ideally under matched pretraining and resources. Similarly, the claim that greedy inference suffices would be stronger with a beam search or dynamic-oracle comparison.
- Oracle/upper-bound and error analysis are underdeveloped. The semantic oracle is acknowledged to be imperfect (and 1.5% of sentences cannot be encoded in English 2009), but the paper does not report oracle upper bounds or analyze the impact on final scores. A breakdown of lost arcs due to transition limitations, non-projectivity, and self-arguments would clarify headroom. Error taxonomy (syntax vs semantics, in/out of domain, long-distance, crossing arcs) is also missing.
- Statistical rigor and fairness. The improvements over older joint baselines are not accompanied by significance testing or multi-run variance. Hyperparameters are “set intuitively,” and the Syntax-only comparison to Dyer et al. (2015) is not clearly controlled (e.g., identical embeddings, data preprocessing, pseudo-projectivization). Pretrained embeddings trained on Gigaword may give an advantage; the extent of their contribution is not quantified.
- Generalization and data dependence. The model appears data-hungry (notably in Japanese), and multilingual gains rely on pretrained word embeddings. There is no learning-curve analysis, no exploration of character/morphological features (important for morphologically rich languages), and limited discussion of robustness out of domain beyond the Brown split.

Questions to authors
- Ablations: Please provide ablations isolating the effects of (a) action history, (b) full-state stack summaries vs top-k feature templates, (c) pretrained embeddings, (d) copy vs move in syntactic transitions, (e) interleaving constraints, and (f) M-SELF.
- Oracle/upper bound: What is the exact oracle upper bound for semantics given your transition set on CoNLL 2008/2009 (per language)? How many arcs/sentences are unencodable, and what is the measured loss ceiling?
- Greedy vs beam: How does a small beam (e.g., 4–16) affect accuracy/speed? Have you tried dynamic oracles to mitigate exposure bias?
- Pipeline baseline: Can you include a strong pipeline comparison using a competitive syntactic parser (and, if feasible, a modern SRL model) under matched embeddings and resources? For the Hybrid setup, what happens if you expose the full predicted parse to the semantic component?
- Significance and stability: Please report mean±std over multiple random seeds and significance tests for the main comparisons. Also report the contribution of pretrained embeddings via a controlled ablation.
- Predicate identification (CoNLL 2008): How sensitive is end-to-end performance to predicate identification errors? Provide results with gold predicates to separate components.
- Multilingual: Any results with character/morphological features? If not, can you provide learning curves to show data efficiency and potential gains with more data?

Overall, the paper presents a clean, efficient application of stack LSTMs to joint dependency parsing with solid improvements over older joint models. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and the empirical validation lacks key ablations, stronger pipeline baselines, oracle analyses, and significance testing. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the case.
