Summary and claimed contributions:
The paper presents a greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the full parser state with stack LSTMs, removing the need for expert-crafted features and beam search. The model interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions (largely following Henderson et al., 2013) with a few modifications (copy vs. move on buffer items, a new M-SELF action for NomBank self-arcs), and trains a classifier over learned representations of the entire parser state. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English) and CoNLL 2009 multilingual show substantial gains over prior joint models and competitive results with fast runtime.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A simple, fast, greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that learns vector representations of the entire joint parser state via stack LSTMs and dispenses with manual features and beam search, yielding strong accuracy vs. prior joint models and competitive speed.
2) An empirical study isolating the value of jointness and representation learning via Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint variants across English and multiple languages.
3) Practical engineering additions for joint SRL (e.g., M-SELF, copying instead of moving in arc-eager) that enable a clean interleaving of syntax and semantics and efficient decoding.

Strengths:
- Methodological clarity and simplicity: The paper clearly defines an interleaved transition system for joint parsing and a straightforward stack-LSTM architecture that summarizes S, M, B, and action history. The design choice to encode the entire algorithmic state rather than top-of-stack features is principled and easy to follow.
- Strong improvements over prior joint models: On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the Joint model outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009), demonstrating the advantage of full-state representation learning. The ablations (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) support the claim that syntactic signals help SRL and that joint training can help in-domain semantics.
- Speed and practicality: The greedy decoder with stack LSTMs runs in linear time and is reported to be about 2.5x faster than a competitive SRL pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under the authors’ setup. Low memory footprint and single-core training/inference make the approach appealing for practical use.
- Multilingual evaluation: Results across several CoNLL 2009 languages are generally on par with top systems, suggesting the technique is adaptable beyond English. The paper is explicit about reliance on pretrained embeddings and the effect on Chinese.
- Transparent setup: The paper provides hyperparameters, training protocols, and discusses preprocessing (pseudo-projectivization) and predicate handling. The intent to release code is valuable.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty over prior stack-LSTM parsing: Much of the technical machinery (stack LSTMs, action-history conditioning) is directly inherited from Dyer et al. (2015) for syntactic parsing. The primary novel elements for joint parsing (interleaving, copy vs. move, M-SELF) are modest engineering extensions rather than conceptual advances. A clearer differentiation and deeper analysis of what specifically enables the gains would strengthen the contribution.
- Greedy inference without analysis of alternatives: The paper emphasizes greedy decoding, but provides no comparison to beam search or globally trained/decoded variants within this framework. It remains unclear whether a small beam would close the out-of-domain gap (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) or mitigate overfitting.
- Oracle and transition system limitations: The authors acknowledge that the semantic oracle is imperfect (e.g., 1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded) and rely on pseudo-projectivization for syntax. The paper does not quantify the upper bound this imposes, does not examine error propagation due to imperfect oracles, and provides no ablation measuring the impact of M-SELF or copying vs. moving on correctness/completeness.
- Missing ablations and error analysis: Key design choices are not dissected. For example: the contribution of pretrained word embeddings; sensitivity to action-history LSTM; effect of label-embedding dimensions; joint vs. separate parameterization for syntactic and semantic composition; and the impact of defaulting unseen predicates to “.01”. Error breakdowns (labeling vs. attachment, predicate sense errors) are not reported.
- Evaluation and statistical rigor: No significance testing or variance across runs is reported, despite stochastic training and early stopping. The runtime comparison cites “identical settings” but lacks hardware specifics and fails to clarify whether preprocessing, POS/lemma prediction, and I/O are included for both systems. Some comparisons are to pipelines that use richer features (e.g., morphology), while the joint model uses pretrained embeddings—fairness/equivalence should be discussed more carefully.
- Scope of “jointness” in CoNLL 2008: Predicate identification is handled by a separate biLSTM classifier. The integration between predicate identification and the joint parser is minimal; how this affects overall joint modeling claims is not explored.

Questions to the authors:
- Can you report significance tests and mean±std over multiple random seeds for the main English results?
- What is the accuracy upper bound imposed by imperfect semantic oracle conversions and pseudo-projectivization? How often do gold structures become unreachable, and what is the measured impact on dev/test?
- How much do pretrained embeddings contribute? Please provide results without pretraining, and with character-level embeddings as a morphology proxy.
- How crucial is M-SELF? Please provide an ablation and frequency statistics across languages.
- Why restrict to greedy decoding? Could a small beam (e.g., 4–8) improve out-of-domain performance without large runtime costs?
- How is deprojectivization handled post-parsing and how much LAS degradation does it induce? Please quantify.
- The S-SHIFT and S-RIGHT copy-from-buffer modification departs from standard arc-eager. Can you discuss completeness and any invariants violated or maintained by this choice?

Additional comments and suggestions:
- Provide a brief error analysis for SRL (argument identification vs. labeling vs. sense disambiguation) and for syntax (long-distance attachments).
- Consider simple regularizers or domain-robust training (e.g., dropout sweeps, word dropout) to mitigate the Joint model’s out-of-domain drop.
- Explore limited parameter sharing between syntactic and semantic composition functions, or multi-task learning with auxiliary syntax/semantics signals.
- Clarify hardware and timing protocol for fair speed comparisons; include end-to-end timings for all baselines you can run.

Overall assessment:
A solid, well-executed engineering paper with clear gains over prior joint models, strong practicality, and convincing ablations demonstrating the value of learned state representations. Novelty is incremental relative to prior stack-LSTM parsing work, and the experimental analysis could be deeper. I lean toward acceptance if space allows, contingent on strengthening statistical rigor and adding key ablations and oracle/beam analyses.
