- Presents a fast, greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs and recursive composition, extending Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system.
- Key extensions: learned continuous representations of full parser state, purely greedy decoding (no beam), and small transition-system tweaks (buffer-copying, M-SELF).
- Claims: state-of-the-art among prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and strong runtime.
- Contribution: effective joint parser learning whole-state representations with stack LSTMs and greedy inference, outperforming prior joint models and running very fast.
- Contribution: concrete interleaved transition system with practical modifications (arc-eager buffer-copying, M-SELF) and a simple sense-handling strategy.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that learned state representations can replace expensive path-based features, supported by controlled syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid comparisons plus multilingual tests.
- Strength: consistent empirical gains over prior joint models and compelling end-to-end runtime for practical use.
- Strength: clean, unified modeling of the algorithmic state via four stack LSTMs and recursive composition, generalizing Dyer et al.’s approach to joint parsing.
- Strength: sensible, well-motivated transition design (interleaving, buffer-copying, M-SWAP, M-SELF) that improves correctness and coverage.
- Strength: controlled baselines and analysis (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid; WSJ vs Brown) that clarify joint modeling benefits and domain effects.
- Strength: reproducibility emphasis with public embeddings, clear hyperparameters, and promise of open-sourcing code.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—mainly combining existing synchronized transitions with stack LSTMs and greedy decoding; tweaks are incremental.
- Weakness: greedy static-oracle training/decoding risks exposure bias and error propagation; no dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, global loss, or beam-search ablations.
- Weakness: imperfect semantic oracle/encoding—some gold graphs (≈1.5% English 2009) cannot be represented, but impact on training and accuracy is not quantified.
- Weakness: some baseline comparisons may be unfair (hybrid baseline doesn’t expose full syntactic trees to semantics; pipelined competitors use richer/global methods); neural pipeline baselines are missing.
- Weakness: limited deeper analysis and ablations (long-distance errors, crossing arcs, role of action-history LSTM, buffer LSTM, pretrained embeddings, morphology effects).
- Question: for unencodable gold joint parses, how are training targets constructed, what fraction of semantic arcs are lost, and how does this affect dev performance?
- Question: were dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, or beam-search variants tried, and would they help semantics and OOD performance?
- Question: what are ablation impacts of removing action-history LSTM, buffer LSTM, pretrained embeddings, and how much does M-SELF help on NomBank-heavy data?
- Question: do interleaving constraints restrict reachable joint states or force/delay semantic attachments in ways that harm accuracy?
- Question: does the reported runtime include predicate identification and sense disambiguation, and are preprocessing/hardware conditions identical across compared systems?
- Additional comment: adding character-level or morphological features could improve non-English performance with little runtime cost.
- Additional comment: a small-beam or globally normalized variant could improve the accuracy–speed trade-off and reduce overfitting to WSJ.
