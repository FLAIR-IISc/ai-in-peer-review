- Presents a greedy joint syntactic+semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to encode full transition state, replacing hand-engineered features and introducing an M-SELF transition.  
- Methodological contribution: a fully greedy, feature-free joint parser with four stack LSTMs and recursive composition for syntax and semantics.  
- Empirical contribution: strong improvements over prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results, with informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).  
- Engineering contribution: simple, fast, low-memory system with high throughput and promise of open-source release.  
- Methodological strength: well-motivated move from local feature templates to learned state representations mapped to neural composition functions.  
- Empirical strength: thorough evaluation including in-domain and out-of-domain (Brown) tests and ablations probing syntax–semantics interplay.  
- Practical strength: runs on single core with ~512MB, substantially faster than a strong pipeline baseline while maintaining accuracy.  
- Transition design strength: sound and complete adaptation of arc-eager with copying, interleaving constraints, and additions (M-SWAP, M-SELF) for crossings/self-arguments.  
- Reproducibility strength: reasonable training details and intent to release code.  
- Novelty concern: incremental over Dyer et al. (2015); primarily an application of stack LSTMs to an existing joint transition backbone.  
- Inference concern: greedy local training and imperfect oracle can induce suboptimality; lacks beam/global-learning comparisons or quantification of loss.  
- Analysis gap: limited probing of which parts of the learned state (action history, buffer, stacks) or composition functions drive gains.  
- Comparison/fairness caveat: speed and accuracy comparisons may be confounded by differing task setups, features, and resources; no statistical significance tests.  
- Generalization/error-analysis gap: light analysis of OOD drop and failure modes (predicate/argument types, long-distance/crossing arcs, non-projectivity).  
- Question: how much accuracy gap is due to greediness vs. lack of global SRL features; any beam/global experiments and speed/accuracy trade-offs?  
- Question: what fraction of semantic arcs cannot be perfectly encoded by the oracle and the impact of excluding them during training?  
- Question: provide ablations removing individual stack LSTMs or composition functions to quantify their contributions.  
- Question: sensitivity to the fixed interleaving schedule vs. alternative or learned switching?  
- Question: in multilingual experiments, were morphological or character-based embeddings used or only word/POS embeddings?  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed application of learned state representations yielding strong empirical and practical results, but novelty is moderate and further analysis/inference experiments would strengthen the work.
