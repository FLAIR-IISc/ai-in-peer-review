Summary
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). The parser interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions, introduces a new M-SELF action to handle semantic self-dependencies, and eschews hand-crafted features and beam search. On CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English), the approach outperforms prior joint models and achieves results comparable to top pipelined systems, with strong runtime performance. Multilingual results are broadly competitive.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical: A fast, simple joint parser that surpasses prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and attains competitive multilingual performance without feature engineering, while providing solid ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).
2) Methodological: A clean adaptation of stack LSTMs to joint parsing with an interleaved transition system over a shared buffer, including the M-SELF transition for NomBank-style self-arguments.
3) Practical: Competitive end-to-end speed with an open-source implementation and a compact set of hyperparameters/hints that make reproduction feasible.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over prior joint models: The approach consistently improves over Henderson et al.-style joint models (2008/2013) and similar variants, showing the value of learning a representation of the entire parser state rather than relying on local features. The ablations further clarify when joint learning helps (WSJ) and when it may overfit (Brown).
- Simplicity and speed: Purely greedy decoding with learned state representations yields very competitive accuracy at substantially lower runtime (≈2.5× faster than a strong SRL pipeline), making the system attractive for practical use cases.
- Careful engineering of the transition system: The interleaving regime, shared buffer, and copy-vs-move choices are well justified to ensure both syntactic and semantic operations have access to needed items. The addition of M-SELF is a pragmatic fix to a real corpus phenomenon (NomBank self-arguments).
- Useful analysis and baselines: The semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid conditions provide a clear picture of where syntax helps SRL and vice versa, and demonstrate that the joint model’s gains are not simply from better syntax alone.
- Multilingual evidence: Results across multiple CoNLL 2009 languages suggest the approach transfers with minimal language-specific engineering; the role of pretrained embeddings is noted.

Weaknesses
- Limited conceptual novelty relative to prior work: The transition system largely follows Henderson et al., and the modeling approach closely follows Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM parser, extended to semantics. The primary novelty is an integration/application rather than a fundamentally new modeling or inference idea (apart from M-SELF and interleaving details).
- Greedy training/decoding only, no exploration or global normalization: The model uses gold transition supervision and greedy inference without beam search, dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling, or globally normalized training. This likely contributes to overfitting on WSJ and may cap accuracy compared to globally trained/decoded systems.
- Oracle and supervision mismatch for semantics: The authors acknowledge that semantic oracle conversion cannot perfectly encode some gold graphs (e.g., multiple crossing arcs), but the impact is only briefly mentioned (1.5% of English sentences) and not quantified across languages or tied to error patterns. This injects label noise that could bias comparisons and training.
- Evaluation gaps: 
  • No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals for key comparisons.
  • No matched pipeline baseline built from the same stack-LSTM syntactic parser feeding the semantic model, which would isolate the benefit of “jointness” from the benefits of representation learning and faster decoding.
  • Limited error analysis: little insight into which semantic roles or syntactic configurations benefit most or fail (e.g., long-distance, crossing arcs, OOD phenomena).
- Scope limitations: 
  • For CoNLL 2008, predicate identification is handled by a separate biLSTM classifier, not integrated into the joint model, making the “joint” claim partial for that setup.
  • Reliance on predicted POS/lemmas is standard for the task but no analysis of robustness to tagging/lemmatization errors or to missing morphological features (notably relevant for highly inflected languages).
  • Performance in low-resource settings (e.g., Japanese) degrades, suggesting data-hungry training; mitigation strategies are not explored.

Questions to Authors
- Interleaving policy: How sensitive is performance to the specific interleaving rule (syntax-only until S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT triggers a semantic phase until M-SHIFT)? Did you try alternative schedules (e.g., always allowing a subset of semantic actions, or different handoff points)?
- Semantic oracle imperfections: Can you quantify the exact proportion of gold graphs that cannot be encoded per language and the resulting training loss inflation? Do the problematic graphs correlate with specific error types (e.g., long-distance or heavily crossing structures)?
- Training regime: Did you try dynamic oracles, exploration strategies, or beam search during training/decoding? If so, how did they affect WSJ vs. Brown performance?
- Matched pipeline baseline: Can you provide results for a pipeline built from your syntax-only model feeding a semantics-only model (with the same embeddings/features) to disentangle benefits of jointness vs. representation learning?
- Predicate sense disambiguation: For unseen predicates, defaulting to .01 works well in English. How does this strategy fare in other languages, and how sensitive are results to the quality of lemmas and sense frequency distributions?
- Multilingual morphology: Did you attempt character-level or subword embeddings as a proxy for missing morphological features? Any preliminary results?
- Significance and variance: Can you report variance over multiple seeds and test for significance of the main improvements?

Additional comments and suggestions
- A modest beam, global normalization, or exploration (e.g., dynamic oracles) may reduce overfitting to WSJ and help OOD robustness without sacrificing much speed.
- Error analyses (e.g., by role label, dependency distance, crossing arcs, predicate POS) would strengthen the paper and guide future extensions (e.g., better handling of long-distance arguments).
- Given the acknowledged importance of pretrained embeddings, include sensitivity studies (with/without pretraining, different corpora) and per-language runtimes to complete the practical story.
- Integrating predicate identification into the joint model for CoNLL 2008—even as an optional action—would make the “joint” claim more complete.
