Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents a fully greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithmic state via stack LSTMs. It dispenses with expert-crafted features and beam search.
- It introduces a new semantic transition (M-SELF) to handle semantic self-dependencies in CoNLL 2009 and a careful interleaving/copy mechanism that lets syntactic and semantic transitions operate over a shared buffer without losing access.
- Empirically, it outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive in multilingual settings, while being substantially faster than strong pipelines.

Main contributions as I see them
1) A conceptually clean, unified joint parsing architecture that encodes the full joint parser state with stack LSTMs and runs fully greedily without handcrafted features or beam search.
2) A practically useful transition system and interleaving strategy (including M-SELF and copy vs. move design) that make joint syn-sem parsing feasible at high speed.
3) Solid empirical evidence that this end-to-end learned state representation closes much of the gap to feature-rich pipelines and beats prior joint models.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Moving joint syn-sem parsing to a pure representation-learning regime that encodes the entire parser state is a bold and meaningful step beyond prior joint models that relied on limited local contexts, hand-engineered features, or decoding with beams. Extending stack LSTMs to a synchronized joint transition system is, in my view, the primary contribution.
- Strong empirical case against well-chosen baselines: The model surpasses Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and variants on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and performs competitively across multiple languages. The ablations (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid, Joint) are informative and show that joint modeling benefits syntax and semantics in-domain.
- Speed and simplicity: The end-to-end pipeline is fast (≈178s on CoNLL 2009 test), nearly 2.5x faster than a strong SRL pipeline. The system avoids expensive feature extraction and exact inference, yet retains competitive accuracy.
- Transition system design: The M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments addresses a practical annotation quirk cleanly. The copy vs. move design and interleaving constraints are well-motivated and enable semantics to see the needed items without separate staging.
- Engineering completeness: Pretrained embeddings, modest hyperparameters, dropout, and a reproducible setup make the approach accessible. The promise of open-source code is valuable for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited head-to-head with current SRL SOTA: While the paper is honest about being behind top pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015), the analysis remains somewhat superficial. More detailed error analysis (by label types, long-distance vs short, crossing arcs) would clarify where the representation-learning approach still lags and where it shines.
- Oracle/transition coverage issues: The paper acknowledges that 1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the oracle transition sequence due to multiple crossing arcs. The impact on training (noise introduced by unfaithful supervision) and test-time behavior is not quantified; a small ablation (with/without these sentences) or a discussion of failure modes would strengthen confidence.
- Out-of-domain generalization: Joint overfits to WSJ relative to Hybrid on Brown. While not fatal, the paper could probe the causes (e.g., predicate sense bias, action-history memorization, reliance on POS tags) and explore simple mitigations (regularization, word dropout, sense smoothing, or small beams).
- Missing ablations on design choices: The core architectural choices (copy vs move, interleaving schedule, four-stack setup, effect of action-history A, pretrained vs learned embeddings) are not ablated. Even brief studies would help disentangle which design elements drive the gains.
- Morphology and small-data regimes: Multilingual results suggest dependence on pretrained embeddings and suffer in small-data settings (Japanese). The paper mentions morphology/characters as future work, but given their straightforward integration into this framework, a preliminary experiment would make the multilingual story more convincing.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the training-time impact of sentences whose gold cannot be exactly encoded (1.5%)? How are they handled in the loss, and do they correlate with particular semantic phenomena?
- How critical is the copy vs. move design? What happens if you revert to the original arc-eager moves and adjust semantic access differently (e.g., caching buffer-front embeddings)?
- Did you try small beams or dynamic oracles to mitigate greedy errors? Even beam=2 might address the Brown gap without hurting speed much.
- How sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings and their domain? Any numbers for English without pretraining, or with alternative embeddings?
- For unseen predicates, defaulting to “01” works well in English; how does this behave in other languages, and did you consider character/subword predicate sense representations?
- Can you provide parameter counts and memory footprint per language to substantiate the claim of simplicity?

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper introduces a clear and novel joint parsing paradigm—learning the entire joint parser state with stack LSTMs and decoding greedily—that advances the field’s direction even if it does not top every leaderboard. The design is elegant, fast, and empirically validated across tasks and languages. With a bit more analysis and ablation, this work can serve as a strong baseline and a platform for future improvements (e.g., morphology, small beams, global constraints).
