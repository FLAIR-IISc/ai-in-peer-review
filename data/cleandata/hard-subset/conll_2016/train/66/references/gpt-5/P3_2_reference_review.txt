Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn a continuous representation of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, shared buffer, and action history). The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2008; 2013) with some modifications (e.g., copy instead of move in arc-eager syntax actions to keep items available to semantics; a new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments). The model uses no hand-crafted features or beam search, and is trained with cross-entropy over allowed transitions. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual) show the model outperforms prior joint parsers and is competitive with pipelines, while being fast.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) An end-to-end greedy joint S+SRL transition system with stack-LSTM state representations that outperforms prior joint systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 and runs fast.
2) A practical interleaving scheme and small but crucial transition set/design choices for joint parsing (buffer copying for syntax actions; M-SELF; M-SWAP usage) that enable a purely greedy procedure.
3) Evidence that learned representations of parser state can obviate expensive hand-crafted syntactic path features for dependency SRL in joint parsing.

Strengths
- Clear, unified architecture with strong empirical performance and speed: The paper demonstrates consistent gains over previous joint systems (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) across CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, and competitive multilingual results. The runtime is compelling (≈178s single-core on CoNLL09 EN), ~2.5x faster than at least one competitive pipeline under matched conditions.

- Careful transition-system engineering for the joint setting: Copying rather than moving buffer items during S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT is a simple, elegant way to preserve information for semantics. The M-SELF transition handles NomBank self-arguments; M-SWAP supports crossing semantic arcs. The interleaving protocol (syntax actions until a copy-to-stack, then semantic actions until M-SHIFT) is well-motivated and keeps the procedure linear-time.

- Full parser state representation without hand-crafted features: Using four stack LSTMs (S, M, B, A) and recursive composition functions for syntactic/semantic attachments and predicate senses is principled and reuses strengths of Dyer et al. (2015). The model avoids global syntactic features like predicate–argument paths yet remains competitive.

- Controlled variants and analyses: The paper includes Semantics-only, Syntax-only, and Hybrid variants. Results showing Joint > Hybrid in-domain but Hybrid > Joint out-of-domain are informative about the benefits and overfitting tendencies of joint training. The Syntax-only LAS slightly surpasses Dyer et al. (2015), suggesting joint modeling does not harm syntax.

- Practicality and reproducibility: Implementation details, hyperparameters, and training setup are described; pretraining setup (structured skip-gram) is reported; code availability is promised. POS/lemma settings align with CoNLL protocols.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior work: The main innovation is applying stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system and interleaving regime. While effective, the core modeling ideas (stack-LSTM parser state; greedy transition prediction) are directly inherited from Dyer et al. (2015). The joint transition set is largely from Henderson et al. (2013) with modest extensions (copy semantics, M-SELF). The paper would be stronger with deeper algorithmic contributions (e.g., improved joint constraints, training with exploration, or joint global normalization).

- Greedy decoding and static supervision without robustness mechanisms: The model uses greedy inference and supervised oracle sequences. There is no beam, dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, or global normalization—approaches known to reduce exposure bias and search errors in transition systems. The out-of-domain regression of Joint vs Hybrid likely reflects this. An ablation with small beams or dynamic oracle training would clarify the trade-offs.

- Oracle coverage and representational limits for semantics: The authors acknowledge that the semantic oracle is imperfect (≈1.5% English sentences cannot be encoded correctly even with M-SWAP). The impact of these unreachable gold states on training and evaluation is not quantified. Without a characterization of which structures are missed (e.g., density of long-range, crossing arcs), it is hard to assess ceiling effects introduced by the transition inventory.

- Insufficient ablations on representation components: While the stack-LSTM architecture is justified, there are no ablations for: removing the action-history stack; removing buffer representation; using non-recursive composition vs ternary composition; removing pretrained embeddings; effect of M-SELF; copying vs moving in S actions. Such ablations would substantiate the claim that representation learning “obviates” full-syntax features and pinpoint which components drive gains.

- Comparability and statistical rigor: Claims of “significant” improvements over joint baselines are not backed by statistical testing. For multilingual settings, the extent to which gains are due to pretrained embeddings vs modeling choices is not disentangled (notably for Chinese). There is also limited error analysis (e.g., by role label, arc length, predicate type), which would illuminate where joint modeling helps or fails.

Questions to authors
1) Oracle coverage: How many gold semantic arcs (or sentences) are not representable by your transition set in each dataset/language, and what is the observed performance drop attributable to this? Can you report results excluding these cases?

2) Greedy vs beam/dynamic oracle: Did you try small beams (e.g., 4–16) or dynamic oracle training? What is the speed/accuracy trade-off and does it mitigate the OOD degradation?

3) Ablations: What is the impact of (a) removing the action-history stack; (b) using only S and M (no B); (c) removing recursive composition (i.e., composing only heads or using a bag/attention over stack)? Which component is most critical?

4) Predicate identification (CoNLL 2008): How sensitive is the end-to-end joint performance to the 91.43 F1 predicate detector? Can you provide end-to-end scores broken down by correct vs incorrect predicate detection?

5) Copy vs move in S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT: Quantify the effect of copying on accuracy and speed. Does copying increase memory/time significantly? Any adverse interactions with semantics (e.g., duplicated items causing conflicts)?

Additional comments
- Please include statistical significance testing for key comparisons and report standard deviations over multiple runs (neural models can vary noticeably).
- Clarify the learning rate schedule (“decay rate of 0.1 each epoch” is ambiguous—multiply by 0.9 or by 0.1?).
- Consider reporting breakdowns by semantic role label, arc length, and predicate POS to substantiate where joint modeling helps most.
- For multilingual, a version without pretrained embeddings would help isolate the contribution of the model vs resources; adding character/morphology features could be a strong extension.
- Code release is important for reproducibility; including scripts to reproduce CoNLL setups and pseudo-projectivization would be helpful.

Overall, this is a solid, practical paper with strong empirical results and a clean joint parsing design. Its primary value is demonstrating that a fast, feature-lean, greedy joint parser can outperform prior joint systems and approach competitive pipelines. The main limitations are modest novelty beyond adapting known components, reliance on purely greedy/static training, and limited analysis/ablations.
