Summary and claimed contributions
- A greedy, transition-based joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs, removing hand-crafted features and beam search.
- A concrete transition system for joint parsing with minor extensions (e.g., copying rather than moving buffer items, M-SELF for self-loops), plus an interleaving schedule between syntactic and semantic actions.
- Empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual) showing improvements over prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al.) and competitive speed; variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) inform the role of joint learning.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains over prior joint models: The joint model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and related contemporaneous joint approaches on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, indicating that learning a representation of the global parser state is beneficial versus locally scoped features and beam-search joint models. This addresses a long-standing gap in joint modeling where pipelines often dominated.
- Practical, simple, and fast: Fully greedy inference with state summarization yields strong speed (177.6±18 s on CoNLL-2009 En test, single core), and the architecture is conceptually straightforward to implement. The reported speedup over a well-known pipeline is attractive for real-world deployments.
- Ablations illuminate the joint signal: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint comparisons are informative. They corroborate that syntax helps SRL (semantics-only < hybrid/joint), and that learning semantics jointly does not meaningfully harm syntax. The out-of-domain degradation (Hybrid > Joint) highlights a realistic trade-off.
- Multilingual results: The method ports to multiple languages with competitive performance, without language-specific feature engineering and with modest resource needs. This supports the generality of the approach.
- End-to-end modeling choices: Integrating predicate sense prediction in the transition system and handling NomBank self-arguments via M-SELF are well-motivated engineering decisions that improve completeness of the joint framework.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established components: The core modeling idea is a direct application of stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015) to the Henderson-style joint transition system, with minor transition tweaks and a fixed interleaving schedule. The paper’s main differences (whole-state representation, greedy search) are incremental, not a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution to joint parsing.
- Incomplete validation and ablations: Key claims (that “entire state” and greedy decoding suffice) are not stress-tested. Missing experiments include: beam vs greedy; ablations on which stacks matter (remove A/history, remove B, only top-of-stack features); effect of composition functions (gs/gm/gd); representation choices (pretrained vs learned-only, POS/lemma contributions); and schedule sensitivity. Without these, it is difficult to attribute gains to specific design choices or to assess robustness.
- Oracle and search limitations not quantified: The oracle conversion is imperfect (≈1.5% of En CoNLL-2009 sentences), and the semantic crossing-arc limitations are acknowledged. There is no reported upper bound on achievable accuracy under the transition system and interleaving constraints, nor an estimate of the impact of these limitations on final metrics. Greedy search is prone to error propagation; no dynamic oracle or exploration training is used, and there is no analysis of compounding errors.
- Comparisons are not fully controlled: While the paper is transparent that SOTA pipelines with global inference and extra features/annotations still perform better, the fairness and scope of comparisons remain unclear. For example, pipelines exploit full trees and global SRL inference; here only partial structures and greedy search are used. But then the core claim—“no expert-crafted features” being sufficient—would be stronger if compared against equally neural baselines with strong encoders and identical resources. Statistical significance tests are not reported.
- Analysis is shallow; generalization concerns: The out-of-domain degradation of the joint model (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) suggests overfitting to WSJ. There is no detailed error analysis (e.g., long-distance arguments, crossing arcs, nominal predicates, low-frequency senses), nor learning-curve or low-resource study. The multilingual results depend on pretrained embeddings; the paper notes that removing them hurts Chinese, but does not quantify systematically.

Questions to authors
- Attribution of gains: Which components are necessary? Please provide ablations for (a) removing action-history stack A; (b) using only top-of-stack features vs entire-state summaries; (c) removing gd (predicate/sense composition); (d) removing pretrained embeddings and POS/lemma inputs; and (e) varying the number of LSTM layers/hidden sizes.
- Greedy vs beam: Can you quantify the effect of beam search (small beams) on accuracy/speed to support the claim that greedy suffices? Relatedly, did you try dynamic oracles or exploration training to mitigate error propagation?
- Oracle limitations: What is the empirical upper bound imposed by the imperfect oracle and transition constraints (including M-SWAP and M-SELF) on CoNLL 2008/2009? Can you estimate how often gold structures are unreachable and how that affects final scores?
- Schedule sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the interleaving policy between syntax and semantics? Did you try alternative schedules (e.g., semantics-first for some tokens, or joint decision over action types without hard switching)?
- Statistical significance and robustness: Are improvements over Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and other joint baselines statistically significant on WSJ and Brown? Can you provide variance across random seeds and learning curves?
- Error analysis: Please include breakdowns by predicate type (verbal vs nominal), argument distance, crossing vs non-crossing arcs, and sense frequency. Do M-SELF transitions materially help nominal predicate cases?
- Domain robustness: Given Hybrid > Joint on Brown, can you add regularization or domain-robust training (e.g., stronger dropout, data augmentation) to reduce overfitting, or show that the gap is small with tuned hyperparameters?
- Reproducibility: You promise open-source code; please clarify exact preprocessing, tokenization, and any differences from shared-task scripts, as well as hardware for training time.
