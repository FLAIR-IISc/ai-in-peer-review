Summary and claimed contributions
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire algorithmic state with stack LSTMs. It adapts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2008/2013), introduces an M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments, and removes hand-crafted features in favor of learned representations. Experiments on CoNLL 2008/2009 show the model outperforms prior joint models and is competitive with top pipeline systems in accuracy while being substantially faster. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid vs. joint) and multilingual results are reported.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A learned representation of the full joint parser state via stack LSTMs coupled with a fully greedy decoder, yielding strong accuracy among joint models and fast runtime. Compared with earlier joint systems and Henderson-style models, this is a meaningful empirical advance.
2) An effective joint transition system that interleaves syntactic and semantic operations, including a new M-SELF transition, and design choices (copying vs. moving buffer items) that enable semantics without expensive feature extraction.
3) A thorough empirical study (English CoNLL 2008/2009 and multilingual 2009) with ablations demonstrating the syntax–semantics interactions and speed–accuracy tradeoffs, and an open-source implementation.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over previous joint models with clear ablations. The joint model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009), isolating the value of learning full parser-state representations instead of relying on local features or beam search. The ablation showing Semantics-only < Hybrid < Joint in-domain substantiates that syntax helps SRL and that joint learning helps over pipelining (at least in-domain).
- Competitive with top CoNLL systems while being much faster. Although pure pipeline systems with hand-engineered features or exact inference still lead in absolute accuracy, the proposed model is comparable on many metrics and runs ~2.5× faster than a strong SRL pipeline, making a strong practical case.
- Simplicity and removal of hand-crafted features. The parser requires no path-based or global syntactic features, relying on stack LSTMs and composition functions. This reduces engineering overhead and makes the approach portable to new languages, as supported by multilingual results.
- Clear modeling choices and reproducibility details. The paper specifies the transition inventory, constraints, composition functions, training objective, hyperparameters, and runtime/memory, and promises open-source code. This is valuable for the community and facilitates follow-up work.
- Careful treatment of task-specific issues. The model accounts for predicate identification (CoNLL 2008) via a lightweight biLSTM classifier, handles unseen predicates via sensible defaulting, and adds M-SELF for nominal self-arguments—practical choices that improve end-to-end viability.

Weaknesses
- Limited conceptual novelty relative to Dyer et al. (2015). The main novelty is the application of stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system with a few design tweaks (copying instead of moving buffer items; M-SELF). While the empirical advances are solid, the modeling ideas are largely an adaptation/combination of prior components rather than a new algorithmic principle for joint parsing.
- Greedy decoding without exploration exacerbates exposure bias; overfitting out-of-domain. The model is trained with a static oracle and uses purely greedy inference. The Brown results where Hybrid > Joint suggest joint training overfits to WSJ, and the lack of beam search, dynamic oracles, or scheduled sampling may be a major limiting factor for robustness.
- Oracle conversion for semantic transitions is imperfect. The paper notes that for ~1.5% of English sentences the gold joint parse cannot be exactly encoded, and multiple crossing arcs further degrade oracle fidelity. This weakens the learning signal and complicates attribution of errors. An analysis or mitigation (e.g., improved transition inventory or constrained decoding) is missing.
- Comparisons and analysis could be deeper. While the paper situates results w.r.t. prior systems, it lacks statistical significance testing, fine-grained error analysis (e.g., argument types, long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs), and ablations on key components (pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, composition functions). It also does not report the impact of copying vs. moving buffer items, or the contribution of M-SELF quantitatively.
- Language-specific considerations are limited. The multilingual experiments are promising, but the absence of morphological features or character/subword representations penalizes highly inflected languages. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the gap or provide interim alternatives (e.g., char-CNN/LSTM).

Questions to authors
- Can you report statistical significance for the main English test set comparisons (e.g., vs. Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.) and for Joint vs. Hybrid on WSJ and Brown?
- How much does each component contribute? Please provide ablations for: pretrained embeddings on/off; action-history stack on/off; composition functions vs. simple pooling; copying vs. moving buffer items; and the M-SELF transition’s impact (given self-arg frequency is 6.68% of arcs).
- Did you try modest beams or dynamic oracles? Even a small beam or scheduled sampling might improve OOD robustness. Any preliminary results?
- Oracle mismatch: how often does the oracle produce suboptimal sequences for semantic crossing arcs, and what is the observed effect on training loss/accuracy? Could introducing an additional semantic transition (beyond M-SWAP) reduce these failures?
- Predicate sense defaulting: The 91.22% correctness of defaulting to “01” is reported by type for English. What is the token-level impact, and how does defaulting behave in other languages?
- Runtime reproducibility: What CPU model and BLAS settings were used for the runtime comparison to Lei et al. (2015)? Is the reported speed including POS/lemma prediction and predicate identification (for 2008)?

Additional comments and suggestions for improvement
- Explore lightweight robustness techniques (scheduled sampling, dynamic oracles, or small beams) to address exposure bias and improve OOD performance.
- Add character/subword features or simple morphological tags where available to improve multilingual performance, particularly for morphologically rich languages.
- Provide an error analysis focusing on long-distance semantic arcs, crossing structures, and particular SRL roles, and relate errors to the interleaving schedule (syntactic left attachments before semantic left attachments).
- Consider alternative interleaving strategies (learned or adaptive) rather than a fixed schedule, which may be overly constraining.
- Include significance testing and more detailed ablations to clarify where the gains come from.

Overall recommendation: borderline accept. The paper offers a strong, fast joint parser with clear empirical gains over prior joint models and good engineering value. The novelty is incremental relative to stack-LSTM parsing, and analysis is somewhat limited, but the contribution is solid and practically useful.
