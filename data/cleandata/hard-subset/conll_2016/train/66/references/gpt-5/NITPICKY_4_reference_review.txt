Summary and claimed contributions
- A greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns a continuous representation of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs (one each for syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history), eliminating hand-crafted features and outperforming prior joint models on CoNLL-2008/2009 English; multilingual results are competitive.
- A transition system for joint parsing adapted from Henderson et al. with two notable tweaks: copying instead of moving buffer items to enable interleaved syntax/semantics, and a new M-SELF transition to handle NomBank self-dependencies; includes M-SWAP for crossing arcs.
- An efficient implementation with reported single-core runtime improvements over a strong SRL pipeline baseline.

Strengths
- Solid empirical gains over previous joint models. On both CoNLL-2008 and 2009 (English), the joint model improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and variants, demonstrating the benefit of whole-state representations relative to more local features in prior joint work.
- Simple, fast, and resource-light. The purely greedy decoder with learned state summarization yields linear-time parsing and good throughput (reported ~178s on CoNLL-2009 test on one core) with modest memory (<512MB), making this attractive for practical deployment.
- Clear, unified architecture. Using four stack LSTMs to summarize S, M, B, and A is elegant and directly aligns with transition-based parsing; the recursive composition functions for syntactic, semantic, and predicate-sense attachments are well specified.
- Sensible experimental variants. Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint variants help isolate where gains come from and show expected trends (Semantics-only < Hybrid/Joint; some overfitting in-domain).
- Reasonable multilingual coverage. The approach transfers without language-specific feature engineering and is competitive on several CoNLL-2009 languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. The transition inventory is largely inherited from Henderson et al. (2013), and the core modeling idea (stack LSTMs encoding parser state) comes from Dyer et al. (2015). The main technical deltas—copy vs. move, M-SELF, and greedy decoding without beam—are incremental. The paper would be stronger with new learning or inference ideas (e.g., dynamic oracles, better training for greediness) or a deeper theoretical analysis of why whole-state summaries help.
- Greedy training/inference without exploration. The model is trained with a static oracle and decoded greedily. There is no comparison to small beams, no dynamic oracle, no cost-sensitive training, and no scheduled sampling/DAgger. Given known error propagation in greedy transition systems, an ablation showing that greediness does not leave substantial accuracy on the table is missing.
- Oracle and transition coverage issues are under-analyzed. The semantic oracle is imperfect (1.5% of sentences), M-SWAP is restricted to avoid infinite loops, and a rigid interleaving schedule is imposed (semantic transitions only between S-SHIFT/RIGHT and next M-SHIFT). The paper does not quantify how often gold graphs are unreproducible under these constraints, how such cases are handled in training, or the impact on accuracy. No justification or ablation for the chosen interleaving policy is given.
- Evaluation and analysis are thin. Claims of “significant” improvements have no statistical testing. There are no ablations of key components (action-history LSTM A, composition functions gs/gm/gd, pretrained vs. random embeddings, effect of M-SELF, effect of copying vs. moving, pseudo-projectivization). Error analyses (e.g., arc length, crossing structures, predicate senses, OOD behavior) are absent. The observation that Joint overfits OOD is not investigated (no regularization or domain adaptation discussion).
- Methodological and presentation issues:
  - Notation/overloading: At denotes both allowed transition set and action-history stack; Eq. (1) uses concatenation “[st;mt;bt,at]” with a comma and mixes At both as a function and a set. Minor typos abound (“concate- nation”, “correspodning”, “dismabiguated”, “endto-end”), and table/figure references rely on supplementary material not shown.
  - Hyperparameters: Dropout candidates include 1.0 (degenerate). Learning rate decay “0.1 each epoch” is ambiguous. Stopping criterion “when dev performance does not improve for ~6–7 hours” is ad hoc. No per-language hyperparameters are provided.
  - Reproducibility: Code is “to be released on publication”; runtime comparison lacks hardware specs and software stack details (compiler/BLAS), and it’s unclear whether 2008 predicate identification time is included in end-to-end figures.
  - Fairness/confounds: External Gigaword embeddings are used; the extent to which gains depend on them (especially for Chinese) is not quantified. No morphological features are used but competitors sometimes do; discussion is deferred to future work.

Questions to authors
- How often is the gold joint structure unreproducible under your transition constraints (imperfect oracle, M-SWAP restrictions, interleaving schedule) across languages, and how do you train on such sentences (skip, approximate sequence, heuristics)? Please quantify impact on dev accuracy.
- Did you try beam search (even beam=4–8) or dynamic-oracle training? What is the accuracy/runtime trade-off relative to purely greedy?
- Ablations: What is the contribution of (a) the action-history LSTM A, (b) pretrained embeddings, (c) composition functions vs. simple head-word representations, (d) the M-SELF transition, and (e) copy vs. move in S-SHIFT/RIGHT?
- Interleaving policy: Why require semantic actions only between S-SHIFT/RIGHT and M-SHIFT? Did you try alternative schedules (e.g., allowing occasional semantic actions elsewhere)? Any empirical differences?
- Significance and robustness: Are improvements over Henderson et al. (2013) and your variants statistically significant on WSJ and Brown? Please report tests and CIs.
- Runtime comparison: What exact hardware, compiler, and BLAS were used for your timing and for Lei et al.’s code? Does the 2008 end-to-end time include predicate identification?
- Default predicate sense “01”: How sensitive are results to this heuristic for unseen predicates? What proportion of predicate tokens (not types) fall into this case at test time?
- Multilingual setup: Did you tune hyperparameters per language? Could you report results without pretrained embeddings to quantify their contribution per language?
