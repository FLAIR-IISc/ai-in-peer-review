- Paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to encode full parser state and trained with locally normalized softmax.

- Main contribution 1: Practical, fast joint parser that encodes full joint parsing state with stack LSTMs and achieves strong accuracy without hand-crafted features or beam/global decoding.

- Main contribution 2: Careful integration of joint syntactic–semantic transitions (including M-SELF) and an interleaving scheme enabling purely greedy decoding.

- Main contribution 3: Empirical analysis and ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) showing learned state representations can replace pipeline features and clarifying joint learning effects.

- Strength: Elegant modeling of full parser state via four stack LSTMs, removing need for hand-crafted path/tree features and improving over prior joint transition parsers.

- Strength: Excellent accuracy–speed trade-off — greedy decoding attains near state-of-the-art joint performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 English with large speed gains.

- Strength: Well-designed semantic transition system (M-SELF, M-SWAP, copy-based arc-eager) addressing self-arcs and crossing arcs in dependency SRL.

- Strength: Solid ablation study that provides useful evidence about the role of syntactic information and when joint vs hybrid training helps.

- Strength: Reproducibility and modest resource footprint, public code, and reliance on known architectures increase accessibility.

- Weakness: Limited novelty over Dyer et al. (2015); modeling and composition largely extend existing syntactic techniques to the joint case.

- Weakness: Greedy locally normalized training invites label bias and exposure bias; no dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling, beam, or global normalization explored.

- Weakness: Oracle conversion imperfect — 1.5% of English sentences aren't perfectly encoded; per-language/domain upper bounds and impact not quantified.

- Weakness: Interleaving constraints force syntax-left-first which may bias decisions and be suboptimal for some languages or long-distance semantics; no alternative interleaving ablation.

- Weakness: Predicate identification handled separately for CoNLL 2008, weakening end-to-end jointness and complicating pipeline comparisons.

- Weakness: Limited multilingual adaptation — no character/subword or explicit morphology handling for morphologically rich languages; sensitivity to pretrained embeddings noted.

- Question 1: Report exact oracle F1/LAS upper bound per language and domain, training-time oracle accuracies, and fraction of gold arcs lost to conversion?

- Question 2: Were alternative interleaving schemes (semantic-first or fully mixed) tried, and what were their effects on accuracy and speed; is left-first syntax empirically necessary?

- Question 3: Did you try small beam or globally normalized training to address label bias, and how did accuracy vs speed trade off?

- Question 4: For unseen predicates defaulted to “01”, what is the token-level impact and does this generalize across languages?

- Question 5: Were pretrained embeddings fine-tuned, how sensitive are results to embedding source/size, and would character/subword models help low-resource languages?

- Question 6: Provide error analysis by phenomenon (crossing arcs, nominal predicates, long-distance arguments) showing which errors joint training reduces versus hybrid/pipeline.

- Overall evaluation: Solid engineering contribution with moderate conceptual novelty — clean integration, convincing empirical results, recommended for acceptance provided deeper analysis of interleaving, oracle limits, and training bias is added.
