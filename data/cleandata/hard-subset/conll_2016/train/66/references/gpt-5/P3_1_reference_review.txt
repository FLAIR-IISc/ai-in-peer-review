Summary and main contributions
This paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to learn dense representations of the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, shared buffer, and action history). The transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with modifications: (i) interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions over a shared buffer with copy (not move) semantics for arc-eager actions; (ii) an additional semantic transition (M-SELF) to handle NomBank-style self-dependencies; and (iii) recursive composition functions for syntactic arcs, semantic arcs, and predicate-sense composition. The model is trained with locally normalized softmax over allowed actions and run with greedy inference. It outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive in multilingual settings, while being very fast.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, fast joint parser that encodes the full joint parsing state with stack LSTMs and achieves strong accuracy without hand-crafted features or beam/global decoding.
2) A careful integration of joint syntactic–semantic transitions, including M-SELF, and an interleaving scheme that enables purely greedy decoding.
3) Empirical analysis showing that learned state representations can successfully replace expensive pipeline features, with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarify the role of joint learning.

Strengths
- Clear, effective modeling of full parser state without feature engineering: Using four stack LSTMs to summarize S, M, B, and action history gives the classifier access to all partial structures, providing an elegant alternative to path- or tree-based feature templates. The result improves over prior joint transition-based parsers (e.g., Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.) that rely on localized or expert features.

- Strong accuracy-speed trade-off: Purely greedy decoding delivers near–state-of-the-art joint performance for CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results, with substantial speed gains over pipeline systems. This is valuable for practical deployment and large-scale processing.

- Well-designed transition system for semantics: The inclusion of M-SELF to handle self-arcs and the allowance for crossing arcs via M-SWAP address well-known pain points in dependency-based SRL. The copy-based arc-eager adaptation maintains semantic access to buffer elements and is thoughtfully justified.

- Solid ablation study: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint variants provide useful evidence about the contribution of syntactic information to SRL and joint training. The finding that joint training helps in-domain while hybrid generalizes better out-of-domain is informative for practitioners.

- Reproducibility and reasonable resource footprint: The reliance on publicly described architectures (stack LSTMs), release of code, and modest memory/CPU demands increase the work’s impact and accessibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to Dyer et al. (2015): Much of the modeling (stack LSTMs for state summarization, recursive composition) is a direct extension from syntactic parsing to joint parsing. The transition inventory follows Henderson et al., with relatively incremental modifications (copy semantics and M-SELF). The conceptual advance is therefore more in application/integration than in algorithmic novelty.

- Greedy, locally normalized training with static oracles: The model uses a locally normalized softmax over allowable actions and greedy inference. This invites label bias and exposure bias. There is no exploration/dynamic oracle, cost-sensitive learning, or global normalization. The paper would be strengthened by quantifying accuracy–speed trade-offs with a small beam, scheduled sampling/dynamic oracles, or globally normalized variants.

- Imperfect oracle conversion for semantic transitions: The paper notes that 1.5% of English sentences (and potentially more in other languages) cannot be perfectly encoded by the transition sequence. This imposes an upper bound below gold and introduces training noise. The magnitude of this cap is not quantified per language/domain, and the performance impact is not analyzed.

- Interleaving constraints may bias decisions: For each token, left-side syntactic dependencies are resolved before left-side semantic dependencies and syntax must “go first” until a shift. This hard constraint might be suboptimal for certain languages or long-distance semantic attachments. An ablation with alternative interleaving policies (e.g., syntactic and semantic competition at each step; or semantic-first for predicates) would clarify its effect.

- Predicate identification handled separately in 2008: The joint claim is weakened for CoNLL 2008 where predicate identification is done by a separate biLSTM classifier. While consistent with the task, it reduces the end-to-end jointness and makes comparisons to pipeline systems less clean.

- Limited multilingual adaptation: No use of morphology or character-level representations in morphologically rich languages; results suggest sensitivity to pretraining (e.g., Chinese). Including char/subword encoders or simple morphological features could be important for fair cross-lingual comparisons.

Questions to authors
1) Oracle and upper bounds: What is the exact oracle F1/LAS upper bound induced by your transition encoding for each language (and by domain in English)? Can you report training-time oracle accuracies and the fraction of gold arcs lost due to conversion?

2) Interleaving policy: Did you explore alternative interleaving schemes (semantic-first, or fully mixed decision spaces) and what were the effects on accuracy and speed? Is the left-first syntactic constraint necessary empirically?

3) Local vs global training: Did you try a small beam or globally normalized training (e.g., CRF objective) to mitigate label bias, and if so, how did accuracy and speed change?

4) Predicate sense defaulting: For unseen predicates, defaulting to “01” is correct for 91% by type in English. What is the token-level accuracy impact and how does this generalize to other languages?

5) Pretrained embeddings: Were pretrained embeddings fine-tuned? How sensitive are results to the embedding source/size? For languages with smaller data, would character/subword models help?

6) Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative/quantitative analysis by phenomenon (crossing arcs, nominal predicates, long-distance arguments), and which errors are reduced by joint training vs hybrid/pipeline?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid engineering contribution that demonstrates that learned state representations can effectively replace hand-crafted joint features, delivering competitive accuracy with excellent speed. The conceptual novelty is moderate, but the integration is clean, the empirical results are convincing for joint models, and the paper will be useful to the community. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that a deeper investigation of interleaving, oracle limitations, and training bias would strengthen the work.
