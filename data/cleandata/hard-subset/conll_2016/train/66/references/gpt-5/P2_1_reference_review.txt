Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the full parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) with stack LSTMs. It dispenses with hand-engineered features and beam search, and introduces a new transition (M-SELF) to handle CoNLL-2009 nominal self-arguments.
- Empirically, it surpasses prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008/2013; Gesmundo et al., 2009) on CoNLL-2008/2009 English, is comparable to top shared-task systems in multilingual settings, and runs substantially faster than competitive pipelines. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) elucidate the utility of joint modeling and syntax for SRL.
- The work demonstrates that learned representations of the entire parsing state can replace costly, global syntactic features typically used for SRL in pipelines, while maintaining competitive accuracy and much better speed.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated modeling advance: Encoding the entire parser state with stack LSTMs is a principled way to replace expert-crafted, global features in joint parsing. The design decision to share the buffer across syntactic and semantic transitions, with interleaving constraints, is coherent and grounded in prior transition systems. The addition of M-SELF is a practical and necessary extension for CoNLL-2009.
- Strong and broad empirical validation: The model outperforms prior joint systems on CoNLL-2008/2009 English and is competitive multilingualy. The ablation suite (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) is particularly informative: (i) semantics-only underperforms, confirming syntax’s utility; (ii) joint beats hybrid in-domain, suggesting mutual benefits; (iii) hybrid outperforming joint out-of-domain is a useful diagnostic of overfitting. These results substantiate the central claims.
- Simplicity and speed: Entirely greedy inference with learned features yields a 2.5x speedup over a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015), with low memory footprint and single-threaded CPU feasibility. This is a meaningful practical contribution, especially for large-scale or latency-sensitive settings.
- Reasonable methodological soundness: The transition inventory and constraints are carefully specified; composition functions for arcs and predicate senses are clear; the scoring function and training objective are standard and appropriate. The paper is technically clear overall, with concrete implementation choices and hyperparameters described.
- Relevance and potential for extension: Many orthogonal advances (morphology, global inference, reranking, multi-resource training) could be layered on this framework. The approach may also generalize to other joint structured parsing tasks.

Weaknesses
- Not state-of-the-art in SRL accuracy: While strong for a greedy joint model without hand-engineered syntactic features, it lags behind best pipelines with global features and exact/DP inference (e.g., Täckström et al., Lei et al., FitzGerald et al.). The paper acknowledges this, but a deeper quantitative comparison (e.g., per-label or long-distance argument performance) would clarify where the gap remains.
- Limited analysis of errors and design choices: The paper provides minimal error analysis (no breakdown by predicate type, distance, crossing arcs, or argument roles), and little ablation on architectural choices (e.g., impact of the action-history LSTM, effect of removing the interleaving constraint, or comparing copy vs move behavior on the buffer for syntax). Such analyses would strengthen the causal link between design and gains.
- Oracle mismatch and learning biases: The authors note imperfect oracle conversion for semantics (multiple crossing arcs, 1.5% of sentences affected). It is unclear how often the induced transition gold deviates from the target graph and how this impacts training/inference. Without dynamic oracles, greedy training may be brittle; an exploration of dynamic-oracle training or scheduled sampling could be valuable, or at least an empirical estimate of the performance loss due to oracle imperfections.
- Generalization and overfitting: The hybrid model outperforming joint out-of-domain suggests the joint learner may overfit to WSJ syntax-semantics correlations. The paper lacks regularization or domain robustness analysis (e.g., stronger dropout sweeps, pretraining variants, or domain adaptation baselines), and no statistical significance tests or multiple-run variance are reported.
- Multilingual setup under-specified: While multilingual results are encouraging, the role of pretrained embeddings appears crucial (notably for Chinese), but the paper does not quantify sensitivity to embedding quality/availability, nor does it exploit morphology, which is known to matter in languages like Czech. This limits the diagnostic value of the multilingual experiments.

Questions to authors
- Oracle coverage: What fraction of training arcs/sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the transition sequence for semantics across languages, and how does removing those sentences or approximating them affect performance?
- Dynamic oracle/training: Did you attempt dynamic-oracle training or exploration strategies to mitigate exposure bias from greedy training? If so, what were the effects?
- Design ablations: How much do (a) the action-history LSTM, (b) the interleaving constraint, and (c) the choice to copy rather than move items during syntactic transitions each contribute? Can you report deltas?
- Pretrained embeddings: Can you quantify the performance drop without pretrained embeddings per language, and sensitivity to embedding dimensionality/corpus size? Are character-based embeddings a viable substitute where morphology is important?
- Robustness and variance: What is the variance across random seeds (accuracy and runtime)? Are observed improvements over prior joint systems statistically significant?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate. The joint transition system and stack LSTM application are natural extensions of prior work, but the full-state encoding for joint parsing and M-SELF transition are meaningful contributions.
- Soundness: Good, though limited by oracle mismatch and lack of global training/inference.
- Significance: Good. The method advances the state of joint parsing and offers a strong speed/accuracy trade-off.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Good. The method is well described; hyperparameters are listed; code release is promised (please ensure it includes training scripts and pretrained resources).

I lean toward acceptance for its clean modeling idea, solid empirical improvements over prior joint systems, and practical impact via speed, while encouraging the authors to strengthen analysis and robustness.
