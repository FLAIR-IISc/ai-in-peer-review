Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack S, semantic stack M, buffer B, and action history A) with stack LSTMs. It interleaves syntax and semantics over a shared buffer and uses learned composition functions for syntactic arcs, semantic arcs, and predicate senses.
- It introduces a semantic transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies from NomBank and adapts arc-eager actions by “copying” rather than moving to retain buffer access for semantics.
- Empirically, it claims to outperform prior joint models (Henderson et al., 2008/2013; Gesmundo et al., 2009; Titov et al., 2009) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, be competitive multilingually, and be fast, with ablations (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid vs Joint).

Strengths
- Strong empirical showing against prior joint models with a simple, unified architecture. The consistent gains over Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) credibly demonstrate the value of learning a representation of the full parser state rather than relying on local handcrafted features.
- Practicality and speed. The reported single-core runtime is compelling, and the design (greedy decoding, no global features, small memory footprint) suits realistic deployments.
- Clear, modular transition system that synchronizes syntactic and semantic decisions over a shared buffer. The explicit interleaving constraints are well-motivated and intuitively align with left-context availability.
- Sensible model variants. Reporting Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid, and Joint clarifies the contributions of syntax to SRL, and reveals domain effects (e.g., Joint overfitting to WSJ vs Hybrid on Brown).
- Coverage of NomBank self-arguments via M-SELF, and allowance for reentrancy via non-destructive M-LEFT/M-RIGHT, are thoughtful and account for SRL graph idiosyncrasies.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. Methodologically, this is largely an adaptation of Dyer et al. (2015) to a known joint transition system (Henderson et al., 2013) with a new self-loop operator and a “copy vs move” tweak. There is no new learning or inference algorithm (still greedy with local cross-entropy), no dynamic oracle, no global objective, and no beam or exact decoding comparison. The contribution is more engineering integration than conceptual advance.
- Ambiguities in representing DAG semantics with tree-structured composition. The composition functions gs/gm are defined over ternary trees, yet semantic graphs allow multiple parents and crossing arcs. Because M-LEFT/M-RIGHT do not remove dependents, the same token can receive multiple incoming arcs and be involved in multiple compositions. It is unclear: (a) how vector representations are updated when a node acquires multiple parents; (b) whether composed vectors are consistent across different partial structures; and (c) whether duplicates/pointers are used to avoid state drift. This is a core modeling detail that needs precise specification.
- Incomplete training/oracle description. The semantic oracle is not perfect; 1.5% of English sentences cannot be exactly encoded. The paper does not state how training handles unreachable gold states/arcs (discard sentences? approximate sequences? ignore arcs?), nor quantify this for other languages. Constraints on M-SWAP (“repetitive swaps disallowed”) are under-specified; completeness and termination guarantees are not formalized. Exposure bias is likely severe (pure teacher forcing, greedy decoding) with no exploration/dynamic-oracle training.
- Experimental gaps and weak analysis. 
  - No ablations on core ingredients (pretrained embeddings vs learned, action-history stack A, composition functions, interleaving constraints, copy vs move). 
  - No comparison to beam search or global/dynamic-oracle training on the same transition set. 
  - No statistical significance tests; error analysis is minimal. 
  - Predicate sense defaulting to “01” is only justified for English; multilingual impact is not reported. 
  - The pseudo-projectivization choice is not compared against alternatives; the effect on LAS/SRL is unknown.
- Clarity, notation, and formatting issues that impede reproducibility.
  - The “copy” semantics for S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT vs M transitions need precise, pointer-based definitions; current text risks ambiguity about identity management and buffer/stack consistency.
  - Overloaded notation (A as action stack vs At as allowed transition set). Mixed delimiters in equations ([st;mt;bt,at]) and minor typos (“dismabiguated,” “correspodning”) recur. 
  - Several figures/tables are referenced but not fully interpretable here (e.g., Tables 2–4, Figures 1–5). Numbers are sometimes referred to only relatively (e.g., “slightly better than 89.56 LAS”) rather than stated. 
  - Runtime comparisons omit predicate-identification time (for CoNLL 2008) and hardware details (CPU model), and exclude pretagging/embedding costs.

Questions to authors
- How exactly are semantic DAGs represented with the ternary composition functions when a node has multiple incoming arcs? Do you maintain a single canonical vector per token/fragment, or multiple context-specific composed vectors? If the former, how is composition order handled and information aggregated?
- How do you handle sentences whose gold parses are not encodable by the transition system (1.5% in English)? Do you drop them, approximate with heuristic transitions, or ignore some arcs? What fraction is this in each non-English dataset?
- Did you try dynamic-oracle training, beam search, or global objectives? If so, what were the trade-offs; if not, why not?
- Please provide ablations: without pretrained embeddings; without the action-history stack; removing M-SELF; replacing “copy” with conventional “move”; and disabling interleaving (i.e., pipeline within the same architecture).
- Clarify the predicate sense defaulting strategy for unseen predicates in non-English languages and report its accuracy by language.
- Report statistical significance and include exact numbers for all systems in Tables 2–4. Also specify hardware for runtime and whether predicate identification time is included for CoNLL 2008. 
- Will the released code include the oracle conversion and all training scripts to reproduce the reported numbers?
