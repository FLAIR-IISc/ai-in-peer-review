Summary and main contributions
- A joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that learns a vector representation of the entire parsing state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs, enabling fully greedy inference without hand-crafted features or beam search.
- A synchronized transition system for joint parsing adapted to work with shared buffer access (copy vs. move) and a new M-SELF transition to model NomBank self-arguments; plus an interleaving schedule that ensures both syntactic and semantic structures are produced online.
- Strong empirical results: new state of the art among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual performance, and significant speed advantages; informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarify the contribution of joint modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Learning a compact representation of the entire joint parser state and committing to a fully greedy decoder is a clean, modern alternative to pipelines and beam/global search. This pushes the field toward feature-free, fast joint analysis and provides a reusable blueprint for other structured tasks.
- Clear algorithmic advances for joint transitions: The copy-based adaptation of arc-eager to share the buffer across syntax/semantics, the explicit interleaving protocol, and the M-SELF transition are well-motivated and practically important to make synchronized joint parsing work without expensive features.
- Strong empirical evidence vs. relevant baselines: The model consistently outperforms prior joint systems (Henderson et al. and successors) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, and is competitive internationally. The ablations convincingly show (i) joint benefits over semantics-only and (ii) that adding semantics does not hurt syntax (syntax-only baseline remains strong).
- Efficiency and practicality: End-to-end single-core runtime is substantially faster than a competitive pipeline, with small memory footprint. Given comparable accuracy to pipelines for many settings, this is compelling for large-scale use.
- Multilingual breadth: Results on multiple languages (with and without large training data) indicate the approach adapts with minimal language-specific engineering. Pretrained embeddings are leveraged effectively.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to Dyer et al. (2015): Much of the modeling contribution reuses the stack-LSTM paradigm and composition functions; the main novelty is applying it to a synchronized joint transition system. While valuable, the methodological leap is smaller than the positioning suggests.
- Limited error and behavior analysis: The paper reports scores but offers little insight into error types (e.g., long-distance roles, crossing arcs, predicate sense confusion) or where greedy decoding fails vs. global/beam approaches. No analysis of action-history utility, composition choices, or sensitivity to pretraining.
- Oracle/transition conversion imperfections: The semantic oracle is not exact (1.5% of EN CoNLL 2009 sentences cannot be encoded). The impact on training (e.g., noise, bias) and decoding robustness is not quantified. For a greedy model, such mis-specifications can matter.
- Overfitting and generalization: In CoNLL 2009, Hybrid beats Joint out-of-domain, suggesting the joint learner overfits to WSJ. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore remedies (regularization, domain-robust features, small beam) or provide diagnostics.
- Scope limitations vs. top SRL systems: The work refrains from integrating orthogonal advances (global inference, syntactic path features, multi-resource training), yet comparisons are emphasized. A small set of controlled add-ons (e.g., shallow global constraints, tiny beam) would strengthen the case that the proposed representation is the key driver.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the impact of the imperfect semantic oracle? How do results change if you remove sentences with unencodable gold transitions or approximate them differently?
- How critical are each stack component and the action-history LSTM? Please provide ablations removing A, or replacing stack summaries with top-k elements, to support the “entire state” claim.
- Did you try a small beam (e.g., 4–8) or simple global constraints to mitigate overfitting and improve Brown performance? What is the speed/accuracy trade-off?
- How sensitive is performance to pretrained embeddings (quality, domain, OOV rate)? Any results without pretraining for English, and a breakdown for Chinese where gains were largest?
- What are the most common SRL errors (by role, distance, crossing arcs)? Does M-SWAP meaningfully reduce errors on non-projective semantics?
- For CoNLL 2008 predicate identification, how often does the subsequent parser correct predicate ID mistakes, or are these hard errors? Any joint training with the predicate classifier attempted?
- Could you report per-language runtimes and memory, and the effect of morphology (if available) or character-based embeddings on morphologically rich languages?
- How often is the default “01” sense used at test time, and what is its error rate when invoked?

Overall assessment
This is a timely, clean, and surprisingly effective application of learned parser-state representations to joint syntactic–semantic parsing, coupled with sensible transition-system engineering for synchronization. While some methodological pieces build directly on prior stack-LSTM parsing, the paper advances the state of joint models, is fast, and includes solid ablations. Given the novelty in pushing fully feature-free, greedy joint parsing with competitive accuracy, I lean positive despite the limited analysis and the incremental nature relative to Dyer et al. I encourage acceptance.
