Summary and main contributions
- A transition-based joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire algorithmic state (syntax stack S, semantics stack M, buffer B, action history A) with stack LSTMs and performs fully greedy inference. Compared to prior joint parsers (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; 2013), the key difference is learning dense state representations rather than relying on hand-crafted, locally scoped features.
- An interleaved transition system for joint parsing (arc-eager-style syntax; SRL with M-SWAP, M-PRED and a new M-SELF transition for self-arguments) with constraints to ensure termination; and composition functions to build vector representations of partial structures.
- Empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and multilingual datasets showing improvements over prior joint models and competitive performance with pipeline systems, with strong runtime.

Strengths
- Clear, unified neural architecture for joint parsing: Using four stack LSTMs to summarize S, M, B, A is an elegant extension of Dyer et al. (2015) to the joint setting. This substantially reduces feature engineering and is conceptually clean.
- Strong baselines and ablations: The paper compares joint vs semantics-only, syntax-only, and “hybrid” variants. These ablations support claims about the utility of joint modeling (WSJ) and the risk of overfitting (Brown), and show syntax-only performance competitive with arc-eager neural parsers.
- Practical speed: The reported end-to-end runtime (∼178s single core on CoNLL 2009 English test) is attractive. If verified under identical hardware and preprocessing, this is a meaningful advantage over slower pipelines.
- Sensible engineering choices: Handling nominal self-arguments via M-SELF, and predicate sense fallback to “01” for unseen predicates are practical design decisions that likely contribute to robust behavior.
- Multilingual applicability: The method ports to multiple languages with minimal task-specific customization, and performance is competitive with top CoNLL 2009 systems in most languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The main technical novelty is applying stack LSTMs to a known joint transition system with an interleaving schedule and adding M-SELF. The state representation and greedy classifier are direct extensions of Dyer et al. (2015). The contribution feels incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Incomplete analysis of oracle limitations: The authors acknowledge that the oracle conversion for semantics is imperfect (∼1.5% unreachable gold structures in En-2009). No quantitative upper bound analysis is provided for all datasets nor an error breakdown of how this limits final accuracy. This is important, as the transition inventory and constraints define the true ceiling of the approach.
- Theoretical claims about complexity are under-justified: Asymptotic linear-time complexity is claimed by citing Nivre (2009) for arc-eager syntax. However, the semantics side includes M-SWAP and allows multiple parents per node; the paper does not prove or discuss worst-case bounds for semantics. The claim seems overstated without considering the semantic transitions.
- Greedy decoding without exploration or dynamic oracles: The model trains with standard cross-entropy on gold actions but decodes greedily. No dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, or beam search is explored. This choice likely contributes to the out-of-domain drop and may leave gains on the table. A comparison with a small beam baseline would clarify the decoding trade-offs.
- Reproducibility gaps and fairness of comparisons: 
  - Speed comparison to Lei et al. (2015) lacks detailed hardware parity (CPU model, BLAS, tokenization), tokens/sec, and I/O costs. “Identical settings” via a public repo is insufficient without hardware and environment details.
  - Pretrained embeddings (structured skip-gram on Gigaword) are used; ablations without pretraining are missing (noted for Chinese only). Comparisons to systems without such pretraining may be unfair.
  - Important training details are underspecified: random seeds, number of epochs/updates, gradient clipping, batch size (or purely online?), regularization beyond dropout, and early stopping criteria beyond “6–7 hours.”
- Predicate identification setup (CoNLL 2008) is pipelined: The predicate identification BiLSTM is trained separately; thus the overall system is not fully joint from tokens to semantics. The integration point, error propagation, and its impact on joint performance are under-analyzed.
- Insufficient error analysis: Claims of being “on par” with top systems lack statistical tests and detailed breakdowns (e.g., by arc label, distance, crossing/non-crossing, self-arguments, predicate POS). No qualitative analysis of typical failure modes for semantics vs syntax is provided.
- Hybrid baseline is not a strong pipeline: The “hybrid” setting does not expose full syntactic trees to the semantic component, unlike standard SRL pipelines. This makes it an artificially weak proxy for pipelines and complicates conclusions about joint vs pipeline trade-offs.

Questions to authors
- Oracle coverage: Please report the percentage of unreachable gold joint parses for all datasets (CoNLL 2008, 2009 En/other languages) and estimate the upper bound impact on LAS/SRL F1.
- Complexity: What is the worst-case time complexity considering semantic transitions (including M-SWAP) with your constraints? Can you bound the number of swaps? Is it still linear?
- Decoding: Did you try a small beam (e.g., 4–8) or dynamic oracle training? If so, what are the trade-offs in accuracy vs speed?
- Predicate senses: For M-PRED, how are candidate senses enumerated? Is a per-lemma classifier used, or a shared softmax over all senses? How are unseen lemmas handled in multilingual settings?
- Fairness: Provide ablations without pretrained embeddings for English and other languages; quantify the gains from pretraining. Also clarify if competing systems used comparable external resources.
- Speed: Report tokens/sec with standard deviations and precise hardware (CPU model, RAM), compiler/BLAS, and I/O settings for both your parser and Lei et al. (2015).
- Reproducibility: Specify random seeds, batch size, gradient clipping, number of epochs/updates, and exact early stopping criterion. Will you release scripts to reproduce all tables?

Additional comments
- Several typographical/formatting issues: Inline footnotes (e.g., “1”, “2”, “3”) are hard to follow; Table/Figure references sometimes lack captions in the text; minor spacing and punctuation inconsistencies (e.g., “gd(v,p)” line).
- Please clarify whether the constraints suffice to guarantee termination and avoid dead-ends when semantics allow multiple parents and copying from B. A brief proof sketch would help.
- Consider adding a controlled comparison with a strong pipeline that exposes full syntactic trees to the semantic component to fairly assess joint vs pipeline.
