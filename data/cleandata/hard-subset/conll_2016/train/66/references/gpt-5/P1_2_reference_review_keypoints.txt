- Proposes a greedy transition-based joint parser for syntax and SRL using stack LSTMs over syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history, removing expert features and beam search.
- Adds M-SELF to the semantic transition system to handle CoNLL2009 self-arguments and interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer with validity constraints.
- Shows empirical gains over prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual performance, and fast inference; includes syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid ablations.
- Architectural contribution: adapts stack LSTMs to encode the full joint parsing state with interleaved transitions and a unified scoring function, eliminating handcrafted features and beam.
- Empirical contribution: strong English results on CoNLL 2008/2009 and competitive multilingual performance with substantial speed improvements.
- Engineering contribution: delivers a practical, fast, end-to-end greedy system and a pragmatic M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments.
- Strength: clean, principled extension of stack LSTMs to joint syntactic–semantic parsing that models full state instead of top-k handcrafted features.
- Strength: consistent empirical improvements over prior joint systems and informative ablations showing semantics benefits from syntax and joint training value.
- Strength: practical speed advantage (~178s single-core on CoNLL2009 English) versus slower pipeline alternatives (~440s), suitable for real-time/large-scale use.
- Strength: careful transition and constraint design (shared buffer, copy semantics, interleaving, M-SWAP, M-SELF) enabling reentrancies and crossing arcs.
- Strength: multilingual applicability with competitive CoNLL2009 results and transparent discussion of Chinese/pretrained-embedding effects.
- Weakness: limited analysis of generalization and overfitting—joint model underperforms hybrid out-of-domain without diagnosing causes or offering mitigations.
- Weakness: oracle/upper-bound limitations not quantified—imperfect semantic oracle and ~1.5% unencodable sentences may cap performance but impact is unexplored.
- Weakness: ablations are incomplete—missing studies on beam search, action-history LSTM, pretrained vs random embeddings, M-SELF/M-SWAP contributions, and arc-eager copy vs move.
- Weakness: predicate-sense handling for unseen predicates defaults to “01” heuristically, with no analysis of its frequency or error contribution.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—several hyperparameters “intuitively set,” plain SGD, and no reporting of variance across runs or tuning protocol.
- Q: Can you quantify the oracle gap and how often M-SWAP and M-SELF are used or required for gold arcs?
- Q: How sensitive is performance to beam size and did you try global training (max-margin/dynamic oracles) to reduce exposure bias or Brown gap?
- Q: What is the contribution of each stack LSTM component—e.g., removing action history or restricting stacks to top-k summaries?
- Q: How much do pretrained embeddings contribute—report results without them and compare Brown vs WSJ domain effects?
- Q: What proportion of predicates are unseen at test time and how often does the “01” default cause errors across datasets?
- Suggest performing error analysis by arc type (core vs adjunct), long-distance attachments, and reentrancies to clarify strengths and failure modes.
- Suggest adding character-level or subword embeddings to help morphologically rich languages.
- Recommend reporting multiple random seeds and confidence intervals to strengthen empirical claims and reproducibility.
