Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents a greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that builds on Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system but replaces expert-crafted features and beam search with stack LSTM representations of the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history). It introduces a small extension to handle semantic self-dependencies (M-SELF).
- The model achieves strong empirical performance: it outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, delivers competitive multilingual results, and is substantially faster than strong pipelined SRL systems. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) quantify the mutual benefits of joint learning and the cost of dropping syntax.
- The approach demonstrates that learned state representations can obviate expensive syntax-derived features traditionally used for SRL, while keeping inference greedy and linear-time.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, greedy, fully neural joint dependency parser that learns to represent the whole algorithmic state and outperforms earlier joint models while being fast.
2) An empirical study showing when and how joint modeling helps (vs. semantic-only and hybrid), with multilingual evidence.
3) A transition-system refinement (M-SELF) and careful interleaving of syntactic/semantic decisions enabling efficient, unified decoding.

Strengths
- Clear methodological advance in joint modeling: Using stack LSTMs to summarize the full joint state is a compelling alternative to hand-engineered features. The design is principled, well-motivated, and integrates syntactic and semantic composition functions with action-history conditioning.
- Strong empirical results against relevant baselines: The model consistently surpasses prior joint transition-based systems (Henderson et al. and follow-ups). For CoNLL-2009 English, the joint model is on par with top shared-task systems, despite using greedy inference and no heavy feature engineering.
- Speed and simplicity: The parser is fast (∼178s on CoNLL-2009 En test on a single core) and memory-light, outperforming a strong publicly available SRL pipeline in runtime. This makes it attractive for practical use.
- Informative ablations and variants: The semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid setups isolate the contribution of joint learning, confirm that syntax helps SRL, and show that semantics does not degrade syntax. The out-of-domain behavior (Hybrid outperforming Joint on Brown) is a useful datapoint.
- Multilingual generality: Competitive results across multiple CoNLL-2009 languages with minimal language-specific engineering, and a candid discussion about the role of pretrained embeddings and morphology.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond adaptation: The core ingredients (stack LSTMs for transition parsing; Henderson et al.’s joint transition system) are known. The main novelty is applying them jointly with a few task-specific tweaks (interleaving policy, M-SELF). This is a strong engineering contribution but methodologically incremental.
- Greedy training/inference without exploration: The model uses static oracles and locally normalized decisions with greedy decoding. No dynamic oracle, scheduled sampling, or global training is attempted. This may contribute to overfitting in-domain (Joint vs. Hybrid on Brown) and could limit ceiling performance.
- Oracle/upper-bound mismatch not quantified: The paper notes that 1.5% of English sentences (CoNLL-2009) cannot be exactly encoded by the transition sequence due to multiple crossing arcs, and that pseudo-projectivization is needed for syntax. The impact of these oracle imperfections on attainable accuracy is not analyzed (e.g., upper-bound performance, error categories).
- Comparative fairness and significance: While the paper compares against published numbers, statistical significance of gains over prior joint systems isn’t reported. The runtime comparison to Lei et al. (2015) is useful but may mix hardware/software stacks; more detail on implementation and timing protocol would strengthen the claim.
- Limited analysis: The paper would benefit from deeper error analysis (e.g., where semantics helps/hurts syntax, role-confusion patterns, long-distance attachments), ablations on architectural choices (action history A, copying vs. moving buffer items, interleaving policy), and the effect of pretrained embeddings and dropout. Multilingual results omit morphology/character embeddings, which could materially change rankings.

Questions to authors
- Training/inference: Did you try dynamic oracles, exploration-based training, or globally normalized objectives? If so, how did they affect accuracy vs. speed? If not, do you expect them to mitigate the observed out-of-domain drop for Joint?
- Oracle limitations: Can you quantify the upper bound imposed by imperfect semantic oracle conversion across languages (not just English), and its observed impact on SRL F1? Similarly, what is the effect of pseudo-projectivization on LAS vs. truly nonprojective decoding?
- Interleaving policy: How sensitive are results to the chosen interleaving (syntax makes a buffer-copying move, then semantics until M-SHIFT)? Did you compare alternative schedules (e.g., alternating fixed steps, or joint action sets with a unified classifier)?
- Architectural ablations: What is the contribution of the action-history LSTM A, the composition functions (vs. bagging children), and the copying vs. moving modification to arc-eager? Any results with beam search to probe headroom?
- Robustness and variance: What is the variance across random seeds for key settings? Are the reported scores averages or single runs?

Additional comments
- Including character-level or morphological features seems a straightforward extension likely to improve non-English performance; even a small experiment or discussion of expected gains would be helpful.
- It would be useful to release code upon submission to facilitate reproducibility of timing and accuracy.
- Minor: clarify handling of unseen predicates and the 91.22% “01” default—does this hold across languages, and how sensitive is overall SRL performance to this heuristic?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed engineering paper: a fast, effective joint parser with learned state representations and thorough baseline comparisons. The conceptual novelty is moderate but the empirical and practical value is high.
