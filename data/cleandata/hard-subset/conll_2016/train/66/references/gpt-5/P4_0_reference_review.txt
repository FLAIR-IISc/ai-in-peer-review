Summary
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint parser for syntactic dependency parsing and semantic role labeling (SRL). Building on Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system, it replaces hand-crafted features and beam search with learned vector representations of the full parser state using stack LSTMs. The model maintains stack LSTMs for the syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history; composition functions build vector representations for partially built structures. It introduces an M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies and modifies arc-eager operations to copy rather than move buffer items to enable interleaved syntax/semantics decisions. Training uses oracle transition sequences with cross-entropy and dropout; pretrained “structured skip-gram” embeddings are incorporated. Experiments on CoNLL-2008/2009 (English and multilingual) show the approach outperforms prior joint models and is competitive with top pipeline systems while being substantially faster. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) indicate syntax benefits semantics (in-domain), whereas hybrid can generalize better out-of-domain.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A transition-based joint parsing architecture that learns representations of the entire joint parser state with stack LSTMs and performs fully greedy inference, removing expert-crafted features and beam search.
2) Extensions to the joint transition system (copy semantics for arc-eager, M-SELF, interleaving constraints) enabling efficient construction of joint syntactic trees and semantic graphs with crossing arcs.
3) Empirical evidence across CoNLL-2008/2009 (including multilingual) that the approach surpasses previous joint models and offers favorable speed-accuracy trade-offs; ablations quantify the contribution of syntax to SRL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements over prior joint systems: On CoNLL-2008 and 2009 (English), the method consistently outperforms Henderson et al. and Gesmundo et al., isolating the benefit of full-state representation learning over locally scoped features. The gains are replicated across ablation settings, strengthening the evidence.
- Competitive accuracy with substantially improved efficiency: The end-to-end runtime on CoNLL-2009 English is ~2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline (Lei et al., 2015) on the same hardware, while maintaining comparable semantic F1 to several top participants. The linear-time greedy decoding and compact model are practically attractive.
- Clear, complete technical specification: The transition system (including constraints), stack-LSTM architecture, composition functions, training objective, and hyperparameters are described with enough detail to reproduce. Design choices (copy vs move, interleaving, pseudo-projectivization, M-SWAP) are motivated by the properties of joint syntax–semantics structures.
- Meaningful ablations and cross-lingual evaluation: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint variants clarify where improvements originate (syntax helps semantics in-domain; joint can overfit vs hybrid OOD). Multilingual results are broadly on par with top systems, showing adaptability beyond English.
- Practical handling of predicate sense disambiguation and OOV predicates: The integrated M-PRED and default sense fallback provide a simple, effective solution that reduces reliance on external modules (for CoNLL-2009 where predicates are given).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior components: The transition framework is closely based on Henderson et al., and the stack-LSTM state representation follows Dyer et al. (2015). The main novelty is their integration for joint parsing plus the M-SELF operation and copy/interleaving scheme. The conceptual contribution is thus incremental rather than fundamentally new.
- Imperfect oracle and representational mismatch: The authors note ~1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded as transition sequences due to crossing arcs. The impact on learning and evaluation is not quantified (e.g., error categories, whether these cases disproportionately affect certain labels).
- Out-of-domain generalization: The hybrid model surpasses joint on Brown, suggesting the joint learner may overfit to WSJ. There is no reported regularization or curriculum specifically targeting OOD robustness (e.g., domain-adaptive dropout, data augmentation, or representation calibration), and no analysis of which phenomena degrade.
- Ablation granularity: While variants are reported, finer ablations are missing to isolate the contributions of (a) copying vs moving in syntactic transitions, (b) action history stack, (c) pretrained embeddings, and (d) interleaving schedule. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to specific architectural choices beyond “full-state representation.”
- Feature/resource comparability: Some multilingual gains rely on pretrained embeddings, and morphology is not modeled; comparisons to systems with rich morphological features are therefore not perfectly controlled. The predicate “01” default is justified by type-level statistics; token-level impact is not provided.

Questions to Authors
1) Oracle limitations: What fraction of semantic arcs or sentences are incorrectly encoded in practice across languages, and how does this affect training loss and test performance per label? Have you tried alternative transitions to reduce oracle errors?
2) Interleaving and copying: How sensitive are results to the specific interleaving policy (switching after S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT until M-SHIFT)? If syntactic transitions moved (not copied) buffer items, what accuracy change do you observe?
3) Ablations on representation components: What is the effect of removing the action-history LSTM, disabling pretrained embeddings, or shrinking the composition functions? Please provide delta performance to better localize gains.
4) OOD behavior: Which SRL labels or dependency configurations account for the joint model’s degradation on Brown relative to hybrid? Any evidence that stronger regularization or smaller LSTMs mitigate overfitting?
5) Predicate sense fallback: What is the token-level accuracy when defaulting to “01,” and how often does this fallback trigger in CoNLL-2009 test sets?
6) Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative or quantitative analysis of common SRL errors (e.g., long-distance arguments, adjuncts, nominal predicates) and whether M-SELF reduces errors for NomBank cases?
7) Multilingual specifics: For languages with rich morphology (e.g., Czech), how much would character or morpheme embeddings help? Have you experimented with such inputs?
8) Efficiency details: Please report training time to convergence and average actions per sentence to contextualize the runtime figures.
