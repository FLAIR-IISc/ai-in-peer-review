Summary and contributions
- The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire parser state with stack LSTMs. It interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions, adds a new M-SELF transition to handle nominal self-arguments, and dispenses with hand-crafted features.
- Empirically, it surpasses prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, delivers competitive multilingual results, and is substantially faster than strong pipelines. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) quantify the contribution of joint learning and of syntax to SRL.

Main contributions as I see them
1) A practical, fast, feature-free joint parser that encodes full algorithmic state with stack LSTMs and uses greedy inference; this demonstrably improves over prior joint transition-based approaches.
2) An effective training and inference design (interleaved transitions, composition functions, M-SELF) that makes joint parsing feasible without beam/global optimization.
3) Comprehensive experiments across CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual), with informative ablations and runtime comparisons.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over prior joint models: The method consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on English CoNLL 2008/2009, supporting the claim that learning representations of the entire parser state is beneficial versus localized feature scopes. The ablations further show that jointly modeling syntax and semantics improves in-domain SRL over semantics-only and a hybrid pipeline.
- Practicality and speed: The parser is very fast (≈178s for CoNLL 2009 English on a single core) and low-memory, substantially faster than a strong SRL pipeline (Lei et al., 2015). This matters for large-scale or latency-sensitive applications, and aligns with the design goal of avoiding expensive feature extraction.
- Methodological clarity and completeness: The transition system and constraints are clearly specified; the interleaving protocol is sensible; the composition functions for syntax, semantics, and predicate senses are explicit. The addition of M-SELF closes an empirical gap (NomBank self-arguments) and makes the system better aligned to CoNLL 2009 phenomena.
- Breadth of evaluation: Results span CoNLL 2008 (including predicate identification), CoNLL 2009 English, and multiple languages. The multilingual results are broadly competitive with top shared-task systems, showing adaptability of the approach. Hyperparameters and training details are well documented.
- Ablations and comparisons: The syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants isolate the impact of joint learning and syntax. The hybrid vs joint comparison is especially informative (joint better in-domain; hybrid better OOD), highlighting a realistic generalization trade-off.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The core architectural idea (stack LSTMs) is inherited from Dyer et al. (2015), and the transition inventory/interleaving from Henderson et al. (2013). The main novelty is their combination for joint parsing plus the M-SELF transition and fully greedy inference. While practically valuable, the conceptual advance is moderate.
- Greedy inference and limited training signal: The model uses local, greedy decisions without beam search, global normalization, or exploration/dynamic oracles. The OOD drop (hybrid > joint on Brown) suggests overfitting to in-domain transition distributions. It would strengthen the work to test a small beam or globally normalized training, or at least to discuss why greedy works as well as it does and where it fails.
- Oracle/upper bound mismatch: The paper notes the semantic oracle is imperfect (e.g., 1.5% of English CoNLL 2009 sentences cannot be exactly encoded), and pseudo-projectivization for syntax. This introduces an upper bound below 100% and training noise. The impact on final metrics is not quantified; without this, it’s hard to know how much headroom is left.
- Limited analysis: There is little error analysis (e.g., by arc length, crossing vs non-crossing, label-wise F1, predicate sense vs argument labeling contributions) or robustness analysis (sentence length, domain). No ablation on pretrained embeddings, M-SELF, or pseudo-projectivization is provided, and no variance across random restarts is reported.
- Comparability and features: While the paper is clear that it eschews feature-rich pipelines, the comparisons to the strongest SRL systems (e.g., global DP-based or reranked) show a gap remains. The model also does not incorporate morphology, which likely hurts in languages like Czech; character-based or morphological features could be straightforward to add and might meaningfully improve multilingual performance.

Questions to authors
- Oracle/upper bound: What fraction of gold graphs are unencodable for CoNLL 2008 and for each 2009 language? What is the empirical performance loss (or an upper bound estimate) attributable to the imperfect oracle?
- Pretrained embeddings: How much do pretrained embeddings contribute on each language (especially Chinese)? Please report an ablation without pretraining.
- Stability: What is the variance across random seeds (mean±std over multiple runs) for key metrics? Are the reported differences statistically significant?
- Greedy vs small beam/global: Did you try a small beam, global normalization, or dynamic oracle training? How does speed/accuracy trade off?
- Predicate handling: For unseen predicates defaulting to “01,” how often is this invoked at test time, and what is the error contribution from predicate sense disambiguation vs argument labeling?

Assessment against reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. A thoughtful integration of known components with small but useful additions (M-SELF, fully greedy state encoding).
- Quality: Good. Sound method, appropriate baselines, solid results, and clear ablations; some missing analyses and oracle quantification.
- Clarity: Good. Transitions, architecture, and training are well explained; supplemental example helps.
- Significance: Good. Advances the state of joint parsing relative to prior joint systems and offers a practical, fast alternative to pipelines.
- Reproducibility: Reasonable. Hyperparameters and data are specified; code promised upon publication; reporting of variance and oracle impact would improve this.

Overall, the paper meets the criteria well on quality, clarity, and practical significance, with moderate novelty. I lean positive, especially for CoNLL’s audience interested in joint models and efficient parsing.
