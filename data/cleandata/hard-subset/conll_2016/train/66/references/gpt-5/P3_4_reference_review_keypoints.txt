- Proposes a fast, fully greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that encodes full parser state with four stack LSTMs and scores actions with a linear layer.
- Introduces a concrete synchronized transition system with practical changes: copying buffer items for syntax, a new M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments, and interleaving rules to constrain semantic actions.
- Shows empirical improvements over prior joint transition-based models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, competitive multilingual results, and strong runtime efficiency.
- Strength: clean integration of Henderson-style joint transitions with Dyer-style stack-LSTM state representations, removing reliance on hand-crafted path features.
- Strength: controlled variants (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) demonstrate that joint training yields in-domain gains and the model outperforms earlier joint systems.
- Strength: greedy decoder with compact neural features achieves large runtime improvements versus pipeline approaches while preserving accuracy.
- Strength: reasonable multilingual generalization without language-specific engineering, with clear benefit from pretrained embeddings (notably Chinese).
- Strength: coherent, implementable modeling choices (S/M/B/A LSTMs, recursive composition functions, transition constraints) and adequate training details with promised code release.
- Weakness: methodological novelty is modest — main ideas (stack LSTMs, Henderson-style transitions) are known; contributions are largely practical/engineering.
- Weakness: greedy decoding is vulnerable to exposure and label bias; no comparisons to beam search, global normalization, or dynamic oracles are provided.
- Weakness: the semantic oracle is imperfect (cannot encode ~1.5% of gold graphs) but the paper does not quantify its impact on training or test errors.
- Weakness: analysis is limited — missing per-component ablations, dependency-length/label/crossing-arc analyses, pretrained-embedding effects across languages, and qualitative error analysis.
- Weakness: comparisons omit pipeline hybrids (e.g., feeding strong external parses into this model) and ignore morphology/character modeling that may hurt some languages.
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the interleaving schedule and did the authors try allowing multiple semantic actions before returning to syntax or learning switching?
- Question: what is the quantitative impact of the imperfect semantic oracle — dev/test scores for encodable vs non-encodable graphs and characterization of failure cases?
- Question: did the authors try beam search, globally normalized training, or dynamic oracles to mitigate greedy search errors?
- Question: please provide ablations removing the action-history LSTM, predicate-sense composition gd, pretrained embeddings, and recursive composition.
- Question: for unseen predicates defaulting to “01,” what is the token-level accuracy of that heuristic and its effect on out-of-domain performance?
- Question: can the authors report SRL F1 by arc length, label type (core vs adjunct), and presence of crossing arcs to support claims about recovered path information?
- Overall: a well-executed, timely engineering contribution with modest novelty; empirical and practical impact is real and the paper is lean-accept-worthy if it adds search/training analyses, deeper oracle/error analysis, or ensures reproducibility via code.
