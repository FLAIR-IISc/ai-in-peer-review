- Presents a greedy joint syntactic+semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to encode full parser state (S, M, B, action history).  
- Uses a transition system based on Henderson et al. with an interleaving schedule and a new M-SELF action for nominal self-arguments.  
- Removes hand-crafted features, relying on learned representations and pretrained structured skip-gram embeddings; trains with cross-entropy over allowable transitions.  
- Empirically outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive with pipeline SOTAs while being substantially faster.  
- Ablation experiments (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) quantify the contribution of joint prediction and show Joint > Hybrid in-domain but Hybrid > Joint OOD.  
- Main contribution: a fast, feature-free, greedy joint parser that learns full parser-state representations via stack LSTMs and achieves best-reported results among prior joint models.  
- Methodological contribution: practical transition system and neural composition scheme for joint syntax–semantics, including buffer sharing and M-SELF.  
- Empirical claim: full-state neural representations can replace expensive global syntactic features in SRL, giving competitive accuracy with much lower runtime.  
- Strength: broad and significant empirical results, including multilingual portability and notable runtime improvements (~2.5x vs a strong pipeline).  
- Strength: technically sound and clearly described integration of stack LSTMs, composition functions, and interleaving protocol.  
- Strength: thorough ablations, runtime/memory reporting, and reproducibility details with promised open-source code.  
- Strength: practical for real-time or large-scale use due to greedy linear-time decoding and small memory footprint.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—mainly applying existing stack-LSTM machinery to a known joint transition system with small extensions.  
- Weakness: greedy inference leads to OOD generalization issues (overfits WSJ vs Brown); no exploration of small-beam or globally normalized training to mitigate search errors.  
- Weakness: no quantified oracle/upper-bound for LAS/SRL given transition constraints, leaving unclear how much error is due to transitions vs. model.  
- Weakness: lack of statistical significance testing and limited analysis of the impact of pretrained embeddings or run-to-run variance (except for Chinese).  
- Weakness: limited error analysis—no detailed decomposition by predicate sense vs argument attachment, role confusions, long-distance/crossing arcs, or morphology effects in multilingual settings.  
- Questions for authors: what are oracle upper bounds (LAS, SRL F1) under your transition system and how often gold cannot be encoded?  
- Questions for authors: did you try small-beam decoding or globally normalized training, and does it improve Brown performance without large speed loss?  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive are results to pretrained embeddings and random seeds (English delta without pretrained vectors)?  
- Questions for authors: could joint training incorporate predicate identification (vs separate BiLSTM classifier) and what were results if tried?  
- Questions for authors: how were hyperparameters tuned across languages—reused or re-tuned per language—and any dev-set overfitting risk?  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed empirical paper with moderate novelty but valuable practical gains; recommend acceptance with suggested analyses (oracle bounds, lightweight global search, deeper error/embedding sensitivity analysis).
