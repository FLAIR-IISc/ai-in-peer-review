Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a greedy, transition-based joint parser for syntactic dependencies and semantic role labeling (SRL) that encodes the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs over the syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history. It dispenses with expert-crafted features and beam search.
- It introduces a small extension to the semantic transition system (M-SELF) to handle CoNLL 2009 self-arguments, and interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer with constraints that ensure valid derivations.
- Empirically, the model outperforms prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, is competitive across languages, and is fast; ablations examine syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid setups.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Architectural contribution: adapting stack LSTMs to encode full joint parsing state (syntax+semantics) with interleaved transitions and a unified scoring function, eliminating expert features and beam search.
2) Empirical contribution: strong results against prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual performance with substantial speed gains.
3) Engineering contribution: a practical, fast, end-to-end greedy system, including a minor but pragmatic transition (M-SELF) to support NomBank self-arguments.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling: The paper cleanly extends the stack LSTM paradigm from pure syntax to joint syntactic–semantic parsing, representing the entire state (stacks, buffer, action history) rather than top-k handcrafted features. This offers a compelling demonstration that learned representations can replace expensive feature engineering in joint parsing.
- Strong empirical gains vs prior joint systems: The model consistently improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and related joint approaches on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. The ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) are thoughtful and show that (a) semantics benefits from syntax, (b) joint training helps in-domain, and (c) even the syntax-only variant is competitive with prior neural transition parsers.
- Speed and practicality: A single-core runtime of ~178s on CoNLL 2009 English test is a meaningful advantage over strong pipelines (e.g., Lei et al., ~440s), reinforcing the value of greedy inference for real-time or large-scale settings without large quality loss.
- Careful transition design and constraints: The shared buffer with copy semantics, interleaving scheme, and constraints are well thought out to allow semantic reentrancies and crossing arcs (M-SWAP), and the M-SELF action pragmatically handles frequent NomBank self-arguments.
- Multilingual applicability: Results across several CoNLL 2009 languages are competitive with top systems, indicating the approach is not English-specific. The discussion about Chinese performance and pretrained embeddings is candid and informative.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of generalization and overfitting: The Joint model underperforms the Hybrid variant out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting overfitting to WSJ. The paper acknowledges this but stops short of analyzing why (e.g., reliance on lexicalized action histories, predicate-sense defaults, or pretrained embeddings) or offering mitigation (regularization choices, domain-adversarial training, data augmentation).
- Oracle/upper-bound limitations not quantified in impact: The semantic oracle is imperfect (multiple crossings), and ~1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the transition sequence. The paper does not quantify how much this caps the achievable performance of the greedy model or affects error patterns. An upper-bound analysis or exclusion study would clarify the ceiling.
- Ablations could go deeper: We see syntax-only/semantics-only/hybrid/joint, but key architectural choices are not ablated: effect of beam search (even small), role of action-history LSTM, impact of pretrained vs learned embeddings, contribution of M-SELF and M-SWAP, and the copy vs move tweak in arc-eager. Such ablations would strengthen the claim that full-state representation is the primary driver of gains.
- Predicate sense handling for unseen predicates is heuristic: Defaulting to “01” is practical but may bias in-domain results and hurt out-of-domain. There is no analysis of its frequency or error contribution, nor a comparison to a lightweight sense predictor conditioned on context.
- Reproducibility and hyperparameter tuning: While many details are provided and code is promised, several hyperparameters are “intuitively set,” optimizer is plain SGD with decay, and there is no discussion of variance across runs. Given known instability in neural transition parsing, reporting mean/std over multiple seeds and a tuning protocol would increase confidence.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the oracle gap? What is the labeled/unlabeled upper bound imposed by the transition encoding errors on CoNLL 2009 English? How often are M-SWAP and M-SELF used, and what fraction of gold arcs require them?
- How sensitive is performance to beam size? Even a small beam (e.g., 4–8) may reduce the Brown gap. Did you try global training (e.g., max-margin, dynamic oracles) to mitigate exposure bias?
- What is the contribution of each stack LSTM component? If you remove A (action history) or restrict st/mt/bt to top-k elements instead of full summaries, how does accuracy change?
- How much do pretrained embeddings contribute? Please report results without pretrained embeddings for English (as you note for Chinese) and on Brown vs WSJ to assess domain effects.
- Predicate sense default: What proportion of test-time predicates are unseen? What is the error rate attributable to the “01” default on Brown and on other languages?

Additional comments
- Consider error analysis by arc type (core vs adjunct roles), long-distance attachments, and reentrancies to illuminate where joint modeling helps or fails.
- Given multilingual results and lack of morphology, a character-level or subword embedding variant may be a simple way to boost highly inflected languages.
- Reporting multiple random seeds and confidence intervals would strengthen the empirical claims.
