Summary
The paper presents a greedy, transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns continuous representations of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. The system follows and extends the synchronized transition framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), interleaving syntactic and semantic operations over a shared buffer, introducing a new M-SELF transition for semantic self-dependencies, and integrating predicate sense disambiguation within the parser. The model eschews hand-crafted features, relying instead on learned representations (including pretrained word embeddings) and achieves linear-time decoding. Experiments on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English and multilingual) show improvements over prior joint models, with competitive speed and accuracy.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A joint syntax–semantics transition system with stack LSTM-based representations of the full algorithmic state, enabling purely greedy decoding without hand-crafted features, including architectural choices (buffer-copying for semantic access, interleaving constraints) and a new M-SELF transition for self-arguments.

2) Empirical validation that this approach outperforms previous joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is competitive on CoNLL 2009 multilingual, while being substantially faster than strong pipelined SRL systems.

3) Controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that evidence the utility of joint modeling and the learned representations.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over prior joint models: On both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English), the joint model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009), supporting the claim that learning representations of the full parser state is beneficial relative to localized, feature-driven state encodings. The paper also shows competitive multilingual results.

- Strong speed–accuracy tradeoff: The greedy decoder achieves an end-to-end runtime of ~178s on CoNLL 2009 English (single CPU core), approximately 2.5x faster than a competitive SRL pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) while remaining within the top tier of accuracy for joint settings. Linear asymptotic complexity and reported wall-clock measurements underpin the practicality claim.

- Methodological simplicity without expert features: The model removes dependence on expensive syntactic path features typical in SRL, yet attains comparable performance. The use of stack LSTMs to summarize parser stacks and action history is a coherent, well-motivated extension of prior work in syntactic parsing to the joint setting.

- Well-specified training and setup: The paper details transitions, constraints, composition functions, optimization (dropout, SGD), hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, pseudo-projectivization, and evaluation protocols aligned with the shared tasks (predicted POS/lemmas). The inclusion of syntax-only/semantics-only/hybrid variants is informative.

- Targeted architectural innovations: The buffer-copying modification to arc-eager enables synchronized semantics; the interleaving policy ensures both structures are produced; the M-SELF transition addresses NomBank self-arguments. These are technically sound and practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Accuracy still behind strongest pipelined/global systems: While competitive among joint models, the system trails state-of-the-art SRL pipelines with global inference or richer features (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015). The paper argues orthogonality but lacks experiments integrating even light-weight global constraints or reranking to quantify potential gains.

- Limited analysis of oracle and decoding effects: The semantic oracle is imperfect (e.g., 1.5% of sentences in CoNLL 2009 EN cannot be exactly encoded), but the impact on training and evaluation is not quantified (e.g., how often gold transitions are unrepresentable, how that affects loss or error patterns). Similarly, no comparison to beam search or dynamic oracles is provided to assess the cost of greedy decoding in terms of error propagation.

- Ablation depth: Although variants (syntax-only/semantics-only/hybrid) are informative, the contributions of key components are not isolated. Missing ablations include: effect of pretrained embeddings vs learned-only; role of the action-history LSTM; impact of composition functions vs simpler pooling; contribution of the M-SELF transition; effect of pseudo-projectivization; and the interleaving constraint vs alternative scheduling. Such ablations would strengthen claims about what drives the improvements.

- Out-of-domain generalization: The hybrid model outperforming joint on the Brown set suggests overfitting of the joint model to WSJ. The paper notes this but provides no mitigation (e.g., regularization tuning, domain-adaptive training, data augmentation) or analysis of which components overfit.

- Multilingual setup gaps: The model omits morphological features (not uncommon), but for highly inflected languages this likely limits performance. The paper notes potential remedies (morphology or character-level embeddings) but does not evaluate them. For Chinese, the reliance on pretrained embeddings is asserted as critical, yet quantitative comparisons without them are not shown.

Questions to authors
- Oracle effects: What fraction of training instances across languages contain semantic structures that cannot be exactly encoded by your transition set? Can you report performance when excluding such sentences or with an alternative oracle? How sensitive is training to these oracle mismatches?

- Greedy vs beam: Did you try small beams or dynamic oracles? What is the speed–accuracy tradeoff, and does a beam close the remaining gap to pipelined/global systems?

- Component ablations: Can you provide ablations for pretrained embeddings, action-history LSTM, and composition functions? What is the contribution of M-SELF, and how frequent are self-arguments across languages?

- Interleaving policy: Have you explored alternative interleaving strategies (e.g., semantics-first for certain tokens) or learned scheduling? Does the copy vs move modification materially affect performance?

- Statistical significance: Are the reported improvements over prior joint systems statistically significant on standard test sets? Please include significance testing where applicable.

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound, with novel and practical extensions of stack LSTMs to joint parsing, and strong empirical results among joint models. Further analysis and modest extensions (beam/global constraints, ablations, oracle study) would solidify the contributions.
