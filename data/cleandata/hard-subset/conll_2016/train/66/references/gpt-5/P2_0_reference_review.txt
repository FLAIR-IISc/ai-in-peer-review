Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents a fast, greedy joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history), adapting the Henderson et al. (2008/2013) synchronized transition system and Dyer et al. (2015) stack-LSTM machinery.
- Main empirical claims: (i) improves over prior joint models (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English; (ii) competitive multilingual results on CoNLL 2009; (iii) substantially faster than strong pipeline SRL systems. The paper also studies variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) to quantify the utility of joint learning.
- Additional technical contributions: a new M-SELF transition to handle NomBank self-arguments; an interleaving schedule and a copy-based variant of arc-eager to keep buffer items visible to both syntax and semantics.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advance over prior joint models with a simple and fast approach. The paper reports consistent gains over Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009) on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English, which directly targets the paper’s stated goal (strong joint parsing without hand-crafted features). This addresses a well-known weakness of earlier joint systems (complex search, brittle feature engineering).
- Strong engineering for speed with reasonable accuracy. The greedy, single-core runtime (≈178s on CoNLL 2009 English test) is compelling. The speedups over a representative pipeline SRL baseline (Lei et al., 2015) are meaningful for practical use, and the design choices (copy vs move, interleaving, stack LSTM summaries) are well justified by the need to keep both structures available during action selection.
- Thoughtful experimental design and comparisons. The inclusion of syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants cleanly diagnoses where joint learning helps. Findings are plausible: joint beats hybrid in-domain (mutual benefit), while hybrid does better OOD (less overfitting), and semantics-only lags, reinforcing the value of syntax for SRL.
- Solid multilingual evaluation. Results are competitive with top CoNLL 2009 systems across several languages, with a sensible discussion of where the model depends on pretrained embeddings and where limited data (Japanese) hurts, demonstrating some robustness and generality.
- Methodological soundness and clarity. The transition system, constraints, stack-LSTM architecture, and composition functions are clearly specified, with equations and data structures spelled out. Training details (objective, optimization, dropout, hyperparameters) are sufficient to reimplement, and an open-source release is promised.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to combining known components. The core idea is a natural synthesis of Henderson-style joint transitions with Dyer’s stack LSTMs and standard greedy transition-based parsing. While effective, the methodological novelty is incremental; the new M-SELF transition and copy-based arc-eager tweak are small, task-specific additions.
- Greedy inference may cap performance; no exploration of decoding alternatives. The paper explicitly opts for greedy decoding, but does not test small beams or dynamic oracles to quantify headroom. Given that global search and exact inference have yielded gains in SRL, even a brief ablation on beam size would strengthen the case that the speed-accuracy tradeoff is favorable.
- Evaluation gaps and statistical rigor. The text states “significant” improvements, but no significance testing or confidence intervals are reported. It would be helpful to quantify the gains over prior joint systems with tests and to report variance over multiple training runs, especially given stochastic training and dropout.
- Oracle mismatch and unreachable gold states are acknowledged but not analyzed. The authors note that 1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded by the transition system. It would be useful to quantify the impact on training (e.g., how are losses computed when gold is unreachable?) and whether errors cluster around specific SRL phenomena (crossing arcs, control, nominal predicates).
- Comparisons to SOTA are limited by feature differences; little attempt to bridge the gap. While the paper carefully positions itself against feature-rich pipelines and exact inference systems, it could include a stronger hybrid baseline that feeds full predicted syntactic parses (not just stack/buffer partials) to the semantic parser to isolate the benefit of joint learning versus feature availability. Similarly, a morphology-aware variant for highly inflected languages would strengthen the multilingual story.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to pretrained embeddings? Can you report results without pretrained embeddings for English and quantify the drop? For Chinese you note a strong dependency; is the same true in English/German/Spanish?
- Did you attempt beam search (even size 4–8) or dynamic oracles? If so, what was the speed/accuracy tradeoff?
- For the 1.5% of sentences whose gold transitions are unreachable, how is training handled? Do you terminate the sequence early, or do you use a heuristic repair? What fraction of SRL errors at test time are of the same crossing-arc types?
- How much does the copy-versus-move modification of arc-eager contribute? Can you provide an ablation that reverts to standard arc-eager and quantifies the loss on SRL?
- Can you provide variance across multiple runs (different random seeds) and, if feasible, significance testing versus the strongest prior joint baselines?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed paper that advances joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing with a simple, fast neural transition-based model and produces clear gains over prior joint approaches. While novelty is moderate, the empirical contribution, speed, and clarity make it a good fit for CoNLL. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the evaluation and analysis questions above.
