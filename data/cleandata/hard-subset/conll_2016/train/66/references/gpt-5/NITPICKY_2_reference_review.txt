Summary and claimed contributions
- A greedy joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that encodes the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, action history) with stack LSTMs, avoiding hand-crafted features and beam search.
- A synchronized transition system adapted from arc-eager plus semantic actions, including a new M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies, and an interleaving schedule between syntax and semantics using a shared buffer with “copy” operations.
- Empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual) showing improvements over prior joint models, competitive semantic performance vs pipeline systems, and strong speed.

Strengths
- Clear engineering of a simple, fast, greedy joint model. The stack-LSTM encoding of the entire state is elegant, removes feature engineering, and yields substantial speed-ups (claimed ~2.5x vs a strong pipeline).
- Strong results against prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al. 2008/2013; Gesmundo et al. 2009). The ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) are informative and align with expectations about the benefits of syntax for SRL.
- The transition inventory is carefully designed for joint dependency outputs, including M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments and M-SWAP for crossing semantic arcs. The interleaving protocol is well-specified.
- Multilingual evaluation (German, Spanish, Chinese, Japanese, Czech) indicates adaptability without language-specific features; pretrained embeddings help in low-resource/non-English settings.
- The paper clearly positions itself relative to state-of-the-art SRL systems using global decoding and heavy feature engineering, framing orthogonality rather than claiming to beat them.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond Dyer et al. (2015). The main technical idea—stack LSTMs to summarize parser state—comes directly from Dyer et al.; here it is applied to joint parsing with a largely inherited transition system (Henderson et al., arc-eager). The interleaving schedule (switching at S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT and M-SHIFT) and buffer-copy tweak are heuristic; there is no principled justification, analysis of alternatives, or learning of the interleaving policy. The new M-SELF action is a minor extension.
- Oracle, soundness, and completeness issues are under-specified. The semantic oracle is imperfect “due to multiple crossing arcs,” and 1.5% of English sentences cannot be correctly encoded. How are such cases handled in training (discarded? approximated?), and what is the effect on accuracy? Is the joint transition system sound and complete for the CoNLL formalisms under the proposed constraints (duplicate-arc prevention, anti-infinite-swap), especially with copy vs move in arc-eager? The linear-time claim references Nivre (2009), but M-SWAP and reentrancies can increase action count; a concrete complexity argument for the semantic graph case is missing.
- Evaluation and comparison details are insufficient or potentially unfair. Statistical significance is not reported; no variance across seeds is given. Speed comparisons lack precise hardware, implementation, and threading details; the comparison to Lei et al. (2015) may not be apples-to-apples (different languages, toolchains, CPU/GPU usage). For multilingual, reliance on large Gigaword embeddings gives an advantage not used by some compared systems; the contributions of embeddings vs model should be disentangled. Deprojectivization accuracy for syntax is not reported; the impact on LAS is unknown. The 2008 setting uses a separate bidirectional LSTM predicate identifier (zero-order); this undermines the “joint” claim and complicates comparability to pipeline systems that may optimize predicate identification jointly with SRL components.
- Training methodology and reproducibility are weak. Many hyperparameters are “set intuitively.” Early stopping is tied to wall clock (“6–7 hours”) rather than dev performance; this is brittle. No information about random seeds, number of epochs, batch size, gradient clipping, or learning rate schedules beyond a coarse decay. It is unclear whether pretrained embeddings are fine-tuned. Results are presented as single numbers without error bars. Code “on publication” limits reproducibility at review time.
- Methodological choices likely bias outcomes but are not analyzed. The interleaving enforces that left syntactic dependencies are resolved before left semantic dependencies; this induces a structural bias whose effect is unexamined. Constraints that forbid repeated arcs/predicates and limit swaps may prune valid derivations; trade-offs are not quantified. The semantics-only baseline discards syntax entirely; a stronger baseline would condition on predicted syntax (full tree) with learned neural features to better isolate the benefit of joint learning vs access to syntax.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues impede readability. There are stray/misplaced footnotes (1–5), garbled symbols (e.g., elementwisemax, concatenation delimiters [; ,]), inconsistent notation for labels (`, l, r), variable name collisions (A used as action history and Allowed set), missing figures/tables in the excerpt (e.g., Tables 2–4, Figures 1–5), and typos. Claims such as “asymptotically more expensive” lack precise complexity comparisons. These issues suggest the paper needs careful proofreading.

Questions to authors
- How exactly do you handle training instances whose gold joint parses are not encodable by your transition system (the 1.5% English cases and other languages)? Are they dropped, approximated, or modified? Quantify the impact.
- Provide statistical significance tests and variance across at least 3–5 random seeds for the main metrics. Also report token-level predicate sense disambiguation accuracy, especially for unseen predicates where you default to .01.
- Justify the interleaving policy. Did you try alternatives (e.g., always semantic-first for each token, or learned policies)? What is the effect on accuracy and speed?
- Provide a concrete complexity analysis including the effect of M-SWAP and reentrancies on action counts. What is the worst-case number of transitions per token?
- Clarify speed comparisons: exact hardware (CPU model, BLAS/GPU usage), implementation language, threading for both your system and Lei et al. (2015). Report decoding speed for other baselines you compare to when possible.
- Ablations: effect of (i) action-history stack A, (ii) copy vs move in arc-eager, (iii) pretrained embeddings vs random, and (iv) fine-tuning embeddings.
- For CoNLL 2008, how does predicate-identification F1 propagate to end-to-end SRL metrics? Did you try joint training with the parser?
- How do you deprojectivize syntactic trees at test time and what is the deprojectivization error? Report LAS before/after.
- For multilingual, what external data was used for embeddings per language? Did you use any morphological features or character-based models; if not, can you quantify the gap on morphologically rich languages?
- Can you provide an error analysis (by arc type, distance, reentrancy, crossing arcs, self-arguments) to substantiate where the model helps/hurts?
