Summary and Contributions
- The paper presents a fully greedy, transition-based joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that represents the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs. It dispenses with hand-crafted features and beam search.
- It introduces a practically motivated transition inventory for joint parsing, including a new M-SELF action to handle semantic self-dependencies and a copy-based arc-eager adaptation that permits synchronized syntax–semantics over a shared buffer.
- Empirically, it substantially improves over prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, is competitive multilingually, includes informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint), and is fast with low memory usage.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Learning a dense representation of the full joint parser state and committing to fully greedy inference is a notable departure from feature-engineered pipelines and constrained inference. This pushes the field toward simpler, faster, and more learnable joint models and convincingly demonstrates that expensive syntactic features/path representations can be replaced by learned state summaries.
- Clear empirical gains over prior joint models: The joint system consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and other joint baselines on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, isolating the benefit of the learned state representation. The multilingual results are broadly competitive with top systems.
- Well-designed transition system for joint parsing: The interleaving of syntax/semantics, the copy vs. move adjustment to arc-eager, and the inclusion of M-SELF and M-SWAP are carefully engineered to respect properties of dependency SRL (e.g., reentrancies, crossing arcs). This is a valuable contribution independent of the neural architecture.
- Thorough and informative ablations: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint comparisons make a strong case for joint learning on in-domain data, while quantifying trade-offs (e.g., joint overfitting vs. hybrid robustness out of domain).
- Practicality and speed: The model is lightweight, CPU-friendly, and substantially faster than strong pipeline competitors, with an appealing accuracy–speed trade-off. This increases its potential impact in real-world settings.

Weaknesses
- Gap to top SRL systems and limited global modeling: While strong for a greedy joint model, results still trail state-of-the-art SRL systems that leverage global inference, rich syntactic features, and reranking. The paper does not explore remedies (beam search, global normalization, oracles) that might narrow this gap while preserving many of the proposed benefits.
- Oracle limitations and training mismatch: The semantic oracle is imperfect in the presence of multiple crossing arcs, which creates unreachable gold states during training. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify its frequency per language or its impact on convergence and error types. This raises questions about the upper bound and the fairness of comparisons versus models with exact oracles.
- Overfitting and domain robustness: On Brown, hybrid outperforms joint, suggesting that the joint model overfits to WSJ. The paper does not analyze why the learned joint state representation fails to generalize (e.g., reliance on in-domain syntactic regularities), nor propose simple mitigations (regularization, pretraining strategies, or domain-adaptive training).
- Confounding factors in the “joint” claim for CoNLL 2008: Predicate identification is handled by a separate BiLSTM classifier; syntax is pseudo-projectivized. The contribution of the joint transition system vs. these auxiliary components is not disentangled, making it hard to attribute gains cleanly to joint modeling in this setting.
- Limited analysis: The work lacks detailed error analysis, ablations on design choices (e.g., copy vs. move, action-switch schedule), sensitivity to pretrained embeddings, and statistical significance reporting. Such analyses would strengthen the causal claims and provide guidance for future adoption.

Questions to Authors
- Oracle quality: What proportion of training sentences per language have unreachable gold transitions in the semantic oracle? Did you try loss shaping or dynamic oracles to mitigate this? How does this affect learning curves and final F1?
- Beam/global variants: Did you experiment with small beams, global normalization, or cost-augmented training? How does accuracy vs. speed trade off, and does a small beam reduce Brown-domain overfitting?
- Copy vs. move: How critical is the copy modification to arc-eager? If you revert to standard move semantics, what is the impact on SRL and speed? Any measured latency from maintaining two copies?
- Action interleaving: Did you try alternative schedules (e.g., semantics-first, or alternating at fixed intervals)? How sensitive is performance to the current constraint that left-side syntax precedes left-side semantics?
- Predicate sense handling: For unseen predicates defaulting to .01, how often is this wrong in CoNLL 2009 test data, and what is the performance impact? Would a small backoff disambiguator help?
- Domain robustness: Which error types grow most on Brown for joint vs. hybrid? Are there simple regularization or data augmentation strategies that recover the Brown drop without hurting WSJ?
- Multilingual reliance on pretraining: How much do results degrade without pretrained word vectors? Any preliminary gains from character-level or morphology-aware embeddings?

Overall evaluation
- This submission takes a bold and timely step toward feature-free, fast, and effective joint parsing by representing the entire parser state with stack LSTMs and committing to greedy inference. Despite some analysis gaps and a residual performance gap to highly engineered pipelines with global inference, the conceptual advance and strong empirical results over prior joint models merit acceptance. I recommend acceptance.
