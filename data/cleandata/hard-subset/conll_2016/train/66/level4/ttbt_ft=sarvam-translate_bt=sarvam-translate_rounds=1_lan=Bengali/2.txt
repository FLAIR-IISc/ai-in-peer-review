General Comments
================
This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008).

The authors claim two main differences:

- Vector representation is used for the state of the entire parser, rather than the configuration of the top element/last parser in the stack.
- The algorithm is a simple greedy search.

The main idea is to take advantage of stack LSTMs so that the vector representing the parser's state remembers potentially large scoped syntactic features, which are known as defining features for semantic role labeling (e.g., paths between the predicate and the candidate role-filling head).

The system has been tested on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset.

The authors compare their system's performance with previously published performance,

founding that their system is better than the 2008/2009 system, but less so than recent proposals (see Table 3 below).

However, they emphasize that the proposed system does not require any hand-crafted features,

and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well-written and describes enough work on the recently popular LSTMs applied to Henderson et al.'s algorithm,

which now seems somewhat far-fetched.

I have some reservations about the choice of the simple greedy algorithm:

It does not give comparable results with some of the work cited.

The instance beam-search performance did not require too much extra work or space.

Further Comments / Questions
==================================

Section 2:
A comment on the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would help better understand the paper's goal.

A summary of the set of transitions used in this work and the differences between Henderson et al. should be provided.

In its current form, it is difficult to say what has been reused directly from Henderson et al. and what is new/slightly modified.

Section 3.3
Why do you need the addition of the predicate word and its unambiguous meaning?

This seems redundant since the unambiguous meaning is specific to a predicate?

Section 4
The organization of sections 4.1 / 4.2 is confusing about multilingualism.

Conll 2008 focused on the English language, and CoNLL 2009 extended the widely used work to several other languages.
