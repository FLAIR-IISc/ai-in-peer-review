

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, extending Henderson et al. (2008) with two key innovations: (1) the use of vector representations for the *entire* parser state (rather than just stack top elements or recent configurations), and (2) a plain greedy decoding strategy. The core insight leverages stack LSTMs to encode long-distance syntactic dependencies—critical for semantic role labeling (e.g., predicate-head paths—into the state vector. Evaluated on CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009, the system achieves competitive results against 2008/2009 baselines but lags behind recent SOTA systems (Table 3). The authors rightly emphasize its feature-agnostic design and efficiency from greedy decoding, and the paper is well-written with significant technical effort. However, the choice of greedy search undermines comparability with several cited works, and adding beam search would have required minimal effort.  

**Major Concerns**  
The primary limitation is the greedy decoding strategy. This renders the reported results incomparable to many recent systems (e.g., those using beam search or structured prediction), which dominate the field. The authors acknowledge the system’s speed advantage but omit that beam search would likely improve performance with negligible computational overhead. Since Table 3 already compares to multiple greedy-based prior works, including beam-searched results would have strengthened the analysis and addressed a critical gap in the evaluation.  

**Specific Comments**  
*Section 2 (Task Definition)*: The distinction between A1 and C-A1 links (used in CoNLL 2008/2009) is unclear. A brief clarification of how these link types define the semantic role labeling task would aid readers in understanding the parser’s objective. Additionally, a concise comparison of the transition system with Henderson et al. (2008) is needed. The current description makes it difficult to discern which transitions are reused versus modified.  

*Section 3.3 (Predicate Representation)*: The concatenation of the predicate word with its disambiguated sense (e.g., "eat" + "eat.01") appears redundant. Since the sense tag is already specific to the predicate context, this representation adds no new information beyond what the sense tag alone conveys. The authors should justify this design choice or simplify the feature.  

*Section 4 (Multilingual Setup)*: The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 conflates language scope. CoNLL 2008 was English-only; CoNLL 2009 extended to multiple languages. The paper states the model was tested "on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 data set" (Section 4), but Section 4.1 describes English-only experiments. Clarify whether the multilingual results (Table 4) include all CoNLL 2009 languages or are restricted to English, and explicitly state how multilingual training differs from the English-only setup.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The work makes a meaningful contribution by applying stack LSTMs to a joint parser framework, demonstrating how state vectors can capture long-range dependencies relevant to semantic parsing. The approach is elegant and aligns with recent trends in neural transition systems. However, the evaluation’s lack of beam search limits its impact, and minor ambiguities in task definition and feature design weaken the presentation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.  

**Recommendation**  
**Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The paper’s core idea is sound and well-executed, but the evaluation gap (greedy vs. beam) and minor technical ambiguities require resolution before acceptance. Specifically:  
1. Add beam-searched results (even at k=5) for key baselines to enable fair comparison.  
2. Clarify the task definition (A1/C-A1) and transition system modifications in Section 2.  
3. Justify or remove the redundant predicate-sense concatenation in Section 3.3.  
4. Correct the multilingual scope description in Section 4 (e.g., "Experiments on CoNLL 2009 used the full multilingual dataset, while Section 4.1 focuses on English as a baseline").  

The paper’s technical merits and clear writing are commendable, but these revisions are essential to ensure the claims about performance and novelty are fully supported. With these changes, it would be a strong contribution to the field.
