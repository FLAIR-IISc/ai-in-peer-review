

General comments
================
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors identify two key innovations:  
- The parser state is represented using vectorial embeddings for the entire state, rather than relying solely on the top elements of the stack or the last parser configuration.  
- The algorithm employs plain greedy search.  

The core contribution leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a state vector that retains memory of potentially long-distance syntactic features—known to be critical for semantic role labeling (e.g., the dependency path between a predicate and its candidate role filler head). The system is evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset.  

The authors compare their results against previously reported performances, demonstrating competitive scores against the 2008/2009 systems but lower scores than more recent approaches (see Table 3). They emphasize that their system requires no hand-crafted features and achieves speed through its simple greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written and details substantial work, building on recent LSTM applications to the Henderson et al. framework—a strategy now recognized as visionary.  

However, I have significant reservations about the choice of plain greedy search. This choice renders the results incomparable to several cited works that use more sophisticated decoding (e.g., beam search). Including beam-searched results would have required minimal additional effort or space and would have strengthened the evaluation.  

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:  
Clarify the purpose of both A1 and C-A1 links to better define the target task. Additionally, provide a concise summary contrasting the transitions used here with those in Henderson et al. (2008). Currently, it is unclear which elements are directly reused versus modified.  

Section 3.3:  
Explain why the representation concatenates the predicate word with its disambiguated sense. This appears redundant since the disambiguated sense is inherently specific to the predicate.  

Section 4:  
The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing regarding multilingualism. CoNLL 2008 was English-only, while CoNLL 2009 extended the shared task to multiple languages. The text should reflect this distinction clearly.
