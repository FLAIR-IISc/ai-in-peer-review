- Proposes a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs for full parser state and greedy inference, claiming no expert features and linear-time parsing with competitive CoNLL 2008/2009 English results.  
- Innovation 1: Uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state rather than only top stack items, reducing need for handcrafted features.  
- Innovation 2: Employs greedy inference instead of beam search, yielding substantial speed gains.  
- Strength: Stack LSTM representation is a significant technical contribution that removes expensive expert-crafted features, useful for greedy parsing.  
- Strength: Achieves strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, outperforming previous joint models and matching some pipeline systems.  
- Strength: Greedy inference gives large speed advantages (e.g., ~177.6±18s on CoNLL 2009 English test on one core), making it practical.  
- Weakness: Using greedy inference complicates fair comparison to prior work that uses beam search; no beam-search results are provided for direct comparison.  
- Weakness: Paper does not adequately address lower performance relative to recent state-of-the-art systems that use expert features and global reranking, leaving justification for a feature-free approach weak.  
- Weakness: Some comparisons to recent work are misleading, claiming to outperform previous joint models without properly comparing to other greedy systems with different feature engineering.  
- Question: Why wasn't beam search implemented to provide a more comprehensive and directly comparable evaluation?  
- Question: For Chinese results, how much of the improvement comes from pretrained embeddings versus the model architecture?  
- Question: Why default unseen predicate senses to "01", and how does this choice impact overall performance?  
- Comment: Paper should more clearly differentiate the authors' contributions from Henderson et al. (2008) and summarize transition differences.  
- Comment: Section 3.6 on predicate sense disambiguation is partly redundant since senses are predicate-specific.  
- Comment: Multilingual results should explicitly note CoNLL 2008 is English-only while CoNLL 2009 covers multiple languages.
