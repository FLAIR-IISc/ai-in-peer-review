- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent entire algorithm state.
- Claims two main contributions: a novel transition system learning full parser-state representations, and demonstrating neural nets succeed on joint syntactic–semantic parsing where linear models struggled.
- Argues neural networks remove need for expert-crafted features by automatically learning relevant representations.
- Significant contribution to joint parsing, showing neural models can handle joint parsing without expensive feature engineering.
- Transition system is well-motivated and clearly described.
- Strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008–2009 English datasets, outperforming previous joint parsing models.
- Notable parsing speed (≈177.6±18 s on CoNLL 2009 test set on a single core) suitable for practical use.
- Weakness: paper does not explain in depth why neural nets outperform linear models beyond “automatic feature learning,” especially regarding parameter efficiency.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to top non-joint approaches that use full syntactic features (e.g., Roth & Woodsend 2014; Lei et al. 2015) which report higher semantic performance.
- Weakness: lacks a dynamic oracle for the transition system, limiting theoretical analysis and comparison with other transition-based parsers.
- Weakness: does not discuss limitations for low-resource languages or morphologically complex languages.
- Question: please elaborate on how neural network parameter efficiency (vs. linear models) drives performance gains beyond automatic feature learning.
- Question: please provide a dynamic oracle for the transition system (at least as supplementary material) to clarify theoretical limits and comparisons.
