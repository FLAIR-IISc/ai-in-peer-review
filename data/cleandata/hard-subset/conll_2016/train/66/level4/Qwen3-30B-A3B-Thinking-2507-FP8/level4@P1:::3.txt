

Summary and Main Contributions:
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, learning a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack LSTMs. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) a novel transition system that improves upon Henderson et al. (2008) by learning representations for the entire parser state rather than just top stack items, and (2) demonstrating that neural networks can successfully handle joint syntactic-semantic parsing where previous linear models struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features without expert-crafted features.

Strengths:
The paper makes a significant contribution to the joint parsing field by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively handle joint syntactic-semantic parsing without relying on expensive feature engineering. The authors' transition system is well-motivated and clearly described, and the results on CoNLL 2008-2009 English datasets are compelling, with their model outperforming all previous joint parsing models. The key insight that neural networks can learn effective representations for the entire parser state is particularly valuable for greedy parsing approaches where linguistic theory offers limited guidance for feature design. The model's speed (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) is also noteworthy for practical applications.

Weaknesses:
The paper does not adequately address why neural networks outperform linear models beyond the "automatic feature learning" argument. A more precise explanation would be valuable, particularly regarding the parameter efficiency of neural networks compared to linear models when jointly learning multiple tasks. While the authors claim their model outperforms previous joint models, they do not sufficiently compare against the best non-joint approaches that use full syntactic parse features (e.g., Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), which achieve higher performance on semantic tasks. The paper also lacks a dynamic oracle for their transition system, which would help clarify the theoretical limits of their approach and make the comparison with other transition-based parsers more meaningful. Finally, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the limitations of their model when applied to languages with limited training data or morphological complexity.

Questions to Authors:
Could you elaborate on why the neural network representation is more effective than linear models for this joint parsing task beyond the general "automatic feature learning" argument? Specifically, how does the parameter efficiency of neural networks compared to linear models contribute to their performance advantage?

Would you be able to provide a dynamic oracle for your transition system, at least as a supplementary material, to better understand the theoretical limits of your approach compared to other transition-based parsers?
