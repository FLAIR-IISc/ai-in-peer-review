

This paper timely revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint modeling represents a key advancement underlying the success of transition-based neural parsers.  

The work makes two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system that demonstrates superior performance compared to the Henderson (2008) baseline. Second, they establish that neural networks achieve success on this task where linear models previously failed. While the authors attribute this to neural networks' ability to automatically select relevant features, an additional advantage merits note: neural networks achieve greater parameter efficiency. In linear models, joint learning necessitates learning weights for every feature-class pair, leading to an exponentially larger parameter space. Neural networks circumvent this by learning feature representations implicitly, reducing the overall parameter burden.  

The transition system likely generalizes to other neural architectures, such as Andor (2016)'s global beam-search model. Further orthogonal improvements could yield state-of-the-art results. It would be valuable to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if deferred to an appendix or follow-up work. The structure appears analogous to the arc-eager oracle: the M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the buffer's initial word and the semantic stack, while M-D excludes arcs between the stack's top word and the buffer. The L and R actions each exclude only the reverse arc, with no additional exclusions.
