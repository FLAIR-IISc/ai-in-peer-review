

Summary and Contributions
The paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, building on Henderson et al. (2008) with two key innovations: (1) using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser's state rather than just top items on the stack, and (2) employing a greedy inference algorithm instead of beam search. The authors claim this approach eliminates the need for expert-crafted features while achieving competitive performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 English tasks, with the added benefit of linear time complexity.

Strengths
1. The stack LSTM representation of the entire parser state is a significant technical contribution that eliminates the need for expensive, expert-crafted features. This is particularly valuable for greedy parsing approaches where linguistic theory offers limited guidance for feature design.
2. The system achieves strong results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets, outperforming previous joint parsing models. The authors demonstrate that their approach can match the performance of pipeline systems that use more complex feature engineering.
3. The greedy inference algorithm provides substantial speed advantages (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), making the system practical for real-world applications where speed matters.

Weaknesses
1. The use of greedy inference rather than beam search creates a fundamental comparison issue with several cited works that use more sophisticated search procedures. The authors acknowledge this limitation but fail to provide beam-searched performance for direct comparison, making it difficult to assess the true impact of their representation learning approach.
2. The paper does not adequately address the performance gap with recent state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015), which use expert-crafted features derived from full syntactic parses and global reranking. While the authors correctly note these systems use orthogonal advances, they don't provide a compelling rationale for why their feature-free approach is justified when it results in lower performance.
3. The comparison to recent work is misleading in several places. For instance, the paper claims their system "outperforms all previous joint parsing models" without adequately addressing how their results compare to systems that use the same greedy approach but with different feature engineering.

Questions to Authors
1. The paper states the greedy algorithm is "linear time, including feature extraction," but fails to provide beam-searched results for comparison. Why wasn't beam search implemented to make the evaluation more comprehensive and directly comparable to other systems?
2. The authors note that their Chinese parser relies on pretrained embeddings for superior performance, but don't quantify how much of the performance difference comes from embeddings versus the model architecture. Could they clarify this distinction?
3. For predicate sense disambiguation, the paper states the system defaults to the "01" sense for unseen predicates, which is correct for 91.22% of predicates by type. Why is this sense chosen as the default, and how does this affect overall performance metrics?

Additional Comments
The paper's organization would benefit from clearer differentiation between the authors' contributions and those of Henderson et al. (2008). A brief summary of transition differences would help readers understand what's truly novel. The discussion of predicate sense disambiguation in Section 3.6 seems redundant, as the disambiguated sense is inherently specific to each predicate. The multilingual results in Section 5.3 would be clearer if the paper explicitly stated that CoNLL 2008 was English-only while CoNLL 2009 extended to multiple languages.
