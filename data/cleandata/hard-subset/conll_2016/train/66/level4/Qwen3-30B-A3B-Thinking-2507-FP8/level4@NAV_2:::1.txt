

Summary: This paper presents a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that combines Henderson et al.'s (2008, 2013) transition system with Dyer et al.'s (2015) stack LSTM architecture. The primary contribution is demonstrating that learning a unified state representation via stack LSTMs enables competitive joint parsing performance without expert-crafted features. A secondary contribution is establishing a practical linear-time greedy parsing solution (177.6±18s on CoNLL 2009 English test set) that outperforms prior joint models while avoiding the computational expense of beam search or full syntactic pipeline features.

Strengths:  
1. The parser achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks among joint models, surpassing all prior work including Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and variants. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the stack LSTM state representation for joint parsing.  
2. The methodology is rigorously evaluated across multiple domains (WSJ, Brown) and languages (English, German, Spanish, Chinese), with clear ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) showing complementary benefits of joint learning.  
3. The runtime efficiency (2.5× faster than Lei et al., 2015) is a significant practical contribution, particularly for real-world applications where parsing speed matters.  
4. The technical implementation is well-justified with thorough hyperparameter tuning, appropriate embedding choices, and clear architectural explanations.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The core technical contribution lacks novelty as it directly combines two existing components (Henderson's transition system and Dyer's stack LSTM) rather than proposing a fundamentally new approach. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently differentiate their integration.  
2. The Chinese performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings (without which performance matches others), suggesting the model may not generalize well to low-resource languages without external resources.  
3. The system does not incorporate state-of-the-art SRL features (e.g., syntactic path features from full parse trees) used in top-performing pipeline systems (e.g., Roth and Woodsend, 2014), limiting its competitive edge against those approaches.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How would the model's performance change if it incorporated syntactic path features from the full parse tree (as in Roth and Woodsend, 2014), and why were these not included given their proven effectiveness?  
2. The Chinese results depend on pretrained embeddings—can the model achieve comparable results on Chinese without them, or is this a fundamental limitation for low-resource languages?
