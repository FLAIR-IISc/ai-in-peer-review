- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs that removes hand-crafted features and achieves SOTA on CoNLL 2008/2009 English with linear time complexity.  
- Contribution: First successful application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing, showing neural state representations can replace expensive feature engineering.  
- Contribution: Empirical validation—outperforms prior joint models and matches top pipeline systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.  
- Contribution: Practical runtime efficiency—end-to-end parsing in ~177s on CoNLL 2009 English, ~2.5× faster than a comparable system.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results without pipeline features or beam search, with informative ablation studies isolating joint learning benefits.  
- Strength: Practical impact—linear-time complexity and substantial speedup make the model applicable to real-world systems.  
- Strength: Rigorous methodology and clear presentation; multilingual experiments show generalizability beyond English.  
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—combines existing transition system and stack LSTMs without new algorithmic innovations.  
- Weakness: Chinese results heavily depend on pretrained embeddings, raising questions about architecture vs. embedding effects.  
- Weakness: Joint model shows overfitting on out-of-domain Brown corpus and lacks analysis explaining this failure.  
- Question: Is the Brown overfitting due to insufficient training data or a fundamental limitation of joint learning?  
- Question: How would the model perform on low-resource languages without pretrained embeddings (e.g., using character-level inputs)?  
- Overall: Well-executed empirical paper with significant practical results but incremental methodological novelty; recommended for accept.
