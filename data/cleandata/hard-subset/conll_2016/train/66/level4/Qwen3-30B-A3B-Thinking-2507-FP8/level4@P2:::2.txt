

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing, building on Henderson et al. (2008) with two key innovations: (1) a stack LSTM representation of the *entire* parser state (replacing expert-crafted features), and (2) a greedy inference algorithm. The primary contribution is the use of stack LSTMs to capture long-range syntactic dependencies (e.g., predicate-argument paths) without manual feature engineering. The secondary contribution is the linear-time greedy parsing, enabling faster inference than beam-search alternatives. The tertiary contribution is competitive multilingual performance without additional linguistic resources.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel state representation**: The stack LSTM approach effectively replaces expensive hand-crafted features (e.g., predicate-argument paths) used in prior SRL systems. On CoNLL 2008, the model outperforms all prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; Titov et al., 2009), achieving the highest semantic F1 (75.5%) and LAS (84.1%) among joint learners—demonstrating that end-to-end learning can match pipeline-based systems without expert features.  
2. **Speed and practicality**: The greedy parser runs in 177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data (single-core), 2.5× faster than state-of-the-art pipeline systems (Lei et al., 2015: 439.9±42 seconds). This efficiency, combined with open-source code, makes the approach suitable for real-world applications.  
3. **Multilingual adaptability**: The model achieves top-3 performance on non-English CoNLL 2009 datasets (e.g., German, Spanish), with Chinese relying on pretrained embeddings. This shows robustness across languages without language-specific tuning, unlike many competing systems.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Greedy inference limits comparability**: The paper claims "best performance among joint models," but the greedy approach excludes beam-searched results (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013), making direct comparisons invalid. The authors cite beam-search as "asymptotically slower," yet omit a single beam-search result (even with beam size=1) to contextualize their greedy gains. Without this, claims of "superiority" lack evidence against the most common baseline.  
2. **Underutilized orthogonal improvements**: The model’s performance lags behind state-of-the-art SRL systems (e.g., Roth & Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) that integrate *orthogonal* advances (e.g., global reranking, syntactic features from full parses). The paper acknowledges these gaps but fails to explore integration (e.g., using the parser’s output as input to a reranker). This undermines the claim that "future work" can bridge the gap.  
3. **Unclear task definition**: Section 2 ambiguously describes the semantic dependencies (e.g., "A1" and "C-A1" links in Figure 1). The paper does not clarify how these align with CoNLL 2009’s predicate-argument schema, making it difficult to assess whether the parser handles edge cases (e.g., self-dependencies in NomBank) correctly. This weakens the validity of the evaluation.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The greedy inference choice excludes beam-search comparisons. Could you provide a single beam-search result (beam=1) to clarify whether greedy inference *causes* the performance gap versus beam-search, or if the state representation alone drives gains?  
2. The model’s performance on out-of-domain data (Brown corpus) lags behind Hybrid and Joint (Table 3). Does the greedy algorithm exacerbate overfitting to WSJ, or is this due to the lack of syntactic features?  
3. The paper states that "many advances in [expert systems] are orthogonal to our model." Given that syntactic features (e.g., from full parses) are used in top SRL systems, why not integrate them *within* the greedy framework (e.g., via external parser features)?
