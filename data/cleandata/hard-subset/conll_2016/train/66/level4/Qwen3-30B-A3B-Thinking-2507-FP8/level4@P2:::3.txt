

**Summary**  
This paper introduces a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire algorithmic state. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel transition system that extends Henderson et al. (2008) by operating on a more comprehensive state representation, and (2) the demonstration that neural network-based joint parsing can outperform prior models without relying on expert-crafted features. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008–9 English shared tasks, with the key advantage being the ability to learn effective representations of the full parsing state.

**Strengths**  
1. **Superior performance on joint parsing tasks**: The model achieves the best results among joint parsing systems on CoNLL 2008–9 English shared tasks, outperforming all prior joint learning models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008; Titov et al., 2009). Notably, it matches or exceeds pipeline systems (e.g., Johansson and Nugues, 2008) without requiring expensive syntactic features. This demonstrates that neural representations can effectively replace hand-crafted features in joint parsing.  

2. **Efficiency and practicality**: The system runs in linear time with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core—nearly 2.5× faster than state-of-the-art pipeline approaches (e.g., Lei et al., 2015 at 439.9±42 seconds). This makes it highly suitable for real-world applications, and the authors plan to release an open-source implementation.  

3. **Insightful ablation studies**: The paper thoroughly evaluates four configurations (Joint, Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid), revealing nuanced insights about the interplay between syntax and semantics. For example, the Joint model outperforms Hybrid in the training domain (WSJ), while Hybrid excels out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting the Joint model may overfit to WSJ. These results clarify when joint learning provides the most value.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient explanation for neural network success**: The authors attribute their model's success to neural networks' ability to "learn which features to extract," but they do not address a critical advantage: neural networks' parameter efficiency compared to linear models. In linear models, joint learning requires learning weights for each feature-class pair, exponentially increasing parameters. Neural networks avoid this by sharing representations across tasks. This key point needs elaboration to distinguish their contribution from generic neural advantages.  

2. **Lack of dynamic oracle**: The paper does not provide a dynamic oracle for the transition system, which would theoretically guarantee optimal training. While the authors note the transition system resembles arc-eager, they fail to explain how the oracle would be constructed or why it is unnecessary. This omission weakens the theoretical foundation of their approach.  

3. **Limited comparison with recent neural SRL work**: The paper acknowledges that state-of-the-art SRL systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015) use orthogonal improvements (e.g., expert features, exact inference) but does not discuss how their model could integrate these. It would strengthen the contribution to address whether their approach could be combined with these techniques to achieve even better results.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims neural networks outperform linear models due to "automatic feature learning," but the parameter economy of neural networks (vs. linear models' feature-class weight explosion) seems equally important. Could you clarify which factor is more significant and provide evidence?  

2. The transition system appears to resemble arc-eager, but the authors do not derive a dynamic oracle. How would the oracle handle semantic transitions like M-SWAP and M-SELF? Would it require modifications to the transition set?  

3. The paper states that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model," but does not explore integration. Could you comment on how your approach might incorporate techniques like low-rank tensor compression (Lei et al., 2015) or exact inference (Täckström et al., 2015) without compromising speed?
