

Summary: This paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that leverages stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. It achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks without using pipeline features or beam search, while maintaining linear time complexity.

Contributions (ordered by strength):  
1. First successful application of stack LSTMs to joint syntactic-semantic parsing, demonstrating that neural state representations can replace expensive hand-crafted features (e.g., path-based features) while matching or exceeding pipeline-based SOTA systems.  
2. Empirical validation of the approach’s effectiveness: Outperforms all prior joint parsing models (including Henderson et al. 2008, 2013) on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, with results comparable to top pipeline systems (Zhao et al. 2009).  
3. Practical runtime efficiency: Achieves end-to-end parsing in 177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data (single core), outperforming comparable systems (e.g., Lei et al. 2015 at 439.9±42 seconds) by 2.5×.

Strengths:  
1. **Strong empirical results with clear validation**: The joint model significantly surpasses all prior joint-learning approaches on CoNLL 2008 (Table 2) and matches top CoNLL 2009 systems (Table 3). Crucially, it achieves this *without* using pipeline features (e.g., full syntactic trees for path features) or beam search, validating the core claim that stack LSTMs can replace feature engineering. The ablation studies (Syntax-only/Semantics-only/Hybrid) further isolate the value of joint learning.  
2. **Practical impact and efficiency**: The linear-time complexity (177s vs. 439s for Lei et al.) makes the model immediately applicable to real-world systems, as explicitly highlighted by the authors. This addresses a key limitation of prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al. 2013), which relied on slower beam search.  
3. **Rigorous methodology and presentation**: The transition system (Table 1), architecture (Figure 3), and training details (Section 3–4) are clearly explained. The hyperparameter choices are well-justified, and the multilingual results (Table 4) demonstrate generalizability beyond English, though Chinese performance relies on pretrained embeddings (see Weaknesses).

Weaknesses:  
1. **Incremental novelty**: The approach combines established components (Henderson’s transition system and Dyer’s stack LSTM) without introducing new algorithmic innovations. While the *application* to joint parsing is novel, the core techniques are not, limiting the theoretical contribution. The paper acknowledges this ("Many advances... are orthogonal to our model"), but this should be emphasized more in the contribution statement.  
2. **Chinese results heavily depend on embeddings**: As noted in Table 4, the Chinese parser’s strong performance relies on pretrained embeddings; without them, it performs at par with other systems. This raises questions about whether the model’s success stems from the embeddings or the architecture, and whether the approach generalizes to low-resource languages without such resources.  
3. **Overfitting in joint model on out-of-domain data**: The joint model underperforms the Hybrid variant on the Brown corpus (out-of-domain, Table 3), suggesting overfitting to the WSJ training distribution. The authors attribute this to "joint overfitting" but provide no analysis of why the joint model fails here (e.g., insufficient data, feature mismatch). This limits confidence in the model’s robustness.

Questions to Authors:  
1. The joint model’s overfitting on Brown (Table 3) suggests a distributional mismatch. Could you clarify whether this is due to insufficient training data or a fundamental limitation of the joint learning approach?  
2. The Chinese results (Table 4) are heavily dependent on pretrained embeddings. How would the model perform on a low-resource language *without* such embeddings (e.g., using character-level inputs as in Ballesteros et al. 2015)?  

Overall, this is a well-executed study that convincingly demonstrates the practical viability of stack LSTMs for joint parsing. While the novelty is incremental, the empirical results and efficiency gains are significant for the field. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of the overfitting issue and clearer articulation of the contribution as an *application* rather than a *methodological innovation*. Accept.
