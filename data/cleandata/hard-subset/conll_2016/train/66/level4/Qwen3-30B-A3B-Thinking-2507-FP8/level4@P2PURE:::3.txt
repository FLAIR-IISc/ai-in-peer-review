

This paper presents a long-overdue return to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint modeling represents one of the most promising approaches underlying the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The authors make two key contributions. First, they introduce a novel transition system that improves upon the Henderson (2008) system it builds upon. Second, they demonstrate that neural networks achieve success on this task where linear models previously failed. While the authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features, an additional advantage merits mention: neural networks are significantly more parameter-efficient. In linear models, jointly learning two tasks requires learning weights for each feature-class pair, leading to substantially more parameters. Neural networks avoid this burden through their structured feature representation.

I anticipate that the proposed transition system would perform well with other neural architectures, such as Andor et al.'s (2016) global beam-search model. Further orthogonal improvements are also possible, and extensions of the authors' approach could achieve state-of-the-art results. 

It would be valuable to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or future work. Initial analysis suggests similarity to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action excludes all semantic arcs connecting the buffer's first word to the semantic stack, while M-D excludes arcs between the stack's top word and the buffer. The L and R actions each exclude the reverse arc and no others.
