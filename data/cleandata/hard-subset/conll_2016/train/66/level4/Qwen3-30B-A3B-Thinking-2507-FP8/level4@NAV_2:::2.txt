

Summary
The paper introduces a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, building upon the algorithmic framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The primary contributions are: (1) a novel approach using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser's algorithmic state rather than just top stack elements or recent states; (2) the demonstration that a greedy inference algorithm can achieve competitive performance without expert-crafted features; and (3) the presentation of a fast, end-to-end parsing system with runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core.

Strengths
1. The paper successfully demonstrates how stack LSTMs can replace expensive feature engineering typically used in semantic role labeling, capturing syntactic path features through the parser's algorithmic state representation. This is a significant contribution to the field as it shows how neural representations can substitute for linguistically motivated features.
2. The work provides strong empirical validation across two CoNLL shared tasks (2008 and 2009), demonstrating superior performance over previous joint parsing models while avoiding the need for pipeline architectures that require full syntactic parse trees.
3. The implementation of a greedy parsing algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction, represents a practical contribution to the field, offering a system that is nearly 2.5 times faster than comparable pipeline approaches while maintaining competitive performance.

Weaknesses
1. The exclusion of beam search results makes direct comparison to several cited works impossible, as many of those approaches use beam search with varying beam sizes. Including even one beam size (e.g., beam=5) would have strengthened the comparison and made the results more comparable.
2. The transition set description is insufficiently detailed in Section 2, making it difficult to discern exactly what modifications were made to Henderson et al.'s original transition set versus what was directly reused. A clear comparison table would have clarified this.
3. The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 regarding multilingual evaluation is confusing, as CoNLL 2008 focused exclusively on English while CoNLL 2009 extended to multiple languages, yet the paper does not clearly distinguish these in the methodology.

Questions to Authors
1. In Section 3.3, the paper concatenates word predicate representations with disambiguated sense representations. Since the disambiguated senses are specific to each predicate, why is this concatenation necessary rather than simply using the sense representation directly?
2. Could you provide beam search results (at least for beam size 5) to enable direct comparison with previous work that uses beam search?
3. The multilingual results in Section 5 show that Chinese performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings. Could you clarify how your model would perform without these embeddings, and what alternative approaches might be used for languages with limited pretrained resources?
