

This paper proposes a Stack LSTM parser that integrates Henderson et al.'s (2008, 2013) joint syntactic-semantic transition-based parsing framework with Dyer et al.'s (2015) Stack LSTM architecture for syntactic parsing. The resulting system achieves notable results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of combining these established components.  

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. The methodology is rigorously presented, with thorough justification for hyperparameters and a compelling discussion of results. The related work provides a strong foundation, and the experimental analysis is both transparent and insightful. The only minor concern pertains to the Chinese embeddings used in the experiments; while the system performs well on English tasks, the reliance on pre-trained embeddings suggests that high-quality, domain-specific lexical resources might yield greater gains than incremental model refinements.  

Regarding novelty, the approach does not introduce a fundamentally new parsing mechanism—Henderson et al. established the core semi-synchronized transition system years earlier, and Dyer et al. introduced the Stack LSTM framework. However, the paper’s value lies in its timely and effective synthesis of these existing components into a unified system, addressing a clear gap in the literature. The authors successfully demonstrate that this combination delivers competitive performance without requiring novel architectural innovations. This is not a case of incremental work but rather a pragmatic and well-executed application of established techniques to produce a robust, practical parser.  

The contribution is significant precisely because it bridges a critical need: a unified, efficient parser for joint syntactic-semantic analysis. While the methodological building blocks are not new, their integration represents a meaningful step toward scalable, end-to-end parsing systems. The results are compelling, and the paper effectively argues for the viability of this approach in real-world settings.  

In summary, this is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the practical utility of combining established parsing paradigms. The minor embedding concern does not detract from the overall quality or impact, and the lack of methodological novelty is outweighed by the system’s clear utility and robust performance. The work is highly relevant to the CONLL community and deserves publication.
