

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state. The main contributions are:  
1. Demonstrating that a stack LSTM representation effectively replaces expert-crafted features in joint syntactic-semantic parsing, eliminating the need for complex feature engineering.  
2. Showing that a greedy inference algorithm achieves competitive performance compared to previous joint parsers while maintaining linear time complexity.  
3. Providing a highly efficient implementation that processes CoNLL 2009 English test data in 177.6±18 seconds on a single core—2.5× faster than state-of-the-art pipeline approaches.  

**Strengths**  
1. The paper convincingly demonstrates that a stack LSTM representation of the entire parser state enables strong performance without expert-crafted features, which is a significant step toward reducing manual feature engineering in joint parsing. The model outperforms all prior joint parsing systems on CoNLL 2008 and achieves competitive results on CoNLL 2009, matching or exceeding top pipeline systems without their computational overhead.  
2. The implementation is exceptionally efficient, with a runtime that is 2.5× faster than comparable pipeline systems (e.g., 177.6±18 seconds vs. 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al., 2015). This practical advantage makes the approach suitable for real-world applications where speed is critical.  
3. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-justified, including ablation studies (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid variants), multilingual results, and detailed comparisons to prior work. The analysis of out-of-domain performance (Brown corpus) provides valuable insights into the model’s generalization capabilities.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. The novelty is limited, as the work primarily combines two existing techniques: Henderson et al.'s transition system for joint parsing and Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM for syntactic parsing. The paper does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic or architectural innovation but rather applies these components to a joint parsing task.  
2. The reliance on high-quality pretrained embeddings for certain languages (e.g., Chinese) is not adequately addressed. The model's performance on Chinese is notably dependent on these embeddings, and the paper does not explore how this limitation might affect low-resource languages or whether the approach could be adapted to use character-level representations instead.  
3. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems is incomplete. The authors acknowledge that their model does not match the performance of top systems that use expert-crafted features (e.g., Roth and Woodsend, 2014), but they do not experiment with integrating these features into their framework to demonstrate potential improvements. This omission weakens the claim that their approach is superior to pipeline-based methods.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why the joint model outperforms pipeline approaches, particularly regarding the specific advantages of the stack LSTM representation over features derived from full syntactic parses?  
2. How would the model perform if pretrained embeddings were not available for a language (e.g., a low-resource language with limited training data)? Would character-based embeddings (as used in syntactic parsing by Ballesteros et al., 2015) be a viable alternative?  
3. The paper notes that the joint model overfits to the WSJ training domain (as evidenced by the Hybrid model outperforming it on the Brown test set). Could the authors elaborate on the reasons for this overfitting and suggest potential mitigations for future work?
