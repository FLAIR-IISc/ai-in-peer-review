

This paper revisits the long-overdue challenge of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint parsing frameworks represent a key aspect of the success of transition-based neural parsers. The authors make two primary contributions: first, they introduce a novel transition system that significantly outperforms the Henderson (2008) baseline it extends; second, they demonstrate that neural networks achieve success on this task where linear models previously failed. The authors attribute this to neural networks' ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, a more fundamental advantage lies in parameter efficiency: linear models require a dedicated weight for each feature-class pair, leading to a combinatorial explosion in parameters when jointly learning syntactic and semantic tasks. Neural networks, by contrast, achieve comparable or better performance with far fewer parameters due to their shared representation learning.

I suspect the proposed transition system would generalize well to other neural architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor et al. (2016). The authors' approach also lends itself to numerous orthogonal improvements—extensions could readily yield state-of-the-art results. The paper would benefit from a more thorough exploration of these avenues in future work.

A notable omission is the absence of a dynamic oracle for the transition system. While the authors acknowledge the need for one, deriving it—perhaps as an appendix or follow-up—would strengthen the methodological foundation. The system appears analogous to the arc-eager oracle but with specific constraints. The M-S action prohibits all semantic arcs between the buffer's first word and the semantic stack, while M-D blocks arcs between the stack's top word and the buffer. The L and R actions each exclude reverse arcs (e.g., L excludes arcs from the current item to the stack, R excludes arcs from the stack to the current item), with no additional exclusions. Formalizing this oracle would clarify the transition system's theoretical properties and potentially improve training efficiency.
