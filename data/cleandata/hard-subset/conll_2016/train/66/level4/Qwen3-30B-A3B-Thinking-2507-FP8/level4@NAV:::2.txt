

Review- General comments
================
The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two primary distinctions:  
- Utilization of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than solely the top stack elements or recent parser configurations  
- Adoption of a straightforward greedy search algorithm  

The core innovation leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a state vector that retains memory of extensive syntactic features—critical for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and its candidate role filler). The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. While the results are competitive against 2008/2009 systems, they underperform relative to recent approaches (see bottom of Table 3). The authors stress the absence of hand-crafted features and the efficiency of the greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written, detailing substantial work that applies modern LSTMs to Henderson et al.’s algorithm—a framework now recognized as prescient.  

I have reservations about the greedy search choice: it compromises comparability with several cited works. Including beam-searched results would have required minimal additional effort or space.  

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:  
Clarify the purpose of both A1 and C-A1 links to better define the paper’s target task. Provide a concise summary contrasting the transition sets used here versus Henderson et al. (2008) to distinguish reused elements from novel modifications.  

Section 3.3:  
Explain the necessity of concatenating word predicates with their disambiguated senses, as this appears redundant given that disambiguated senses are predicate-specific.  

Section 4:  
The organization of Sections 4.1/4.2 regarding multilingualism is confusing. Note that CoNLL 2008 focused exclusively on English, while CoNLL 2009 extended the shared task to multiple languages.
