

This paper provides a long overdue return to the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint modeling represents one of the most promising approaches underpinning the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The authors make two key contributions. First, they introduce a novel transition system that appears superior to the Henderson (2008) system it extends. Second, they demonstrate that neural network models achieve success on this task where linear models had previously failed. While the authors attribute this success to neural networks' ability to automatically select relevant features, an additional advantage merits mention: neural networks are significantly more parameter-efficient in joint learning. Linear models require learning a weight for each feature-class pair, leading to a quadratic increase in parameters when jointly modeling multiple tasks. Neural networks avoid this scalability bottleneck.

I suspect the proposed transition system would generalize effectively to other neural architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). Further orthogonal improvements could yield state-of-the-art results. The authors' method shows strong potential for future extensions.

It would be valuable to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even as an appendix or future work. The system appears analogous to the arc-eager oracle: the M-S action excludes all semantic arcs connecting the buffer head word to words on the semantic stack, the M-D action excludes all arcs connecting the stack top word to words in the buffer, and the L/R actions each exclude the reverse arc without additional restrictions.
