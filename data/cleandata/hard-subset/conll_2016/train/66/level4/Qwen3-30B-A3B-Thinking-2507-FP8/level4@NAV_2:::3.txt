

Summary of the paper: This work introduces a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs. The approach employs greedy inference with linear time complexity and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008-2009 English shared tasks without using expert-crafted features from full syntactic parses.

Main contributions:
1. A novel transition-based approach for joint syntactic-semantic parsing that learns representations of the entire algorithmic state rather than just top items on the stack, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features.
2. Demonstration that neural network approaches can successfully handle joint parsing without the expensive feature engineering previously required, achieving better performance than previous joint models.
3. A greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity that significantly outperforms previous joint parsing models in terms of speed (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data vs. 439.9±42 seconds for pipeline systems).

Strengths:
1. The paper successfully demonstrates that neural networks can handle joint parsing without expert-crafted features from full syntactic parses, which previous linear models struggled with. This represents a significant methodological contribution that simplifies the parsing pipeline while maintaining performance.
2. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks without relying on expensive features or complex decoding procedures, making it more practical for real-world applications.
3. The linear time complexity (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core) represents a substantial speed improvement over previous joint parsing systems (e.g., 2.5× faster than Lei et al. (2015)), making it particularly valuable for practical applications.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not adequately explain why the neural network approach is more parameter-efficient for joint learning compared to linear models, which the authors briefly suggest as an advantage but do not substantiate with evidence or analysis.
2. The authors do not provide a dynamic oracle for their transition system, which would help clarify the theoretical properties of the approach and potentially improve performance.
3. The paper does not explore whether the transition system would work with other neural network architectures (e.g., beam search models), which would help establish the generality of their approach and potentially lead to further performance improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more detailed evidence or analysis regarding the parameter efficiency claim for neural networks in joint learning compared to linear models? Is this a theoretical property or empirical observation?
2. Would it be possible to derive a dynamic oracle for your transition system? How would it differ from the arc-eager oracle, given the specific constraints of your semantic transitions?
3. Have you considered testing your transition system with other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016), to determine whether the transition system's benefits are architecture-agnostic?
