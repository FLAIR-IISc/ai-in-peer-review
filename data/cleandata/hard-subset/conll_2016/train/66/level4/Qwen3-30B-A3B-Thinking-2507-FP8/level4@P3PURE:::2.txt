

General Comments
================
This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors identify two primary innovations:  
- The entire parser state is represented via vectorial representations, rather than relying solely on the top elements of the stack or the last parser configuration.  
- The algorithm employs a simple greedy search strategy.  

The key insight leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a state vector that retains memory of potentially wide-scope syntactic features—known to be critical for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and a candidate role filler head). The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. Results show competitive performance against the 2008/2009 systems but lower scores than more recent approaches (see Table 3, bottom). The authors emphasize the absence of hand-crafted features and the efficiency gains from the greedy algorithm.  

The paper is well-written and demonstrates substantial work, building on the popularity of LSTMs to apply Henderson et al.’s algorithm—a framework now recognized as somewhat visionary. However, I have reservations about the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, as it renders results incomparable to several cited works. Including beam-searched performance would have required minimal additional effort or space and would have strengthened the evaluation.  

Detailed Comments
=================
Section 2:  
A brief explanation of the purpose of both A1 and C-A1 links would improve clarity regarding the target task. Additionally, a summary contrasting the transitions used in this work with those of Henderson et al. is needed. The current description makes it difficult to discern what is directly reused versus newly modified.  

Section 3.3:  
Why are representations concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense necessary? This appears redundant, as disambiguated senses are inherently specific to a predicate.  

Section 4:  
The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing regarding multilingual coverage. CoNLL 2008 focused exclusively on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the task to multiple languages. The paper should clarify this progression.
