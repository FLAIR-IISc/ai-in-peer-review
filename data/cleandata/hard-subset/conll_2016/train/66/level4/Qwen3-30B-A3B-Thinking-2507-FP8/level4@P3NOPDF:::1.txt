

This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser that synthesizes key elements from Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic-semantic transition-based parsing and Dyer et al. (2015) on stack LSTM syntactic parsing. The integration of Henderson's transition system with Dyer's stack LSTM architecture yields compelling results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating strong performance relative to existing joint parsing frameworks.  

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured, with a thorough exposition of the system architecture, hyperparameter choices, and experimental setup. The related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution within the broader landscape of transition-based parsing, while the results section provides detailed analysis, including ablation studies that substantiate the model's design decisions. The methodology is sound, and the discussion of empirical findings is insightful, particularly regarding the model's handling of syntactic-semantic dependencies.  

A minor concern arises regarding the Chinese embeddings used in the experiments. While the paper justifies their selection, it suggests that high-quality pre-trained embeddings (e.g., from larger corpora or more sophisticated training) could potentially yield greater gains than further model refinement. This is a valid point for future work but does not undermine the current contribution.  

The primary critique centers on novelty. The core components—Henderson's transition system and Dyer's stack LSTM—are well-established in the literature. Consequently, the proposed parser does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. However, this is not a significant drawback: the paper successfully bridges these established techniques into a cohesive, high-performing system for joint parsing, a task where such integration had not been previously demonstrated at scale. Henderson et al. laid the groundwork for semi-synchronous joint parsing years ago, and Dyer et al. introduced the stack LSTM framework; this work provides a timely, practical realization of their combined potential. As the field has long awaited a robust implementation of this synthesis, the authors have filled an important gap.  

The results are particularly noteworthy for their competitiveness with state-of-the-art joint parsers on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks, often matching or exceeding prior work while maintaining computational efficiency. The clarity of the presentation ensures the contribution is readily reproducible and accessible to the community.  

In summary, this is a strong, well-executed paper that advances the practical application of established parsing techniques. While the method itself is not novel, its successful integration and empirical validation represent a valuable contribution to the field. The writing, analysis, and results are all of high quality, and the minor embedding concern does not detract from the paper's overall merit. This work is a significant step toward realizing the potential of joint parsing frameworks and deserves publication in CONLL_2016.
