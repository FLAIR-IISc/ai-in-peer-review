

This paper makes a timely and valuable contribution by revisiting joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing through the lens of modern neural network models. The authors demonstrate that neural approaches can successfully tackle this problem—where prior linear models struggled—while introducing a refined transition system that builds upon Henderson (2008). This work is particularly significant as joint parsing remains a key factor in the success of transition-based neural parsers, and the authors provide compelling evidence that neural architectures overcome historical limitations.  

The paper’s two core contributions are well-motivated and clearly articulated. First, the proposed transition system (which extends Henderson’s framework) demonstrates superior performance over its predecessor, particularly in handling complex semantic dependencies. Second, the authors establish that neural networks achieve state-of-the-art results on this task, attributing this success to their ability to automatically learn relevant features. While this attribution is valid, the paper overlooks a critical advantage of neural models: parameter efficiency. In linear models, joint learning of semantic and syntactic tasks requires learning weights for every feature-class pair, leading to a combinatorial explosion in parameters. Neural networks, by contrast, leverage shared representations and non-linear feature interactions to achieve comparable or better performance with significantly fewer parameters. This efficiency is a substantial, underdiscussed benefit that merits explicit acknowledgment, as it addresses a major practical barrier to joint parsing in resource-constrained settings.  

The authors’ claim that their transition system would generalize to other neural architectures (e.g., Andor et al.’s global beam-search model) is well-founded. Indeed, the modularity of the proposed transition system suggests it could integrate with diverse neural frameworks, potentially yielding further gains. The paper’s core methodology is robust, and I anticipate that extensions of this approach—such as incorporating richer contextual embeddings or multi-task learning—will push results closer to state-of-the-art. The authors are encouraged to explore these directions in future work.  

A minor but important suggestion concerns the transition system’s oracle. While the system’s mechanics appear analogous to the arc-eager oracle (with M-S and M-D actions excluding specific semantic arc pairs, and L/R actions blocking reverse arcs), deriving a dynamic oracle would strengthen the theoretical foundation. This could be addressed concisely in an appendix or follow-up work, as it would provide a more rigorous comparison to prior systems and clarify the system’s optimality guarantees. For instance, the M-S action’s exclusion of arcs between the buffer’s initial word and the semantic stack, and M-D’s exclusion of arcs between the stack’s top word and the buffer, warrant formalization to ensure the oracle aligns with the transition rules.  

The empirical evaluation is thorough, with results on standard datasets (e.g., CoNLL-2009) demonstrating clear improvements over prior joint parsing methods. The ablation studies effectively isolate the impact of the new transition system and neural features, reinforcing the paper’s claims. However, the discussion of feature engineering could be expanded: while the authors note neural models automate feature extraction, a brief comparison of handcrafted versus learned features would further underscore their advantage.  

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that advances joint parsing research meaningfully. The transition system is a clean, effective improvement over existing work, and the neural approach’s success—coupled with its parameter efficiency—highlights a compelling path forward for dependency parsing. The minor suggestions (dynamic oracle, feature discussion) are easily addressable and would elevate the work further. The paper is well-suited for CONLL_2016, offering both theoretical insight and practical utility for the parsing community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
