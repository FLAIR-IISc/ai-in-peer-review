- Introduces a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the full algorithmic state.  
- Contributions: a novel transition system extending Henderson et al. (2008) and demonstration that neural joint parsing outperforms prior models without hand-crafted features, achieving SOTA on CoNLL 2008–9 English.  
- Superior joint parsing performance: best results among joint parsers on CoNLL 2008–9 and matches/exceeds pipeline systems without expensive syntactic features.  
- Efficient and practical: linear-time runtime (~177.6±18s on single core for CoNLL 2009 English test), ~2.5× faster than leading pipeline approaches; authors plan to open-source.  
- Insightful ablation studies: evaluated Joint, Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid setups; Joint wins in-domain (WSJ) while Hybrid generalizes better out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting Joint may overfit WSJ.  
- Insufficient explanation for neural success: paper credits automatic feature learning but omits discussion of neural parameter efficiency and shared representations vs. linear models' feature-class weight explosion.  
- Missing dynamic oracle: no dynamic oracle derivation for the transition system is provided, weakening theoretical guarantees and leaving semantic-specific transitions unexplained.  
- Limited comparison with recent neural SRL work: does not explore integrating orthogonal advances (e.g., expert features, exact inference, low-rank tensor methods) to potentially boost performance.  
- Question: Clarify whether automatic feature learning or parameter-efficiency/shared representations are the dominant reason for neural superiority, and provide evidence.  
- Question: How would a dynamic oracle be constructed for this transition system, especially for semantic transitions like M-SWAP and M-SELF, and would the transition set need modification?  
- Question: Can the model incorporate techniques like low-rank tensor compression or exact inference (Lei et al., Täckström et al.) without compromising speed?
