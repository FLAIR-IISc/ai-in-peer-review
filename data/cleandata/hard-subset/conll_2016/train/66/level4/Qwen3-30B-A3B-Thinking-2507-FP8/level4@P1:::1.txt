

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack LSTMs. The approach combines Henderson et al.'s (2008, 2013) synchronized transition system with Dyer et al.'s (2015) stack LSTM technique, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features while achieving competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that learning representations of the entire parser state (not just top stack elements) enables a greedy parsing algorithm to match or exceed the performance of previous joint parsing models. The authors show that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 English shared task and performs comparably to top systems on CoNLL 2009, despite not using the expensive expert-crafted features that characterize many high-performing pipeline systems. The second contribution is the practical efficiency of their implementation, with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data (on a single core), which is 2.5× faster than comparable pipeline systems. The third contribution is the thorough ablation study that demonstrates the value of joint learning, showing that the Semantics-only model underperforms the Joint model on the training domain, while the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of learning entire-state representations for joint parsing, showing that this approach can match or exceed the performance of systems using expensive expert-crafted features. The implementation is efficient and practical, with a runtime that is significantly faster than state-of-the-art pipeline systems. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including multilingual results, ablation studies, and comparisons across multiple baselines. The writing is clear, the methodology is sound, and the hyperparameters are well-justified.

Weaknesses: The work lacks significant novelty, as it primarily combines two existing techniques (Henderson's transition system and Dyer's stack LSTM) without introducing new algorithmic or architectural innovations. The Chinese results heavily depend on high-quality pretrained embeddings, which may not be available for all languages, limiting the model's applicability in low-resource settings. The paper does not adequately address how to handle unseen predicate senses at test time, relying on a simple default ("01" sense) that may not generalize well to new predicate types, especially for languages with limited training data.

Questions to authors: 
1. How would you modify the system to handle unseen predicate senses more effectively, especially for low-resource languages?
2. The paper notes that Chinese performance relies heavily on pretrained embeddings. Could you elaborate on how this dependency might affect the model's applicability in resource-constrained settings?
3. The authors state that their greedy approach outperforms previous joint parsing models. However, the paper does not compare against the most recent pipeline systems that use similar feature sets but employ exact inference (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015). Could you clarify why these systems are not included in the comparison?

The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and makes a valuable contribution to the joint parsing literature. While the novelty is limited, the practical efficiency and strong empirical results make this work a useful addition to the field. The authors have successfully demonstrated that learning entire-state representations can effectively replace expensive expert-crafted features, and the implementation is both efficient and ready for practical deployment. The minor concern about Chinese embeddings (where performance appears heavily dependent on high-quality pretrained embeddings) is worth noting but does not undermine the paper's main contributions.
