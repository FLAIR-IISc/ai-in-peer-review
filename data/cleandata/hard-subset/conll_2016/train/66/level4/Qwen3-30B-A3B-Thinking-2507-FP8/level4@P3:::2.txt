

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent the entire algorithmic state. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **A novel representation of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs**, which eliminates the need for hand-crafted features that traditionally capture long-range dependencies (e.g., paths between predicates and arguments). This approach enables the model to learn these features automatically during training.

2. **Demonstrated performance gains over previous joint parsing models** on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, achieving the best results among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics without relying on full syntactic parses or expert-crafted features.

3. **Significant computational efficiency** due to the greedy inference algorithm, with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core—nearly 2.5x faster than state-of-the-art pipeline approaches.

## Strengths
1. **Compelling demonstration of the value of full-state representation**: The paper effectively shows that stack LSTMs capturing the entire parser state can replace expensive hand-crafted features traditionally used in semantic role labeling (SRL). The experimental results on CoNLL 2008 demonstrate clear improvements over previous joint parsing models, including Henderson et al. (2008), which the authors build upon. This is a significant contribution to the understanding of how neural architectures can replace feature engineering in joint parsing.

2. **Strong empirical validation with comprehensive results**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple datasets (CoNLL 2008 English, CoNLL 2009 English, and multilingual CoNLL 2009), with ablation studies comparing syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint models. The results consistently show that the full-state representation outperforms previous approaches, with the joint model achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2009 shared task (excluding only Zhao et al.'s system that used expert-crafted features).

3. **Notable computational efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that the greedy approach achieves competitive performance with substantially lower computational requirements. The runtime advantage (177.6±18 seconds vs. 439.9±42 seconds for Lei et al.'s pipeline) makes this system particularly valuable for practical applications where speed is critical.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of beam search comparison**: The paper's greedy approach is a significant limitation when comparing to other state-of-the-art systems, as many of the cited works (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015) use beam search or optimal decoding. The authors acknowledge this in the paper but fail to provide any beam-searched results for their model, making it difficult to assess whether the greedy approach is truly competitive. It would have been a straightforward addition to include beam search results (even with a small beam size) to better contextualize their findings.

2. **Insufficient explanation of performance gap with state-of-the-art SRL**: While the paper acknowledges that their system underperforms compared to recent SRL systems that use expert-crafted features (e.g., Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015), it doesn't adequately explain why. The authors claim their approach is orthogonal to these methods, but without a clear analysis of the specific features they're missing, the contribution appears less significant than claimed.

3. **Confusing presentation of multilingual results**: The paper's organization of multilingual results (Section 4.2) is confusing, as it discusses CoNLL 2008 (which focused on English) and CoNLL 2009 (which included multiple languages) in the same section. The multilingual results in Table 4 are presented without sufficient context about the language-specific challenges, making it difficult to assess the model's true multilingual capabilities.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide beam-searched performance results for your model (even with a small beam size) to better contextualize your greedy approach's performance relative to other joint parsing systems?

2. The paper states that your system "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems." Could you elaborate on which specific features are most critical that your model is missing, and whether you see a path to incorporating these without significantly increasing computational complexity?

3. The paper mentions that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for superior performance. Could you clarify whether this is a limitation of the model or whether the authors believe character-based embeddings would be sufficient for resource-poor languages like Chinese?

4. The multilingual results in Table 4 show that the model performs well across languages, but the paper doesn't discuss language-specific challenges. Could you elaborate on how the model handles morphological richness (e.g., in German or Czech) and whether you see this as a limitation for highly inflective languages?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how neural representations can replace hand-crafted features in joint parsing, and the computational efficiency is particularly compelling. With the suggested clarifications and additional results, this work would be even stronger.
