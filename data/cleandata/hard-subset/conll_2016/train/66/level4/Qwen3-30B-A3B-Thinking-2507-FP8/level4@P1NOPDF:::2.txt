

General Comments  
================  
This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key innovations:  
- The parser uses vector representations for the entire state rather than just the top stack elements or recent configurations.  
- The algorithm employs a straightforward greedy search.  

The core insight leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a state vector that encodes long-distance syntactic dependencies—features critical for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and its candidate role filler head). The system is evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 shared task.  

Results show competitive performance against 2008/2009 systems but lag behind recent approaches (see Table 3). The authors note the system requires no hand-crafted features and achieves efficiency through its greedy approach. The paper is well-structured and demonstrates substantial work building on LSTM techniques applied to Henderson et al.’s algorithm, which now appears notably prescient.  

I have concerns about the greedy search choice: it renders results incomparable to several cited works that use beam search. Adding beam-searched results would have required minimal effort and space but would have strengthened the evaluation.  

Detailed Comments  
==================  
**Section 2**  
Clarify the roles of both A1 and C-A1 links to better define the paper’s target task. Also, explicitly summarize differences between the transition sets used here and Henderson et al.’s, as it is unclear which elements are reused versus modified.  

**Section 3.3**  
Why concatenate the word predicate with its disambiguated sense? Since the sense is inherently tied to the predicate, this seems redundant.  

**Section 4**  
The organization of Sections 4.1–4.2 is confusing regarding multilingual support. CoNLL 2008 focused solely on English, while CoNLL 2009 extended the task to multiple languages. The paper should clarify how the system handles this distinction.
