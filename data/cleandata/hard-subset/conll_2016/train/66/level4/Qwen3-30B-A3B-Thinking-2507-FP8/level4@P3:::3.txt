

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
The paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, using stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) a novel transition system that improves upon Henderson et al. (2008) with new semantic transitions (M-SWAP, M-SELF), and (2) the successful application of neural network representations to joint parsing without expert-crafted features. As I see them, the primary contribution is the demonstration that a full state representation with stack LSTMs enables competitive joint parsing performance without expensive feature engineering. The second contribution is the practical efficiency of the greedy parsing approach, which achieves significant speed improvements over pipeline methods. The third contribution is the thorough ablation study that demonstrates the value of joint learning for both syntax and semantics.

## Strengths
1. **Novel state representation approach**: The paper makes a compelling case that stack LSTMs enable effective representation of the entire parsing state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. This is a significant methodological contribution that addresses a key limitation of previous joint parsing models. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008-9 without using the expensive syntactic features that dominate other high-performing systems.

2. **Computational efficiency**: The paper's claim of 177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core is a major practical advantage. This represents a 2.5x speed improvement over the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) and makes the approach viable for real-world applications. The authors provide a clear comparison to other systems, demonstrating that their method achieves competitive results with substantially reduced computational requirements.

3. **Thorough ablation studies**: The paper's comparative analysis of Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint models provides strong evidence for the value of joint learning. The results show that while syntax benefits semantic parsing (Joint > Hybrid), semantic parsing also benefits syntax (Joint > Syntax-only), and the Hybrid approach shows promise for out-of-domain data. This careful analysis significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art SRL systems**: While the paper correctly states that their approach does not incorporate orthogonal advancements (like expert-crafted features or reranking), the evaluation lacks direct comparison to systems that could be combined with their method. For instance, incorporating the interaction features from Lei et al. (2015) or the exact inference from Täckström et al. (2015) could substantially improve results. The paper claims their approach is "comparable to the top systems," but doesn't provide evidence of how much performance would improve if these orthogonal techniques were integrated.

2. **Insufficient treatment of non-projectivity**: The paper acknowledges that "1.5% of English sentences in the CoNLL 2009 English dataset" have incorrect transition sequences due to non-projective structures, but doesn't address how this affects the overall results or propose solutions. Given that non-projective structures are common in natural language, this limitation should be more thoroughly discussed and potentially addressed.

3. **Lack of dynamic oracle**: The paper doesn't provide a dynamic oracle for the transition system, which would be valuable for understanding the theoretical limits of their approach. The authors note that their transition system is similar to arc-eager, but without a dynamic oracle, it's difficult to assess whether their model is limited by the transition system itself or by the learning approach. This would be a valuable addition for future work.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more details about the impact of the 1.5% non-projective sentences on your results? How would the performance change if these sentences were handled correctly?

2. The paper states that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model." Could you elaborate on which specific orthogonal improvements would most significantly boost your model's performance, and whether you plan to incorporate any of these in future work?

3. The authors claim that "the neural network is much more economical" in terms of parameters compared to linear models. Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of parameter counts between your neural model and a comparable linear model (with the same feature set)?

4. The paper uses pretrained embeddings for Chinese, but not for other languages. Could you explain why this was necessary for Chinese and how this affects the model's generalizability to other languages without large pretraining resources?
