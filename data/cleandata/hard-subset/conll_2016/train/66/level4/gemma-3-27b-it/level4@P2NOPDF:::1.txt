This paper introduces a Stack LSTM parser that builds upon prior work in joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing (Henderson et al., 2008, 2013) and stack LSTM syntactic parsing (Dyer et al., 2015). By combining the transition system from Henderson et al. with the Stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al., the authors demonstrate promising results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving competitive performance with existing joint systems.

I found this paper to be a strong contribution. It is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains both the motivation and implementation details. The related work section is comprehensive and effectively contextualizes the presented approach. Furthermore, the results are compelling and warrant attention. The methodology itself is robust; my only minor reservation concerns the potential impact of the Chinese embeddings used. While the model performs well, it raises the question of whether the benefits of a highly sophisticated model can be maximized with equally strong embedding representations. It is possible that improved embeddings could yield even more significant gains. 

The system description is particularly clear, providing sufficient detail for reproducibility. The hyperparameter choices are appropriately justified, demonstrating a thoughtful experimental design. The discussion of the results is insightful and offers valuable analysis.

While the system doesn’t introduce fundamentally novel techniques – the foundations of semi-synchronised joint syntax-semantic transition-based parsing were laid by Henderson et al. several years ago, and the Stack LSTM architecture was recently presented by Dyer et al. – its value lies in the effective integration of these existing components.  In my view, a parser of this design was a logical and necessary next step, and this paper successfully delivers on that expectation.  The authors should be commended for realizing this combination and demonstrating its efficacy.
