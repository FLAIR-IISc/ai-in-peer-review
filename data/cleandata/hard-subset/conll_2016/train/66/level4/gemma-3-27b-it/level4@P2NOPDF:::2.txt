This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, building upon the foundational work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors introduce two key modifications: the utilization of vectorial representations for the entire parser state—rather than relying solely on the stack top or recent configurations—and the implementation of a straightforward greedy search algorithm. The core idea leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a comprehensive representation of the parser’s state, capturing long-range syntactic features crucial for semantic role labeling, particularly the dependency path between a predicate and its potential argument. 

The system was evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. Results demonstrate competitive performance against systems from the original 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, though slightly lower than more recent approaches (as noted in Table 3). The authors highlight the system's advantages – its elimination of hand-crafted features and its efficiency due to the simple greedy search. Overall, the paper is well-written and describes a significant body of work, effectively applying modern LSTM techniques to an algorithm initially proposed by Henderson et al., which appears increasingly prescient.

However, I have some reservations regarding the choice of a purely greedy search algorithm. This design choice limits the comparability of the results with those of some cited works. Including beam search performance, even briefly, would have provided a more comprehensive evaluation and would not have significantly increased the computational burden or paper length.

**More Detailed Comments and Questions:**

**Section 2:** Clarification regarding the inclusion of both A1 and C-A1 links would enhance the reader's understanding of the target task. Furthermore, a concise summary outlining the differences between the transition system employed in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be valuable. Currently, it is unclear which components are directly inherited and which have been introduced or modified.

**Section 3.3:** The rationale for concatenating the word predicate with its disambiguated sense is unclear. Given that word senses are specific to their predicate, this concatenation appears somewhat redundant. A justification for this representation choice would be appreciated.

**Section 4:** The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual aspect of the work. CoNLL 2008 focused exclusively on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the evaluation to multiple languages. This distinction should be more clearly presented in the section structure.
