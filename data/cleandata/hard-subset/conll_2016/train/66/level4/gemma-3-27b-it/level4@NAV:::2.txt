## Review

This paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, drawing inspiration from the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key innovations: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state – as opposed to focusing solely on stack top elements or recent configurations – and the implementation of a straightforward greedy search algorithm. The central concept revolves around leveraging stack LSTMs to create a state vector that retains information about potentially long-range syntactic features, known to be crucial for semantic role labeling, such as the dependency path between a predicate and its potential arguments.

The system’s efficacy is evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. Performance comparisons with previously published results demonstrate competitive results against systems from 2008 and 2009, though slightly lower performance than more recent approaches (see Table 3). Importantly, the authors emphasize that their system achieves these results without relying on hand-engineered features and benefits from the speed offered by the simple greedy decoding process. The paper is clearly written and details a significant body of work, successfully applying recent advances in LSTM technology to the Henderson et al. framework, which now appears particularly prescient. 

However, I have some concerns regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm. This design choice limits the comparability of the results to certain cited works. Including beam search performance, even with a modest increase in computational effort and space, would strengthen the evaluation.

**More Detailed Comments / Questions:**

**Section 2:** Clarification regarding the inclusion of both A1 and C-A1 links would improve understanding of the task being addressed. Furthermore, a concise summary outlining the differences between the transition system used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be beneficial. The current description makes it difficult to discern what elements have been directly adopted and what has been newly introduced or modified.

**Section 3.3:** The rationale for concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense in the representations is unclear, as the disambiguated sense is inherently predicate-specific and appears redundant.

**Section 4:** The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 was primarily focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task broadened the scope to include several additional languages.
