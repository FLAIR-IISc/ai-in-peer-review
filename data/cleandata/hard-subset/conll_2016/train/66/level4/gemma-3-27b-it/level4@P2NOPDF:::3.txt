This paper revisits the important problem of jointly learning semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint models have been a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers, and this work represents a valuable return to this approach.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, built upon the foundation of Henderson (2008), which appears to offer improvements over its predecessor. Second, they demonstrate the effectiveness of a neural network model in tackling this joint parsing task, achieving success where previous linear models struggled. The authors attribute this improved performance to the network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, I believe an additional advantage of the neural network architecture warrants consideration: its parameter efficiency. Linear models, when applied to joint problems, require learning a weight for every feature/class combination, leading to a potentially large parameter space. Neural networks, in contrast, are significantly more parsimonious in their parameterization. 

While the results are promising, I anticipate the proposed transition system could be readily adapted to work effectively with other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model presented by Andor (2016). Furthermore, numerous orthogonal improvements are possible, and I expect that extensions to this method will yield state-of-the-art results.

A particularly valuable addition, perhaps included in an appendix or as a direction for future work, would be an attempt to develop a dynamic oracle for the presented transition system. At first inspection, the oracle appears closely related to that of the arc-eager system. The M-S (Merge-Semantic) action effectively prohibits any semantic arcs connecting the word at the head of the buffer to words already on the semantic stack. Similarly, the M-D (Merge-Dependency) action prevents semantic arcs from forming between the top element of the stack and words in the buffer. The L (Left) and R (Right) actions appear to each preclude the immediate reverse arc, and no others. Exploring this connection further would strengthen the analysis and provide a practical tool for evaluating the system.
