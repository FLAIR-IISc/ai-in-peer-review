## Review of “A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependencies”

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser's state and employing a greedy inference algorithm for efficiency. The core idea is to learn representations that obviate the need for hand-crafted features, particularly those capturing long-range syntactic information crucial for semantic role labeling. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating competitive performance, particularly given its simplicity and speed.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The paper demonstrates the successful adaptation of stack LSTMs – previously shown to benefit syntactic dependency parsing – to the more complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This allows the model to capture broader contextual information without relying on explicitly engineered features. This is the most significant contribution, as it shows a pathway to simpler, yet effective, joint models.
2. **Competitive Performance with a Greedy Algorithm:** The authors achieve strong results on the CoNLL datasets using a remarkably simple greedy inference algorithm. This is notable because most state-of-the-art systems utilize more complex decoding schemes. While the performance doesn’t surpass the very best systems, it demonstrates that a well-represented state can allow for effective parsing without expensive search.
3. **Reproducibility & Efficiency:** The authors highlight the system's speed – significantly faster than some comparable models – and commitment to releasing an open-source implementation. This contributes to the broader research community by providing a readily available and efficient baseline for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a manner that is accessible to researchers familiar with dependency parsing and neural networks.
2. **Strong Baseline:** The proposed system establishes a strong baseline for joint syntactic and semantic parsing, particularly in the context of models without extensive feature engineering. The comparison to previous joint parsing models and pipeline systems provides a valuable benchmark.
3. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, rather than focusing solely on the stack top, is a clever approach and a key innovation of this work. This allows the model to learn representations that capture wider contextual dependencies.
4. **Practical Considerations:** The emphasis on computational efficiency and the plan to release an open-source implementation are commendable and demonstrate a commitment to practical impact. The runtime comparison is valuable.
5. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, including multilingual results, provide a solid empirical foundation for the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Compared to State-of-the-Art:** While competitive, the system's performance falls short of the best results reported on the CoNLL datasets, particularly when compared to systems utilizing richer features and more sophisticated decoding algorithms. This limits the impact of the work.
2. **Justification for Greedy Decoding:** The authors do not thoroughly justify the choice of a purely greedy decoding strategy. While it contributes to the system’s speed, the potential performance gains from beam search or other decoding methods are not adequately addressed. A brief comparison, even with limited results, would strengthen the argument for the greedy approach.
3. **Lack of Detailed Transition Analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of the learned transitions and their behavior could provide valuable insights into the model’s strengths and weaknesses. Understanding *why* certain transitions are preferred could inform future improvements.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to identify specific types of errors made by the system. This would help pinpoint areas where the model struggles and suggest directions for future research.
5. **Organization of Experimental Results:** The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is slightly confusing regarding multilinguality. A clearer separation of the CoNLL 2008 (English-only) and 2009 (multilingual) experiments would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a brief comparison of performance (even on a smaller subset of the data) using a beam search decoding strategy? This would help assess the trade-off between speed and accuracy.
2.  Can you elaborate on the differences between the set of transitions used in your work and those originally proposed by Henderson et al. (2013)? A clearer description of the modifications would be helpful.
3.  In Section 3.3, why is it necessary to concatenate the word predicate and its disambiguated sense into a single representation? Wouldn't the sense information be sufficient on its own?
4.  Did you observe any instances where the greedy decoding strategy resulted in suboptimal or inconsistent parses? If so, can you provide examples?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of A1 and C-A1 links in Section 2 could benefit from a slightly more detailed explanation for readers unfamiliar with the CoNLL datasets.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used in the experiments for easier reproducibility.
*   The reference to "structured skipgram" embeddings could include a brief explanation of what they are and why they were chosen.
