The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key distinctions: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than limiting them to the top stack elements or recent configurations, and the implementation of a purely greedy search algorithm. The core idea leverages stack LSTMs to maintain a vector representation of the parser state that captures potentially long-range syntactic features, which are known to be important for semantic role labeling – particularly the path between a predicate and its candidate role filler head. The system was evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset, with performance comparisons to previous systems. Results demonstrate competitive performance relative to systems from 2008 and 2009, but lower scores compared to more recent approaches (as shown at the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize that their system requires no hand-crafted features and benefits from the speed of the greedy search algorithm. The paper is well-written, clearly describing a significant body of work that applies recent advances in LSTMs to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears particularly prescient. 

However, I have concerns regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, as it limits the comparability of the results to some cited works. Including beam search performance would have represented a reasonable addition to the work, requiring minimal extra effort and space.

Section 2:
A clarification regarding the inclusion of both A1 and C-A1 links would improve understanding of the target task. Additionally, a summary of the differences between the transitions used in this work and those of Henderson et al. would be beneficial. Currently, it is difficult to discern what components are directly reused from Henderson et al. and what is novel or modified.
Section 3.3
The rationale for concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense in the representations is unclear, as the disambiguated sense is predicate-specific and thus potentially redundant.
Section 4
The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing with regard to multilinguality. The CoNLL 2008 shared task focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the evaluation to several other languages.
