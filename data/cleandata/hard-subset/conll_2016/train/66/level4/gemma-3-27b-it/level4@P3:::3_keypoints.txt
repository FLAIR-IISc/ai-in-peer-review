- Revisits joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using transition-based stack LSTMs, showing strong results without manual feature engineering.  
- Main contribution: a transition system building on Henderson et al. (2008) with stack LSTMs that achieves state-of-the-art among joint models and highlights neural parameter efficiency.  

Strengths:
- Achieves competitive/state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 for joint parsing, outperforming prior joint models.  
- Novel application of stack LSTMs to represent full parser state, removing need for extensive manual features.  
- Greedy inference is linear-time and fast in practice, making the model suitable for real-time use.  
- Demonstrates multilingual adaptability on CoNLL 2009, indicating reduced reliance on language-specific features.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparisons to recent strong baselines and reranking systems (e.g., Lei et al., Täckström et al.), weakening claims of overall competitiveness.  
- Greedy decoding may limit accuracy; trade-offs between speed and optimality are not deeply explored.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of the transition system and specific contributions over Henderson et al. (2008).  
- No effort to derive or evaluate a dynamic oracle for the transition system, missing deeper error/robustness analysis.  

Questions for authors:
- Please provide an ablation study isolating the impact of stack LSTM components.  
- What are the training time and resource requirements for the model?  
- Which specific techniques from other systems do you think would most improve performance and how would they integrate?  
- Can you analyze errors on the out-of-domain Brown corpus to explain performance degradation?  

Additional comments:
- The point about neural parameter efficiency vs. linear models is insightful and could be expanded.  
- Reference to Andor (2016) is likely misplaced since that work focuses on global beam-search, which this paper does not use.  
- Clarify how pseudo-projectivization interacts with the transition system and whether it affects learned transitions.
