This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Dyer et al. (2015). Specifically, it integrates a transition system for semi-synchronized joint syntactic-semantic parsing with stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state. The authors demonstrate competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance among jointly learned models.

The primary contribution of this work is the effective combination of a transition-based joint parsing framework with stack LSTMs for state representation. This allows the model to learn without relying on hand-crafted features, previously crucial for strong performance in joint syntactic and semantic parsing. A second, related contribution is the demonstration that a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm can achieve strong results in this complex joint task, contrasting with many prior systems relying on more computationally expensive decoding methods. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation on multiple datasets (including multilingual data) provides a valuable benchmark and analysis of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint syntactic-semantic parsers on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its competitiveness with more complex, feature-engineered systems. The comparable performance on multilingual data further validates the model’s generalizability.
2. **Novel Combination of Techniques:** While the individual components – transition-based parsing and stack LSTMs – are not entirely new, their integration within a joint parsing framework is a significant contribution. The paper effectively leverages recent advances in representation learning to address a long-standing challenge in NLP: avoiding error propagation in cascaded pipelines.
3. **Efficiency and Scalability:** The linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a notable advantage, especially for practical applications where processing speed is critical. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 English test data highlights the efficiency of the implementation.
4. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model. The authors provide sufficient justification for design choices, including hyperparameters, and offer a comprehensive discussion of related work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** The core methodologies—transition-based parsing and stack LSTMs—were established in prior work. While the *combination* is valuable, the paper’s novelty primarily lies in the application of existing techniques to a specific problem rather than the invention of fundamentally new methods.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on the Chinese dataset is particularly reliant on pretrained word embeddings. This suggests a potential limitation in the model’s ability to learn robust representations from scratch, particularly for languages where high-quality pretrained embeddings are unavailable.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of embedding impact would be beneficial.
3. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors correctly acknowledge that their system does not achieve state-of-the-art performance when compared to systems utilizing more extensive feature engineering, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. While these advancements are acknowledged as orthogonal, a more direct comparison and discussion of the trade-offs between complexity, performance, and efficiency would strengthen the paper.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors made by the model could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different pretrained embedding strategies (e.g., different embedding sizes, training corpora) on performance, particularly for languages beyond English?
2. How does the performance of the model vary with the size of the training dataset? Is there evidence of overfitting on the WSJ data, as suggested by the performance difference between the training and out-of-domain datasets in the CoNLL 2009 experiments?
3. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the greedy inference algorithm? Are there scenarios where beam search or other decoding strategies might significantly improve performance, and if so, what is the computational cost?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is strong and provides a good context for the presented research.
*   The availability of an open-source implementation is a valuable contribution.
*   The authors’ acknowledgement of the system's limitations and suggestions for future work are appreciated.
