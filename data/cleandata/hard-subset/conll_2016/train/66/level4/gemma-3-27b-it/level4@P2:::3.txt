This paper revisits joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint modeling represents a key strength of transition-based neural network parsers, and this work demonstrates promising results in this area.

The primary contribution of this work is a novel transition system for joint parsing, building upon and improving the Henderson et al. (2008) framework.  A secondary, but significant, contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of a neural network approach to this problem, achieving strong performance where previous linear models struggled. The authors attribute this success to the network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, an additional factor may be the parameter efficiency of neural networks compared to linear models – neural networks avoid the need to learn weights for every feature/class pairing, particularly beneficial in joint modeling scenarios.

The strength of this submission lies in its demonstration of a viable, efficient approach to joint parsing. The reported performance is competitive with, and in some cases surpasses, previous joint models, and achieves results comparable to strong pipeline systems on the CoNLL datasets. The speed of the proposed model (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test data) is also a notable advantage for practical applications. The multilingual results further indicate the model’s generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The comparison to both joint and pipeline systems establishes a clear benchmark.
2. **Efficient Implementation:** The linear runtime complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a significant practical benefit, as highlighted by the authors. This makes the model applicable in resource-constrained environments or real-time applications.
3. **Effective Representation Learning:** The use of Stack LSTMs effectively captures the parser’s state without relying on manually engineered features, a key advance that simplifies the modeling process and potentially improves generalization.
4. **Well-Defined Transition System:** The presented transition system, derived from Henderson et al. (2008), provides a clear and interpretable framework for joint parsing, facilitating analysis and potential future extensions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Transition System:** While the transition system is effectively utilized, its foundation in Henderson et al. (2008) limits its novelty. The primary innovation lies in the application of Stack LSTMs *within* this existing framework.
2. **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art:** The model does not achieve state-of-the-art performance when compared to systems employing expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. This suggests there is still room for improvement, particularly in incorporating these orthogonal advancements.
3. **Greedy Inference:** The reliance on greedy inference may limit performance compared to methods employing more sophisticated decoding strategies like beam search. While the linear runtime is a benefit, exploring methods to improve accuracy without sacrificing efficiency would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Dynamic Oracle Discussion:** The paper does not delve into the complexities of deriving a dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system. While acknowledged as a potential area for future work, a more detailed discussion or preliminary analysis of this challenge would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the potential for integrating advancements like optimal decoding or external datasets. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges or modifications required to incorporate these techniques into your current framework?
2.  Regarding the M-S and M-D transition constraints, can you provide a more formal description or justification for these constraints? A clearer understanding of their purpose would be helpful.
3.  While the Stack LSTM architecture is described, how sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of LSTM hyperparameters (e.g., hidden state dimension, number of layers)? Were extensive hyperparameter tuning experiments performed?
