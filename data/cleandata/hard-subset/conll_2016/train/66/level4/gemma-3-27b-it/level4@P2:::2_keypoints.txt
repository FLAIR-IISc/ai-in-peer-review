- Transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to remove hand-crafted features.  
- Primary contribution: demonstrates joint parsing without extensive feature engineering via learned representations.  
- Secondary contribution: a fast, greedy joint parser with competitive CoNLL 2008–2009 performance and an open-source implementation.  
- Strength: novel application of stack LSTMs to represent full parser state, capturing long-range dependencies and simplifying feature design.  
- Strength: competitive results with a simple greedy algorithm, offering a good accuracy-speed trade-off (runtime reported 177.6s on CoNLL 2009).  
- Strength: clear, comprehensive presentation with reproducible details and multilingual evaluation.  
- Weakness: limited comparisons to strong, feature-rich state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification and exploration of greedy search; no beam search results presented.  
- Weakness: lack of ablation study to quantify contributions of different components (individual stacks, composition functions).  
- Question: please compare computational cost (training/inference) to strong baselines using complex features or decoding.  
- Question: have you experimented with beam search; if so, what gains; if not, why not?  
- Question: what are the dimensionalities of word/POS/learned embeddings and how does concatenation affect performance; any diminishing returns?  
- Question: clarify transition-set differences relative to Henderson et al. (2013) with an explicit list of changes.  
- Comment: add a brief explanation of the A1 vs C-A1 distinction for CoNLL readers.  
- Comment: reorganize Sections 4.1/4.2 to separate English and multilingual results for clarity.  
- Comment: soften or justify the claim that predicate representation is redundant, since there may be reasons to include it.
