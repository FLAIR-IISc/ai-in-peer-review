## Review for CONLL 2016

**General Comments**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, drawing inspiration from the foundational work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors identify two key distinctions from previous approaches: the utilization of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than focusing solely on stack-top elements or recent configurations, and the implementation of a straightforward greedy search algorithm. The core idea centers on leveraging stack LSTMs to create a state vector that retains memory of potentially long-range syntactic features, which are known to be crucial for accurate semantic role labeling – particularly paths between predicates and candidate argument heads.

The system is evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. Performance is compared to previously published results, demonstrating competitive performance against systems from the original 2008/2009 shared tasks, though somewhat lower scores compared to more recent proposals (as indicated at the bottom of Table 3). Importantly, the authors emphasize that their system requires no hand-crafted features and benefits from the speed of the employed greedy search. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and describes a significant body of work. It builds upon the increasingly popular framework of LSTMs and re-examines the Henderson et al. (2008) algorithm, which now appears particularly prescient. However, I have reservations regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, as it limits the comparability of the results to some of the cited related work. Including beam search performance, even as supplemental material, would strengthen the evaluation.

**More Detailed Comments / Questions**

**Section 2:**

The presence of both A1 and C-A1 links warrants further clarification to better understand the specifics of the target task. A concise summary outlining the differences between the transition system used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be highly beneficial. As it currently stands, it is difficult to discern which components are directly inherited from Henderson et al. and which represent novel contributions or modifications.

**Section 3.3:**

The rationale for concatenating representations of the word predicate *and* its disambiguated sense is unclear. Given that disambiguated senses are inherently predicate-specific, this concatenation appears redundant. Could the authors elaborate on the motivation for this design choice?

**Section 4:**

The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing regarding the handling of multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused exclusively on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task expanded the scope to include several other languages. The presentation could benefit from a clearer delineation of the experimental setup and results for each language.
