This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Dyer et al. (2015). Specifically, it integrates a transition system from Henderson et al. with stack LSTMs from Dyer et al., achieving strong results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating the effectiveness of applying stack LSTMs to the task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. This allows for learning representations of the entire parser state without relying on manually crafted features, achieving competitive performance compared to systems that do. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, surpassing previous joint models and achieving comparable performance to pipeline systems. Finally, the paper provides a clear and well-documented implementation of a joint parsing system with a fast, linear-time greedy inference algorithm, making it potentially useful in practical applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming prior joint models and approaching the performance of more complex pipeline systems. This demonstrates the viability of the approach and its potential for real-world applications.
2. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The adaptation of stack LSTMs to joint parsing is a significant achievement. By learning representations of the entire parser state, the model avoids the need for extensive feature engineering, simplifying the system and potentially improving generalization.
3. **Clear and Thorough Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the transitions, statistical model, and experimental setup. This clarity facilitates understanding and reproducibility.
4. **Efficient Inference:** The linear-time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a major advantage, making the parser practical for large-scale parsing tasks. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 test set further highlights this efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of existing techniques is effective, the core components – transition-based joint parsing and stack LSTMs – are not novel in themselves. The work primarily demonstrates a successful application of these techniques to a specific problem.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance on the multilingual data, particularly Chinese, appears heavily reliant on the quality of pretrained word embeddings. This raises concerns about the transferability of the model to languages with limited embedding resources.
3. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The performance, while competitive, falls short of the very best systems reported in the literature. These top-performing systems often rely on more complex features, optimal decoding strategies, and/or additional training data, indicating potential areas for future improvement.
4. **Constraints of Greedy Decoding:** The use of a greedy decoding algorithm, while contributing to efficiency, may limit the model's ability to explore the full search space and potentially achieve higher accuracy.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of pretrained word embeddings, particularly for languages other than English? Could you provide results without pretrained embeddings or with different embedding sources?
2. Did you experiment with beam search decoding, and if so, what was the impact on performance and runtime?
3.  Could you elaborate on the types of expert-crafted features used in the pipeline systems that outperform your model and discuss the feasibility of integrating some of these features into your approach?
