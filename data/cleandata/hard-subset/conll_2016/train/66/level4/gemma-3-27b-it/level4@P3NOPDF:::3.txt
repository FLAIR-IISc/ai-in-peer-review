This paper revisits the important problem of jointly modeling semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network architectures. Joint models have been a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers, and this work provides a strong contribution in that direction.

The paper presents two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, building upon the Henderson (2008) system, which appears to offer improvements. Second, they demonstrate the effectiveness of a neural network approach to this joint parsing task, achieving success where previous linear models struggled. The authors rightly attribute this improvement to the network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, I believe an additional advantage of neural networks in this context deserves consideration. Linear models require learning a weight for *each* feature/class pair, leading to a significant parameter increase when jointly modeling multiple tasks. Neural networks, in contrast, are considerably more parameter-efficient, potentially enabling more robust learning with limited data.

The proposed transition system seems well-designed and I suspect its benefits would extend beyond the specific neural network architecture employed in this work. It would be interesting to investigate its performance with other neural parsing models, such as the global beam-search approach of Andor (2016). Furthermore, numerous orthogonal improvements are conceivable, and I anticipate that extensions to this method will yield state-of-the-art results. 

A valuable addition to this work, perhaps as an appendix or in future research, would be an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for the presented transition system. Initial observation suggests a strong similarity to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S (Merge-Semantic) action effectively precludes any semantic dependency arcs originating from the word at the head of the buffer to any word currently on the semantic stack. Conversely, the M-D (Merge-Dependency) action prevents semantic arcs extending from the top of the stack to words within the buffer. The L (Left) and R (Right) actions, in contrast, appear to exclude only the reversed arc – the immediate dependency arc that would be created by applying the opposite shift – and no others. A formalization of this intuition would strengthen the paper and aid reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field of dependency parsing. The paper is clearly written and the experimental results are convincing. The authors have identified a valuable research direction and provided a strong foundation for future work.
