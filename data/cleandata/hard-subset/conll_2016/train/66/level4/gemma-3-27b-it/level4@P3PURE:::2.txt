The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key differences: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than relying on only the top stack elements or recent parser configurations, and the implementation of a plain greedy search algorithm. The central idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to ensure that the parser state vector retains memory of potentially long-range syntactic features, which are known to be crucial for semantic role labeling—particularly the path between the predicate and the candidate role filler head. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. Results demonstrate performance competitive with systems from 2008 and 2009, though lower than more recent proposals (as shown at the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize that their system requires no hand-crafted features and benefits from the speed of the simple greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written and describes a substantial contribution, building on the recent popularity of LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears remarkably prescient.

However, I have reservations regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, as it limits the comparability of the results to some of the cited works. Providing performance results using beam search, for instance, would not have significantly increased the computational burden or space requirements.

**More detailed comments / questions:**

**Section 2:** A brief explanation of the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would aid in understanding the target task. Additionally, a summary of the differences between the transition system used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be helpful. Currently, it is difficult to discern what components are directly reused from the original work and what has been newly introduced or modified.

**Section 3.3:** The necessity of concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense in the representations is unclear, as the disambiguated sense is specific to the predicate itself and therefore seemingly redundant.

**Section 4:** The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing in relation to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused specifically on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the evaluation to several other languages.
