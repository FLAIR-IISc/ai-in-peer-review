This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint modeling has been a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper presents two main contributions. First, the authors introduce a new transition system that appears to outperform the Henderson (2008) system upon which it is built. Second, they demonstrate that a neural network approach succeeds on this task, where linear models previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, I believe there’s another significant advantage to using a neural network in this context, worth further consideration. In a linear model, a weight must be learned for each feature/class pair. Consequently, jointly learning two problems requires estimating a much larger number of parameters. Neural networks are considerably more parameter-efficient in comparison.

I suspect that the proposed transition system would perform well with a variety of other neural network architectures, for example, the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). Many other orthogonal improvements could also be explored. I anticipate that extensions to the authors’ method will yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be valuable to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if presented as an appendix or in future work. At first glance, it appears to share similarities with the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action prevents any semantic arcs from being created between the word at the head of the buffer and words currently on the semantic stack. Similarly, the M-D action prohibits semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and words in the buffer. The L and R actions, on the other hand, appear to each exclude only the reverse arc, with no other restrictions.
