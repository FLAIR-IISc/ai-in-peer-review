This paper introduces a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies. Building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008), the authors address limitations of prior joint parsing models by employing stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state – eliminating the need for hand-crafted features – and utilizing a greedy inference algorithm for efficiency. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that effective joint parsing can be achieved without extensive feature engineering, leveraging recent advances in representation learning. A secondary contribution is the implementation and evaluation of a fast, greedy joint parser that achieves competitive performance on CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks. The authors also provide a practical, open-source implementation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the complete parser state for joint syntactic and semantic parsing is a significant strength. This approach effectively captures long-range dependencies and obviates the need for complex, manually-designed features traditionally crucial for semantic role labeling. This simplifies model development and potentially improves generalization.
2. **Competitive Performance with a Simple Algorithm:** The parser achieves strong results on the CoNLL datasets, comparable to more complex pipeline-based systems, despite utilizing a straightforward greedy inference algorithm. This highlights the effectiveness of the learned representations and suggests a promising trade-off between performance and computational cost. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 is a clear advantage.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, the model architecture, and the experimental setup. The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008) is well-established, and the authors provide sufficient detail to facilitate replication. The inclusion of a multilingual evaluation (CoNLL 2009) is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the paper demonstrates competitive performance, the comparison to state-of-the-art systems (particularly those leveraging expert-crafted features and optimal decoding as discussed in the conclusion) feels somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge this, but a more direct comparison, even if highlighting the trade-offs, would strengthen the evaluation.
2. **Justification for Greedy Search:** The decision to use a purely greedy search algorithm is not fully justified. The authors mention speed as a primary benefit, but the potential performance gains from beam search or other decoding strategies are not thoroughly explored. The lack of beam search results makes it difficult to assess the full potential of the model and limits its comparability to other approaches.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of different components, such as the individual stack LSTMs or the different composition functions, could provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior and identify areas for improvement. For example, how much performance is lost by removing one of the LSTM stacks?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a brief comparison of the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) of your system to other strong baselines, including those that use more complex feature engineering or decoding strategies?
2.  Have you experimented with beam search decoding? If so, what performance gains were observed? If not, what were the primary reasons for not exploring this option?
3.  In Section 3.3, you mention concatenating word and POS embeddings with learned embeddings. What is the dimensionality of each embedding, and what impact does this concatenation have on model performance? Is there a diminishing return at a certain point?
4.  Could you clarify the differences in the transition sets used in your work compared to Henderson et al. (2013)? A more explicit listing of added, removed, or modified transitions would be helpful.

**Additional Comments:**

*   In Section 2, a brief explanation of the distinction between A1 and C-A1 links would be beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the CoNLL datasets.
*   The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 regarding multilinguality is slightly confusing. Consider restructuring to clearly delineate the single-language (English) results from the multilingual results.
*   The phrasing "redundant since the disambiguated sense are specific to a predicate" in the review of Section 3.3 might be too strong. The authors might have a reason for including the predicate representation, even if it seems intuitively redundant.
