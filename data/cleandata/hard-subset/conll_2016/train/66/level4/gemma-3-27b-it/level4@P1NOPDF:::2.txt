The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key differences: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than relying on only the top elements of the stack or the most recent parser configurations; and the implementation of a straightforward greedy search algorithm. The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to ensure that the vector representing the parser’s state retains memory of potentially long-range syntactic features, which are known to be crucial for semantic role labeling (such as the path between the predicate and the candidate role filler head). The system was tested on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. 

The authors compare their system’s performance to previously reported results, demonstrating competitive performance compared to systems submitted to the 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, but lower performance than more recent proposals (as shown at the bottom of Table 3). They emphasize, however, that their system requires no hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simplicity of the greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written and describes a substantial amount of work, building upon the now-popular use of LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which appears to have been somewhat ahead of its time.

I do have reservations regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm. This limits the comparability of the results to some of the cited works. It would not have required significant additional effort or space to include, for example, performance figures obtained using a beam search.
