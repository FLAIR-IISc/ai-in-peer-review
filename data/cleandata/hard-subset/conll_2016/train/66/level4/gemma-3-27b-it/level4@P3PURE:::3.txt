This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advancements in neural network models. Joint modeling represents a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, which appears to outperform the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, they demonstrate that a neural network approach succeeds on this challenging task, where linear models had previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, I believe an additional advantage merits consideration: neural networks offer greater parameter efficiency compared to linear models. In a linear model, a weight must be learned for each feature/class pair, leading to a substantial increase in parameters when jointly learning multiple tasks. Neural networks, in contrast, are considerably more economical in this regard.

I anticipate that the proposed transition system would perform effectively with various other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). Furthermore, numerous orthogonal improvements are possible, and I expect extensions of this work to yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be beneficial to investigate a dynamic oracle for this transition system, potentially as supplementary material or in future work. The system appears conceptually similar to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action prohibits semantic arcs connecting the word at the start of the buffer with words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action prevents semantic arcs between the top word on the stack and words in the buffer. The L and R actions, conversely, appear to each exclude only the reverse arc.
