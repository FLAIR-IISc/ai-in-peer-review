This paper introduces a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging the algorithmic framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and the stack LSTM architecture proposed by Dyer et al. (2015). The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results among models designed for joint syntactic and semantic parsing.

The primary contribution of this work is the effective integration of stack LSTMs within a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing framework. By learning representations of the entire parser state – rather than relying on hand-crafted features or focusing solely on the stack top – the model achieves strong performance without the computational expense of previous approaches. A second, notable contribution lies in the demonstration that a greedy decoding algorithm, combined with this state representation, can yield results comparable to more complex decoding strategies. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation and detailed analysis, including comparisons to both pipeline and joint models, provides valuable insight into the trade-offs between feature engineering, decoding complexity, and overall system performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on established benchmark datasets (CoNLL 2008 & 2009). The model achieves performance competitive with, and in some cases exceeding, previous state-of-the-art systems, particularly those employing pipelined approaches. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed architecture and training methodology.
2. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to the joint parsing problem is a key strength. The authors successfully demonstrate that these representations can effectively capture the necessary information for accurate parsing, obviating the need for extensive feature engineering. This simplifies the modeling process and offers potential for improved generalization.
3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model, including the transition system, stack LSTM architecture, and training procedure. The inclusion of an example run (referenced in the text) further aids understanding. The justification of hyperparameter choices also strengthens the reproducibility of the work.
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The authors perform a thorough experimental analysis, including ablation studies (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid) and comparisons with a range of baseline and state-of-the-art systems. This provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed model and its components.
5. **Computational Efficiency:** The model’s linear time complexity and reported runtime are significant advantages. This makes the parser practical for real-world applications where speed is critical.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the integration of existing techniques is effective, the paper’s core innovation lies primarily in the combination, rather than the creation, of novel methods. The foundational components – the transition system (Henderson et al.) and stack LSTMs (Dyer et al.) – were established in prior work. 
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance, particularly on the multilingual data, appears sensitive to the quality of pretrained word embeddings. The discussion regarding Chinese performance specifically highlights this. This reliance could limit performance in low-resource languages where high-quality embeddings are unavailable.
3. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While competitive, the model does not surpass the very best performing systems which leverage extensive feature engineering, global optimization (reranking), or auxiliary datasets. The authors acknowledge this, but it's worth reiterating that there remains a gap between their approach and the current state-of-the-art.
4. **Simplifying Assumption in Semantic Transitions:** The handling of semantic self-dependencies with the `M-SELF` transition, while addressing a specific CoNLL issue, feels like a pragmatic solution rather than an elegant theoretical resolution. It might be beneficial to discuss potential limitations of this approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the impact of different pre-trained embedding strategies on the multilingual performance? Specifically, did you experiment with cross-lingual embeddings or other techniques to mitigate the dependence on high-quality embeddings for each language?
2.  The discussion mentions the potential for incorporating morphological features. Have you conducted any preliminary experiments with character-based word embeddings as an alternative to morphological features, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the `M-SELF` transition, could you discuss potential scenarios where this simplified approach might lead to errors or suboptimal parsing?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Lei et al. (2015)’s runtime should include a link to their publicly available implementation, as mentioned in the paper.
*   The paper consistently uses "algorithmic state" which is a somewhat vague term. Defining this more precisely could improve clarity.
