This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser based on the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing and Dyer et al. (2015) on stack LSTM syntactic parsing. The use of the transition system from the former and the stack LSTM from the latter yields strong results compared to previous joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. It is well-written and clearly explained, with a thorough discussion of related work and compelling results. The methodology is sound, and the system description is lucid, with hyperparameters well-justified and the discussion insightful.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful integration of stack LSTMs into a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser. While the individual components – the transition system of Henderson et al. and the stack LSTMs of Dyer et al. – were previously established, this paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their combination for joint parsing, achieving competitive results without relying on manually engineered features. A second, noteworthy contribution lies in the demonstration that strong performance can be achieved with a simple, greedy inference algorithm, offering advantages in speed and practicality compared to more complex decoding strategies. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and languages, including a detailed analysis of performance variations, strengthens the validity of the findings.

One potential limitation is that the approach does not introduce fundamentally novel methods; it builds upon existing techniques. However, as the authors acknowledge, the integration and demonstration of this approach were a logical and valuable step, and the resulting system’s performance is competitive with, and in some cases surpasses, previous work. The authors also note that their system's performance doesn't match state-of-the-art systems which leverage more complex features and decoding schemes. 

**Questions to Authors:**

*   The paper mentions the use of pretrained embeddings for the Chinese experiments, which significantly improve performance. Could you elaborate on the specific pretrained embeddings used and provide results without them to illustrate the improvement more clearly?
*   In section 4.2, you mention that the Semantics-only model underperforms Hybrid and Joint as expected. Could you elaborate on the specific reasons for this underperformance, beyond the general expectation that syntax aids semantics? Are there particular error patterns observed in the Semantics-only model?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The statement "very good embeddings can be more informative than a very clever model..." in the original review reflects a personal opinion and is not a formal criticism of the paper.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the running time of the proposed system compared to other relevant approaches.
