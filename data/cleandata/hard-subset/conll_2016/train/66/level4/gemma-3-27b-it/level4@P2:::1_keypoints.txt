- Introduces a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using Henderson et al.’s framework and stack LSTMs, achieving strong results on CoNLL 2008/2009.  
- Main contribution: effective integration of stack LSTMs to learn full parser-state representations, reducing need for hand-crafted features.  
- Shows that greedy decoding with these state representations can match more complex decoding strategies.  
- Provides comprehensive experimental evaluation and analysis, including ablations and comparisons to pipeline and joint models.  

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008 & 2009, competitive with or exceeding prior joint parsing systems.  
- Effective use of stack LSTMs to capture parser state, simplifying feature engineering and aiding generalization.  
- Clear, thorough presentation with detailed model description, example runs, and hyperparameter justification.  
- Comprehensive experimental analysis and ablation studies that illuminate component contributions.  
- Linear-time complexity and reported runtime make the parser computationally efficient and practical.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: core methods (transition system, stack LSTMs) are integrations of existing techniques rather than new algorithms.  
- Performance depends on quality of pretrained embeddings, hurting multilingual/low-resource performance (notably Chinese).  
- Does not surpass best systems that use heavy feature engineering, global optimization, or extra data.  
- The `M-SELF` semantic transition is a pragmatic workaround for CoNLL issues and may lack theoretical elegance or have limitations.  

Questions for authors:
- Did you try cross-lingual or alternative pretrained embedding strategies to reduce multilingual sensitivity?  
- Any experiments with character-based embeddings or morphology-aware representations as alternatives to explicit morphological features?  
- Could `M-SELF` cause specific error cases or suboptimal parses—can you characterize those scenarios?
