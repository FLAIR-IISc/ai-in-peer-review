Draft review:
General comments
================
This paper presents a transition-based dependency parser that jointly models syntactic and semantic dependencies. The approach builds upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008), introducing two key innovations: learning vector representations of the entire parser state—rather than focusing on stack top elements—and employing a greedy search algorithm for inference. The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to implicitly capture long-range syntactic features crucial for semantic role labeling, effectively bypassing the need for explicit, hand-crafted features. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating competitive performance compared to previous joint parsing systems, albeit trailing state-of-the-art pipeline-based approaches. A key strength highlighted is the model’s speed and lack of reliance on manually engineered features. The paper is well-written and presents a significant contribution by applying recent advances in representation learning to a foundational parsing framework.

I have some reservations regarding the exclusive use of a greedy decoding algorithm. While simplifying inference, it limits direct comparability with systems employing more sophisticated search strategies. Including results with a beam search, even as supplemental material, would strengthen the evaluation.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
Clarification on the distinction between A1 and C-A1 links would enhance understanding of the task.
A more explicit summary of the differences between the transition system used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be helpful. Currently, it's unclear which aspects are directly inherited and which are novel or modified.

Section 3.3
The rationale for concatenating word and predicate sense representations seems redundant, given that predicate senses are lexeme-specific. Could the authors elaborate on the benefit of this concatenation?

Section 4
The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing with respect to multilingual evaluation. CoNLL 2008 focused primarily on English, while CoNLL 2009 extended the task to multiple languages. This distinction could be made clearer.
