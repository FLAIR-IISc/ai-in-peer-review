This paper revisits the important problem of jointly modeling semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network architectures. Joint models are a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, which appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system upon which it builds. Second, they demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks on this challenging joint task, where linear models have previously underperformed. The authors attribute this success to the network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, an additional advantage of the neural network approach warrants consideration: while linear models require learning a weight for every feature/class combination – a parameter count that expands rapidly in joint modeling – neural networks are significantly more parameter-efficient. 

I anticipate that the proposed transition system would be compatible with a range of other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model described in Andor (2016). Furthermore, numerous orthogonal improvements could be explored. I expect that extensions to this method have the potential to achieve state-of-the-art results.

Ideally, future work would investigate the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, perhaps as an appendix or in a follow-up publication. Initial assessment suggests similarities to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action prohibits semantic arcs originating from the word at the head of the buffer and targeting words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action prevents semantic arcs originating from the top of the stack and targeting words in the buffer. The L and R actions, in contrast, appear to each preclude only their corresponding reverse arc.
