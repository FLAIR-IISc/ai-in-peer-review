This paper revisits the problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, effectively applying recent advances in neural network modeling to a traditionally challenging task. Joint modeling remains a key advantage offered by transition-based neural network parsers.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating strong performance in joint syntactic and semantic parsing using a transition-based system with stack LSTMs, achieving state-of-the-art results among models that jointly learn both syntactic and semantic structures. Specifically, the authors present a transition system that builds upon Henderson et al. (2008), incorporating stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state without relying on manually engineered features. A secondary, though significant, contribution lies in showcasing the effectiveness of neural networks in this joint learning scenario, where linear models have historically struggled. While the authors attribute this success to automated feature learning, the parameter efficiency of neural networks—avoiding the exponential growth in parameters needed for feature/class pairs in linear models—is also a noteworthy factor. 

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed model achieves competitive and, in some cases, state-of-the-art performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, surpassing previous joint parsing models. This demonstrates the efficacy of the approach and its practical relevance. The comparison to both joint and pipeline models provides a clear benchmark of the system’s capabilities.
2. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state is a significant technical contribution. By learning a representation that captures the global context of the parsing process, the model effectively circumvents the need for extensive feature engineering, a common bottleneck in traditional parsing systems. This simplification is particularly valuable for greedy inference.
3. **Efficient Greedy Inference:** The linear time complexity of the greedy inference algorithm is a major advantage.  In contrast to computationally expensive optimal decoding methods, this efficiency makes the model suitable for practical, real-time applications.  The reported runtime on the CoNLL 2009 test set further supports this claim.
4. **Multilingual Adaptability:** The model’s performance on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset suggests its potential for cross-lingual transfer and applicability to a broader range of languages. The results indicate the model is not overly reliant on language-specific features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the paper compares against several joint models, it primarily benchmarks against systems from 2008/2009.  The performance gap with more recent, state-of-the-art systems that utilize sophisticated features and reranking strategies (e.g., Lei et al., 2015, and Täckström et al., 2015) is substantial. A more direct comparison, perhaps with a focused ablation study on feature importance, would strengthen the paper.
2. **Greedy Decoding Limitations:** While efficient, the greedy decoding strategy inherently limits the potential for finding optimal parses. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of the trade-offs between efficiency and accuracy would be beneficial. Exploring alternative decoding strategies, even briefly, would demonstrate an awareness of this limitation.
3. **Transition System Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the proposed transition system itself. The improvements over Henderson et al. (2008) are asserted but not thoroughly dissected. A more in-depth discussion of the impact of specific transitions on performance would provide valuable insights.
4. **Oracle Transition Analysis:** The absence of an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for the transition system is a missed opportunity.  Understanding the limitations and potential inconsistencies of the transition system's oracle would contribute to a more complete evaluation. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study comparing the performance of the model with and without the stack LSTM components? This would help isolate the contribution of this specific technique.
2.  What is the computational cost of training the model? While runtime is reported for parsing, training time and resource requirements are not mentioned.
3.  The paper mentions the potential for integrating advancements from other systems. Could you elaborate on specific features or techniques that you believe would yield the most significant improvements, and how they could be integrated into the current framework?
4.  The performance on the out-of-domain Brown corpus data is lower than on the in-domain WSJ data.  Could you analyze the types of errors made on the Brown corpus data to understand the limitations of the model when applied to different domains?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the parameter efficiency of neural networks compared to linear models is insightful and could be further expanded.
*   The reference to Andor (2016) seems misplaced. Andor (2016) is about global beam-search, which the authors explicitly *don't* use.
*   Consider clarifying the relationship between the pseudo-projectivization step and the transition system. Does the pseudo-projectivization influence the types of transitions that are learned or applied?
