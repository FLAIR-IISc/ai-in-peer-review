Draft review:
General comments
================
The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two key differences: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than focusing on the top elements of the stack or recent configurations, and the employment of a simple greedy search algorithm. The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to capture potentially long-range syntactic features, which are known to be important for semantic role labeling—for example, the path between a predicate and its arguments. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. The authors demonstrate that their system performs competitively with previous systems from 2008 and 2009, but acknowledges it lags behind more recent approaches (see the bottom of Table 3). They emphasize that their system requires no hand-crafted features and benefits from the speed of the greedy algorithm. 

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial contribution, building on recent advances in LSTMs and refining the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears particularly insightful. However, I have some reservations regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm. This limits direct comparability with some cited works. Providing results with beam search, even as supplemental material, would strengthen the paper.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
A brief explanation of the distinction between A1 and C-A1 links would aid in understanding the task. A summary outlining the differences between the transition system used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be beneficial. As it stands, it’s difficult to discern what is directly reused versus what is new or modified.

Section 3.3
Why are representations concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense necessary? This seems redundant, as disambiguated senses are specific to predicates.

Section 4
The organization of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the task to multiple languages. Clarifying this organization would improve readability.
