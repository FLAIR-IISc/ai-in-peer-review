This paper revisits the task of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. The success of transition-based neural network parsers is, in part, attributable to the benefits of employing joint models – a key aspect highlighted in this work.

The paper presents two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, appearing to offer improvements over the Henderson (2008) system upon which it builds. Second, the work demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this joint parsing problem, an area where linear models previously faced challenges. While the authors correctly identify the neural network’s ability to learn relevant features as a source of this success, I believe parameter efficiency also plays a significant role. Linear models require learning a weight for each feature/class combination, leading to a substantial increase in parameters when jointly modeling multiple tasks. Neural networks, conversely, are more parsimonious in this regard.

I anticipate that the proposed transition system could perform well with a range of other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model proposed by Andor (2016). Numerous further enhancements and orthogonal improvements are also conceivable, suggesting potential for the authors’ method to be extended towards state-of-the-art performance.

A valuable addition would be an attempt to develop a dynamic oracle for this transition system, perhaps as an appendix or in future work. Initial observation suggests a strong resemblance to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action effectively prohibits semantic arcs originating from the buffer's head, and the M-D action prevents arcs targeting words in the buffer from the semantic stack. The L and R actions appear to each preclude their corresponding reverse arc, and no others.
