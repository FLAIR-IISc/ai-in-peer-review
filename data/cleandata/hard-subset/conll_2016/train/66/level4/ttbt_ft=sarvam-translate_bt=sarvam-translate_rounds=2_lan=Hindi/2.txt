General Notes
================
This paper presents a combined syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008).

The authors claim two main differences:

- Instead of configuring the top elements of the stack/final parser, vector representations are used to represent the entire parser's state.
- The algorithm is a simple greedy search.

The main idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so that the vector representing the parser's state potentially has a memory of syntactic features with a large range, which are considered important features for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between the predicate and the candidate role label head).

The system has been tested on the CoNLL 2008 data set (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 data set.

The authors compare their system's performance with previously reported performances, showing that their system performs well compared to the 2008/2009 system, but less so than recent proposals (see Table 3 below).

However, they emphasize that the proposed system does not require any hand-crafting features and is faster due to the simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial amount of work based on recently popular LSTMs, which has been applied to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now seems somewhat far-sighted.

I have reservations about the choice of the simple greedy algorithm:

- It does not present comparable results to some of the works cited.
- The example beam-search-optimized performance will not have more extra work or space.

More Detailed Notes / Questions
==================================

Section 2:
A comment on the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links will help in better understanding the intended function of the paper.

A summary of the difference between the set of transitions used in this work and the set of Henderson et al. should be provided.

In its current form, it is difficult to tell what has been directly reused from Henderson et al. and what is new/slightly modified.

Section 3.3
Why do you need a representation that combines the predicate word and its ambiguous meaning?

Does it seem unnecessary because the ambiguous meanings are specific to a predicate?

Section 4
4.1 / 4.2 sections are confused about multilingualism.

Conll 2008 focused on English, and CoNLL 2009 extended the shared work to some other languages.
