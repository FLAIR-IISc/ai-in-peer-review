This paper presents recent insights from neural network models into a much-anticipated revisiting problem: joint semantic and syntactic dependency analysis. Joint models are among the most promising candidates for the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

There are two contributions here. Firstly, the authors present a new transition system that seems to be better than Henderson's (2008). The other contribution is showing that neural networks have succeeded in this problem, where linear models previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn which features to extract. However, I think there is another advantage of neural networks here, which is worth mentioning. In a linear model, you have to learn a weight for each feature/class pair. This means that if you learn two problems jointly, you have to learn many more parameters. In this case, the neural network is much more efficient.

I doubt that the transition-system will work in the same way as various other neural network models, such as Andor's (2016) global beam-search model. Many more orthogonal improvements could be made. I hope that the authors' method extensions will produce state-of-the-art results.

It would be nice to see an attempt to create a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if it is only in an appendix or in follow-up work. At first glance, it seems similar to the Arc-Eger Oracle. The M-S action omits all the semantic between words at the beginning of the word stack, and the M-D action omits all the semantic between words at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to omit the reverse arc, nothing else.
