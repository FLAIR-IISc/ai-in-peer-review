This paper presents a long-pending review on the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, applying recent insights from neural network models. Joint models are among the most promising things about the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

There are two contributions here. First, the authors present a new transition system that seems better than the Henderson (2008) system it is based on. The second contribution is to show that neural networks succeed on this problem where earlier linear models struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn which features to extract. However, I think there is another advantage of neural networks here that may be worth mentioning. In a linear model, you need to learn one weight for each feature/class pair. This means that if you learn two problems jointly, you will have to learn many more parameters. Neural networks are much more economical in this regard.

I doubt that the transition-system will work as well with other different neural network models, for example, Endor's (2016) global beam-search model. Many other orthogonal improvements could be made. I hope that the authors' method will yield cutting-edge results in detail.

It would be good to see an attempt to obtain a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if it is only in an appendix or in a follow-up work. At first glance, it seems similar to the Arc-Eager Oracle. The M-S action buffer excludes all semantic arcs between words at the beginning of the word and semantic stack, and the M-D action buffer excludes all semantic arcs between words at the top of the word and buffer. The L and R actions seem to exclude each reverse arc, and none of the others.
