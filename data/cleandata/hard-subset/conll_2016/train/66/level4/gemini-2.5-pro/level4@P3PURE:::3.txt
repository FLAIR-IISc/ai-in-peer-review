This paper revisits the important and overdue problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing by applying recent advances in transition-based neural network models. The success of these models makes their application to joint parsing a particularly promising research direction.

The paper presents two main contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system that appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, it demonstrates that a neural network model can succeed at this joint task where previous linear models have struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn feature representations. It may also be worth noting another advantage: whereas a linear model must learn a weight for each feature/class pair, a number that grows substantially when learning two tasks jointly, a neural network is significantly more economical with its parameters in such a setting.

The proposed transition system is a strong contribution, and I suspect it would also perform well when integrated with other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor et al. (2016). The authors' approach opens the door to many other orthogonal improvements, and I expect that future extensions of this method could achieve state-of-the-art results.

A valuable direction for future work would be the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, which could be included in an appendix or a follow-up paper. At first glance, the oracle appears to have similarities to the standard arc-eager oracle. The M-S action seems to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the front of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions each appear to exclude only the reverse arc.
