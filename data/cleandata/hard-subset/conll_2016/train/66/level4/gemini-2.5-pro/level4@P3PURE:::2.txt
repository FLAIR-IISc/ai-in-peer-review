**General Comments**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two main differences from the original work: 1) the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than just for the top elements of the stack or the most recent parser configurations, and 2) the implementation of a simple greedy search algorithm.

The key idea is to leverage stack LSTMs, enabling the vector that represents the parser's state to retain a memory of long-range syntactic features. These features, such as the path between a predicate and a candidate role filler's head, are known to be crucial for semantic role labeling. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. The authors compare their system's performance against previously published results. The comparison shows that the proposed system performs well relative to the systems from the 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, but is less competitive with more recent approaches (as noted at the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize, however, that their system does not require any hand-crafted features and is fast due to its simple greedy search algorithm.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial body of work. It builds upon the recently popular Stack LSTMs, applying them to the Henderson et al. (2008) algorithm, which now appears to have been somewhat visionary.

I have reservations, however, regarding the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, as this makes the results difficult to compare with some of the cited works that use more complex search methods. It seems that implementing beam search would not have required significant additional work or space, and providing those results would have strengthened the comparison.

**More Detailed Comments / Questions**

**Section 2:**
*   An explanation for the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would help clarify the target task.
*   A summary of the differences between the transition set used in this work and the one from Henderson et al. (2008) should be provided. In the paper's current form, it is difficult to discern which transitions are directly reused from Henderson et al. and which are new or have been slightly modified.

**Section 3.3:**
*   Why is it necessary to create a representation by concatenating the predicate word with its disambiguated sense? This approach seems redundant, given that the disambiguated senses are already specific to a particular predicate.

**Section 4:**
*   The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual experiments. For clarity, the CoNLL 2008 shared task focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the evaluation to several other languages. The presentation should more clearly distinguish the setup and results for these two different tasks.
