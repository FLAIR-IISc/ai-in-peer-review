This paper introduces a Stack LSTM parser that builds upon the foundational work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing and the stack LSTM architecture from Dyer et al. (2015). Specifically, the authors adapt the transition system from the former to the neural architecture of the latter, achieving compelling results when benchmarked against other joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

This is a strong submission with many commendable qualities. The paper is exceptionally well-written and concepts are explained effectively. The review of related literature is thorough, and the experimental results are both significant and thought-provoking. The methodology is robust, with one minor observation regarding the Chinese embeddings, which suggests that high-quality embeddings can be as influential as a sophisticated model architecture. Furthermore, the system is described in sufficient detail, the choice of hyperparameters is well-reasoned, and the discussion provides valuable insights.

The primary weakness, if any, is the limited novelty of the proposed system. The approach is an effective synthesis of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new method, as Henderson et al. established the basis for semi-synchronized joint parsing several years ago, and Dyer et al. introduced the stack LSTM more recently. That being said, the development of such a parser feels like a logical and necessary next step for the field. In this sense, the contribution is both timely and welcome, and the authors have done an excellent job in its design and evaluation.
