- Revisits joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing with a greedy, linear-time transition-based parser using stack LSTMs to learn full parser-state representations without hand-crafted features.  
- Demonstrates that stack LSTMs can effectively learn features for the complex joint parsing task, succeeding where prior linear models were limited.  
- Proposes a novel transition system for joint parsing that appears to improve on the Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) system.  
- Applies modern neural representation learning to an important joint parsing problem, showing dense parser-state representations outperform local-feature joint models.  
- Reports strong empirical results, achieving the best performance among joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, validating learned representations over hand-crafted syntactic features.  
- Provides a solid foundation for future work, noting that orthogonal improvements (e.g., beam search, alternative architectures) could further boost performance.  
- Weakness: paper underemphasizes parameter-efficiency as another key neural advantage—compact shared representations versus combinatorial parameter growth in linear models.  
- Weakness: exclusive focus on a greedy stack-LSTM model makes it hard to separate the benefits of the novel transition system from the chosen neural architecture; other decoding strategies might yield different accuracy.  
- Question: Have you considered or analyzed a dynamic oracle for this transition system to enable non-greedy search (e.g., beam search), and how similar is it to the arc-eager oracle?
