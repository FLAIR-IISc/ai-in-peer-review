This paper provides a welcome return to the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances from neural network models. The ability to build joint models is one of the most promising applications of modern transition-based neural parsers.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system that appears to improve upon the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, it demonstrates that a neural network can succeed at this joint task, which had proven challenging for previous linear models. The authors attribute this success to the network's ability to learn feature representations automatically, but another key advantage is worth noting. In a linear model, a distinct weight must be learned for each feature-class pair, causing the number of parameters to grow substantially when learning two problems jointly. Neural networks are considerably more parameter-efficient in this respect.

I suspect the proposed transition system is not specific to the model presented and could be successfully integrated with other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search parser of Andor et al. (2016). Given that many other orthogonal improvements are possible, extensions to this method are likely to achieve state-of-the-art results.

A valuable direction for future work would be the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, perhaps presented in an appendix or a follow-up paper. At first glance, the system bears some resemblance to an arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action appears to preclude all semantic arcs between the first word on the buffer and words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action precludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and words on the buffer. The L and R actions each seem to exclude only the reverse arc.
