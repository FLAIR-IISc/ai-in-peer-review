### Summary and Contributions

This paper revisits the problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, applying recent advances in neural network models, specifically stack LSTMs. The proposed model is a transition-based parser that learns a vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) to make greedy, linear-time parsing decisions. The authors demonstrate that this approach, which uses no hand-crafted features, achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that stack LSTMs can effectively model the complex state of a joint syntactic-semantic parser.** The model's success in outperforming previous joint models, which relied on more limited state representations and feature engineering, highlights the power of learning dense representations for the entire algorithmic state.
2.  **The development of a fast, simple, and effective greedy parser for a complex structured prediction task.** By obviating the need for expensive feature extraction and using a linear-time inference algorithm, the parser is highly practical and establishes a new and powerful baseline for future research in joint modeling.

### Strengths

1.  **Timely and Significant Application of Neural Methods:** The paper successfully applies a powerful and modern technique (stack LSTMs) to a classic and important NLP problem. Given the success of neural transition-based parsers for syntactic parsing, this work is a logical and overdue extension to the more challenging joint parsing task, and the strong results validate the approach.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Among Joint Models:** The model surpasses all previous joint parsing systems on the standard English benchmarks. This is a significant result, demonstrating that automatically learned representations can be more effective than the carefully engineered, locally-scoped features used by prior systems like Henderson et al. (2013).

3.  **Efficiency and Simplicity:** The parser is greedy and operates in linear time, making it very fast and suitable for practical applications. The elegance of the feature-free approach is a major advantage, removing the need for domain expertise and laborious feature engineering that characterized earlier systems.

4.  **Excellent Foundation for Future Work:** The proposed architecture is a strong starting point for further improvements. As the model is largely orthogonal to techniques like beam search (e.g., Andor et al., 2016) or the integration of more sophisticated pre-trained embeddings, it is likely that future extensions incorporating such methods could lead to further performance gains, potentially closing the gap with top-performing pipeline systems.

5.  **Insightful Model Ablations:** The experiments comparing the Joint model to Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid variants provide a clear and controlled analysis of the benefits of joint learning within this framework. This strengthens the paper's conclusions regarding the mutual benefit of syntax and semantics in the proposed architecture.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core transition system is a straightforward extension of the Henderson et al. (2013) algorithm, with the main novelty being the addition of the `M-SELF` action. The primary contribution lies not in the parsing algorithm itself, but in the successful application of a powerful statistical learning model (stack LSTMs) to it.

2.  **Gap with State-of-the-Art Pipeline Systems:** While the parser is the best-performing joint model, it does not match the overall state-of-the-art set by pipeline systems that leverage global features, optimal decoding, and external resources. This is an acknowledged limitation, but it frames the contribution as an advance for a specific class of models (greedy, joint) rather than a breakthrough for the task as a whole.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Oracle Training:** The paper does not discuss the oracle used to generate the gold transition sequences for training. For a complex, interleaved transition system like this one, the design of a static or dynamic oracle is non-trivial and has important implications for the model's theoretical capabilities and practical performance. A brief discussion of the oracle would strengthen the paper's technical depth.

### Questions to Authors

Could you please elaborate on the oracle used to generate the training data? At first glance, the conditions for a dynamic oracle seem complex due to the interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions. For example, does an `M-SHIFT` action preclude any future semantic arcs between the shifted word and the remaining words on the buffer? Similarly, does an `M-REDUCE` action preclude arcs between the reduced word and the buffer? A clarification of these conditions, even if a static oracle was used, would be very helpful for understanding the model's search space.
