This paper revisits the important problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, effectively applying modern transition-based neural network techniques. The recent success of neural parsers has renewed interest in joint models, making this work both timely and relevant.

The paper offers two principal contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system for the joint task, which appears to be an improvement upon the system proposed by Henderson et al. (2008). Second, they demonstrate that a neural network model can achieve strong performance on this task, a domain where previous efforts with linear models have faced significant challenges.

The authors rightly attribute their model's success to the neural network's ability to learn feature representations automatically. However, another key advantage, which the authors might consider discussing, is the parameter efficiency of neural networks in a joint learning setting. Whereas linear models require a distinct weight for each feature-class pair—leading to a combinatorial explosion of parameters for multiple tasks—a neural network can learn shared, compact representations, making it inherently more economical for joint modeling.

The proposed transition system is well-designed and appears flexible enough to be integrated with other neural parsing architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor et al. (2016). Given the potential for this and other orthogonal improvements, future extensions of this work show significant promise for achieving state-of-the-art results on this challenging task.

A valuable addition to this work, perhaps in an appendix or future publication, would be the derivation of a dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system. The system shows some similarity to an arc-eager oracle; for instance, the M-S action appears to preclude semantic arcs between the head of the buffer and words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action precludes arcs between the top of the syntactic stack and words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to function by excluding the creation of a reverse arc. A formal analysis of a dynamic oracle would further solidify the theoretical underpinnings of this promising system.
