- Summary: A greedy, transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs learns full parser-state representations without hand-crafted features, achieving SOTA among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and fast linear-time performance.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates a highly effective feature-free, stack-LSTM based approach for joint parsing that rivals strong pipelined systems.

- Contribution 2: Presents a conceptually simple, computationally efficient, and extensible joint structured-prediction model (greedy inference, linear time).

- Strength 1: Excellent empirical results—significantly outperforms prior joint models and matches several feature-engineered pipelines.

- Strength 2: Model simplicity and efficiency—automatic feature learning, greedy decoding, and fast parsing (CoNLL 2009 test in <3 minutes on one core).

- Strength 3: Thorough ablation and comparisons—clear controlled experiments (Joint vs Hybrid vs Semantics-only) showing benefits of joint modeling.

- Strength 4: Promising foundation—architecture is general and amenable to orthogonal improvements (beam search, char embeddings, more data).

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in the transition system—largely builds on Henderson et al. (2013) with only the M-SELF addition.

- Weakness 2: No beam search comparison—omits evaluation of beam vs greedy, leaving potential accuracy gains unexplored.

- Weakness 3: Incomplete justification of neural advantages—paper stresses automatic feature learning but underemphasizes parameter sharing benefits of neural models.

- Question 1: Have the authors considered a dynamic oracle to train recovery from errors, and what are the challenges for a synchronized syntactic-semantic oracle?

- Question 2: Can the authors elaborate on the role of parameter sharing in the LSTM representations versus traditional linear models with large action-specific parameter sets?
