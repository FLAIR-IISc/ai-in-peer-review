**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly models syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The core of the method is the application of stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer), thereby avoiding the need for hand-crafted feature engineering. The model is an effective integration of the joint transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). The authors evaluate their parser on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing models.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The first successful application of stack LSTMs to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The work demonstrates that learning a holistic representation of the parser state is a viable and powerful alternative to feature engineering, resulting in a model that is both fast (linear time) and highly accurate.
2.  A strong empirical result, establishing a new state-of-the-art for joint models on the standard CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks. The paper includes a thorough evaluation with valuable ablation studies that clarify the benefits of the joint approach over syntax-only and semantics-only variants.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Performance and Efficiency:** The proposed model achieves the best parsing performance to date among systems that jointly learn syntax and semantics on the CoNLL 2008/2009 tasks. This strong result is achieved with a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm, which is reported to be significantly faster than other top-performing systems. This combination of high accuracy and computational efficiency makes the system compelling for practical applications.
2.  **Methodological Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the underlying transition system, the stack LSTM architecture, and how the two are integrated. The model architecture, composition functions, and training details are described with sufficient clarity to facilitate reproducibility.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors include several insightful model variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that effectively probe the contribution of the joint modeling components. The discussion is balanced and honest, correctly positioning the work with respect to more complex, feature-engineered pipeline systems and acknowledging limitations such as out-of-domain performance drops. The multilingual results further underscore the model's robustness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The main weakness of the work is its limited methodological novelty. The proposed system is a skillful and effective combination of two existing lines of research: the semi-synchronized transition system for joint parsing (Henderson et al., 2013) and the stack LSTM for parsing (Dyer et al., 2015). While the resulting system is new and its performance is impressive, the work is more of an application of existing advanced techniques to a new problem rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new parsing method.
2.  **Unclear Contribution of Embeddings vs. Model Architecture:** The paper notes that the strong performance on Chinese is heavily reliant on the use of pretrained "structured skip-gram" embeddings. This raises the question of how much of the performance gain across all languages is due to the power of these embeddings versus the sophistication of the stack LSTM architecture. An ablation study or baseline using a simpler classifier with the same high-quality embeddings would have helped to better isolate the architectural contribution of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the strong reliance on pretrained embeddings for the Chinese results, could you provide more detail on the performance gap between using and not using these embeddings? To better understand the contribution of the stack LSTM architecture itself, have you considered a simpler baseline that uses the same powerful embeddings but with a standard feed-forward classifier that only considers features from the top elements of the stacks and buffer?
