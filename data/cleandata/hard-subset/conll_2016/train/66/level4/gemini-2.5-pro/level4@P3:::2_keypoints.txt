- Joint transition-based parser that produces syntactic and semantic dependency graphs using stack LSTMs to learn a dense state representation.  
- Architectural change: replaces hand-crafted parser-state features with learned vectors for syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history.  
- Uses a simple, fast greedy inference algorithm and achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 with competitive speed.  
- Main contribution: effective application of stack LSTMs to learn a feature-free, holistic parser state that captures long-distance syntactic information for SRL.  
- Main contribution: a fast, high-performing joint parser demonstrating a favorable speed–accuracy trade-off without complex decoding.  
- Strength: shows feature-free representation learning can replace manual feature engineering for joint syntactic and semantic analysis.  
- Strength: model is simple and efficient (linear-time), offering practical advantages over pipelined or globally decoded systems.  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation and ablations (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that quantify joint benefits and analyze overfitting.  
- Weakness: exclusive use of greedy inference limits fair comparison to beam-search systems and leaves unclear how much performance is due to representation versus search.  
- Weakness: unclear delineation of algorithmic novelty relative to Henderson et al. (2013); lacks concise comparison of transition inventory, constraints, and interleaving logic.  
- Weakness: underperforms top pipelined SRL systems, suggesting incremental greedy parsing may miss information available from globally optimized syntactic trees.  
- Question: why use only greedy decoding—please provide results with a small beam (e.g., k=8 or 16) to separate search effects from representation.  
- Question: motivation for predicate composition gd(v,p) that combines word and sense vectors—have you tried using only the sense embedding?  
- Question: please provide an explicit summary of differences from Henderson et al. (2013) beyond the M-SELF transition (actions, constraints, interleaving).
