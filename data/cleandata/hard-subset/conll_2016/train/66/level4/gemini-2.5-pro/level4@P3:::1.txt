### Summary

This paper presents a transition-based parser that performs joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model's architecture is a well-conceived integration of two recent lines of work: the synchronized transition system for joint parsing from Henderson et al. (2013) and the Stack Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for syntactic parsing from Dyer et al. (2015). The core idea is to use Stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) without relying on any hand-crafted features. The parser makes greedy, linear-time decisions based on this learned representation. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all previous joint models.

### Contributions

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance for Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parsing:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that a greedy, transition-based parser using Stack LSTMs can achieve state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on the competitive CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. This work successfully shows that learning a representation of the entire parser state can surpass previous joint models that used more local features or beam search.

2.  **An Efficient, Feature-Free Model for a Complex Task:** The paper presents a fast, linear-time parser for a complex joint prediction task. By obviating the need for expensive, hand-crafted feature extraction (such as syntactic path features commonly used in semantic role labeling), the model provides an elegant and efficient framework. The authors report a significant speed advantage over other high-performing systems. The promise of an open-source release further strengthens this contribution, providing a strong and practical baseline for future research.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Evaluation:** The paper's claims are well-supported by strong results on standard, competitive benchmarks. The model consistently outperforms previous joint parsing systems, including the Henderson et al. (2013) system from which its transition system is derived. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering both the 2008 and 2009 CoNLL shared tasks, including multilingual datasets, and providing insightful comparisons with various model variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint).

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, from the transition system to the Stack LSTM architecture and composition functions. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, including data preprocessing, hyperparameter choices, and training procedures, which instills confidence in the work and facilitates reproducibility.

3.  **Elegant Model Design and Efficiency:** The proposed model is a clean and effective combination of existing powerful techniques. It achieves its strong performance using a simple greedy inference algorithm, in contrast to more complex systems that rely on beam search, global reranking, or intricate feature engineering. The resulting parser is not only accurate but also very fast, making it highly practical for downstream applications.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of their results. The analysis of the different model variants (e.g., the Hybrid model outperforming the Joint model on out-of-domain data) offers valuable insights into the model's behavior and potential overfitting. The authors are also commendably clear about the limitations of their work, correctly positioning it relative to state-of-the-art pipelined systems that still hold an performance edge.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** The main weakness of this work is that it is primarily an application of existing methods to a new combination of tasks. The transition system is adopted from Henderson et al. (2013), and the Stack LSTM architecture is directly from Dyer et al. (2015). While the integration is non-trivial and the results are significant, the paper does not introduce a new parsing algorithm or a fundamental new neural architecture. The contribution is more empirical and engineering-focused than theoretical.

2.  **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art Pipelined Systems:** While the parser is the best-performing *joint* model, it does not achieve the overall state-of-the-art on these tasks. As the authors acknowledge, top-performing systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Roth and Woodsend, 2014; Lei et al., 2015) are often pipelined approaches that leverage rich syntactic features extracted from a complete parse tree. This suggests that the learned representations from the Stack LSTM, while powerful, may not yet fully capture the same crucial long-distance or structural information that explicit syntactic features provide for semantic role labeling.

3.  **Unclear Contribution of Embeddings vs. Model:** The paper notes that "our Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for its superior performance; without them (not shown), it was on par with the others." This raises a concern about the extent to which the model's strong performance, particularly in multilingual settings, is attributable to the novel architecture versus the power of high-quality, pre-trained embeddings. Quantifying this effect would help to better isolate the contribution of the model itself.

### Questions to Authors

1.  As mentioned in the discussion of the multilingual results, the Chinese parser's superior performance is heavily reliant on the pre-trained word embeddings. Could you provide the specific results for the Chinese parser both with and without these embeddings? This would greatly help in assessing the relative contributions of the model architecture and the pre-trained representations.

2.  Your analysis shows that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on the out-of-domain test set, which you attribute to overfitting in the Joint model. Do you have any intuition about what specific aspects of the joint training process might be leading to this overfitting on the WSJ training data? For instance, are there particular syntactic phenomena that correlate with semantic roles in WSJ but not in the Brown corpus?
