This paper offers a timely re-examination of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. The development of effective joint models stands as one of the key promises of successful transition-based neural parsers.

The work presents two primary contributions. The first is a novel transition system that appears to be an improvement upon the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. The second contribution is demonstrating the success of a neural network on this task, a problem where linear models have previously faced significant challenges. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's capacity for automatic feature extraction. However, another advantage of the neural network, which may be worth discussing, is its parameter efficiency. In a linear model, a distinct weight must be learned for every feature-class combination, causing the number of parameters to grow substantially when learning two problems jointly. The neural network architecture is considerably more economical in this regard.

The proposed transition-system would likely be compatible with other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model from Andor (2016). Given that many other complementary improvements could be integrated, it is expected that future extensions of this method will produce state-of-the-art results.

It would be valuable to see an attempt to formulate a dynamic oracle for this transition system, perhaps in an appendix or as part of subsequent work. A preliminary analysis suggests a resemblance to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action seems to preclude all semantic arcs between the word at the head of the buffer and words currently on the semantic stack, while the M-D action precludes semantic arcs between the topmost word on the stack and words remaining in the buffer. The L and R actions appear to exclude the reverse arc, but no others.
