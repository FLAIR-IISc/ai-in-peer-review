**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that performs joint syntactic and semantic dependency analysis. The model's architecture is inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008), but it replaces hand-crafted features with learned vector representations of the entire parser state—including the stacks and buffer—using stack LSTMs. Inference is conducted via a linear-time greedy algorithm. The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared task datasets, where it outperforms previous joint models and demonstrates competitive performance against many pipeline systems of that era, while being significantly faster than more recent state-of-the-art models.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The successful application of stack LSTMs to learn a comprehensive representation of the parser state for the complex task of *joint* syntactic and semantic parsing. This demonstrates that rich, implicitly learned features can effectively replace the need for hand-engineered, arbitrarily-scoped syntactic features (e.g., predicate-argument paths) that are critical for semantic role labeling.
2.  An efficient and high-performing joint parser. By combining the learned state representations with a simple greedy search, the system achieves strong results on standard benchmarks while maintaining a fast, linear-time runtime, making it a practical tool for downstream applications.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective Application of Representation Learning:** The paper convincingly extends the stack LSTM approach from syntactic parsing to the more challenging joint syntactic-semantic domain. By learning a single vector representation for the entire parser state, the model implicitly captures the long-distance syntactic information crucial for semantic role labeling without requiring explicit and expensive feature extraction. This is a well-executed application of modern representation learning techniques to a classic structured prediction problem.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model establishes a new state-of-the-art among joint parsing systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks. It clearly surpasses the performance of the Henderson et al. (2013) system, upon which its transition system is based, providing strong evidence for the superiority of its learned state representations over more localized, feature-based methods.
3.  **High Efficiency:** A key strength of the system is its efficiency. The greedy, linear-time inference algorithm results in very fast parsing speeds, as demonstrated by the runtime comparison with a recent state-of-the-art system. This makes the model highly attractive for practical applications where a trade-off between accuracy and speed is necessary.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Inference Strategy:** The paper's exclusive reliance on a greedy inference algorithm is a significant limitation. While this choice maximizes speed, it makes direct performance comparisons with state-of-the-art systems that employ beam search or global inference difficult. The reported gains from the learned representations might be partially offset by the limitations of a purely greedy approach. Including results from a simple beam search would have provided a more complete assessment of the model's capabilities and would not have required substantial additional implementation effort.
2.  **Lack of Clarity on Algorithmic Novelty:** The paper builds heavily on the transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but fails to clearly delineate its own modifications. While a new `M-SELF` transition is introduced, a concise summary comparing the full transition set and constraints against the prior work is absent. This ambiguity makes it difficult for the reader to precisely isolate the novel algorithmic contributions beyond the integration of stack LSTMs.
3.  **Confusing Presentation of Multilingual Results:** The organization of the experimental section is somewhat unclear. The CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English results are presented in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, but the multilingual results, which are part of the CoNLL 2009 task, are discussed much later in Section 5. A more integrated presentation of the CoNLL 2009 experiments would improve the paper's readability and logical flow.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 3.3, the composition function for predicates, `gd(v,p)`, combines the vector for a word `v` with a vector for its disambiguated sense `p`. Since predicate senses (e.g., `run.01`) are already specific to a lemma (`run`), this composition appears potentially redundant. Could you clarify the motivation for this design and whether it provides a tangible benefit over simply using a learned embedding for the full sense ID?
2.  Could you provide a more explicit summary of the differences between your transition system and the one used by Henderson et al. (2013)? Beyond the new `M-SELF` transition, are there other modifications to the set of actions or the constraints that govern them?
3.  For context, it would be very helpful to understand the performance impact of the greedy decoding strategy. Have you performed any experiments with beam search? This would help disentangle the gains from the representation learning from the potential losses of a simple search algorithm and clarify how the model might perform in a less constrained setting.
