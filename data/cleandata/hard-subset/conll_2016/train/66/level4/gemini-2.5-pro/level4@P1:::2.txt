### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependencies. The approach is inspired by the synchronized, transition-based framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) but introduces two key modifications. First, it uses stack LSTMs to learn a vector representation of the entire parser state (stacks and buffer), eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. Second, it relies on a simple greedy inference algorithm. The central motivation is that the stack LSTM's summary of the parser state can implicitly capture long-range and structural information (such as the syntactic path between a predicate and an argument), which is known to be crucial for semantic role labeling but typically requires expensive feature extraction from a complete parse tree.

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared task datasets. The proposed model outperforms previous joint parsing systems and demonstrates competitive performance against many of the systems from the original shared tasks. While it does not reach the performance of state-of-the-art systems that use complex, hand-engineered features or global inference, the authors highlight its speed and simplicity as significant advantages.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of stack LSTMs to learn a comprehensive representation of the parser state for joint syntactic and semantic parsing. This approach successfully replaces traditional, manually engineered features with learned representations, demonstrating a modern and effective modeling strategy for this complex task.

2.  A secondary contribution is the demonstration that a fast, greedy, feature-free parser can achieve strong results, outperforming previous, more complex joint models on standard benchmarks. This highlights the power of the learned representations, which are rich enough to support accurate decisions even without a more complex search algorithm.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Modern Modeling:** The paper successfully integrates a powerful representation learning technique (stack LSTMs) into a well-established joint parsing framework. By learning a representation of the entire parser state, the model circumvents the need for the complex, hand-crafted features that have traditionally dominated SRL systems. This is a clean and compelling approach that demonstrates the power of these methods for structured prediction. The work is a natural and well-executed extension of recent advances in neural dependency parsing to the more complex joint syntax-semantics setting.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results for a Joint Model:** The model establishes a new state of the art among *joint* parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets, convincingly surpassing the performance of the Henderson et al. systems from which it is derived, as well as other contemporary joint models. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed representation learning approach.

3.  **Efficiency and Simplicity:** The model is both fast and conceptually simple. By using a greedy, linear-time algorithm and avoiding expensive feature extraction, the parser is highly efficient, making it practical for real-world applications. This stands in contrast to many high-performing systems that rely on slower, global inference or feature-rich pipelines. The fact that this simple architecture performs so well is a significant strength.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Exclusive Reliance on Greedy Inference:** While speed is presented as a key advantage, the exclusive use of a greedy algorithm is a notable limitation. This choice makes it difficult to fairly compare the model's representational power against state-of-the-art systems that employ beam search or other global inference methods. It leaves open the question of whether the performance gap relative to top systems is due to the model's inherent limitations or simply the search algorithm. Including results with even a small beam search would have provided a more complete picture of the model's capabilities and would likely not have required significant additional work or manuscript space.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Algorithmic Novelty vs. Prior Work:** The paper could be clearer in delineating its contributions relative to Henderson et al. (2013). While the core modeling and learning approach is new, the transition system is largely adopted from their work. A more explicit summary or comparison table detailing the exact differences in the transition set (e.g., the new M-SELF transition) versus what is directly reused would improve the paper's clarity and help readers quickly grasp the precise algorithmic novelties.

3.  **Performance Gap to Pipelined State of the Art:** While the model surpasses previous *joint* systems, it still lags behind the overall state-of-the-art systems on these tasks, as acknowledged by the authors (Table 3). These top-performing systems often use pipelined architectures with rich syntactic features and global inference. Although the authors' model is intentionally simpler and faster, this performance gap raises questions about what is lost by abandoning full syntactic parses as a feature source and whether the joint model, as implemented, is truly capturing all the necessary syntactic information for high-end semantic analysis.

### **Questions to Authors**

*   **Section 2:** A brief comment on the distinction between core roles (e.g., A1) and continuation roles (e.g., C-A1) in the data would be helpful for readers not deeply familiar with the CoNLL shared task annotation style. This would clarify the task being solved without requiring external reading.
*   **Section 3.3:** The composition function `gd(v, p)` combines the vector for the predicate word `v` with the vector for its disambiguated sense `p`. Since a sense (e.g., 'expect.01') is already specific to a lemma ('expect'), this composition seems potentially redundant. Could you clarify the motivation for this? Have you experimented with using only the sense embedding `p` to represent the disambiguated predicate?
*   **Section 4:** The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is slightly confusing regarding the multilingual experiments. Section 4.1 discusses CoNLL 2008 (English-only) but also presents hyperparameters for multiple languages. Section 4.2 discusses CoNLL 2009, which is the multilingual task, but the main multilingual results appear much later in Table 4. Could the experimental setup be reorganized for better clarity, perhaps by grouping all English experiments first, followed by a distinct section for the multilingual setup and results?
