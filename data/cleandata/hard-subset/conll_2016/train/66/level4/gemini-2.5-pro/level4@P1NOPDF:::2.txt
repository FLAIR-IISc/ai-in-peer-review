General comments
================
This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two main departures from the original work:
- Vector representations are used for the entire parser state, rather than just for the top elements of the stack or a limited history of parser configurations.
- The parser employs a simple greedy search algorithm.

The key idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so that the vector representing the parser state can encode long-range syntactic features. Such features, like the syntactic path between a predicate and a candidate argument, are known to be critical for semantic role labeling.

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 shared task datasets. The authors compare their system’s performance to previously reported results, showing that it performs favorably against other systems from 2008 and 2009, but does not achieve the performance of more recent models (as noted at the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize, however, that their system is fast due to its greedy algorithm and does not require any hand-crafted features.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial body of work. It effectively applies the now-popular stack LSTM architecture to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which in retrospect appears visionary.

My main reservation concerns the choice of a simple greedy search algorithm. This decision makes the results difficult to compare directly with many of the cited works that use more powerful search methods. Providing results from a beam search, for instance, would likely not have required significant additional work or space and would have strengthened the paper's empirical claims.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
- A comment on the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would help clarify the target task for the reader.
- The paper should also provide a summary of the differences between the transition set used in this work and the one in Henderson et al. (2008). In its current form, it is difficult to distinguish which components are directly reused and which are new or slightly modified.

Section 3.3
- Why are the representations for a predicate formed by concatenating the word and its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, as a given sense is already specific to a particular predicate.

Section 4
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual evaluation. For clarity, it might be helpful to remind the reader that the CoNLL 2008 shared task focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 task extended the evaluation to several other languages. This context would make the structure of the results section easier to follow.
