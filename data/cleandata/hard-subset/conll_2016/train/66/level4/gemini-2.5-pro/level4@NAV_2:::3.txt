### Summary of the paper
This paper revisits the problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing by applying a transition-based parser that utilizes stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the complete parser state. The proposed model is greedy, operates in linear time, and eschews hand-crafted features. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A successful demonstration that modern representation learning techniques, specifically stack LSTMs, can be effectively applied to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The model's success, where previous linear models have shown more limited results, is a significant finding, particularly its ability to automatically learn relevant features from the entire parser state.
2.  The development of a novel transition system for joint parsing, which appears to be an improvement upon the Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) system on which it is based.

### Strengths
1.  This work addresses the important and challenging problem of joint parsing by effectively applying recent advances in neural network models. The paper shows that learning a dense representation of the parser state can outperform previous joint models that relied on more local features, providing a compelling direction for future research in this area.
2.  The empirical results are strong. The model achieves the best performance among joint learning systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. This validates the central claim that automatically learned representations can replace the expensive, hand-crafted syntactic features typically used in high-performance pipelined systems.
3.  The paper provides a solid foundation upon which further improvements can be built. As the authors themselves note, many orthogonal advances (e.g., global decoding strategies like beam search, alternative neural architectures) could be integrated with the proposed transition system. This suggests that extensions of this work have the potential to achieve state-of-the-art performance.

### Weaknesses
1.  The paper attributes the model's success to the neural network's ability to learn features automatically. While this is a key factor, the discussion could be strengthened by acknowledging another critical advantage of neural networks in this joint setting: parameter economy. Linear models require learning a distinct weight for each feature-label combination, which can lead to a combinatorial explosion of parameters for a joint task. In contrast, neural networks learn shared, compact representations, making them inherently more scalable and efficient for multi-task problems like this one.
2.  The paper focuses exclusively on a greedy, stack-LSTM based model. While fast, this makes it difficult to disentangle the contribution of the novel transition system from the specific neural architecture chosen. It seems likely that the transition system itself is a key contribution that could perform equally well, or perhaps better in terms of accuracy, when paired with other neural decoding strategies, such as the global beam-search models that have recently shown strong performance in syntactic parsing.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The model is trained using a static oracle derived from the gold-standard parse trees. Have you considered the possibility of developing a dynamic oracle for this transition system? It seems prima facie similar to the arc-eager oracle, but a more formal analysis, even if brief, would be valuable for enabling future work on non-greedy search algorithms with this system.
