### General Comments

This paper presents a transition-based dependency parser that performs joint syntactic and semantic parsing, drawing inspiration from the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two primary contributions: the use of vector representations for the entire parser state, rather than just for top stack elements or recent configurations, and the implementation of a simple greedy search algorithm.

The core idea is to leverage Stack LSTMs to create a comprehensive vector representation of the parser's state. This allows the model to encode long-distance syntactic features, such as the path between a predicate and a candidate argument, which are known to be crucial for accurate semantic role labeling.

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. The authors compare their system's performance against previously published results, demonstrating that it is competitive with systems from the 2008/2009 era but falls short of more recent, state-of-the-art models (as shown in Table 3). However, the authors emphasize that their system has the advantages of not requiring any hand-crafted features and being computationally efficient due to its greedy decoding strategy.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial body of work that successfully applies modern LSTM-based techniques to the Henderson et al. (2008) framework, which this work shows was forward-thinking. My main reservation concerns the choice of a simple greedy algorithm. While this approach benefits speed, it makes the results difficult to compare directly with many of the cited works that use more complex search methods like beam search. Including results from a beam search decoder, for example, would not have significantly increased the paper's length and would have enabled a more direct and compelling comparison with other leading systems.

### Detailed Comments & Questions

**Section 2: Task and Model**

*   Could the authors comment on the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links in the data? An explanation would help clarify the specific details of the target task.
*   The paper should explicitly summarize the differences between its set of transitions and the one proposed by Henderson et al. (2008). In its current form, it is difficult for the reader to distinguish which transitions are reused directly, which are modified, and which are entirely new.

**Section 3.3: Feature Representations**

*   Could the authors clarify the motivation for creating representations by concatenating a predicate's word embedding with its disambiguated sense embedding? This appears redundant, as a given sense is already specific to its predicate.

**Section 4: Experiments**

*   The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual evaluation. The text should more clearly state that the CoNLL 2008 task was English-only, whereas the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the evaluation to multiple languages. The presentation might be improved by restructuring these sections to first present the primary English results and then explicitly introduce the multilingual extension and its corresponding outcomes.
