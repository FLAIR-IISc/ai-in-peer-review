### **Summary**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic dependency parsing and semantic role labeling. The approach is inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008) but introduces two primary innovations: (1) the use of stack LSTMs to create a comprehensive vector representation of the entire parser state, rather than just its top elements, and (2) the adoption of a simple greedy search algorithm. The core insight is to leverage the memory capacity of stack LSTMs to capture long-range syntactic features, such as the path between a predicate and a potential argument, which are crucial for accurate semantic role labeling. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual) shared task datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against systems from that era but lagging behind more recent, feature-rich, or search-intensive methods. The authors rightly emphasize that their model is fast and does not rely on any hand-crafted features.

### **Strengths**

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and represents a substantial body of work. The application of modern neural techniques, specifically stack LSTMs, to the Henderson et al. (2008) framework is a compelling and timely contribution. This successfully revives and modernizes an approach that was arguably ahead of its time, demonstrating how deep learning can elegantly capture complex structural history in a parser's state.

A significant advantage of the proposed model is its independence from hand-crafted features, which simplifies the pipeline and improves its generality across languages. Furthermore, the system's reliance on a greedy algorithm makes it computationally efficient and fast, which is a valuable practical consideration.

### **Weaknesses**

My primary reservation concerns the exclusive use of a greedy search algorithm for decoding. While this choice contributes to the system's speed, it significantly limits the performance and makes a direct comparison with many state-of-the-art systems difficult. Most recent high-performing parsers, including several cited by the authors, rely on beam search to explore a larger search space and mitigate the impact of early decision errors.

The inclusion of experiments using even a small beam size would have greatly strengthened the paper's claims. Given the existing framework, implementing beam search would likely not have required a prohibitive amount of additional work or manuscript space, but it would have provided a much clearer picture of the model's full potential and its standing relative to contemporary systems. As it stands, it is difficult to determine whether the performance gap between this system and more recent proposals is due to the core model architecture or simply the limitations of the greedy search.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 2: Model and Transitions**
*   For readers less familiar with the specifics of the CoNLL shared tasks, a brief explanation of the distinction between core (e.g., A1) and continuation (e.g., C-A1) arguments would improve clarity.
*   The paper would benefit from a more explicit summary of the differences between the transition set used here and the one from Henderson et al. (2008). It is currently challenging for the reader to distinguish which transitions are directly reused, which are modified, and which are entirely new.

**Section 3.3: Input Representations**
*   Why is it necessary to concatenate the embeddings for the predicate word and its disambiguated sense? Since a given sense (e.g., `run.01`) is unique to its predicate (`run`), this seems to introduce redundancy. Is there a specific benefit to providing both pieces of information to the model?

**Section 4: Experiments**
*   The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is slightly confusing with respect to the datasets. The CoNLL 2008 shared task was English-only, while the CoNLL 2009 task introduced multilingual evaluation. The current structure seems to blur this distinction. It would be clearer if the paper explicitly stated that Section 4.1 focuses on the English-only 2008 dataset, and Section 4.2 addresses the multilingual 2009 dataset.
