- Summary: Transition-based joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to learn full parser-state representations, with linear-time greedy inference; evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009, outperforming prior joint models and running much faster than recent SOTA.

- Contribution 1: Applies stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics, replacing hand-crafted features with learned vector representations of the entire parser state.

- Contribution 2: Presents an efficient, high-performing joint parser that achieves strong benchmark results using simple greedy, linear-time inference.

- Strength 1: Effectively extends representation learning to capture long-distance syntactic information needed for semantic role labeling without explicit engineered features.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical results, establishing a new state-of-the-art among joint systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and outperforming the Henderson et al. (2013) baseline.

- Strength 3: High efficiency—greedy linear-time decoding yields very fast parsing suitable for downstream applications.

- Weakness 1: Reliance on greedy inference limits comparisons to beam- or globally-inferred SOTA systems and may mask search-related performance losses.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient clarity on algorithmic novelty and exact differences from Henderson et al. (2013); only M-SELF is highlighted without a concise transition/constraint comparison.

- Weakness 3: Experimental presentation is confusing—multilingual CoNLL 2009 results are poorly integrated and placed late, hurting readability.

- Question 1: Why compose word vector v with sense vector p (gd(v,p)) instead of using a single learned embedding for the full sense ID, and does this composition give measurable benefit?

- Question 2: Please provide an explicit, concise summary of differences between your transition system and Henderson et al. (2013), beyond the M-SELF transition.

- Question 3: Have you tried beam search or other non-greedy decoding, and if so, how does that affect performance and the interpretation of gains from representation learning?
