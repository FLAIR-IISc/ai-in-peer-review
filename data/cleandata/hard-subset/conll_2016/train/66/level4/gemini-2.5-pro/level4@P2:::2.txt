### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic and semantic dependency structures. The model's core innovation lies in its use of stack Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) to learn a comprehensive vector representation of the entire parser state, including the syntactic stack, semantic stack, and input buffer. This approach enables the model to implicitly capture complex, long-range features without any manual feature engineering. The parser employs a purely greedy inference algorithm, resulting in linear-time complexity. The authors evaluate their system on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared task datasets, demonstrating that their model outperforms previous joint parsing systems and achieves competitive results against other top-performing systems from that period, while being notably fast.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Application of Stack LSTMs for Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parsing:** The primary contribution is the successful application of stack LSTMs to represent the complete state of a joint parser. By encoding the contents of the syntactic stack, semantic stack, and input buffer into a single vector, the model can learn representations that implicitly capture features crucial for semantic role labeling, such as the syntactic path between a predicate and its argument, without explicit feature extraction. This feature-free approach is a significant departure from prior work that relied heavily on hand-crafted features.

2.  **An Efficient and High-Performing Greedy Joint Parser:** The paper demonstrates that a simple, greedy, linear-time parser can achieve strong results on a complex joint task. The system's performance surpasses previous joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks and is competitive with more complex pipelined systems of its time. This work effectively establishes a strong baseline for fast and accurate joint parsing, highlighting the power of learned representations to compensate for simpler search algorithms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Analysis:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments, including several ablations (e.g., "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," "Hybrid"), which effectively isolate the benefits of the joint modeling approach. This rigorous evaluation clearly demonstrates the model's effectiveness.

2.  **Methodological Elegance and Efficiency:** The system's design is clean and compelling. By replacing complex, hand-engineered feature sets with learned representations via stack LSTMs, the model simplifies the parsing pipeline. This, combined with a linear-time greedy search, results in a very fast and practical system, as evidenced by the reported parsing speeds. This favorable trade-off between accuracy and efficiency is a significant strength.

3.  **Well-Written and Well-Positioned Work:** The paper is clearly written and effectively contextualizes its contributions within the existing literature. It builds intelligently upon the algorithmic foundation of Henderson et al. (2008; 2013) and the neural architecture of Dyer et al. (2015), representing a solid and well-motivated piece of research.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparability due to Greedy Inference:** The most significant weakness is the exclusive use of a greedy search algorithm. While this ensures high speed, it makes it difficult to fairly compare the model's representational power against state-of-the-art systems that employ beam search or global inference. The performance gap reported against more recent systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015) could be attributed to the search strategy rather than the underlying model itself. Including results from a simple beam search would have provided a more complete evaluation of the model's potential and would likely not have required substantial additional space or implementation effort.

2.  **Unclear Delineation from Prior Transition Systems:** The paper is heavily based on the transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but does not clearly summarize the differences between their system and the original. While the addition of the `M-SELF` transition is noted, a more explicit comparison (e.g., in a table or dedicated paragraph) would greatly improve clarity. In its current form, it is difficult for the reader to precisely identify the algorithmic novelties beyond the introduction of the new transition.

3.  **Confusing Structure of the Experimental Section:** The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing, particularly regarding the multilingual experiments. The paper first discusses the CoNLL 2008 task, then the CoNLL 2009 task (where the various model ablations are introduced), and only later presents the multilingual results for the CoNLL 2009 task. Restructuring this section—for instance, by grouping all CoNLL 2009 experiments (English, multilingual, and ablations) together—would improve the paper's readability and logical flow.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 3.3, the composition function `gd(v,p)` is used to create a representation for a predicate by combining the vector for the word `v` with the vector for its disambiguated sense `p`. Since predicate senses are lemma-specific (e.g., "expect.01" is distinct from "run.01"), it seems that the sense vector `p` would already contain all the necessary information, making its composition with the word vector `v` potentially redundant. Could you please clarify the motivation behind this design choice?
