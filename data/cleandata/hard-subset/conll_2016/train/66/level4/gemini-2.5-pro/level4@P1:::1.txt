### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency graphs. The model adapts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and integrates it with the stack LSTM architecture proposed by Dyer et al. (2015). By learning a dense representation of the entire parser state (stacks, buffer, and action history), the model can perform greedy, linear-time inference without relying on manually engineered features. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it demonstrates strong performance compared to previous joint parsing models.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel application of stack LSTMs to joint syntactic-semantic parsing.** This is the first work to successfully extend the stack LSTM framework, previously used for syntactic parsing, to the more complex task of jointly modeling syntax and semantics. This demonstrates the architecture's flexibility and power in handling complex, structured prediction tasks.
2.  **State-of-the-art results among joint parsing models.** The proposed model significantly outperforms previous joint systems, including the one on which its transition system is based (Henderson et al.), on the standard CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks. This establishes a new performance baseline for this class of models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The primary strength of this paper is its impressive empirical results. The model sets a new state of the art among joint syntactic-semantic parsers on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks. The fact that this performance is achieved with a simple greedy inference algorithm and without any hand-crafted features highlights the effectiveness of learning representations of the complete parser state.
2.  **Clarity and Thoroughness.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide a detailed description of their transition system, the stack LSTM architecture, and the composition functions. The related work section is comprehensive, and the experimental setup and results are presented with clarity. The discussion of the results is insightful, comparing the model to a wide range of relevant systems.
3.  **Sound Methodology and Analysis.** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation, including ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that provide valuable insights into the interactions between the syntactic and semantic components. The hyperparameters are described and justified, contributing to the work's reproducibility. The analysis is honest, acknowledging where the model falls short of pipelined systems that use global features or additional resources.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty.** The primary weakness of this work lies in its methodological novelty. The proposed model is a direct and, arguably, straightforward combination of the transition system from Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and the stack LSTM architecture from Dyer et al. (2015). The paper does not introduce a fundamentally new parsing algorithm or neural architecture. However, this should not significantly detract from the paper's value. This combination is a natural and important step for the field, and the authors deserve full credit for being the first to successfully implement, evaluate, and demonstrate its effectiveness. In many ways, this is the joint parser that the community has been waiting for since the introduction of stack LSTMs.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The paper notes that the Chinese parser's superior performance is heavily reliant on the pretrained word embeddings. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model to the quality of these embeddings? For instance, how much of the performance gain on Chinese can be attributed to the embeddings versus the model architecture itself? This raises a broader question about the relative importance of high-quality, pre-trained representations versus architectural innovations within this framework.
