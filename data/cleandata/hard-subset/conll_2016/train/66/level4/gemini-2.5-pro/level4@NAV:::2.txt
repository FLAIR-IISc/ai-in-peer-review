Review- General comments
================
This paper introduces a transition-based dependency parser for joint syntactic and semantic analysis, drawing inspiration from the model proposed by Henderson et al. (2008).
The authors highlight two primary departures from the original work:
- The use of vectorial representations for the complete parser state, as opposed to only the top stack elements or recent configurations.
- The adoption of a simple greedy search algorithm.
The core contribution is the use of stack LSTMs, which enables the parser's state vector to retain memory of broad-scoped syntactic features. These features, such as the path between a predicate and a candidate role filler, are recognized as being crucial for effective semantic role labeling.
The proposed system is evaluated on the English CoNLL 2008 dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset.
The authors compare their model's performance against previously published results, demonstrating competitive performance against systems from the 2008 and 2009 period, but not against more recent state-of-the-art models (see Table 3). However, they stress that their system benefits from requiring no hand-crafted features and from the efficiency of its greedy search algorithm.
The paper is clearly written and details a substantial body of work, modernizing the visionary Henderson et al. algorithm with the now-popular LSTM architecture.
My main reservation, however, concerns the choice of a simple greedy algorithm. This decision makes the reported results difficult to compare directly with several cited works that use more complex search methods.
Including performance metrics from a beam search, for example, would not have significantly increased the workload or page count and would have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
Clarification on the distinction and concurrent presence of A1 and C-A1 links would be beneficial for a clearer understanding of the paper's target task.
The paper should include a summary contrasting the transition set used here with the one from Henderson et al. As it stands, it is challenging to discern which transitions are directly adopted from the original work and which are novel or modified.

Section 3.3
Could the authors clarify the rationale for concatenating representations of the predicate word with its disambiguated sense? This appears redundant, given that a disambiguated sense is inherently specific to a particular predicate.

Section 4
The structure of sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual experiments. It would be helpful to more clearly delineate the discussion, remembering that the CoNLL 2008 task was English-only, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task introduced multilingual data.
