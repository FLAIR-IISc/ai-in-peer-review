- Proposes a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser using stack LSTMs to encode the full parser state, removing hand-crafted features.  
- Employs a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm and reports fast, competitive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009.  
- Main contribution: novel use of stack LSTMs to combine syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer into a single learned state vector.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates a simple, efficient greedy joint parser that outperforms prior joint models and serves as a strong baseline.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance with comprehensive experiments and ablation studies showing benefits of joint modeling.  
- Strength: methodologically elegant and efficient—learned representations replace complex feature engineering with a fast linear-time parser.  
- Strength: clearly written and well-situated within prior work (builds on Henderson et al. and Dyer et al.).  
- Weakness: reliance on greedy inference limits fair comparison to beam/global methods and may understate the model’s representational power.  
- Weakness: insufficiently explicit comparison with prior transition systems (Henderson et al. 2013); differences beyond M-SELF are unclear.  
- Weakness: experimental section organization is confusing—CoNLL 2009 experiments, ablations, and multilingual results should be grouped more logically.  
- Question: why compose word vector v with sense vector p via gd(v,p) when lemma-specific sense vectors might already contain necessary information?
