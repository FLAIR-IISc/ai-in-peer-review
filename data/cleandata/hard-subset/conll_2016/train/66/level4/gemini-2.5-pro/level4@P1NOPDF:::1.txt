This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The proposed model effectively combines the semi-synchronous, transition-based system of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the Stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). By applying this powerful neural architecture to the joint parsing framework, the authors achieve strong results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against other state-of-the-art joint systems.

This is a strong, well-executed paper with several key strengths. It is well-written, clearly motivated, and the methodology is thoroughly explained. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work, properly situating their contribution within the field. The system is described in detail, the choice of hyperparameters is well-justified, and the discussion provides valuable insights. The methodology is generally sound, although I have a minor concern regarding the Chinese embeddings: the results on this dataset suggest that the quality of the word embeddings may, in some cases, be a more significant factor in performance than the sophistication of the model architecture itself.

The primary weakness of the paper, if any, is its limited novelty. The proposed system is a straightforward, albeit effective, combination of two established lines of research. As such, it does not introduce a fundamentally new parsing method, but rather integrates existing, powerful components.

However, this should be considered a minor criticism. This integration represents a logical and important next step for the field. The community has arguably been anticipating a parser that successfully merges these two advances, and this paper delivers an excellent implementation and a thorough empirical evaluation. For this reason, the work is a welcome and valuable contribution.
