This paper presents a Stack LSTM model for joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The proposed system effectively combines the semi-synchronized, transition-based framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the Stack LSTM architecture introduced by Dyer et al. (2015). The model is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it achieves strong and interesting results compared to previous state-of-the-art joint parsing systems.

The paper has many strengths. It is exceptionally well-written and clearly explained, with a thorough review of related work that effectively situates the contribution within the field. The methodology is sound, the system description is detailed, and the choice of hyperparameters is well-justified, which should facilitate reproducibility. Furthermore, the discussion of the results is insightful.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its conceptual novelty. The core contribution is an integration of two recent, influential lines of work rather than the introduction of a new parsing paradigm. The foundations for semi-synchronised joint transition-based parsing were laid by Henderson et al. several years ago, while the Stack LSTM was introduced last year by Dyer et al. As such, the proposed system can be viewed as a logical, albeit incremental, combination of these existing components.

However, this is a minor criticism. The integration itself is a valuable and non-trivial piece of engineering, resulting in a highly effective parser that many in the community have likely been anticipating. By demonstrating the successful application of the Stack LSTM to this complex joint task, the authors have provided a strong benchmark and validated an important research direction. The work is solid, the execution is excellent, and the results are significant.

A minor concern arises from the experiments on Chinese data, where the high-quality pre-trained word embeddings appear to contribute substantially to the model's performance. This observation raises an interesting question about the relative importance of sophisticated model architectures versus high-quality input representations, suggesting that the latter can be a dominant factor in performance.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper describing a solid and well-executed piece of research. While its originality is perhaps limited, it represents an important and timely contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The paper is a pleasure to read and presents a system that successfully synthesizes state-of-the-art techniques to advance the field.
