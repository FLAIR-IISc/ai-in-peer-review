### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency graphs. The model's architecture is a well-motivated combination of Henderson et al.'s (2013) synchronized transition system for joint parsing and Dyer et al.'s (2015) stack Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for learning representations of the parser state. By using stack LSTMs to represent the entire state of the syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer, the model makes decisions without relying on manually engineered features. The resulting parser is fast, with linear-time complexity, and achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that learned representations of the entire parser state can supplant hand-crafted features in the complex task of joint syntactic-semantic parsing.** The key contribution is showing that the stack LSTM architecture, previously proven for syntactic parsing, is highly effective for the more intricate joint task. This allows a simple, greedy model to outperform previous, more complex joint systems that used beam search or intricate features.

2.  **The development of a fast, high-performance joint parser.** By using a greedy inference algorithm, the parser achieves linear-time performance, making it significantly faster than state-of-the-art pipeline models. This combination of high accuracy (relative to other joint models) and efficiency makes the system a valuable practical tool.

3.  **A thorough experimental analysis, including well-designed ablation studies.** The authors compare their joint model against syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants, providing clear insights into the benefits and trade-offs of the joint learning approach on both in-domain and out-of-domain data.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance with a simple and efficient model.** The parser achieves state-of-the-art results among all previously published joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English datasets. This is a significant achievement, especially given that the model uses a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm and does not require the complex, hand-crafted features or expensive global inference common in many top-performing NLP systems. This work makes a strong case for the power of representation learning in structured prediction.

2.  **The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and methodologically sound.** The authors do an excellent job of situating their work within the context of related research, clearly explaining their transition system and model architecture, and justifying their experimental design. The inclusion of ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provides a convincing analysis of the model's components and the benefits of joint learning.

3.  **High potential for impact and reproducibility.** The parser's speed and performance make it a practical and useful tool for downstream applications. The authors' commitment to releasing an open-source implementation is commendable and will be a valuable contribution to the community, enabling further research and application.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The novelty of the method itself is limited.** The paper's main contribution is the successful application and integration of two existing lines of work: the transition system from Henderson et al. (2013) and the stack LSTM architecture from Dyer et al. (2015). While the engineering and empirical results are strong and valuable, the work does not introduce a fundamentally new parsing algorithm or neural network architecture.

2.  **The model does not outperform state-of-the-art pipeline systems.** While the paper achieves the best performance among *joint* models, it falls short of the overall state-of-the-art on these tasks, which is still held by pipeline systems that use a full syntactic parse to extract rich features for semantic role labeling. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it underscores that the learned representations from a partial, incrementally-built parse do not yet fully capture the valuable global information present in a complete syntactic tree.

3.  **The strong multilingual results appear heavily dependent on high-quality pretrained embeddings.** The paper notes that the excellent performance on Chinese, for example, is due to the use of powerful pretrained word embeddings. This raises questions about the model's robustness in scenarios where such resources are not available, and how much of the performance gain is attributable to the model architecture versus the quality of the embeddings.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance on the Chinese dataset is particularly strong. You mention this is largely due to the pretrained embeddings. Could you quantify this effect by providing the results for the Chinese parser trained without these embeddings? This would help clarify the division of credit between the model's architecture and the external resources.

2.  The `M-SELF` transition is a novel and necessary addition to handle self-dependencies in the CoNLL 2009 data. Could you provide a brief analysis of its impact? For instance, what is the accuracy on predicting these specific arcs, and what is the effect on the overall semantic F1 score if this transition is removed from the model?
