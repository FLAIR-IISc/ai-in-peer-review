### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based, greedy parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. It utilizes stack LSTMs to learn a dense representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. The model achieves state-of-the-art results among joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and demonstrates strong performance on multilingual data, all with a linear-time complexity.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Demonstration of a highly effective feature-free approach for joint parsing:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a greedy, transition-based parser using learned representations of the entire parser state can achieve competitive performance on the complex task of joint syntactic-semantic parsing. This work successfully extends the stack LSTM approach, previously shown effective for syntactic parsing, to a more complex joint task. It surpasses previous joint models and rivals several strong pipelined systems, validating the power of this approach for structured prediction problems that require integrating information from multiple linguistic levels.

2.  **An efficient and extensible model for joint structured prediction:** The paper presents a model that is both conceptually simple and computationally efficient. By combining a greedy inference algorithm with learned representations, the parser achieves linear-time performance, making it highly practical for downstream applications. This work provides a strong foundation for future research in joint modeling, as orthogonal improvements (e.g., beam search, character-level embeddings) can be readily integrated.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The model achieves excellent empirical results, setting a new state-of-the-art for joint syntactic-semantic parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English benchmarks. It significantly outperforms its direct predecessor (Henderson et al., 2013) and other prior joint models. Crucially, its performance is comparable to several strong, feature-engineered pipelined systems, which is a significant achievement for a joint model that avoids cascading errors by design.

2.  **Model Simplicity and Efficiency:** The model is conceptually simple and computationally efficient. By using a greedy inference algorithm and learning features automatically via stack LSTMs, the parser avoids both the complexity of hand-crafted feature engineering and the computational overhead of beam search. Its reported linear-time performance and fast parsing speed (parsing the CoNLL 2009 test set in under three minutes on a single core) make it highly practical for real-world applications.

3.  **Thorough Ablation and Comparative Analysis:** The paper includes a well-designed set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the joint approach. The comparisons between the `Joint`, `Hybrid`, and `Semantics-only` models clearly show that joint modeling is beneficial for in-domain performance. This controlled analysis provides valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information within this modeling framework.

4.  **Promising Foundation for Future Work:** The proposed approach is general and extensible. The core idea of learning a representation of the full parser state is not tied to this specific transition system. As the authors note, many orthogonal improvements could be integrated, such as global inference with beam search, character-based embeddings for morphology, or training on additional data sources. This work provides a strong and promising baseline for future research in joint structured prediction, with clear potential to achieve even higher performance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Transition System:** The paper builds heavily on the synchronized algorithm of Henderson et al. (2013), with the main algorithmic addition being the `M-SELF` action to handle self-dependencies. While the successful *application* and *adaptation* of this system in a neural context is a key strength, the novelty of the transition system itself is limited. The paper could be clearer in positioning its algorithmic contribution as an effective adaptation rather than a fundamentally new system.

2.  **Absence of Beam Search Comparison:** The choice of a purely greedy inference strategy, while excellent for speed, may limit the model's peak accuracy. Recent work in syntactic parsing has shown that beam search can provide significant gains over greedy decoding for neural transition-based models. While the authors' model is already strong, the lack of comparison to a beam search variant leaves open the question of how much performance is being left on the table for the sake of efficiency. Including such a comparison, even if just on the development set, would have strengthened the paper's conclusions about the speed-accuracy trade-off.

3.  **Potential for Stronger Justification of the NN's Advantage:** The authors attribute the success of the neural network to its ability to automatically learn features. While this is certainly a factor, another key advantage of neural models in joint learning settings is their capacity for parameter sharing. In a linear model, a large number of parameters are needed to score feature-action pairs, and this number grows substantially in a joint task with an expanded action set. The neural network's architecture allows for a much more compact and economical parameterization through shared representations. A brief discussion of this aspect would provide a more complete picture of why this approach is so effective.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper trains the model using a static oracle derived from the gold-standard parse. Have the authors considered developing a dynamic oracle for this transition system? A dynamic oracle allows the model to learn to recover from its own errors during training, which often improves performance. Could you elaborate on the challenges of designing such an oracle for this joint system, particularly regarding the synchronized syntactic and semantic decisions?

2.  The paper attributes the success of the neural model primarily to its ability to automatically learn features. Could you also comment on the role of parameter sharing? A traditional linear model would require separate feature weights for every action type, which can be parameter-intensive for a joint model with a large action space. To what extent do you believe the shared, dense representation learned by the LSTMs contributes to the model's efficiency and generalization, beyond just automatic feature extraction?
