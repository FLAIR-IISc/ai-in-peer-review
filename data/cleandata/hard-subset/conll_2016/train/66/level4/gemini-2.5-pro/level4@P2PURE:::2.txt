General Comments
================
This paper presents a transition-based dependency parser that performs joint syntactic and semantic parsing, inspired by the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two primary distinctions from the original model: first, the entire parser state is represented vectorially, rather than only the top elements of the stack or recent configurations; second, the parser employs a simple greedy search algorithm.

The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to create a state representation that captures long-range syntactic features, which are known to be crucial for semantic role labeling (e.g., the syntactic path between a predicate and a candidate argument).

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 shared task datasets. The authors compare their system's performance against previously published results, demonstrating that it is competitive with systems from the 2008/2009 era but is outperformed by more recent approaches (as noted at the bottom of Table 3). However, they emphasize that their system has the advantages of not requiring hand-crafted features and being fast due to its greedy search strategy.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial body of work, building upon the recently popular LSTM models and applying them to the Henderson et al. (2008) algorithm, which appears to have been visionary.

My main reservation concerns the choice of a simple greedy algorithm, which hinders direct comparability with several of the cited works that use more complex search methods. It seems that including results from a beam search would not have required significant additional effort or space and would have enabled a more direct and informative comparison.

More Detailed Comments / Questions
==================================
Section 2:
*   An explanation of the distinction between A1 and C-A1 links would help clarify the target task.
*   The paper should provide a summary of the differences between the transition set used in this work and the one from Henderson et al. (2008). In its current form, it is difficult to discern which transitions are directly reused and which are novel or modified.

Section 3.3
*   Could the authors clarify the need for a representation that concatenates the predicate word with its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, as disambiguated senses are already predicate-specific.

Section 4
*   The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to the multilingual experiments. The CoNLL 2008 shared task focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 task extended the evaluation to other languages. The presentation should more clearly delineate the setup for each dataset.
