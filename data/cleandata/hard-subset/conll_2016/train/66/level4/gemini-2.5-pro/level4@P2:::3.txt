### Summary and Contributions

This paper revisits the task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, applying a modern neural network architecture to a transition-based framework. The proposed model uses stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), removing the need for hand-crafted, discrete features. The parser makes greedy, linear-time decisions based on this learned state representation. On the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, the system outperforms all previously published joint parsing models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A successful application of representation learning to joint syntactic-semantic parsing.** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a stack LSTM-based model, which learns to represent the entire parser state, can effectively tackle this complex joint task. This approach surpasses previous joint models that relied on more limited, feature-based state representations and/or more complex search algorithms like beam search.
2.  **An effective and efficient greedy model for a joint task.** The paper shows that a simple, greedy inference procedure can be highly effective when combined with a powerful representation learner. The model's linear-time complexity and strong performance make it a practical and compelling baseline for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles an important and challenging problem.** Jointly modeling syntax and semantics is a long-standing goal in NLP, intended to mitigate error propagation in traditional pipelines. This paper provides a strong modern baseline for this task, successfully applying recent advances in neural parsing to a problem where linear models have previously struggled to show a clear advantage.
2.  **Strong empirical results and analysis.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing systems on the standard CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. The ablation studies (e.g., "Syntax-only," "Semantics-only," "Hybrid") are particularly valuable, providing clear insights into the benefits and trade-offs of the joint modeling approach compared to pipelined or single-task variants.
3.  **Model simplicity and efficiency.** By relying on learned representations, the model avoids the need for complex and expensive feature engineering, a significant bottleneck in many previous systems. The greedy, linear-time inference makes the parser very fast, which is a major practical advantage for downstream applications.
4.  **High potential for future extensions.** The authors rightly note that many orthogonal improvements from state-of-the-art systems (e.g., global inference, advanced embeddings, external knowledge) have not been incorporated. This work provides a strong and promising foundation, and it is easy to see how extensions to this method could lead to further state-of-the-art results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The parameter efficiency of the neural model is not discussed.** The authors attribute the model's success to its ability to automatically learn features. While true, another significant advantage of neural models over linear models in joint tasks is parameter efficiency. In a linear model, adding a second task requires learning a new set of weights for each feature/class pair, leading to a large increase in parameters. In contrast, the neural network can learn shared representations, making it a more compact and potentially better-generalizing model for multi-task learning. Highlighting this aspect would strengthen the motivation for the neural approach.
2.  **The novelty is primarily in application, not architecture.** The core component, the stack LSTM, is adapted directly from prior work on syntactic parsing (Dyer et al., 2015). The contribution is therefore the successful and non-trivial application of this architecture to a more complex, joint problem. The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion of why this specific architecture was chosen over other contemporary neural models, such as those employing global beam-search decoding (e.g., Andor et al., 2016), which might also be well-suited to this task.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper notes that a gold parse can be deterministically converted into a transition sequence for training, implying a static oracle. Could you provide more detail on the logic of this oracle? At first glance, the transition system seems to share properties with the arc-eager system, where certain actions represent hard commitments. For instance, the `M-SHIFT` action seems to preclude future semantic arcs between the newly shifted item and any items remaining on the semantic stack. Likewise, `M-REDUCE` seems to finalize the arcs for the popped item relative to the buffer. Could you clarify how the oracle navigates these decisions? Have you considered developing a dynamic oracle for this system to allow for exploration during training?
