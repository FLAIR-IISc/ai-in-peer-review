This paper presents a welcome and timely revisitation of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, applying recent advances from neural network models to this challenging task. The prospect of effective joint modeling is one of the most significant promises of modern transition-based neural parsers, and this work makes a solid contribution in that direction.

The paper offers two primary contributions. The first is a novel transition system for the joint task, which appears to be an improvement upon the Henderson et al. (2008) system on which it is based. The second, and perhaps more significant, contribution is the demonstration that a neural network model can succeed at this joint task where previous linear models have struggled.

The authors attribute this success primarily to the neural network's ability to learn effective feature representations automatically, obviating the need for extensive manual feature engineering. This is a valid and important point. However, I believe there is another crucial advantage of the neural approach that is worth highlighting. In a linear model, learning requires a distinct weight for every feature-class combination. In a joint learning scenario like this, the number of such combinations can explode, drastically increasing the number of parameters to be learned. In contrast, a neural network learns a more compact, shared representation in its hidden layers, making it significantly more parameter-efficient and inherently better suited for multi-task problems. Mentioning this aspect could further strengthen the motivation for the chosen architecture.

The work is highly promising, and I expect that extensions built upon this foundation could achieve state-of-the-art results. The proposed transition system appears to be a general and modular contribution. It is likely compatible with other recent neural parsing architectures, such as the globally-normalized beam-search parser of Andor et al. (2016). Integrating the system with this or other architectures represents an exciting avenue for future work.

A valuable addition to the paper, perhaps in an appendix or as a direction for future research, would be the derivation of a dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system. The system bears some resemblance to the arc-eager system, and an initial analysis suggests how such an oracle might function. For instance, it appears that:
*   The M-S action precludes any semantic arcs between the first word on the buffer and the words currently on the semantic stack.
*   The M-D action precludes any semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words remaining in the buffer.
*   The syntactic L and R actions seem to only exclude the reverse dependency arc between the two tokens involved, and no others.

Exploring a dynamic oracle would be a significant step toward addressing error propagation and further improving the parser's performance. Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-designed and effective method.
