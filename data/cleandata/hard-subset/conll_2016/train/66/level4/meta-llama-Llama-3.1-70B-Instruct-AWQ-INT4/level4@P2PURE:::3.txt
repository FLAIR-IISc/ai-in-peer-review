This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models are a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two significant contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system, which appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. The second contribution is the demonstration that neural networks can succeed in this task, where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is worth noting: unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, neural networks are more economical in terms of parameter learning, particularly when jointly learning multiple problems.

The transition system proposed in the paper is likely to be compatible with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are numerous orthogonal improvements that could be explored, and it is expected that extensions to the authors' method will yield state-of-the-art results.

A potential direction for future work is the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, possibly in an appendix or follow-up work. At first glance, the system appears to share similarities with the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to exclude the reverse arc, with no additional exclusions.
