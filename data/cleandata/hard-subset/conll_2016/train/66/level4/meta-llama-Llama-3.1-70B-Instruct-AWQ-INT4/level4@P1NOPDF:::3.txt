This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models are a particularly promising area of research, building on the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two key contributions. First, the authors propose a new transition system that appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. The second contribution is the demonstration that neural networks can successfully tackle this problem, where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, I believe there is another advantage of neural networks in this context, worth mentioning. In linear models, learning requires assigning a weight to each feature/class pair, resulting in a substantial increase in the number of parameters to be learned when tackling two problems jointly. In contrast, neural networks are more economical in this regard.

I suspect that the proposed transition system could be effectively paired with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are numerous orthogonal improvements that could be explored, and I expect that extending the authors' method will yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be beneficial to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if it is only presented in an appendix or in follow-up work. At first glance, the proposed system appears similar to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to exclude the reverse arc, with no additional exclusions.
