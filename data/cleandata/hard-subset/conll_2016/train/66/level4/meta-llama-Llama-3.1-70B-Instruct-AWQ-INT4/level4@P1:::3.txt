This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models are a promising direction for transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it presents a new transition system that improves upon the Henderson (2008) system it is based on. Second, it demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models had previously struggled. The authors attribute the success of the neural network to its ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is their efficiency in parameter learning. Unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, neural networks can learn to represent multiple problems with a more compact set of parameters. This is particularly beneficial when jointly learning two problems, as it reduces the number of parameters that need to be learned.

While the transition system presented in the paper is innovative, it is likely that other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016), could also be used in conjunction with this system. Furthermore, there are many orthogonal improvements that could be made to the authors' method, and it is expected that extensions to this work will produce state-of-the-art results.

One potential area for future work is the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system. At first glance, the system appears to be similar to the arc-eager oracle, with the M-S action excluding all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, and the M-D action excluding all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to each exclude the reverse arc, and no other. Exploring this further, even if only in an appendix or in follow-up work, could provide valuable insights into the system's behavior.

The paper's evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art systems that use expert-crafted features and more computationally expensive models. The authors' decision to use a greedy inference algorithm and to learn a representation of the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs is well-motivated and contributes to the system's efficiency.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed analysis of the system's performance on specific aspects of the task, such as predicate sense disambiguation or semantic role labeling. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the implications of the proposed approach for the broader field of natural language processing.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, and its findings have important implications for the development of more efficient and effective parsing systems.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel transition system that improves upon previous work in the field.
* The use of neural networks to automatically learn relevant features is a significant advantage over linear models.
* The system's efficiency and ability to learn a compact representation of the algorithmic state are major benefits.
* The paper's evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the implications of the proposed approach for the broader field of natural language processing.
* A more detailed analysis of the system's performance on specific aspects of the task would be beneficial.
* The derivation of a dynamic oracle for the transition system is an area for future work.
