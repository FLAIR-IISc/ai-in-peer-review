**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser is based on the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing and Dyer et al. (2015) on stack LSTM syntactic parsing. The use of the transition system from the former and the stack LSTM from the latter shows promising results compared to the joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

**Contributions of the Paper**

The contributions of this paper are:

1. The development of a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state.
2. The application of the parser to the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving competitive results compared to other joint systems.
3. The demonstration of the effectiveness of the parser in handling both syntactic and semantic dependencies, without the need for expert-crafted features.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper presents a well-written and well-explained methodology, making it easy to follow and understand.
2. The related work is thoroughly discussed, providing a clear understanding of the context and the contributions of the paper.
3. The results are interesting and competitive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed parser.
4. The discussion is informative, providing insights into the strengths and limitations of the parser.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The proposed system lacks originality, as the work of Henderson et al. and Dyer et al. has already established the basis for semi-synchronised joint syntax-semantic transition-based parsing and stack LSTM syntactic parsing.
2. The use of pre-trained embeddings for the Chinese language may be seen as a limitation, as it may not be directly comparable to other systems that do not use such embeddings.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the parser to incorporate morphological features, which are commonly used in other systems?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the effectiveness of the parser in handling out-of-domain data, and how it compares to other systems in this regard?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of originality, given that the proposed system builds upon existing work?
