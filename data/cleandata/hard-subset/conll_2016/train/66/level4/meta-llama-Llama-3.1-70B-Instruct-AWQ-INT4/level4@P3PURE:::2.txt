General Comments
================

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences between their approach and the original work: the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser's state, rather than just the top elements of the stack or the last parser's configurations, and the employment of a plain greedy search algorithm. The key idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to enable the parser's state vector to capture potentially large-scoped syntactic features, which are known to be decisive for semantic role labeling, such as the path between the predicate and the candidate role filler head. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their system's performance to previously reported results, demonstrating that it performs well compared to the 2008 and 2009 systems, but less so compared to more recent proposals (cf. the bottom of Table 3). They highlight that the proposed system does not require any hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written and describes a substantial amount of work, building on the recently popular LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears to have been somewhat visionary.

I have reservations regarding the choice of the simple greedy algorithm, as it renders the results not directly comparable to some of the cited works. Providing beam-searched performance, for instance, would not have required significant additional work or space.

More Detailed Comments and Questions
=====================================

Section 2:
A comment on the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would facilitate a better understanding of the target task of the paper. A summary of the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al. would be beneficial. In its current form, it is challenging to discern what is directly reused from Henderson et al. and what is new or slightly modified.

Section 3.3:
Why do you need representations that concatenate the word predicate and its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, as the disambiguated senses are specific to a predicate.

Section 4:
The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing regarding multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused on English, and the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended it to a few other languages.
