General Comments
================

This paper proposes a novel joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, drawing inspiration from Henderson et al.'s (2008) joint parser. The authors highlight two primary differences: the utilization of vectorial representations for the entire parser's state, rather than just the top elements of the stack or last parser configurations, and the employment of a straightforward greedy search algorithm. The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs, enabling the parser state vector to retain memory of large-scoped syntactic features that are crucial for semantic role labeling, such as the path between the predicate and candidate role filler head.

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. The authors compare their system's performance to previously reported results, demonstrating competitive performance compared to 2008 and 2009 systems, although not as strong as more recent proposals (as seen in Table 3). Notably, the proposed system does not require hand-crafted features and boasts speed due to the simple greedy algorithm. The paper is well-written, presenting a substantial body of work that builds upon the popular LSTMs and applies them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears visionary.

However, I have some reservations regarding the choice of the simple greedy algorithm, as it limits the comparability of the results to some of the cited works. Providing beam-searched performance would not have required significant additional effort or space.

More Detailed Comments / Questions
==================================

Section 2:
A brief discussion on the inclusion of both A1 and C-A1 links would enhance the understanding of the paper's target task. A summary of the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and those employed by Henderson et al. would be beneficial. In its current form, it is challenging to discern what aspects are directly reused from Henderson et al. and what is new or slightly modified.

Section 3.3:
The use of representations that concatenate the word predicate and its disambiguated sense seems redundant, as disambiguated senses are specific to a predicate.

Section 4:
The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing, particularly regarding multilinguality. It would be helpful to clarify that CoNLL 2008 focused on English, while CoNLL 2009 extended the shared task to a few other languages.
