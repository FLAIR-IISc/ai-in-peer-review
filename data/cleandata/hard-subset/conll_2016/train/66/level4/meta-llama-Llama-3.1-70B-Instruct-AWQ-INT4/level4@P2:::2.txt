**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser is inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008) but differs in two main ways: it uses vectorial representations for the whole parser's state and employs a plain greedy search algorithm. The system is tested on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets and achieves competitive performance compared to previous joint models.

**Contributions**

1. The use of stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state, allowing for the capture of large-scoped syntactic features that are important for semantic role labeling.
2. The development of a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that achieves competitive performance without requiring hand-crafted features.
3. The demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed system on multiple languages, including English, Chinese, and others.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-written and substantial amount of work that builds on recent advances in LSTMs.
2. The proposed system achieves competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets.
3. The use of stack LSTMs allows for the capture of large-scoped syntactic features that are important for semantic role labeling.
4. The system is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

1. The choice of a simple greedy algorithm may limit the system's performance compared to more recent proposals that use more advanced search algorithms.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of the proposed system's performance to other state-of-the-art systems.
3. The organization of the experimental sections could be improved to better reflect the multilingual nature of the CoNLL 2009 shared task.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed system's performance to other state-of-the-art systems, including those that use more advanced search algorithms?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of the simple greedy algorithm, and would it be possible to incorporate more advanced search algorithms into the system?
3. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing steps used to handle out-of-vocabulary words and unseen predicates at test time?
