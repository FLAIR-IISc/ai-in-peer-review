This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models are a promising direction for transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper's main contributions are two-fold. First, it presents a new transition system that improves upon the Henderson (2008) system it is based on. Second, it demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models had previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is their parameter efficiency, particularly when jointly learning multiple problems.

The transition system appears to be well-designed and could potentially work with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are many orthogonal improvements that could be made to the authors' method, and I expect extensions to produce state-of-the-art results.

A potential area for future work is the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, which could be explored in an appendix or follow-up work. At first glance, the transition system seems similar to the arc-eager oracle, with the M-S action excluding all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, and the M-D action excluding all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to each exclude the reverse arc, and no other.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a well-designed transition system for joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, which improves upon previous work.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models had previously struggled.
3. The paper highlights the parameter efficiency of neural networks, particularly when jointly learning multiple problems.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the transition system and its similarities to other oracles, such as the arc-eager oracle.
2. The authors could explore the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system in more detail.
3. The paper could discuss potential orthogonal improvements to the authors' method and their potential impact on the results.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide more insight into the design decisions behind the transition system and how it improves upon previous work?
2. How do you think the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features contributes to its success in this task?
3. Are there any plans to explore the derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system in future work?
