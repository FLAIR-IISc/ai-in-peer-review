**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser that uses stack long short-term memories (LSTMs) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser is designed to jointly produce syntactic and semantic dependencies, and it is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs, which allows for the learning of a representation of the entire algorithm state.
2. The parser is designed to be greedy, with a linear time complexity, making it efficient and scalable.
3. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the parser on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and well-designed approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, which is a challenging task in natural language processing.
2. The use of stack LSTMs is a key innovation in the paper, allowing for the learning of a representation of the entire algorithm state.
3. The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the parser on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's use of a simple greedy algorithm may limit the parser's performance, as it does not allow for beam search or other optimization techniques.
2. The paper's evaluation is limited to the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, and it would be beneficial to evaluate the parser on other datasets and languages.
3. The paper's discussion of the parser's performance is limited, and it would be beneficial to provide more detailed analysis and comparison with other state-of-the-art parsers.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the design choices behind the use of a simple greedy algorithm, and whether you considered using beam search or other optimization techniques?
2. How do you plan to extend the parser to other languages and datasets, and what challenges do you anticipate in doing so?
3. Can you provide more detailed analysis and comparison of the parser's performance with other state-of-the-art parsers, including those that use beam search or other optimization techniques?
