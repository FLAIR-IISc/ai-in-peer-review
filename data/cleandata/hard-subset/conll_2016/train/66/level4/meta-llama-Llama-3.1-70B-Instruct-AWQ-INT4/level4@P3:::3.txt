This paper performs an overdue circling-back to the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, applying the recent insights from neural network models. Joint models are one of the most promising things about the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

**Summary of the Paper**
The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies. The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs) and employs a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieve the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**
1. The authors present a new transition system that seems better than the Henderson (2008) system it is based on.
2. The authors show that the neural network succeeds on this problem, where linear models had previously struggled, and attribute this success to the ability of the neural network to automatically learn which features to extract.
3. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their model in jointly learning syntax and semantics, achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing using neural networks, which is a promising direction for future research.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in jointly learning syntax and semantics.
3. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its components.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed model, which could be an important area for future research.
2. The authors do not compare their model with other state-of-the-art systems that use different approaches to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the proposed model for natural language processing applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the training process, including the hyperparameter settings and the optimization algorithm used?
2. How do you handle out-of-vocabulary words in the proposed model, and what is the impact on performance?
3. Have you considered applying the proposed model to other languages or datasets, and what are the potential challenges and opportunities in doing so?

**Additional Comments**
The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its components. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in jointly learning syntax and semantics. Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing using neural networks, and it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
