This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. The authors present two main contributions. Firstly, they introduce a new transition system that improves upon the Henderson (2008) system it is based on. Secondly, they demonstrate the effectiveness of a neural network approach to this problem, which had previously been challenging for linear models. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of the neural network approach is its efficiency in parameter learning, particularly when jointly learning multiple problems. In contrast, linear models require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, resulting in a significant increase in parameters when tackling multiple problems simultaneously.

The paper's strengths include its innovative application of neural network models to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, as well as its thorough evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks. The authors' analysis of the results and discussion of the implications of their findings are also noteworthy.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of a dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system. Deriving such an oracle, even if only in an appendix or follow-up work, would be a valuable contribution to the field. The authors' transition system appears to share similarities with the arc-eager oracle, and exploring these connections further could provide additional insights.

In terms of contributions, I would argue that the paper's primary contribution is the demonstration of the effectiveness of neural network models for joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing. While the new transition system is an important aspect of the paper, it is the neural network approach that enables the successful application of this transition system to the problem at hand.

The authors' discussion of related work is thorough, and they provide a clear comparison of their approach to other joint modeling approaches. However, it would be beneficial to see a more in-depth analysis of the similarities and differences between their approach and other neural network-based models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016).

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, and its findings have important implications for the development of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing models.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the derivation of the dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system, and how it relates to the arc-eager oracle?
2. How do you envision the neural network approach being extended to incorporate other features or improvements, such as morphological features or character-based word embeddings?
3. Can you provide a more in-depth comparison of your approach to other neural network-based models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016)?
