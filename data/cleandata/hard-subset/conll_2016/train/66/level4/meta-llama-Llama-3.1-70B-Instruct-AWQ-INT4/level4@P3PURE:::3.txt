This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models are a key factor in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two main contributions. Firstly, the authors propose a new transition system that appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2004) system on which it is based. Secondly, they demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is worth noting: unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, neural networks are more economical in terms of parameter learning, especially when jointly learning two problems.

I suspect that the proposed transition system could be successfully integrated with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor et al. (2016). There are numerous orthogonal improvements that could be made to further enhance the authors' method, and I expect that extensions to this approach will yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be beneficial to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if it is presented in an appendix or as follow-up work. At first glance, the system appears similar to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions seem to each exclude the reverse arc, with no other exclusions.
