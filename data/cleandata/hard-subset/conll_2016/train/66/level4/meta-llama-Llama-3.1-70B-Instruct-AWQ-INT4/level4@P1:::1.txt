This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser based on the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing and Dyer et al. (2015) on stack LSTM syntactic parsing. The use of the transition system from the former and the stack LSTM from the latter shows interesting results compared to the joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

The paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories. The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs.
2. A greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity.
3. State-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-written, well-explained, and easy to follow.
2. The related work is well-covered, and the authors provide a clear description of their system.
3. The results are interesting and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. The methodology is sound, and the hyperparameters are justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The proposed system lacks originality, as it builds upon existing work by Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Dyer et al. (2015).
2. The use of pretrained Chinese embeddings may be more informative than the model itself, which raises concerns about the model's ability to generalize.

**Questions to authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of originality, considering that their work builds upon existing research?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the effectiveness of their model compared to other state-of-the-art systems, particularly those that use expert-crafted features?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their model to incorporate morphological features, which could potentially improve performance in highly inflective languages?

**Additional comments:**

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear description of their system. The results are interesting, and the discussion is well-informed. However, the lack of originality is a concern, and the authors should address this issue in their response.
