**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences: (1) vectorial representations are used for the whole parser's state, instead of the top elements of the stack or the last parser's configurations, and (2) the algorithm is a plain greedy search. The key idea is to take advantage of stack LSTMs to keep memory of potentially large-scoped syntactic features, which are known to be decisive features for semantic role labeling.

**Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state.
2. The parser is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, showing competitive performance compared to previous systems.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in learning representations for the entire algorithmic state, without relying on expert-crafted features.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-written and substantial amount of work, building on the recently popular LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm.
2. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their approach, including the architecture and training procedure.
3. The results show competitive performance compared to previous systems, especially considering that the proposed system does not require any hand-crafted features.

**Weaknesses**

1. The choice of a simple greedy algorithm may limit the comparison to other works that use more advanced search strategies, such as beam search.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al.
3. The organization of the experimental sections (4.1 and 4.2) could be improved to better reflect the multilingual aspect of the CoNLL 2009 shared task.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al.?
2. Why do you need representations concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense, given that the disambiguated sense is specific to a predicate?
3. Would it be possible to provide results using a more advanced search strategy, such as beam search, to compare to the greedy algorithm?
