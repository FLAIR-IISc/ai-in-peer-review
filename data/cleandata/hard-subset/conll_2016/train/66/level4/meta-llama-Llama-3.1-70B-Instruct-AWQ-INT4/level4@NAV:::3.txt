This paper revisits the important problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Notably, joint models have been instrumental in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two key contributions. Firstly, the authors introduce a novel transition system that appears to be an improvement over the Hendersen (2008) system upon which it is based. Secondly, they demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models had previously encountered difficulties. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is worth noting: unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, thereby increasing the number of parameters when jointly learning two problems, neural networks are more parameter-efficient.

It is likely that the proposed transition system would be compatible with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are numerous orthogonal improvements that could be explored, and it is anticipated that extensions of the authors' method will yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be beneficial to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, potentially in an appendix or follow-up work. At first glance, the system bears resemblance to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action precludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and those on the semantic stack, while the M-D action precludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and those in the buffer. The L and R actions appear to each preclude the reverse arc, with no additional exclusions.
