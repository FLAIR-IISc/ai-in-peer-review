This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. The success of transition-based neural network parsers has made joint models a promising area of research.

The paper makes two key contributions. Firstly, it introduces a new transition system, which appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system it builds upon. Secondly, it demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of neural networks in this context is worth noting. Unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, neural networks are more economical in terms of parameter learning, particularly when jointly learning two problems.

The proposed transition system is likely to be compatible with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are also several orthogonal improvements that could be explored to further enhance the authors' method, which could potentially lead to state-of-the-art results.

It would be beneficial to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or as follow-up work. At first glance, the transition system bears some resemblance to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions appear to exclude the reverse arc, with no additional exclusions.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, and its contributions have the potential to lead to further advancements in the field.
