**General Comments**

The paper presents a novel joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, drawing inspiration from Henderson et al.'s (2008) joint parser. The authors highlight two primary differences between their approach and the original work: (1) the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser's state, rather than relying solely on the top elements of the stack or the last parser's configurations, and (2) the employment of a plain greedy search algorithm.

The key idea behind this approach is to leverage stack LSTMs to capture potentially large-scoped syntactic features, which are known to be crucial for semantic role labeling. These features, such as the path between the predicate and the candidate role filler head, can be effectively represented by the parser's state, allowing for improved performance.

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their system's performance to previously reported results, demonstrating competitive performance compared to the 2008 and 2009 systems, although slightly lower compared to more recent proposals (cf. bottom of Table 3). Notably, the authors emphasize that their system does not require hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well-written and describes a substantial amount of work, building upon the recently popular LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears to have been somewhat visionary.

However, I have some reservations regarding the choice of the simple greedy algorithm, as it may render the results not directly comparable to some of the cited works. Providing additional results using beam search, for instance, would not have required significant additional work or space.

**More Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 2**

A brief explanation of the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would enhance the reader's understanding of the target task. Furthermore, a summary of the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and those employed by Henderson et al. would be beneficial. In its current form, it is challenging to discern what aspects are directly reused from Henderson et al. and what is new or slightly modified.

**Section 3.3**

The need for representations that concatenate the word predicate and its disambiguated sense is not entirely clear. Since disambiguated senses are specific to a predicate, this approach seems redundant. The authors may want to provide additional justification for this design choice.

**Section 4**

The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing, particularly regarding multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused on English, while CoNLL 2009 extended the shared task to a few other languages. The authors may want to reorganize these sections to provide clearer comparisons between the different languages and datasets.
