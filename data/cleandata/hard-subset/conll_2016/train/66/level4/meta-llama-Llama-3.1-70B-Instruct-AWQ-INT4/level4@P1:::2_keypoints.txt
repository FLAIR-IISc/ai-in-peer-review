- Paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by Henderson et al. (2008).  
- Authors claim two main differences from prior work: using vectorial representations for the entire parser state (via stack LSTMs) and employing a plain greedy search.  
- Key idea: leverage stack LSTMs to capture large-scoped syntactic features critical for semantic role labeling.

Contributions:
- Proposes a novel joint syntactic–semantic parser using stack LSTMs to represent the whole parser state.  
- Evaluates the parser on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, reporting competitive performance.  
- Demonstrates learning of entire algorithmic-state representations without hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- Well-written paper applying contemporary LSTM techniques to a joint parsing algorithm.  
- Clear, detailed description of architecture and training procedure.  
- Competitive results achieved without reliance on expert-designed features.

Weaknesses:
- Use of a simple greedy algorithm may limit comparability to systems using beam or other advanced search strategies.  
- Insufficient discussion of differences between this work’s transition set and Henderson et al.’s transitions.  
- Experimental section organization (4.1 and 4.2) could better reflect the multilingual aspects of CoNLL 2009.

Questions to authors:
- Please detail the differences between the transition set used here and that of Henderson et al.  
- Why concatenate word predicate representations with their disambiguated senses when the sense is specific to a predicate?  
- Can you provide results using a stronger search strategy (e.g., beam search) to compare against the greedy approach?
