**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences: (1) vectorial representations are used for the whole parser's state, instead of the top elements of the stack or the last parser's configurations, and (2) the algorithm is a plain greedy search. The key idea is to take advantage of stack LSTMs to keep memory of potentially large-scoped syntactic features, which are known to be decisive features for semantic role labeling.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs.
2. The system is tested on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, showing competitive performance with previously reported systems.
3. The authors emphasize that the proposed system does not require any hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-written and substantial amount of work, building on the recently popular LSTMs.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its differences from previous work.
3. The system's performance is competitive with previously reported systems, especially considering that it does not require any hand-crafted features.

**Weaknesses**

1. The choice of a simple greedy algorithm may limit the system's performance, as it renders results not directly comparable to some of the cited works.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al.
3. The organization of the experimental sections (4.1 and 4.2) is confusing, and it would be helpful to clarify the multilingual aspects of the CoNLL 2009 shared task.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al.?
2. Why do you need representations concatenating the word predicate and its disambiguated sense, as this seems redundant since the disambiguated sense is specific to a predicate?
3. Would it be possible to provide beam-searched performance results to make the system's performance more comparable to other works?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, some minor points could be improved, such as providing a summary of the differences between the set of transitions used in this work and that of Henderson et al. Additionally, the organization of the experimental sections could be clarified to better reflect the multilingual aspects of the CoNLL 2009 shared task.
