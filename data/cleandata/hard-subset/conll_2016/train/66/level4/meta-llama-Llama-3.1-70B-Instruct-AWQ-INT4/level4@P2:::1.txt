This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser based on the work of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing and Dyer et al. (2015) on stack LSTM syntactic parsing. The use of the transition system from the former and the stack LSTM from the latter shows interesting results compared to the joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories. The parser is based on a transition-based algorithm that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieve state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs.
2. The authors propose a transition-based algorithm that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies.
3. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-written and well-explained, making it easy to follow.
2. The related work is comprehensive and provides a good background on the topic.
3. The results are interesting and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. The methodology is sound, and the hyperparameters are justified.
5. The discussion is interesting and provides insights into the strengths and limitations of the model.

**Weaknesses**

1. The proposed system lacks originality, as it builds on existing work by Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Dyer et al. (2015).
2. The model relies heavily on pre-trained embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the stack LSTMs are implemented and how they are used to learn the representation of the entire algorithm state?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting to the WSJ domain, as mentioned in the paper?
3. Can you provide more insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to other state-of-the-art models that use expert-crafted features and reranking?
