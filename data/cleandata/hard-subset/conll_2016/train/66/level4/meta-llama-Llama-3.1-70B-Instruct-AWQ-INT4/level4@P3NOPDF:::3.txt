This paper revisits the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. The authors' decision to explore this area is well-motivated, as joint models have shown great promise in the success of transition-based neural network parsers.

The paper makes two key contributions. Firstly, it introduces a new transition system, which appears to be an improvement over the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Secondly, it demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this problem, an area where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute the success of their approach to the neural network's ability to automatically learn relevant features. However, another advantage of using neural networks in this context is worth noting. Unlike linear models, which require learning a weight for each feature/class pair, neural networks are more economical in terms of the number of parameters that need to be learned, particularly when jointly learning two problems.

The proposed transition system seems flexible enough to be combined with various other neural network models, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are numerous orthogonal improvements that could be explored, and it is likely that extensions to the authors' method will yield state-of-the-art results.

One potential avenue for future work would be to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or as follow-up research. At first glance, the transition system bears some resemblance to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically, the M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions appear to exclude the reverse arc, with no other exclusions. Investigating this further could provide additional insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed transition system.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling case for the use of neural networks in joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, and the proposed transition system is a valuable contribution to the field. With further refinements and extensions, this work has the potential to achieve state-of-the-art performance.
