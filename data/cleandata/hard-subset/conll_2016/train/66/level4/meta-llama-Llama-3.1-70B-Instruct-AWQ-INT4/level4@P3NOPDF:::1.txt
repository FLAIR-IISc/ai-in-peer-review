This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser that combines the joint syntactic/semantic transition-based parsing approach of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the stack LSTM syntactic parsing method of Dyer et al. (2015). The integration of these two frameworks yields interesting results, particularly when compared to joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.

The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and effectively situates itself within the relevant body of research. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, which serves as a solid foundation for their proposed methodology. The results presented are indeed compelling, and the authors' sound methodology is well-supported by their experimental design. Specifically, the description of the system is clear, and the authors provide a well-reasoned justification for their chosen hyperparameters. The discussion section is also engaging, offering valuable insights into the implications of the results.

One minor concern regards the use of Chinese embeddings, which suggests that high-quality embeddings can be more informative than a complex model. However, this does not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

If there is one area where the paper falls short, it is in terms of originality. The work of Henderson et al. laid the groundwork for semi-synchronized joint syntax-semantic transition-based parsing several years ago, and Dyer et al. introduced the stack LSTM last year. As such, the proposed system does not introduce a fundamentally new method. Nevertheless, the authors' implementation of this parser is a valuable contribution, and it is satisfying to see this work being done. In my opinion, the field was in need of such a parser, and I am glad that the authors have taken on this task.

Overall, the paper is well-crafted, and the authors' efforts have resulted in a valuable addition to the field.
