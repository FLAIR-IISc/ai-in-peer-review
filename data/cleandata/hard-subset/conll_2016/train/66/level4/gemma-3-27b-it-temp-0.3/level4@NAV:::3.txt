This paper revisits the important problem of jointly learning semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network modeling. Joint modeling remains a key factor in the performance gains observed in transition-based neural network parsers.

The work presents two primary contributions. First, the authors introduce a novel transition system, which appears to outperform the previously established Henderson (2008) system. Second, they demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks in tackling this joint parsing task, an area where linear models have historically faced challenges. The authors correctly identify the neural network’s ability to automatically learn relevant features as a source of this success. However, I believe an additional advantage merits consideration: neural networks offer greater parameter efficiency compared to linear models. Specifically, linear models require learning a weight for each feature/class combination, leading to a substantial increase in parameters when jointly modeling multiple tasks – a challenge that neural networks mitigate.

While the proposed transition system is promising, I anticipate it would generalize well to other neural network architectures, such as the global beam-search model proposed by Andor (2016). Furthermore, numerous orthogonal improvements could be explored. I expect that further development of this method has the potential to achieve state-of-the-art results.

A valuable addition would be an attempt to develop a dynamic oracle for this transition system, potentially as supplementary material or in future work. Initial observation suggests similarities to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action effectively prohibits semantic arcs originating from the word at the head of the buffer to words already on the semantic stack, and the M-D action prevents semantic arcs extending from the top of the stack to words in the buffer. The L and R actions, conversely, appear to each preclude only their respective reverse arcs.
