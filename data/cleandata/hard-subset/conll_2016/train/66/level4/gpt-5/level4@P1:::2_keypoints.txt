- Joint syntactic–semantic transition parser using stack LSTMs for whole-state representations and purely greedy, linear-time inference (no hand-crafted features).  
- Evaluated on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual); outperforms prior joint models, competitive with older top systems, but behind recent SRL systems using engineered features or global inference; promising runtime and open-source code.  
- Contribution: simple, fast joint parser that learns whole-state representations via stack LSTMs, dispensing with hand-crafted features while achieving strong CoNLL performance.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that whole-state representation learning narrows the gap to pipeline systems and yields competitive multilingual results.  
- Contribution: practical engineering of linear-time greedy inference with competitive accuracy and low runtime.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains among joint models, substantially outperforming Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.  
- Strength: Competitive accuracy without expert-crafted syntactic features, close to top shared-task pipelines from 2008/2009.  
- Strength: Speed and simplicity—purely greedy stack-LSTM approach gives linear-time parsing and ~2.5× faster end-to-end runtime than a strong pipeline baseline.  
- Strength: Clear architecture and informative ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) showing predictable, interpretable trends.  
- Strength: Well-written paper that thoughtfully extends Henderson et al.’s framework.  
- Weakness: Greedy inference limits comparability to systems using beam search or global inference; small-beam results would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness: Insufficiently clear summary of transition-set differences from Henderson et al.; a concise list of changes is needed.  
- Weakness: Minor clarity issues in task description—examples with both A1 and C-A1 can confuse readers unfamiliar with CoNLL conventions.  
- Weakness: Section organization around multilinguality (split 4.1 vs 4.2) can obscure that only 2009 is multilingual; reorder or clarify.  
- Weakness: Possible redundancy in predicate representation—concatenating word and disambiguated sense needs justification and empirical comparison.  
- Question: Clarify handling and interpretation of continuation roles (A1 vs C-A1) and how the transition system models them.  
- Question: Provide a compact summary of differences from Henderson et al. (copy vs move behavior, interleaving constraints, M-SELF, M-SWAP changes).  
- Question: Why concatenate word representation with disambiguated sense for predicate composition, and what empirical gain vs using only sense or lemma?  
- Question: Did you try small-beam decoding or easy-first variants, and how do they trade off accuracy vs runtime compared to pure greedy?  
- Question: Could Section 4 be clarified to state up front that CoNLL 2008 is English-only and CoNLL 2009 is multilingual?
