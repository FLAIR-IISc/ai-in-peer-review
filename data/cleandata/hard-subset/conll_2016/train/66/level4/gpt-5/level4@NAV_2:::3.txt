This paper revisits the problem of jointly predicting syntactic and semantic dependencies, bringing recent neural modeling insights to bear. Joint models are among the most promising outcomes of transition-based neural parsers.

The work has two contributions. First, it introduces a revised transition system that appears to improve over the Henderson (2008) system it extends. Second, it shows that a neural model performs well on this joint task where linear models had struggled, attributing the gain to automatic feature induction. I would add another likely advantage of the neural approach: in linear models, one must estimate a weight for every feature/class pair, so jointly learning multiple tasks greatly inflates the number of parameters, whereas neural architectures share representations and are more parameter-efficient.

I expect the proposed transition system would also pair well with other neural architectures, such as the globally normalized beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are many orthogonal enhancements that could be layered on; I anticipate extensions of the authors’ method could achieve state-of-the-art performance.

It would be useful to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or as follow-up work. At first glance it looks analogous to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action would rule out all semantic arcs between the buffer-front word and items on the semantic stack, and the M-D action would rule out all semantic arcs between the stack-top word and items remaining in the buffer. The L and R actions each seem to exclude only the reverse arc, and no others.
