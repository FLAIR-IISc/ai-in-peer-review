This paper offers a much-needed revisit to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint modeling remains one of the most compelling aspects of the success of transition-based neural parsers.

The paper makes two contributions. First, the authors introduce a new transition system that appears to improve upon the Henderson (2008) system it extends. Second, they show that a neural network handles this task well where linear models had previously struggled. The authors attribute this to the network’s capacity to learn which features to extract automatically. I think there is an additional benefit worth noting: in a linear model, one must learn a weight for every feature/class combination, so jointly learning two problems greatly increases the number of parameters. Neural networks, by contrast, share representations and are considerably more parameter-efficient.

I suspect the transition system would perform similarly well with other neural architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are many orthogonal enhancements that could be applied, and I expect extensions of the authors’ method to yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be useful to attempt a derivation of a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or subsequent work. At first glance, it resembles the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action rules out all semantic arcs between the word at the front of the buffer and the items on the semantic stack, and the M-D action rules out all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the items in the buffer. The L and R actions each appear to rule out only the reverse-direction arc, and nothing else.
