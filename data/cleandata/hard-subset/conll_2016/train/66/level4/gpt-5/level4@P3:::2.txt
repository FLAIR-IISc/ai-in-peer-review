Summary
This paper proposes a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependencies and semantic role labels using stack LSTMs to represent the full parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). The model performs greedy inference with linear-time complexity, avoids hand-crafted features, and achieves strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 shared tasks (English and multilingual). The authors report best results among prior joint models and competitive performance relative to pipeline systems, with substantially faster end-to-end runtime.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, feature-light joint parsing architecture that learns a compact representation of the entire parser state via stack LSTMs and composes both syntactic and semantic structures on the fly.
2) A fast, purely greedy joint inference procedure with linear-time feature extraction and runtime, demonstrating that joint syntax–semantics can be competitive without expert-crafted features or global search.
3) Empirical evidence and ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) that quantify the benefit of joint learning and the trade-offs against pipelined and single-task baselines across CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual).

Strengths
- Clear methodological advance in representation learning for joint parsing: Using stack LSTMs to summarize all parser data structures removes reliance on expensive, hand-engineered features (e.g., path-based features), while still capturing long-range contextual information helpful for SRL.
- Strong empirical validation and careful baselines: The paper evaluates on standard CoNLL 2008/2009 splits, includes syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint variants, and compares against prior joint systems. The joint model consistently improves over earlier joint approaches, supporting the core claim.
- Practical efficiency: The end-to-end runtime (single core) is substantially faster than competitive pipeline systems, making the approach attractive for production use and large-scale processing.
- Thoughtful engineering for joint structure: The unified buffer with interleaved syntactic/semantic transitions, addition of M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments, and controlled constraints on transitions demonstrate careful adaptation of transition-based parsing to joint syntax–semantics.
- Reproducibility: The paper specifies training details and hyperparameters, relies on publicly known pretraining, and promises open-source code, which will facilitate adoption and comparison.

Weaknesses
- Greedy decoding limits comparability and potentially accuracy: Many referenced systems employ beam search, global normalization, or exact inference. Without beam or global variants, it is difficult to disentangle modeling gains from search errors. The out-of-domain drop (Hybrid > Joint on Brown) suggests overfitting and/or sensitivity to greedy decisions.
- Insufficient clarity on transition differences from Henderson et al.: The paper states it largely follows the synchronized algorithm but introduces several deviations (copy vs move, interleaving policy, M-SELF). A concise, explicit summary of additions and modifications would aid reproducibility and attribution.
- Limited analysis of learned representations and errors: The paper argues that stack LSTMs recover long-range features, but offers little diagnostic evidence (e.g., performance by predicate–argument distance, crossing arcs, label confusions, or error attribution to syntax vs semantics). Such analysis would strengthen the claim that learned state representations replace expensive global features.
- Predicate representation design not fully justified: The composition of word and disambiguated sense vectors seems redundant given lexeme-specific senses; more motivation or ablations (e.g., sense-only vs word+sense) would clarify the benefit.
- Evaluation organization and scope could be clearer: The multilingual setup is mainly in CoNLL 2009, whereas CoNLL 2008 is English-only. As written, Sections 4.1/4.2 mix these settings, which may confuse readers. Also, the partial oracle mismatch for semantic transitions (due to multiple crossings) raises questions about upper bounds and training noise that are not quantified.

Questions to Authors
- Could you report beam-search results (small beams) and/or globally normalized training (e.g., with a CRF objective or cost-augmented beam) to assess the impact of search? If not feasible, can you quantify oracle accuracy vs achieved accuracy to estimate search errors?
- Please summarize, in one place, all departures from Henderson et al. (2008/2013): copying vs moving between buffer/stack, interleaving policy, added transitions (M-SELF), constraints, and any changes to right/left actions. A compact table or paragraph would suffice.
- What empirical benefit do you observe from composing predicate word and sense (gd)? Have you tested sense-only or word-only representations, or measured sensitivity for unseen predicates defaulting to .01?
- Can you clarify the role of A1 vs C-A1 links in your target SRL graphs, and whether your transition system and evaluation treat coreference/control labels in a special way?
- How many English sentences (and what percentage across languages) cannot be exactly encoded by your transition oracle due to crossing semantics, and how does this affect training/evaluation?
- Could you disaggregate results by predicate–argument distance and crossing arc frequency to support the claim that stack LSTMs recover long-range/global features?
- For multilingual results, how much do pretrained embeddings contribute? Please provide with/without-pretraining ablations, and indicate whether any language uses morphology (or whether character-level embeddings help).

Additional comments
- Please reorganize Section 4 to make the multilingual vs English-only setup explicit: (a) CoNLL 2008 (English, with predicate identification); (b) CoNLL 2009 English (no predicate identification); (c) CoNLL 2009 multilingual.
- Minor: fix typos (e.g., “dismabiguated” → “disambiguated”), and consider adding a brief note explaining C-A1 and similar labels on first use.
- Reporting: Include hardware specs for runtime, and confirm whether times include both syntax and semantics with predicate disambiguation.

Overall, this is a solid, clearly written paper that advances joint syntax–semantics with a simple and efficient neural architecture. Addressing the search comparability issue (even with small beams), clarifying the transition differences, and adding targeted analyses would strengthen the contribution and its empirical claims. I lean positive, contingent on these clarifications.
