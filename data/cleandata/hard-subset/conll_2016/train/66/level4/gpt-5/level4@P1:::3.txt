Summary
This paper revisits joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using recent neural transition-based methods. The authors propose a joint transition system inspired by Henderson et al. (2008), coupled with stack LSTMs that learn representations of the entire parser state. The parser performs greedy, linear-time inference, requires no hand-crafted features, and achieves strong results on the CoNLL 2008–2009 English tasks, with competitive multilingual performance and very fast runtime.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A refined joint transition system for synchronized syntactic and semantic parsing. Compared to Henderson et al. (2008), the system introduces design choices (e.g., operating on a shared buffer with copy semantics, semantic transitions like M-SELF, and interleaving constraints) that make greedy, linear-time joint parsing practical and effective.

2) A demonstration that neural representations of the full algorithmic state (via stack LSTMs) enable accurate joint parsing without expert-crafted features, outperforming prior joint models that relied on linear classifiers and local feature templates.

I would also note an important, underemphasized advantage of the neural approach for joint learning: parameter economy. Unlike linear models that require separate weights for each feature/class pair—and thus expand parameter counts substantially when tasks are combined—neural models amortize parameters across outputs via shared representations, which likely contributes to the observed gains.

Strengths
- Effective joint transition system: The interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions over a shared buffer, along with actions such as M-SWAP and M-SELF, provides a clean mechanism to build both structures incrementally. The design seems strictly stronger than the Henderson et al. (2008) system it builds on.

- Strong empirical results among joint models: The method improves over prior joint parsers on CoNLL 2008–2009 English, confirming that learning full-state representations is particularly beneficial in joint settings where hand-crafted features are costly or unavailable.

- Practicality and speed: The greedy, linear-time parser with learned features is fast end-to-end, making it appealing for real-world use compared to pipelines or globally decoded systems.

- Minimal feature engineering: The model eschews expert-crafted features while remaining competitive with systems that rely on extensive syntactic feature extraction. This is especially compelling for SRL, where traditional systems depend on expensive path-based features.

- Generality: The approach adapts across languages with competitive results, suggesting that the transition design and learned representations are broadly applicable.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of where gains come from: The paper attributes improvements primarily to automatic feature learning, but another plausible factor—parameter economy in joint learning—is not discussed or empirically examined. An ablation or analysis of representation sharing would strengthen the claims.

- Architectural specificity: While the results validate stack LSTMs, the transition system likely works well with other neural architectures (e.g., global beam-search models such as Andor et al., 2016). The paper does not explore whether the improvements are tied to stack LSTMs per se or to neural full-state representations more generally.

- Training without a dynamic oracle: The model is trained with static supervision for a greedy decoder. A dynamic oracle for this transition system seems within reach (by analogy to arc-eager), and could reduce error propagation. Its absence leaves potential accuracy untapped.

Questions to Authors
- Dynamic oracle: Have you considered deriving a dynamic oracle for your semantic transitions? At first glance, the behavior seems analogous to arc-eager:
  • M-SHIFT appears to preclude all semantic arcs between the buffer-front word and any words on the semantic stack.
  • M-REDUCE appears to preclude all semantic arcs between the top of the semantic stack and any words remaining in the buffer.
  • M-LEFT and M-RIGHT each seem to exclude only the reverse arc, and no others.
  Is this characterization correct, and if so, could a dynamic oracle be implemented along these lines?

- Architectural generality: Do you expect the proposed transition system to perform similarly with other neural controllers (e.g., global beam search or globally normalized models)? If you tried such variants, how did they compare?

- Attribution of gains: Can you comment on the role of parameter sharing/economy in the neural model’s joint learning performance versus linear models? Have you observed evidence (e.g., parameter counts, learning curves, or ablations) that supports this explanation?

Overall, this paper presents a well-designed joint transition system and a strong neural instantiation, delivering fast and accurate joint parsing without hand-crafted features. I expect that combining this transition system with complementary neural training techniques (e.g., dynamic oracles, global normalization/beam search) could further improve accuracy and push toward state-of-the-art results.
