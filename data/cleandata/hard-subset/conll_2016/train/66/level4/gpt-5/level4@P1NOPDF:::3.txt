This paper revisits the long-standing problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, applying recent advances in neural models. Joint models are among the most promising outcomes of transition-based neural parsers’ success.

There are two main contributions. First, the authors present a new transition system that appears superior to the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, they show that a neural network handles this joint task well, whereas linear models had previously struggled. They attribute this success to the network’s ability to automatically learn which features to extract. I would add another likely advantage: in a linear model, one must learn a separate weight for each feature–class pair, so jointly modeling two tasks greatly increases the number of parameters. Neural networks, by sharing learned representations, are much more economical in this respect.

I suspect the proposed transition system would work just as well with a range of other neural architectures, for example the globally normalized beam-search model of Andor (2016). Many other orthogonal improvements are also possible. I expect extensions of the authors’ approach to achieve state-of-the-art results.

It would be valuable to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if provided only in an appendix or in follow-up work. At first glance, it seems analogous to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action rules out all semantic arcs between the buffer head and the items on the semantic stack, and the M-D action rules out all semantic arcs between the top of the stack and items in the buffer. The L and R actions each seem to rule out only the reverse-direction arc, and no other.
