General comments
================
The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences:
- the entire parser state is encoded as a vector representation, rather than just the top stack elements or the most recent configurations;
- the algorithm uses purely greedy search.

The key idea is to exploit stack LSTMs so that the parser’s state vector retains information about potentially large-scope syntactic features known to be decisive for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and a candidate role filler head). The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 English dataset and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their results with previously reported performance, showing that the system performs well relative to the 2008/2009 systems but less well than more recent approaches (see the bottom of Table 3). They also emphasize that their method requires no hand-crafted features and is fast due to its simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well written and presents substantial work, building on the recently popular LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which now appears somewhat visionary. However, I have reservations about using a simple greedy algorithm: it makes the results not directly comparable to some of the cited work. Including beam-search results would likely have required only modest additional effort and space.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
- A brief explanation of why both A1 and C-A1 links appear would clarify the target task.
- Please summarize how your transition set differs from that of Henderson et al. In its current form, it is difficult to determine what is directly reused from Henderson et al. versus what is new or modified.

Section 3.3:
- Why do you need representations that concatenate the predicate word and its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, since the disambiguated sense is specific to a predicate.

Section 4:
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focused on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extended the setup to several other languages.
