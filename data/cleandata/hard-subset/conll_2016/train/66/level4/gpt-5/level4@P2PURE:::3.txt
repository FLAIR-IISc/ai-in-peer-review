This paper offers a much-needed revisit of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural network models. Joint models remain one of the most promising aspects of transition-based neural parsers’ success.

The paper makes two contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system that appears to improve upon the Henderson (2008) system it extends. Second, it demonstrates that a neural network performs well on this joint parsing problem, where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the network’s capacity to learn feature representations automatically. I would add another likely advantage: in linear models, each feature/class pair requires a separate parameter, so jointly learning two tasks dramatically increases the number of parameters. Neural networks can share representations and are therefore more parameter-efficient in this setting.

I also suspect the proposed transition system would work well with other neural architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are many orthogonal enhancements one could apply, and I expect extensions of the authors’ method to yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be valuable to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or subsequent work. At first glance, it seems analogous to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action appears to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the front of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, and the M-D action appears to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions each seem to exclude only the reverse arc, and nothing else.
