- Summary: Revisits joint syntactic+semantic dependency parsing with a neural, transition-based, greedy linear-time parser using stack LSTMs; strong English CoNLL 2008–2009 results, competitive multilingual performance, and very fast runtime.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a refined joint transition system (shared buffer with copy semantics, M-SELF, interleaving constraints) making greedy linear-time joint parsing practical and effective.

- Contribution 2: Shows that neural full-state representations via stack LSTMs enable accurate joint parsing without hand-crafted features, outperforming prior linear-feature joint models.

- Additional contribution note: Neural approach yields parameter economy by amortizing parameters across outputs, likely aiding joint learning gains.

- Strength: Effective transition design — interleaving syntactic/semantic actions (e.g., M-SWAP, M-SELF) yields a cleaner, stronger system than Henderson et al. (2008).

- Strength: Strong empirical results among joint models on CoNLL datasets, validating full-state neural representations for joint parsing.

- Strength: Practicality and speed — greedy, linear-time inference with learned features is fast and appealing for real-world use.

- Strength: Minimal feature engineering — competes with systems that use expensive, hand-crafted syntactic/SRL features.

- Strength: Generality — approach adapts across languages with competitive multilingual performance.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of why gains occur — no empirical study separating effects of feature learning from parameter-sharing/economy.

- Weakness: Architectural specificity — unclear whether gains are due to stack LSTMs specifically or any neural full-state controller (no comparisons to other controllers).

- Weakness: Trained with static supervision for greedy decoding; lacks a dynamic oracle, leaving error-propagation reduction unexplored.

- Question: Dynamic oracle — can a dynamic oracle be derived for the semantic transitions (M-SHIFT, M-REDUCE, M-LEFT/M-RIGHT) as with arc-eager systems?

- Question: Architectural generality — would the transition system perform similarly with other neural controllers (global beam-search or globally normalized models)?

- Question: Attribution of gains — can the authors provide evidence (parameter counts, learning curves, ablations) showing parameter sharing/economy explains joint-model improvements?

- Overall: Well-designed joint transition system and strong neural instantiation; combining with dynamic oracles or global training could further boost accuracy.
