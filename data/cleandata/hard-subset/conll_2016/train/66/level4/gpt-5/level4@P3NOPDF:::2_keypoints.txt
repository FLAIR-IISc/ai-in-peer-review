- Summary: Joint syntactic–semantic transition-based parser using stack LSTMs and greedy decoding; competitive with older systems but behind recent approaches; emphasizes feature-free modeling and speed.

- Strength: Clear, well-written presentation building on substantial prior work.

- Strength: Effective use of stack LSTMs to model full parser states, reducing manual feature engineering.

- Strength: Joint modeling of syntax and semantics in a single transition system remains relevant.

- Strength: Solid experimental coverage on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual).

- Concern: Exclusive reliance on greedy inference limits comparability to beam-search or globally trained systems; beam results should be reported.

- Concern: Task setup and transition inventory are insufficiently documented—unclear what is reused from Henderson et al. (2008) versus what is new.

- Concern: Experimental organization confuses multilinguality and the relation between CoNLL 2008 and CoNLL 2009; dataset roles should be made explicit.

- Section 2 issue: Clarify treatment of continuation roles (C-*) vs primary roles (A*) in the transition system and evaluation (collapse, propagate, or separate).

- Section 2 issue: Provide a concise summary/table listing transitions that are identical, modified, or new relative to Henderson et al. (2008).

- Section 3.3 issue: Justify concatenating predicate word form with disambiguated sense and provide ablation (sense-only, form-only, concatenated).

- Inference strategy: Report small-beam (e.g., 4–8) or dynamic-oracle experiments, at least on English, to separate search effects from modeling capacity.

- Experiments: Explicitly separate English-only (CoNLL 2008) and multilingual (CoNLL 2009) results and align training/preprocessing/evaluation protocols with each dataset.

- Experiments: State whether hyperparameters/models are shared across languages or tuned per language and explain incorporation of language-specific resources (e.g., morphology).

- Results: Include inference-time speed comparisons (tokens/sec) versus strong baselines and a brief error analysis highlighting main failure modes (e.g., long-distance arguments, adjuncts, sense errors).

- Results: Indicate whether reported scores are averages over multiple runs and provide standard deviations given neural variance.

- Overall assessment: Well-executed, clean, fast, and feature-light revisit of joint parsing/SRL; main limitation is greedy decoding—addressing documentation, representation justification, reorganization, and beam/search experiments would substantially strengthen the paper.
