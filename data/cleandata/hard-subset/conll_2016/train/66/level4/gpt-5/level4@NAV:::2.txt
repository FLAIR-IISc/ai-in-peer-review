Review- General comments
================
The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main distinctions:
- they employ vector representations for the entire parser state rather than just the top stack elements or the most recent configurations
- the decoding algorithm is a straightforward greedy search
The central idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so the state vector retains potentially large-scope syntactic information, which is known to be crucial for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and the head of a candidate argument). The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their results to previously reported numbers, showing that their system performs well relative to the 2008/2009 systems, but worse than more recent methods (cf. bottom of Table 3). They emphasize that the approach requires no hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm. The paper is clearly written and reports substantial work, building on the recently popular LSTMs and applying them to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which in hindsight seems quite visionary. I have reservations about relying solely on a greedy algorithm: it makes the results not directly comparable to some of the cited works. Including, for example, beam-search results would not have required much additional effort or space.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
A brief note on why both A1 and C-A1 links are present would clarify the target task. A summary of how your transition set differs from that of Henderson et al. would also help; as written, it is difficult to determine what is reused versus what is new or slightly modified.

Section 3.3
Why do you need representations that concatenate the predicate word with its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, since disambiguated senses are predicate-specific.

Section 4
The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focuses on English, and the CoNLL 2009 shared task extends it to several other languages.
