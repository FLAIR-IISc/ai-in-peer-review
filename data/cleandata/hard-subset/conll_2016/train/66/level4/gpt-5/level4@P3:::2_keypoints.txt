- Summary: Proposes a transition-based joint parser using stack LSTMs for syntax and SRL with greedy linear-time inference, achieving strong CoNLL 2008/2009 results and fast runtime.
- Contribution 1: Feature-light joint architecture that composes syntactic and semantic structures via stack LSTMs representing full parser state.
- Contribution 2: Fast, purely greedy joint inference with linear-time feature extraction showing competitiveness without hand-crafted features or global search.
- Contribution 3: Empirical evaluation and ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) quantifying joint-learning benefits across English and multilingual CoNLL datasets.
- Strength: Removes reliance on hand-engineered features by summarizing parser data structures with stack LSTMs while capturing long-range context for SRL.
- Strength: Strong empirical validation with careful baselines and consistent improvements over prior joint systems.
- Strength: Practical efficiency — substantially faster single-core end-to-end runtime than pipeline systems.
- Strength: Thoughtful engineering adaptations (unified buffer, interleaved transitions, M-SELF, transition constraints) for joint parsing.
- Strength: Reproducibility — training details, hyperparameters, pretrained resources specified and code promised.
- Weakness: Greedy decoding may limit accuracy and comparability to beam/global methods; out-of-domain drop suggests sensitivity to greedy choices.
- Weakness: Insufficiently clear description of departures from Henderson et al.; needs a concise summary of algorithmic differences.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of learned representations and errors — lacks diagnostics by distance, crossing arcs, label confusions, and syntax-vs-semantics error attribution.
- Weakness: Predicate representation (word+sense composition) not fully justified; needs ablations (sense-only vs word+sense).
- Weakness: Evaluation organization unclear (mixing CoNLL 2008/2009 settings) and oracle/partial-oracle issues for crossing semantics not quantified.
- Question: Please report beam-search or globally normalized training results, or at least oracle vs achieved accuracy to quantify search errors.
- Question: Provide a concise summary of all departures from Henderson et al. (copy vs move, interleaving policy, M-SELF, constraints, action changes).
- Question: What empirical benefit comes from composing predicate word and sense vectors; any sense-only/word-only ablations or handling of unseen predicates?
- Question: Clarify treatment and distinction of A1 vs C-A1 links and handling of coreference/control labels in transitions and evaluation.
- Question: How many sentences (English and multilingual) cannot be exactly encoded by the oracle due to crossing semantics, and how does that affect training/evaluation?
- Question: Can results be disaggregated by predicate–argument distance and crossing-arc frequency to support claims about recovering long-range features?
- Question: For multilingual results, how much do pretrained embeddings contribute; provide with/without pretraining ablations and note morphology/character-level use.
- Additional: Reorganize Section 4 to clearly separate CoNLL 2008 English, CoNLL 2009 English, and CoNLL 2009 multilingual experiments.
- Additional: Fix typos (e.g., “disambiguated”), explain labels like C-A1 on first use, and include hardware specs and whether runtime includes predicate disambiguation.
- Overall: Solid, clearly written paper that advances joint syntax–semantics with an efficient neural architecture; acceptance recommended contingent on addressing search comparability, transition clarifications, and targeted analyses.
