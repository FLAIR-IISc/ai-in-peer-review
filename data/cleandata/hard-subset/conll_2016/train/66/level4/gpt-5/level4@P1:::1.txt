Summary and contributions
- This paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that combines the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). The model learns a representation of the full parser state and uses greedy inference.
- As I see it, the primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that this combination yields strong performance compared to prior joint systems on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks.
- A secondary contribution is the clarity and completeness of the system description, experimental setup, and hyperparameter choices, which make the work easy to understand and evaluate.

Strengths
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well written and well explained. The narrative is easy to follow, and the related work is thorough and appropriate.
- Sound methodology: The approach is methodologically solid. The integration of the transition system with stack LSTMs is executed cleanly, and the training and evaluation protocols follow established practices for the CoNLL 2008/2009 tasks.
- Transparent system description: The model architecture and transition system are described clearly, making it straightforward to understand how the full parser state is represented and used in decision making.
- Justified hyperparameters: Hyperparameters are reported and justified, which supports reproducibility and helps readers assess the experimental design.
- Strong empirical results: The parser shows compelling performance compared to previous joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. The results are interesting and suggest that learning full state representations can effectively replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing.

Weaknesses
- Limited originality: The core ideas come directly from prior work—Henderson et al.’s semi-synchronized joint transition system and Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM for syntactic parsing. As such, the method itself is not particularly novel. While this combination is valuable and, in my view, overdue, the paper’s originality is limited.
- Dependence on embeddings (Chinese): I have a minor concern about the reliance on high-quality pretrained embeddings, especially for Chinese. The results suggest that very strong embeddings may contribute substantially to the gains, potentially more than the modeling innovations. This raises questions about how much of the improvement stems from the architecture versus the embeddings.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Could you quantify the impact of pretrained embeddings on performance across languages, particularly Chinese? For example, can you report ablations with and without pretrained embeddings to isolate their contribution relative to the model?
- Beyond combining existing components, what do you see as the key methodological insight or technical contribution in your work? Clarifying this would help address the originality concern.

Overall evaluation
I like this paper a lot. It is clearly written, methodologically sound, and empirically compelling. Although the approach is not fundamentally new, it delivers the parser many in the community have been expecting: a clean, greedy joint syntactic–semantic parser that learns full-state representations and performs strongly on CoNLL 2008/2009. I am glad to see this design carried out and evaluated so carefully.
