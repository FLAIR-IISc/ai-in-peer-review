This paper revisits joint syntactic–semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based neural approach. Joint models are a natural beneficiary of recent advances in neural transition-based parsing, and this work demonstrates that promise in a fast, greedy setting.

I see two main contributions:
1) A revised transition system for joint parsing that improves on the Henderson et al. (2008) framework it builds upon. The empirical results suggest the new system is more effective than prior joint variants derived from the same family.
2) A demonstration that neural representations make joint parsing work where linear models have struggled. Beyond the authors’ point about automatic feature learning, a further advantage is parameter economy: linear models require separate weights for each feature–class pair, so joint learning multiplies parameters. The neural architecture amortizes parameters through shared hidden representations, making joint learning more data- and parameter-efficient.

I expect the proposed transition system would work equally well with other neural training and inference regimes, such as the globally normalized beam-search model of Andor et al. (2016). Many orthogonal enhancements could also be layered on (e.g., global normalization, reranking, richer pretraining, or more elaborate contextual encoders). Extensions in these directions could plausibly yield state-of-the-art results.

A useful addition would be a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only partial or approximate (an appendix would suffice). At first glance, it seems close to the arc-eager oracle. Intuitively:
- M-SHIFT appears to rule out any semantic arcs between the buffer front and items on the semantic stack.
- M-REDUCE appears to rule out semantic arcs between the top of the semantic stack and items remaining in the buffer.
- M-LEFT and M-RIGHT seem to preclude the reverse arc once an arc is created, but not others.
If these properties hold (or can be adapted), an oracle could facilitate more robust training and potentially reduce error propagation in the greedy setting.

Overall, the paper makes a timely and valuable contribution by revisiting joint parsing with modern neural state representations, providing a simple, fast parser that outperforms previous joint systems and offers a strong platform for further improvements.
