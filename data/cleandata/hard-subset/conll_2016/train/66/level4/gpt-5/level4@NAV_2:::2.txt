General comments
================
The paper introduces a joint transition-based dependency parser for syntax and semantics, building on the joint framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two primary differences:
- the model encodes the entire parser state as a vector, rather than relying on top-of-stack items or recent configurations
- inference is purely greedy

The central premise is to use stack LSTMs so the state representation can retain broad contextual information that approximates wide-scope syntactic cues known to be important for SRL (e.g., the path between a predicate and a candidate argument head).

The system is evaluated on CoNLL 2008 (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets. The comparisons against prior work indicate strong performance relative to contemporaneous 2008/2009 systems, though it trails more recent approaches (see bottom of Table 3). The authors stress that their approach avoids hand-engineered features and is efficient thanks to its greedy search.

Overall, the paper is clear and presents a substantial piece of work, leveraging recent LSTM techniques applied to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which in retrospect was prescient. My main concern is the exclusive use of a simple greedy search: this limits comparability with several cited methods. Including, for instance, beam-search results would not add much complexity or space, and would strengthen the empirical story.

More detailed comments / questions
==================================
Section 2:
A brief explanation of why both A1 and C-A1 relations can coexist would clarify the target representation and task setup.

It would be helpful to concisely summarize how the transition set here differs from Henderson et al.’s, to make clear what is reused versus what is new or modified. As written, it is hard to disentangle inherited components from innovations.

Section 3.3
Why is it necessary to compose a representation that concatenates the word (predicate) with its disambiguated sense, given that senses are predicate-specific? This seems potentially redundant; please justify the design choice.

Section 4
The delineation between Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilingual coverage. CoNLL 2008 is English-only, whereas CoNLL 2009 extends to multiple languages; the text could better reflect this distinction.
