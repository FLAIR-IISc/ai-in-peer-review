General comments
The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences:
- they use vector representations for the entire parser state, rather than just the top of the stack or the most recent configurations
- the algorithm uses purely greedy search

The key idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so that the state representation retains potentially long-range syntactic information known to be crucial for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and a candidate role-filler head).

The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their results with previously reported systems, showing competitive performance relative to 2008/2009 systems, but weaker performance compared to more recent approaches (see the bottom of Table 3). They emphasize that the proposed system requires no hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well written and describes substantial work, building on the now-popular LSTMs, applied to the Henderson et al. algorithm, which in retrospect appears somewhat visionary. However, I have reservations about relying solely on a greedy algorithm: this choice makes results not fully comparable to some cited works that use search. Reporting beam-search results would likely require minimal additional implementation and space and would strengthen the empirical comparison.

Detailed comments and questions
Section 2:
- Please clarify the presence of both A1 and C-A1 links to better define the target task and how continuation roles are handled.
- Provide a concise summary of how your transition set differs from that of Henderson et al. (2008). As written, it is hard to determine what is directly reused versus what is new or modified.

Section 3.3:
- Why do you need representations that concatenate the predicate word and its disambiguated sense? This seems redundant, since disambiguated senses are predicate-specific.

Section 4:
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focuses on English, while the CoNLL 2009 shared task extends to multiple languages. Please make the separation between the English-only and multilingual settings clearer (datasets, preprocessing, and evaluation protocols).
