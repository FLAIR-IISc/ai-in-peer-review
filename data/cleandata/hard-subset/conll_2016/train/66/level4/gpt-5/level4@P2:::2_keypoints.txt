- Presents a transition-based joint parser that predicts syntactic dependencies and semantic role labels using stack LSTMs and greedy inference.
- Key departures from prior work: whole-state vector representations (stack LSTMs) replacing hand-crafted features, and fully greedy, linear-time inference.
- Introduces an M-SELF transition to handle self-dependencies and interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer.
- Strong empirical performance on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual) among joint models, trading some accuracy for speed and simplicity.
- Demonstrates that stack LSTMs encoding full algorithmic state enable competitive joint syntax–semantics parsing without handcrafted features.
- Proposes a practical, fast joint transition system (including M-SELF and interleaving policy) and provides ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).
- Provides an efficient implementation with fast end-to-end runtime, suitable for practical use.
- Paper is clear and well written, leveraging representation learning to replace costly manual features.
- Empirical validation shows the joint model outperforms earlier joint approaches, supporting whole-state representations for transition decisions.
- Ablation studies help disentangle the effects of syntax and joint training, and reveal in-domain gains vs out-of-domain overfitting.
- Linear-time inference with integrated feature computation yields substantially faster runtime than high-performing pipelines.
- Multilingual results are competitive across languages despite minimal language-specific engineering.
- Limiting decoding to greedy search reduces comparability with beam/global-inference systems and likely leaves accuracy on the table.
- The paper does not clearly enumerate what is novel versus inherited from Henderson et al.’s transition systems.
- The predicate-sense composition (gd(v,p)) is not well motivated; an ablation comparing sense-only vs word+sense is needed.
- Evaluation structure blurs CoNLL 2008 vs CoNLL 2009 setups; multilingual hyperparameters, embeddings, and morphological-feature usage are under-specified.
- Analysis lacks detailed error diagnostics (long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs, impact of imperfect oracle conversion).
- Request to report beam-search or dynamic-oracle results to show accuracy/speed trade-offs relative to greedy decoding.
- Ask for a concise summary of differences from Henderson et al.: action inventory, copy vs move behavior, interleaving policy, and constraints.
- Clarify handling and consistency enforcement when core and continuation roles (e.g., A1 and C-A1) both appear.
- Provide empirical evidence or an ablation supporting predicate word+sense composition versus sense-only or back-off for unseen predicates.
- Clarify multilingual setup: per-language hyperparameter tuning, use of morphological features, and effect on languages like Czech.
- Report the oracle upper bound imposed by imperfect transition encoding (e.g., the ~1.5% of English sentences not perfectly encoded) and its effect on LAS/SRL.
- Provide error analyses explaining Brown vs WSJ overfitting in joint training, e.g., role of predicate–argument paths or crossing arcs.
- Overall: solid, timely contribution showing full-state neural representations make a simple, greedy joint parser competitive; clarifying algorithmic innovations and adding light non-greedy experiments would strengthen the paper.
