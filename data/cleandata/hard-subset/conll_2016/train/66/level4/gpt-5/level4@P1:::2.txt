Summary and contributions
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The two main differences are: (1) the use of stack LSTMs to learn vector representations of the entire parser state (rather than only top-of-stack items or recent configurations), eliminating hand-crafted features; and (2) purely greedy inference with linear-time feature extraction and decoding. The motivation is that stack LSTMs can retain broad contextual information (e.g., predicate–argument paths) that are known to be crucial for SRL, without expensive feature engineering.

The system is evaluated on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual). It outperforms prior joint models on English 2008/2009, is competitive with top systems from those shared tasks, but lags behind more recent SRL systems that rely on expert-designed features, global inference, or additional annotations (as noted at the bottom of Table 3). The model is fast due to its greedy design, and the paper promises open-source code.

Contributions as I see them (in order of importance):
- A simple, fast, joint transition-based parser that learns whole-state representations via stack LSTMs and dispenses with hand-crafted features, yet achieves strong performance on CoNLL 2008/2009.
- An empirical demonstration that whole-state representation learning can close much of the gap to pipelines while outperforming prior joint models, with multilingual results comparable to top shared-task systems.
- A practical engineering contribution: linear-time, greedy inference with competitive accuracy and low runtime.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results among joint models: The joint parser substantially outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and related joint approaches on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, supporting the claim that learning whole-state representations is beneficial.
- No hand-engineered features and competitive accuracy: Despite omitting expert-crafted syntactic features (e.g., explicit path features), the model delivers semantic and overall performance comparable to top shared-task pipelines from 2008/2009, and within striking distance of more recent SRL systems that exploit orthogonal advances.
- Speed and simplicity: The purely greedy, stack-LSTM-based approach provides linear-time parsing and impressive end-to-end runtime (177.6±18 s on CoNLL 2009 English test), about 2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline under identical conditions.
- Clear architecture and ablations: The syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint variants are informative. They show predictable trends (e.g., semantics benefits from syntax; joint improves in-domain, hybrid more robust out-of-domain), which lends credibility to the analysis.
- Well-written and substantial: The paper is clearly presented and builds thoughtfully on the Henderson et al. framework, which (in hindsight) was prescient for joint transition-based parsing with learned representations.

Weaknesses
- Greedy inference limits comparability: Many cited systems use beam search or global inference. Reporting beam-search results (even with a modest beam) would strengthen comparability and help disentangle model capacity from search errors. It seems feasible to include such results without major additional complexity or space.
- Insufficient clarity on transition set differences: The paper states it largely follows Henderson et al. (2013) but introduces additional transitions (e.g., self-dependencies) and modified behaviors (e.g., copying vs. moving buffer items). A concise summary of what is reused vs. changed would make the contribution clearer.
- Minor clarity issues in task description: The presence of both A1 and C-A1 links in examples can confuse readers unfamiliar with CoNLL label conventions. A brief explanation would help.
- Section organization around multilinguality: The split between Sections 4.1 (CoNLL 2008) and 4.2 (CoNLL 2009) makes it easy to miss that only 2009 includes multilingual datasets. Clarifying this upfront or reorganizing slightly would improve readability.
- Potential redundancy in predicate representation: The model composes a word’s representation with its disambiguated sense for M-PRED decisions. Since senses are lexeme-specific, it is not obvious why concatenating both is necessary; a brief justification would be helpful.

Questions to authors
- Section 2: In examples, both A1 and C-A1 can appear. Could you briefly clarify the intended treatment/interpretation of continuation roles and how your transition system handles them?
- Transitions vs. Henderson et al.: Please provide a compact summary of differences from Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), including: copying vs. moving items in S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT, constraints on interleaving syntax/semantics, the M-SELF transition for self-dependencies, and any changes to M-SWAP constraints.
- Section 3.3 (predicate composition): Why is it necessary to concatenate the word representation with the disambiguated sense, given senses are predicate-specific? What empirical gain did you observe compared to using only the sense or only the lemma?
- Search: Did you try small-beam decoding or easy-first variants? If so, how did accuracy/runtime trade off against pure greedy inference?
- Section 4 organization: Since CoNLL 2008 is English-only and CoNLL 2009 is multilingual, could you clarify this earlier in Section 4 or adjust headings to make the scope of multilingual experiments immediately clear?
