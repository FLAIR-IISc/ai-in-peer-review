- Paper proposes a fast transition-based joint syntactic+semantic parser using stack LSTMs, interleaved transitions, an M-SELF action, and greedy linear-time decoding; achieves best prior joint English results and competitive multilingual scores.

- Main contribution: simple, efficient joint parser that represents full algorithmic state with stack LSTMs, removing hand-crafted features and enabling accurate greedy decoding.

- Main contribution: practical transition system and training recipe (buffer-copying for syntax, M-SELF for self-arguments, interleaving constraints) delivering linear-time inference with modest resources.

- Main contribution: empirical evidence that learned representations can replace expensive pipeline features — outperforming earlier joint systems and approaching pipeline SOTA while being substantially faster.

- Strength: Strong empirical results and compelling efficiency (≈178s end-to-end on CoNLL 2009 English vs ≈440s for a competitive pipeline).

- Strength: Clear architectural insight — encoding full parser state with stack LSTMs allows accurate greedy decisions without expert features; M-SELF and buffer-copying are effective simplicity-driven fixes.

- Strength: Thoughtful experimental design and ablations across CoNLL 2008/2009 and multilingual settings, with useful model variants and implementation details (embeddings, predicate backoff).

- Strength: Attractive speed–accuracy trade-off and small memory footprint, suitable for large-scale or latency-sensitive applications.

- Strength: Honest framing and positioning, acknowledging pipeline systems still lead in some respects and highlighting orthogonal future integrations.

- Weakness: Limited novelty in the transition system and no dynamic oracle; main novelty is neural state representation, and static greedy training may exacerbate error propagation.

- Weakness: Oracle and search limitations under-analyzed — semantic oracle imperfections (~1.5% of sentences) not quantified, and no search vs. model error analysis or small-beam experiments.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to alternative neural architectures (e.g., BiLSTM feature extractors, beam/global models) to isolate the contribution of stack LSTMs vs. transition design.

- Weakness: Generalization/overfitting concerns — joint model underperforms hybrid OOD (Brown); lacks diagnostics on where joint training helps or hurts.

- Weakness: Dependence on pretrained embeddings and lack of morphology modeling in multilingual experiments; missing ablations on embedding impact.

- Question: Is a dynamic oracle feasible for the interleaved transition system and what gains might it yield?

- Question: Did you try small-beam decoding (e.g., beam=4/8) and what are the speed/accuracy trade-offs, especially OOD?

- Question: How often does the oracle produce suboptimal sequences due to crossing arcs and what is the measured impact on training loss and test accuracy?

- Question (ablations): Accuracy without pretrained embeddings across languages? Incremental contribution of M-SELF? Sensitivity to the action-history stack A?

- Question: How frequently is the “01” predicate default used at test time and what is its error rate relative to gold senses?

- Additional note: Emphasize that neural models are more parameter-efficient than linear models via shared representations, which likely explains part of the gains.

- Minor writing suggestions: clarify the interleaving constraint early and summarize error types where joint parsing helps versus hurts.
