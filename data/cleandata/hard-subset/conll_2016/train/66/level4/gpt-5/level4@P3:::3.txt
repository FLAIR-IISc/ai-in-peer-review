Summary and contributions
This paper presents a fast, transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependencies and semantic role graphs using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state. The system interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions over a shared buffer, adds a M-SELF transition for self-arguments, and uses greedy inference with linear-time complexity. On CoNLL 2008–2009 English, it achieves the best accuracy among prior joint models and competitive multilingual results, with strong speed-accuracy trade-offs.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, efficient joint parser that learns continuous representations of the full algorithmic state (stacks, buffer, action history) via stack LSTMs, eliminating expert-crafted features and enabling fully greedy decoding while surpassing prior joint syntax–semantics models.
2) A practical transition system and training recipe for joint parsing (e.g., buffer-copying for syntax, M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments, constraints for interleaving), yielding linear-time inference and modest resource requirements.
3) Empirical evidence that representation learning can replace expensive pipeline features: the joint model outperforms earlier joint systems and approaches SOTA pipelines in accuracy while being substantially faster.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results among joint models with compelling efficiency. The joint system improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and other joint approaches on CoNLL 2008–2009 English. The reported end-to-end runtime (≈178s on CoNLL 2009 English test, single core) substantially outpaces competitive pipelines (e.g., Lei et al., ≈440s), supporting the practical value of the approach.

2) Clear architectural insight: full-state representation with stack LSTMs for both syntax and semantics. Encoding the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) allows the model to dispense with hand-engineered features and still make accurate greedy decisions. The added M-SELF transition and buffer-copying design are simple, effective solutions to dataset-specific and joint-interaction constraints.

3) Thoughtful experimental design and ablations. The paper evaluates on CoNLL 2008/2009, includes multilingual experiments, and provides useful variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid), which illuminate how syntax helps semantics (joint beats semantics-only in-domain) and how joint learning can overfit (hybrid generalizes better OOD). Predicate-lemma backoff for unseen senses and the pretrained embeddings setup are well described.

4) Good speed–accuracy trade-off and small memory footprint. The model’s linear-time, greedy execution with integrated feature learning is attractive for large-scale or latency-sensitive applications.

5) Framing and positioning are honest and careful. The paper acknowledges that some pipeline systems with expert-crafted features and exact inference remain ahead, and explicitly separates orthogonal advances that could be integrated in the future.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty in the transition system and lack of dynamic oracle. The transition inventory is largely adapted from Henderson et al. with targeted extensions (e.g., M-SELF). The main novelty lies in neural state representation rather than parsing algorithms. Moreover, training uses a static oracle and greedy decoding. Without a dynamic oracle or exploration-based training, the model is susceptible to error propagation; this likely contributes to the observed in-domain vs. out-of-domain gap. A dynamic oracle for the interleaved syntax/semantics setting would strengthen the training and analysis.

2) Oracle and search limitations are under-analyzed. The paper notes that the semantic oracle is imperfect due to crossing arcs and that ≈1.5% of English sentences cannot be exactly encoded, but the impact on training accuracy and final performance is not quantified. Similarly, there is no analysis of search errors vs. model errors, nor results for small-beam decoding to assess whether modest search increases would help without sacrificing much speed.

3) Comparisons to alternative neural architectures are missing. Given that the core idea is full-state representation learning, it is natural to ask whether similar gains would arise with other neural models (e.g., global/beam search transition models, BiLSTM feature extractors with MLP scoring). The paper would benefit from a discussion or a small experiment to tease apart the contribution of stack LSTMs vs. the transition design.

4) Generalization and overfitting concerns. The joint model underperforms the hybrid model OOD (Brown), suggesting overfitting to WSJ syntax–semantics correlations. More diagnostics (e.g., predicate/argument type breakdowns, length sensitivity, rare-word impact) would clarify where joint training helps or hurts.

5) Dependence on pretrained embeddings and limited morphology. Multilingual results rely on pretrained embeddings (notably for Chinese). The model does not incorporate morphology, which likely hurts in morphologically rich languages. While this is acknowledged as future work, even a brief ablation quantifying the effect of embeddings would be informative.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Dynamic oracle: Is a dynamic oracle feasible for your interleaved transition system? For instance, can you characterize the loss-avoiding action set for M-SHIFT, M-REDUCE, M-LEFT, and M-RIGHT (mirroring arc-eager dynamics) and quantify potential gains?
- Beam search: Did you try small-beam decoding (e.g., beam=4/8)? What are the speed and accuracy trade-offs, especially OOD?
- Oracle imperfections: How often does the oracle produce suboptimal transition sequences due to crossing arcs, and what is the measured impact on training loss and test accuracy?
- Ablations: 
  • What is the accuracy without pretrained word embeddings across languages?
  • What is the incremental contribution of M-SELF (given self-arguments are ≈6.7% of arcs)?
  • How sensitive are results to the action-history stack A?
- Predicate sense defaulting: How frequently is the “01” default used at test time, and what is its error rate relative to gold senses? Any analysis of systematic sense errors?

Additional comments
- It may be worth explicitly noting that neural models are more parameter-efficient than linear models in joint settings: sharing internal representations can avoid the feature×label explosion inherent in linear classifiers, which likely contributes to the observed gains.
- Minor writing suggestions: clarify the interleaving constraint early (when and why control switches between S and M), and summarize the error types where joint helps vs. hurts.
