General comments
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by the joint parser of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors claim two main differences:
- They use vector representations for the entire parser state, rather than focusing on the top elements of the stack or the last parser configurations.
- The algorithm relies on plain greedy search.

The key idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so that the vector encoding the parser state retains information about potentially large-scope syntactic features, which are known to be decisive for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between the predicate and the candidate role-filler head).

Experiments are conducted on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. The authors compare their results to previously reported performances, showing that their system performs well relative to the original 2008/2009 systems, but lags behind more recent proposals (cf. the bottom of Table 3). They emphasize that the proposed system requires no hand-crafted features and is fast due to the simple greedy algorithm.

The paper is well written and describes substantial work, building on the recently popular LSTMs, applied to the Henderson et al. algorithm—which, in retrospect, appears somewhat visionary. However, I have reservations about the choice of a purely greedy algorithm: it makes the results not directly comparable to some of the cited works. It would not have required much additional effort or space to report, for instance, beam-search performance.

More detailed comments and questions
Section 2:
- A brief comment explaining the simultaneous presence of both A1 and C-A1 links would help clarify the target task.
- Please provide a concise summary of the differences between your transition set and that of Henderson et al. In its current form, it is difficult to determine what is directly reused versus what is new or slightly modified.

Section 3.3:
- Why do you concatenate the predicate word with its disambiguated sense in the representation? This seems redundant, since disambiguated senses are specific to a predicate.

Section 4:
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focuses on English, whereas the CoNLL 2009 shared task extends to several other languages; this distinction should be made clearer.
