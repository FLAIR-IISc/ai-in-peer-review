Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependency trees and semantic role graphs using stack LSTMs to encode the entire parsing state. The approach eliminates hand-engineered features, achieves linear-time inference (including feature extraction), and reports strong results on the CoNLL 2008–2009 English tasks, with competitive multilingual performance and notable speed.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A fast, feature-light joint syntax–semantics parser that learns dense representations of the full algorithmic state via four stack LSTMs (syntax, semantics, buffer, action history), including an extension to the transition set (M-SELF) and integrated predicate sense disambiguation.
2) Strong empirical evidence that full-state neural representations enable joint parsing to outperform prior joint models, with results comparable to leading pipelines and substantial runtime gains.
3) A set of ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that quantify how syntax and joint modeling affect SRL performance, including in- vs out-of-domain behavior.

Strengths
1) Clear and rigorous methodology with reproducibility in mind. The paper cleanly formulates the joint transition system, the stack-LSTM state encoding, composition functions for syntactic and semantic structures, training objective, and constraints. Hyperparameters and implementation details (e.g., dropout, pretrained embeddings, learning rates, runtime, memory footprint) are documented well, supporting reproducibility.

2) Strong empirical results with compelling comparisons and ablations. The joint model surpasses prior joint parsers (e.g., Henderson et al.) on CoNLL 2008–2009 English, and is competitive with top pipeline systems despite using no expert-crafted features or global decoding. The variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) convincingly show that syntax helps SRL and that joint learning brings additional gains in-domain, while also revealing out-of-domain trade-offs.

3) Efficiency and practicality. The greedy parser runs in linear time and delivers a reported 177.6±18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English on a single core—about 2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline under identical settings. This speed and simplicity make the approach attractive for real-world deployments.

4) Thoughtful design choices tailored to joint parsing. Interleaving syntactic and semantic transitions over a shared buffer, the use of action history, and the introduction of M-SELF for nominal self-arguments demonstrate attention to the specific demands of joint dependency and SRL parsing.

5) Breadth across languages. The multilingual experiments, while not the primary focus, indicate the approach generalizes beyond English and is broadly competitive with leading systems.

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty. The core ingredients—the synchronized transition system and the stack-LSTM architecture—are largely borrowed from prior work (Henderson et al.; Dyer et al.). The novelty lies primarily in their combination and careful engineering for joint parsing rather than in a fundamentally new learning or inference method.

2) Gap to state-of-the-art SRL systems that exploit full syntax and global inference. The joint model trails the best pipeline systems that use rich, globally optimized features and exact/structured inference. While orthogonal, this limits the headline empirical advance and suggests the proposed method may underutilize global structural information that benefits SRL.

3) Generalization and overfitting concerns. The joint model’s advantage over the pipeline-like hybrid diminishes or reverses out of domain (Brown), indicating potential overfitting to WSJ when optimizing joint decisions. More analysis (e.g., by dependency type or predicate class) would clarify where joint learning helps or hurts.

4) Heavy reliance on pretrained embeddings and limited analysis of their impact. The multilingual results, especially in Chinese, appear to depend strongly on pretrained embeddings. There is limited disentanglement of gains attributable to representation learning versus architecture, and no ablation quantifying this for English.

5) Incomplete oracle conversion for semantics and limited error analysis. Because some gold semantic graphs cannot be perfectly encoded by the transition sequence (due to crossing arcs), there is a potential mismatch between training signals and the target structures. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify its impact or provide detailed error breakdowns.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- How much of the joint model’s gain comes from the M-SELF transition and the copy-vs-move modification to arc-eager? Please provide ablations.
- Did you try small beams or exploration (e.g., dynamic oracles) to narrow the gap with globally optimized SRL while retaining most of the speed? Any empirical trends?
- Can you quantify the contribution of pretrained embeddings on English (in-domain and out-of-domain) and on each non-English language?
- How sensitive is performance to hyperparameters such as action-history size, composition dimensions, and dropout? Any training stability issues?
- Could you provide an error analysis highlighting where joint modeling helps (e.g., long-distance arguments, attachment ambiguities) and where it hurts relative to the hybrid?

Additional Comments
- The integrated default “01” sense fallback is practical; an ablation showing the impact of this heuristic on overall SRL would be helpful.
- Character-level or morphological features could plausibly reduce reliance on word embeddings and improve results in morphologically rich languages; outlining this as future work is appropriate.
- Clarifying how frequently the oracle mismatch for semantics occurs in each language and its measurable impact on training or evaluation would strengthen the analysis.

Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed, clearly written paper that delivers a fast, effective joint parser without hand-crafted features and establishes new performance levels for joint models on CoNLL 2008–2009. While the methodological novelty is modest, the work fills an important gap by showing that stack-LSTM state representations and a carefully designed transition system yield competitive accuracy and excellent speed in joint syntax–semantics parsing. I recommend acceptance.
