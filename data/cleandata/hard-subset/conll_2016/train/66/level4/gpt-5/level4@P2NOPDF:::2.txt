General comments
The paper introduces a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The authors highlight two main differences:
- They represent the entire parser state with vectors, rather than only the top of the stack or the most recent configurations.
- They use a purely greedy decoding algorithm.

The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs so the parser’s state vector can retain long-range syntactic information known to be important for semantic role labeling (e.g., the path between a predicate and a candidate argument head). The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 dataset (English) and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 dataset. Results are compared against prior work: performance is competitive with systems from the 2008/2009 shared tasks but lags behind more recent approaches (see the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize that their model uses no hand-crafted features and is fast due to its simple greedy search.

The paper is clearly written and presents substantial work that builds on recent advances in LSTMs, applied to the Henderson et al. framework, which in retrospect appears somewhat visionary. My main reservation concerns the choice of a purely greedy algorithm: this limits comparability with several cited systems. Reporting beam-search results (even with small beams) would likely entail modest additional effort and space while enabling more direct comparisons.

More detailed comments / questions
Section 2:
- A brief explanation of why both A1 and C-A1 links appear would clarify the target task and annotation conventions.
- Please summarize how your transition inventory differs from that of Henderson et al. (2008). As written, it is hard to discern what is reused versus what is new or modified.

Section 3.3:
- Why do you concatenate the predicate form with its disambiguated sense in the representation? This seems redundant, as senses are predicate-specific.

Section 4:
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. CoNLL 2008 focuses on English, while CoNLL 2009 extends to multiple languages; please clarify how the experimental setup and results align with these settings.
