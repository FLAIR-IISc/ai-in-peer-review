Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a Stack-LSTM–based transition parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. It combines the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015), and evaluates the resulting greedy, linear-time parser on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks. The primary contribution is a careful integration that learns representations of the full parser state without hand-crafted features, demonstrating strong empirical performance among joint models on these benchmarks. A secondary contribution is a clear, reproducible description of the system and training setup.

Strengths
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is clearly written and well structured. The system description is easy to follow, and the related work is appropriately scoped and informative.
- Sound methodology: The approach is technically solid, drawing a coherent line from prior work on synchronized transitions and stack LSTMs to a practical joint parser. The choices around training, constraints, and interleaving of syntactic and semantic actions are well justified.
- Strong empirical results: The model delivers competitive or superior performance compared to prior joint systems on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, showing the value of learning full parser-state representations without expert-crafted features.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters and implementation details are documented, aiding replication and fair comparison. The discussion of runtime is useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The method largely integrates the transition system of Henderson et al. with the stack LSTM of Dyer et al. As such, it is better characterized as a compelling combination and thorough evaluation of known components than as a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.
- Embedding dependence (Chinese): The results suggest that high-quality pretrained embeddings may account for a substantial portion of the gains for Chinese. This raises the question of how much improvement stems from the model versus the embeddings, and whether the conclusions hold with weaker or no pretraining.

Overall assessment
Despite limited methodological novelty, this is a well-executed paper that the community has been anticipating: a clear, efficient, and competitive joint parser that dispenses with hand-crafted features by learning full parser-state representations. The writing, experimental setup, and analysis are strong, and the results on CoNLL 2008/2009 are compelling. I am pleased to see this integration carried out and evaluated carefully.
