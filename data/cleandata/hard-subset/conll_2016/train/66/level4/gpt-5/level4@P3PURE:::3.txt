This paper offers an overdue revisit to the problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, bringing recent advances in neural network models to bear. Joint models are among the most promising outcomes of the success of transition-based neural parsers.

There are two main contributions. First, the authors introduce a new transition system that appears to improve upon the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, they demonstrate that a neural network model succeeds on this joint task where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this success to the network’s ability to learn useful features automatically. I would add that neural networks also enjoy a parameter-efficiency advantage in the joint setting: in a linear model, one must learn a weight for each feature/class pair, so jointly modeling two tasks substantially increases the number of parameters, whereas a neural network can share representations more economically.

I suspect the proposed transition system would work just as well with a range of other neural architectures, such as the global beam-search model of Andor (2016). There are also many orthogonal improvements that could be incorporated. I expect extensions of the authors’ approach to yield state-of-the-art results.

It would be valuable to see an attempt to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or deferred to follow-up work. At first glance, the situation seems similar to the arc-eager oracle. The M-S action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and the words on the semantic stack, and the M-D action excludes all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and the words in the buffer. The L and R actions each exclude the reverse arc, and no other.
