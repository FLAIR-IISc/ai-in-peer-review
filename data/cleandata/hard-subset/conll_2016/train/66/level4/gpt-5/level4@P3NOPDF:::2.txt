Summary and contributions
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic transition-based dependency parser inspired by Henderson et al. (2008). The two main differences claimed are: (i) the use of vectorial representations for the entire parser state, rather than only the top of the stack or recent configurations, enabled by stack LSTMs; and (ii) a purely greedy decoding algorithm. The key idea is that stack LSTMs can retain information about long-range syntactic context—such as the path between a predicate and a candidate argument head—that is important for semantic role labeling (SRL). Experiments are conducted on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual). The system is competitive with contemporaneous 2008/2009 systems but lags behind more recent approaches (as shown at the bottom of Table 3). The authors emphasize the absence of hand-engineered features and the speed benefits of greedy inference.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written presentation that builds on a substantial body of prior work.
- Effective use of stack LSTMs to model full parser states, reducing manual feature engineering.
- Joint modeling of syntax and semantics in a single transition system, following a line of research that remains relevant.
- Solid experimental coverage of both English and multilingual benchmarks.

Concerns and areas for improvement
- The exclusive reliance on greedy inference limits comparability with several cited systems that use beam search or global training. Reporting beam-search results (even with a small beam) would strengthen the empirical claims and provide a fairer comparison.
- Some aspects of the task setup and transition inventory are insufficiently documented for readers to assess what is reused from Henderson et al. (2008) versus what is new or modified.
- The organization of the experimental sections creates confusion about multilinguality and the relation between CoNLL 2008 and CoNLL 2009.

Detailed comments and questions
Section 2 (task setup and transitions)
- The simultaneous presence of A1 and C-A1 links would benefit from clarification. Please explain the treatment of continuation roles (C-*) and how they interact with primary roles in your transition system and evaluation (e.g., whether you collapse, propagate, or treat them as distinct labels during training/inference).
- Provide a concise summary of the differences between your transition set and that of Henderson et al. (2008). A table or bullet list indicating which transitions are identical, which are modified, and which are new would make the contribution clearer. As written, it is hard to disentangle what is directly reused from what is adapted for your joint model or for the stack-LSTM architecture.

Section 3.3 (representations)
- You concatenate the predicate word form with its disambiguated sense. This appears potentially redundant because senses are predicate-specific. Please justify this design choice: for example, does the word form help with rare or unseen senses, cross-predicate parameter sharing, or cross-lingual transfer? An ablation comparing sense-only, form-only, and concatenated representations would clarify the contribution of each component.

Inference strategy
- While the commitment to a greedy algorithm yields speed and simplicity, it weakens the comparison to systems using beam search or global normalization. Reporting results with a small beam (e.g., 4–8) and/or dynamic oracles would help isolate how much of the performance gap to recent systems is due to search versus modeling capacity.
- If resource constraints preclude beam experiments across all settings, a focused study on English in CoNLL 2008 would still be informative.

Section 4 (experiments and multilinguality)
- The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilingual experiments. CoNLL 2008 is English-only, while CoNLL 2009 adds multiple languages. Please make this structure explicit up front, clearly separate English-only results from multilingual ones, and ensure that training resources, preprocessing, and evaluation protocols are aligned with each dataset’s conventions.
- Clarify whether hyperparameters and model choices are shared across languages or tuned per language. Briefly discuss how language-specific resources (e.g., morphological features in CoNLL 2009) are incorporated into the model.

Results and comparisons
- The paper shows that the proposed model compares favorably with earlier systems but is behind more recent approaches. Since you emphasize feature-free modeling and speed, consider adding:
  - Inference-time speed comparisons (tokens/sec) versus strong baselines, to quantify the practical benefits of greediness.
  - A brief error analysis identifying the main categories where performance lags recent systems (e.g., long-distance arguments, adjunct roles, sense disambiguation).
- Specify whether the reported scores are averaged over multiple runs or from a single run, and include standard deviations if applicable, given the known variance of neural models.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed piece of work that revisits a joint parsing/SRL framework with modern neural architectures, yielding a clean, fast, and feature-light system. The main limitation is the exclusive use of greedy decoding, which reduces the strength of the empirical claims relative to beam-based or globally normalized counterparts. Addressing the points above—especially by clarifying the transition inventory versus Henderson et al. (2008), explaining the predicate/sense representation choice, reorganizing the multilingual experiments, and providing beam-search results or speed/accuracy trade-offs—would considerably strengthen the paper.
