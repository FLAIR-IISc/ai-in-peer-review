This paper revisits the long-standing problem of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, bringing recent neural techniques to bear on a setting where linear models have historically struggled. The work is timely: joint models are among the most promising directions in transition-based parsing, and a careful reexamination with modern architectures is overdue.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system that improves on the Henderson (2008) framework on which it is based. Second, it demonstrates that a neural model performs well on joint dependency parsing, in contrast to prior linear approaches. The authors attribute the neural model’s gains to its ability to learn useful features automatically. I agree with this explanation and would add that neural models are also more parameter-efficient for joint learning: linear models require a separate weight for each feature/class pair, so moving from single-task to joint prediction dramatically expands the parameter space. Neural architectures amortize feature extraction across tasks, enabling more economical sharing and improved generalization.

Strengths:
- Clear and relevant problem framing, with a well-motivated return to joint parsing under modern neural architectures.
- A well-designed transition system that appears to be a principled improvement over prior work.
- Empirical evidence that a neural transition-based parser can succeed on the joint task where linear models lag, supporting the paper’s central claim.
- The approach seems compatible with a range of neural parsing frameworks; I suspect the transition system could be integrated with other architectures (e.g., the global beam-search model of Andor et al., 2016) and benefit from orthogonal advances.

Suggestions and questions:
- Dynamic oracle: It would be valuable to derive (or at least sketch) a dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system, even if only in an appendix or as follow-up work. At first glance, the oracle seems analogous to arc-eager. Specifically, the M-S action appears to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the start of the buffer and any words on the semantic stack, while the M-D action seems to exclude all semantic arcs between the top of the stack and any words remaining in the buffer. The L and R actions each appear to exclude only the reverse arc. Formalizing and proving these properties would clarify the system’s error-correction behavior and could enable more effective training.
- Generality: Given the likely portability of the transition system, it would help to briefly discuss how it might integrate with other neural decoders (e.g., global training, beam search, or higher-order scoring) and what challenges that might pose.
- Analysis: If space permits, an ablation probing which aspects of the architecture and feature representations drive the joint gains would strengthen the argument that neural feature learning and parameter sharing are key. Even a compact analysis could illuminate whether improvements stem mainly from better syntactic context modeling, semantic role regularization, or cross-task sharing in the encoder.
- Error characteristics: A short qualitative error analysis could reveal where the joint model most benefits over separate models (e.g., resolving syntactic ambiguities via semantic cues or vice versa).

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely contribution that demonstrates meaningful advances in joint dependency parsing with neural transition-based models. The new transition system is well motivated, and the empirical results substantiate the claim that neural architectures can handle the joint task more effectively than linear models, likely due to both automatic feature learning and the parameter efficiency inherent in shared neural representations. I expect that integrating this transition system with more global training strategies and complementary improvements could yield state-of-the-art performance. A dynamic oracle derivation would further strengthen the work, clarifying the training signal and aiding future extensions. I recommend acceptance.
