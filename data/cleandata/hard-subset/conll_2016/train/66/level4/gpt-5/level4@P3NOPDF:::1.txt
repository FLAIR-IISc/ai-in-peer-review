This paper presents a Stack-LSTM parser that combines the joint syntactic–semantic transition-based framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) with the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). By adopting Henderson’s transition system and Dyer’s neural stack mechanism, the authors report strong performance on the CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 shared tasks, often outperforming prior joint systems.

Overall, I found the paper clear, well-structured, and thorough. The writing is crisp, the exposition is easy to follow, and the related work is well-situated and comprehensive. The methodology appears sound, with careful design choices and justified hyperparameters. The system description is detailed enough to enable replication, and the discussion of results is thoughtful and appropriately cautious. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical value of unifying established transition systems with modern recurrent architectures.

I do have one minor methodological concern regarding the Chinese experiments: the strong impact of the pretrained embeddings raises the possibility that high-quality representations may account for a substantial portion of the gains in that setting. This does not detract from the overall contribution, but it suggests that the improvements might be driven as much by data and initialization as by the model architecture itself. A clearer analysis of the contribution of embeddings—e.g., ablations with different embedding sources or randomly initialized embeddings—would strengthen the conclusions.

Regarding novelty, the proposed system is primarily a synthesis of two influential lines of work rather than a fundamentally new method. Henderson et al. laid the foundation for semi-synchronized joint syntactic–semantic transition-based parsing, and Dyer et al. introduced the stack LSTM architecture the previous year. As such, the originality lies less in inventing new machinery and more in demonstrating an effective integration and applying it to a challenging joint parsing task. In my view, this integration is both timely and useful: many in the community have anticipated such a parser, and this paper delivers a careful, high-quality realization that advances the state of the art in a practical way.

In summary, this is a strong, well-executed paper with clear contributions:
- A principled combination of a proven joint transition system with a powerful neural stack representation.
- Competitive and often superior results on CoNLL-2008/2009.
- Clear exposition, justified hyperparameters, and an insightful discussion.

My main suggestion for improvement is to more explicitly quantify the role of pretrained embeddings (particularly for Chinese) through targeted ablations or analyses. This would clarify how much of the observed improvement stems from representation quality versus model architecture. Otherwise, I am very positive about the work and consider it a valuable contribution to joint syntactic–semantic parsing.
