Summary
The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic dependencies and semantic role labels. It adapts the incremental, synchronized parsing framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) but departs in two key ways: (1) it learns a vector representation of the entire parser state via stack LSTMs, eliminating hand-engineered features, and (2) it uses fully greedy inference with linear-time feature extraction. The model introduces a semantic M-SELF transition to handle self-dependencies, and interleaves syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer. Experiments on CoNLL 2008 (English) and CoNLL 2009 (multilingual) show strong performance among joint models, especially given the simplicity and speed of the approach, though it trails more recent systems that use global inference, richer features, or extra supervision.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Demonstration that stack LSTMs encoding the full algorithmic state enable competitive joint syntax–semantics parsing with no handcrafted features and purely greedy decoding.
- A practical, fast joint transition system (including M-SELF and a specific interleaving policy) with ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarify the roles of syntax and joint training for SRL, including in-domain vs out-of-domain behavior.
- An efficient implementation with strong end-to-end runtime, suggesting practical utility.

Strengths
- Clarity and scope: The paper is well written and presents a substantial, coherent model that leverages recent representation learning advances to replace costly, manually designed features (e.g., predicate–argument path).
- Empirical validation against prior joint models: On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the joint model surpasses earlier joint approaches (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.), supporting the claim that whole-state representations improve transition decisions.
- Informative ablations: The syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid setups help disentangle whether syntax helps SRL and whether joint learning helps beyond pipelining. The contrast between WSJ and Brown clearly shows joint training’s in-domain gains and out-of-domain overfitting.
- Efficiency: Linear-time inference with integrated feature computation yields substantially faster end-to-end runtime than high-performing pipelines; this is valuable for real-world applications.
- Multilingual results: The model transfers reasonably across languages and is competitive with top CoNLL 2009 systems in many cases, despite its simplicity and lack of language-specific engineering.

Weaknesses
- Decoding limitations and comparability: Restricting to greedy search makes results not directly comparable to systems using beam search, global inference, or reranking, and likely leaves accuracy on the table. Reporting beam-search or dynamic-oracle results would strengthen the case without undermining the speed claims.
- Incomplete articulation of novelty vs prior transition systems: The paper could more crisply enumerate what is inherited from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) and what is new or modified (e.g., copy vs move behavior in RIGHT/SHIFT, interleaving policy, constraints, M-SELF, M-SWAP usage). This would help assess algorithmic contribution beyond the representation learner.
- Justification of predicate-sense composition: The choice to compose the word representation with a sense embedding (gd(v, p)) is not well motivated given that senses are lexeme-specific. An ablation isolating the contribution of sense-only vs word+sense composition would clarify necessity and avoid perceived redundancy.
- Evaluation organization and scope: Section 4’s structure blurs the boundary between the English-only CoNLL 2008 and the multilingual CoNLL 2009 tasks. The multilingual setup (hyperparameters, embeddings, morphological features) is not fully specified and limits interpretability, especially for morphologically rich languages.
- Analysis depth: The paper lacks detailed error analysis (e.g., long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs, argument alternations), and does not quantify the impact of the imperfect oracle conversion (for sentences that cannot be perfectly encoded). Such analysis would contextualize remaining gaps to state-of-the-art SRL.

Questions to authors
- Can you report beam-search results (e.g., small beams) and/or dynamic-oracle training for the joint parser? What is the accuracy/speed trade-off relative to greedy?
- Please provide a concise, self-contained summary of all differences from Henderson et al.’s transition systems: action inventory (including M-SELF), copy vs move behavior, interleaving policy, and constraints (e.g., on SWAP and predicate repetition).
- How do you handle cases where both A1 and C-A1 (or other core vs continuation roles) appear? Do you predict both, and how is consistency enforced in the transition sequence?
- What empirical evidence supports composing predicate word and sense embeddings (gd(v, p)) versus using sense-only representations or a learned back-off for unseen predicates? An ablation would be helpful.
- Please clarify the multilingual setup: Were hyperparameters tuned per language? Were morphological features used anywhere? For languages like Czech, how much does the absence of morphology hurt LAS and SRL?
- What is the oracle upper bound imposed by the imperfect transition encoding (e.g., the 1.5% English sentences that cannot be perfectly encoded)? Can you estimate its effect on LAS/SRL?
- Do you have error analyses on Brown vs WSJ that explain the observed overfitting in joint training? For example, are predicate–argument paths or crossing arcs disproportionately affected?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, timely contribution showing that full-state neural representations can make a simple, greedy joint parser competitive with earlier joint models, while being fast and feature-free. Clarifying the algorithmic innovations over prior transition systems and adding a lightweight non-greedy decoding experiment would significantly strengthen the paper and its comparability to recent work.
