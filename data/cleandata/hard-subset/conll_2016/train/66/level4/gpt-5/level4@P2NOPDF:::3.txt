This paper offers a timely revisit of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, leveraging recent advances in neural transition-based models. Joint modeling remains one of the most compelling strengths of modern neural parsers.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it introduces a new transition system that appears to improve upon the Henderson (2008) system on which it is based. Second, it demonstrates that a neural network approach succeeds on this joint task where linear models have previously struggled. The authors attribute this to automatic feature learning, which is plausible. I would add that neural models also gain from parameter sharing: linear models must learn a separate weight for each feature–class pair, so jointly learning two tasks greatly inflates the parameter space, whereas neural networks can share hidden representations across tasks, making joint learning more parameter-efficient.

I suspect the proposed transition system would perform well with a range of neural architectures, for example the globally normalized beam-search parser of Andor et al. (2016). There are several orthogonal enhancements one could explore, and I expect that such extensions could push this approach to state-of-the-art performance.

It would also be valuable to derive a dynamic oracle for this transition system, even if only in an appendix or follow-up work. At first glance, it seems analogous to the arc-eager oracle. Specifically: the M-S action appears to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the front of the buffer and any words currently on the semantic stack; the M-D action appears to exclude all semantic arcs between the word at the top of the stack and any words remaining in the buffer; and the L and R actions each appear to exclude only the reverse-direction arc, and nothing more.

Overall, this is a clear and well-motivated contribution that demonstrates the promise of neural joint parsing with a stronger transition system and offers a path toward further improvements.
