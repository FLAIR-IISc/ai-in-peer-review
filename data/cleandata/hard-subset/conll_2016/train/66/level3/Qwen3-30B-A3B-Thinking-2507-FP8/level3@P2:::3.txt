

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on CoNLL 2008-9 English shared tasks without using expert-crafted features, outperforming previous joint parsing models. 

The main contributions of this work, as I see them (not as claimed by authors), are:

1. A novel application of stack LSTMs to jointly learn syntactic and semantic dependencies without expert-crafted features, demonstrating that neural representations can effectively replace hand-designed features in joint parsing.
2. A practical, linear-time greedy parser with fast runtime (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), making it suitable for real-world applications.
3. Empirical validation that joint learning without full syntactic tree features can achieve competitive performance, challenging the assumption that full syntactic parses are necessary for high-performance semantic parsing.

## Strengths

1. The paper makes a compelling empirical case that neural networks can successfully replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing. Previous joint models (Henderson et al., 2008; Titov et al., 2009) relied on hand-designed features that required linguistic expertise, but this work shows that a neural approach with no such features can match or exceed their performance. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it demonstrates that representation learning can effectively capture the complex interactions between syntax and semantics without human intervention.

2. The runtime efficiency of the proposed parser is impressive (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), which is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) under identical settings. This practical advantage makes the approach highly relevant for real-world applications where speed is critical, and it demonstrates that joint parsing need not be computationally prohibitive.

3. The work provides a clean, elegant solution to the joint parsing problem by using stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state, rather than focusing on just the top items on the stack. This approach is simple yet effective, and it avoids the need for complex feature engineering. The authors also provide a clear ablation study (Semantics-only, Syntax-only, Hybrid) that helps establish the relative benefits of joint learning versus separate parsing.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks a dynamic oracle for the transition system, which could potentially improve training efficiency and performance. The authors note that the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect due to crossing arcs, but they don't explore how a dynamic oracle might address this issue. This is a notable omission, especially since previous work (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013) has addressed similar issues with dynamic oracles.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how their approach could be combined with state-of-the-art techniques that use full syntactic parses for semantic feature extraction. As noted in the paper, systems like Zhao et al. (2009) and Lei et al. (2015) achieve better performance by using pipeline approaches with full syntactic trees. It would be valuable to see if the authors' model could be enhanced by incorporating these techniques, which might lead to even better results.

3. The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why their neural approach outperforms previous joint models, especially since the transition system is largely derived from Henderson et al. (2008). The authors credit success to automatic feature learning, but they don't provide a detailed comparison of what specific features the neural model learns that the previous linear models missed. A deeper analysis of the learned representations could provide valuable insights into the strengths of the neural approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is imperfect due to crossing arcs. Could you elaborate on how you would derive a dynamic oracle for your transition system, and how it might improve performance?

2. Your paper states that your model achieves competitive performance without using expert-crafted features from full syntactic parses. How would your approach perform if combined with the best pipeline systems that use full syntactic trees for semantic feature extraction? Could this combination achieve even better results while maintaining efficiency?

3. The authors claim that neural networks are more parameter-efficient for joint tasks than linear models. Could you provide a comparison of the number of parameters or training time between your approach and previous joint parsing models that used linear classifiers with hand-crafted features?
