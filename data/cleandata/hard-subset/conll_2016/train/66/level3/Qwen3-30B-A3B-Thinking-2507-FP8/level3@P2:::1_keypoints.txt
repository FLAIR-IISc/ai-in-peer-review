- Joint transition-based parser using stack LSTMs to produce syntactic and semantic dependencies, removing need for expert-crafted features.  
- Main contributions: integrate stack LSTMs into joint parsing, show greedy parser is competitive on CoNLL 2008/2009, and achieve large speedup (177.6s vs. 439.9s).

- Practical efficiency: greedy model matches best joint parsers while being substantially faster, beneficial for real-world use.  
- Strong empirical validation: comprehensive evaluation on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual) with ablation studies showing benefits of joint learning.  
- Clear methodology and implementation: detailed transition system, stack LSTM description, justified hyperparameters, and promise of open-source code.

- Limited methodological novelty: primarily an integration of Henderson et al.’s transition system and Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM rather than a new technique.  
- Ambiguity in Chinese results: performance appears heavily dependent on high-quality pretrained embeddings, casting doubt on architecture-driven gains.  
- Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art: insufficient analysis of why the model trails top non-joint systems and the role of handcrafted features.  
- Limited out-of-domain evaluation: only Brown corpus tested, offering limited insight into generalization.

- How much performance gain is attributable specifically to stack LSTMs versus the transition system or training procedure?  
- Please provide Chinese results without pretrained embeddings to clarify model vs. embedding contributions.  
- How would performance change if expert-crafted features (e.g., SRL path features) were incorporated?

- Overall: well-written paper with solid contributions and notable speed advantages; addressing novelty and embedding-dependence concerns would strengthen the work.
