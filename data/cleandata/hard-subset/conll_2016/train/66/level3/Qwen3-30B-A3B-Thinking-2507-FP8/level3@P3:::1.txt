

# Review for CONLL_2016 Paper

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that integrates Henderson et al.'s joint parsing framework with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM architecture. The authors propose a system that learns a representation of the entire algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, and buffer) using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. Evaluated on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, the model achieves competitive results compared to existing joint parsing approaches.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. A practical and effective integration of stack LSTMs with joint syntactic-semantic parsing that demonstrates strong performance on standard benchmarks without expert-crafted features
2. A clear, well-documented implementation and evaluation of the joint parsing approach that serves as a valuable reference for future work
3. A compelling speed advantage over competing approaches (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, nearly 2.5x faster than Lei et al.'s pipeline system)

## Strengths

1. **Competitive performance with strong empirical validation**: The paper demonstrates that their joint model significantly outperforms previous joint parsing systems on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics. The results are thoroughly documented with clear comparisons to relevant prior work, showing consistent improvements over Henderson et al. (2008) and other joint systems. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of their model variants (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint), which helps establish the value of their joint learning approach.

2. **Exceptional clarity and thorough related work**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with a strong, contextualized related-work section that thoroughly examines prior joint parsing approaches. The authors clearly position their work within the field, explaining both the theoretical foundations and practical implementations of previous approaches. The system description is clear, with well-structured explanations of the transition system, stack LSTM architecture, and composition functions, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

3. **Sound methodology with practical considerations**: The authors provide thorough justification for their methodological choices, including hyperparameter selection, training procedures, and evaluation metrics. The implementation details are clear, and the paper demonstrates the practical value of their approach with a runtime analysis showing significant speed advantages over competing approaches. The authors also thoughtfully address the limitations of their model and provide a clear path for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: While the integration of Henderson et al.'s joint parsing framework with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM is valuable, the paper does not present a fundamentally new methodological contribution. The core techniques are built directly upon prior work, and the authors themselves acknowledge that "the integration was awaited and is valuable" rather than revolutionary. This limits the paper's theoretical contribution to the field, particularly when compared to more innovative neural network approaches in parsing.

2. **Potential over-reliance on embedding quality**: The Chinese results raise a significant concern about the role of pretrained embeddings in the model's performance. The authors note that "without [pretrained embeddings], [the Chinese parser] was on par with the others," suggesting that the superior Chinese performance may be primarily driven by high-quality embeddings rather than the model innovation. This undermines the claim that the model itself is responsible for the performance gains in Chinese, particularly when Chinese embeddings are known to be challenging to obtain.

3. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art approaches**: While the paper acknowledges that "many advances in those systems are orthogonal to our model," it does not sufficiently explore how their approach could be integrated with the more sophisticated features and pipelines used by top-performing systems. The authors compare to relevant prior joint systems but do not address how their model might complement the advanced features used in state-of-the-art SRL systems like Roth and Woodsend (2014) or Lei et al. (2015), which could have strengthened their contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper notes that the Chinese parser relies on pretrained word embeddings for superior performance. Could you provide more detail on the specific embedding sources used for Chinese, and whether the performance difference would persist with different embedding sources or training procedures?

2. Given that your model achieves strong results without expert-crafted features, how would you suggest integrating your approach with the more sophisticated feature engineering used in state-of-the-art SRL systems to potentially close the performance gap with those approaches?
