

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing that learns representations for the entire algorithmic state using stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for hand-crafted features. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) the use of stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state rather than just top items on stacks, and (2) a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity. However, as I evaluate these contributions, I find that the most significant contribution is the demonstration that neural representations can replace expensive hand-crafted features for semantic role labeling, while the greedy inference is a secondary practical advantage. The primary contribution is the use of stack LSTMs to capture long-range dependencies without hand-crafted features, which enables the parser to achieve competitive performance without the need for complex feature engineering.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of stack LSTMs to joint parsing**: The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating how stack LSTMs can replace hand-crafted features that have traditionally been essential for semantic role labeling. The authors effectively show that their model can capture the same information as expensive features (like predicate-argument paths) through learned representations, which is a significant insight for the field. This approach simplifies the parsing pipeline while maintaining performance.

2. **Practical performance and speed**: The model achieves an impressive end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is 2.5 times faster than comparable pipeline systems. This speed advantage makes the approach highly practical for real-world applications, especially in resource-constrained settings where computational efficiency is critical.

3. **Clear presentation and technical soundness**: The paper is well-written and builds logically on recent advances in stack LSTMs (Dyer et al., 2015). The architecture is well-motivated, the implementation details are thorough, and the evaluation on multiple datasets provides comprehensive evidence of the model's capabilities.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of beam search results**: The paper's claim of "linear time" is only valid for greedy decoding, but most high-performing parsers use beam search to improve accuracy. The authors do not report any results with beam search, making it impossible to assess how much performance they might be sacrificing for speed. This is a significant limitation since the paper claims to outperform all previous joint parsing models, but the results don't show how their greedy model compares to the same model with beam search.

2. **Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: While the paper competes well with older CoNLL 2008/2009 systems, it falls behind more recent approaches that use additional features, pipelines, or reranking (Zhao et al., 2009; Lei et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address how their approach could be combined with these more advanced techniques to close the performance gap.

3. **Lack of ablation studies on stack LSTM**: The paper claims the stack LSTM representation is key to their approach, but doesn't provide ablation studies showing how much performance comes from the stack LSTM versus the greedy inference or other components. Without this evidence, it's unclear whether the stack LSTM representation is the primary source of their gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform all previous joint parsing models, yet Table 3 shows the model falls behind Zhao et al. (2009), who also used a pipeline approach. How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction? Did you compare with the same model architecture (e.g., same feature set) or did Zhao et al. use additional features?

2. The paper states that the model uses greedy inference for linear time complexity, but many high-performing parsers use beam search. Did you run experiments with beam search, and if so, what were the results? If not, why not include these results to better understand the tradeoff between speed and accuracy?

3. In Equation 5, the authors concatenate the word vector with the predicate sense vector. Why not use a more sophisticated interaction function (e.g., element-wise multiplication or a learned transformation) instead of simple concatenation? How did you determine that concatenation was optimal?

4. The paper mentions that the model is faster than pipeline systems, but doesn't compare to other neural joint parsing models. How does your model's runtime compare to Dyer et al. (2015) and other recent neural parsers?
