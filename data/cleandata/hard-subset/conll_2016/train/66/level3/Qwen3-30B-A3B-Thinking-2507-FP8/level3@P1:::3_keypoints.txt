- Contributions: empirical demo that neural joint parsing can be competitive without hand-crafted features.  
- Contributions: technically sound transition system extending Henderson et al. (2008) using stack LSTMs to represent full parsing state.  
- Contributions: practical implementation with impressive runtime efficiency.  

- Strength: achieves SOTA among joint models on CoNLL 2008–9 English, outperforming prior joint approaches.  
- Strength: fast runtime (≈177.6±18s on CoNLL 2009 English test), ~2.5× faster than a comparable pipeline model.  
- Strength: elegant, theoretically sound architecture that is parameter-efficient by modeling full state with stack LSTMs.  

- Weakness: does not explain why it still lags top pipeline systems that use hand-crafted features.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of what aspects of the neural representation drive improvements over linear models.  
- Weakness: transition system struggles with non-projective semantic structures (oracle conversion not perfect; crossing arcs).  
- Weakness: does not explore integration with other neural advances (e.g., character embeddings, morphological features) that could help low-resource languages.  

- Q: Why does the joint neural model outperform prior joint models without hand-crafted features—what specific representation aspects help?  
- Q: How does the model handle semantic parses requiring non-projective structures beyond the current transition system?  
- Q: What improvements might come from integrating other neural architectures (e.g., Andor 2016), and why weren’t they included?  
- Q: Can you clarify theoretical computational complexity advantages of the stack LSTM approach versus other neural parsing methods?  

- Overall: valuable contribution showing neural joint parsing is a strong, efficient alternative to pipeline systems and opens directions to close remaining performance gaps.
