- Proposes a joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state with greedy decoding.  
- Authors claim two novelties: (1) vectorial representation of the complete parser state and (2) greedy inference.  
- Evaluated on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (multilingual); results competitive with older systems but behind recent state-of-the-art.  
- Primary contribution: effectively applying stack LSTMs to encode long-range syntactic features, showing neural representations can replace hand-crafted features.  
- Novelty of the transition system is unclear—paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from Henderson et al. (2008).  
- Greedy decoding is not a significant contribution (many prior systems used it); its performance limitations are not adequately addressed.  
- Strength: well-written and builds substantially on recent LSTM techniques.  
- Strength: clear demonstration that stack LSTMs can replace expert-crafted features in joint parsing.  
- Strength: practical advantage of fast inference (177.6 seconds for CoNLL-2009 English test).  
- Major weakness: use of greedy decoding without beam search limits comparability and may underestimate model capability.  
- Major weakness: Section 2 omits clarification on key transition differences and the role of A1/C-A1 links.  
- Major weakness: Section 3.3’s concatenation of predicate word and sense vectors seems redundant and is unexplained; Section 4’s multilingual results are confusing about CoNLL-2008 vs CoNLL-2009 scope.  
- Recommendation: provide beam-search results, clarify transition differences, and justify vector composition choices to strengthen claims.
