- Summary: Proposes a transition-based neural parser with stack LSTMs for joint syntactic/semantic parsing, using a novel transition system and greedy inference to achieve SOTA on CoNLL 2008/2009 while being much faster.

- Contribution: New transition system extending Henderson (2008) with M-SELF and M-SWAP to handle semantic self-dependencies and crossing arcs while preserving linear-time greedy parsing.

- Contribution: Shows neural networks can perform joint parsing without expert-crafted features, outperforming previous linear models on standard benchmarks.

- Contribution: Demonstrates superior parameter efficiency of neural models, using fewer feature/class weights yet capturing complex dependencies.

- Strength: Strong empirical validation that neural joint parsing can replace expert features and beat prior linear baselines.

- Strength: Practical efficiency—state-of-the-art accuracy with substantially faster runtime (≈2.5× faster than Lei et al. 2015).

- Strength: Well-engineered, modular transition system compatible with other neural enhancements (e.g., global beam search).

- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to more recent neural joint parsing work (2014–2015), weakening the SOTA claim.

- Weakness: No dynamic oracle provided; reliance on a static oracle adapted from arc-eager may be suboptimal for their system.

- Weakness: Limited error analysis—insufficient exploration of out-of-domain degradation and specific semantic dependency failures.

- Question: For the 1.5% of sentences whose transition sequence cannot encode the gold parse, is this a limitation of the transition system or the oracle, and how does it affect evaluation?

- Question: Which expert-crafted features (e.g., path or full syntactic features) would most improve performance, and how could they be integrated without losing greedy linear-time parsing?

- Question: How could character-based embeddings or other morphological features be integrated into the architecture to better handle inflected languages?
