- Summary: Transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that uses stack LSTMs to encode full parser state, removing hand-crafted features; main contribution is applying stack LSTMs to joint modeling, with greedy inference and runtime efficiency as secondary contributions.

- Strength — Novel state representation: Uses stack LSTMs to learn vector representations of stacks/buffer/action history, replacing expert features and outperforming prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 (ablation supports this).

- Strength — Runtime efficiency: Greedy inference is substantially faster (177.6s vs 439.9s for a competing system), making the approach practical for real-world deployment.

- Strength — Strong baseline comparison: Thorough comparisons to historical and contemporary joint models and multilingual results demonstrate generalizability and competitive performance.

- Weakness — Greedy inference limits comparability: Excluding beam search omits common SOTA decoding approaches, making the "best among joint models" claim potentially misleading without beam-search results or explicit caveats.

- Weakness — Redundant predicate vector composition: Concatenating word and sense vectors for predicates appears unnecessary (senses are lexeme-specific) and is unjustified, possibly harming efficiency.

- Weakness — Ambiguous transition differences: The paper fails to clearly distinguish its transitions from Henderson et al. (2008/2013), especially regarding M-SELF and buffer handling, leaving novelty unclear.

- Question: How would beam search (e.g., width=5) impact performance and why was it excluded?

- Question: Why concatenate word and sense vectors for predicates; does ablation show any benefit?

- Question: How does the M-SELF transition integrate with interleaved transition constraints and affect parsing stability?
