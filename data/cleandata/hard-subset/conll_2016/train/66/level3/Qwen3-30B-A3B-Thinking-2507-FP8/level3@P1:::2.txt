

Summary: The paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state, eliminating hand-crafted features. Its primary contribution is the application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics, with secondary contributions being the greedy inference approach and demonstrated runtime efficiency.

Strengths:
1. **Novel state representation**: The paper's core contribution—using stack LSTMs to learn vector representations of the full parser state (stacks, buffer, action history)—is well-executed and effectively replaces expert-crafted features. This directly addresses the key limitation of prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008) that relied on sparse linguistic features. The ablation study (Section 5) convincingly shows this approach outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009, achieving state-of-the-art results for its era without any hand-engineered features.
2. **Runtime efficiency**: The greedy inference (177.6s for CoNLL 2009 English test data) is a significant practical advantage over competing systems (e.g., Lei et al., 2015 at 439.9s). This is well-justified and clearly presented as a key benefit for real-world deployment, especially since the paper avoids expensive beam search or global reranking.
3. **Strong baseline comparison**: The thorough comparison against both historical (2008/2009 shared task winners) and contemporary joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., 2013) provides robust context for the claimed improvements. The multilingual results (Table 4) further demonstrate generalizability, with performance matching top systems across languages.

Weaknesses:
1. **Greedy inference limits comparability**: The exclusion of beam search (common in state-of-the-art parsers) is a critical methodological gap. The paper claims "best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics," but this excludes all systems using beam search (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Täckström et al., 2015). Without beam-search results, the claimed superiority is misleading—e.g., the joint model may underperform when using optimal decoding. The authors should report beam-search results or explicitly acknowledge this limitation.
2. **Redundant predicate vector composition**: Section 3.3 concatenates word vectors and predicate sense vectors (Equation 5), but this appears redundant. Since senses are lexeme-specific (e.g., "run.01"), the word vector already encodes the lemma, making the concatenation unnecessary. The paper lacks justification for this design choice, which could harm efficiency without benefit.
3. **Ambiguous transition differences**: Section 2 fails to clearly distinguish the paper's transitions from Henderson et al. (2008/2013). For example, the handling of semantic self-dependencies (M-SELF) and the buffer interaction during syntactic/semantic transitions (e.g., "copy vs. move" in S-RIGHT) need explicit comparison to prior work to establish novelty. Without this, the claimed "two key differences" are unclear.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would beam search (e.g., width=5) impact the joint model's performance? Could you provide preliminary results or discuss why it was excluded?
2. Why concatenate word vectors and sense vectors for predicates (Equation 5) when senses inherently depend on the lemma? Does ablation show any performance gain?
3. In Section 2.2, the "M-SELF" transition for self-dependencies (Figure 2) is critical for CoNLL 2009, but the paper does not clarify how it integrates with the interleaved transition constraints. How does this affect parsing stability?
