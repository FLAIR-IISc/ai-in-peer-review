

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state. The main contributions are: (1) a novel integration of Henderson et al.'s joint parsing framework with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTMs, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features; (2) a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity (including feature extraction); and (3) strong empirical results on CoNLL 2008-9 shared tasks, outperforming all previous joint parsing models.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of learning representations for the entire parser state rather than relying on hand-crafted features. The empirical results are robust, with the system achieving state-of-the-art performance among joint parsing models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, surpassing previous joint models like Henderson et al. (2008) and variants. The computational efficiency is particularly impressive (177.6±18 seconds to parse CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), making it practical for real-world applications. The paper is exceptionally well-written with a clear explanation of the technical approach, thorough justification of hyperparameters, and a strong related-work section that accurately positions the contribution within the literature.

Weaknesses: The primary weakness is the limited methodological novelty - the work primarily combines two existing techniques (Henderson's joint parsing framework and Dyer's stack LSTMs) rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. While the integration is valuable and well-executed, it represents a natural extension of prior work rather than a significant conceptual advance. The Chinese performance appears heavily dependent on high-quality pretrained embeddings, with the paper noting that without them, the Chinese parser was "on par with the others" - suggesting the strong results may be driven more by embedding quality than model innovation. The paper also fails to adequately explain why greedy inference outperforms beam search in this context, which is a common choice for such parsers. Finally, while the authors acknowledge their model falls behind state-of-the-art SRL systems that use expert-crafted features, they don't explore how their approach could be combined with these features to achieve further improvements.

Questions to Authors: Could you elaborate on why greedy inference outperforms beam search in this specific context, given that beam search is typically preferred for parsing tasks? How might the model be adapted to incorporate expert-crafted features (like path features) without losing the computational advantages of greedy inference? The Chinese results appear highly dependent on pretrained embeddings - could you provide more details about the quality and nature of these embeddings, and whether the model would perform well with standard word embeddings?
