

# Review for CONLL_2016

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state. The main contributions are: (1) a novel integration of stack LSTMs into a joint syntactic-semantic parsing framework that eliminates the need for expert-crafted features; (2) a demonstration that a greedy parsing approach with this architecture can achieve competitive performance on CoNLL 2008/2009 shared tasks; and (3) a significant speed advantage over previous systems (177.6 seconds vs. 439.9 seconds for Lei et al. 2015) while maintaining competitive accuracy.

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical efficiency without sacrificing performance**: The paper demonstrates that their greedy parser achieves competitive results (matching the best joint parsers) while being substantially faster than previous systems. The 177.6-second runtime for the entire CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core is a significant practical advantage over slower pipeline approaches, making this work valuable for real-world applications.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation comprehensively covers both CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets (English and multilingual), with thorough ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that clearly demonstrate the benefits of joint learning. The results show that the joint model outperforms all previous joint parsing systems on these shared tasks.

3. **Clear methodology and implementation**: The paper provides a detailed description of the transition system, stack LSTM architecture, and training procedures. The hyperparameter selection is well-justified, and the open-source implementation promise enhances reproducibility. The related work section is particularly strong, clearly positioning this work within the field.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited methodological novelty**: The core contribution is the integration of two existing approaches (Henderson et al.'s joint parsing transition system and Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM), rather than introducing a fundamentally new technique. While the integration is valuable, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of why this particular combination is particularly effective.

2. **Ambiguity in Chinese results**: The paper's strong Chinese performance appears heavily dependent on high-quality pretrained embeddings (the authors note "without them, it was on par with others"). This raises questions about whether the model innovation itself is driving the results or if the embeddings are the primary factor. The paper should clarify this relationship.

3. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper acknowledges that their model doesn't match the best non-joint systems (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009), but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how much of this gap is due to the joint learning approach versus the absence of expert-crafted features. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's claims about the value of their approach.

4. **Limited out-of-domain evaluation**: The out-of-domain evaluation is restricted to the Brown corpus for CoNLL 2009 English, which provides limited insight into the model's generalization capabilities. Additional out-of-domain testing would strengthen the paper's claims about the robustness of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How much of the performance gain compared to previous joint parsers can be attributed specifically to the stack LSTM representation versus other aspects of the model (e.g., the transition system or training procedure)?

2. The paper notes that Chinese results rely heavily on pretrained embeddings. Could you provide results for the Chinese model without these embeddings to clarify the contribution of the model architecture versus the embedding quality?

3. The paper states that their model doesn't match the best non-joint systems. Could you provide more specific details about how their model would compare if it incorporated expert-crafted features from those systems (e.g., "path" features used in SRL)?

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-written and makes a solid contribution to the field of joint parsing. The methodological soundness is evident in the thorough evaluation and careful ablation studies. The practical speed advantage is particularly noteworthy for real-world applications. The minor concerns about novelty and Chinese embeddings don't detract significantly from the paper's value, but addressing them would strengthen the contribution.
