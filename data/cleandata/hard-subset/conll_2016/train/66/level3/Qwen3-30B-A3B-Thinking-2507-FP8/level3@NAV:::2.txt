

This paper presents a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, avoiding hand-crafted features and using greedy decoding. The authors claim two main novelties: vectorial representation of the complete parser state and greedy inference. The system is evaluated on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (multilingual) datasets, reporting competitive results with older systems but lagging behind recent state-of-the-art approaches.

The primary contribution is the effective application of stack LSTMs to encode long-range syntactic features for semantic parsing, demonstrating that neural representations can replace expert-crafted features. However, the claim of novelty in the transition system is unclear, as the paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from Henderson et al. (2008). The greedy decoding approach is not a significant contribution, as many prior systems used this method, and its performance limitations are not adequately addressed.

Strengths include a well-written paper that builds substantially on recent LSTM techniques, a clear demonstration of how stack LSTMs can replace hand-crafted features in joint parsing, and the practical advantage of fast inference (177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data).

Major weaknesses include: (1) The use of greedy decoding without beam search results severely limits comparability with other systems and potentially underestimates the model's capabilities; (2) Section 2 lacks clarification on key transition differences from Henderson et al. and the role of A1/C-A1 links; (3) Section 3.3's concatenation of predicate word and sense vectors appears redundant and unexplained; (4) Section 4's organization of multilingual results is confusing regarding CoNLL-2008 vs. CoNLL-2009 scope.

The paper's most significant contribution is the demonstration that stack LSTMs can effectively represent the entire parser state for joint parsing, though the performance gains are modest compared to recent work. The authors should provide beam-search results, clarify transition differences, and justify their vector composition choices to strengthen their claims.
