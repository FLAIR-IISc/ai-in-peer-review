- Main claim: transition-based joint syntactic/semantic parser using stack LSTMs; core contribution is showing stack LSTMs can encode long-range syntactic features, while greedy search is a practical trade-off rather than a novelty.  
- Strength: demonstrates stack LSTMs can learn predicate-filler and other long-range features, removing need for hand-crafted features.  
- Strength: implementation is very fast (≈177.6±18s on CoNLL-2009 EN single core), making it practical and faster than some prior systems.  
- Strength: clearly connects to recent LSTM advances and extends Henderson et al.’s framework with well-justified architecture choices.  
- Weakness: reliance on greedy search limits comparability with state-of-the-art systems that use beam search; beam-search results are missing.  
- Weakness: Section 2 is unclear about A1 and C-A1 links and their role/differences from Henderson et al. (2008).  
- Weakness: Section 3.3 concatenates predicate and sense vectors without justification; this may be redundant and harms efficiency claims.  
- Weakness: Section 4 is confusing about CoNLL-2008 vs CoNLL-2009 experimental scope and should separate setups more clearly.  
- Weakness: results are competitive with older systems but not clearly positioned against more recent state-of-the-art methods using crafted features or better search.  
- Question: Why choose greedy search over beam search, and would beam search likely improve performance?  
- Question: Please clarify the role and differences of A1 and C-A1 links versus Henderson et al.’s transitions.  
- Question: Why concatenate predicate word and disambiguated sense vectors instead of using a single composed vector—what advantage does this provide?  
- Overall recommendation: promising and well-executed with notable speed and conceptual insight, but needs beam-search results and clarification of methodological ambiguities before publication.
