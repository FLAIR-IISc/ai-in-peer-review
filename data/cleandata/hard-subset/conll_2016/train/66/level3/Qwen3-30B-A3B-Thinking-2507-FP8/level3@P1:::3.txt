

This paper presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire parsing state using stack LSTMs, avoiding the need for expert-crafted features. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) an empirical demonstration that neural network-based joint parsing can achieve competitive performance without expensive hand-crafted features, (2) a technically sound transition system that extends Henderson et al. (2008) with stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state, and (3) a practical implementation with impressive runtime efficiency.

Strengths: 
First, the paper successfully demonstrates that neural networks can learn effective representations for joint parsing without expert-crafted features, achieving state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008-9 English tasks among joint models. This is significant because previous joint models struggled to match pipeline systems that used expensive features derived from full syntactic parses. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence that their model outperforms all previous joint parsing models, including those by Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), while using a completely different approach.

Second, the runtime performance is impressive: 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core, which is nearly 2.5 times faster than the pipeline model of Lei et al. (2015) (439.9±42 seconds). This practical advantage makes their approach particularly valuable for real-world applications where speed is critical.

Third, the architecture is elegant and theoretically sound. By using stack LSTMs to represent the entire parsing state rather than just top items on the stack, the model avoids the need for complex feature engineering while still capturing necessary contextual information. The authors demonstrate that this approach is more parameter-efficient for joint tasks than linear models, as it requires fewer feature/class weights to achieve comparable performance.

Weaknesses:
First, the paper claims to outperform previous joint models but doesn't adequately address why their approach is better than the top pipeline systems (e.g., Zhao et al. 2009) that used carefully designed features. The authors acknowledge they don't match these systems, but don't provide a clear explanation for this gap, which is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to advance the state of the art in joint parsing.

Second, the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why the neural approach works better than previous linear models. The authors attribute success to "automatic feature learning," but don't explore what specific aspects of the neural representation benefit joint parsing, leaving this as an open question for future work.

Third, the transition system appears to have limitations for handling non-projective semantic structures. The authors note that "the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect despite using the MSWAP action due to the presence of multiple crossing arcs." This suggests the model may struggle with certain semantic structures that are common in real data.

Fourth, the paper doesn't explore how their approach could be integrated with other recent advances in neural NLP, such as character-based embeddings or morphological features, which could potentially improve performance, especially for low-resource languages.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on why the joint parsing model outperforms previous joint models despite not using hand-crafted features? Is there a specific aspect of the neural representation that particularly benefits joint parsing?
2. How does the model handle cases where the semantic parse requires non-projective structures that cannot be handled by the current transition system?
3. The authors mention their transition system is compatible with other neural architectures (e.g., Andor 2016), but don't explore this. Could you discuss potential improvements from incorporating these approaches, and why they weren't included in this work?
4. The paper claims the model is efficient, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity with previous approaches. Could you clarify the theoretical computational advantages of the stack LSTM approach versus other neural parsing methods?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to joint parsing by demonstrating that neural networks can learn effective representations for joint syntactic-semantic analysis without expensive hand-crafted features. While the model doesn't match the absolute state of the art, it provides a strong alternative to pipeline approaches with significant runtime advantages, making it particularly relevant for practical applications. The work opens promising directions for future research in neural joint parsing that could further close the performance gap with pipeline systems.
