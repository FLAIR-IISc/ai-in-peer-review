

# Review of "Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Neural Networks" (CONLL 2016)

## Summary
This paper introduces a transition-based neural parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The authors propose a novel transition system that extends Henderson et al. (2008) and employs stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, eliminating the need for expert-crafted features. The model uses greedy inference (not beam search) and achieves state-of-the-art results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint parsing models while being significantly faster (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core).

## Main Contributions
1. A new transition system for joint parsing that improves upon Henderson et al. (2008) by handling semantic self-dependencies (M-SELF) and accommodating crossing semantic arcs (M-SWAP), while maintaining a linear-time greedy algorithm.
2. Empirical demonstration that neural networks can successfully perform joint parsing without expert-crafted features, overcoming limitations of previous linear models that struggled with joint tasks.
3. Demonstration that neural networks achieve superior parameter efficiency for joint parsing tasks, requiring fewer feature/class weights than linear models while still capturing complex dependencies.

## Strengths
1. **Significant empirical validation of neural networks for joint parsing**: The paper provides compelling evidence that neural networks can effectively replace expert-crafted features for joint parsing, achieving better results than previous linear models (including Henderson et al. 2008 variants) on standard benchmarks. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as joint parsing has historically been challenging for neural approaches.

2. **Practical performance and efficiency**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results while being significantly faster than competing approaches (nearly 2.5x faster than Lei et al. 2015). This practical advantage makes the model suitable for real-world applications, which is often overlooked in theoretical NLP work.

3. **Well-designed transition system with broad compatibility**: The transition system is carefully engineered to handle the complexities of joint parsing while maintaining linear time complexity. The architecture is sufficiently modular to be compatible with other neural approaches (e.g., Andor 2016's global beam search), suggesting potential for further improvements and state-of-the-art results through orthogonal enhancements.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited comparison with more recent neural approaches**: The paper compares primarily with older linear models and pipeline approaches, but doesn't adequately address how the model compares to more recent neural joint parsing work (e.g., from 2014-2015) that might have achieved better results. This weakens the claim that their model represents the state-of-the-art.

2. **Lack of dynamic oracle**: The paper acknowledges the need for a dynamic oracle (in the appendix suggestion) but doesn't provide one. The current oracle appears to be a static adaptation of the arc-eager oracle, which may not be optimal for their transition system, potentially affecting the fairness of the comparison with other systems.

3. **Insufficient exploration of linguistic limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why their model underperforms on out-of-domain data (Brown corpus) compared to the hybrid model, or why it struggles with certain types of semantic dependencies. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper mentions that "the transition sequence incorrectly encodes the gold-standard joint parse" for 1.5% of sentences in CoNLL 2009. Could you clarify how this affects the evaluation and whether it's a limitation of your transition system or the oracle?

2. You state that your model "does not match that of the top expert-crafted feature-based systems." Could you elaborate on which specific features (e.g., path features, full syntactic parse features) would most significantly improve your model's performance, and how you would integrate them without compromising the greedy, linear-time approach?

3. The paper suggests that morphological features could improve performance in inflected languages. Given your architecture's compatibility with other neural approaches, could you discuss how your model might integrate character-based embeddings (as in Ballesteros et al. 2015) to handle morphological variation?
