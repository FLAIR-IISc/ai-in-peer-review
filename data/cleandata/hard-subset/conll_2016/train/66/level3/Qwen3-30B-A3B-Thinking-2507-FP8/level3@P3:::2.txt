

# Review of "Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing with Stack LSTMs"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, inspired by Henderson et al. (2008) but incorporating recent LSTM techniques. The authors claim two main novelties: using vectorial representations for the entire parser state (rather than just top stack elements) and employing a plain greedy search instead of beam search. However, upon critical analysis, the primary contribution is the demonstration that stack LSTMs can effectively encode long-range syntactic features (such as predicate-filler paths) that would otherwise require expensive hand-crafted features. The greedy search is a practical trade-off rather than a novel contribution.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Insight on Feature Learning**: The paper successfully demonstrates that stack LSTMs can learn to encode long-range syntactic features necessary for semantic role labeling, eliminating the need for expensive hand-crafted features like predicate-filler paths. This is a significant conceptual contribution to joint parsing, showing how neural networks can automatically discover useful representations during parsing.

2. **Practical Implementation and Speed**: The parser achieves remarkable speed (177.6±18 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core), making it highly practical for real-world applications. This speed advantage over state-of-the-art systems (e.g., 2.5x faster than Lei et al., 2015) is a substantial practical contribution.

3. **Clear Connection to Recent Advances**: The paper is well-written and clearly builds upon recent LSTM techniques while extending Henderson et al.'s framework. The architecture and implementation choices are well-justified and demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Greedy Search Limitation**: The paper's use of a simple greedy search severely limits comparability with the state of the art, as most recent systems use beam search. The authors should have reported beam-search results to properly evaluate their model's potential. This is a major limitation in an NLP paper where search strategy significantly impacts performance.

2. **Unclear Section 2**: Section 2 lacks clarity on the presence and role of A1 and C-A1 links (critical for understanding the parsing task). The authors should explicitly explain these in the transition description and clarify how they differ from Henderson et al.'s framework. The current presentation makes it difficult to understand the parsing algorithm's novelty.

3. **Redundant Vector Composition**: Section 3.3's concatenation of predicate word and disambiguated sense vectors appears redundant. The authors should explain why this is necessary rather than using a single composed vector. This lack of justification undermines the model's efficiency claims.

4. **Confusing Multilingual Section**: Section 4's organization is confusing regarding CoNLL-2008 vs. CoNLL-2009 scope, particularly in subsections 4.1/4.2. The paper should clearly separate the two datasets' experimental setups to avoid reader confusion.

5. **Limited Results Context**: While the results are competitive with older 2008/2009 systems, the paper fails to adequately acknowledge that they lag behind more recent proposals. The authors should more clearly position their work relative to state-of-the-art systems that use expert-crafted features or better search algorithms.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you choose to use a greedy search instead of beam search, given that it limits comparability with the state of the art? Would you expect a beam search version to significantly improve performance?

2. Can you clarify the role of A1 and C-A1 links in the parsing process as shown in Figure 1? How do they differ from the transitions described in Henderson et al. (2008)?

3. In Section 3.3, why concatenate the predicate word vector and disambiguated sense vector rather than using a single composed vector? What specific advantage does this composition provide?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of how neural networks can encode long-range syntactic features for semantic role labeling. The practical speed advantage is notable, and the paper is well-written. However, the lack of beam-search results and several unclear sections significantly limit its impact. The authors should address these weaknesses before publication, particularly by reporting beam-search results and clarifying the ambiguities in the methodology. With these improvements, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
