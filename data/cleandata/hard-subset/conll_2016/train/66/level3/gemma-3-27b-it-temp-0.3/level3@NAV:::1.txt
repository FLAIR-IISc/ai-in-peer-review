## Review of "A Stack LSTM Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing"

This paper presents a transition-based parser that effectively combines Henderson et al.’s joint syntactic/semantic transition system with Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM architecture. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving competitive results and, in some cases, outperforming previous joint models. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and providing a comprehensive overview of related work. The experimental setup is sound, with well-justified hyperparameters and a thorough evaluation.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful *integration* of these two previously established frameworks. While neither the transition system nor the stack LSTM is novel in isolation, their combination within a joint parsing model, and the demonstration of its effectiveness, represents a valuable advancement. A second contribution is the demonstration that competitive joint parsing performance can be achieved *without* relying on hand-crafted features, leveraging the representational power of LSTMs instead. Finally, the speed of the greedy inference algorithm (177.6 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English) is a practical benefit, making the system potentially useful in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of existing work in joint parsing and related areas, contextualizing their contribution effectively.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of established transition systems and stack LSTMs, combined with a well-defined experimental setup, lends credibility to the results.
*   **Practical Efficiency:** The linear time complexity and fast runtime of the greedy inference algorithm are significant advantages.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core components of the system (transition system and stack LSTM) are not novel. The primary contribution is the integration of these existing techniques.
*   **Potential Embedding Bias:** The authors acknowledge a concern that the strong performance, particularly on Chinese, may be heavily influenced by the quality of the pre-trained embeddings. This raises the question of how much of the improvement is due to the model itself versus the embeddings.
*   **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare to previous joint models, the strongest performing systems often rely on pipelines with carefully engineered features and/or reranking. A more direct comparison to these systems, even if not fully replicated, would strengthen the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide an ablation study to quantify the contribution of the pre-trained embeddings, perhaps by training with randomly initialized embeddings or embeddings trained on a smaller, less comprehensive corpus?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the LSTM hidden state dimension and the dropout rate?
3.  The paper mentions that the oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions is not perfect. Could you quantify the impact of this imperfect conversion on the reported results?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of error analysis. Identifying common error patterns could provide insights into the limitations of the model and suggest directions for future research.
