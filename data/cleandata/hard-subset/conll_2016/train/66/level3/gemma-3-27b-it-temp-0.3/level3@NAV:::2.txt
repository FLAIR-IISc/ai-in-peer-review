## Review of "A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser with Stack LSTMs and Greedy Decoding"

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to encode long-range syntactic features within the parser state, enabling improved semantic role labeling without relying on hand-crafted features. The authors claim novelty in using vectorial representations for the entire parser state and employing a plain greedy search for decoding.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs for encoding parser state in a joint syntactic-semantic framework *without* extensive feature engineering. The results show competitive performance on the CoNLL-2008/2009 datasets, indicating that the learned representations can effectively capture relevant information for both tasks. The speed of inference due to the greedy decoding is also a notable practical advantage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Clear Improvement:** The paper establishes a strong baseline by building upon the Henderson et al. (2008) framework and demonstrably improves upon it. This provides a clear indication of the benefits of the proposed approach.
2. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state is a well-motivated and effective technique. The authors convincingly argue that this allows the model to capture long-range dependencies crucial for SRL.
3. **Fast Inference:** The use of a greedy decoding algorithm results in a significantly faster parsing speed compared to more complex, feature-rich pipelines, making it potentially useful for real-time applications.
4. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparability due to Greedy Decoding:** The most significant weakness is the exclusive reliance on a greedy decoding algorithm. While fast, it limits direct comparability to state-of-the-art systems that employ beam search or other more sophisticated decoding strategies. Reporting beam search results is crucial to assess the true potential of the model.
2. **Lack of Clarity Regarding Transitions:** Section 2 needs to provide a more detailed comparison of the transitions used in this work versus those in Henderson et al. (2008). Simply stating differences isn't enough; a clear explanation of *why* these changes were made and their impact on the model is needed.
3. **Unclear Motivation for Concatenation:** The concatenation of the predicate word and sense vectors in Section 3.3 seems redundant and lacks a clear justification. The authors should elaborate on the reasoning behind this design choice.
4. **Organization of Results:** The organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing. The presentation of results for CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 should be more clearly delineated, and the multilingual aspect should be addressed more explicitly.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide beam search results for your model? How does performance change with different beam widths?
2.  Please provide a more detailed explanation of the differences between the transitions used in your model and those in Henderson et al. (2008), and justify the changes you made.
3.  What is the rationale behind concatenating the predicate word and sense vectors in Section 3.3? Does this improve performance, and if so, why?
4.  Could you clarify the organization of Section 4, specifically the scope of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 with respect to the CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 datasets?
