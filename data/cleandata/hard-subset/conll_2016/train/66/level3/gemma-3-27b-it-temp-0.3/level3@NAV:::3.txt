## Review of “A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing”

This paper revisits the problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based framework and neural networks. The authors present a system that leverages stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser’s state, achieving competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, and demonstrating the potential of neural networks to overcome limitations of previous linear models in this joint task. The primary contribution lies in the successful application of stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics without relying on hand-crafted features, a significant departure from prior state-of-the-art systems. A secondary contribution is the proposed transition system, which builds upon Henderson (2008) and incorporates a new transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over previous joint parsing models, including the foundational work of Henderson et al. (2008), on standard benchmark datasets. Achieving competitive performance with a simpler, feature-lean model is a significant accomplishment.
2. **Effective Use of Neural Networks:** The successful application of stack LSTMs for joint parsing highlights the power of neural networks to automatically learn relevant features, reducing the need for extensive feature engineering. This is particularly valuable in a joint task where feature interactions can be complex.
3. **Efficient Inference:** The greedy inference algorithm maintains linear time complexity, making the system practical for real-world applications, as demonstrated by the reported runtime on the CoNLL 2009 test data. This is a notable advantage over more complex, optimal decoding approaches.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the transition system, the neural network architecture, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of examples and a detailed description of the transitions aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Transition System:** While the addition of the M-SELF transition is a useful extension, the core transition system is largely based on existing work (Henderson et al., 2008). The novelty primarily resides in the neural network architecture applied to this system.
2. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to state-of-the-art systems is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge the superior performance of systems using expert-crafted features and global decoding, a more direct comparison with these systems, even if computationally more expensive, would strengthen the evaluation.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study examining the contribution of different components of the neural network architecture (e.g., the different stack LSTMs, the composition functions) would provide further insight into the model’s behavior and identify areas for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the oracle conversion of semantic parses to transitions is not perfect. Could you elaborate on the frequency and impact of these imperfect conversions on the reported results?
2.  The M-SELF transition seems specifically designed for handling self-dependencies. Could you discuss whether this transition introduces any challenges or limitations in terms of generalization to other types of semantic dependencies?
3.  Given the success of the stack LSTM approach, have you explored integrating other recent neural architectures, such as transformers, into the joint parsing framework?
