- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic and semantic parser using stack LSTMs that combines Henderson et al.'s transition system with Dyer et al.'s neural architecture.  
- Avoids expert-crafted features by learning representations of the entire parser state.  
- Demonstrates competitive results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks with linear-time greedy parsing.  
- Introduces a new semantic transition (M-SELF) for self-dependencies and a mechanism for predicate sense disambiguation.  
- Provides an open-source implementation to support reproducibility and practical use.  
- Contribution: effective integration of prior transition system and stack LSTM architecture enabling efficient joint parsing without feature engineering.  
- Contribution: empirical evaluation showing state-of-the-art performance among joint parsers and robustness across multiple languages.  
- Contribution: computational efficiency and runtime advantages over many pipeline approaches.  
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with thorough explanations and hyperparameter discussion.  
- Strength: strong empirical rigor and multilingual evaluation.  
- Strength: linear-time efficiency and suitability for resource-constrained settings.  
- Strength: code release increases reproducibility and impact.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—mainly a combination of existing methods rather than fundamentally new ideas.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on high-quality pretrained embeddings, limiting low-resource applicability.  
- Weakness: signs of overfitting in the joint model, reducing out-of-domain generalizability.  
- Question: how does performance degrade without high-quality pretrained embeddings or in low-resource languages?  
- Question: can overfitting be mitigated via additional regularization or domain adaptation?  
- Question: how does the model compare to recent SRL approaches using contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT)?  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address generalizability and reliance on pretrained embeddings.
