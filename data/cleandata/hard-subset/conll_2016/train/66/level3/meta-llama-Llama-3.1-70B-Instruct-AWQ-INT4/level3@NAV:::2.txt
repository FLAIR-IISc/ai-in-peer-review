**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a transition-based approach with stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithm state and uses a greedy inference algorithm, which allows for fast and efficient parsing. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, and the results show that it outperforms previous joint models and is competitive with state-of-the-art systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint parsing that uses a transition-based approach with stack LSTMs to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state.
2. **Greedy inference algorithm**: The paper introduces a greedy inference algorithm that allows for fast and efficient parsing, which is a significant improvement over previous joint models.
3. **No expert-crafted features required**: The paper shows that the system can achieve competitive results without requiring expert-crafted features, which is a significant advantage over previous systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Fast and efficient parsing**: The paper demonstrates that the system can parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set in 177.6±18 seconds on a single core, which is significantly faster than previous systems.
2. **Competitive results**: The paper shows that the system achieves competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, outperforming previous joint models and state-of-the-art systems.
3. **No expert-crafted features required**: The paper demonstrates that the system can achieve competitive results without requiring expert-crafted features, which is a significant advantage over previous systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the system on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, which may not be representative of all possible parsing tasks.
2. **No comparison to other joint models**: The paper does not compare the system to other joint models that use different approaches, such as graph-based or neural network-based models.
3. **No analysis of error types**: The paper does not provide an analysis of the types of errors made by the system, which could provide insight into its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and how the optimal hyperparameters were selected?
2. How does the system handle out-of-vocabulary words and unseen predicates?
3. Can you provide more details on the composition functions used to obtain vector representations of parse fragments?
4. How does the system handle non-projective syntactic trees and multiple crossing arcs in the semantic parses?
5. Can you provide more details on the open-source implementation of the parser and how it can be used in practical settings?
