**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithm state and uses greedy inference with linear time complexity. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieve competitive results compared to existing joint systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack LSTMs.
2. **Greedy Inference with Linear Time Complexity**: The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, making it efficient for practical applications.
3. **Competitive Results on CoNLL Shared Tasks**: The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieve competitive results compared to existing joint systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach to Joint Parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack LSTMs.
2. **Efficient Inference Algorithm**: The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, making it efficient for practical applications.
3. **Competitive Results**: The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieve competitive results compared to existing joint systems.
4. **Well-Written Paper**: The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and has a strong related-work section.
5. **Sound Methodology**: The methodology is sound and well-justified, with clear system description and justified hyperparameters.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Methodological Originality**: The paper builds on prior work by Henderson et al. and Dyer et al., and the integration of their approaches, while valuable, is not entirely novel.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The authors rely on pretrained embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.
3. **Limited Comparison to Other Approaches**: The authors do not compare their approach to other joint parsing models, such as those using graph-based or pipeline-based approaches.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to incorporate morphological features, which are used by other state-of-the-art systems?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training procedure, including the number of epochs and the convergence criteria?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is mentioned in the paper as a potential problem for the joint model?
