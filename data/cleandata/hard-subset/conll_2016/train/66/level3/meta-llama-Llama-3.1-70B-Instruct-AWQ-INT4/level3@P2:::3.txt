**Summary**

The paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a transition-based approach with a stack LSTM to learn representations of the entire algorithmic state. The parser achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models that use expert-crafted features. The authors attribute their success to the ability of neural networks to automatically learn features, and the reviewer notes that neural networks are also more parameter-efficient for joint tasks than linear models.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Improved Transition System**: The paper proposes a new transition system that improves upon the one introduced by Henderson et al. (2008). The new system uses a stack LSTM to learn representations of the entire algorithmic state, which allows it to capture long-range dependencies and contextual information.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Networks**: The paper provides an empirical demonstration that neural networks can succeed in joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, where linear models have struggled in the past.
3. **Automatic Feature Learning**: The authors attribute their success to the ability of neural networks to automatically learn features, which eliminates the need for expert-crafted features.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models that use expert-crafted features.
2. **Improved Efficiency**: The use of neural networks allows for more parameter-efficient modeling of joint tasks, which can lead to faster training and inference times.
3. **Flexibility**: The transition system is likely compatible with other neural architectures, such as global beam-search, which could lead to further improvements in performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison**: The paper only compares its results to previous joint models that use expert-crafted features, and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art models that use different approaches.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of the transition system or the neural network architecture, which could provide insights into why the model is successful.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The paper relies on pretrained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages or domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **Dynamic Oracle**: Can the authors derive a dynamic oracle for the transition system, which could provide a more efficient and effective way to train the model?
2. **Compatibility with Other Architectures**: Can the authors provide more details on how the transition system could be integrated with other neural architectures, such as global beam-search?
3. **Theoretical Analysis**: Can the authors provide a theoretical analysis of the transition system and the neural network architecture, which could provide insights into why the model is successful?
