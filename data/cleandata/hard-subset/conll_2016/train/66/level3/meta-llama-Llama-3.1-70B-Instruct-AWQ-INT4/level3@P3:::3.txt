This paper revisits joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing using neural networks, and presents a promising joint transition-based model. The authors' contributions are two-fold: they introduce a new transition system, which improves upon the one proposed by Henderson (2008), and provide empirical evidence that neural networks can succeed where linear models struggled.

The authors' transition system is designed to jointly produce syntactic and semantic dependencies, and is based on the incremental structure building approach of Henderson et al. (2008). However, their system differs from previous work in two key ways: it learns representations for the parser's entire algorithmic state, rather than just the top items on the stack or the most recent parser states, and it uses entirely greedy inference rather than beam search.

The authors' use of a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithmic state is a key innovation of their approach. The stack LSTM allows the parser to capture information that would be distant from the "top" of the stack, and enables the learner to make use of this information when making parsing decisions.

The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, and demonstrate that it outperforms all previous joint parsing models, including those of Henderson et al. (2008) and variants. They also show that their model is comparable to the top systems at CoNLL 2009 in terms of multilingual results.

The authors attribute the success of their model to its ability to automatically learn features, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. They also note that neural networks are more parameter-efficient for joint tasks than linear models, requiring fewer feature/class weights.

The paper's strengths include its introduction of a new transition system and its empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of neural networks for joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing. The authors' use of a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithmic state is also a key innovation of their approach.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their model. While they report that their model outperforms previous joint parsing models, they do not provide a detailed breakdown of the types of errors that their model makes, or how these errors compare to those made by other models.

Another potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their model to other neural network-based approaches to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing. While they note that their model is comparable to the top systems at CoNLL 2009 in terms of multilingual results, they do not provide a detailed analysis of how their model compares to other neural network-based approaches in terms of its performance on specific tasks or datasets.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing using neural networks. The authors' introduction of a new transition system and their empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of neural networks for this task are key contributions of the paper.

2. Empirical evidence that neural networks can succeed where linear models struggled for joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing.
3. A demonstration of the effectiveness of using a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithmic state for joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing.
