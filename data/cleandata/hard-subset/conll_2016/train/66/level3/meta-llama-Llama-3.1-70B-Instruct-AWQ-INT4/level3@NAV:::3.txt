**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a transition-based approach with neural networks. The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithmic state using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs) and achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. **New Transition System**: The authors propose a new transition system that improves upon the one introduced by Henderson et al. (2008). This system allows for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.
2. **Neural Network-based Joint Parsing**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using neural networks for joint parsing, which outperforms previous linear models.
3. **Efficient Parsing**: The parser achieves linear time complexity and is very fast, with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English tasks, outperforming previous joint models.
2. **Efficient and Fast**: The parser is very fast and achieves linear time complexity, making it suitable for practical applications.
3. **No Expert-Crafted Features**: The parser does not require expert-crafted features, which are often expensive to extract and may not be available for all languages.
4. **Multilingual Support**: The parser adapts easily to non-English languages and achieves competitive results on the CoNLL 2009 multilingual task.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overfitting**: The parser may overfit to the training data, as seen in the results on the CoNLL 2009 English task.
2. **Limited to Greedy Inference**: The parser uses greedy inference, which may not be optimal for all cases.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Word Embeddings**: The parser's performance on non-English languages may depend on the availability of high-quality pretrained word embeddings.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in future work?
2. Can you provide more details on how the parser handles out-of-vocabulary words and unseen predicates at test time?
3. Have you considered using other neural network architectures, such as graph-based models, for joint parsing?
