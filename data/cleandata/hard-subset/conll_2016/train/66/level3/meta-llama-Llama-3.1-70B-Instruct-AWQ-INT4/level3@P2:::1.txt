**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithm state and uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008-2009 English shared tasks and report the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The authors propose a novel approach to joint parsing by combining Henderson et al.'s joint syntactic/semantic transition system with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM. This integration enables the model to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state and achieve competitive results on the CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks.
2. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of stack LSTMs in capturing long-range dependencies and learning a representation of the entire algorithm state. This allows the model to bypass expensive features used in traditional syntactic and semantic parsing.
3. **Greedy Inference Algorithm**: The authors propose a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. This makes the model efficient and scalable for practical applications.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive Results**: The authors report competitive results on the CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks, outperforming existing joint systems.
2. **Well-Justified Methodology**: The authors provide a clear system description, justified hyperparameters, and a strong related-work section.
3. **Efficient Inference Algorithm**: The greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity makes the model efficient and scalable.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Methodological Originality**: The authors build upon prior work by Henderson and Dyer, and the integration, while valuable, does not introduce significant methodological innovations.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The authors' use of pretrained embeddings, particularly for Chinese, may drive performance more than the model innovations themselves.
3. **Limited Generalizability**: The authors only evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008-2009 shared tasks, and it is unclear how well the model generalizes to other languages or tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to incorporate morphological features, which are used by top-performing systems in highly inflective languages like Czech?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the effectiveness of their greedy inference algorithm and how it compares to beam search or other inference methods?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, particularly in the Joint model, which performs well in-domain but not out-of-domain?
