**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a Stack LSTM parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies. The parser learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories. The greedy inference algorithm has linear time, including feature extraction. The parser is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, and it obtains the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper presents a novel joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithm state using stack long short-term memories.
2. The parser achieves the best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using stack LSTMs for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach**: The paper presents a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using stack LSTMs. This approach is different from previous approaches that use expert-crafted features or pipeline syntax and semantics.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The parser achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models.
3. **Efficient Inference**: The greedy inference algorithm has linear time, including feature extraction, making it efficient for practical applications.
4. **Well-Written Paper**: The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and has a strong related-work section.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Methodological Originality**: The paper builds on prior work by Henderson et al. and Dyer et al., and the integration of their ideas is not particularly novel.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The parser's performance on the Chinese dataset relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings, which may not be available for all languages.
3. **Limited Comparison**: The paper only compares the parser's performance to a limited set of previous joint models and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the parser to incorporate morphological features, which are used by other state-of-the-art systems?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and how they arrived at the chosen hyperparameters?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is evident in the results on the out-of-domain Brown test set?
