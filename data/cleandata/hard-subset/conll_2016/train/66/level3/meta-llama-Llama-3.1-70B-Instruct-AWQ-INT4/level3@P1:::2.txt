**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire parser state using stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser uses a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. The authors evaluate their system on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and report competitive results with older systems, despite not using expert-crafted features of the full syntactic parse.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing**: The paper proposes a novel approach to joint parsing that learns a representation of the entire parser state using stack LSTMs. This approach enables the parser to capture long-range syntactic features useful for semantic role labeling.
2. **Greedy inference algorithm**: The paper presents a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity, including feature extraction. This approach is faster and more efficient than beam search algorithms used in previous works.
3. **Competitive results on CoNLL datasets**: The authors evaluate their system on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and report competitive results with older systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficient inference algorithm**: The greedy inference algorithm used in the paper is faster and more efficient than beam search algorithms used in previous works.
2. **No hand-crafted features required**: The paper's approach does not require expert-crafted features of the full syntactic parse, making it more flexible and adaptable to different languages and domains.
3. **Competitive results**: The authors report competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparability**: The use of a simple greedy algorithm limits the comparability of the paper's results to other works that use beam search algorithms.
2. **Lack of beam search results**: The paper does not report beam search results, which would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the system's performance.
3. **Unclear organization**: The organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality, and the paper could benefit from a clearer summary of the differences between the transitions used here and those in Henderson et al.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide beam search results to facilitate a more comprehensive evaluation of the system's performance?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of limited comparability with other works that use beam search algorithms?
3. Can you clarify the organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 with respect to multilinguality?
4. Can you summarize the differences between the transitions used here and those in Henderson et al. to clarify the novelty of your approach?
5. Why do you concatenate predicate word and disambiguated sense vectors in Section 3.3? Does this approach provide any benefits over using a single vector representation?
