**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a joint syntactic and semantic dependency parser that uses a transition-based approach with a stack long short-term memory (LSTM) to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state. The parser is trained on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks and achieves state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics. The paper also presents a new transition system that improves over the previous system proposed by Henderson et al. (2008).

**Contributions**

1. **New Transition System**: The paper presents a new transition system that improves over the previous system proposed by Henderson et al. (2008). The new system uses a stack LSTM to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state, which allows for more accurate parsing.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Networks**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of neural networks in joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, where linear models have struggled.
3. **Automatic Feature Learning**: The paper credits the success of the model to automatic feature learning, which allows the model to learn relevant features from the data without the need for expert-crafted features.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The paper presents a model that achieves state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.
2. **Efficient Parsing**: The paper presents a model that is very fast, with an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test data on a single core.
3. **Flexibility**: The paper suggests that the transition system is likely compatible with other neural architectures, such as global beam-search, and that many orthogonal improvements could yield state-of-the-art results.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Comparison**: The paper only compares the model to other joint models and does not compare it to pipeline models that use expert-crafted features.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Word Embeddings**: The paper notes that the model's performance on the Chinese dataset relies heavily on pretrained word embeddings.
3. **Limited Handling of Morphological Features**: The paper notes that the model does not handle morphological features, which could potentially improve performance, especially in highly inflective languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the model to incorporate morphological features, and what benefits do you expect from this extension?
2. Can you provide more details on how the transition system can be used with other neural architectures, such as global beam-search?
3. How do you plan to derive a dynamic oracle for the transition system, and what benefits do you expect from this?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of joint semantic and syntactic dependency parsing, and the results are impressive. However, there are some limitations to the paper, such as the limited comparison to pipeline models and the dependence on pretrained word embeddings. The paper also suggests several avenues for future work, such as extending the model to incorporate morphological features and deriving a dynamic oracle for the transition system.
