**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based parser that leverages stack long short-term memories (LSTMs). The parser is designed to learn a representation of the entire algorithm state, rather than relying on expert-crafted features. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Integration of Stack LSTMs with Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing**: The authors combine the transition-based parsing approach of Henderson et al. with the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. to create a novel parser that jointly learns syntax and semantics.
2. **Competitive Results on CoNLL 2008 and 2009**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.
3. **Efficient and Scalable Parsing**: The authors' parser has a linear time complexity and achieves an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-Designed Architecture**: The authors' integration of stack LSTMs with joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing is well-designed and effective.
2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.
3. **Efficient and Scalable Parsing**: The authors' parser has a linear time complexity and achieves an end-to-end runtime of 177.6±18 seconds to parse the CoNLL 2009 English test set on a single core.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Methodological Originality**: The authors' approach builds on prior work by Henderson et al. and Dyer et al., and while the integration is valuable, it may not be considered methodologically original.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings**: The authors' results on the Chinese dataset may be driven more by the quality of the pretrained embeddings than by the model innovations themselves.
3. **Limited Comparison to Other Approaches**: The authors' comparison to other approaches is limited, and it may be beneficial to include more comparisons to other state-of-the-art models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the potential issue of overfitting to the WSJ training domain, as evidenced by the Hybrid model's performance on the Brown test set?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the preprocessing steps used to convert the semantic parses into transitions, and how they handle cases where the oracle conversion is not perfect?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their model to incorporate morphological features, which may be beneficial for highly inflective languages like Czech?
