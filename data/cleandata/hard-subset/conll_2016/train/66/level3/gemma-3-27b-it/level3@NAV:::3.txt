## Review of “A Transition-Based Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing”

This paper revisits the problem of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based approach with neural networks, specifically Stack LSTMs. The authors present a system that achieves competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, surpassing previous joint models and approaching the performance of pipeline-based systems. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are threefold. First, the successful application of Stack LSTMs to *jointly* model syntactic and semantic dependencies without hand-engineered features is a notable achievement, demonstrating the power of representation learning in this complex task. Second, the proposed modification to the transition system (handling self-dependencies with M-SELF) is a pragmatic addition that improves performance. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, the paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that neural networks can succeed in joint parsing where earlier linear models struggled, suggesting a promising direction for future research.

The strengths of this submission lie in its clear presentation, solid experimental results, and efficient implementation. The authors demonstrate a significant improvement over the original Henderson et al. (2008) work, successfully leveraging neural networks for automatic feature learning. The speed of the proposed system (177.6s for CoNLL 2009) is also a compelling advantage. The detailed ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provide valuable insights into the interplay between syntactic and semantic information and the benefits of joint modeling. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems, even those utilizing more complex architectures and features, is fair and informative.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The transition system, while effective, feels incremental rather than fundamentally novel. The authors acknowledge this, positioning their work as building on Henderson et al. (2008), but a more in-depth discussion of why their transition choices are advantageous would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t delve deeply into error analysis. Understanding *where* the system fails could inform future improvements. While the authors mention the limitations of greedy decoding, they do not explore potential mitigation strategies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions a potential for overfitting in the Joint model on the out-of-domain data. Could you provide further analysis into the specific types of errors observed in the Joint model compared to the Hybrid model on the Brown corpus?
2. The M-SELF transition was added to address self-dependencies. Were there other transition modifications considered, and if so, why was M-SELF chosen over alternatives?
3. Could you discuss the computational cost of training the Stack LSTMs, particularly concerning memory usage for larger datasets or more complex models?
