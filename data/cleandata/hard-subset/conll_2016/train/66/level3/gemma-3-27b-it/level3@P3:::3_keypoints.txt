- Paper applies a transition-based neural model with stack LSTMs for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing, achieving strong CoNLL 2008/2009 results without hand-crafted features and using greedy decoding.
- Contribution: demonstrates stack LSTMs effectively learn parser-state representations for joint parsing, removing need for manual feature engineering.
- Contribution: adds an M-SELF transition to handle self-dependencies in the CoNLL datasets.
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that neural joint models outperform earlier linear joint approaches.
- Strength: achieves state-of-the-art or competitive empirical results on standard joint parsing datasets.
- Strength: maintains simplicity and high efficiency (e.g., ~177.6 ± 18s on CoNLL 2009 English) via greedy decoding.
- Strength: paper is clearly written with well-explained architecture and motivation.
- Strength: addresses a relevant problem—joint modeling to reduce pipeline error propagation.
- Strength: thorough experiments including ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and multilingual tests.
- Weakness: core transition system largely follows Henderson et al. (2008); novelty is limited aside from M-SELF.
- Weakness: does not outperform the best pipeline systems that use heavy feature engineering and reranking.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative error analysis to show where the model fails and which linguistic phenomena are challenging.
- Weakness: oracle limitations due to non-projectivity and crossing arcs may cause suboptimal performance; dynamic oracle suggested.
- Question: request quantification of the performance gain specifically attributable to the M-SELF transition.
- Question: ask for details on observed overfitting on the Brown corpus and mitigation strategies tried.
- Question: inquire whether a lightweight reranking step was considered to boost performance further.
- Question: request concrete examples of hard-to-resolve errors and potential remedies.
- Additional comment: deriving a dynamic oracle is a promising direction for future work.
- Additional comment: runtime comparison to Lei et al. (2015) and suggestion to try character-based embeddings (Ballesteros et al., 2015) for better multilingual performance.
