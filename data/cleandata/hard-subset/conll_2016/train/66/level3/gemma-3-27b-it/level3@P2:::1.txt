This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state. The approach builds upon the algorithmic framework of Henderson et al. (2008) and the representation learning techniques introduced by Dyer et al. (2015), achieving competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks. The core contribution lies in the successful integration of these prior works, demonstrating that learning a representation of the entire parsing state – without relying on hand-crafted features – can yield strong results in joint syntactic-semantic parsing. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of competitive performance with significantly faster runtime compared to pipeline-based state-of-the-art systems. Finally, the multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 provide further evidence of the model’s generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates compelling empirical results on standard benchmark datasets (CoNLL 2008 & 2009). The proposed model outperforms previous joint parsing models and achieves performance comparable to strong pipeline systems, particularly considering its significantly faster runtime (177.6 seconds for CoNLL 2009 English test data). This is a substantial practical benefit.
2. **Effective Integration of Existing Techniques:** The paper skillfully combines the strengths of two well-established approaches – the transition-based framework of Henderson et al. and the stack LSTM representations of Dyer et al. This integration isn’t trivial; it effectively addresses the challenge of learning useful representations for joint parsing without relying on extensive feature engineering. The clear articulation of how these components work together is a significant strength.
3. **Clear and Thorough Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the transition system, stack LSTMs, and training procedure. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm and detailed descriptions of the various components contribute to the reproducibility and understandability of the work.
4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of related work, situating their contribution within the broader context of joint syntactic-semantic parsing and representation learning. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and acknowledges the limitations and potential improvements of their approach.
5. **Practical Speed Advantage:** The reported runtime is a key strength. The linear time complexity and fast execution speed make this parser a practical solution for applications where efficiency is critical. This is clearly highlighted and contrasted with slower, feature-rich pipeline systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the integration of existing techniques is valuable, the paper lacks significant methodological novelty. The core components – transition systems and stack LSTMs – are borrowed from prior work. The primary innovation lies in the specific application and combination of these techniques to the joint parsing problem.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The performance, particularly in the multilingual experiments, appears to rely heavily on the quality of the pretrained word embeddings. The discussion acknowledges this for Chinese, but a more thorough analysis of the impact of embedding quality across all languages would be beneficial. It is unclear how much of the multilingual success is due to the model itself versus the embeddings.
3. **Performance Gap to State-of-the-Art:** Although competitive, the model’s performance still falls short of the very best state-of-the-art systems that utilize expert-crafted features, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. While the authors acknowledge this, further investigation into strategies for bridging this gap – potentially by incorporating some of these advancements – would strengthen the work.
4. **Oracle Transition Sequence Imperfections:** The paper notes imperfections in the oracle transition sequence conversion due to crossing arcs. While acknowledged, a more quantitative analysis of how this impacts performance would be valuable, and potentially motivate exploration of methods to mitigate this issue.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of each component (stack LSTMs, specific transitions, etc.) to the overall performance? Specifically, what is the performance degradation when removing the stack LSTM component and relying on feature-based representations?
2. How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the pretrained word embeddings, especially for languages other than Chinese? Have you experimented with different embedding initialization strategies or fine-tuning the embeddings during training?
3. The paper highlights the speed advantage of the greedy inference. Have you explored techniques to improve the accuracy of the greedy algorithm, such as beam search with a small beam size, without significantly sacrificing speed?
4. Regarding the imperfections in oracle transition sequence conversion, can you quantify the percentage of sentences affected and the associated performance impact?

**Additional Comments:**

- The discussion of related work regarding Krishnamurthy and Mitchell (2014) and Lewis et al. (2015) could be expanded to more directly address the differences in task setup and evaluation metrics.
- A brief discussion of the model’s limitations regarding long-range dependencies would be valuable.
