## Review of “A Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parser with Stack LSTMs and Greedy Decoding”

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the framework established by Henderson et al. (2008). The core idea – utilizing stack LSTMs to encode long-range syntactic features for improved semantic role labeling – is sound and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the potential of representation learning to circumvent the need for costly, hand-crafted features. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the successful application of stack LSTMs to a joint syntactic-semantic parsing task *within a purely greedy decoding framework*. While stack LSTMs have been previously used in syntactic parsing, demonstrating their effectiveness in this combined setting is a valuable addition. A second, less prominent contribution is the demonstration that competitive performance can be achieved without relying on manual feature engineering. Finally, the system's speed, stemming from the greedy decoding, is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Well-Written & Technically Sound:** The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and builds substantially on existing LSTM techniques. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
*   **Novel Application of Stack LSTMs:**  The use of stack LSTMs for encoding the entire parser state is a reasonable and effective approach, effectively capturing long-range dependencies.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The system achieves competitive results on the CoNLL-2008 and 2009 datasets, outperforming previous joint models.
*   **Fast Inference:** The purely greedy decoding strategy results in a fast and efficient parser, making it potentially useful in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Greedy Decoding Limitation:** The reliance on a greedy algorithm is a significant limitation. While speed is a benefit, it hinders direct comparability to state-of-the-art systems that employ beam search or other more exhaustive decoding methods. Reporting beam search results is crucial to understand the full potential of the model.
*   **Clarification Needed in Section 2:** The role of both A1 and C-A1 links in the model’s understanding of the task needs further explanation. Additionally, Section 2 would benefit from a clearer summary of the differences between the transitions used in this work and those proposed by Henderson et al. (2008) to better highlight the novelty.
*   **Questionable Concatenation:** The rationale behind concatenating the predicate word and its disambiguated sense vector (Section 3.3) is unclear and appears redundant.
*   **Organization of Section 4:** The organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing regarding multilinguality. The scope of CoNLL-2008 versus CoNLL-2009 should be clarified upfront.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide justification for concatenating the predicate word and its sense vector? Does this improve performance, and if so, why?
2.  Please provide a detailed comparison of the transitions used in your model versus those in Henderson et al. (2008). What specific modifications were made, and what was the motivation behind these changes?
3.  What were the computational costs and performance results when you implemented beam search?
