This paper presents a transition-based dependency parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The core idea is to leverage stack LSTMs to encode long-range syntactic features within the parser state, enabling more informed semantic role labeling. The authors claim novelty in using vectorial representations for the entire parser state and employing a plain greedy search for decoding.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The paper demonstrates a successful application of stack LSTMs to encode the parser state in a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing framework. This allows the model to capture long-range dependencies without relying on explicitly engineered features, which is a valuable contribution toward simpler and more robust parsing systems.
2. **Competitive Performance without Hand-Crafted Features:** Achieving competitive, though not state-of-the-art, results on the CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 datasets *without* relying on any hand-crafted features is a notable accomplishment. This highlights the power of the proposed representation learning approach.
3. **Fast Inference via Greedy Decoding:** The use of a greedy decoding algorithm results in a significantly faster parsing speed compared to systems utilizing beam search or other more complex decoding strategies, making the approach practical for real-time applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is very well written and clearly explains the proposed approach. The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008) is explicitly stated, and the improvements are clearly articulated. The organization is logical, making it easy to follow the technical details.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The system is evaluated on two standard datasets (CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009), providing a fair comparison with existing approaches. The inclusion of results on both English and multilingual data strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Demonstrated Efficiency:** The reported runtime of 177.6±18 seconds for parsing the CoNLL 2009 English test data is a significant advantage, particularly for applications where speed is critical. This efficiency is a direct result of the greedy decoding strategy.
4. **Successful Integration of LSTM Techniques:** The paper effectively leverages recent advances in LSTM technology, specifically stack LSTMs, to improve performance on the joint parsing task. This demonstrates a strong understanding of both parsing frameworks and neural network architectures.
5. **Open-Source Availability:** The authors' commitment to making their implementation publicly available promotes reproducibility and further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limiting Use of Greedy Decoding:** The primary weakness of the paper is the exclusive reliance on a greedy decoding algorithm. While this contributes to the system's speed, it severely limits comparability to other state-of-the-art systems which employ beam search or other more sophisticated decoding techniques. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it significantly weakens the claim of achieving “best parsing performance among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics.” Results with beam search *must* be presented to properly contextualize the performance.
2. **Lack of Clarity Regarding A1 and C-A1 Links:** Section 2 lacks sufficient explanation regarding the role and presence of both A1 and C-A1 links. The impact of these links on the overall parsing process isn't clearly explained. Clarifying this would improve understanding of the model's representation of semantic relationships.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Transition Novelty:** The paper claims novelty in its use of transitions but doesn't adequately summarize the differences between the transitions used in this work and those presented in Henderson et al. (2008). A table or detailed explanation outlining these differences is necessary to substantiate the claim of innovation.
4. **Redundant Concatenation of Predicate Vectors:** The motivation for concatenating the predicate word and disambiguated sense vectors in Section 3.3 is unclear and appears redundant. A justification for this concatenation is needed; otherwise, it seems unnecessarily complex.
5. **Confusing Dataset Organization in Section 4:** The organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing with respect to multilinguality. It's not immediately clear which results apply to CoNLL-2008 and which apply to CoNLL-2009, hindering the reader's ability to easily compare performance across datasets. The scope of each subsection should be explicitly stated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide results using beam search decoding? How do these results compare to the performance reported using greedy decoding, and how does it affect the ranking of your system relative to other state-of-the-art approaches?
2.  Please provide a detailed explanation of the A1 and C-A1 links and their role in the semantic representation within your model.
3.  Could you provide a clear summary (e.g., a table) outlining the specific differences between the transitions used in your work and those presented in Henderson et al. (2008)?
4.  What is the rationale behind concatenating the predicate word and disambiguated sense vectors in Section 3.3? Does this concatenation demonstrably improve performance, and if so, how?
5.  Could you restructure sections 4.1 and 4.2 to clearly delineate the results for CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009, respectively?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural dependency parsing, even if they are not directly comparable due to differences in the task formulation.
*   Investigating the impact of different LSTM architectures or hyperparameters might lead to further performance improvements.
