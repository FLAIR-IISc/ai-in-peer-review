- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs.
- Achieves top results among joint models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks.
- Learns representations of the entire parser state, avoiding hand-crafted features.
- Uses a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm with practical runtime (177.6s on CoNLL 2009 test).
- Introduces a refined transition system including a new M-SELF transition to handle self-dependencies.
- Empirically demonstrates stack LSTMs effectively model joint syntax and semantics where linear methods struggled.
- Neural approach reduces reliance on expert features and is parameter-efficient compared to linear models.
- Results represent a significant improvement over previous joint models, not just incremental gains.
- Clear and concise presentation of model and experiments, though an example run would help.
- Model generalizes across multiple languages on CoNLL 2009 datasets.
- Still underperforms compared to state-of-the-art pipeline systems using expert-crafted features or optimal decoding.
- Lacks detailed quantification of the accuracy vs. efficiency trade-offs with stronger baselines.
- Limited exploration of alternative neural architectures and global decoding strategies.
- Transition system description is complex and would benefit from a dynamic oracle for clarity and reproducibility.
- Heavy reliance on pre-trained embeddings without thorough analysis of embedding source or training-from-scratch effects.
- Requests to authors: provide computational cost comparisons to pipeline systems, report experiments with alternative architectures, supply a dynamic oracle, and analyze sensitivity to embedding quality.
