This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The core contributions lie in the utilization of stack LSTMs to represent the complete parser state—eliminating the need for hand-crafted features—and the employment of a greedy inference algorithm for efficiency. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, showcasing the potential of learning representations to bypass expensive, traditionally-engineered features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Use of Stack LSTMs for Joint Parsing:** The application of stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state is a significant contribution.  Previous work often focused on representing only the top of the stack, or recent states. This approach allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies crucial for semantic role labeling, effectively mimicking the benefits of features like predicate-argument paths without explicit engineering. This representation learning approach is particularly valuable in greedy models where feature design is more challenging.

2. **Efficient Greedy Decoding:** The adoption of a purely greedy inference algorithm delivers a considerable speed advantage.  The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for parsing the CoNLL 2009 English test data is impressive and positions this model as a potentially practical solution for real-time applications.  This speed is achieved without sacrificing substantial accuracy compared to more computationally intensive methods.

3. **Strong Baseline Performance:** The model achieves competitive results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, outperforming earlier joint parsing models and approaching the performance of pipeline-based systems. While not state-of-the-art, this result demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and provides a strong foundation for future work. The multilingual results on CoNLL 2009 are also encouraging, suggesting a degree of cross-lingual transferability.

4. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivations, methodology, and results. The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008, and subsequent developments) is well-established, making the contribution of this work easily understood. The inclusion of examples helps to illustrate the key concepts and the parsing process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison Due to Greedy Decoding:** The primary limitation of this work is the reliance on a greedy decoding algorithm. While providing speed benefits, it inherently restricts the model’s ability to explore the full search space and may prevent it from achieving optimal performance.  The lack of comparison to results obtained with beam search, even with a modest beam width, makes it difficult to assess the full potential of the proposed model and limits its comparability to other state-of-the-art systems that typically employ more sophisticated decoding strategies.

2. **Insufficient Clarification of Transition System:**  Section 2 needs further clarification regarding the semantic transitions, specifically the role and distinction between A1 and C-A1 links in the context of the joint parsing task.  A more detailed explanation of how these links are handled within the parser's framework would improve understanding.  Additionally, a clearer articulation of the differences in the transition systems employed compared to Henderson et al. (2013) would better highlight the novelty of this work.

3. **Redundancy in Input Representation:** The concatenation of predicate word vectors and disambiguated sense vectors in Section 3.3 appears redundant.  The sense vector already encapsulates information about the predicate’s meaning.  Justification for this concatenation, or an ablation study demonstrating its impact on performance, is needed to support this design choice.

4. **Organization and Scope of Experimental Section:** The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing regarding the multilingual aspect of the experiments. The discussion of CoNLL 2008 and 2009 feels disjointed and does not clearly delineate the scope of each experiment (e.g., which datasets were used for each analysis). A more structured presentation would improve clarity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you experimented with beam search decoding? If so, how did the performance compare to the greedy decoding approach presented in the paper?
2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the A1 and C-A1 links in the context of the joint parsing task, and how they are handled by the proposed parser?
3. What is the motivation for concatenating the predicate word vector and the disambiguated sense vector? Does this concatenation contribute significantly to performance, and have you performed any ablation studies to assess its impact?
4. Could you clarify the scope of the experiments in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, specifically delineating which datasets were used for each analysis and which languages were included in the multilingual experiments?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's reference to "pseudo-projectivization" could be briefly explained for readers unfamiliar with the technique.  It would also be valuable to discuss the potential impact of error propagation from POS tagging and lemmatization on the performance of the parser, given that these are provided as input.
