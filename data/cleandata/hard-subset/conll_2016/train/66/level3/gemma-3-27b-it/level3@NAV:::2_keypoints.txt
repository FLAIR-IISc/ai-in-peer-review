- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs and greedy decoding.  
- Argues that representation learning with stack LSTMs can replace costly hand-crafted features.  
- Main contribution: successful application of stack LSTMs to joint syntactic–semantic parsing within a purely greedy decoding framework.  
- Secondary contribution: shows competitive performance without manual feature engineering.  
- Practical benefit: greedy decoding yields fast inference.  
- Strength: well-written, technically sound, and grounded in relevant literature.  
- Strength: novel use of stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state and capture long-range dependencies.  
- Strength: achieves competitive results on CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009, outperforming previous joint models.  
- Strength: fast and efficient parser due to greedy decoding.  
- Weakness: reliance on greedy decoding limits comparability to beam-search or more exhaustive methods; beam-search results are needed.  
- Weakness: Section 2 needs clearer explanation of A1 vs C-A1 links and a concise summary of transition differences from Henderson et al. (2008).  
- Weakness: unclear rationale for concatenating predicate word and its disambiguated sense vector; appears redundant.  
- Weakness: Section 4’s organization is confusing about multilinguality and the scopes of CoNLL-2008 vs CoNLL-2009.  
- Question: justify concatenating predicate word and sense vector and report whether it improves performance.  
- Question: provide a detailed comparison of transitions versus Henderson et al. (2008), including specific modifications and motivations.  
- Question: report computational costs and performance when using beam search.
