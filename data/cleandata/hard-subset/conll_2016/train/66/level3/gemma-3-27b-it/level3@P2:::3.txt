This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser's state without relying on hand-crafted features. The model achieves strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, surpassing previous joint parsing models and achieving comparable results to pipeline approaches.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a neural network-based approach, specifically utilizing stack LSTMs within a transition-based parsing framework, can effectively learn joint syntactic and semantic dependencies without the need for extensive feature engineering. This is particularly significant given the historical challenges of jointly modeling these dependencies using linear models, where feature design proved crucial and often complex. A secondary contribution lies in the refined transition system, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008) by incorporating modifications to better facilitate joint learning and efficient greedy inference. While the transition system itself isn't radically novel, the successful integration with the stack LSTM architecture is key to the observed performance gains. Finally, the authors contribute a fast and efficient parsing system, achieving a runtime of approximately 178 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test set.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results convincingly show that the learned representations, combined with the refined transition system, allow the model to outperform previous joint parsing models. The comparison to pipeline systems highlights the potential of joint modeling to avoid error propagation.
2. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the parser state is a crucial aspect of the success. By learning representations for the entire algorithmic state, the model avoids the limitations of previous approaches that focused on only the top items on the stack or recent parser states. This allows for richer contextual information to be considered during parsing decisions.
3. **Efficiency and Scalability:** The greedy inference algorithm ensures linear time complexity, making the parser practical for real-world applications. The reported runtime is significantly faster than many competing systems, especially those relying on complex feature engineering and optimal decoding procedures. This efficiency makes the model appealing for tasks requiring rapid parsing.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model, including the transition system, the stack LSTM architecture, and the training procedure. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm in the supplementary material further enhances understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Transition System:** While the transition system builds upon previous work and is effectively integrated into the neural network framework, the individual transitions themselves are largely derived from existing approaches (Nivre, 2008; Henderson et al., 2013). The primary innovation lies in the combination of this system with the stack LSTM representation learner, rather than the transitions themselves.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper acknowledges that the model’s performance does not yet match the best-performing systems that utilize expert-crafted features and optimal decoding. While this is a fair assessment, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific limitations and potential avenues for improvement to bridge this gap. Further analysis comparing feature importance could reveal opportunities for targeted improvements.
3. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The reliance on pretrained word embeddings introduces a potential dependency on the quality and domain relevance of these embeddings. While the authors address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words, a more thorough analysis of the impact of different embedding strategies would be valuable.
4. **Limited Multilingual Analysis:** Although the model is evaluated on multiple languages, the analysis of multilingual performance is relatively brief. A more detailed investigation of the model’s ability to generalize across languages, and potential adaptations for low-resource languages, would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential for integrating the proposed transition system with other neural network architectures, such as global beam-search decoding (e.g., Andor et al., 2016)? What challenges might arise, and how could they be addressed?
2.  The paper mentions that the transition sequence for some CoNLL 2009 examples is incorrectly encoded. Could you provide more details on the frequency and nature of these errors, and how they might impact the evaluation results?
3.  You mention that the “semantics-only” model underperforms. Can you provide further insight into *why* it underperforms? Is there a specific type of dependency or linguistic phenomenon that causes issues for this model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors’ intention to release an open-source implementation is greatly appreciated and will facilitate further research in this area.
*   A discussion of the computational resources required for training and inference would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   Exploring the use of character-based word embeddings to address the limitations of pretrained word embeddings could be a valuable future research direction.
