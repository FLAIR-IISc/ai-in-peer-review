This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008). The authors address a key challenge in natural language processing – the error propagation inherent in pipeline systems – by learning a joint model. The primary contribution of this work lies in the application of stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state, enabling the model to learn useful features without relying on hand-crafted features. A secondary, though significant, contribution is the demonstration that a greedy decoding algorithm can achieve competitive performance in this joint parsing task, offering a substantial speed advantage over systems employing more complex search strategies. Finally, the thorough evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets provides a valuable benchmark for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to encode the entire parser state is a strong point. This allows the model to capture long-range dependencies and implicitly learn features that would otherwise require manual engineering. The demonstrated performance improvement over previous joint models, particularly those relying on manually designed features, highlights the effectiveness of this approach. The authors successfully demonstrate that representation learning can obviate the need for costly, expert-crafted features, as observed in prior work for syntactic parsing.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The adoption of a greedy decoding algorithm results in a linear time complexity and, critically, a significantly faster parsing speed compared to systems utilizing more sophisticated (and computationally expensive) decoding schemes like beam search or dynamic programming. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds for the CoNLL 2009 English test data is a considerable advantage in practical applications, especially when considering real-time processing requirements.

3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The connection to prior work (Henderson et al., 2008) is clearly established, and the modifications made in this paper are explained in a concise manner. The inclusion of a detailed description of the transition system and the statistical model enhances the reproducibility of the work.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 datasets, including a multilingual experiment, provides a robust assessment of the model’s performance. The comparisons with existing systems, both pipeline-based and joint models, offer valuable insights into the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis with Strong Baselines:** While the results are competitive with older systems, the performance lags behind state-of-the-art models that leverage more complex features, optimal decoding, or additional datasets. The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion of the specific reasons for the performance gap would be beneficial. In particular, a more direct comparison against systems like Lei et al. (2015) – discussing the trade-offs between feature complexity, decoding strategy and runtime – is needed.

2. **Justification of Greedy Decoding:** The exclusive reliance on a greedy decoding algorithm is a potential limitation. While the speed advantage is undeniable, it raises concerns about suboptimal parse selection. The authors do not provide any justification for *why* a greedy approach is sufficient, nor do they present results from experiments using a beam search strategy. Reporting beam search performance would significantly strengthen the argument that the speedup doesn’t come at a substantial accuracy cost.

3. **Lack of Clarity on Transition Details:** Section 2 could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the transitions used, particularly concerning the nuances of A1 and C-A1 links within the SRL graph. A clearer comparison of the transitions implemented in this work versus those in Henderson et al. (2013) would also help highlight the specific contributions of this paper beyond the introduction of stack LSTMs.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  In Section 3.3, you concatenate the word vector and the disambiguated sense vector for the predicate. Why is this concatenation necessary? Doesn’t the sense vector already encapsulate information present in the word vector? Could the performance be maintained or improved by using only the sense vector?

2.  The organization of Sections 4.1 and 4.2 is somewhat confusing. It is not immediately clear from the text whether CoNLL-2008 experiments were limited to English or included multilingual results as well. Please clarify the scope of each subsection and clearly delineate the experimental setup for each dataset.

3.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of applying the model to languages with rich morphology? What strategies might be employed to mitigate these limitations, such as incorporating character-based word embeddings?

**Additional Comments:**

- It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the hyperparameters used in the experiments, especially for the different models (e.g., Syntax-only, Semantics-only).
- The supplementary material mentioned in the text (e.g., an example run of the algorithm) should be readily available to reviewers.
- The code availability is a significant plus and contributes to the reproducibility of the research.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The use of stack LSTMs to learn representations of the parser state is a promising approach, and the achieved performance is competitive with existing methods. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the justification of the greedy decoding algorithm and a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art baselines, would further strengthen the paper's impact.
