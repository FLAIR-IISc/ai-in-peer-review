## Review for CONLL_2016 Paper

This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies. The authors build upon the work of Henderson et al. (2008) and Dyer et al. (2015), integrating a stack LSTM network into a joint parsing framework. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that learning representations of the entire parser state, using stack LSTMs, obviates the need for expert-crafted features and achieves competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, even outperforming previous joint parsing models. The efficiency of the greedy inference algorithm, with linear time complexity, is also highlighted.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the successful integration of stack LSTMs into a joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing system, demonstrating the effectiveness of learning entire parser states for this task. While the individual components (transition-based parsing, stack LSTMs) are not novel, their combination represents a valuable advance.  Furthermore, the competitive performance achieved without relying on hand-engineered features is significant. A secondary contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation on the established CoNLL datasets, providing a solid benchmark for future research and demonstrating the applicability of the approach to multiple languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The parser achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks, surpassing previous joint parsing models.  This is supported by detailed results presented in Tables 2 and 3, clearly demonstrating improvements over baseline models like Henderson et al. (2008) and Gesmundo et al. (2009).  The results on the CoNLL 2009 multilingual data (Table 4) further support the generalizability of the approach.
2. **Efficient Inference:** The use of a greedy inference algorithm results in linear time complexity, making the parser practical for real-world applications. The reported runtime of 177.6±18 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data highlights this efficiency, and is favorably compared to slower pipeline models like Lei et al. (2015). This is a significant advantage, particularly for large-scale parsing tasks.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the complex methodology. The detailed description of the transition-based procedure, the stack LSTM architecture, and the statistical model allows for easy understanding and potential replication of the work. The inclusion of Figure 3 is particularly helpful in visualizing the system architecture.
4. **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work in joint parsing and neural network approaches to SRL. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and positions the current work effectively within the existing literature. The discussion of limitations and potential future directions is also well-considered.
5. **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using standard datasets and evaluation metrics. The hyperparameter tuning process is described, and the ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) provide valuable insights into the contributions of different components of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the integration of stack LSTMs into a joint parsing framework is a valuable contribution, the underlying components (transition-based parsing, stack LSTMs) are not novel. The core innovation lies in the combination of existing techniques rather than the introduction of fundamentally new methods.
2. **Dependence on Pretrained Embeddings:** The strong performance on the multilingual datasets, particularly Chinese, may be heavily influenced by the quality of the pretrained word embeddings. The authors acknowledge this, but further analysis investigating the relative contributions of the model and the embeddings would be beneficial.  It’s unclear how much of the gain is due to the model itself versus superior embeddings.
3. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper demonstrates competitive performance, the comparison to truly state-of-the-art SRL systems (e.g., those leveraging full syntactic parses and reranking) reveals a performance gap.  The authors acknowledge this, attributing it to the lack of expert-crafted features and optimal decoding. However, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency would be valuable.
4. **Oracle Transition Issues:** The mention of imperfect oracle conversion of semantic parses into transitions, affecting 1.5% of the CoNLL 2009 English dataset, introduces a potential source of error that should be further discussed. While acknowledged, the impact on the reported results is not quantified.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the contribution of the pretrained embeddings, particularly for the multilingual results?  Perhaps an experiment with randomly initialized embeddings would clarify the model’s dependence on them.
2.  Regarding the imperfect oracle transition conversion: can you estimate the impact of this error on the reported LAS and F1 scores?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating character-level embeddings or morphological features, especially for languages with rich morphology?

**Additional Comments:**

*  It would be helpful to include a discussion of the memory requirements of the model, particularly as the size of the stack LSTMs increases.
* The code availability is a significant positive aspect of this work.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing. While the methodological novelty is limited, the successful integration of existing techniques and the achievement of competitive performance, coupled with the efficiency of the approach, make it a worthwhile addition to the CONLL 2016 workshop. I recommend acceptance.
