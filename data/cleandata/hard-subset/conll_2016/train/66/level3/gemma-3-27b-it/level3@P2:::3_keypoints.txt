- Main contribution: transition-based neural parser using stack LSTMs that jointly learns syntactic and semantic dependencies without hand-crafted features, with strong CoNLL 2008/2009 results.  
- Secondary contribution: refined transition system based on Henderson et al. integrated with stack LSTMs to enable effective greedy inference.  
- Efficiency: fast, linear-time greedy parser (~178 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English test).  
- Empirical strength: outperforms previous joint parsing models and is competitive with pipeline approaches, reducing error propagation.  
- Effective use of stack LSTMs: models full parser state for richer contextual decisions beyond top-stack items.  
- Presentation: clear, well-written paper with detailed methodology and example run.  
- Limited novelty in transitions: transition system largely derived from prior work; main novelty is the neural integration.  
- Not yet state-of-the-art: performance lags best systems that use expert-crafted features and optimal decoding.  
- Dependence on pretrained embeddings: reliance on embeddings raises OOV and domain-quality concerns needing deeper analysis.  
- Limited multilingual analysis: multilingual evaluation is brief; lacks detailed study of generalization and low-resource adaptation.  
- Question on integration: unclear how the transition system would combine with other architectures (e.g., global beam-search) and what challenges would arise.  
- Question on encoding errors: transition sequence encoding errors in some CoNLL 2009 examples need frequency and impact clarification.  
- Question on semantics-only model: need explanation why the semantics-only model underperforms and which dependency types cause issues.  
- Additional: open-source release is planned and welcomed for further research.  
- Additional reproducibility note: report computational resources required for training and inference.  
- Additional future direction: explore character-based embeddings to address pretrained-embedding limitations.
