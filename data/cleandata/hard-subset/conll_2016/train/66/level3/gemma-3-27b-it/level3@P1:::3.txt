This paper presents a transition-based parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, leveraging recent advances in representation learning with stack LSTMs. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, achieving the best results among models that jointly learn syntax and semantics. The core idea is to learn representations of the entire parser state, eschewing expert-crafted features and employing a greedy inference algorithm with linear time complexity.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are twofold. First, the paper presents a refined transition system built upon the foundation of Henderson et al. (2013), incorporating a new transition (M-SELF) to handle self-dependencies, common in datasets like CoNLL 2009. While building on existing work, this refinement is critical for achieving state-of-the-art results. Second, and perhaps more significantly, the work empirically demonstrates the efficacy of neural network-based approaches – specifically stack LSTMs – in jointly modeling syntactic and semantic dependencies. This is a crucial finding, given the historical difficulties in achieving strong performance in joint models using traditional linear approaches. The authors correctly point out that the success stems from the ability of neural networks to automatically learn effective features, reducing the reliance on costly and hand-engineered features.  I would add that the inherent parameter efficiency of neural networks in representing complex interactions, compared to the numerous feature/class weight combinations in linear models, likely contributes to the success in this joint task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks among models learning syntax and semantics jointly. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are not just incrementally better; they represent a significant leap over previous joint models.
2. **Efficient Inference:** The use of a greedy inference algorithm results in a linear-time parsing process, making the model practical for real-world applications. The reported runtime of 177.6 seconds on the CoNLL 2009 English test data is highly competitive, particularly given the model's performance.  This addresses a common criticism of more complex, higher-accuracy parsers – computational cost.
3. **Effective Use of Stack LSTMs:** The application of stack LSTMs to represent the parser’s state proves highly successful. The authors demonstrate that these LSTMs can effectively capture the necessary information for joint parsing without requiring extensive feature engineering. This simplification of the feature set is a major strength of the approach.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and the experimental setup. The inclusion of an example run of the algorithm (referenced in the text) would be beneficial, but the current description is still quite comprehensive.
5. **Multilingual Performance:** The results on the multilingual CoNLL 2009 datasets show that the model is not limited to English and can generalize reasonably well to other languages. This demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the paper compares favorably to previous *joint* models, it acknowledges that its performance still falls short of state-of-the-art systems that utilize expert-crafted features and/or optimal decoding. Although these systems are often computationally expensive, it’s important to explicitly quantify the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.  A more detailed ablation study, perhaps comparing different combinations of learned features and expert-crafted features, would be valuable.
2. **Limited Exploration of Architectural Variations:** The paper focuses primarily on the stack LSTM architecture. While this choice is justified, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the potential for incorporating other neural architectures, such as those explored in Andor et al. (2016) which employs global beam search. The authors mention orthogonal improvements, but a brief discussion of these possibilities would strengthen the paper.
3. **Transition System Oracle:** The description of the transition system and the constraints placed on it is somewhat complex. A dynamic oracle that illustrates which transitions are permissible in a given state would be highly beneficial. Specifically, the interplay between M-SHIFT, M-REDUCE, M-LEFT, and M-RIGHT actions and their relationship to a well-defined arc-eager oracle is not entirely clear. Providing such an oracle, either in the appendix or as a follow-up resource, would significantly enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the system.
4. **Reliance on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The model relies on pre-trained word embeddings. While this is a common practice, the paper does not thoroughly investigate the impact of different embedding sources or the potential benefits of learning embeddings from scratch.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (runtime and memory usage) of your model versus the strongest pipeline-based systems on the CoNLL datasets?
2. Have you experimented with alternative neural architectures, such as those using attention mechanisms or global decoding strategies (e.g., Andor et al., 2016)? If so, could you briefly describe the results?
3.  Would you be willing to provide a dynamic oracle for the transition system, illustrating permissible transitions in a given state? This would greatly aid in understanding and reproducing the system.
4. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and source of the pre-trained word embeddings? Have you explored learning the embeddings from scratch?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The combination of a refined transition system and the effective use of stack LSTMs results in a high-performing and efficient parser. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work.
