- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly predicts syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs, integrating Henderson et al. (2008) and Dyer et al. (2015).
- Core contribution: learning a full parsing-state representation without hand-crafted features yields strong joint parsing results.
- Secondary contribution: competitive accuracy with substantially faster runtime than pipeline systems.
- Demonstrates multilingual generalizability with results on CoNLL 2009.
- Strong empirical performance on CoNLL 2008 & 2009, outperforming previous joint models and matching strong pipeline systems in many cases.
- Effective integration of transition-based parsing and stack LSTM representations to avoid extensive feature engineering.
- Paper is well-written with clear methodology, example runs, and reproducible descriptions.
- Thorough related work review situating the contribution and acknowledging limitations.
- Practical speed advantage: linear-time complexity and fast execution make the parser useful for efficiency-critical applications.
- Limited methodological novelty: core components are borrowed and the main novelty is their combination/application.
- Heavy reliance on pretrained word embeddings, with unclear contribution of embeddings vs. model—especially in multilingual settings.
- Still lags behind the absolute state-of-the-art that uses expert features, advanced decoding, or extra data.
- Oracle transition sequence conversion has imperfections due to crossing arcs; impact on performance is not fully quantified.
- Requests ablation study quantifying each component’s contribution (e.g., removing stack LSTMs).
- Asks about sensitivity to embedding quality, alternative initialization, and fine-tuning across languages.
- Suggests exploring improvements to greedy inference (e.g., small-beam search) to boost accuracy without much speed loss.
- Asks for quantification of how many sentences are affected by oracle conversion issues and resulting performance impact.
- Recommends expanding discussion comparing to Krishnamurthy & Mitchell (2014) and Lewis et al. (2015) and addressing limitations on long-range dependencies.
