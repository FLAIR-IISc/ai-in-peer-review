## Review

This paper revisits joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing using a transition-based neural network model. The authors demonstrate strong results on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, outperforming previous joint models and achieving competitive performance with pipeline systems, despite eschewing hand-crafted features and employing greedy decoding. The core idea is leveraging stack LSTMs to learn representations of the entire parser state, enabling automatic feature learning for both syntactic and semantic parsing.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs to Joint Parsing:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of stack LSTMs for learning representations of parser state in a joint syntactic and semantic parsing framework. This allows the model to achieve strong performance *without* relying on manually engineered features, a major advantage in terms of portability and development effort.
2. **Improved Transition System:** While building on Henderson et al. (2008), the inclusion of the M-SELF transition to handle self-dependencies is a specific, valuable refinement to the transition system, addressing a particular challenge in the CoNLL datasets. This showcases an understanding of the specific data characteristics.
3. **Empirical Validation of Neural Networks for Joint Modeling:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that neural networks can succeed in joint syntactic and semantic parsing where earlier linear models struggled. This is not just about achieving better numbers, but demonstrating a paradigm shift in how these tasks can be approached. The comparison to previous joint models highlights the benefits of neural representation learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on standard datasets (CoNLL 2008 & 2009) for joint parsing. This is the most compelling argument for acceptance. The results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The use of a greedy decoding algorithm results in a remarkably fast parsing system (177.6 ± 18 seconds on CoNLL 2009 English). This efficiency is a significant practical benefit, especially compared to more complex, reranking-based systems.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and design choices. The architecture is illustrated effectively, and the motivation for each component is well-articulated.
4. **Relevance to the Field:** Joint modeling remains an important area of research in NLP, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues inherent in pipeline systems. This paper offers a promising approach towards more robust and efficient joint parsing.
5. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors perform several ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) which offer valuable insight into the contributions of syntactic information and the benefits of joint modeling. The multilingual experiments also demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Transition System:** The transition system is largely based on existing work (Henderson et al., 2008), with the M-SELF transition being the primary addition. While effective, the core algorithmic contribution isn't groundbreaking. The paper's strength lies in the application of neural networks *to* this system.
2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While competitive, the system does not surpass the very best performing pipeline systems, which leverage extensive feature engineering and reranking. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a relevant point. The gap suggests there's still room for improvement and integration with more sophisticated techniques.
3. **Lack of Analysis of Errors:** The paper presents quantitative results, but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails could provide valuable insights for future work. What types of errors are most common? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that are particularly challenging?
4. **Oracle Limitations:** The authors mention issues with the transition oracle due to non-projectivity and crossing arcs. This could be a source of sub-optimal performance and warrants further investigation. A dynamic oracle, as suggested in the appendix, could address this.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the impact of the M-SELF transition? How much performance gain is attributable specifically to its inclusion?
2. You mention the potential for overfitting in the joint model on the out-of-domain Brown corpus. Can you provide more details about this overfitting, and any attempts to mitigate it?
3. Given that the system is relatively fast, have you explored integrating a lightweight reranking step to potentially improve performance further?
4. Could you provide examples of errors the system makes that are particularly difficult to resolve, and discuss potential avenues for addressing them?

**Additional Comments:**

The suggestion for deriving a dynamic oracle for the transition system is excellent and could be a valuable addition to future work. The comparison to Lei et al. (2015) runtime is helpful for contextualizing the efficiency of the proposed approach. The reference to Ballesteros et al. (2015) regarding character-based word embeddings is a relevant suggestion for improving multilingual performance.
