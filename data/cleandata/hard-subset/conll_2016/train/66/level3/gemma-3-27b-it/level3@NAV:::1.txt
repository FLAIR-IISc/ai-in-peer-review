## Review of "Stack LSTM Parser for Joint Syntactic and Semantic Dependency Parsing"

This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the transition system of Henderson et al. and the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. The authors evaluate their approach on the CoNLL 2008/2009 shared tasks, demonstrating competitive and, in some cases, state-of-the-art results among joint models. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and providing a thorough overview of related work. The system description is detailed, and hyperparameter choices are justified.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the successful integration of Henderson et al.'s joint transition system with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM approach. While neither component is novel in itself, the combination allows for a strong joint model *without* relying on expensive, hand-crafted features, achieving competitive performance in a fast, greedy manner. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough empirical evaluation on the CoNLL datasets, providing a strong benchmark and analysis of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive performance on both CoNLL 2008 and 2009, surpassing prior joint models and approaching the performance of more complex pipeline systems. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the combined architecture.
2. **Clear and Comprehensive Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The methodology, including the transition system, LSTM architecture, and training procedure, is explained in a clear and concise manner.
3. **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the contribution within the broader landscape of joint parsing and SRL.  It acknowledges the strengths of competing approaches (feature-rich pipelines) and justifies the focus on a feature-lean, efficient model.
4. **Efficient Inference:** The greedy inference algorithm, coupled with the stack LSTM, results in a relatively fast parsing speed compared to other state-of-the-art systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Methodological Novelty:** As acknowledged by the authors, the core components of the system (transition system and stack LSTM) are not novel. The primary innovation is the integration, which, while valuable, doesn't represent a significant theoretical breakthrough.
2. **Potential Embedding Bias:** The authors rightly point out the potential influence of high-quality Chinese embeddings on performance. It is unclear to what extent the improvements observed are due to the model itself versus the quality of the pretrained embeddings. A more controlled experiment varying embedding quality would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While results are compared to prior joint models, the comparison to the very best performing (and often pipeline-based) systems is somewhat limited. Expanding this comparison, perhaps with a streamlined reproduction of one key baseline, would further strengthen the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your results to the quality of the pretrained word embeddings, particularly for the Chinese dataset? Were any experiments conducted using different or randomly initialized embeddings?
2. The paper mentions the possibility of overfitting on the WSJ data. Did you explore regularization techniques (beyond dropout) to mitigate this issue?
3. Can you comment on the computational cost of training compared to the baselines you mention?
