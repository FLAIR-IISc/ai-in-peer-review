This paper presents a Stack LSTM parser for jointly producing syntactic and semantic dependencies, building upon the transition system of Henderson et al. (2008) and the stack LSTM architecture of Dyer et al. (2015). The authors demonstrate competitive results on the CoNLL 2008/2009 shared tasks, outperforming prior joint models and achieving performance comparable to strong pipelined systems.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Integration of Stack LSTMs into a Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parser:** The most significant contribution is the successful integration of stack LSTMs into a joint parsing framework. This demonstrates the effectiveness of representation learning in bypassing the need for extensive, hand-crafted features typically required in joint models. This integration was a logical next step given prior work, but its successful implementation and demonstration of competitive performance are valuable.
2. **Strong Baseline for Joint Parsing:** The paper establishes a strong, fast, and relatively simple baseline for joint syntactic-semantic parsing that doesn’t rely on complex feature engineering or optimal decoding. The linear runtime is a practical benefit.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis:** The thorough evaluation on the CoNLL 2008/2009 datasets, including ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) and multilingual results, provides valuable insights into the behavior of the model and the interplay between syntactic and semantic parsing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in detail, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The authors effectively build upon prior work, referencing it appropriately.
2. **Sound Methodology & Justified Hyperparameters:** The methodological choices are well-justified, particularly the use of stack LSTMs and the arc-eager transition system. The hyperparameter tuning process, while not exhaustive, is described, and the choices are reasonable based on prior work and the development set performance. The ablation studies further strengthen confidence in the design choices.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results among *joint* parsing models on the CoNLL datasets. While not surpassing the best *pipelined* systems, the performance is competitive, especially considering the model's simplicity and efficiency. The runtime advantage is notable.
4. **Strong Related Work Section:** The related work section is comprehensive and effectively situates the paper within the broader context of joint parsing and neural network approaches to SRL. The authors acknowledge limitations and point to opportunities for future work.
5. **Open-Source Release:** The planned release of an open-source implementation is a significant contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness is the lack of substantial methodological novelty. The paper primarily *integrates* existing techniques (Henderson et al.'s transition system, Dyer et al.'s stack LSTMs) rather than proposing fundamentally new approaches. While valuable, this limits the paper’s impact.
2. **Potential Influence of Chinese Embeddings:** The paper acknowledges a potential concern regarding the reliance on high-quality pre-trained embeddings, particularly for the Chinese language results. It’s possible that the performance gains are, at least partially, attributable to the quality of the embeddings rather than solely to the model’s innovations. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but it’s a valid point.
3. **Dependence on Data Size:** The multilingual results suggest the model’s performance is sensitive to the amount of training data. The lower performance on Japanese, a smaller dataset, supports this observation. This limitation isn’t addressed in detail.
4. **Greedy Decoding:** While the linear runtime is an advantage, the use of greedy decoding potentially limits the overall accuracy. Beam search or other more sophisticated decoding strategies might yield further improvements, although at the cost of increased computational complexity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the sensitivity of the results to the quality of the pre-trained embeddings, specifically for languages other than English? Perhaps an experiment with randomly initialized embeddings?
2.  Were any attempts made to mitigate the overfitting observed on the WSJ data (as indicated by the improved performance of the Hybrid model on the Brown corpus)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the oracle transition conversion and its potential impact on the reported results? Was there any analysis of instances where the oracle conversion failed?
4. How would the performance of the model change if morphological features were incorporated, particularly for morphologically rich languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could benefit from a brief mention of recent advances in end-to-end neural SRL models that don’t rely on explicit parsing, such as those based on transformers.
*   The formatting of Table 1 is slightly awkward, and could be improved for readability.
