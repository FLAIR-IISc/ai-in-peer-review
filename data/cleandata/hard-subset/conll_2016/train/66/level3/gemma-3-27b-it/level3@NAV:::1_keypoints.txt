- Paper proposes a Stack LSTM parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies by integrating Henderson et al.'s transition system with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM and evaluates on CoNLL 2008/2009, showing competitive/state-of-the-art joint-model results.

- Primary contribution: effective integration of a joint transition system with stack LSTMs to build a fast, greedy, feature-lean joint parser without hand-crafted features.

- Secondary contribution: thorough empirical evaluation on CoNLL datasets providing a strong benchmark and analysis.

- Strength — Strong empirical results: outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and approaches pipeline-system performance.

- Strength — Clear and comprehensive explanation: paper is exceptionally well-written with detailed methodology and justified hyperparameters.

- Strength — Thorough related work: comprehensive literature coverage and clear positioning of the contribution.

- Strength — Efficient inference: greedy stack LSTM inference yields relatively fast parsing speed.

- Weakness — Limited methodological novelty: core components are existing methods; novelty is primarily their integration rather than a theoretical advance.

- Weakness — Potential embedding bias: improvements (notably on Chinese) may stem from high-quality pretrained embeddings rather than model design; controlled tests are needed.

- Weakness — Limited comparison to strong baselines: insufficient comparison to top-performing pipeline systems; reproducing a key baseline would strengthen claims.

- Question — How sensitive are results to the quality of pretrained word embeddings, particularly for Chinese; were different or random embeddings tested?

- Question — Were additional regularization techniques (beyond dropout) explored to address possible overfitting on WSJ?

- Question — What is the computational cost of training compared to the baseline systems mentioned?
