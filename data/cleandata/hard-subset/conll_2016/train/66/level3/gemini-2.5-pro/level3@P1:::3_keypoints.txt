- Summary: Transition-based parser using stack LSTMs to jointly model syntax and semantics with greedy linear-time inference; outperforms prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and is faster.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates that a neural, transition-based model with stack LSTMs can learn end-to-end representations for joint syntactic-semantic parsing without hand-crafted features.

- Contribution 2: Proposes a simple, greedy, linear-time parser that is efficient, adaptable across languages, and requires minimal language-specific engineering.

- Contribution 3: Adapts an arc-eager transition system to the joint setting and introduces an M-SELF action to handle nominal predicate self-dependencies.

- Strength 1: Achieves the best reported scores among joint parsers on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, substantially improving on Henderson et al. (2013).

- Strength 2: Model simplicity and efficiency (greedy, linear-time) yield practical speed advantages (~2.5x faster than a recent pipelined system) and removal of manual feature engineering.

- Strength 3: Ablation studies (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) are insightful, quantifying the value of syntax for semantics and revealing domain-adaptation effects.

- Strength 4: Provides a modular foundation for future work (e.g., beam search, global normalization) to further close the gap with pipelined systems.

- Weakness 1: Despite leading among joint models, it still falls short of top pipelined systems that use full parses and global inference/reranking.

- Weakness 2: Training with a static oracle induces exposure bias; absence of a dynamic oracle limits learning to recover from errors.

- Weakness 3: The transition system is largely an incremental adaptation of prior work, with limited novelty beyond the M-SELF addition.

- Question 1: Have you considered building a dynamic oracle for this interleaved joint system, and what challenges do you foresee?

- Question 2: Why might joint training worsen out-of-domain performance—is the model learning spurious, domain-specific syntactic-semantic correlations?

- Question 3: Would incorporating beam search or global syntactic features likely close the performance gap with top pipelined systems, or are more fundamental changes needed?
