- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic+semantic parser using stack LSTMs to learn dense parser-state representations, replacing hand-crafted features, with greedy inference and evaluation on CoNLL 2008/2009.

- Contribution: demonstrates that learned continuous-state representations (via stack LSTMs) can replace discrete, hand-engineered features for semantic role labeling.

- Contribution: builds a fast, greedy, linear-time joint parser that is computationally efficient and a strong baseline.

- Strength: elegant, principled model — stack LSTMs capture recursive stack structure and allow deep stack information to influence decisions.

- Strength: strong empirical gains over prior joint models (notably Henderson et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.

- Strength: high efficiency — much faster end-to-end parsing time compared to slower pipeline systems.

- Strength: clear, well-written presentation with insightful ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid).

- Weakness: exclusive reliance on greedy decoding risks error propagation and leaves unclear whether architecture or search limits performance versus state-of-the-art.

- Weakness: insufficient clarity about what is novel in the transition system vs. Henderson et al. (2013); lacks a consolidated summary of modifications.

- Weakness: confusing experimental section structure — dataset-specific hyperparameters and multilingual results are poorly organized across sections.

- Question: Have you tried beam search, and can you report results to separate modeling capacity from search limitations?

- Question: Please explicitly summarize the differences between your transition system and Henderson et al. (2013) (e.g., list of modified actions/constraints).

- Question: Why include both word vector v and sense vector p in predicate composition gd(v,p); have you tried using only sense embeddings?

- Question: Please clarify for readers the distinction between core arguments (e.g., A1) and continuation arguments (e.g., C-A1) in CoNLL 2009 examples.
