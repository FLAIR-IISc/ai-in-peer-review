### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly models syntactic and semantic dependencies. The model is an adaptation of the synchronized, incremental parsing framework of Henderson et al. (2013), but replaces hand-crafted features with learned representations of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history) using stack LSTMs. The parser makes decisions using a purely greedy inference algorithm, which results in a fast, linear-time system. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared task datasets (including multilingual data for 2009), demonstrating that their approach outperforms previous joint parsing models and is competitive with strong pipelined systems from that era, though it does not match the performance of more recent state-of-the-art systems.

### Contributions
1.  The primary contribution is the successful application and extension of the stack LSTM architecture to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The work effectively demonstrates that a learned, continuous-vector representation of the complete parser state can replace the need for extensive, manually engineered features, which were a mainstay of previous high-performing systems in this task.
2.  A secondary contribution is the development and evaluation of an efficient (linear-time) and simple joint parser. By relying on a greedy search, the system is significantly faster than many competing models, making it a strong candidate for practical applications where parsing speed is a critical factor. The paper provides a compelling case that strong representations can compensate for a simpler search algorithm.
3.  The paper also provides a valuable set of ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that cleanly isolates the benefits of the joint modeling approach within their framework, showing that syntax and semantics mutually benefit each other, especially on in-domain data.

### Strengths
1.  **Effective Use of Learned Representations:** The paper does an excellent job of extending the stack LSTM model from syntax-only parsing (Dyer et al., 2015) to a much more complex joint task. This is a non-trivial adaptation, requiring the management of multiple stacks and interleaved transition types. The model's ability to outperform previous feature-based joint models without any hand-crafted features is a strong testament to the power of learning representations for the entire algorithmic state.
2.  **Impressive Performance for a Greedy System:** The results are very strong for a model that relies solely on greedy decoding. It surpasses several previous joint models that used more complex search mechanisms. This indicates that the learned state representations are rich enough to enable accurate local decision-making, which is a key finding.
3.  **Efficiency and Simplicity:** A major advantage of this work is its focus on efficiency. The linear-time complexity and the elimination of the feature engineering pipeline make the system fast and conceptually simple. The authors substantiate this by reporting a runtime that is 2.5 times faster than a recent state-of-the-art system, making it highly practical.
4.  **Clarity and Solid Experimental Design:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the relationship to prior work is well-established. The experiments are thorough, covering multiple datasets and languages, and the inclusion of different model variants (joint, hybrid, etc.) provides insightful analysis.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Exclusive Reliance on Greedy Decoding:** The most significant weakness is the lack of results using beam search. While the fast, greedy approach is positioned as a contribution, it also prevents a full assessment of the model's potential. It is unclear whether the performance gap with current state-of-the-art systems is a limitation of the model's representational capacity or simply a consequence of the suboptimal search strategy. Including results with even a small beam would provide a much clearer picture of the model's strengths and weaknesses and allow for a fairer comparison with other top systems that universally employ beam search.
2.  **Performance Gap with State-of-the-Art:** The model, while strong compared to its contemporaries from 2008-2010, lags considerably behind more recent work on the CoNLL 2009 task (e.g., Lei et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015). The authors acknowledge this, arguing that those systems incorporate orthogonal advances. However, the performance difference is large enough to question the current model's competitiveness at the cutting edge. A stronger link to these recent advances, or a demonstration that a simple modification like beam search could close the gap, would strengthen the paper's impact.
3.  **Lack of Clarity on Algorithmic Novelty:** The paper builds heavily on the transition system of Henderson et al. (2013) but does not clearly summarize the differences beyond the addition of a single M-SELF transition. For readers not intimately familiar with the Henderson et al. system, it is difficult to isolate the novel algorithmic contributions from the novel neural modeling. A brief, explicit comparison of the transition sets would improve the paper's clarity and help properly credit the contributions.

### Questions to Authors
1.  As discussed in the weaknesses, could you provide results using beam search? This would be invaluable for understanding whether the performance is primarily limited by the search algorithm or the model itself.
2.  In Section 3.3, the composition function for predicates, `gd(v, p)`, uses a concatenation of the word vector `v` and the predicate sense vector `p`. Since a predicate sense like `run.01` is already tied to the lexeme `run`, does the sense vector `p` not already contain the relevant information from `v`? Could you clarify the motivation for this design choice, as it appears potentially redundant?
