- Presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency graphs using stack LSTMs and no hand-crafted features.  
- Evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009, outperforming prior joint models and matching strong pipelined systems while running in linear time and very fast in practice.  
- Main contribution: empirically shows stack LSTMs can learn a dense representation of the full parser state to enable effective greedy joint parsing decisions.  
- Main contribution: introduces an improved transition system (including M-SELF) and an interleaving strategy to map joint dependency graphs to a single action sequence.  
- Strength: strong empirical results with thorough experiments and ablations (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that quantify joint modeling benefits.  
- Strength: simple, powerful modeling paradigm that replaces heavy feature engineering and generalizes well across languages.  
- Strength: high future impact—transition system can be combined with better search (beam/global) or other neural architectures for further gains.  
- Weakness: paper lacks detailed description of the oracle/training procedure for converting gold graphs to transition sequences, hindering reproducibility.  
- Weakness: limited architectural novelty since stack LSTMs are adopted from prior work (Dyer et al., 2015).  
- Question for authors: please provide detailed oracle logic for generating gold transition sequences, and clarify whether the oracle is static or dynamic and how semantic action ambiguities (e.g., M-SWAP) are resolved.
