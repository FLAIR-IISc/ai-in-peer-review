- Presents a greedy, linear-time transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies by combining Henderson et al.'s transition system with Dyer et al.'s stack LSTM state representation, achieving strong CoNLL 2008/2009 results and fast runtime.

- Primary contribution: first strong empirical demonstration that stack LSTM state representations can effectively replace hand-crafted features in joint syntactic–semantic parsing.

- Secondary contribution: thorough empirical evaluation on CoNLL 2008/2009 with informative ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and clear reporting of speed and setup.

- Strength: sets a new state-of-the-art among joint models and is competitive with feature-rich pipeline systems while remaining greedy and feature-free.

- Strength: paper is clear, methodologically sound, and provides detailed descriptions and hyperparameters that aid reproducibility.

- Strength: provides deep analysis and ablation studies revealing interactions between syntax and semantics and diagnosing issues like out-of-domain overfitting.

- Weakness: limited methodological novelty since it integrates existing transition system and stack LSTM rather than proposing new algorithms or architectures.

- Weakness: reliance on high-quality pretrained embeddings (notably for Chinese) raises concerns that gains may partly stem from embeddings rather than the model itself; ablations across languages are missing.

- Weakness: joint model overfits in-domain and underperforms the hybrid pipeline on out-of-domain data (Brown corpus), suggesting tighter coupling may reduce robustness to domain shift.

- Question: please provide ablation results (with vs. without pretrained embeddings) for other languages to separate embedding effects from architectural benefits.

- Question: can the authors explain why the hybrid model generalizes better out-of-domain and whether alternative regularization or multi-task strategies could mitigate the joint model's overfitting?

- Question: how does the 1.5% oracle conversion failure for complex crossing semantic arcs limit semantic performance, and how does this oracle bound compare to that of the syntactic arc-eager parser?
