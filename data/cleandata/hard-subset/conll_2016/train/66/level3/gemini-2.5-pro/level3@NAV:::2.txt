**Summary & Contributions**

This paper proposes a joint syntactic and semantic transition-based dependency parser, building upon the framework of Henderson et al. (2008). The model's core idea is to use stack LSTMs to learn a single vectorial representation for the entire parser state (stacks and buffer), which then informs a greedy classifier for selecting the next transition. The primary contribution of this work is the effective application of this end-to-end neural architecture to the joint parsing task. This approach successfully encodes complex, long-range syntactic features useful for semantic role labeling without relying on any hand-crafted features, resulting in a system that is conceptually simple and computationally efficient.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective Representation Learning:** The main strength of this work is its demonstration that stack LSTMs can learn a comprehensive representation of the parser's state sufficient for joint syntactic-semantic analysis. By replacing traditional feature engineering with learned representations, the paper presents an elegant and modern approach to a complex problem.

2.  **Strong Grounding and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and builds substantially on established work in both transition-based parsing and recent neural network techniques. The authors clearly position their contributions relative to prior art.

3.  **Efficiency:** The model’s reliance on a greedy inference algorithm makes it very fast, a significant practical advantage over more complex systems that use beam search or global inference. This makes the proposed parser a potentially valuable tool for downstream applications where speed is critical.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Evaluation of Search Strategy:** The most significant weakness is the exclusive use of a greedy search algorithm. This makes it difficult to fairly compare the model's performance against contemporary systems that almost universally use beam search. Without a beam search baseline, it is impossible to know whether the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems is due to limitations in the model's representations or simply the sub-optimal search procedure. This is a critical omission for a parsing paper.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Sections:** Several sections require revision for clarity. Section 2 should explicitly summarize the differences between the proposed transition system and that of Henderson et al. to better highlight the paper's novelty. Further clarification on the role of specific semantic links (e.g., A1 vs. C-A1) would also improve readers' understanding of the task.

3.  **Questionable Design Choices and Organization:** In Section 3.3, the motivation for concatenating predicate word and sense vectors is unclear, as this appears redundant. Additionally, the organization of the experimental setup in Section 4 is confusing, as it does not clearly delineate the scope of the multilingual evaluation between the CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 subsections.
