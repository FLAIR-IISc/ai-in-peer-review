### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependency graphs. The model's key innovation is its use of stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire parser state—encompassing the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. This approach completely eschews hand-crafted features. The parser is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it is shown to outperform all previous joint parsing models and achieve performance competitive with strong pipelined systems of the era. A significant practical advantage is its linear-time complexity, making it very fast.

### Main Contributions
1.  **An Effective Demonstration of Representation Learning for Joint Parsing:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that a neural network, specifically a stack LSTM, can learn a powerful and sufficient representation of a complex joint parser state to make effective greedy decisions. This overcomes a major hurdle for previous joint models, which often struggled to define feature sets that could compete with the rich, global features available to pipelined systems.

2.  **An Improved Transition System for Joint Parsing:** The paper introduces a modified transition system based on Henderson et al. (2013) that is well-suited for this modeling approach. This includes a novel `M-SELF` action to handle nominal predicates and an effective interleaving strategy for syntactic and semantic actions, which together enable a clean mapping from joint dependency graphs to a single action sequence.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper presents convincing results that establish a new state-of-the-art for joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 benchmarks. The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, including valuable ablations (Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid) that clearly isolate and quantify the benefits of the joint modeling approach. The reported speed (177s for the CoNLL 2009 test set) is a significant practical advantage over more complex models.

2.  **A Simple and Powerful Modeling Paradigm:** The work successfully shows that the complex feature engineering that characterized earlier parsing systems can be replaced by a more general representation learning framework. This not only simplifies the model but also makes it more adaptable to new languages, as demonstrated by the strong multilingual results. The neural approach is also likely more parameter-efficient than a comparable linear model, which would require an explosion of feature conjunctions to capture joint syntactic-semantic interactions. Here, a shared hidden representation (`yt`) can capture these interactions more compactly.

3.  **High Potential for Future Impact:** The proposed transition system and parsing framework serve as a strong foundation for future work. The greedy search algorithm is a design choice, not a fundamental limitation. The core transition system could readily be combined with more powerful search techniques like beam search or integrated into different neural architectures (e.g., global models like Andor et al., 2016). This makes the paper's contribution a valuable building block that could lead to even stronger results when combined with such orthogonal improvements.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Clarity on Oracle Training Procedure:** The paper states that training instances are converted into transition sequences via a "deterministic operation," but the details of this oracle are not provided. The design of the training oracle is critical for the performance and reproducibility of a transition-based parser. It is unclear how ambiguities are resolved or how the oracle for semantic transitions (especially `M-SWAP`) is defined. This lack of detail makes it difficult to fully assess the mechanics of the system and reproduce the work.

2.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core neural component, the stack LSTM, is adopted directly from prior work by Dyer et al. (2015) on syntactic parsing. While the application to the more complex joint parsing task is novel and highly effective, the work is primarily an application of an existing technique rather than a fundamental advance in neural network architectures. This is a minor weakness, as the strong results and novel application are significant in their own right.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide more detail on the oracle used to generate the gold transition sequences from the training data? Specifically, how does the oracle decide the sequence of semantic actions (`M-LEFT`, `M-RIGHT`, `M-SWAP`, etc.) given the state of the stacks and buffer? Is this a static oracle that assumes the parser is always in a gold-standard configuration, or a dynamic oracle that can provide guidance from non-gold states? A description of the oracle logic, even in an appendix, would be very helpful.
