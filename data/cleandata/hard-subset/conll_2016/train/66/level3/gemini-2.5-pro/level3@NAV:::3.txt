This paper presents a transition-based neural network model for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. Using Stack LSTMs to represent the entire parser state (stacks and buffer), the model learns to make greedy parsing decisions without relying on hand-crafted features. The proposed system outperforms previous joint models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of modern representation learning for this challenging joint problem.

**Contributions:**
1. The primary contribution is a compelling empirical demonstration that a neural, transition-based approach can successfully model joint syntax and semantics. The work shows that learning dense representations of the parser state overcomes major hurdles that limited earlier linear models, effectively revitalizing this research direction.
2. A secondary contribution is a new transition system for joint parsing, which extends the work of Henderson et al. (2008) to better handle specific linguistic phenomena in the target datasets.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper successfully shows the power of neural methods for a task where joint modeling has historically struggled to outperform pipeline systems. By leveraging Stack LSTMs for automatic feature induction, the authors achieve state-of-the-art results among joint parsing systems, clearly illustrating the benefits of this approach.
2. The work implicitly highlights a critical advantage of neural networks for joint tasks: parameter efficiency. Compared to linear models that would require a combinatorial explosion of feature templates to model syntax-semantics interactions, the neural architecture learns compact, shared representations, making the problem more tractable.
3. The proposed framework is modular and promising for future research. The transition system is likely compatible with more sophisticated decoding methods, such as global beam-search (e.g., Andor et al., 2016). This work combined with orthogonal improvements could lead to models that close the gap with top pipeline systems.

**Weaknesses:**
1. A significant weakness is the lack of a formal dynamic oracle for the proposed transition system. This is standard for proving the completeness of a transition-based parser and is necessary for training with exploration. Its absence leaves the theoretical soundness of the algorithm underexplored.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Could you comment on the feasibility of deriving a dynamic oracle for your transition system? The logic seems related to an arc-eager oracle with specific semantic constraints. A formal oracle would strengthen the contribution and potentially improve performance through exploration-based training.
