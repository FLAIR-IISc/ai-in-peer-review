### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly models syntactic and semantic dependencies. The core of the model is its use of stack long short-term memories (LSTMs) to learn a single, dense vector representation of the entire parser state, including separate stacks for syntax and semantics, the buffer of upcoming words, and the history of actions taken. This approach obviates the need for hand-crafted, feature engineering. The parser employs a greedy, linear-time inference procedure. The authors evaluate their model on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, demonstrating that it outperforms all previous joint models and achieves competitive performance with many strong pipelined systems, while being significantly faster.

### Contributions
1.  **Effective Application of Neural Networks to Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parsing:** The primary contribution of this work is the strong empirical demonstration that a neural, transition-based model can succeed at the challenging task of joint parsing where previous linear models have often struggled to outperform pipelined approaches. By using stack LSTMs to learn representations of the complete parser state, the model effectively captures the complex interactions between syntactic and semantic decisions without relying on brittle, manually engineered features. This work provides a compelling proof-of-concept for the viability of end-to-end learned representations in this joint task.
2.  **An Efficient and Adaptable Joint Parsing Framework:** The paper proposes a simple and fast greedy parsing algorithm with linear-time complexity. This efficiency is a significant practical contribution, making the parser suitable for downstream applications. The model's success across multiple languages with minimal adaptation further highlights the strength of its representation learning approach, which automatically discovers relevant features rather than relying on language-specific engineering.
3.  **An Improved Transition System for Joint Parsing:** The paper introduces an updated transition system for joint parsing, adapting the arc-eager syntactic algorithm and integrating a new `M-SELF` action to handle self-dependencies common in nominal predicates. This system, while an incremental improvement over Henderson et al. (2013), is a necessary component that enables the neural model to handle the full complexity of the task.

### Strengths
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Among Joint Models:** The paper's strongest argument for acceptance is its empirical results. The model achieves the best reported scores among all joint parsing systems on the CoN_LL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. It significantly surpasses the performance of the Henderson et al. (2013) system, from which it inherits its core algorithmic structure, providing clear evidence for the superiority of its learned state representations over more locally-scoped, feature-based models.
2.  **Model Simplicity and Efficiency:** The greedy, linear-time nature of the parser is a major advantage. In an era of increasingly complex and computationally expensive models, a simple yet powerful approach is highly valuable. The authors provide a compelling speed comparison, showing their system is 2.5 times faster than a recent state-of-the-art pipelined system. This efficiency, combined with the lack of manual feature engineering, makes the system practical and easily adaptable.
3.  **Insightful Ablation Studies:** The experiments on the CoNLL 2009 data, which compare the full joint model to Syntax-only, Semantics-only, and Hybrid variants, are well-designed and provide valuable insights. They clearly quantify the benefit of including syntactic information for semantic parsing (Joint vs. Semantics-only) and offer an interesting finding regarding domain adaptation, where the pipelined Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model on out-of-domain data, suggesting the joint model is more prone to overfitting on training set-specific correlations.
4.  **A Strong Foundation for Future Research:** This work successfully revitalizes the idea of joint transition-based parsing. The proposed architecture is modular and opens up several avenues for future work. The transition system and state representation could be combined with more powerful search methods like beam search or integrated into globally normalized models, which could help close the remaining performance gap with the best pipelined systems.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Performance Gap with Top Pipelined Systems:** While the model is the best *joint* parser, it does not achieve state-of-the-art performance on the overall task when compared to the top *pipelined* systems (e.g., Täckström et al., 2015; Roth and Woodsend, 2014). These systems leverage features from a complete syntactic parse and often use global inference or reranking. The paper acknowledges this gap, but it remains a significant limitation. The promise of joint modeling is to overcome the error propagation of pipelines, yet here the pipeline systems remain superior.
2.  **Reliance on a Static Oracle:** The model is trained using a static oracle that deterministically converts a gold-standard parse into a single correct sequence of transitions. This methodology suffers from exposure bias: the model is never trained on states resulting from its own mistakes. Transition-based parsers often benefit from training with a dynamic oracle, which teaches the model how to recover from errors. The lack of a dynamic oracle may be limiting the model's performance, and its development for this complex, interleaved transition system is a non-trivial but important challenge that is not addressed.
3.  **Limited Novelty in the Transition System:** The paper presents its transition system as a key contribution. However, the system is largely an adaptation of the arc-eager algorithm to the joint framework of Henderson et al. (2013), with the main addition being the `M-SELF` transition. While functional and necessary, this is an incremental modification rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. The contribution here is more in the successful application of a neural model to this system than in the design of the system itself.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Your training relies on a static oracle. Have you considered the development of a dynamic oracle for this joint system? What specific challenges do you foresee in defining optimal actions for non-gold states, especially given the interleaved nature of the syntactic and semantic transitions?
2.  The out-of-domain experiments show that the Hybrid model outperforms the Joint model, suggesting the Joint model overfits to the WSJ training data. Could you elaborate on why joint training might exacerbate this issue? Is the model learning spurious correlations between syntactic and semantic phenomena that are specific to the source domain?
3.  Your work demonstrates the power of greedy inference with rich, learned representations. Do you believe that incorporating beam search into your parser could close the performance gap with the top-performing pipelined systems, or are more fundamental changes, such as access to global syntactic features, necessary?
