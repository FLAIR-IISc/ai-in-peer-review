- Paper proposes a transition-based joint syntactic+semantic dependency parser using stack LSTMs to learn full parser-state representations, with greedy linear-time decoding; evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009, outperforming prior joint models but below recent SOTA.

- Contribution: extends stack LSTM architecture to joint syntactic and semantic parsing, replacing extensive hand-crafted features with learned state representations.

- Contribution: presents an efficient, simple linear-time greedy joint parser that is substantially faster (≈2.5×) than a recent strong system.

- Contribution: provides ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) showing mutual benefits of joint modeling, especially in-domain.

- Strength: effectively uses learned representations to manage multiple stacks and interleaved transitions, outperforming previous feature-based joint models.

- Strength: achieves impressive accuracy despite using purely greedy decoding, indicating rich local state representations.

- Strength: emphasizes efficiency and simplicity, offering a practical, fast alternative to more complex systems.

- Strength: clear writing, well-explained architecture, thorough multilingual experiments and model variants.

- Weakness: relies exclusively on greedy decoding with no beam-search results, leaving unclear whether search or model capacity limits performance.

- Weakness: lags behind more recent state-of-the-art CoNLL 2009 systems, raising questions about competitiveness without additional advances.

- Weakness: insufficiently clear algorithmic novelty over Henderson et al. (2013); differences in transition sets (beyond M-SELF) are not explicitly summarized.

- Question: please provide results using beam search to assess whether search or model capacity is the bottleneck.

- Question: clarify motivation for concatenating the word vector and predicate sense vector gd(v,p), since sense vectors may already capture lexeme information.
