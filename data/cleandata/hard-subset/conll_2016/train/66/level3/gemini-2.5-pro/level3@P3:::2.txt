### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a transition-based parser that performs joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model is heavily inspired by the incremental framework of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) but modernizes it by incorporating stack Long Short-Term Memory networks (stack LSTMs). The central idea is that stack LSTMs can learn a dense, vectorial representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), thereby eliminating the need for hand-crafted, discrete features. The authors claim two main contributions: the use of these vectorial state representations and the use of a simple greedy inference algorithm, which makes the parser very fast. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL-2008 and CoNLL-2009 shared task datasets, where it outperforms previous joint models like Henderson et al. (2013) but lags behind more recent, feature-rich pipeline systems.

### Contributions

1.  **Effective Application of Stack LSTMs for Joint Syntactic-Semantic Parsing:** The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of stack LSTMs to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The paper demonstrates that a continuous vector representation of the complete parser state can effectively learn the long-range and structural information (e.g., syntactic paths between predicates and arguments) necessary for semantic role labeling. This obviates the need for traditional, manually engineered feature sets, which is a significant step forward for this class of models.
2.  **A Fast, Feature-Free Joint Parser:** The paper presents a system that is both simple and efficient. By relying entirely on learned representations and a greedy decoding strategy, the parser achieves linear-time complexity. The reported speed is a practical advantage, and the fact that it achieves respectable performance without feature engineering makes the approach compelling and potentially more adaptable to different languages and domains.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Integration of Modern Neural Techniques:** The paper does an excellent job of building upon recent advances, particularly the stack LSTM model from Dyer et al. (2015), and applying them to the more challenging joint parsing task from Henderson et al. (2013). The motivation is clear: using the representational power of LSTMs to implicitly capture the complex state features that are difficult to design by hand. The empirical results, showing a clear improvement over the Henderson et al. baseline, validate this architectural choice.
2.  **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Features:** A major strength of the proposed model is its ability to perform well without any discrete, hand-crafted features. This is a significant advantage over many previous state-of-the-art systems that relied on complex and computationally expensive feature sets derived from the full syntactic tree. This feature-free approach not only simplifies the model but also enhances its generalizability, as demonstrated by the competitive multilingual results.
3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The parser's design, centered on a greedy, linear-time inference algorithm, makes it very fast. The reported end-to-end runtime is impressive and stands in contrast to slower, more complex pipeline models or models requiring global inference. This efficiency makes the system a practical choice for downstream applications where both speed and quality are important.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the transition system, the stack LSTM architecture, and the experimental setup, placing their work firmly within the context of related research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparability Due to Greedy Inference:** The most significant weakness is the exclusive reliance on a greedy search algorithm for evaluation. While speed is an advantage, comparing a greedy model to prior work that uses beam search (including the Henderson et al. systems this work builds upon) is not an apples-to-apples comparison. It is unclear how much of the performance gap with state-of-the-art systems is due to the model's representational capacity versus the impoverished search. Reporting results with even a small beam search would be necessary to make a stronger claim about the model's effectiveness and to fairly assess its standing in the literature.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Sections:** Certain parts of the paper need clarification to strengthen the contributions and improve reader understanding.
    *   **Section 2 (Task Definition):** The paper should provide more clarity on the semantic dependencies. For instance, the example in Figure 1 shows an A1 link, while the text mentions that nominal predicates can be their own arguments (e.g., with C-A1). For readers not deeply familiar with the CoNLL-2009 task, the distinction and potential interaction between these argument types are unclear.
    *   **Section 2 (Novelty of Transitions):** While the paper is based on Henderson et al. (2013), it introduces at least one new transition (M-SELF) and modifies others (e.g., copy vs. move semantics for S-SHIFT). The paper would be strengthened by a concise summary of all differences between the transition system used here and the one in the original work to better delineate the paper's algorithmic novelty.
3.  **Questionable Model Design Choice and Organization:**
    *   **Section 3.3 (Composition Function):** The composition function for predicates, `gd(v, p) = tanh(Zd[v;p] + ed)`, concatenates the vector for the word `v` with the vector for its disambiguated sense `p`. Since the sense (e.g., `expect.01`) already contains the lemma (`expect`), this concatenation appears redundant. The motivation for this design choice should be explained or justified.
    *   **Section 4 (Experimental Setup):** The organization of subsections 4.1 and 4.2 is confusing. Hyperparameter details for multiple languages are presented under the CoNLL-2008 section, while the multilingual evaluation is primarily a CoNLL-2009 task. The ablation models (e.g., Semantics-only) are described in the CoNLL-2009 section but seem generally applicable. Reorganizing this section would improve readability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The decision to use a purely greedy search makes direct comparison with many prior systems difficult. Could you provide results using beam search? This would help disentangle the effects of the search algorithm from the representational power of your stack LSTM-based model and provide a fairer comparison to previous work.
2.  In your predicate composition function `gd` (Section 3.3), you concatenate the word vector `v` with the sense vector `p`. Could you clarify the motivation for this, as the sense label seems to already contain the predicate lemma information present in the word vector?
3.  Could you add a brief summary to Section 2 that explicitly lists the differences between your transition system and the one used by Henderson et al. (2013)? This would help clarify the algorithmic contributions of your work.
4.  To aid readers unfamiliar with the task, could you briefly clarify in Section 2 the role of core arguments (e.g., A1) versus continuation arguments (e.g., C-A1) and whether they can co-occur?
