**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a transition-based parser for joint syntactic and semantic dependency parsing. The model is inspired by the incremental, synchronized algorithm of Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) but makes a significant departure in its modeling approach. Instead of relying on hand-crafted features extracted from the parser state, it uses stack LSTMs to learn a dense vector representation of the entire state, including the syntactic stack, semantic stack, input buffer, and action history. The parsing decisions are made by a classifier that operates on this learned state representation using a purely greedy inference algorithm. The system is evaluated on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 shared tasks, where it outperforms previous joint models and demonstrates competitive performance against strong pipeline systems of that era, while being significantly faster.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A successful application of stack LSTMs to the complex task of joint syntactic and semantic parsing. The key finding is that learned, continuous-space representations of the full parser state can effectively replace the need for discrete, hand-engineered syntactic features (such as predicate-argument paths) that have historically been crucial for high-performance semantic role labeling. This provides a compelling proof-of-concept for end-to-end representation learning in this domain.
2.  The development of a fast and effective greedy joint parser. By combining the stack LSTM architecture with a simple greedy search, the authors present a model that is linear in time and achieves strong results without the computational overhead of beam search or complex feature extraction, making it a valuable and practical baseline for future work in neural joint parsing.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Model:** The paper's core strength lies in its clean and powerful modeling approach. By replacing the complex, manually-designed feature templates of traditional transition-based parsers with a learned representation of the entire parser state, the model provides a more general and less brittle solution. The use of stack LSTMs is a well-motivated choice that naturally captures the recursive structure of the parser's data structures and allows information from deep within the stacks to influence parsing decisions. This demonstrates a significant step forward from earlier joint models that relied on more local state information.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance Against Prior Joint Models:** The experimental results clearly show the effectiveness of the proposed method. The model significantly outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013), upon which its transition system is based, as well as all other joint parsing models on the CoNLL 2008 and 2009 English shared tasks. This provides convincing evidence that the learned state representations are superior to the feature-based approaches used in previous joint systems.

3.  **High Efficiency:** The parser is exceptionally fast due to its greedy, linear-time inference process and the avoidance of expensive feature extraction. The reported end-to-end parsing time (177.6s for the CoNLL 2009 test set) is a major practical advantage over slower pipeline systems that rely on global features or reranking. This efficiency makes the model highly suitable for real-world applications where speed is a critical factor.

4.  **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. It effectively motivates the problem, explains the technical details of the model (including the transition system and the stack LSTM architecture), and situates the work within the relevant literature. The ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) are insightful and help to disentangle the contributions of the joint learning framework.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Exclusive Reliance on Greedy Inference:** The most significant weakness is the sole use of a greedy decoding algorithm. While this ensures speed, it is well-known that greedy search can be prone to error propagation. Nearly all state-of-the-art parsing systems (both syntactic and semantic) employ beam search to explore a larger search space and mitigate early decision errors. By not reporting results with a beam, the paper presents an incomplete picture of the model's full potential. It is difficult to determine whether the performance gap compared to more recent state-of-the-art systems is a limitation of the model architecture itself or simply a consequence of the suboptimal search strategy. This makes a fair comparison to other top systems challenging.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Transition System Novelty:** The paper states that the transition system "largely follow[s]" Henderson et al. (2013), with the main addition being the `M-SELF` action. However, a more explicit summary of the differences would be beneficial. For instance, the paper notes that `S-SHIFT` and `S-RIGHT` *copy* an item from the buffer rather than moving it, a subtle but important detail. A consolidated description of all modifications to the base algorithm would help readers better isolate the contributions of the neural representation learner from those of the underlying transition system.

3.  **Confusing Experimental Section Structure:** The organization of Section 4 is somewhat confusing. Section 4.1 is titled "CoNLL 2008" but discusses hyperparameters (e.g., embeddings for Chinese, German, Spanish) that are only relevant for the multilingual CoNLL 2009 task. Section 4.2, titled "CoNLL 2009", focuses almost exclusively on the English dataset and the model variants. The multilingual results are then presented later in Section 5. Reorganizing the experimental setup to more clearly separate the datasets and their respective configurations would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The decision to use a purely greedy inference algorithm is a major limitation for comparing the model's capabilities against other state-of-the-art systems. Have you experimented with beam search? Given the known benefits of beam search in transition-based parsing, providing these results would offer a much clearer view of the upper bound of your model's performance and help disentangle the effects of the model architecture from the search algorithm.

2.  Could you please elaborate on the key differences between the transition system used here and the one in Henderson et al. (2013)? A brief summary table or list comparing the actions and constraints would be very helpful for understanding the specific algorithmic contributions of your work beyond the introduction of the `M-SELF` transition.

3.  In Section 3.3, the composition function for predicates `gd(v, p)` is an affine transformation of the concatenation of the word vector `v` and a learned sense vector `p`. Since predicate senses are lexeme-specific (e.g., `expect.01`), the word vector `v` (for `expect`) seems redundant when the sense vector `p` (for `expect.01`) is already present. Could you clarify the motivation for including both in the composition? Have you explored using only the sense embedding?

4.  For readers less familiar with the specifics of the CoNLL 2009 task, could you briefly clarify the distinction between core arguments (e.g., `A1`) and continuation arguments (e.g., `C-A1`) that appear in the example figure? This would improve the self-containedness of the paper.
