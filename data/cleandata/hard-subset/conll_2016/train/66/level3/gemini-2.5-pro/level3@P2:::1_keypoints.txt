- Presents a transition-based joint syntactic-semantic parser combining Henderson et al.'s synchronized transition system with Dyer et al.'s Stack LSTM to learn dense parser-state representations.  
- The parser is greedy with linear-time complexity and achieves new state-of-the-art results among joint models on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English).  

Contributions:
- Successfully applies Stack LSTM to joint syntactic-semantic dependency parsing, removing the need for hand-crafted features.  
- Provides a fast, accurate joint parser that outperforms prior joint models and serves as a strong practical baseline.  

Strengths:
- Empirically strong: significantly outperforms previous joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English.  
- Demonstrates that representation learning can replace complex feature engineering in greedy transition systems.  
- Efficient and practical: greedy linear-time parser with a fast end-to-end runtime.  
- Clear writing, solid experimental design, useful ablation studies, and commitment to release code for reproducibility.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: mainly a combination of two existing techniques rather than a new algorithm or architecture.  
- Still behind pipelined state-of-the-art on absolute semantic F1, which use richer syntactic features and global inference.  
- Possible over-reliance on high-quality/specialized embeddings (e.g., structured skipgram for Chinese), raising questions about robustness.  

Questions for authors:
- Can you report English results using standard embeddings (GloVe, fastText) to isolate the model's contribution from embedding quality?  
- Which pipelined techniques (global features, reranking, beam search) could be integrated into the Stack LSTM framework to close the gap with pipelined SOTA, and what challenges would that entail?
