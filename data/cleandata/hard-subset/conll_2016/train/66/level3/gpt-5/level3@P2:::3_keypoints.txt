- Paper introduces a greedy, linear-time transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic dependency trees and SRL graphs using a stack-LSTM encoding of full parser state.
- Key empirical contribution: a feature-free, greedy joint parser that achieves state-of-the-art among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English and is substantially faster than pipeline baselines.
- Representation contribution: demonstrates that compact neural representations of the entire parser state (stack-LSTMs) replace hand-engineered local features and improve parameter efficiency and accuracy.
- Transition-system contribution: designs a synchronized transition system (including M-SWAP and new M-SELF) that is simple, linear-time, and effective across languages.
- Strength: Strong joint-model results with compelling runtime (≈178s single-core for CoNLL 2009 English), ~2.5× faster than a strong pipeline.
- Strength: Clean, feature-free architecture that encodes full algorithm state and overcomes limits of locally scoped handcrafted features.
- Strength: Careful ablation study (semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, joint) showing syntax helps SRL and joint training benefits in-domain performance.
- Strength: Practical transition set and engineering covering key SRL phenomena (crossing arcs, nominal self-arguments) in a compact linear-time algorithm.
- Strength: Representation and transition design are compatible with more powerful training/inference regimes, enabling future improvements.
- Weakness: Still lags the very best pipeline/structured SRL systems that use rich syntactic features, global inference, reranking, or multi-resource training.
- Weakness: Greedy, locally normalized decisions risk exposure bias; no beam search or global training explored.
- Weakness: Semantic oracle and transition space are incomplete (≈1.5% of sentences unencodable), introducing training noise that is not quantified.
- Weakness: Joint training may overfit in-domain data (hybrid outperforms joint on CoNLL 2009 Brown); limited regularization/domain-robustness analysis.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of parameter efficiency and the contents of learned representations; no quantitative or qualitative probing provided.
- Question: Can a dynamic oracle be derived for the synchronized transition system to reduce exposure bias and improve out-of-domain generalization?
- Question: Have beam search or globally normalized training methods (CRF loss, early updates, max-violation) been tried, and what obstacles exist?
- Question: What proportion of training arcs are unencodable due to crossing constraints per language, and how much dev performance is lost if those cases are filtered?
- Question: How does defaulting unseen predicates to “01” generalize to other languages (e.g., German, Chinese) and OOV predicate-sense behavior?
- Question: How sensitive is the model to POS/lemma errors—what are SRL/syntax deltas with gold vs. predicted tags/lemmas?
- Question: Were morphological features or character-based embeddings explored for morphologically rich languages (Czech/Spanish/German), and what was their effect?
- Additional comment: Include significance tests for key comparisons (joint vs. hybrid; joint vs. prior joint and pipeline baselines).
- Additional comment: Provide per-label SRL F1 and error breakdowns (argument span/head errors, predicate senses) to clarify where joint inference helps or hurts.
- Additional comment: For reproducibility, release code with hyperparameter ranges, random seeds, training times per language, and pretrained embeddings used.
