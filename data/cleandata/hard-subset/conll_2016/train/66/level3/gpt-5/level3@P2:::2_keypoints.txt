- Paper: joint transition-based parser using stack LSTMs for syntax+SRL, greedy linear-time inference, with M-SELF for semantic self-dependencies.
- Contribution 1: Shows learned continuous joint parser state via stack LSTMs can replace hand-crafted features and yields state-of-the-art joint-model performance on CoNLL-2008/2009 (multilingual).
- Contribution 2: Engineering: simple, fast, greedy joint parser with linear-time feature extraction and controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) to probe joint benefits.
- Contribution 3: Modeling: integrates four stack LSTMs (syntax, semantics, buffer, action history) in a synchronized transition system and adds M-SELF for nominal self-arguments.

Strengths
- Empirical: Consistently outperforms prior joint approaches on English CoNLL-2008/2009 and matches top systems in several languages.
- Multilingual generalization: Strong cross-language results shown.
- Speed/practicality: Fully greedy decoding is ~2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline under comparable settings.
- Minimal feature engineering: Removes need for path/global syntactic features while maintaining competitive SRL accuracy.
- Experimental design/reproducibility: Informative baselines, ablations, extensive hyperparameter/training details, and promised open-source code.

Weaknesses
- Search/inference: No beam-search or globally normalized comparisons to quantify accuracy–speed trade-offs.
- Novelty clarity: Insufficient concise comparison to Henderson et al. (2008/2013) on transition inventory, constraints, and interleaving policy.
- Architecture/ablations: Key design choices (predicate composition, label dims, M-SELF, dependence on pretrained embeddings, action-history LSTM) lack thorough ablation and justification.
- Comparability/upper bounds: Missing oracle/upper-bound analyses and explicit quantification of gaps due to decoding vs feature availability.
- Presentation: Sections and examples (A1 vs C-A1, Section 4 organization, Section 2.2 copy vs move) are unclear and hinder readability.

Questions to authors
- Search: What accuracy/speed trade-off do modest beam sizes (e.g., 4, 8) or global normalization yield vs greedy?
- Transition differences: Please provide a concise table/figure comparing your transition system to Henderson et al., including copy vs move and interleaving policy.
- A1 vs C-A1: Why are both A1 and C-A1 present in the example, what is their intended semantics, and how does the system prevent duplication?
- Predicate composition: Why use concatenation for gd(v,p)? Any ablations with gating, bilinear/tensor, residuals, or different label embeddings?
- Oracle/upper bound: How often does the semantic oracle fail due to crossing arcs and what are per-language oracle F1/coverage numbers?
- Pretrained embeddings: How sensitive are results to pretrained embedding quality/size, particularly for Chinese and low-resource languages, and results without pretraining?
- Error analysis: Can you provide breakdowns by error type (argument head attachment vs label confusions, long-distance arguments, nominal predicates)?

Additional comments
- Report joint training time vs pipeline training time and confirm hardware/software parity used in speed comparisons.
- Clearly state when competing systems use extra resources (FrameNet, etc.) to ensure fair comparisons.
- Briefly explain why copying from the buffer (rather than moving) is critical when interleaving transitions to clarify Section 2.2.
