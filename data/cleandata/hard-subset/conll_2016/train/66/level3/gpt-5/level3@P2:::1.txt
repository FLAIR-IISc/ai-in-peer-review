Summary
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that adapts Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system and replaces hand-crafted features and beam search with learned representations of the full parser state using stack LSTMs (Dyer et al.). The model performs greedy, linear-time inference and learns vector compositions for partial syntactic and semantic structures, including a new M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the system achieves the best results among joint models, and competitive semantic role labeling (SRL) performance relative to strong pipeline systems, while being substantially faster. Multilingual results are generally on par with top shared-task systems. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) quantify the contribution of joint modeling.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A fast, greedy joint dependency parser that learns a representation of the entire algorithmic state via stack LSTMs and achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint syntax–semantics models on CoNLL 2008/2009, with strong runtime efficiency.
- Secondary: An evidence-based analysis of joint vs. pipelined settings through semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid variants, demonstrating when joint training helps and where it overfits (in-/out-of-domain).
- Tertiary: Practical extensions of the transition system for the joint setting, including interleaving of syntactic/semantic actions and the M-SELF transition for nominal predicate self-arcs, plus a robust treatment of predicate sense disambiguation within the transition system.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance among joint parsers and competitive with pipelines: The proposed model consistently outperforms prior joint models (e.g., Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. While not surpassing the very best feature-engineered pipelines, it reaches comparable semantic performance with a simpler, unified approach.
- Runtime efficiency with linear-time greedy inference: The model achieves end-to-end parsing roughly 2.5× faster than a strong recent SRL pipeline under identical hardware/settings, making it attractive for practical deployment without sacrificing much accuracy.
- Clear ablations and careful experimental design: The semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid variants are well-chosen to isolate the value of joint modeling and the availability of syntactic signals. The reported trends (joint > hybrid in-domain; hybrid > joint out-of-domain) are informative and honest about generalization.
- Solid, well-justified modeling choices and implementation: The system description is precise, constraints are clearly enumerated, and the composition functions for syntactic, semantic, and predicate-sense nodes are well motivated. Hyperparameters and training details (dropout, learning rates, pretrained embeddings) are explained sufficiently for reproduction.
- Well-written with strong related work coverage: The exposition is lucid, the transition system is easy to follow, and the paper situates the work appropriately among joint, pipeline, and neural SRL approaches, highlighting orthogonal advances that could be integrated.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core ideas—Henderson-style joint transitions plus Dyer-style stack LSTMs—are known; the contribution is a thoughtful integration rather than a new algorithmic paradigm. The new M-SELF action and interleaving constraints are practical but incremental.
- Confounding effect of pretrained embeddings (especially Chinese): The multilingual gains—most notably in Chinese—appear to depend strongly on high-quality pretrained embeddings. Without a comprehensive analysis across languages (with/without pretraining), it is hard to separate the benefits of the modeling approach from representation quality.
- Joint overfitting out of domain: The hybrid model outperforming joint on Brown suggests the joint training overfits WSJ distributions. The paper notes this but does not explore remedies (e.g., domain-adaptive regularization, data augmentation, or modest beam search to reduce myopic decisions).
- Gap to best-in-class SRL pipelines remains: The model trails top systems that use global inference, rich syntax-derived features, or additional annotations (e.g., FrameNet). While many techniques are orthogonal, the lack of experiments integrating even a subset (e.g., simple global constraints or limited beam search) limits the case for joint learning as a superior practical alternative.
- Oracle/transition coverage limitations and analysis: The semantic oracle is imperfect due to crossing arcs, and ~1.5% of English sentences cannot be perfectly encoded with the transition sequence. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the downstream impact or provide an error analysis of these failures.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are the English and multilingual results to the presence/quality of pretrained embeddings? Can you report full results across all languages with and without pretraining, and quantify the delta?
- Did you try small beams or dynamic oracles? Even a tiny beam (e.g., 4–8) may mitigate greediness without sacrificing much speed. Any indicative results?
- Can you quantify the impact of the imperfect semantic oracle on final SRL F1? Are the affected sentences concentrated in particular phenomena (e.g., heavy crossing)?
- For CoNLL 2008, how is the predicate identification LSTM integrated with the parser? Is error propagation analyzed (e.g., PI errors vs. SRL errors)?
- What is the training stability across random seeds? Any notable variance in performance, especially for the joint model?

Additional comments
- The multilingual section would benefit from lightweight morphological features or character-based embeddings to better reflect performance in morphologically rich languages.
- A brief qualitative error analysis (e.g., typical joint wins and losses vs. hybrid) would strengthen the narrative around when joint modeling helps.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid, well-executed integration with strong empirical results among joint systems and excellent efficiency. Despite modest methodological novelty, the contribution is valuable and timely, and the paper is clearly written and reproducible. I lean toward acceptance.
