Summary
This paper revisits joint syntactic–semantic dependency parsing with a neural transition-based parser that uses stack LSTMs to represent the full algorithmic state and performs greedy, linear-time inference. The authors introduce a transition system inspired by Henderson et al. (2008/2013), augmented for semantic self-dependencies and interleaved syntax/semantics decisions, and show strong results on CoNLL 2008–2009, especially among joint models, with competitive speed.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical contribution: a clean, convincing demonstration that neural representations of the full joint parsing state substantially outperform prior joint models that relied on locally scoped or linear-feature models; this clarifies that automatic feature learning breaks a long-standing bottleneck in joint parsing and is more parameter-efficient than linear models for joint tasks (fewer explicit feature/class weights).
2) Algorithmic contribution: a refined joint transition system that improves on Henderson (2008) by interleaving syntax/semantics, adding M-SELF for self-arguments, and enabling efficient greedy decoding; the design is likely compatible with other neural architectures and training paradigms.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over prior joint systems on CoNLL 2008–2009, including ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that isolate where joint modeling helps; the Brown vs. WSJ analysis is informative.
- Strong engineering: linear-time greedy decoding with end-to-end speed competitive with or faster than pipelines, making the approach practically attractive.
- Sound architectural choice: full-state stack-LSTM encoding removes expensive expert-crafted features while preserving necessary context; evidence suggests better parameter efficiency than linear models in joint settings.
- Broad applicability: the transition system appears compatible with global/beam-search training (e.g., Andor et al., 2016) and other orthogonal improvements (character/morphology, contextual embeddings, cost-augmented decoding), suggesting a viable path to state-of-the-art.

Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of training signals: purely static supervision without a dynamic oracle risks exposure bias, and overfitting is visible out-of-domain (Hybrid > Joint on Brown).
- Transition-system analysis is light: it is unclear how much each addition (e.g., M-SELF, interleaving constraints) contributes; no ablation of transition inventory.
- Comparisons to strong modern non-neural or globally trained baselines are indirect; the paper stops short of testing beam/global training, which could clarify whether gains stem from representation or search.

Questions to Authors
- Can you derive and include a dynamic oracle for your transition set? The M-SHIFT/M-REDUCE/M-LEFT/M-RIGHT actions resemble an arc-eager oracle with selective exclusion of semantic arcs; what obstacles remain?
- How compatible is your system with global normalization/beam search (e.g., Andor 2016)? Any preliminary results?
- Can you provide ablations on M-SELF and the syntax–semantics interleaving constraint? How much do they each contribute to accuracy and speed?
