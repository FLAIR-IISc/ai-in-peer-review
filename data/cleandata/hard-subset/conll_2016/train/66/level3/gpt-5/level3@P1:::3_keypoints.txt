- Presents a fast, greedy, transition-based parser using stack LSTMs that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies, matching pipelines while running in linear time.
- Empirically shows full-state neural representations plus greedy inference enable accurate, fast joint syntactic–semantic parsing without hand-crafted features, outperforming prior joint models across languages.
- Introduces a practical joint transition system and training recipe (copy-on-shift, interleaving schedule, constraints, M-SELF) that makes linear-time joint decoding simple to implement.
- Provides evidence that learned representations can replace expensive syntactic-path features for SRL, supported by ablation studies (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint).
- Strong empirical gains over prior joint models and competitive performance vs. pipeline baselines with substantially faster runtime.
- Full-state stack-LSTM removes hand engineering and costly feature extraction, reducing runtime and implementation effort.
- Clear ablations clarify sources of gains and show joint modeling does not harm syntax, with syntax helping semantics on WSJ.
- Practical and scalable: linear-time inference including feature extraction, low memory footprint, and suitable end-to-end runtimes.
- Multilingual applicability: competitive results across multiple CoNLL 2009 languages under shared-task constraints.
- Limited novelty in transition system and learning paradigm—mostly builds on prior transitions and standard stack-LSTMs with modest extensions.
- Greedy decoding without beam search, global normalization, or exploration risks error propagation and limits semantic F1 and out-of-domain robustness.
- Imperfect semantic oracle and underspecified training details; unclear how oracle ceiling and single-run variance affect results.
- Comparisons to strongest pipelines are partial; orthogonal advances (morphology, global decoding, reranking) and broader runtime comparisons are not explored.
- Limited analysis of design choices such as the fixed interleaving schedule, copy-on-shift vs. move, M-SWAP frequency, and predicate-sense heuristics.
- Can a dynamic oracle be defined for this joint transition set (including M-SELF and copy-on-shift), or for a restricted subset?
- Have you tried beam search or globally normalized training, and what are the speed/accuracy trade-offs or incompatibilities?
- How sensitive is performance to the interleaving policy, and what is the measured benefit of copy-on-shift vs. move?
- What proportion of gold semantic arcs are unrealizable by your transitions, and what is the oracle upper bound on F1?
- Please report mean/std across multiple random seeds and statistical significance versus prior joint models.
- Did you explore character/morphological embeddings for morphologically rich languages (e.g., Czech) to compensate for missing morphological features?
- Approach should be compatible with global beam-search training and other neural enhancements to reach top-tier SRL without sacrificing speed.
- Neural model offers parameter efficiency versus large sparse-feature linear models, which benefits deployment.
