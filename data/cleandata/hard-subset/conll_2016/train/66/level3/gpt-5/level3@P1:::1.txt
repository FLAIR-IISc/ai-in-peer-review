Summary:
The paper presents a transition-based joint parser that produces syntactic dependency trees and semantic role graphs with a single, greedy, linear-time inference procedure. It adopts the synchronized transition system of Henderson et al. for joint syntax/semantics and replaces expert-crafted feature templates with neural representations of the full parser state via stack LSTMs (Dyer et al.). The authors add an M-SELF transition to handle semantic self-dependencies and interleave syntactic and semantic actions over a shared buffer. Extensive experiments on CoNLL 2008 and 2009 (English and multilingual) show the model outperforms prior joint parsers and is competitive with pipelines that use richer, more expensive features, while being substantially faster.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance):
1) Empirical demonstration that learning a dense representation of the entire joint parser state enables accurate and fast joint syntax–semantics parsing with greedy inference, surpassing prior joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 and approaching pipeline systems.
2) Engineering/algorithmic refinements to the joint transition system (e.g., the M-SELF action and copy-based buffer interaction) that broaden coverage (self-arguments) and maintain synchronization without expert features, coupled with a thorough ablation (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and multilingual evaluation.
3) Practical contribution: a simple, efficient, end-to-end system (linear-time, CPU-friendly) that removes hand-crafted features and promises open-source release.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results for joint parsing: The model consistently improves over prior joint approaches (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. The gains are meaningful given those models were long-standing baselines for joint learning. Moreover, performance is close to feature-rich pipelines while using only greedy decoding and no global reranking.
- Clear architectural motivation and execution: Replacing hand-engineered features with stack LSTMs that summarize S/M/B/A structures is well justified and implemented cleanly. The paper carefully explains interleaving of syntactic and semantic transitions and constraints to ensure validity, making the model reproducible.
- Informative ablations and baselines: The syntax-only, semantics-only, and hybrid variants provide concrete evidence about the value of joint learning (e.g., joint > hybrid in-domain, semantics-only < both), and the syntactic component is competitive with strong neural baselines. This helps isolate where gains come from.
- Efficiency and practicality: The greedy, linear-time decoder and end-to-end runtime (∼3 minutes on a single core for CoNLL 2009 English) make the approach appealing for real-world usage, especially compared to pipelines with expensive feature extraction and decoding.
- Breadth and quality of evaluation: The paper includes CoNLL 2008 (with predicate identification), CoNLL 2009 English and multilingual results, and clearly states preprocessing (pseudo-projectivization), hyperparameters, and training details. The related work section is comprehensive and fair, situating the contribution appropriately.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: The work combines a known joint transition system (Henderson et al.) with stack LSTMs (Dyer et al.) and largely reuses existing ideas (e.g., composition functions, action-history LSTM). While the integration is valuable and non-trivial, there is little conceptual innovation in modeling or learning beyond the M-SELF transition and the particular interleaving strategy.
- Overfitting and domain robustness: The joint model underperforms the hybrid variant out-of-domain (Brown), suggesting sensitivity to training-domain syntax–semantics correlations. There is limited analysis of robustness or techniques (e.g., regularization, domain adaptation) to mitigate this.
- Dependence on pretrained embeddings (especially Chinese): Multilingual results indicate that Chinese performance benefits notably from high-quality pretrained embeddings; without them, performance drops to parity with prior systems. This raises the question of how much of the multilingual gains come from representation learning vs. the transition architecture itself.
- Incomplete oracle for semantic transitions and limited analysis: The semantic oracle is imperfect for sentences with multiple crossing arcs; the paper acknowledges a portion of sentences cannot be encoded exactly. There is no quantitative error analysis of how often this affects decoding, what structures are missed, or whether M-SWAP alleviates practical errors sufficiently.
- Lack of exploration of alternative inference/training strategies: The parser is fully greedy; there is no comparison to small-beam decoding, dynamic oracles, scheduled sampling, or cost-augmented training that might further stabilize learning and improve out-of-domain generalization with minimal runtime costs.

Questions to Authors:
- How much do M-SELF and the copy-vs-move buffer design contribute? Can you report ablations removing M-SELF or reverting to standard arc-eager moves to quantify their impact on CoNLL 2009 English?
- Can you quantify the effect of pretrained embeddings on English as well (not just Chinese)? For example, report joint model performance with random vs. pretrained embeddings, and with/without POS embeddings.
- Did you try small-beam decoding (e.g., beam=4 or 8)? What are the accuracy/runtime trade-offs, especially for out-of-domain data?
- How sensitive is the model to hyperparameters (stack LSTM size, action embedding size, dropout)? A brief sensitivity analysis would help assess reproducibility and robustness.
- For the semantic oracle imperfections, what percentage of gold graphs are unrecoverable under your transition system in each language, and what is the estimated upper bound this imposes?

Additional Comments:
- The predicate identification setup for CoNLL 2008 (BiLSTM classifier) is reasonable; please clarify whether its predictions are used during training for joint parsing or only at test time, and whether error propagation from predicate identification materially affects SRL results.
- An error analysis (e.g., by semantic label, crossing arcs, long-distance dependencies) would strengthen the paper and inform future work on where joint models still lag pipelines.
- The promised open-source release is important for impact; please include scripts for data preprocessing (pseudo-projectivization, oracle generation) to facilitate replication.
