Summary
This paper presents a greedy, linear-time transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs. The key technical ingredient is a stack-LSTM representation of the entire parser state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history), enabling feature-free decision making over a synchronized set of syntactic and semantic transitions. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the model achieves the best results among joint syntax–semantics parsers, with strong runtime efficiency. Variants disentangling syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid (pipeline-like), and joint settings further illuminate the role of syntactic signals for SRL.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical advance: A strong, feature-free, greedy joint parser that achieves state-of-the-art performance among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, while being substantially faster than competitive pipelines. This is the primary contribution.
- Representation learning for joint parsing: A demonstration that learning compact neural representations of the entire joint parser state (via stack LSTMs) overcomes limitations of hand-engineered, locally scoped features used by prior joint models, delivering better parameter efficiency and accuracy.
- Transition system design and engineering: A synchronized transition system for joint syntactic/semantic dependency parsing (including M-SWAP and a new M-SELF action) that is simple, linear-time, and practically effective across multiple languages.

Strengths
- Strong results among joint models with compelling speed. On CoNLL 2008/2009 English, the system outperforms prior joint approaches (e.g., Henderson et al., 2008, 2013) and matches the best non-joint systems in some metrics. The end-to-end runtime (≈178s on a single core for CoNLL 2009 English) is 2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline, making the method attractive for real-world applications.
- Clean, feature-free architecture with full-state encoding. The stack-LSTM summarizes the entire algorithm state instead of relying on expert-crafted local features. This cleanly addresses a long-standing drawback in joint models (lack of robust, wide-context features during incremental parsing) and explains the substantial gains over earlier linear/locally-scoped models.
- Careful ablations clarify the value of joint learning. The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint variants are thoughtfully compared. Results show (i) syntax helps SRL (semantics-only < hybrid/joint) and (ii) joint training benefits in-domain performance, establishing a clear empirical narrative.
- Practical transition set and engineering. The synchronized transitions, including M-SWAP for crossing arcs and M-SELF for nominal self-arguments, yield a compact, linear-time algorithm that nonetheless covers the core SRL phenomena present in CoNLL data.
- Broad applicability and extensibility. The transition system and state representation are likely compatible with more powerful neural training/inference regimes (e.g., globally normalized models or beam search), suggesting a path to close the remaining gap to top pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Not competitive with the very best pipeline/structured systems. Despite strong results among joint models, the proposed approach still lags top SRL systems that leverage rich syntax-derived features, global inference, reranking, or multi-resource training (e.g., Zhao et al., 2009; Björkelund et al., 2010; Lei et al., 2015; Täckström et al., 2015; FitzGerald et al., 2015). The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of what errors persist and how joint greedy inference limits ultimate accuracy.
- Exposure bias and lack of global training/decoding. Decisions are locally normalized and made greedily, which is known to induce search errors. No beam search, global normalization, or cost-augmented training is explored. Given the compatibility of the transition system with global training (e.g., Andor et al., 2016), this seems like low-hanging fruit.
- Incomplete oracle and potential representational mismatch. The semantic oracle is imperfect (due to multiple crossing arcs), and the transition space cannot encode 100% of gold structures (≈1.5% of sentences). This creates unavoidable training noise and may bias the model. The impact of these oracle limitations on final performance is not quantified.
- Overfitting to in-domain data in joint mode. On CoNLL 2009 Brown, the hybrid model outperforms joint, suggesting that joint training overfits WSJ distribution. Regularization strategies and domain-robustness analyses are limited; more diagnostics could guide practical deployment.
- Limited analysis of parameter efficiency and learned representations. The claim that neural feature learning is more efficient than large linear feature spaces is plausible, but no quantitative evidence (e.g., parameter counts vs. accuracy, feature/class weight comparison) or qualitative probing of learned representations is provided.

Questions to Authors
- Dynamic oracle: Can you derive a dynamic oracle for the synchronized transition system (especially for M-SHIFT/M-REDUCE/M-LEFT/M-RIGHT, analogous to arc-eager)? Would this reduce exposure bias and help out-of-domain generalization?
- Global training/decoding: Have you tried beam search, globally normalized training (e.g., CRF loss / early updates), or max-violation? Any preliminary results or obstacles?
- Oracle completeness: What proportion of training arcs are unencodable due to crossing constraints in each language, and how much dev performance do you lose if you filter those cases?
- Predicate sense generalization: Defaulting unseen predicates to “01” works well in English; how does this strategy behave in other languages (e.g., German, Chinese)? Any analysis of OOV predicate senses?
- Sensitivity to upstream predictions: How sensitive is the model to POS/lemma errors? Can you report SRL/Syntax deltas when replacing gold vs. predicted tags/lemmas on dev?
- Multilingual morphology: Did you explore adding morphological features or character-based embeddings, and if so, how do they affect Czech/Spanish/German?

Additional comments
- Reporting: Please include significance tests for key comparisons (joint vs. hybrid; joint vs. Henderson et al., 2013; joint vs. top pipeline baselines where possible).
- Diagnostics: Per-label SRL F1 and error breakdowns (argument span/head errors, predicate senses) would clarify where joint inference helps or hurts.
- Reproducibility: Since you plan to release code, please document hyperparameter ranges, random seeds, and training times per language, and consider releasing pretrained embeddings used.
