Summary
The paper presents a fast, greedy, transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic and semantic dependencies using stack LSTMs to represent the full algorithmic state (syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history). The model interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions, replaces hand-engineered features with learned representations, and introduces a small set of adjustments to the transition system (e.g., copy-on-shift and M-SELF) to support joint decoding. On CoNLL 2008–09 English, it surpasses prior joint models and achieves competitive performance vs. pipelines, while running in linear time with respect to sentence length.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that full-state neural representations plus greedy inference enable accurate, fast joint syntactic–semantic dependency parsing without expert-crafted features, outperforming prior joint models and approaching pipeline baselines. This result is robust across English and several CoNLL 2009 languages.
2) A practical and compatible joint transition system and training recipe: copy-on-shift to keep items accessible to both modules, an interleaving schedule, constraints to prevent invalid states, and a new M-SELF action to handle self-arguments in NomBank. This engineering makes linear-time joint decoding feasible and simple to implement.
3) Evidence that learned representations can substitute for expensive syntactic-path features commonly used in SRL, supported by ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) and by achieving good performance without global decoding or beam search.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over prior joint models with a simple, fast parser: The joint model beats Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and related joint approaches on CoNLL 2008–09 English. It is within range of top pipelines while being almost 2.5× faster than a strong SRL pipeline in reported settings.
- Full-state representation learning removes hand engineering and costly feature extraction: The stack-LSTM summarizes the entire parser state, avoiding global syntactic features like predicate–argument paths. This materially reduces feature engineering effort and runtime, and is especially compelling for greedy transition-based parsing where local handcrafted features are hard to design.
- Clear ablations elucidate where gains come from: The semantics-only, syntax-only, hybrid, and joint variants demonstrate that syntax helps semantics (WSJ) and quantify overfitting risk (Brown). The syntax-only variant slightly improves over a comparable arc-eager LSTM baseline, showing that joint modeling does not harm syntax.
- Practicality and scalability: Linear-time inference including feature extraction, low memory footprint, and end-to-end runtimes suitable for real use. The approach should be easy to adopt and extend.
- Multilingual applicability: Results are competitive across multiple languages under shared-task constraints, indicating the method generalizes beyond English.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in transition system and learning paradigm: The transition inventory largely follows Henderson et al. (2013) with modest extensions (copy-on-shift, M-SELF) and standard stack-LSTM modeling (Dyer et al., 2015). The main contribution is empirical, not algorithmically novel.
- Greedy decoding without exploration or global normalization: No beam search, global normalization, or learning with exploration; no dynamic oracle. Error propagation likely limits semantic F1 and out-of-domain robustness (as the Hybrid outperforms Joint on Brown). Integrating global training (e.g., globally normalized or beam-aware training) seems orthogonal yet unexplored.
- Imperfect semantic oracle and under-specified training details: The paper acknowledges that the semantic transition oracle is imperfect for a non-trivial subset of sentences; it is unclear how much this ceiling affects final results. Moreover, no statistical significance tests or multi-run variance are reported; this is important for neural models with dropout and SGD.
- Comparisons to the strongest pipelines remain partial: While the paper contextualizes against state-of-the-art SRL pipelines, it does not combine orthogonal advances (morphology, global decoding, reranking, extra annotations) or quantify the marginal effect of each on this architecture. The runtime comparison focuses on one public system; broader speed/accuracy trade-offs vs. other high-performing pipelines would strengthen the case.
- Limited analysis of design choices: The fixed interleaving schedule (syntax until an S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT, then semantics until M-SHIFT) is sensible but not ablated. The impact of copy-on-shift vs. move, M-SWAP frequency, and predicate default-sense heuristic on errors is not deeply analyzed.

Questions to Authors
- Dynamic oracle: Can you define a dynamic oracle for this joint transition set (including M-SELF and copy-on-shift)? If not fully, could you provide an oracle for a restricted subset (e.g., no crossing arcs) or a cost-augmented objective approximating oracle guidance?
- Global training/decoding: Have you tried beam search or globally normalized training (e.g., max-margin/CRF over transitions)? If so, what is the speed/accuracy trade-off? If not, do you foresee any incompatibilities with this transition system?
- Interleaving and copy-on-shift: How sensitive is performance to the interleaving policy? Did you try allowing limited semantic actions before syntax or alternative schedules? What is the measured benefit of copy-on-shift vs. move (in arc-eager) for joint performance?
- Oracle imperfections: What proportion of gold semantic arcs cannot be realized by your transitions in practice, and how does that bound test-set F1? Can you report an “oracle upper bound” from your transition inventory?
- Variance and significance: Please report mean/std across multiple random seeds for the main results and significance vs. prior joint models.
- Multilingual features: Did you explore character/morphological embeddings to compensate for missing morphological features in CoNLL 2009? Any preliminary evidence on languages like Czech?

Additional comments
- The approach should be compatible with global beam-search training and other neural architectures; combining these orthogonal enhancements could plausibly reach the very top tier of SRL performance without sacrificing speed.
- One attractive property not emphasized is parameter efficiency: the neural model can be more compact than linear models with large sparse feature templates, which matters for deployment.
