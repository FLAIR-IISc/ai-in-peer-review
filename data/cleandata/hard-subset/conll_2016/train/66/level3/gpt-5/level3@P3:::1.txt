Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces a greedy, linear-time joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that merges Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system with Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM state representation. The model learns continuous representations of the full parser state (syntax stack, semantics stack, buffer, and action history) and dispenses with hand-crafted features or beam search.
- Empirically, it achieves the strongest performance reported to date among joint models on CoNLL 2008/2009 English, with multilingual results comparable to top systems in CoNLL 2009, while being substantially faster than strong pipelines. Ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) quantify the role of syntactic information for SRL and joint training.
- The work also contributes a minor but practically useful extension to the transition inventory (M-SELF) to handle semantic self-dependencies in CoNLL 2009, and demonstrates an efficient implementation with modest memory use and open-sourcing plans.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results among joint models and competitive overall. The joint parser surpasses prior joint systems (e.g., Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL 2008/2009 English. On CoNLL 2009 English, the Joint system is on par with shared-task winners except for the best feature-rich pipelines, and it outperforms earlier joint baselines. The syntax-only variant slightly improves over Dyer et al. (2015) arc-eager LAS, showing that the architecture is solid even for pure syntax.
- Clear, rigorous methodology and system description. The transition system is well-specified, with constraints that guarantee valid states; the neural architecture and composition functions for syntax, semantics, and predicate senses are clearly defined; training objective and optimization details are sufficiently precise to enable reproduction. Hyperparameters are justified (dev tuning where appropriate) rather than left opaque.
- Fast, practical decoding without hand-crafted features. The parser runs in linear time with greedy inference, and the reported runtime (≈178s on CoNLL 2009 English test on one CPU core) is ~2.5× faster than a strong publicly available pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under matched settings. This makes the approach attractive for real-time or large-scale settings.
- Informative ablations and analyses. The semantics-only vs hybrid vs joint comparison shows (i) syntax helps SRL; (ii) joint training helps in-domain but can overfit, as seen by hybrid outperforming joint out-of-domain (Brown). This is a valuable, nuanced empirical insight for the community.
- Well-written with strong related work. The paper is easy to follow, places the work correctly among joint decoding, pipeline SRL, and neural approaches, and fairly discusses why pipelines can still dominate and what this work does and does not attempt (e.g., no global reranking, no exact DP inference).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological originality. The core novelty lies in integrating two previously known components (Henderson et al.’s joint transition system and Dyer et al.’s stack LSTM). While this integration is important and overdue, the conceptual advance is incremental (with the exception of the small M-SELF addition).
- Heavy reliance on pretrained embeddings in some languages, especially Chinese. The multilingual section notes that Chinese gains are driven by strong pretrained word embeddings; without them, performance is merely on par with others. This raises the possibility that part of the reported gains (especially multilingual) are due more to representation quality than to the joint model per se. A controlled ablation on embedding quality across languages would clarify this.
- Greedy inference without exploration or global training may cap accuracy. The model trades accuracy for speed by using greedy decoding and local training. While this is an explicit design choice, the paper lacks an experiment with small-beam decoding or simple global objectives to quantify the headroom, especially given that top systems use global features or exact inference. The observed out-of-domain overfitting suggests potential benefits from modest global regularization or beam search.
- Oracle/transition limitations and error analysis are underexplored. The oracle mapping from gold graphs to transitions is imperfect for semantics (≈1.5% of English sentences cannot be encoded exactly). The paper does not analyze how much this upper bound affects final performance or where the model fails (e.g., crossing arcs, long-distance roles, self-dependencies). A brief error breakdown would make the contribution more diagnostic.
- Comparability and significance. While the comparisons to prior joint systems are appropriate, some key claims (e.g., best among joint models) would be strengthened by statistical significance testing on WSJ/Brown. In addition, it would help to clarify any overlap between pretraining corpora and evaluation domains and how hyperparameter tuning was controlled across languages.

Questions to authors
- How much of the improvement over Henderson et al. (and Gesmundo et al.) remains if you remove pretrained embeddings and train all word vectors from scratch? Can you report English results with/without pretraining to separate representation from modeling effects?
- For Chinese, can you quantify the delta attributable to pretraining (e.g., dev/test performance with random vs pretrained embeddings), and provide similar analyses for German/Spanish?
- Did you try small-beam decoding (e.g., beam=4 or 8) or globally normalized training (e.g., CRF/softmax over action sequences)? What is the speed–accuracy tradeoff?
- What fraction of errors are tied to the oracle’s inability to encode gold graphs? Can you estimate an oracle upper bound and show performance on the subset of sentences where the oracle is exact?
- How often is M-SELF triggered, and what is its marginal impact (ablation without M-SELF)?
- Are the reported improvements over Dyer et al. (2015) for syntax-only statistically significant? What accounts for the gain (architecture differences vs preprocessing)?
- Can you release the exact pretraining setup (vocabulary cutoff, tokenization, casing) and confirm no evaluation text was included in pretraining for languages other than English AFP?

Additional comments
- Minor: Since multilingual results lag for morphologically rich languages, character/subword embeddings or explicit morphology could be an easy extension likely to yield further gains.
