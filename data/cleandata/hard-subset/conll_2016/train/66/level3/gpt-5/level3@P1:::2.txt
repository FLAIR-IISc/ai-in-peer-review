Summary
The paper presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs. The model encodes the entire parser state with stack LSTMs over the syntactic stack, semantic stack, buffer, and action history, replacing hand-crafted features with learned representations. Inference is fully greedy and linear-time, enabled by state summaries that stand in for expensive syntactic-path features. The system introduces an M-SELF transition to handle CoNLL 2009 nominal self-arguments and adapts arc-eager transitions with a copy operation to share the buffer across syntax and semantics. Experiments on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (English and multilingual) show best performance among prior joint models and competitive results with pipelined systems, with substantially faster runtime.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A simple, fast joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that learns a full-state representation with stack LSTMs and achieves the best reported results among joint models on CoNLL-2008/2009 English, while requiring no hand-crafted features.
- Secondary: A practical transition system for joint parsing that interleaves syntax and semantics via shared buffer access (copying in S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT) and adds an M-SELF transition to cover nominal self-arguments in CoNLL-2009.
- Secondary: An empirical comparison of joint vs pipelined vs single-task settings (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid), clarifying where joint training helps and where it overfits.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements over prior joint models with much simpler inference. On CoNLL-2008/2009 English, the joint model consistently outperforms Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and Gesmundo et al. (2009), supporting the claim that full-state representation learning is beneficial.
- Competitive accuracy with fast runtime. The end-to-end runtime (≈178s on CoNLL-2009 English, single core) is about 2.5x faster than a strong pipelined SRL system (Lei et al., 2015). This makes the approach attractive for practical deployment and for use as a backbone in larger systems.
- No hand-crafted features, yet competitive with feature-rich pipelines. The model replaces syntactic-path and other global features with learned state representations, simplifying engineering and enabling portability across languages. Multilingual results are on par with top systems in most non-English CoNLL-2009 datasets.
- Clear ablation baselines and analysis of jointness. The syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint variants are well chosen. Results show syntax benefits SRL (semantics-only < hybrid/joint), and joint learning helps in-domain SRL while slightly overfitting out-of-domain—useful insights for the community.
- Thoughtful transition design for joint parsing. The buffer-sharing via copy (vs move) and the new M-SELF transition are practical design choices that address real data phenomena (crossing arcs, self-arguments), and they are cleanly integrated.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond adapting stack LSTMs to the joint setting. While the engineering is sound, most components (stack LSTMs, arc-eager parsing, greedy inference) are established; the main novelty is their combination and the M-SELF transition. A deeper technical contribution (e.g., learning-to-search, global normalization, structured inference) would strengthen the paper.
- No comparison to beam search or globally normalized training. The paper emphasizes greedy decoding, but does not quantify the cost/benefit relative to small-beam search. Reporting beam sizes vs accuracy/speed and/or exploring dynamic oracles would provide a fairer comparison and might close gaps to pipeline SOTA.
- Insufficient ablations on representation choices. It is unclear how much each component contributes: pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, composition functions (gs/gm/gd), M-SELF, M-SWAP, copy vs move in syntactic transitions. An ablation table would clarify where gains come from.
- Limited analysis of semantic errors and role phenomena. The claim that state representations obviate path features would benefit from targeted evaluation: per-role F1, long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs, and nominal predicates. Current analysis focuses on aggregate scores.
- Clarity issues and organization. Section 2 would benefit from a concise, explicit list of differences from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) (e.g., copy vs move, interleaving policy, new actions). The role of A1 vs C-A1 in examples is underspecified. Section 4 mixes CoNLL-2008/2009 and multilingual settings in a way that is hard to follow; reorganization would help.

Questions to Authors
- Beam search: What is the accuracy/speed trade-off for small beam sizes (e.g., 4, 8)? Does joint parsing benefit more or less from beam than syntax-only? Did you try globally normalized objectives or dynamic oracles?
- Ablations: How much do pretrained embeddings contribute? What happens if you remove the action-history stack? What is the effect of the copy vs move change in S-SHIFT/S-RIGHT on SRL accuracy?
- Composition choices: In gd(v, p), why combine word and sense embeddings via a tanh affine over [v; p] instead of using only the sense embedding or a multiplicative/bilinear interaction? Can you report an ablation comparing these options?
- Transition coverage: You note ≈1.5% of English sentences in CoNLL-2009 cannot be perfectly encoded by the oracle due to multiple crossing arcs. How does this impact training and evaluation, and does M-SWAP fully mitigate this in practice?
- Overfitting: Joint > Hybrid in-domain but < Hybrid out-of-domain. Have you tried stronger regularization, data augmentation, or domain-adaptive fine-tuning to mitigate this?
- Multilingual morphology: Did you attempt to incorporate character-based or morphological features? Any preliminary results, especially for Czech/Japanese?

Additional Comments
- Please clarify the presence and interpretation of both A1 and C-A1 links in Section 2’s example, and how the transition system handles continuation roles.
- Add a succinct summary of how your transitions differ from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) in one place.
- Reorganize Section 4 so that CoNLL-2008 (English), CoNLL-2009 English, and CoNLL-2009 multilingual are clearly separated, with explicit dataset/task differences.
- Consider reporting per-role and distance-based breakdowns, and sensitivity to predicate frequency and nominal vs verbal predicates.
