Summary
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that follows Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition framework but replaces hand-engineered features and local-context representations with stack LSTMs over the entire parser state. The model performs greedy, linear-time decoding and is evaluated on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (English and multilingual), achieving results competitive with older shared-task systems while trailing more recent, feature-rich or globally decoded approaches.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A joint transition system whose decisions are conditioned on vectorial summaries of the full algorithmic state via stack LSTMs, eliminating hand-crafted features and enabling long-range syntactic cues for SRL.
- Secondary: A fast, practical greedy joint parser with systematic ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) that clarify where joint modeling helps.
- Tertiary: A broad empirical study across CoNLL-2008/2009, including multilingual settings, demonstrating portability and competitive performance against earlier joint models.

Strengths
- Strong design: Leveraging stack LSTMs to encode S, M, B, and action history provides a principled way to capture predicate–argument paths and other long-range cues without feature engineering.
- Empirical clarity: The ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) substantiate claims about the utility of joint training and the trade-offs in/out-of-domain.
- Practicality: Greedy decoding yields very fast inference (≈178s on CoNLL-2009 English test, single core) with competitive accuracy, making the method attractive for real-time or large-scale use.
- Writing and positioning: The paper is clear, well organized overall, and situates contributions against both Henderson-style joint parsers and contemporary SRL systems.

Weaknesses
- Search comparability: Exclusive reliance on greedy decoding limits comparability to prior transition-based work; beam-search results (and/or global inference) are needed to separate modeling from search effects.
- Missing methodological clarity: Section 2 does not crisply enumerate differences from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) (e.g., copying vs moving items, new M-SELF, interleaving policy); a concise summary is necessary to assess novelty.
- Labeling clarity: The roles of A1 vs C-A1 (continuation) links are not explained, impeding task understanding and error analysis.
- Redundancy concern: Section 3.3 concatenates predicate lemma and sense vectors; since senses are lemma-specific, this may be redundant—an ablation or rationale is needed.
- Experimental organization: Sections 4.1/4.2 blur the 2008 predicate-identification setting versus 2009 pre-identified predicates and multilingual scope; clearer delineation and per-language details would help.

Questions to Authors
- Please report beam-search results (varying beam widths) and quantify speed–accuracy trade-offs.
- Enumerate all transition/system differences from Henderson et al. and their measured impact.
- Clarify the semantics of A1 vs C-A1 and how your transition set represents continuation links.
- Justify lemma+sense concatenation for predicates; provide ablations (sense-only, gating).
- Clarify which languages are included in 4.2, what embeddings/morphology are used, and how multilingual hyperparameters were tuned.
- How does imperfect semantic oracle conversion bound performance? Provide upper-bound estimates or diagnostics.
