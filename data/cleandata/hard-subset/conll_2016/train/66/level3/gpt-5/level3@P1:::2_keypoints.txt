- Presents a transition-based parser that jointly produces syntactic dependency trees and SRL graphs using stack LSTMs to encode full parser state.  
- Replaces hand-crafted features with learned state representations and uses greedy linear-time inference.  
- Introduces an M-SELF transition for CoNLL-2009 nominal self-arguments and adapts arc-eager transitions with a copy operation to share the buffer.  
- Achieves best reported results among prior joint models on CoNLL-2008/2009 English and competitive multilingual results, with substantially faster runtime.  
- Primary contribution: a simple, fast joint syntactic–semantic parser learning full-state representations without hand-crafted features.  
- Secondary contribution: a practical transition system interleaving syntax and semantics via buffer-copying and adding M-SELF to handle nominal self-arguments.  
- Secondary contribution: empirical comparison of joint, pipelined, and single-task settings clarifying when joint training helps or overfits.  
- Strength: Strong empirical improvements over prior joint models, supporting full-state representation learning.  
- Strength: Competitive accuracy with much faster end-to-end runtime (~2.5x faster than a strong pipelined SRL system).  
- Strength: No hand-crafted features while remaining competitive and portable across languages.  
- Strength: Clear ablation baselines (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, joint) highlighting the effect of joint learning.  
- Strength: Thoughtful transition design (buffer-copy and M-SELF) addressing crossing arcs and self-arguments.  
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty beyond applying established components (stack LSTMs, arc-eager, greedy inference) to the joint setting.  
- Weakness: No comparison to beam search or globally normalized training to quantify trade-offs versus greedy decoding.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablations on representation choices (pretrained embeddings, action-history stack, composition functions, copy vs move).  
- Weakness: Limited error analysis of semantic phenomena (per-role F1, long-distance dependencies, crossing arcs, nominal predicates).  
- Weakness: Clarity and organization issues—needs explicit differences from Henderson et al., clearer examples, and better section organization.  
- Question: What is the accuracy/speed trade-off for small beam sizes (e.g., 4, 8), and were globally normalized objectives or dynamic oracles tried?  
- Question: How much do pretrained embeddings and the action-history stack contribute, and what is the effect of copy vs move on SRL accuracy?  
- Question: Why combine word and sense embeddings via tanh affine in gd(v,p) rather than using only sense or other interactions, and can you ablate this?  
- Question: How do the ≈1.5% of English sentences with multiple crossing arcs affect training/evaluation, and does M-SWAP mitigate this fully?  
- Question: Have you tried stronger regularization, data augmentation, or domain-adaptive fine-tuning to address joint model overfitting out-of-domain?  
- Question: Did you attempt character-based or morphological features for multilingual datasets, especially Czech/Japanese?  
- Additional comment: Clarify the A1 vs C-A1 links and how continuation roles are handled in the transition system.  
- Additional comment: Add a succinct summary of differences from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) in one place.  
- Additional comment: Reorganize experiments section to separate CoNLL-2008 English, CoNLL-2009 English, and CoNLL-2009 multilingual, and report per-role and distance-based breakdowns.
