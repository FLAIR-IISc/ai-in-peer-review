Summary
The paper presents a joint syntactic–semantic dependency parser that integrates Henderson et al.’s synchronized transition system with stack LSTMs, enabling a greedy, linear-time model that learns state representations without hand-crafted features. Evaluated on CoNLL 2008/2009 (English and multilingual), the system attains the best results among joint models on English and competitive multilingual performance, while being fast. The exposition is clear, with a thorough related-work section and a well-justified methodology.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical integration of joint syntactic–semantic transitions with stack LSTMs yielding a strong, feature-light, greedy parser that sets a new bar among joint systems on CoNLL 08/09 English.
2) Careful empirical study, including syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid, and joint settings, clarifying when joint modeling helps and where it overfits (WSJ vs Brown).
3) An efficient implementation demonstrating substantial speedups over competitive pipelines.

Strengths
- Competitive accuracy among joint models with a simple, linear-time greedy approach; clear wins over prior joint systems (e.g., Henderson et al.) support the value of full-state representations.
- Strong ablations: semantics-only lags hybrid/joint, and syntax-only slightly improves over prior stack-LSTM syntactic parsers, evidencing mutual reinforcement between syntax and semantics.
- Excellent clarity: the transition inventory, constraints, composition functions, and hyperparameters are described precisely; training choices (dropout, LR schedule) are justified and reproducible.
- Practicality: compelling runtime (∼178s on CoNLL 2009 English) and modest memory footprint, making the method attractive for deployment.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: the work primarily combines established components (Henderson transitions + Dyer stack LSTMs). The integration is valuable but not conceptually new.
- Embedding confound for Chinese: reported multilingual gains appear sensitive to high-quality pretrained embeddings; without them, performance is “on par,” tempering claims of model-driven improvement.
- Greedy inference may cap ceiling performance; comparisons to beam search or global training/inference are not provided, and error propagation in semantics with crossing arcs is only partially mitigated.
- Oracle/convertibility limitations for semantic transitions (crossing arcs) introduce noise; the impact on final accuracy is not quantified.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the contribution of pretrained embeddings (especially Chinese) via controlled experiments with/without pretraining across languages?
- How does beam search or dynamic-oracle training affect accuracy/runtime trade-offs in this joint setting?
