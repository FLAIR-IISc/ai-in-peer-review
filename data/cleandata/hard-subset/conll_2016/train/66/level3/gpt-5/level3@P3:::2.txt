Summary
The paper proposes a joint transition-based parser that simultaneously builds syntactic dependency trees and semantic role labeling (SRL) graphs. It adopts Henderson et al.’s synchronized algorithmic framework but replaces hand-crafted features with learned vector representations of the entire parser state via stack LSTMs, and performs greedy decoding. The model composes syntactic and semantic structures recursively, incorporates predicate sense disambiguation via an M-PRED transition, and adds an M-SELF transition for NomBank self-arguments. Experiments on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (English and multilingual) show results competitive with prior joint models and older pipeline systems, while being notably fast. However, it lags behind more recent SRL systems that use global inference and/or rich feature engineering.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Neural representation of full joint parser state with stack LSTMs for synchronized syntax+semantics: A clean instantiation showing that learned state summaries obviate expensive, long-range syntactic features for SRL within an incremental joint parser.
2) Simple, fast joint decoder: A plain greedy transition system with linear-time inference and no hand-crafted features that still outperforms earlier joint models on CoNLL-2008/2009 and is competitive with older pipelines.
3) Practical extensions to Henderson-style transitions: Introduction of M-SELF for NomBank self-arguments and a copy-based adaptation of arc-eager transitions to support interleaved syntax/semantics on a shared buffer.

Strengths
- Strong, clear integration of stack LSTMs into joint parsing: The architecture elegantly summarizes S, M, B, and action history, enabling the model to capture long-range dependencies (e.g., predicate–argument paths) without explicit feature extraction. This is a substantive methodological advance over earlier Henderson-style models that relied on local contexts.
- Solid empirical validation vs prior joint parsers: On CoNLL-2008/2009 English, the joint model consistently improves over Henderson et al. (2008, 2013) and related joint approaches. The comparison is meaningful because the transition system and task setup are closely aligned.
- Thorough controlled variants: The Syntax-only, Semantics-only, Hybrid, and Joint settings isolate the benefits of joint modeling. The findings (Semantics-only < Hybrid < Joint in-domain; Hybrid > Joint out-of-domain) are informative and actionable.
- Practical efficiency: The greedy decoder’s runtime and memory footprint are compelling for real-world use. The claimed 2–3× speedup over a strong pipeline (Lei et al., 2015) under identical conditions is a useful data point.
- Clear, well-written paper: The description of the transition system, composition functions, and training procedure is generally lucid; leveraging recent LSTM techniques within a classic framework is well-motivated.

Weaknesses
- Limited search undermines comparability and ceilings: Only greedy decoding is reported. Given that much prior work uses beam search or global inference, the absence of beam or dynamic-oracle training results makes it hard to assess headroom and to compare fairly. This also weakens the claim that stack-LSTM state is sufficient for SRL decisions; an accompanying beam study would strengthen the case.
- Incomplete analysis of modeling choices: Section 3.3 concatenates predicate word and sense embeddings, which seems redundant post-disambiguation; no ablation quantifies its necessity. Similarly, the contribution of the action-history LSTM, label embeddings, and pretrained embeddings is not isolated. Error analysis is limited, especially on OOD Brown.
- Clarity gaps in the transition system description: Section 2 does not clearly summarize how the transitions differ from Henderson et al. (2008/2013) beyond copy-vs-move, interleaving policy, and M-SELF. The role and evaluation treatment of both A1 and C-A1 edges is not explained; readers unfamiliar with continuation roles may be confused about target structures.
- Evaluation organization and scope: Section 4.1/4.2 organization is confusing regarding multilinguality and the differences between the 2008 and 2009 settings. It would help to clearly separate English vs multilingual results and to emphasize the 2008 predicate-identification setup vs 2009 pre-identified predicates. Also, the effect of the imperfect semantic oracle (non-derivable gold transitions) is not quantified as an upper bound on attainable accuracy.
- Lagging behind recent SRL systems: While expected given design goals, the gap to recent globally-decoded neural SRL systems remains substantial, and the paper does not experiment with simple add-ons (beam, dynamic oracle, mild global scoring, char/morph features) that could narrow the gap while preserving much of the speed.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report beam search results (e.g., beam=4/8) and/or dynamic-oracle training for the joint model? How do accuracy and runtime trade off? Does beam particularly help the semantic graph?
- Section 3.3: Why concatenate predicate word and sense embeddings after disambiguation? Would using the sense vector alone (or a gated composition) suffice? Please provide an ablation.
- Are label embeddings for syntax and semantics shared or separate? You separate Z and biases, but are l and r drawn from distinct embeddings? Any effect if shared?
- Section 2: Please provide a concise bullet list of differences from Henderson et al. (2008/2013): copy vs move, interleaving policy, constraints, M-SELF, treatment of SWAP, initial buffer configuration, and any changes in legality constraints.
- A1 and C-A1 edges: Do you predict both for the same predicate where PropBank has continuation roles? How are continuation edges evaluated in the dependency-SRL setting? Please clarify with an example and how the transition system generates both links.
- Oracle limitations: What fraction of training instances contain semantic structures that are not derivable by your transitions, and what is the resulting theoretical upper bound? Do you drop, approximate, or modify such instances during training?
- Multilingual settings: Were hyperparameters and pretrained embeddings fixed across languages? Did you attempt character/morphological features, especially for Czech and other morphologically rich languages?

Additional Comments
- Consider adding a brief error analysis contrasting Joint vs Hybrid on WSJ vs Brown to substantiate the overfitting hypothesis and to highlight which semantic roles benefit from joint modeling.
- The copy vs move deviation from arc-eager is crucial—highlight it earlier and motivate it with a small diagram showing why semantics needs access to the buffer item.
- Reporting per-role F1 (core vs adjunct) and arc-length breakdowns would better support the claim that stack LSTMs capture long-range features.
- Minor: unify notation for A (action history) across equations; clarify whether At depends on At−1 beyond the legality constraints. Clarify whether predicate identification in 2008 uses the same embeddings as the parser.
