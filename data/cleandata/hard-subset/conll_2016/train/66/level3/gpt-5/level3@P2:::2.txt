Summary and contributions
This paper presents a joint transition-based parser for syntactic dependencies and semantic role labeling (SRL) that represents the entire parser state using stack LSTMs and performs greedy, linear-time inference. The model interleaves syntactic and semantic transitions over shared stacks/buffer and introduces a small extension (M-SELF) to handle semantic self-dependencies. Experiments on CoNLL-2008 (English) and CoNLL-2009 (English and multilingual) show the system outperforms prior joint models and is competitive with pipeline systems while being substantially faster.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical contribution: Demonstration that a learned, continuous representation of the full joint parser state (via stack LSTMs) can obviate hand-crafted features and yield state-of-the-art performance among joint models on CoNLL-2008/2009, with strong multilingual results.
2) Engineering contribution: A simple, fast, greedy joint parser with linear-time feature extraction and inference, achieving practical throughput and competitive accuracy; carefully controlled variants (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) illuminate where joint modeling helps.
3) Modeling/algorithmic details: A clean integration of four stack LSTMs over syntax, semantics, buffer, and action history within a synchronized transition system, including the M-SELF transition for nominal self-arguments.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance among joint models and solid multilingual generalization. The joint system consistently surpasses earlier joint approaches (Henderson et al., Gesmundo et al., Titov et al.) on CoNLL-2008/2009 English, and is on par with top systems in several non-English languages. The ablations (syntax-only, semantics-only, hybrid) further validate the value of joint information sharing, especially in-domain.
- Practicality and speed. The fully greedy decoding with linear-time feature extraction is compelling: end-to-end runtime on CoNLL-2009 English is ~2.5× faster than a strong pipeline baseline under comparable settings, making the method attractive for large-scale or latency-sensitive applications.
- Minimal feature engineering with competitive accuracy. The method removes reliance on expensive path-based and global syntactic features that dominate high-performing SRL pipelines, yet remains competitive, highlighting the value of learned state representations for joint parsing.
- Clear experimental design with informative baselines. The inclusion of semantics-only, syntax-only, and hybrid variants provides a controlled comparison to pipeline-style training and helps diagnose where joint modeling helps versus overfits (e.g., Brown vs WSJ).
- Reproducibility. The paper provides extensive hyperparameter details, training procedures (including dropout, learning rate schedule), and pretraining setup, and promises open-source code.

Weaknesses
- Limited search and missing beam/ globally normalized comparisons. The decision to use purely greedy decoding is reasonable for speed, but the paper does not quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off. Reporting beam-search or globally normalized (oracles/IL) variants would clarify how much accuracy is left on the table relative to comparable transition systems and whether greedy is indeed the best Pareto point.
- Insufficient clarification of novelty relative to Henderson et al. (2008/2013). The paper adopts their synchronized transition framework but does not concisely summarize the exact differences in the transition inventory, constraints, and interleaving policy beyond M-SELF and copy vs move. A clear side-by-side comparison would better establish the algorithmic novelty.
- Under-motivated architectural choices and limited ablations. The composition for predicates gd(v, p) uses concatenation of word and sense vectors; it is unclear why this is preferable to other fusions (e.g., gating, bilinear/tensor). More ablations on (i) predicate composition forms, (ii) label-embedding dimensions, (iii) impact of M-SELF, (iv) dependence on pretrained embeddings (especially for Chinese), and (v) action-history LSTM (A) would strengthen the claims about representation learning.
- Comparability to stronger SRL systems and upper bounds. While pipelines with global inference and extra resources outperform the joint model, the paper could better contextualize gaps by (i) providing oracle upper bounds given transition constraints (crossing arcs not perfectly encodable), and (ii) explicitly quantifying how much is due to decoding (greedy vs exact) versus feature availability (full-tree features vs partial stacks).
- Presentation issues that hinder clarity. Section 2’s example introduces both A1 and C-A1 links without clarifying their roles/necessity; Section 2 should be explicit about how such coreference/control-like links are represented by the transition system. Section 4’s organization is confusing: 4.1/4.2 mix CoNLL-2008 vs CoNLL-2009 scope and the multilingual experiments; clearer structuring would help readers navigate datasets, settings, and variants.

Questions to authors
- Search/inference: What is the accuracy impact of modest beam sizes (e.g., 4, 8) or global normalization? Can you report beam vs greedy results and speed to establish a Pareto curve?
- Transition system: Please provide a concise table or figure summarizing all differences from Henderson et al. (2008/2013), including copy vs move semantics, interleaving policy, and added constraints, and discuss how each affects expressivity and training stability.
- A1 vs C-A1: In the running example, why are both A1 and C-A1 present? What is the intended semantics, and how does the transition inventory ensure such links are licensed and not duplicated?
- Predicate composition: Why choose simple concatenation for gd(v, p)? Did you try alternatives (e.g., gating, bilinear interactions, residual addition)? Any ablation on predicate-sense vectorization?
- Oracle/upper bound: How often does the semantic oracle fail to encode gold graphs due to crossing arcs, and what is the resulting upper bound on F1 per language? Can you report oracle accuracy/coverage?
- Pretrained embeddings: How sensitive is performance to pretrained embedding quality/size, especially in Chinese and low-resource settings (e.g., Japanese)? Any results without pretraining across languages?
- Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative or quantitative error breakdowns (argument head attachment vs label confusions; long-distance arguments; nominal predicates) to illustrate where stack LSTMs help or fail?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting joint training vs pipeline training time, not only inference time, and confirm hardware/software parity for the speed comparison.
- Clarify fairness in comparisons to systems using extra resources (e.g., FrameNet) and note when such resources are used.
- Minor: a brief note on why copying from buffer (instead of moving) is critical when interleaving would make Section 2.2 easier to follow.
