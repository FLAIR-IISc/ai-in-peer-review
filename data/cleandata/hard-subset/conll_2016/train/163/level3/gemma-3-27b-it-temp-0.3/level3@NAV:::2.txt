## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors do encode PoS information and can reveal inconsistencies in annotation guidelines. A key contribution lies in the detailed error analysis, identifying specific cases where embedding-based predictions diverge from BNC annotations, highlighting potential areas for refinement in both tagging schemes and linguistic understanding of boundary cases. The authors also explore the distribution of PoS information across vector components, finding it’s not limited to a few key features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the thorough analysis of misclassified words. This goes beyond simply reporting accuracy and delves into *why* the model makes errors, revealing interesting linguistic phenomena and potential annotation issues (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns, inconsistencies with adverbs). This provides valuable insights beyond the classification task itself.
2. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper successfully demonstrates that distributional semantic models capture information related to PoS, even without explicit tagging during training. This reinforces the connection between syntax and semantics, and provides a data-driven approach to understanding grammatical categories.
3. **Exploration of Distributed Representation:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, rather than concentrated in a few, is a valuable observation. This supports the holistic nature of distributional semantics and challenges simplistic views of how grammar is encoded in embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty & Framing:** The central claim – that embeddings contain PoS information – is not novel. Significant prior work in distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., Blunsom & Cohn, Yatbaz et al.) is omitted, diminishing the perceived contribution. The paper should reframe its focus: instead of simply showing *that* embeddings contain PoS information, it should investigate whether embedding-based distinctions *improve* parsing or other NLP tasks compared to using original tags or Universal POS (UPOS) tags.
2. **Questionable UPOS Switch:** The decision to convert to UPOS tags is not adequately justified. The original, more fine-grained BNC tags might reveal more nuanced distinctions and lead to more interesting error analysis.
3. **Insufficient Contextualization:** The paper lacks sufficient discussion of how the BNC was originally PoS-tagged (using CLAWS4 and Template Tagger). Understanding the limitations of these taggers is crucial for interpreting the error analysis. Footnote 3 is particularly unclear regarding failed attempts and the relevance of Brown clusters.
4. **Trivialization of Proper Noun Distinction:** The observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns is presented as a significant finding, but this distinction is largely driven by function word distributions ("the") and lacks deeper linguistic justification.
5. **Focus on Frequent Words:** The analysis primarily focuses on frequent word/tag pairs. Examining the errors on low-frequency “tail” words is crucial for assessing the generalizability of the findings and identifying potential issues with rare word representation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the results of the error analysis change if you had retained the original, more fine-grained BNC tags instead of converting to UPOS?
2.  Can you elaborate on the failed attempts mentioned in Footnote 3? What were the specific criteria for failure, and why were Brown clusters not considered?
3.  How do the errors identified by your classifier compare to known errors or limitations of the CLAWS4 and Template Tagger systems used to create the BNC annotations?
4.  Have you investigated the performance of your classifier on low-frequency words, and if so, what patterns emerged in the error analysis for those words?
