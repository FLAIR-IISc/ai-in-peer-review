## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors train word embeddings on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, and then use these embeddings to train a logistic classifier to predict POS tags. They demonstrate that the embeddings do encode POS information, allowing for the identification of words with distributional patterns atypical for their assigned POS, potentially revealing annotation inconsistencies or supporting a more nuanced, ‘soft’ view of POS categories. The work includes thorough cross-validation and testing on other corpora, with clearly presented and analyzed results.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the *empirical validation and quantification of the link between distributional semantics and POS categories*. While the idea that word meaning is reflected in context (and therefore, embeddings) is well-established, this paper provides concrete evidence of this relationship specifically concerning POS, and demonstrates its robustness across datasets. A secondary contribution lies in the *identification of potential annotation errors and boundary cases* within the BNC, highlighted by the misclassifications of the trained model. This offers a novel method for corpus refinement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Solid Empirical Foundation:** The experiments are well-designed and executed, utilizing a large, established corpus (BNC) and a standard POS tagset (UD).
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The use of cross-validation and testing on independent corpora (UD Treebank) strengthens the validity of the findings. The results are clearly presented and analyzed.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.
*   **Interesting Findings:** The identification of words with atypical distributional patterns for their POS tags is insightful and opens avenues for further linguistic investigation. The finding that POS information is distributed across many vector components is also noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core methodology – training embeddings and using them for POS prediction – is relatively standard. The paper doesn't introduce any new NLP or machine learning techniques. It’s an application of existing methods to a specific linguistic question.
*   **Superficial Analysis of Errors:** While the paper identifies misclassified words, the analysis of *why* these errors occur could be more in-depth. More detailed linguistic analysis of the error cases would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Existing POS Tagging Methods:** The paper doesn't compare the performance of the embedding-based classifier to state-of-the-art POS taggers. This would provide a better benchmark for assessing the effectiveness of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the possibility of using this approach for resource-poor languages. Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of applying this method in such scenarios, particularly regarding the size and quality of the training corpus required?
2.  The analysis of misclassifications highlights potential annotation errors. Have you investigated these cases further by manually reviewing the original BNC annotations?
3.  Could you provide more details on the specific linguistic features that contribute to the misclassifications observed (e.g., specific syntactic contexts, semantic properties)?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from citing more recent work on POS tagging and induction using embeddings, such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016), as suggested by the conference guidelines.
