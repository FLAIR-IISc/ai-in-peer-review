- Demonstrates that word embeddings encode PoS information and a classifier can identify words whose distributions deviate from assigned tags.
- Main contribution: thorough exploration of model errors to reveal linguistic insights and annotation inconsistencies.
- Error analysis is detailed and linguistically contextualized, identifying types of misclassifications and soft PoS boundaries.
- Shows reasonable PoS prediction accuracy can be achieved from embeddings alone, without contextual features.
- Practical implication: distributional models can flag potential annotation errors useful for corpus refinement.
- Experiments are clearly presented and reproducible.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—claim that embeddings capture PoS is already established in prior work.
- Weakness: Framing as “redefining PoS classes” is misleading; should consider embeddings as complementary to tagsets or parsing.
- Weakness: Claim that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” contradicts literature showing substantial syntactic information.
- Weakness: Conversion to UPOS is inadequately justified; original finer-grained BNC tags might reveal more nuance.
- Weakness: Footnote about failed attempts (Brown clusters etc.) is ambiguous and needs clarification (who, criteria, relevance).
- Question: How does the classifier’s performance compare to baseline BNC-trained PoS taggers (e.g., NLTK, spaCy) on the same test set?
- Question: What specific criteria defined “ambiguous” lemmas in Section 5 and why was this filtering necessary?
- Question: Why use the BNC given its automatic annotation—would a manually tagged corpus yield different insights?
- Additional comments: Replace \citealt with \newcite and add discussion on limitations of using only distributional information for PoS tagging.
