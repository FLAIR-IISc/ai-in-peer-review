This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze misclassifications to reveal inconsistencies in annotation or support the notion of ‘soft’ PoS boundaries. A key finding is that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, not concentrated in a few. The primary contribution lies in the detailed error analysis of the classifier, which uncovers potential annotation errors and areas of ambiguity in PoS assignments within the BNC. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that even without contextual information, a classifier trained on distributional vectors can achieve reasonable PoS prediction accuracy. Finally, the paper empirically shows that PoS information is broadly distributed across the embedding space.

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the thorough analysis of misclassified words. The authors don’t simply report performance metrics; they delve into *why* the classifier makes mistakes, uncovering interesting linguistic phenomena like the ambiguity between nouns and adjectives (e.g., ‘materialist’) and highlighting potential inconsistencies in the BNC’s annotation (e.g., adverb tags for adjectives like 'plain'). This is valuable for anyone working with or analyzing PoS-tagged corpora. The connection to existing annotation issues, like those found in the Penn Treebank, strengthens the argument.

2. **Demonstration of PoS Encoding in Embeddings:** The paper successfully demonstrates that distributional semantics models *do* encode information about PoS, even without explicit tagging during training (in Section 5). The ability to achieve an accuracy of 84% on a test set using only distributional vectors and a relatively simple classifier is a significant result. This supports the idea that semantic and syntactic information are intertwined in word embeddings.

3. **Empirical Evidence of Distributed Representation:** The finding that PoS information isn’t localized to a few vector dimensions, but is instead distributed across many, is a notable observation. This aligns with the general principles of distributed representation learning and provides further insight into how embeddings capture linguistic information. The exploration of the number of features needed for optimal performance (Figure 3) provides concrete evidence for this claim.

4. **Clear Methodology and Reproducibility:** The authors provide a clear description of their data processing steps, embedding training procedure, and classification setup. The availability of the mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags (mentioned in footnote 2) contributes to the reproducibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The central claim – that word embeddings contain PoS information – is not novel. A significant body of prior work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) has already demonstrated this. The paper acknowledges related work, but doesn’t adequately position its contribution within the existing literature. Section 5, in particular, lacks sufficient discussion of previous distributional PoS induction methods.

2. **Framing and Motivation:** The paper frames its investigation in terms of ‘redefining’ PoS classes. However, the connection to practical applications, such as improved parsing or downstream NLP tasks, is missing. The authors should consider reframing the work to focus on whether the distinctions revealed by embeddings *improve* performance on tasks that rely on PoS tags, rather than simply questioning the existing tagset.

3. **Contradictory Statement on Semantic vs. Syntactic Information:** The assertion that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate and contradicts the paper’s own review of related work (Section 2), which cites examples demonstrating significant syntactic information in embeddings (e.g., Mikolov et al., CoVe). This undermines the credibility of the introductory claims.

4. **Questionable Tagset Conversion:** The decision to convert BNC tags to the Universal Dependencies tagset is potentially limiting. The finer-grained distinctions in the original BNC tagset might be more revealing for the analysis of annotation inconsistencies. The rationale for this conversion isn't fully justified.

5. **Analysis Bias:** The analysis focuses heavily on frequent word/tag pairs. A more comprehensive investigation should examine the classifier’s performance on low-frequency words and the types of errors it makes in those cases. The “long tail” of the vocabulary is likely to reveal additional insights into the limitations and strengths of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose to convert the BNC tags to the Universal Dependencies tagset? Did you consider the potential loss of information from the finer-grained original tags?

2.  How does the performance of your classifier compare to existing state-of-the-art PoS taggers on the BNC test set?

3.  Could you provide more details about the failed attempts mentioned in footnote 3? What were the specific methods used, and what criteria were used to determine failure? Were Brown clusters considered and why were they not employed?

4.  Given the wealth of prior work on distributional PoS induction, how do your methods and findings differentiate themselves from existing approaches? What specific novel insights does this work offer?



**Additional Comments:**

-   Change sentential citation style to \newcite{}.
-   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of relying solely on distributional information and the potential benefits of combining it with other features (e.g., morphological information, contextual features).
- Further investigation into the components of the embedding that contribute the most to each POS tag is warranted.
