## Review for "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantics, as captured by word embeddings, and part-of-speech (POS) tagging. The authors train word embeddings on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags and demonstrate that these embeddings contain information relevant to POS classification. They further analyze misclassifications to reveal potential inconsistencies in annotation and support the notion of “soft” POS affiliations.

**Summary:** The paper presents a systematic exploration of how well POS information is encoded within word embeddings. By training embeddings on a large, tagged corpus and employing a logistic classifier, the authors validate the distributional hypothesis in relation to grammatical categories. The analysis of classification errors provides intriguing insights into boundary cases and potential annotation issues within the BNC.

**Contributions:** I identify three primary contributions:

1. **Empirical Validation of POS Encoding in Distributional Semantics:** The core contribution is a robust empirical demonstration, through supervised learning, that distributional word embeddings *do* encode significant POS information. While not entirely novel (as discussed in weaknesses), the scale and clarity of this validation are valuable.
2. **Error Analysis as a Diagnostic Tool for Annotation Quality:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words serves as a novel application of distributional semantics. This analysis doesn't just highlight model limitations; it identifies potential inconsistencies or debatable cases within the BNC annotation scheme, offering a method for corpus refinement.
3. **Quantitative Support for “Soft” POS Boundaries:** The identification of words exhibiting distributional patterns atypical for their assigned POS tags provides empirical evidence supporting the idea that POS categories are not always sharply defined, a claim often discussed in theoretical linguistics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the results are presented in a concise and understandable manner. The use of examples and visualizations (Figures 1 & 2) further enhances clarity.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a strong evaluation methodology, including 10-fold cross-validation and testing on external corpora (UD Treebank). The reporting of F-scores and accuracy provides a comprehensive assessment of the classifier’s performance. The weighted averaging to address class imbalance is a thoughtful methodological choice.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is a major strength. Identifying specific patterns (e.g., numerals representing currency, nouns acting as adjectives) reveals meaningful linguistic insights and potential annotation errors. The comparison with errors from the Stanford tagger adds further nuance.
4. **Reproducibility:** The release of the mappings between BNC and UD tags facilitates reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.
5. **Demonstrated Robustness:** The consistent performance of the classifier even when applied to a different, human-annotated corpus (UD Treebank) demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The central premise – that distributional semantics captures POS information – is not entirely novel. Several prior works (Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016, among others) have already explored this connection. The paper lacks a truly novel methodological contribution; it largely applies established techniques to a new corpus.
2. **Supervised Learning Focus:** While effective, the reliance on supervised learning (logistic regression) feels somewhat conservative. Exploring more sophisticated machine learning models or unsupervised methods for discovering POS boundaries could have added greater novelty.
3. **Limited Exploration of Embedding Parameters:** The paper describes the embedding parameters chosen but doesn't investigate the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. A brief exploration of different vector sizes or training algorithms might have yielded further insights.
4. **UD Tagset Specificity:** The use of the Universal Dependencies tagset, while beneficial for cross-linguistic comparability, may limit the findings' direct applicability to corpora using different tagging schemes. This isn't a critical weakness, but a point to consider.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights inconsistencies in BNC annotation. Have you considered quantitatively assessing the extent of these inconsistencies across the entire corpus, perhaps by applying the trained classifier to the full BNC and identifying systematically misclassified tokens?
2.  You mention the potential for applying this approach to resource-poor languages. Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of creating a small, manually tagged lexicon for initial training in such scenarios? What strategies could be used to mitigate the impact of limited data?
3.  The analysis of vector components suggests POS information is distributed. Could you elaborate on the linguistic interpretation of the most salient components identified? Is there any correlation between these components and known linguistic features (e.g., morphology, syntactic roles)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears incomplete.
*   While the discussion of the ANOVA F-value for feature ranking is interesting, it would be helpful to see a visualization of the top-ranked components and their associated linguistic interpretations.
* Consider adding a discussion section on the limitations of the distributional hypothesis in this context.
