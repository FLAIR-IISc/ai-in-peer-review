- Classifiers trained on BNC embeddings can predict PoS tags, demonstrating embeddings encode PoS information (training F1 0.979, unseen 0.91).
- Detailed error analysis uncovers annotation inconsistencies and “soft” PoS affiliations (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns, adverbs vs adjectives).
- PoS information is distributed across many embedding components rather than concentrated in a few.
- Proposed practical application: use distributional models to pre-tag corpora for resource-poor languages with limited annotations.
- Limited novelty: prior work has already shown distributional vectors contain PoS information; paper under-engages related literature.
- Weak framing/motivation: “redefining” PoS is not well-justified nor linked to downstream task impact.
- The claim that embeddings are “mostly semantic” contradicts established work showing embeddings encode syntactic information.
- Methodological concern: switching to the UPOS tagset is not adequately justified and may obscure informative fine-grained BNC tags.
- Insufficient detail about alternative approaches: failed clustering attempts are under-described and Brown clusters or other baselines aren’t explored.
- Request to compare embedding-based classifications to original BNC/UPOS tags on downstream tasks (e.g., parsing).
- Request justification and analysis of the impact of converting BNC tags to UPOS.
- Request quantification of annotation inconsistencies (e.g., percentage of instances where the model suggests a more plausible tag).
- Ask for discussion of limitations of a purely distributional approach, especially for rare or ambiguous words, and potential remedies.
- Editorial: replace sentential citation style with \newcite{}.
- Suggest further analysis of errors on low-frequency vocabulary to expose model limitations.
- Suggest combining distributional features with morphological information to improve generalization for rare words.
