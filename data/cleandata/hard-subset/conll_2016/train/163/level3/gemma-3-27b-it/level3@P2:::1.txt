## Review for CONLL 2016: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (POS) information is encoded within word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC). The authors train a classifier to predict POS tags based on these embeddings and analyze misclassifications to identify potential inconsistencies in annotation guidelines or reveal ‘soft’ POS boundaries. 

The primary contribution of this work is a robust empirical demonstration, using distributional semantics techniques, that word embeddings *do* capture significant POS information. While the core idea of relating distributional representations to POS tags isn't entirely new, the systematic analysis using a large corpus like the BNC, coupled with thorough error analysis, offers valuable insights into the granularity and limitations of current POS annotation schemes. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed error analysis, which highlights potential annotation inconsistencies within the BNC corpus itself, particularly concerning noun-adjective and numeral boundaries. Finally, the finding that POS information is distributed across numerous vector components – rather than being concentrated in a few key dimensions – is a useful observation that contributes to our understanding of how linguistic information is represented in embedding spaces.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results. The classifier achieves a weighted average F-score of 0.979 on the training set and 0.91 on unseen data, demonstrating that word embeddings are indeed informative for POS prediction. This quantitative evidence provides a strong foundation for the claims made. The inclusion of a transfer experiment to the Universal Dependencies Treebank (with an F-score of 0.99) further validates the robustness of the approach.

2. **Thorough Error Analysis:**  The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a significant strength. The authors don't simply report accuracy numbers; they delve into *why* the classifier makes mistakes, identifying interesting linguistic phenomena like the ambiguity between proper and common nouns, the behavior of passive participles, and the treatment of currency amounts. This level of analysis provides valuable linguistic insights and goes beyond simply validating the method.

3. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate their motivation, methods, and results.  The organization is logical, and the use of tables and figures effectively illustrates the key findings. The connection to existing linguistic theories (e.g., the continuum of grammatical categories) is well-established.

4. **Demonstration of Distributed Representation:** The investigation into the distribution of POS information across vector dimensions, concluding that it’s not localized to a few key features, is a valuable finding. This reinforces the idea that embeddings capture complex, multifaceted linguistic properties. The experiment varying the number of used vector components provides good support for this claim.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core methodology—using word embeddings and a classifier for POS prediction—is not novel. Existing work (which the authors acknowledge but could more comprehensively address – see questions below) has already demonstrated a relationship between distributional semantics and POS. The paper's contribution lies more in the thoroughness of the empirical investigation and the insightful error analysis than in the introduction of new techniques.

2. **Reliance on Existing Annotation:** The entire evaluation is predicated on the quality of the existing POS annotations in the BNC and UD Treebank. While the error analysis *identifies* potential annotation inconsistencies, the paper doesn’t offer a mechanism for automatically resolving them or improving annotation schemes. The observed “errors” could simply reflect genuine linguistic ambiguity.

3. **Limited Scope of Linguistic Analysis:** While the error analysis is insightful, it primarily focuses on identifying inconsistencies with the BNC annotations. A broader comparative analysis against other linguistic resources or theoretical frameworks could strengthen the conclusions.  For example, connecting the observed misclassifications to specific grammatical theories beyond the general notion of “soft” boundaries would add depth.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper briefly mentions related work but could benefit from a more comprehensive review of the literature on POS tagging and induction using word embeddings. Specifically, please discuss how this work relates to papers like Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016) and clearly articulate the differentiating factors.
2.  The analysis of misclassified words suggests potential annotation errors. Could the authors elaborate on the potential impact of these errors on the classifier's performance and discuss strategies for mitigating these issues (e.g., data augmentation, re-annotation)?
3.  The authors mention the potential for applying this approach to resource-poor languages. Could they provide a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges and potential benefits of using distributional models for preliminary POS tagging in such scenarios? What minimal annotation effort would be required?
4.  Concerning the 100-dimensional threshold identified in Figure 3, is there any statistical significance testing to support this finding?




**Additional Comments:**

- The phrase “Users Reference Guide for British National Corpus (XML Edition), Lou Burnard” is not a standard bibliographic citation format. Please revise.
- The anonymization statement is unnecessary for a conference review.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed and insightful study that provides compelling evidence for the relationship between distributional semantics and POS information. While the methodology isn't groundbreaking, the thorough empirical analysis and insightful error analysis make a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for those interested in the intersection of computational linguistics and linguistic theory. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above and responding to the questions posed.
