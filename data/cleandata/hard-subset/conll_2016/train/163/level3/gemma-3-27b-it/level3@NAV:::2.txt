## Review of “Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models”

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, utilizing a classifier approach to identify words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned tags. The authors demonstrate that word embeddings do encode PoS information and that analyzing classification errors can reveal inconsistencies in annotation guidelines or highlight ‘soft’ PoS boundaries. The detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of misclassifications and attempting to contextualize them linguistically, is a notable strength of the work. 

The primary contribution of this paper, as I see it, lies in the thorough exploration of *what* errors a distributional model makes when predicting PoS tags, and the linguistic insights gleaned from those errors. While the overarching claim that embeddings contain PoS information is not novel, the detailed analysis of misclassifications – particularly the identification of potential annotation inconsistencies – is valuable. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that reasonable PoS prediction accuracy can be achieved without relying on contextual information, based solely on distributional representations. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Detailed Error Analysis:** The comprehensive examination of misclassified words is the strongest aspect of the paper. The authors don't simply report accuracy numbers; they delve into *why* the model makes mistakes, offering potential linguistic explanations. This is a significant contribution to understanding the limitations and capabilities of distributional models in representing grammatical categories.
*   **Practical Implications:** The finding that distributional models can flag potential annotation errors has practical implications for corpus linguistics and NLP development. The ability to automatically identify potentially problematic cases could streamline corpus refinement efforts.
*   **Robustness Demonstration:** Achieving reasonable PoS prediction accuracy without any contextual information is a testament to the strength of the distributional representations learned from the BNC.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the experiments are clearly described, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core claim – that embeddings capture PoS information – is well-established in prior work on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012). The paper insufficiently acknowledges this body of work.
*   **Framing of Research Question:** The paper frames the investigation as “redefining PoS classes.” A more appropriate framing would be to assess whether embedding-based distinctions can *complement* existing PoS tagsets or improve parsing performance, rather than suggesting a complete redefinition. The justification for using the BNC's original tags, rather than exploring improvements to parsing directly, is weak.
*   **Contradictory Statements:** The claim that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” contradicts established findings in areas like CoVe and word embedding literature which clearly demonstrate substantial syntactic information encoded within these representations.
*   **Questionable UPOS Conversion:** The switch to UPOS is not adequately justified. The finer-grained original BNC tags might have revealed more nuanced patterns in the error analysis.
*   **Footnote 3 Ambiguity:** The footnote regarding failed attempts is unclear and needs clarification. Details about who conducted the failures, the criteria used, and the relevance of Brown clusters should be provided.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the PoS classifier compare to baseline PoS taggers trained on the BNC (e.g., those available in NLTK or spaCy) when evaluated on the same test set?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used to define “ambiguous” lemmas in Section 5, and why this filtering step was necessary?
3.  Why was the BNC chosen as the corpus, given that its original annotation was performed automatically? Would a manually tagged corpus have yielded different results or insights?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please replace sentential citation style (\citealt) with \newcite{}.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of using only distributional information for PoS tagging.
