## Review for CONLL 2016: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) boundaries. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors do encode PoS information. They leverage misclassifications to identify linguistic inconsistencies in annotation and support the notion of ‘soft’ PoS affiliations, and further explore the distribution of PoS information across vector components. The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, lies in the detailed error analysis and resulting insights into potential inconsistencies within the BNC’s PoS annotation scheme. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration, through classification, of the connection between distributional semantics and traditional linguistic categories.

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed and insightful error analysis:** The analysis of misclassified words (Section 4) is a strong point of the paper. The authors move beyond simply reporting classification accuracy and delve into *why* the classifier makes errors. Identifying cases like currency amounts incorrectly tagged as nouns, or adverbs erroneously labeled as adjectives, provides tangible evidence of ambiguities and potential inconsistencies within the BNC’s annotation guidelines. This level of analysis is valuable and goes beyond a standard evaluation.
2. **Empirical validation of distributional hypothesis in relation to PoS:** The paper provides a clear and compelling demonstration that word embeddings, trained in an unsupervised manner, capture information related to PoS. Achieving an F-score of 0.979 on the training set and 0.91 on unseen data strongly supports the idea that distributional semantics can be leveraged for PoS-related tasks.
3. **Exploration of distributed representation of PoS information:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components (Section 5), rather than concentrated in a few, is interesting and contributes to a better understanding of how embeddings encode grammatical information. This challenges a simplistic view of embeddings and suggests a more nuanced relationship between semantic and syntactic features.
4. **Practical application of findings:** The authors suggest a potential application of their findings to resource-poor languages, proposing to use distributional models to pre-tag corpora with limited manually annotated data. This highlights a potential practical benefit of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty regarding the core finding:** The claim that distributional vectors contain information about PoS is not novel. A substantial body of work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) has already demonstrated this connection through distributional PoS induction methods like clustering. The paper lacks a thorough engagement with this prior work, diminishing the perceived novelty of the central result.
2. **Framing and motivation are lacking:** The paper frames its investigation as “redefining” PoS classes, but it doesn’t fully articulate *why* this redefinition is needed or how it might impact downstream NLP tasks. The authors do not address why corpora are PoS-tagged in the first place (usually for parsing and other syntactic processing) and should instead focus on whether embedding-based distinctions improve performance on such tasks compared to using the original annotations or UPOS tags.
3. **Contradiction with established literature:** The statement that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” is demonstrably incorrect. Prior work (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.) has consistently shown that word embeddings encode significant syntactic information. This contradiction weakens the paper’s theoretical foundation.
4. **Questionable methodological choices:** The decision to switch to the Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset (UPOS) is not adequately justified. The fine-grained distinctions present in the original BNC tags may be more informative and revealing for this type of analysis. The authors should explore the impact of this tagset conversion on their results.
5. **Insufficient discussion of related techniques:** Footnote 3 mentions failed attempts at clustering, but lacks crucial details. It’s unclear who conducted these attempts, what constituted “failure,” and why alternative approaches like Brown clusters (which align well with UPOS) weren't considered.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the classifications achieved using your approach compare to using the original BNC tags or UPOS tags in a downstream NLP task, such as parsing?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the decision to switch to the UPOS tagset? What impact did this conversion have on your findings?
3.  The paper highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Can you quantify the prevalence of these inconsistencies (e.g., percentage of instances where your model predicts a more plausible tag)?
4.  Could you discuss the limitations of using a purely distributional approach for PoS tagging, particularly in handling rare words or ambiguous cases? How could these limitations be addressed?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please replace sentential citation style (e.g., "Aarts et al.,? \Q2008\E") with `\newcite{}`.
*   Further exploration of the low-frequency tail of the vocabulary is recommended. Analyzing errors made on less frequent words could reveal additional insights into the model’s limitations.
*   Consider exploring combinations of distributional features with other features, such as morphological information, to improve generalization and address the challenges posed by rare words.
