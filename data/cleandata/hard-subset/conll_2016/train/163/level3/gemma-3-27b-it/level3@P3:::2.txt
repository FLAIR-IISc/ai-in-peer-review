## Review

This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, investigating whether distributional semantics can reveal insights into grammatical categories and annotation inconsistencies. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings and perform an error analysis to identify outliers. While the detailed error analysis is a valuable contribution, the paper suffers from a lack of novelty, a questionable methodological choice, and a somewhat narrow framing of the research question.

**Contributions:**

1. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The most significant contribution of this work is the thorough and insightful error analysis of the PoS classifier’s predictions. The authors meticulously examine misclassified word types, identifying patterns related to annotation inconsistencies (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns) and boundary cases between PoS categories (e.g., participles, adjectives vs. nouns). This analysis provides concrete examples and linguistic observations that are genuinely valuable.
2. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper reaffirms and demonstrates, with a specific methodology, that word embeddings do encode information about PoS. While not a novel finding in itself, the quantitative results (F-score of 0.979 on the training set, 0.91 on unseen BNC data) and the successful training of a PoS classifier based solely on embeddings provide empirical support for this claim.
3. **Exploration of Distributed PoS Representation:** The investigation into the distribution of PoS-related information across embedding dimensions (finding it’s distributed among at least 100 components) offers a nuance that moves beyond simplistic assumptions of a few key features encoding grammatical information.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Methodology:** The experimental setup, including the use of a well-established corpus (BNC), careful data preprocessing (lemmatization, UPOS conversion), and robust evaluation metrics (F-score, accuracy), demonstrates a sound methodological approach.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The depth of the error analysis sets this work apart. The authors don't simply report accuracy scores but delve into specific error cases, offering linguistic interpretations and highlighting potential issues with existing annotations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the experimental process and findings. The visualizations (Figures 1 and 2) are helpful in illustrating the concept of prototypical vectors.
* **Practical Implications:** The idea of using distributional models for preliminary tagging in resource-poor languages is a potentially valuable application of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The core claim that word embeddings contain PoS information is not novel. A substantial body of work (Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) already established this connection through distributional PoS induction methods. The paper inadequately acknowledges and discusses this related work, diminishing the perceived originality of the findings.
* **Questionable Framing & UPOS Shift:** The paper frames the problem as "redefining PoS classes" with embeddings. However, a more relevant and impactful framing would be to assess whether embedding-based representations *improve* parsing or other NLP tasks *compared to* utilizing traditional PoS tags or the UPOS tagset itself. The switch to UPOS feels arbitrary and potentially obscures more nuanced distinctions present in the original BNC tagset.
* **Misleading Statement on Semantic vs. Syntactic Content:** The assertion that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" contradicts extensive prior research (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.) demonstrating significant syntactic information encoded in word embeddings. This contradicts even information presented in Section 2 of the paper.
* **Insufficient Analysis of Low-Frequency Words:** The analysis focuses heavily on frequent word/tag pairs. A crucial omission is a detailed examination of errors made on low-frequency (tail) words, where distributional methods are often expected to struggle. The classifier’s performance on rare words is a key indicator of its generalization ability.
* **Unclear Footnote 3:** Footnote 3 regarding failed attempts at clustering is unclear. It’s ambiguous whether the failures were experienced by the authors or others, what the specific failure criteria were, and why Brown clusters (which align well with UPOS) were not considered.
* **Trivialization of Proper vs. Common Noun Distinction:** The claim that the difference between proper and common nouns is trivialized by function word distributions lacks sufficient justification. While acknowledged, the analysis doesn't fully explore why the model *still* manages to distinguish them to a considerable degree.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the existing literature on distributional PoS induction, how do you position this work as making a novel contribution? What specific aspect of your approach or findings differentiates it from prior work?
2.  Why was the decision made to switch to the UPOS tagset? How did this impact the granularity of your analysis and potentially the types of insights you could obtain?
3.  Could you provide more details regarding the failed clustering attempts mentioned in Footnote 3? Specifically, what methods were tried, what were the failure criteria, and why were Brown clusters not explored?
4.  What further analysis have you performed on the classifier’s performance with low-frequency words? Do you observe any systematic patterns in the types of errors made on rare words?
5. What alternative morphological or distributional features (beyond lemmatization) have you considered or plan to investigate to improve generalization to rare words?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please change all instances of sentential citation style (e.g., "Mikolov et al., 2013b") to the \newcite{} style.
*   The discussion of Tables 3 & 4 feels disjointed from the preceding analysis. Its relevance needs to be better articulated.
