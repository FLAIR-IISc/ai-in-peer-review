## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors train word embeddings on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, then utilize a logistic classifier to predict POS tags from these embeddings. The evaluation is comprehensive, employing cross-validation on the training corpus and testing on independent corpora. The results demonstrate that word embeddings indeed encode POS information, allowing for the identification of words with atypical distributional patterns for their assigned POS tag. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the empirical validation and quantification of the intuitive connection between distributional semantics and grammatical categories. While the idea that word meaning is reflected in context – and thus potentially in embeddings – is established, this paper provides concrete evidence and a quantitative assessment of this relationship for POS tags. A secondary contribution is the insightful analysis of classifier errors, which reveals potential inconsistencies in existing POS annotation schemes and highlights ‘soft’ boundaries between POS categories. Finally, the finding that POS information is distributed across numerous vector components, rather than concentrated in a few, is a notable observation regarding the internal structure of word embeddings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors present a well-designed experimental setup with robust evaluation metrics (F-score) and cross-validation. Testing on multiple corpora strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The logical flow of argumentation, combined with clear explanations of methods and results, contributes significantly to its readability.
*   **Interesting Error Analysis:**  The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a major strength. It goes beyond simply reporting accuracy and provides valuable linguistic insights into the complexities of POS tagging. The identification of annotation inconsistencies is particularly compelling.
*   **Empirical Grounding:** The work provides empirical support for the notion that POS categories are not always sharply defined, aligning with theoretical perspectives on the continuum of grammatical categories.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core methodology – training embeddings and using a classifier to predict POS tags – is relatively straightforward and builds upon existing work in the field. The paper doesn’t introduce novel NLP or machine learning techniques.
*   **Reliance on Existing Corpora:** The results are dependent on the quality and annotation scheme of the BNC and UD Treebank. While the BNC is well-established, the potential impact of its specific tagging conventions should be acknowledged more explicitly.
*   **Superficial Exploration of Vector Components:** While the paper notes that POS information is distributed across many vector components, the analysis of *which* components are most informative remains limited. 

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of using the BNC's CLAWS4-based tagging as a gold standard, given the identified inconsistencies?
*   You mention the potential for applying this approach to resource-poor languages. Could you discuss the challenges of obtaining even a small set of manually tagged words for initial training in such scenarios?
*   What specific linguistic features do you hypothesize are captured by the most informative embedding components identified in Section 5?



**Additional Comments:**

The authors should definitely include citations to Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016) as suggested by the reviewer guidelines. These works provide valuable context regarding POS tagging and induction using embeddings.
