### Summary of the paper
This paper explores the extent to which part-of-speech (POS) information is encoded within distributional word embeddings. The authors train a Skip-gram word embedding model on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC), where tokens are represented as `lemma_POS` pairs using the Universal Dependencies tagset. They then train a logistic regression classifier to predict the POS tag for a given word vector. The primary goal is not to build a state-of-the-art tagger, but to use the classifier's performance and, more importantly, its errors to analyze the relationship between distributional semantics and grammatical categories. The analysis of misclassified words reveals interesting cases of linguistic ambiguity, potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC, and words that lie on the boundary between traditional POS classes. A second experiment trains a classifier on embeddings of raw lemmas to show that a simple, context-agnostic tagger can achieve reasonable performance, demonstrating the strength of the POS signal in the embeddings alone. The paper concludes that POS information is robustly, yet diffusely, represented across many dimensions of the word vectors.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  A detailed qualitative analysis of the errors made by a POS classifier trained on word embeddings. This analysis provides strong empirical evidence for the notion of "graded" or "soft" POS membership and successfully identifies linguistically interesting "outlier" words and potential inconsistencies in a widely-used corpus.
2.  An empirical demonstration that POS affiliation is distributed across many dimensions of a word vector, rather than being concentrated in a few components. This reinforces the distributed nature of representation in such models.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear and Well-Executed Experiments:** The experimental design is simple, clear, and effectively serves the paper's main goal of analysis rather than performance. The methodology—training embeddings, applying a standard classifier, and analyzing the confusion matrix—is sound and easy to follow. The results are presented clearly and the analysis is methodical and insightful.
2.  **Valuable Qualitative Analysis:** The core strength of this paper lies in Section 4, where the authors perform a deep dive into the classifier's errors. This analysis moves beyond simply reporting accuracy scores and provides valuable linguistic insights. For example, the discussion of nouns that behave adjectivally (e.g., 'hypertext'), verbs that are predominantly used as passive participles, and the inconsistent tagging of currency amounts and years provides concrete, data-driven evidence for well-known linguistic ambiguities and potential annotation issues. The comparison with the Stanford Tagger's error patterns effectively highlights the unique perspective offered by the distributional approach.
3.  **Strong Empirical Grounding for Linguistic Concepts:** The work provides solid quantitative support for the linguistic idea that POS categories are not discrete but exist on a continuum. By identifying words that distributionally cluster with a different POS class than their gold label, the authors offer a method for systematically finding and studying these boundary cases. This is a useful contribution to the field of quantitative and corpus-based linguistics.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The primary weakness of this submission is its lack of methodological novelty for a venue like CONLL. The idea that word embeddings capture syntactic and POS information is well-established in the community. The methods employed—training word2vec embeddings and applying a logistic regression classifier—are standard techniques. While the analysis is interesting, the paper does not propose a new model, algorithm, or technique for an NLP task. This makes the paper feel more like a linguistic analysis paper using computational tools rather than a core NLP contribution.
2.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work:** The related work section is sparse and misses several key contemporary papers on POS tagging that also leverage word embeddings. By 2016, neural network-based POS taggers that use word embeddings as input features were common (e.g., Ling et al., 2015; Plank et al., 2016). The paper would be much stronger if it positioned its findings in the context of this work. For instance, it could discuss how its analysis of embeddings *in isolation* complements or explains the success of more complex, context-aware models that use these same embeddings. Without this context, the work feels somewhat disconnected from the state of the art in POS tagging research.
3.  **Scope of Claims:** While the analysis is interesting, it is based on a single corpus (BNC), one language (English), and one embedding model (Skip-gram). The conclusions about the nature of POS representation are framed quite generally, but the empirical support is narrow. It would be more convincing if the authors discussed whether these findings are expected to generalize to other languages, corpora, or embedding architectures (e.g., GloVe, fastText).

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the novelty of your contribution specifically for the NLP community at CONLL? Given that the methods are standard, is the main contribution the linguistic analysis itself, and if so, how does it advance NLP research beyond confirming known properties of embeddings?
2.  The paper is missing references to contemporary neural POS taggers that heavily rely on word embeddings. How does your analysis of what embeddings capture *in isolation* relate to the way these embeddings are used in more complex, context-aware sequence labeling models? Does your work suggest ways to improve those models?
3.  You mention that initial attempts to *cluster* word vectors failed. Could you provide a few more details on why you think this approach was unsuccessful? This negative result is itself interesting and could strengthen the justification for using a supervised classifier to probe the vector space.
