### Review

**Summary & Contributions**

This paper investigates the relationship between Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes and distributional word embeddings. Using the British National Corpus (BNC), the authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings and conduct an analysis of the classification errors to identify words whose distributional behavior is atypical for their annotated class.

The primary contribution of this work is its detailed and insightful qualitative error analysis. This analysis successfully serves as a diagnostic tool, revealing interesting linguistic phenomena such as graded class membership (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives) and exposing potential inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation guidelines regarding entities like years and currency amounts.

**Strengths**

1.  The strongest aspect of this paper is the qualitative error analysis in Section 4. The authors effectively leverage misclassifications not as failures, but as probes to uncover linguistically meaningful patterns. The discussion provides a valuable, fine-grained perspective on the fuzzy boundaries of PoS classes and specific corpus annotation artifacts.
2.  The paper is well-written, and the experiments are clearly described. The core idea of using a classifier for this diagnostic purpose is straightforward and effective for generating the outlier data used in the analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The paper's claims of novelty are severely undermined by its omission of a substantial body of related work on unsupervised and distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011). The central claim that distributional vectors contain PoS information has been a foundational assumption in that field for over a decade. Consequently, the experiment in Section 5, which builds a simple PoS predictor, lacks novelty and appears unaware of established prior art.
2.  The work lacks a clear motivation tied to downstream applications. It fails to address *why* corpora are PoS-tagged in the first place (e.g., for parsing). A more compelling framing would investigate whether the embedding-derived distinctions improve performance on a task like parsing compared to using the original BNC tags or the mapped UPOS tags. The analysis remains isolated from any practical utility.
3.  The analysis and some core claims have several shortcomings. The statement that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" is inaccurate and contradicts both prior work and the paper’s own findings. The analysis focuses only on frequent words, ignoring the more difficult and critical problem of generalization to low-frequency words. Furthermore, the decision to coarsen tags to UPOS discards potentially interesting fine-grained distinctions that could have been explored.
