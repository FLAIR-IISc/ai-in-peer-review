### Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates the relationship between pre-trained word embeddings and Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes. The primary method involves training a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its distributional vector, which was trained on the BNC. The authors demonstrate that embeddings do contain significant PoS information, achieving high classification accuracy. The main focus of the paper is a detailed error analysis of this classifier. By examining the "outliers"—words that are consistently misclassified—the authors identify cases of graded PoS membership (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives), inconsistencies in the original BNC annotation, and systematic differences between word subclasses (e.g., years behaving more like nouns than numerals). A second experiment explores building a simple PoS predictor using only word embeddings, arguing it could be useful for low-resource settings.

### Contributions
1.  **A Strong Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper's main contribution is the detailed analysis in Section 4. Instead of treating classification errors as simple failures, the authors use them as a tool to probe the fuzzy boundaries of PoS categories. This analysis successfully uncovers linguistically interesting phenomena, such as graded category membership and potential inconsistencies in a widely-used corpus, providing concrete evidence for the "soft" nature of PoS classes.
2.  **A Simple Method for Identifying Annotation Inconsistencies:** The work provides a straightforward, replicable methodology for using distributional models to audit PoS-tagged corpora. By highlighting words whose distributional patterns conflict with their assigned tags, the approach serves as a valuable diagnostic tool for corpus creators and linguists. The comparison with the Stanford Tagger's error patterns effectively shows that this method uncovers a different and complementary set of issues.

### Strengths
1.  **Insightful and Detailed Error Analysis:** The core strength of this paper lies in its thorough examination of the classifier's mistakes. The authors move beyond simple accuracy metrics to provide a qualitative analysis that reveals genuine linguistic insights. For example, identifying that nouns like 'starboard' have "adjectival" contexts or that verbs frequently used in the passive are misclassified as adjectives are compelling demonstrations of how distributional representations capture nuanced syntactic behavior. This makes the paper more of a linguistic investigation than a simple engineering exercise.
2.  **Effective Use of a Simple Model as an Analytical Probe:** The choice of a simple logistic regression classifier is well-justified. It serves not as a state-of-the-art tagger but as a transparent probe into the information encoded in the embeddings. This simplicity allows the authors to focus squarely on the relationship between distributional patterns and PoS tags without confounding factors from a more complex model architecture.
3.  **Valuable Comparison to a Standard Tagger:** The inclusion of an error analysis for the Stanford PoS Tagger is a strong addition. It demonstrates that the authors' distributional approach is not merely rediscovering known ambiguities but is highlighting a different set of boundary cases (e.g., SCONJ->ADV, ADP->ADJ). This comparison successfully argues for the unique value of using embedding-based classifiers as a complementary tool for linguistic analysis.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Significant Omission of Related Work and Lack of Novelty:** The paper's primary claims about novelty are severely undermined by its failure to engage with the extensive literature on unsupervised and distributional PoS induction. The central finding that word embeddings contain PoS information has been a foundational assumption in that field for over a decade (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012). The experiment in Section 5, which builds a PoS predictor from embeddings, essentially re-implements a simplified version of this prior work without acknowledging it, making its results and contributions appear far more novel than they are.
2.  **Misleading Framing and Weak Motivation:** The paper's motivation is framed around "redefining" PoS classes, but it never connects this back to the primary utility of PoS tags in NLP: improving downstream tasks. A much stronger motivation would be to investigate whether the graded distinctions discovered from embeddings can lead to better parsing or machine translation performance compared to the original discrete tags. Furthermore, the statement in the introduction that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" is a significant mischaracterization of prior work (e.g., Mikolov et al.'s syntactic analogies) and directly contradicts the premise of the authors' own work, which is focused on extracting syntactic PoS information.
3.  **Methodological and Analytical Limitations:** The analysis, while strong, suffers from a few questionable decisions.
    a. The switch to the coarse UPOS tagset may obscure more subtle and interesting distinctions that would be visible with the original, fine-grained BNC tags.
    b. The analysis focuses on relatively frequent words (frequency > 100), missing an opportunity to investigate the significant challenge of PoS tagging for rare words in the long tail, where distributional signals are weakest and errors are most common.
    c. Some claims are insufficiently justified. For example, the finding that proper nouns differ from common nouns could be trivially explained by shallow distributional cues (e.g., the absence of determiners like "the") rather than a deeper semantic distinction, and this possibility is not explored.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Footnote 3 states that attempts to "naively cluster word vectors... inescapably failed." Could you clarify whose attempts these were (your own or others'), what clustering algorithms were used, and what defined "failure"? Did you consider methods like Brown clustering, which have historically shown a strong correspondence with PoS classes?
2.  Could you elaborate on the decision to map BNC tags to the coarse UPOS tagset? While this simplifies the classification task, it seems to run counter to the goal of finding nuanced, graded distinctions, which might be more apparent with a finer-grained tagset.
3.  Given the large body of existing work on distributional PoS induction, could you clarify what you see as the novel contribution of the experiment in Section 5, which builds a PoS predictor trained on a small seed set of unambiguous words?
