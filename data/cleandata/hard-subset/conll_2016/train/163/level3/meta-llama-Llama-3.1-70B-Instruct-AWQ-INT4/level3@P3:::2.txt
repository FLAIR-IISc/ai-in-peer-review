This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional semantic models can be used to discover novel insights about grammar and syntax. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags for words based on their embeddings, achieving a high weighted average F-score. The analysis of errors reveals interesting patterns, including inconsistencies in the annotation process and 'outliers' that tend to exhibit behavior similar to another part of speech.

The paper's strongest contribution is its detailed error analysis, which provides valuable insights into the nature of PoS boundaries and the limitations of traditional annotation schemes. The authors' use of a distributional predictor to identify systematic errors and inconsistencies in PoS tags is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Firstly, it fails to address why corpora like the British National Corpus (BNC) were PoS-tagged in the first place, and how the proposed approach relates to traditional NLP tasks such as parsing. The authors should reframe their work to explore whether embedding distinctions help parsing compared to original tags/UPOS.

Secondly, the paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction, including Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom & Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012). This omission undermines the novelty of the results presented in Section 5.

Thirdly, the claim that distributional vectors contain PoS information is not novel, given prior clustering/induction work. The statement that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" is also incorrect, as prior work (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.) shows substantial syntactic information.

The authors' decision to switch to Universal PoS (UPOS) tags is also questionable, as fine-grained original tag distinctions may be more interesting. Furthermore, Footnote 3 is unclear, and the claim that proper nouns differ from common nouns is trivialized by function-word distributions.

The analysis focuses on frequent word/tag pairs, but the paper should examine low-frequency tail words and the classifier's errors there. Exploring alternatives to pure distributional and lemmatization-based morphological features to better generalize PoS for rare words is also necessary.

In terms of formatting, the paper should change the sentential citation style to \newcite{}.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A detailed error analysis of a distributional predictor for PoS tags, revealing interesting patterns and inconsistencies in annotation.
2. A demonstration of the potential of distributional semantic models to discover novel insights about grammar and syntax.
3. A exploration of the connection between PoS tags and word embeddings.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The detailed error analysis provides valuable insights into the nature of PoS boundaries and the limitations of traditional annotation schemes.
2. The use of a distributional predictor to identify systematic errors and inconsistencies in PoS tags is a significant contribution to the field.
3. The paper demonstrates the potential of distributional semantic models to discover novel insights about grammar and syntax.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The omission of substantial related work in distributional PoS induction undermines the novelty of the results.
2. The claim that distributional vectors contain PoS information is not novel, given prior clustering/induction work.
3. The statement that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" is incorrect, and the decision to switch to UPOS tags is questionable.

Questions to authors:

1. How does the proposed approach relate to traditional NLP tasks such as parsing?
2. Can you provide more justification for the claim that proper nouns differ from common nouns?
3. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current approach, such as the focus on frequent word/tag pairs and the use of pure distributional and lemmatization-based morphological features?
