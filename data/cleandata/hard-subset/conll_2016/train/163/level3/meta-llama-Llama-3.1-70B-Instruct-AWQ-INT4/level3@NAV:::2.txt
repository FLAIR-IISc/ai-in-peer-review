**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags for words based on their embeddings and achieve high accuracy. They also analyze the errors made by the classifier and identify patterns that reveal inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines. The paper shows that distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation and can be used to detect outliers and improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Connection between PoS tags and word embeddings**: The paper demonstrates a strong connection between PoS tags and word embeddings, showing that distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation.
2. **Error analysis and outlier detection**: The authors analyze the errors made by the classifier and identify patterns that reveal inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines.
3. **Improving PoS taggers**: The paper shows that distributional vectors can be used to improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers by detecting outliers and correcting errors.

**Strengths**

1. **Strong empirical results**: The paper presents strong empirical results, demonstrating high accuracy in predicting PoS tags and identifying patterns in errors.
2. **Detailed error analysis**: The authors provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by the classifier, which reveals interesting patterns and inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines.
3. **Implications for PoS taggers**: The paper shows that distributional vectors can be used to improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers, which has implications for natural language processing tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper's claim that distributional vectors contain PoS information is not novel, as prior work has shown similar results.
2. **Omission of related work**: The paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction, which weakens the novelty and impact of the results.
3. **Questionable decisions**: The authors' decision to switch to UPOS and ignore low-frequency words may be questionable, as it may limit the generalizability of the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the lack of novelty in their results, and what new insights do they bring to the field?
2. Can the authors provide more justification for their decision to switch to UPOS and ignore low-frequency words?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their work to other languages and explore the effects of different hyperparameters on the performance of distributional models?
