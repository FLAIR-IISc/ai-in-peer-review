**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and analyze the errors to identify outliers and inconsistencies in the annotation process. They also investigate the distribution of PoS information in word embeddings and find that it is spread across multiple components. The paper concludes that distributional models can be used to improve PoS tagging and identify systematic errors in annotation.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Analysis of outliers and inconsistencies in PoS annotation**: The paper provides a detailed error analysis of outliers and inconsistencies in the annotation process, which can be useful for improving PoS tagging and identifying systematic errors.
2. **Investigation of PoS information in word embeddings**: The paper investigates the distribution of PoS information in word embeddings and finds that it is spread across multiple components, which can be useful for improving PoS tagging and understanding the representation of language in distributional models.
3. **Development of a PoS tagger based on word embeddings**: The paper develops a PoS tagger based on word embeddings, which can be useful for improving PoS tagging and providing an alternative to traditional PoS tagging methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Detailed error analysis**: The paper provides a detailed error analysis of outliers and inconsistencies in the annotation process, which can be useful for improving PoS tagging and identifying systematic errors.
2. **Investigation of PoS information in word embeddings**: The paper investigates the distribution of PoS information in word embeddings, which can be useful for improving PoS tagging and understanding the representation of language in distributional models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in results**: The paper's results on the connection between PoS tags and word embeddings and the development of a PoS tagger based on word embeddings are not novel, as similar work has been done in the past.
2. **Omission of related work**: The paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction, which can make the results seem less impressive.
3. **Questionable decision to switch to UPOS**: The paper's decision to switch to Universal Part-of-Speech (UPOS) tags is questionable, as the fine-grained original tag distinctions may be more interesting.
4. **Minor formatting issues**: The paper has minor formatting issues, such as the use of sentential citation style instead of \newcite{}.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more justification for the claim that proper nouns differ from common nouns, beyond the trivial observation that they have different function-word distributions?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of low-frequency tail words and the classifier's errors there?
3. Can you explore alternatives to pure distributional and lemmatization-based morphological features to better generalize PoS for rare words?
4. How do you plan to extend the experiment to other languages typologically different from English?
