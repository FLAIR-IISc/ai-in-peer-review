- Trains a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, achieving a high weighted average F-score.  
- Detailed error analysis uncovers annotation inconsistencies and “outliers” that behave like another PoS—highlighted as the paper’s strongest contribution.  
- Uses a distributional predictor to identify systematic tagging errors and to probe PoS boundaries and annotation limits.  
- Fails to explain why corpora (e.g., BNC) were PoS-tagged and does not relate findings to traditional tasks like parsing; should test whether embeddings help parsing.  
- Omits substantial prior work on distributional PoS induction (Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012), undermining novelty.  
- Claim that embeddings contain PoS information is not novel given earlier clustering/induction studies.  
- The statement that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” is incorrect—prior work shows strong syntactic signal in embeddings.  
- Decision to switch to Universal PoS (UPOS) tags is questionable; fine-grained original tags might be more informative.  
- Footnote 3 is unclear; the asserted distinction between proper and common nouns is trivialized by function-word distribution differences.  
- Analysis concentrates on frequent word/tag pairs; the paper should examine low-frequency tail words and classifier errors on rare items.  
- Needs to explore alternatives beyond pure distributional and lemmatization-based morphological features to better generalize PoS for rare words.  
- Formatting suggestion: change sentential citation style to \newcite{}.  
- Summarized contributions claimed by authors: detailed error analysis of a distributional PoS predictor; demonstration that distributional models can reveal grammar/syntax insights; exploration of PoS–embedding connections.  
- Reviewer questions: how does this relate to parsing; justify the proper vs. common noun claim; how will authors address focus on frequent pairs and feature limitations?
