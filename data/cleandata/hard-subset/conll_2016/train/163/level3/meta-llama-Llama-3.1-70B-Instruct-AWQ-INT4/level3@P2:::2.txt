**Paper Summary and Main Contributions**

The paper "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models" explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and analyze the errors to identify inconsistencies in the annotation process. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. **Error analysis of outliers**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the errors made by the classifier, which reveals inconsistencies in the annotation process and identifies words that exhibit different distributional patterns than their annotated PoS tags.
2. **Distributional vectors as part-of-speech predictors**: The authors demonstrate that distributional vectors can be used to predict PoS tags, even without any pre-existing tags as hints.
3. **Robustness of PoS information in word embeddings**: The paper shows that PoS information is distributed among several components of the word embeddings, making it a robust feature for prediction.

**Strengths**

1. **Detailed error analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the errors made by the classifier, which is essential for understanding the limitations and potential biases of the approach.
2. **Novel application of distributional vectors**: The authors demonstrate a new application of distributional vectors, which is predicting PoS tags without any pre-existing tags as hints.
3. **Robustness of results**: The paper shows that the results are robust across different datasets and evaluation metrics, which increases the confidence in the findings.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in Section 5**: The paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction, which makes the results in Section 5 less novel.
2. **Incorrect statement about embedding relations**: The paper states that embedding relations are "mostly semantic," which contradicts prior work that shows substantial syntactic information in word embeddings.
3. **Questionable decision to switch to UPOS**: The paper switches to the Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset (UPOS) without justification, which may not be the best choice for the task at hand.
4. **Limited analysis of low-frequency tail words**: The paper focuses on frequent word/tag pairs, but it would be beneficial to examine low-frequency tail words and the classifier's errors there.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more justification for switching to UPOS and how it affects the results?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the approach, such as the reliance on frequent word/tag pairs?
3. Can you provide more details about the failed attempts to cluster word vectors into the number of clusters equal to the number of PoS tags?
4. How do you plan to explore alternatives to pure distributional and lemmatization-based morphological features to better generalize PoS for rare words?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the results are interesting. However, there are some minor formatting issues, such as the sentential citation style, which should be changed to \newcite{}. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more discussion about the implications of the results and potential applications in NLP tasks such as parsing.
