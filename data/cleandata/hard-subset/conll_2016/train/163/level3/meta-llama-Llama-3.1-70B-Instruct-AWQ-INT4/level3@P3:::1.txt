This paper presents an investigation into the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors aim to show that distributional information in word vectors encodes POS labels, and that this information can be used to predict POS tags for words.

The authors use the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags as their dataset. They train word embeddings on this corpus using a Continuous Skipgram model and then use a logistic classifier to predict POS tags for words based on their embeddings. The results show that the distributional vectors do contain information about POS affiliation, with a weighted average F-score of 0.979 on the training set and 0.91 on a test set of unseen data.

The authors also analyze the errors made by the classifier and find that they often reveal interesting patterns and inconsistencies in the annotation process. For example, they find that proper nouns are often misclassified as common nouns, and that some words have distributional patterns that are different from their annotated POS tags. They also find that the classifier can detect systematic errors in POS-tagged corpora, such as adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs.

The authors also experiment with training a POS tagger on a distributional model without using pre-existing POS tags as hints. They find that the classifier can still achieve an accuracy of 0.938 on the training set and 0.84 on the test set, even though it only has access to the word's distributional vector and not its context in the sentence.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. Providing empirical grounding for the notion of POS and showing that distributional information in word vectors can be used to predict POS tags.
2. Demonstrating that distributional models can be used to detect systematic errors and inconsistencies in POS-tagged corpora.
3. Showing that POS affiliation is distributed among many components of the word embeddings, not concentrated in one or two specific features.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The authors present a clear and well-written paper that is easy to follow.
2. The results are clearly presented and analyzed, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the implications of their findings.
3. The authors use a robust evaluation methodology, including cross-validation on the same corpus and testing on other corpora.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The authors do not present any new NLP/ML methods, and the experiments are relatively straightforward.
2. The authors could have cited more POS tagging/induction work using embeddings, such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016).

Questions to authors:

1. How do the authors plan to extend this work to other languages and corpora?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the correspondence between particular embedding components and POS affiliation?
3. How do the authors think this work can be applied in practice, for example in POS tagging for resource-poor languages?

Additional comments:

1. The authors could have provided more discussion on the implications of their findings for linguistic theory and the nature of POS categories.
2. The authors could have explored other methods for predicting POS tags, such as using recurrent neural networks or other machine learning models.
