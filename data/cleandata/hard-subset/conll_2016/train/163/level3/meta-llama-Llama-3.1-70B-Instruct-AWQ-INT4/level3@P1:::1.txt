**Summary of the Paper**

The paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors train a word embedding model on the British National Corpus (BNC) and use the resulting vectors to train a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags. The classifier achieves high accuracy, indicating that the word embeddings contain information about POS tags. The authors also analyze the errors made by the classifier and find that they often reveal interesting patterns and inconsistencies in the annotation of the corpus.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Empirical evidence for POS encoding in word embeddings**: The paper provides strong evidence that word embeddings contain information about POS tags, which is a fundamental concept in linguistics.
2. **Analysis of POS boundaries**: The authors show that the classifier's errors often reveal interesting patterns and inconsistencies in the annotation of the corpus, highlighting the complexity of POS boundaries.
3. **Robustness of POS prediction**: The authors demonstrate that the classifier can predict POS tags with high accuracy, even when trained on a limited set of data.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear and well-written paper**: The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The authors provide convincing evidence for their claims, using a range of evaluation metrics and datasets.
3. **Interesting analysis of POS boundaries**: The authors' analysis of the classifier's errors provides new insights into the complexity of POS boundaries.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper's methodology is straightforward and does not introduce any new NLP or ML methods.
2. **Limited contribution to NLP/ML**: While the paper provides empirical evidence for a fundamental concept in linguistics, it does not contribute significantly to the development of NLP or ML methods.
3. **Missing literature**: The authors could have cited more relevant work on POS tagging and induction using embeddings.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their work to other languages, and what challenges do they anticipate?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the analysis of POS boundaries and how they relate to linguistic theory?
3. How do the authors think their work can be used to improve POS tagging and induction in NLP applications?
