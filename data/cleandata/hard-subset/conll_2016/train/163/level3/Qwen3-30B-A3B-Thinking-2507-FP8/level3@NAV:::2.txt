

**Review**

This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings using the British National Corpus (BNC). The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings, analyze errors to identify "outliers," and claim these reveal "soft" PoS boundaries. The main contributions claimed are: (1) demonstrating embeddings contain PoS information, (2) identifying annotation inconsistencies through error analysis, and (3) showing PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions.

Strengths: The error analysis of outliers is thorough and reveals genuine annotation inconsistencies (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, verbs mistaken for adjectives). The demonstration of PoS information distribution across multiple vector dimensions is a reasonable finding. The cross-corpus validation on Universal Dependencies Treebank adds credibility.

Weaknesses: The paper fails to address why BNC was PoS-tagged (for parsing/NLP tasks), making the comparison to parsing performance irrelevant. The core claim about embeddings containing PoS information lacks novelty, as prior work (Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) has already established this. The claim that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" contradicts prior work (Mikolov et al., CoVe) showing substantial syntactic content. The switch to UPOS without justification is problematic, as fine-grained tags may be more linguistically interesting. The analysis focuses exclusively on high-frequency words, omitting low-frequency tail words where PoS ambiguity is likely higher. The omission of key related work undermines the novelty claim.

Questions to authors: Why was the BNC PoS tagging process not contextualized within NLP applications (e.g., parsing), which is the primary purpose of PoS tagging? How do your findings specifically improve parsing performance compared to traditional PoS tags? Why was the switch to UPOS made without justification, especially when fine-grained tags might better reveal linguistic phenomena?
