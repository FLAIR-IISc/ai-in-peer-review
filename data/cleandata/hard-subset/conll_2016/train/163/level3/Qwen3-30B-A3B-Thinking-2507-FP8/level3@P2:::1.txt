

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates whether word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus contain information about part-of-speech (POS) classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags based on word embeddings, demonstrating that the model achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979) on a lemmatized BNC corpus annotated with Universal POS tags. The main contributions are: 

1. Empirical demonstration that distributional semantic models contain robust information about POS categories, supporting the notion of "soft" or "graded" POS boundaries rather than strict categorical distinctions.
2. Systematic analysis of classifier errors to reveal linguistic phenomena and inconsistencies in existing POS annotations, such as proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, verbs behaving like adjectives (passive participles), and currency amounts misclassified as numerals.
3. Evidence that POS information is distributed across many vector components (at least 100 out of 300 dimensions), contradicting the assumption that such information would be concentrated in a few specific dimensions.

## Strengths
1. **Insightful error analysis reveals meaningful linguistic phenomena**: The paper's most significant contribution is not the classifier itself, but the detailed analysis of misclassified words. The authors identify systematic patterns where words straddle POS boundaries (e.g., "white-collar" being classified as a noun rather than an adjective), which reveals genuine linguistic ambiguities and annotation inconsistencies in the BNC. This provides valuable empirical grounding for theoretical discussions about POS boundaries.

2. **Clear demonstration of distributed POS information**: The paper convincingly shows that POS information is distributed across many vector components rather than concentrated in a few, which is a significant insight for understanding how semantic models encode grammatical information. The analysis showing that accuracy increases gradually as more features are added (Figure 3) strongly supports this claim.

3. **Robust evaluation across multiple corpora**: The classifier's performance is validated not only on the training data but also on an independent test set from the BNC and the Universal Dependencies Treebank, with consistent high accuracy (F-score 0.91 and 0.99 respectively). This demonstrates the robustness of the findings beyond a single corpus.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in methodology**: The paper's approach is fundamentally straightforward - training a logistic classifier on word embeddings to predict POS tags. This is a well-trodden path in NLP, with prior work (e.g., Liu et al. 2016, Tsvetkov et al. 2015) already demonstrating similar results. The paper doesn't introduce any new NLP or machine learning techniques, and the experimental design is conventional rather than innovative.

2. **Inadequate literature review**: The paper fails to properly contextualize its work within existing research on POS induction using distributional models. Key papers such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016) that directly address POS induction with embeddings are not cited, making the paper appear disconnected from the broader literature.

3. **Limited practical relevance**: While the paper demonstrates that distributional models contain POS information, it doesn't show how this could be practically applied to improve existing POS tagging systems. The comparison to the Stanford POS tagger (Section 4) is superficial and doesn't address how the authors' approach might complement or improve state-of-the-art taggers.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would you position this work in relation to the growing body of literature on POS induction using distributional models? Specifically, what distinguishes your contribution from prior work by Liu et al. (2016) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015)?

2. The paper demonstrates that POS information is distributed across many vector dimensions. Could you elaborate on why this finding is particularly significant for understanding the relationship between semantic and grammatical representations in distributional models?

3. Given that the classifier achieves 0.938 accuracy on the UD Treebank when trained on BNC embeddings (Section 5), how would you explain the performance gap between the classifier trained on BNC and state-of-the-art POS taggers (which typically achieve >97% accuracy)?
