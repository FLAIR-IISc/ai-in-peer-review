

# Review for "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings, demonstrating that embeddings contain PoS information. They analyze misclassified words to reveal inconsistencies in PoS annotation and show that PoS information is distributed across many vector components. The main contributions are:
1. A detailed analysis of PoS outliers that reveals linguistic insights about PoS boundaries (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives, currency amounts classified as numerals)
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components (not concentrated in one or two features)
3. A method for detecting annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora using distributional models

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of PoS outliers that reveals interesting linguistic phenomena. The authors identify specific cases where annotation inconsistencies exist (e.g., proper nouns incorrectly tagged as common nouns, verbs mistakenly classified as adjectives), which could inform future linguistic studies about PoS boundaries and corpus annotation practices.

2. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across many vector components (rather than concentrated in a few features) is a significant finding that aligns with the underlying principles of embedding models. The authors provide strong evidence through their feature selection experiments, showing that accuracy increases steadily as more features are added.

3. The paper's method for detecting annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora is valuable for corpus linguistics. The comparison with Stanford PoS Tagger shows that distributional models can reveal different error patterns, suggesting they could be a useful tool for corpus quality control and linguistic analysis.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper fails to address the connection between PoS tagging and practical NLP applications. The BNC was PoS-tagged for NLP tasks like parsing, but the paper does not explore whether the embedding-based PoS distinctions improve parsing performance compared to the original tags or Universal PoS (UPOS). This is a significant gap in the paper's contribution and limits its relevance to the NLP community.

2. The paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012). This makes the claimed novelty of the approach questionable, as prior work has already demonstrated that distributional models can capture PoS information.

3. The statement that "relations between dense word vectors (embeddings) in the resulting vector space are of course mostly semantic" is incorrect. Prior work (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.) has shown that embeddings contain substantial syntactic information, which contradicts the paper's own Section 2 and undermines the paper's claims about the nature of information in embeddings.

4. The decision to switch to Universal PoS without justification is questionable. The original BNC tags are more fine-grained, which could have been more informative for linguistic analysis of PoS boundaries. The authors should have justified this choice or compared results with both tagsets.

5. The paper focuses on frequent word/tag pairs, but PoS tagging is particularly challenging for low-frequency words. The authors should have analyzed the classifier's performance on the tail of the frequency distribution and explored ways to better handle rare words.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do your findings about PoS boundaries relate to practical NLP tasks like parsing? Have you considered evaluating whether the embedding-based PoS distinctions improve parsing performance compared to the original BNC tags or UPOS?

2. Why did you choose to use Universal PoS instead of the original BNC tagset, which is more fine-grained and potentially more informative for linguistic analysis of PoS boundaries?

3. The claim that embedding relations are "mostly semantic" contradicts prior work showing embeddings contain substantial syntactic information. Can you clarify this point and provide evidence supporting your claim?
