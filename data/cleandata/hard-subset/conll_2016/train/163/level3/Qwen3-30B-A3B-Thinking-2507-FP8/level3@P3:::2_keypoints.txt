- Summary: Investigates how BNC-trained word embeddings predict PoS tags, training classifiers and analyzing misclassified “outliers” to find annotation inconsistencies and soft PoS boundaries.

- Contribution 1: Provides detailed error analysis of PoS outliers, revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC.

- Contribution 2: Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in a few components.

- Contribution 3: Proposes a method to identify potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora using distributional models.

- Strength 1: Strong linguistic error analysis with concrete examples (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs, proper nouns misclassified due to low counts).

- Strength 2: Empirical demonstration that classifier accuracy improves gradually as more embedding features are added, supporting distributed encoding of PoS.

- Strength 3: Practical utility for corpus annotation and complementary error patterns compared to the Stanford PoS Tagger.

- Weakness 1: Core claim (embeddings contain PoS information) lacks novelty given prior work and insufficient citation of related literature.

- Weakness 2: Mischaracterizes embeddings as “mostly semantic,” contradicting cited work and broader literature showing syntactic content.

- Weakness 3: Poorly framed research question—fails to connect PoS distinctions to downstream NLP utility (e.g., parsing or UPOS comparisons).

- Weakness 4: Questionable methodological choices: unexplained switch to UPOS, focus only on frequent words, omission of Brown clustering, and unclear “failed attempts” in a footnote.

- Weakness 5: Some claims (e.g., proper vs. common nouns) are trivialized by surface cues like function-word distributions and lack deeper justification.

- Question 1: How does this work advance beyond prior distributional PoS induction studies (Christodoulopoulos et al., Blunsom & Cohn, Yatbaz et al.)?

- Question 2: Why switch from BNC’s fine-grained tags to UPOS without justification, and how would results differ with the original tagset?

- Question 3: How do you reconcile the claim that “embedding relations are mostly semantic” with evidence of substantial syntactic information in embeddings?

- Question 4: How would the analysis and classifier errors change if low-frequency words were examined instead of only frequent words?

- Question 5: Please clarify Footnote 3: what were the “failed attempts,” who ran them, and what criteria defined failure?
