

This paper investigates whether word embeddings contain information about part-of-speech (POS) categories by training a logistic classifier to predict POS tags from distributional vectors. The authors use a lemmatized British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies POS tags, train word embeddings on this corpus, and evaluate a classifier that predicts POS based on embeddings. They demonstrate high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on cross-validation), show POS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions, and analyze outliers to reveal annotation inconsistencies.

The primary contributions I identify are: (1) empirical validation that distributional vectors encode POS information, (2) demonstration that outlier analysis can reveal annotation inconsistencies in POS-tagged corpora, and (3) evidence that POS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features.

Strengths: The paper provides clear empirical evidence supporting the hypothesis that distributional semantics contains POS information, which is significant for linguistic theory. The evaluation across multiple corpora (BNC test set, Universal Dependencies Treebank) is thorough and well-presented. The analysis of annotation inconsistencies through outlier detection offers valuable insights for linguistic annotation practices.

Weaknesses: The methodological approach lacks novelty, as similar experiments have been conducted previously (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015; Ling et al., 2015). The literature review fails to cite key works in POS induction using embeddings, weakening the scholarly context. The contribution is primarily empirical rather than methodological, with no novel NLP/ML techniques introduced.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making the findings accessible. However, the lack of novelty and insufficient literature review are significant drawbacks. While the empirical grounding for POS as a "soft" category is valuable for quantitative linguistics venues, the work does not advance the technical state of the art. The authors should address the missing literature citations and clarify how their findings extend beyond existing work.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how their results compare to prior work on POS induction using embeddings, particularly the studies by Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016) that should have been cited.
