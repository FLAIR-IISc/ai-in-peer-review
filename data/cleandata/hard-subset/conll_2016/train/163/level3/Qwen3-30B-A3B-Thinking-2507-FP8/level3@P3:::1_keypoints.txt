- Word embeddings trained on the BNC encode strong POS information; a logistic classifier achieves F1=0.979.  
- Error patterns from the classifier reveal linguistic boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., proper vs. common nouns, verbs vs. adjectives).  
- POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (at least ~100 of 300), not localized to a few features.  
- Provides strong empirical evidence for distributional POS patterns with high accuracy across evaluations.  
- Misclassification analysis yields linguistically meaningful insights and practical value for corpus annotation QA.  
- The paper is clearly written and well organized, with compelling presentation of results and examples.  
- Results generalize beyond BNC to the UD Treebank (F1≈0.99), showing robustness across domains and annotation schemes.  
- The approach lacks methodological novelty; training embeddings + logistic POS classifier is straightforward and previously used.  
- Key related work on POS induction with embeddings is insufficiently cited, weakening the novelty claim.  
- The claim of "redefining" POS classes is overstated; the contribution is primarily empirical validation rather than theoretical change.  
- The paper does not adequately discuss whether POS signals reflect true linguistic structure or statistical artifacts of the corpus.  
- How does your distributed-dimension finding relate to prior work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al. 2015) that links specific dimensions to linguistic features?  
- How would you systematically address rare-word representation limits (beyond noting low corpus frequency)?  
- How do you distinguish genuine annotation errors from cases where the model reflects a different, valid linguistic perspective?
