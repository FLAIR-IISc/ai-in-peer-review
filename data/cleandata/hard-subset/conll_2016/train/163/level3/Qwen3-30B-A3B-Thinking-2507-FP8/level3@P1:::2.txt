

This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tagging and distributional word embeddings using the British National Corpus (BNC). The main contributions are: (1) Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components in word embeddings rather than concentrated in one or two features; (2) Providing a detailed error analysis of outliers to reveal inconsistencies in PoS annotation; (3) Showing that a classifier trained on embeddings can predict PoS tags with high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on unseen BNC data, and 0.99 on Universal Dependencies Treebank).

Strengths: The paper's most significant contribution is its thorough analysis of outlier words that reveal genuine linguistic insights about PoS boundaries. The error analysis identifies specific cases where annotation inconsistencies exist (e.g., 22% of subordinate conjunctions misclassified as adverbs, 17% of numerals as nouns), providing concrete evidence for revising annotation guidelines. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (with accuracy increasing steadily to 0.99 with 100 features) is a valuable empirical finding that challenges the notion that specific vector components encode specific grammatical categories. The paper also successfully applies the method to the Universal Dependencies Treebank, showing robust performance (F-score 0.99) and demonstrating potential utility for resource-poor language processing.

Weaknesses: The paper fails to connect its findings to practical NLP applications, which is a significant oversight. The authors do not address why PoS tagging matters for downstream tasks like parsing, nor do they compare their distributional approach to standard PoS tagging in terms of utility for NLP systems. This omission weakens the paper's relevance to the CONLL community. The claim that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation" is not novel, as prior work (Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) has demonstrated distributional PoS induction through clustering. The paper also incorrectly states that "relations between dense word vectors (embeddings) in the resulting vector space are of course mostly semantic," contradicting Section 2's own discussion of prior work showing substantial syntactic information in embeddings (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.). The switch to Universal PoS (UPOS) tags from BNC's fine-grained tagging is questionable, as the original BNC tags likely contain more nuanced linguistic distinctions. The analysis focuses exclusively on frequent words (BNC frequencies >500), ignoring the low-frequency tail where PoS ambiguity might be most interesting. The unclear footnote 3 about failed attempts to cluster words into PoS classes undermines the paper's methodological rigor.

Questions to Authors: 
1. How would your approach improve actual NLP systems (e.g., parsers) compared to using standard PoS tags or UPOS? Please provide concrete evidence of utility for downstream tasks.
2. Why was the switch to UPOS made, and how does this affect the interpretation of your findings about PoS boundaries? Would using BNC's original fine-grained tags yield different insights?
3. How do you respond to the criticism that your statement about embeddings being "mostly semantic" contradicts prior work showing substantial syntactic information in embeddings? Please address this in your revision.
