- Contribution: Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in one or two features.
- Contribution: Provides detailed error analysis of outlier words revealing inconsistencies in PoS annotation.
- Contribution: Demonstrates a classifier on embeddings can predict PoS with high accuracy (F=0.979 train, 0.91 on unseen BNC, 0.99 on UD Treebank).

- Strength: Thorough outlier analysis yields genuine linguistic insights about PoS boundaries.
- Strength: Error analysis identifies concrete annotation inconsistencies (e.g., 22% of subordinate conjunctions misclassified as adverbs; 17% of numerals as nouns).
- Strength: Empirical finding that accuracy steadily increases with number of features, reaching 0.99 with 100 features, challenges single-component encoding claims.
- Strength: Method generalizes to Universal Dependencies with robust performance (F=0.99), suggesting potential for resource-poor languages.

- Weakness: Paper does not connect findings to practical NLP applications or demonstrate utility for downstream tasks (e.g., parsing).
- Weakness: Claim that embeddings contain PoS information is not novel given prior distributional PoS induction work (Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012).
- Weakness: Statement that embeddings are “mostly semantic” contradicts cited prior work showing substantial syntactic information.
- Weakness: Switching from BNC fine-grained tags to UPOS is questionable and may obscure nuanced linguistic distinctions.
- Weakness: Analysis restricted to frequent words (BNC frequency >500), ignoring low-frequency tail where PoS ambiguity may be most interesting.
- Weakness: Unclear footnote about failed clustering attempts undermines methodological clarity and rigor.

- Question: How would this approach improve actual NLP systems (e.g., parsers) compared to standard PoS tags or UPOS—provide concrete evidence of downstream utility?
- Question: Why switch to UPOS and how does that affect interpretation; would using BNC’s original fine-grained tags yield different insights?
- Question: How do you reconcile the claim that embeddings are “mostly semantic” with prior work demonstrating substantial syntactic information in embeddings?
