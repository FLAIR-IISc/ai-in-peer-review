

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from word embeddings and analyze the resulting "outliers" to identify potential annotation inconsistencies and explore the notion of "soft" PoS boundaries.

## Main Contributions
1. Detailed error analysis of PoS outliers, revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across many vector components (not limited to a few specific features)
3. A method for identifying potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora

## Strengths

1. **Strong error analysis of outliers**: The paper provides a valuable linguistic analysis of misclassified words, revealing meaningful patterns in the BNC annotation. For example, the authors correctly identify that adjectives like "plain" or "clear" are incorrectly tagged as adverbs in BNC, and that proper nouns like "luftwaffe" are sometimes misclassified as common nouns due to insufficient training examples. This analysis offers genuine insights into annotation inconsistencies that could inform future corpus development.

2. **Demonstration of distributed PoS information**: The authors effectively show that PoS information is not concentrated in a few vector components but distributed across many dimensions (Figures 1-3). Their finding that accuracy gradually improves as more features are added (reaching near-perfect performance with ~100 features) provides a useful empirical validation of this property, which is consistent with the underlying principles of embedding models.

3. **Practical application for corpus annotation**: The paper demonstrates how distributional models can identify potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora, which could be valuable for corpus developers. The comparison with Stanford PoS Tagger (Tables 3-4) shows that distributional models reveal different error patterns, suggesting complementary value in error analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core claim**: The paper's main claim—that distributional vectors contain PoS information—is not novel. Prior work by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom & Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012) already demonstrated this through clustering and induction methods. The paper fails to properly acknowledge this substantial body of related work, making the contribution seem incremental rather than novel.

2. **Mischaracterization of embedding properties**: The paper incorrectly states that "embedding relations are mostly semantic," while contradicting its own Section 2 (which references prior work showing substantial syntactic information in embeddings). This mischaracterization undermines the paper's credibility, as Mikolov et al. (2013b), CoVe, and other work have established that embeddings contain substantial syntactic information.

3. **Poor framing of research question**: The paper fails to address why PoS tagging is useful for NLP (e.g., for parsing) and should instead focus on whether embedding-based distinctions improve parsing performance compared to original tags or UPOS. The paper treats PoS as a linguistic curiosity rather than an NLP tool, which misaligns with the intended audience of the CONLL conference.

4. **Questionable methodological choices**: The switch to UPOS without justification is problematic, as the fine-grained BNC tags might have been more interesting for linguistic analysis. The analysis focuses only on frequent words, ignoring the low-frequency tail where PoS tagging is most challenging. The authors also omit discussion of Brown clustering (which aligns well with UPOS) and fail to clarify the "failed attempts" mentioned in Footnote 3.

5. **Trivialized claims**: The claim that "proper nouns differ from common nouns" is trivialized by function-word distributions (e.g., presence of "the") and lacks proper justification. This weakens the paper's overall contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does this work advance beyond the substantial prior work on distributional PoS induction (Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012)? What specific novelty does this paper contribute?

2. Why was the decision made to switch from BNC's fine-grained tags to UPOS without justification? How might the results differ if the authors had used the original BNC tagset?

3. The paper claims "embedding relations are mostly semantic" but contradicts prior work showing substantial syntactic information in embeddings (Mikolov et al., CoVe, etc.). How do you reconcile this with your Section 2 and the broader literature?

4. The analysis focuses only on frequent words. How would the results change if the authors examined low-frequency words and the classifier's errors on them?

5. Can you clarify Footnote 3? What were the "failed attempts," who conducted them, and what was the failure criteria?
