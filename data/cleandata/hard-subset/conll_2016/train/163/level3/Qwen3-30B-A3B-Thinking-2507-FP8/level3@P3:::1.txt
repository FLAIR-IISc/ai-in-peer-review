

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates whether word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) encode part-of-speech (POS) information, using a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags from distributional vectors. The authors demonstrate that word embeddings contain significant POS information (F-score 0.979 on cross-validation), with errors revealing linguistic insights about POS boundary cases. They further show that POS information is distributed across multiple vector components rather than concentrated in specific dimensions.

Primary contributions as I see them:
1. Empirical demonstration that distributional word vectors contain robust POS information, providing quantitative grounding for the notion of "soft" POS categories.
2. Analysis of "outlier" words that reveals inconsistencies in POS annotation (e.g., proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, verbs mistaken for adjectives).
3. Evidence that POS information is distributed across numerous vector components (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), not concentrated in one or two features.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical foundation for POS distributional patterns**: The paper provides clear, quantitative evidence that POS information is embedded in word vectors, with high accuracy (F-score 0.979) across multiple evaluations (cross-validation on BNC, testing on UD Treebank). This is a valuable contribution to understanding what word embeddings actually encode beyond semantics.

2. **Linguistic insights from error analysis**: The analysis of misclassified words reveals meaningful linguistic patterns and potential annotation inconsistencies (e.g., "white-collar" being correctly classified as a noun rather than adjective, currency amounts being correctly classified as numerals). This provides practical value for corpus linguistics and POS annotation quality control.

3. **Clear writing and organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow from hypothesis to methodology to analysis. The presentation of results, particularly the error analysis with concrete examples, is clear and compelling.

4. **Robust evaluation across corpora**: The results generalize beyond the training corpus (BNC) to the UD Treebank (F-score 0.99), demonstrating the approach's robustness to different text domains and annotation schemes.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The approach (training word embeddings and using a logistic classifier for POS prediction) is straightforward and has been used in prior work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al. 2015, Ling et al. 2015). The paper does not present any new NLP or ML methods, which is a significant limitation for a CONLL venue focused on computational linguistics advances.

2. **Insufficient literature review**: The paper fails to cite key related work in POS induction using embeddings (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016). This omission weakens the contribution claim, as the core finding is not new but rather an extension of existing research.

3. **Overstated contribution**: The paper claims to "redefine" POS classes, but the contribution is primarily empirical validation rather than theoretical or methodological advancement. The finding that POS information is distributed across multiple vector components is consistent with prior work on embedding interpretability.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address whether the POS information is truly linguistic or merely a statistical artifact of the training data. This limits the interpretability of the findings for linguistic theory.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your finding that POS information is distributed across multiple vector components relate to Tsvetkov et al. (2015), who demonstrated that specific embedding dimensions correlate with linguistic features? Does your work extend or contradict their findings?

2. The paper states that "the model was just not presented with enough contexts for these words to learn meaningful representations" for rare proper nouns. How would you systematically address this limitation rather than relying on corpus frequency?

3. You claim your approach "reveals hidden inconsistencies of the annotation process." How would you distinguish between true annotation errors and cases where the distributional model captures a different linguistic perspective than the annotation guidelines?
