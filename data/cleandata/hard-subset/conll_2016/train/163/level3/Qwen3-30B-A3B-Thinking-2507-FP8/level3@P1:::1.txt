

Summary: This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (POS) categories. The authors train classifiers to predict POS tags based on word embeddings from the British National Corpus, analyze misclassifications to reveal linguistic insights about POS boundaries, and examine how POS information is distributed across vector dimensions.

Main contributions: 
1. Empirical demonstration that word embeddings contain robust information about POS categories, distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features.
2. Identification of linguistic inconsistencies in POS annotation through analysis of misclassification cases, revealing "soft" or "graded" POS boundaries.
3. Demonstration that POS information can be used to build a basic POS tagger without explicit syntactic context.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides clear empirical evidence supporting the notion of "soft" POS boundaries, with insightful analysis of misclassification cases that reveals genuine linguistic inconsistencies in the BNC annotation (e.g., proper nouns mistakenly tagged as common nouns, verbs mistaken for adjectives due to passive participles). The analysis of 1558 misclassified words reveals meaningful patterns that would be difficult to detect through manual inspection alone.

2. The evaluation is thorough and well-structured, with consistent performance across multiple datasets (BNC test set, UD Treebank). The classifier achieves 0.91 F-score on unseen BNC data and 0.99 F-score on UD Treebank, demonstrating robustness. The comparison with Stanford POS Tagger reveals complementary insights that distributional models can uncover different error patterns than traditional taggers.

3. The analysis of how POS information is distributed across vector dimensions is particularly valuable. The finding that POS information is distributed among at least 100 vector components (rather than concentrated in one or two features) provides important empirical support for the theoretical notion of "soft" POS categories and challenges simplistic views of linguistic categories.

Weaknesses:
1. The methodology is straightforward and lacks novelty. The paper employs standard word embedding techniques and logistic regression classifiers without introducing any new NLP or machine learning methods. This is a replication of known approaches rather than a novel contribution to the field.

2. The literature review is insufficient, failing to cite important recent work on POS tagging using embeddings (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016). The paper should acknowledge that similar approaches have been explored before, particularly in the context of POS induction.

3. The POS tagger built is basic and not competitive with state-of-the-art systems, which is acknowledged by the authors. While the authors note this is intentional for their linguistic analysis goals, it limits the practical significance of the work for the NLP community.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would the results change if using more advanced embedding models (e.g., contextual embeddings like BERT) instead of static word embeddings?
2. Could the identified linguistic inconsistencies (e.g., verbs mistaken for adjectives) be validated through linguistic analysis by domain experts to confirm whether the annotation was indeed inconsistent?
3. How does the distribution of POS information across vector dimensions vary across different languages, particularly those with more complex morphology?
