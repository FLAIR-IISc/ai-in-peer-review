Summary
The paper investigates whether distributional word vectors encode part-of-speech (POS) information. Using a lemmatized, UD-annotated BNC, the authors train word embeddings and a multinomial logistic classifier to predict POS. They evaluate via cross-validation on BNC, test on less frequent BNC items, and transfer to UD English, then analyze misclassifications to reveal graded POS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies. A follow-up experiment trains embeddings without POS tags and shows a context-free POS predictor achieves reasonable accuracy. The writing is clear and results are well presented.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that POS information is robustly present in distributional embeddings and is distributed across many dimensions.
2) A practical error-analysis framework that surfaces graded category behavior and corpus annotation inconsistencies using an embedding-based POS predictor.
3) Resources/protocols: BNC→UD tag mapping and simple, reproducible baselines for context-free POS prediction.

Strengths
- Clear empirical grounding: Strong performance (F≈0.98 CV; 0.91 on held-out BNC; 0.99 on UD lemmas; 0.84 token-level with lemma-only embeddings) supports the central claim that embeddings encode POS.
- Insightful error analysis: Misclassifications yield linguistically meaningful boundary cases (participles, noun–adjective ambiguity, numerals vs years/currency) and uncover likely BNC tagging inconsistencies; contrasts with a Stanford tagger confusion analysis usefully shows complementary insights.
- Transparent, simple methodology: Word2vec + logistic regression with careful frequency weighting, prototypical vectors, and feature ablation provide interpretable findings (e.g., POS signal spread over ≥100 dims).
- Clarity and replicability: The paper is well written; preprocessing and hyperparameters are specified; mappings are released.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Methods are straightforward; the main contribution is empirical confirmation rather than new modeling or theory.
- Potential circularity in the primary experiment: Training embeddings on lemma+POS tokens injects gold POS into the representation space, weakening claims about “unsupervised” discovery; the lemma-only follow-up helps but should be more central.
- Incomplete related work: Missing key references on POS induction/tagging with embeddings (e.g., Lin et al., 2015; Ling et al., 2015; Plank et al., 2016) and stronger positioning relative to that literature.
- Baselines and controls: Lacks comparisons to simple surface-feature baselines (capitalization, affixes, digit patterns), frequency-only baselines, and non-linear classifiers; no statistical tests; limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Generalization scope: Cross-corpus evidence is only within English; claims about broader applicability would be stronger with additional domains/languages.

Questions to Authors
- How much do results change when embeddings are trained strictly on raw tokens/lemmas (no POS in training) for all experiments?
- Can you quantify the contribution of surface cues (capitalization, digits) and frequency vs distributional context?
- Did you control for potential leakage between BNC-derived embeddings and UD evaluation (e.g., shared lemmatization/vocabulary artifacts)?
- Will you release code, trained models, and the BNC–UD mapping?
