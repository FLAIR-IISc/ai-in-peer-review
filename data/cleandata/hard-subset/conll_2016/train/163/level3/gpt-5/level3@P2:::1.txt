Summary and contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on a lemmatized, UD-tagged version of the BNC correlate with parts of speech (PoS), using simple classifiers to (i) predict PoS from word vectors and (ii) expose words whose distributional behavior diverges from their gold tags. It further evaluates a “proper” predictor trained on embeddings built without PoS tags and shows that PoS-relevant information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical analysis showing that distributional vectors can surface “graded” PoS affiliations and highlight annotation inconsistencies and boundary cases across PoS (e.g., years as nouns vs. numerals, passive participles as adjective-like, and BNC adverb/adjective confusions). This is the most novel and linguistically insightful part.
2) Cross-corpus evidence that embeddings encode robust PoS signals, enabling a simple, context-free classifier to reach strong accuracy on UD (and near-perfect cross-validation on BNC when tags are injected during training).
3) Quantitative evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (rather than concentrated in a few), supported by ANOVA-based feature ranking and accuracy curves.

Strengths
- Linguistically meaningful error analysis: The paper goes beyond aggregate scores to analyze misclassifications that align with known fuzzy boundaries (N/ADJ, V/ADJ via participles, numerals vs. years/currencies). The discovery of systematic BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as ADV) is convincing and useful for corpus curation. The contrast with UD further underscores annotation-policy effects.
- Clear, reproducible setup with sensible baselines and cross-corpus checks: The authors specify corpus preparation, embedding hyperparameters, and classifier design, and they report cross-validation on BNC, held-out frequency bands, and application to UD. The robustness on UD (F-score 0.99 for the tag-augmented model; 0.84 accuracy for the tag-free setting) demonstrates portability of the approach.
- Focused, simple methodology yields strong signal: Using logistic regression on off-the-shelf Skip-gram embeddings isolates the contribution of distributional information without confounds from complex architectures. The frequency weighting to balance closed/open classes is reasonable and improves class coverage.
- Insight into feature distribution: The ANOVA-based ranking and “top-k” analysis provide an interpretable view that PoS cues are spread across dozens of dimensions, consistent with the nature of distributional spaces. This is a useful quantitative takeaway for researchers designing downstream models or interpretability studies.
- Clarity of exposition: The paper is well written and easy to follow, with explicit motivations, careful caveats (e.g., “soft” PoS), and informative examples.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in method: The technical approach (word2vec + logistic regression) is straightforward and aligns with prior work showing embeddings encode PoS/morphosyntax. The main novelty is the detailed error analysis, not the modeling; the paper would be strengthened by deeper methodological or analytical innovations (e.g., principled probing, causal interventions, or controlled ablations).
- Potential circularity in the main classifier: The strongest results (F≈0.98–0.99) are obtained when embeddings are trained on lemma+PoS tokens (e.g., love_VERB). This makes the space already conditioned on gold PoS distinctions and may limit how much we can conclude about PoS information arising purely from distributional context. The tag-free UD experiment helps, but is evaluated only on UD and at token OOV is nontrivial.
- Incomplete baselines and metrics: Baselines are majority and single-feature models. Stronger baselines (e.g., Brown clusters; bag-of-contexts; character/morphological features; MLP/SVM; linear probes with L2 regularization) would better contextualize the claim that embeddings are especially informative. Reporting macro-F1 and per-class results (beyond weighted F) would also clarify closed-class performance.
- Fairness of the Stanford tagger comparison: Feeding single tokens to a contextual tagger is not a fair comparison and yields a confusion matrix that is difficult to interpret relative to yours. While the goal is to contrast error profiles, a more apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., replace your model’s inference with type-level predictions; or compare to context-free baselines like cluster lookups) would be more compelling.
- Limited robustness analysis: No variance across random seeds, no hyperparameter sensitivity (window, dimension, negative samples), and the “top-k features” curve is on training accuracy. Reporting multiple runs, dev accuracy for top-k, and OOV treatment would make the conclusions more robust. The UD evaluation drops 27% of types (10% tokens); please analyze coverage effects.

Questions to authors
- Can you report macro-F1 and per-class precision/recall for both BNC and UD settings (including confusion matrices), and indicate performance on closed vs. open classes?
- How sensitive are results to embedding hyperparameters (vector size, window, subsampling) and random seeds? Please include means/standard deviations over multiple runs.
- For the tag-free UD experiment: how many “unambiguous” training lemmas per class, and how does varying the dominance threshold (1/2) affect accuracy?
- Can you provide a fairer baseline that is context-free at inference time (e.g., Brown clusters, averaged contextual counts, char n-gram features) and compare against your embeddings?
- For the BNC inconsistency claims (e.g., ADV vs. ADJ), did you quantify error rates via manual sampling? A small annotation study would solidify the argument.
- Could you release the lemmatization/UD mapping and code (anonymized) to facilitate replication?

Additional comments
- Please expand related work to include embedding-based PoS induction/tagging and probing, e.g., Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), Plank et al. (2016), and other relevant studies of morphosyntactic signals in embeddings. Positioning relative to this literature would clarify what is new here (the analysis and corpus-diagnostics angle).
- Consider a brief discussion on applicability to low-resource settings (how many prototypical seeds per class are needed) and cross-lingual transfer.
