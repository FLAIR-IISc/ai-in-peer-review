- Summary: Paper tests how well distributional skip-gram embeddings encode PoS using BNC and UD labels, trains a logistic classifier, finds high accuracy and meaningful misclassifications, and shows PoS info is distributed across many embedding dimensions.
- Contribution (primary): Proposes a diagnostic framework using embeddings + simple classifier to reveal soft PoS boundaries and audit corpus annotation inconsistencies.
- Contribution (secondary): Provides empirical evidence that PoS affiliation is robustly encoded in standard embeddings and spread across many dimensions (ANOVA/k-best probing).
- Contribution (tertiary): Shows a context-free PoS predictor trained from ~1.5k prototypical lemmas attains reasonable accuracy (~0.84).
- Strength: Clear empirical setup and thorough, reproducible pipeline from BNC preprocessing to UD tags, embedding training, and classification.
- Strength: Insightful error analysis that surfaces linguistically meaningful boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., participles, numerals/years, ADV vs ADJ).
- Strength: Practical utility for corpus auditing as a lightweight tool complementary to taggers, highlighting different types of issues.
- Strength: Probing experiments (ANOVA, k-best features) support the claim that PoS information is distributed, aiding interpretability.
- Strength: Well-written, accessible exposition with transparent hyperparameters and reporting.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—uses standard tools; main value is analysis rather than new modeling.
- Weakness: Possible leakage and evaluation caveats: training on lemma_POS tokens and frequency-weighted cross-validation may inflate separability and accuracy.
- Weakness: Unfair comparison to a contextual tagger—feeding isolated tokens to the Stanford Tagger is not an appropriate baseline.
- Weakness: Missing baselines and controls (Brown clusters, PMI, frequency-only, type-balanced splits) and limited sensitivity analysis across hyperparameters.
- Weakness: Limited scope—English-only and single corpus; claims about graded PoS boundaries would benefit from typologically distinct languages.
- Question: How do results change under leave-one-type-out cross-validation (no identical vectors shared across folds) and without frequency duplication?
- Question: Can the outlier analysis be replicated with embeddings trained on plain lemmas (no POS tags) to rule out leakage?
- Question: What is the sensitivity to embedding hyperparameters (window size, dim, negatives) and to corpus choice (e.g., Wikipedia vs BNC)?
- Question: Can you provide manual validation quantifying what proportion of outliers are annotation errors vs genuine boundary cases?
- Question: For PROPN vs NOUN, how do capitalization/orthographic factors in lemmas affect results and are UD lemma normalizations consistent?
- Additional comment: Cite related work on POS induction and embeddings (Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016; Brown clusters, Turian, Schütze).
- Additional comment: Suggest probing with random orthogonal rotations to test axis-specific vs distributed encoding claims.
- Additional comment: Release mapping scripts, trained models, and flagged outlier lists to facilitate reuse and corpus correction.
- Additional comment: Minor fixes: clean up references, clarify OOV fractions, and report random seed control.
