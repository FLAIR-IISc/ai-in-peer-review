Summary
The paper investigates whether distributional word vectors encode part-of-speech (POS) information and how this can be used to probe POS boundaries, outliers, and annotation inconsistencies. Using a lemmatized British National Corpus (BNC) with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, the authors train skip-gram embeddings and a multinomial logistic classifier to predict POS from word vectors. They report strong cross-validated performance on BNC, good generalization to held-out BNC vocabulary and the UD English Treebank, and detailed error analyses that highlight “graded” POS membership, systematic annotation issues, and interpretable classes of outliers. A follow-up experiment trains embeddings on lemmas only (no POS in the corpus), then learns a POS probe from a small list of prototypical words, achieving reasonable tagging accuracy without sentence context. Analysis of feature ablations suggests POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A careful empirical study demonstrating that distributional word embeddings encode robust, predictive information about POS classes, with extensive, well-analyzed evaluations on BNC and UD.
- Secondary: A methodology and set of analyses for surfacing “POS outliers” that reveal graded category membership and corpus annotation inconsistencies (e.g., PROPN/NOUN, VBN-as-ADJ, numerals vs. nouns).
- Tertiary: Evidence that POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions and a simple, low-resource POS probing setup that performs reasonably without contextual features.

Strengths
- Clear empirical grounding and thorough analysis: The study systematically trains embeddings and a simple logistic classifier, evaluates via cross-validation, held-out BNC vocabulary, and an external UD corpus, and presents confusion patterns and case studies. The qualitative insights (e.g., years behaving as nouns, VBN-as-ADJ, currency amounts) are compelling and linguistically informative.
- Outlier-driven error analysis reveals real annotation issues: The paper uses classifier errors to isolate borderline classes and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adverbs vs. adjectives in BNC), demonstrating practical utility for corpus maintenance and supporting “graded” POS membership.
- Simple, reproducible methodology: The setup (skip-gram, logistic regression, frequency weighting) is transparent and easy to reproduce. The mapping from BNC tags to UD is a useful resource.
- Cross-corpus validation and robustness: Results on the UD English Treebank are strong, reducing concerns about overfitting to BNC idiosyncrasies and supporting the claim that POS is encoded in distributional information.
- Insight on distributed encoding of POS: The top-k feature analysis indicates POS information is not localized in a small number of dimensions, aligning with general intuitions about embeddings and adding a useful empirical probe.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in methods: The core experiments are straightforward (standard skip-gram + logistic regression), and the idea that embeddings encode POS has been noted repeatedly. The work’s contribution is primarily empirical characterization and analysis rather than methodological innovation.
- Potential circularity in the main setup: In the principal analysis, embeddings are trained on lemma+POS tokens and contexts also contain POS-labeled tokens. This risks baking POS into the context signal, inflating separability and potentially overstating how much POS is learnable from purely distributional co-occurrence. The lemma-only follow-up helps, but key findings (e.g., outlier sets) should be replicated with embeddings trained on tokens without POS labels to fully rule out leakage.
- Narrow scope: Experiments are English-only, based on BNC and UD. Claims about graded POS membership and distributional encoding would be stronger with additional languages (especially morphologically rich or typologically diverse ones) or at least multiple English corpora with different genre distributions.
- Limited comparison to related work: The paper should engage more with prior POS tagging/induction using embeddings and neural features, including Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), Plank et al. (2016), and work on dependency-based embeddings and probing methods. Positioning relative to this literature would clarify what is new here.
- Probing methodology could be strengthened: The top-k ANOVA-based dimension ranking is informative, but training accuracy is used as the criterion. A held-out evaluation for the feature ablation, alternative probes (e.g., L2-regularized linear probes with control tasks), or mutual information analyses would make the “distributed across dozens of dimensions” claim more rigorous.

Questions to Authors
- Can you replicate the main outlier analyses with embeddings trained on lemmas only (no POS suffixes in the corpus) to rule out context-tag leakage and verify that the same error patterns persist?
- How sensitive are your conclusions to embedding hyperparameters and context definitions (e.g., window sizes, subword modeling, dependency-based contexts)? Any preliminary results?
- Did you examine per-class precision/recall for the lemma-only POS probe on UD, and how does it compare to simple frequency or majority baselines for ambiguous vs. unambiguous lemmas?
- Could you provide the full confusion matrices for all settings (BNC CV, held-out BNC, UD) and the list of top outliers to facilitate replication?
- How stable are the identified “signature” dimensions (e.g., feature 94 for conjunctions) across random seeds and retrainings?

Additional comments
- Please add missing related work: Lin et al. 2015 (hierarchical recurrent nets for tagging), Ling et al. 2015 (character-based representations for tagging), Plank et al. 2016 (multilingual robustness and neural tagging), and other POS induction/distributional studies; also consider Levy and Goldberg 2014 (dependency-based embeddings) as an alternative context.
- Consider evaluating on an additional English corpus (e.g., ukWaC, Wikipedia) and at least one other language from UD to strengthen generality claims.
- The paper is very clearly written, figures and analyses are easy to follow, and the empirical framing is well suited to a quantitative/empirical linguistics venue. The contribution is primarily empirical insight rather than algorithmic novelty.
