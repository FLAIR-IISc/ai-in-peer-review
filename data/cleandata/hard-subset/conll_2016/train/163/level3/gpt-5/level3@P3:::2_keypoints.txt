- Primary contribution: diagnostic framework showing static BNC embeddings predict UPOS with high accuracy and that interpretable outliers map to annotation inconsistencies or soft PoS boundaries.
- Secondary contribution: qualitative error analysis highlighting boundary cases (participles, noun–adjective alternations, numerals vs years/currency), demonstrating embeddings as corpus-auditing tools.
- Tertiary contribution: empirical finding that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, supported by ablations.

- Strength: careful, insightful error analysis that uncovers plausible corpus inconsistencies and genuine gray areas, providing a useful auditing recipe.
- Strength: solid experimental design (type-level classifier, frequency-weighting, out-of-domain UD check) showing robustness across datasets.
- Strength: clear empirical confirmation that embeddings encode PoS (high F-scores on frequent words; context-free performance) with a reproducible setup.
- Strength: practical utility for low-resource settings—the prototypical-words approach is a simple baseline for bootstrapping tags.
- Strength: ANOVA/ablation results support a distributed representation of PoS signal across components.

- Weakness: limited novelty relative to prior distributional PoS induction work; key related papers are omitted, making the tagging result seem incremental.
- Weakness: framing misses downstream motivation (e.g., parsing) and does not test whether embedding-driven distinctions improve downstream tasks.
- Weakness: the claim that embeddings are “mostly semantic” contradicts prior work and the paper’s own syntactic evidence; needs revision and analysis of window-size effects.
- Weakness: collapsing CLAWS to UPOS discards fine-grained distinctions where diagnostics could be most informative; a parallel original-tag analysis is needed.
- Weakness: footnote 3 is unclear about clustering attempts and omits obvious baselines (e.g., Brown clusters); details on algorithms, cluster counts, and failure criteria are missing.
- Weakness: some confusions (e.g., PROPN vs NOUN) are trivial distributional effects and require deeper controls (function-word contexts, capitalization, genre).
- Weakness: bias toward frequent types and lack of tail analysis—insufficient attention to low-frequency words where distributional features degrade.
- Weakness: no exploration of rare-word remedies (subword/character models, suffix/prefix or morphological features, retrofitting, lexicon backoffs) relevant to tagging.
- Weakness: interpretation of individual embedding dimensions is fragile due to basis non-identifiability and needs tempered claims or fixed-rotation probes.

- Q: How does this method relate to prior distributional PoS induction (Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012)—what is new?
- Q: Why collapse to UPOS for main results—can you replicate analyses on original CLAWS tags and do outlier patterns persist or become more informative?
- Q: Clarify footnote 3—who attempted clustering, which algorithms and cluster counts, and what constituted “failure”? Did you try Brown clustering?
- Q: Do embedding-based PoS predictions or prototypical-distance features help a dependency parser or context-aware tagger compared to gold UPOS or Brown clusters?
- Q: Can you provide error breakdowns by frequency bucket and focused analysis on low-frequency types? How do subword/morphological features change those errors?
- Q: How do results vary with larger context windows or different embedding methods (CBOW vs Skip-gram)?

- Comment: report macro vs micro metrics per tag, clarify train/test splits to avoid leakage, and include variance over random seeds.
