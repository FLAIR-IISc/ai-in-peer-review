Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on BNC and part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The authors train a simple multinomial classifier to predict PoS from embeddings and use misclassifications to analyze “boundary” cases and potential corpus annotation issues. They also build a “PoS-from-lemma” predictor learned from prototypical UD words and study how many embedding dimensions are informative for PoS.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A careful, data-driven error analysis that surfaces linguistically interesting outliers and likely BNC annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adjective/adverb confusion like “plain,” numerals vs nouns for years/currencies, participles behaving adjectivally).
2) Empirical characterization that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (on the order of 100+), rather than concentrated in a few.
3) A simple lemma-level PoS predictor trained from a small set of prototypical words that achieves reasonable accuracy without context, suggesting utility for low-resource scenarios.

Strengths
- Insightful outlier analysis with concrete, corpus-backed findings: The paper leverages misclassifications to identify boundary phenomena (e.g., noun/adjective modification behavior, passive participles) and systematic annotation problems in BNC (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs). The analysis is well-supported with examples and cross-checks against UD, and it demonstrates clear value for corpus auditing.
- Robust empirical signals that embeddings encode PoS: The classifier achieves high scores on BNC (0.979 CV; 0.91 on held-out frequency band) and transfers well to UD lemmas (0.99 F on the subset present in the model), supporting the central observation that embeddings encode substantial PoS information.
- Clear evidence that PoS is not captured by a single dimension: The feature ablation curve and ANOVA-based ranking show smooth improvements up to roughly 100+ dimensions, arguing that the PoS signal is broadly distributed—an informative result for interpretability discussions.
- Practical “prototype-based” PoS prediction: The UD-based, proto-lemma classifier reaches 0.84 token accuracy without any sentence context. While not SOTA, this is a credible baseline for resource-poor languages and an interesting instantiation of “few-seed” learning from embeddings.
- Reproducibility and methodological clarity: The paper provides training details (SG parameters, frequency thresholds, weighting by frequency) and releases tag mappings (anonymized), facilitating replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior distributional PoS induction/clustering literature: The claim that embeddings encode PoS is well-established. The paper omits substantial related work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) on unsupervised/semi-supervised PoS induction from distributional cues. Section 5’s “PoS-from-lemma” result would be stronger if situated against these baselines (and Brown clusters), making the contribution appear incremental otherwise.
- Framing vis-à-vis downstream utility: BNC PoS tags were created for downstream NLP (e.g., parsing). The paper argues embeddings reveal PoS boundaries and inconsistencies, but does not evaluate whether embedding-derived distinctions improve downstream tasks over the original tags or UPOS. A parsing or dependency-labeling experiment would help justify the practical impact of redefining PoS classes.
- Coarse tagset choice and lemmatization may obscure finer insights: Mapping to UPOS and lemmatizing removes useful fine-grained morphological distinctions present in BNC. Since some of the most interesting “graded” phenomena occur at sub-PoS granularity (e.g., verbal/adjectival participles, determiners vs possessives), experiments with the original tagset (or parallel analyses at both granularities) would strengthen the conclusions.
- Ambiguity and baselines in Section 3/Footnote 3: The note that clustering “inescapably failed” is unclear—was this the authors’ attempt, what algorithms/metrics were used, what failure criteria? A comparison to Brown clusters (known to correlate well with UPOS) and to standard unsupervised/semi-supervised PoS induction methods is notably missing.
- Claims about proper vs common nouns need tighter justification: The observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns is plausible but risks triviality due to function word distributions (e.g., “the”). Please quantify which features drive separability (beyond determiners), and report robustness across hyperparameters and corpora. As currently presented, this result seems weaker than other analyses.
- Frequency bias and rare-word generalization underexplored: The study focuses on frequent words (train ≥500; test 100–500) and omits the long tail. Since rare words are critical for PoS tagging, the paper should analyze classifier performance and error patterns on lower-frequency bins, and consider alternatives that help rare words (e.g., character/morphological features, subword embeddings, or distributional + morphological hybrids).

Questions to Authors
- Related work/baselines: Can you compare your “PoS-from-lemma” predictor to Brown clusters and to standard PoS induction baselines (HMM-based, Bayesian models, etc.) on the same UD/BNC splits? How do your methods fare if you use dependency-based or positional contexts?
- Clustering attempts: Please detail the clustering methods you tried (algorithms, k selection, evaluation metrics) and what “failure” means quantitatively. Were there settings (e.g., more/fewer clusters, spectral clustering) that partially recovered PoS structure?
- UPOS vs original BNC tags: Why prioritize UPOS over the fine-grained BNC tags for your main analyses? Can you replicate the outlier findings with the original tagset, and do any new patterns emerge at finer granularity?
- Proper vs common nouns: Which features (e.g., determiners, capitalization proxies, surrounding POS distributions) primarily separate PROPN from NOUN? Can you quantify their contributions and show that the effect is not driven by a small set of function-word contexts?
- Rare words and morphology: How does performance change across frequency bins below 100? Could you incorporate character n-grams or simple suffix features and show gains for rare words?

Additional Comments
- The claim that embeddings are “mostly semantic” conflicts with both prior work and your own findings; suggest rephrasing to acknowledge robust syntactic/functional information in embeddings.
- Consider reporting results with the original (non-lemmatized) tokens to gauge how much morphological inflection contributes to PoS prediction.
- Minor: adopt sentential citation style via \newcite{}; clean up references and figure captions; clarify availability of resources (mappings, code, models).
