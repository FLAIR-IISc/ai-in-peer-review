- Trains embeddings on lemmatized, UPOS-annotated BNC and shows a multinomial classifier can predict PoS with high accuracy; misclassifications reveal graded boundaries and annotation issues.
- Builds a context-free PoS predictor from “prototypical” UD lemmas with reasonable accuracy.
- Analyzes which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS, arguing PoS information is distributed across many components.

- Primary contribution: embedding-based error analysis that surfaces boundary phenomena and BNC annotation inconsistencies, corroborated on UD.
- Secondary contribution: quantitative evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions via component ranking and learning curves.
- Tertiary contribution: simple prototype-based context-free PoS predictor useful for low-resource pretagging, though not novel.

- Strength: insightful error analysis highlighting plausible boundary cases and systematic BNC tagging errors.
- Strength: classifier robustness across datasets—BNC-trained embeddings transfer to UD lemmas.
- Strength: clear, reproducible methodology (logistic regression with frequency weighting).
- Strength: component-level analysis supports distributed PoS representation claim.
- Strength: prototype predictor has practical utility for bootstrapping annotations in resource-poor settings.

- Weakness: limited novelty relative to existing distributional PoS induction and clustering literature; missing direct baselines like Brown clusters.
- Weakness: incomplete related work and inconsistent positioning about syntactic content in embeddings.
- Weakness: unclear motivation regarding downstream impact—uncertain whether corrections help parsing or NER.
- Weakness: UPOS conversion may discard informative fine-grained distinctions; need parallel analysis on native BNC tags.
- Weakness: frequency effects underexplored—low-frequency tail and OOV words need stratified analysis.
- Weakness: methodological clarity issues (e.g., unclear footnote about failed clustering attempts, potential trivial cues driving proper/common noun separation).
