Summary
The paper investigates to what extent distributional word embeddings encode information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, using the British National Corpus (BNC) and Universal Dependencies (UD) labels. The authors train Skip-gram embeddings (300d) and a multinomial logistic classifier to predict PoS from word vectors. They show high accuracy on BNC words and strong performance on UD lemmas, then analyze systematic misclassifications to surface boundary cases (e.g., participles, nominal modifiers) and corpus annotation inconsistencies (e.g., years as NUM vs NOUN, clear/plain tagged as ADV). A second experiment trains embeddings without PoS tags and learns a “context-free” PoS predictor from prototypical UD lemmas, reaching 0.84 accuracy. Finally, a probing analysis suggests PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A diagnostic framework that uses distributional embeddings and a simple classifier to reveal soft PoS boundaries and to audit corpora for likely annotation inconsistencies. The qualitative error analysis is careful and yields linguistically meaningful findings.
- Secondary: Empirical evidence that PoS affiliation is robustly encoded in standard word embeddings and is distributed across many dimensions, supported by controlled probing (ANOVA ranking of features).
- Tertiary: A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained from ≈1.5k “prototypical” lemmas that achieves reasonable accuracy, underscoring the strength of distributional signals alone.

Strengths
- Clear empirical setup and thorough analysis: The pipeline (BNC preprocessing to UD tags, Skip-gram training, logistic classifier) is straightforward and well-documented. The authors sensibly evaluate on unseen BNC words and on UD lemmas, demonstrating robustness beyond the training vocabulary.
- Insightful error analysis with concrete linguistic takeaways: The misclassification patterns (e.g., PROPN↔NOUN, NOUN↔ADJ modifiers, passive participles as ADJ-like, numerals vs years and amounts) neatly illustrate graded PoS membership and genuine annotation inconsistencies. The identification of BNC’s ADV vs ADJ issue (e.g., clear/plain) is especially compelling and practically useful for corpus maintenance.
- Practical utility for corpus auditing: The approach offers a lightweight, reusable tool to flag boundary cases and systematic errors across resources, complementing standard tagger-based diagnostics. The comparison with a mainstream tagger highlights that the embedding-based method surfaces different—and often more linguistically interesting—issues.
- Probing perspective on embeddings: The ANOVA and k-best feature experiments support the claim that PoS information is not confined to one or two dimensions, aligning with current views on distributed semantics and offering interpretability beyond black-box end-to-end taggers.
- Well-written and accessible: The exposition is clear, with sensible design choices and transparent reporting of hyperparameters and results.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The work applies well-known tools (Skip-gram, logistic regression) in a fairly standard way. The main value is the analysis, not new modeling. This fits an empirical linguistics venue but is less compelling as an NLP methods contribution.
- Potential leakage and evaluation caveats in the first experiment: Training embeddings on lemma_POS tokens and then predicting the POS of lemma_POS vectors risks encoding tag-structure regularities into the context space, inflating separability. Moreover, the 10-fold cross-validation uses repeated instances of the same word vector across folds (frequency-weighted), which can overstate performance; a leave-one-type-out split would be more conservative.
- Comparison to a contextual tagger is mismatched: Feeding isolated tokens to the Stanford Tagger is not how such models are intended to be used, weakening the conclusion that the embedding-based diagnostic reveals distinct errors. A fairer comparison would analyze the tagger’s confusion matrix when run on full sentences.
- Missing baselines and controls: It would help to compare against simpler distributional baselines (e.g., Brown clusters, counts of neighboring UD tags, PMI with closed-class cues), frequency-only baselines, or type-balanced training to rule out frequency confounds. Similarly, demonstrating robustness across different window sizes, dimensions, or subword models would strengthen claims.
- Scope: Results are limited to English and a single corpus for embedding training. The conclusions about graded PoS boundaries would be more persuasive with at least one typologically distinct language.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- How do results change under leave-one-type-out cross-validation (no identical vectors shared across folds) and without frequency-weighted duplication?
- Can you replicate the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on plain lemmas (no POS tags) to remove potential leakage in the first experiment?
- What is the sensitivity to embedding hyperparameters (window size, dimension, negative samples) and to corpus choice (e.g., Wikipedia vs BNC)?
- Can you provide a small manual validation study quantifying what proportion of “outliers” truly correspond to annotation inconsistencies vs genuine boundary cases?
- For PROPN vs NOUN, how do capitalization/orthographic factors (if any) in lemmas affect results? Are UD lemma normalizations consistent?

Additional comments
- Related work to cite: Lin et al. (2015) on POS induction with embeddings; Ling et al. (2015) on character-based representations for POS; Plank et al. (2016) on BiLSTM POS tagging with auxiliary tasks. Also earlier distributional/cluster-based POS work (e.g., Schütze, Brown clusters, Turian/Collobert) would situate the study.
- Consider an additional probe: random orthogonal rotation of embeddings before probing; if performance remains, it further supports distributed (not axis-specific) encoding of PoS.
- Provide the mapping scripts, trained models, and lists of flagged outliers to facilitate reuse and corpus correction.
- Minor: Clean up references (years/authors formatting), clarify the fraction of UD tokens/types omitted due to OOV, and add a brief note on random seed control.
