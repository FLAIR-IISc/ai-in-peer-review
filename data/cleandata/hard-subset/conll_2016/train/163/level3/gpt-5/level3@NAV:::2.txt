Summary
The paper investigates how skip-gram embeddings trained on the BNC encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. A logistic classifier over embeddings achieves high accuracy and is used to surface systematic misalignments between distributional behavior and gold tags, yielding a detailed error analysis of “outlier” words. The authors also attempt a context-free PoS predictor from embeddings and analyze which embedding dimensions contribute to PoS discrimination.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A thorough, insightful error analysis showing how embedding-based misclassifications reveal graded PoS boundaries and corpus annotation inconsistencies.
2) Empirical confirmation, with feature analysis, that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than isolated axes.
3) A simple context-free baseline PoS predictor demonstrating nontrivial accuracy and utility for corpus auditing.

Strengths
- Strong, data-driven error analysis: The outlier study is careful and convincing, identifying plausible boundary cases (e.g., participles, adjectival nouns) and surfacing systematic BNC issues (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs). This is the paper’s main value.
- Cross-corpus sanity checks: Applying the classifier to UD vocabulary and contrasting with a Stanford tagger confusion analysis helps triangulate which confusions are uniquely exposed by distributional predictors.
- Clear feature ablation: The ANOVA-based ranking and k-feature curve support the claim that PoS cues are distributed across many dimensions.
- Practical utility: The approach is a useful tool for auditing PoS annotations and for exploratory lexicon analysis, especially for resource-poor settings.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: The claim that distributional vectors encode PoS is well established in work on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) and in embedding literature showing syntactic regularities (e.g., Mikolov et al., subsequent contextual embeddings). Section 5 overlaps substantially with prior induction/clustering baselines and lacks comparative evaluation.
- Framing and claims: The manuscript states embedding relations are “mostly semantic,” which is contradicted by prior work and by its own results. The motivation should connect to the original purpose of BNC PoS (supporting NLP tasks like parsing) and test whether embedding-derived distinctions improve parsing/tagging over original tags or UPOS.
- Tagset choice and related baselines: The switch to UPOS may obscure interesting fine-grained BNC distinctions; there is no comparison to Brown clusters, which often align well with UPOS, nor to simple context-count or morphological baselines.
- Methodological blind spots: Analyses focus on frequent word–tag pairs; the low-frequency tail and classifier failures there are underexplored. Proper vs. common noun “differences” may be confounded by function-word distributions (e.g., “the”); controls are needed. Footnote 3 is unclear about whose failed clustering attempts, criteria for failure, and omission of Brown clusters.
- Minor: Please change sentential citations to \newcite{}.

Questions to Authors
- Can you compare against prior distributional PoS induction and Brown clustering on the same data, and report novelty/accuracy deltas?
- Does replacing gold PoS with embedding-induced classes improve downstream parsing/tagging?
- Why UPOS over original BNC tags? Can you replicate analyses with fine-grained tags?
- Can you analyze rare words with subword/morphological features beyond lemmatization to better generalize PoS for low-frequency items?
- Please clarify Footnote 3 (who attempted clustering, failure criteria, and whether Brown clusters were tried).
