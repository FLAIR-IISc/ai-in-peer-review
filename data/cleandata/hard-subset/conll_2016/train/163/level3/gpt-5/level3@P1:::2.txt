Summary
The paper trains word embeddings on a lemmatized, UPOS-annotated BNC and shows that a multinomial classifier can predict PoS from embeddings with high accuracy; misclassifications surface “graded” PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies. A second experiment builds a context-free PoS predictor by training on “prototypical” UD lemmas and reports reasonable accuracy. Finally, the paper analyzes which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS, arguing that PoS information is distributed across many components.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A careful, data-driven error analysis using an embedding-based PoS predictor to identify boundary phenomena and likely corpus annotation issues (e.g., adjective/adverb confusions, NUM vs NOUN for amounts/years), with cross-corpus corroboration on UD. This corpus auditing perspective is the paper’s most distinctive value.
- Secondary: Empirical quantification that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized in one or two, supported by component ranking and learning curves.
- Tertiary: A simple prototype-based, context-free PoS predictor achieving reasonable accuracy, suggesting a low-resource pretagging utility, though the modeling novelty is limited.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis: The confusion-coverage analysis highlights linguistically plausible boundary cases (e.g., passive participles behaving adjectivally; SCONJ vs ADV; ADJ/NOUN modifiers) and uncovers systematic BNC inconsistencies (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as adverbs). The comparison with a standard tagger’s confusion matrix shows the approach surfaces different, useful signals for corpus auditing.
- Robustness across datasets: The classifier trained on BNC embeddings performs well on UD lemmas, indicating that the learned distributional PoS signals are not purely corpus-specific artifacts.
- Clear, simple methodology: Using logistic regression with frequency-weighted instances is transparent and reproducible, making the analysis easy to interpret and extend.
- Component-level analysis: Ranking dimensions by ANOVA F-values and showing accuracy improvements as k increases nicely supports the claim that PoS is distributed across many features; this is a useful quantitative takeaway for interpretability discussions.
- Practical utility for resource-poor settings: The prototype-based predictor trained on ∼1k unambiguous lemmas is promising as a quick-and-dirty pretagger or for bootstrapping annotation, especially when paired with subsequent human validation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to distributional PoS induction literature: The core claim—embeddings carry PoS information—has been demonstrated in unsupervised PoS induction/clustering and Brown-cluster work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., Blunsom & Cohn, Yatbaz et al.). Section 5’s predictor and the PoS/embedding link should be framed against that body of work, and direct baselines (e.g., Brown clusters, Clark clustering, dependency-based embeddings) are missing.
- Incomplete related work and mismatched positioning: Section 2 both understates syntactic content in embeddings and omits key PoS induction references. The assertion that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” conflicts with prior evidence (and with the paper’s own findings) that they capture substantial syntactic regularities.
- Motivation relative to downstream goals: The paper does not articulate whether the embedding-derived distinctions improve downstream tasks that traditionally motivate PoS tagging (e.g., parsing, NER). Reframing the findings in terms of whether these “soft affiliations” or corrected annotations help parsing or tagging would strengthen the contribution.
- Tagset choice and granularity: Converting BNC to UPOS facilitates cross-corpus comparisons but discards potentially informative fine-grained distinctions (e.g., verbal morphology, subcategories within determiners/pronouns). The analysis would benefit from parallel results on the original BNC tagset to show whether fine-grained categories exhibit similar graded behavior.
- Frequency effects underexplored: The analysis emphasizes the top-10k frequent words and mid-frequency test set, but the low-frequency tail—where embeddings are noisy and PoS decisions are most difficult—deserves deeper attention. Several highlighted errors (e.g., rare proper names) may reflect data sparsity rather than linguistic phenomena. Stratified results and error types by frequency bins are needed.
- Methodological clarity: Footnote 3 is unclear about whose clustering attempts failed, how failure was defined, and why strong baselines like Brown clusters were not pursued. Also, the proper noun vs common noun distinction may be confounded by trivial functional cues (articles, capitalization); more controlled context analysis is needed to justify the claim that embeddings capture nontrivial differences.

Questions to Authors
- Related work and baselines: Please position your approach against unsupervised PoS induction and Brown-cluster baselines, and include comparisons (e.g., cluster-to-PoS mapping accuracy, confusion/coverage patterns). How would dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg) change your PoS results?
- UPOS conversion: Why prioritize UPOS over the original BNC tagset for your primary analyses? Can you replicate key findings on the native, fine-grained tags and report where UPOS collapses distinctions that your method could have detected?
- Frequency stratification: Can you provide performance and error-type distributions across frequency bins (e.g., 5–50, 50–100, 100–500, >500)? How much of the outlier set remains after controlling for frequency and OOV/rare contexts?
- Proper vs common nouns: Beyond articles/determiners and capitalization, which contextual cues drive the separation? Please report the most diagnostic context features or nearest neighbors for correctly separated proper nouns vs those misclassified.
- Prototype selection: How sensitive are results to the “unambiguous” threshold (1/2) and the number of prototypes? Could few-shot selection bias the predictor toward closed-class items?
- Downstream impact: Do the flagged BNC inconsistencies, once corrected, measurably improve a tagger or a dependency parser trained on BNC? A small simulation (before/after) would contextualize the practical value.
- Modeling variants: Have you tried subword-aware embeddings (e.g., fastText) or character n-gram features to improve generalization for rare words, and did they change the error profile?

Additional comments
- Please clarify footnote 3 (who attempted clustering, what algorithms, how failure was defined). Include Brown clusters as a baseline and discuss their known alignment with UPOS.
- Consider analyzing word-form embeddings (not only lemmas) and dependency-based contexts to better capture syntactic signals.
- Minor: adopt \newcite{} for sentential citations and proofread the references for formatting consistency.
