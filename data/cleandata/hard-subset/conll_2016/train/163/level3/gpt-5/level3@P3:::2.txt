Summary and contributions
- Primary: A simple, well-executed diagnostic framework showing that static word embeddings trained on the BNC encode enough signal to predict UPOS tags with high accuracy and that systematic, interpretable outliers often correspond to annotation inconsistencies or soft PoS boundaries.
- Secondary: A detailed qualitative error analysis that surfaces concrete boundary cases (e.g., participles, noun–adjective alternations, numerals vs. years/currency), demonstrating the utility of embedding-based probes as corpus-auditing tools.
- Tertiary: An empirical observation that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, supported by feature ablations.

Strengths
- Careful, insightful error analysis: The paper goes beyond reporting accuracy, mining confusions that reveal plausible corpus inconsistencies (e.g., adverbs vs. adjectives like “plain,” “clear”) and genuine gray areas (participles; nominal modifiers). This is the paper’s most compelling contribution and a useful recipe for auditing tagged corpora.
- Solid experimental design for the stated goal: The type-level classifier, frequency-weighting, and an out-of-domain check on UD English show the effect is robust across datasets and not purely an artifact of the training regime.
- Clear empirical confirmation that embeddings encode PoS: High F-scores on frequent words and decent performance without context in Section 5 provide quantitative support, with a clean, reproducible setup.
- Practical utility for resource-poor settings: The “prototypical words” approach (Section 5) is a simple, low-resource baseline for bootstrapping tags—useful even if not SOTA.
- Component-distribution result: The ANOVA/ablation plot suggests PoS signal is not confined to a few axes, reinforcing the “distributed” nature of the representation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to distributional PoS induction literature: The claim that distributional vectors contain PoS information and can be used to induce/separate PoS is well established. Key related work is omitted (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012), making Section 5’s “tagging” result appear incremental and insufficiently contextualized. Without engaging this literature, the contribution reads as a replication plus case studies.
- Framing and objectives: The paper doesn’t address why the BNC was PoS-tagged in the first place (downstream tasks like parsing) and thus misses an opportunity to test whether embedding-driven distinctions improve those tasks. A more impactful framing would compare: original tags vs. UPOS vs. embedding-predicted tags as features for a parser or a context-aware tagger.
- “Mostly semantic” assertion contradicts prior and own evidence: Section 1 frames embedding relations as “mostly semantic,” which conflicts with abundant literature demonstrating strong syntactic regularities (e.g., Mikolov’s analogies; prior work correlating dimensions with morpho-syntax) and with the paper’s own results (small window; clear syntactic signal). This needs correction and a more precise discussion of how window size influences syntactic vs. semantic information.
- Questionable move to UPOS for the main analyses: Collapsing the rich CLAWS tagset to UPOS discards many interesting fine-grained distinctions (e.g., noun subtypes, verb forms, determiner classes) where distributional diagnostics could be most informative. At minimum, a parallel analysis on the original tags would better test the claimed benefits.
- Footnote 3 is unclear and misses an obvious baseline: It is ambiguous whether the failed clustering attempts are the authors’ or others’, what “failure” means, and how many clusters were used. Moreover, Brown clusters—known to align reasonably with UPOS—are not discussed as a baseline or comparison point.
- Trivial confusions and insufficient justification: The “difference” between proper and common nouns is largely explained by simple distributional cues (e.g., articles like “the”), so the claim needs deeper justification (e.g., control for function-word contexts, capitalization, and genre).
- Bias toward frequent types and lack of tail analysis: The core analysis centers on the top 10k types and frequency-weighted training, with relatively little attention to low-frequency words—precisely where pure distributional features degrade. Error analysis on the tail and ablations isolating frequency effects are needed.
- Rare-word generalization: The paper sticks to distributional and lemma-only features. It should explore or at least discuss alternatives that are known to help rare/novel words: character/subword models, suffix/prefix features, simple morphological analyzers/features, retrofitting, or lexicon-based backoffs. This is especially relevant to Section 5’s tagger.
- Interpretation of dimensions: Claims about specific components (e.g., “dimension 94” for conjunctions) should acknowledge basis non-identifiability of embeddings; component-level interpretability without a fixed rotation or probing framework is fragile.

Questions to authors
- Related work: Please position your method relative to distributional PoS induction (Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012). What is new beyond confirming that embeddings encode PoS?
- UPOS vs. original tags: Why collapse to UPOS for the main results? Can you replicate the analyses on the original CLAWS tags and report whether your outlier findings persist or become more informative?
- Footnote 3: Who attempted clustering, with how many clusters, which algorithm(s), and what were the objective failure criteria? Did you try Brown clustering or other hierarchical/discrete clusterings as a baseline against your classifier?
- Downstream utility: Do embedding-based PoS predictions or prototypical-distance features help a dependency parser or a context-aware tagger compared to gold UPOS or Brown clusters? A small controlled study would significantly strengthen the paper’s relevance.
- Frequency effects and tail: Can you provide error breakdowns by frequency bucket and a focused analysis on low-frequency types? How do subword or simple morphological features change those errors?
- Window size and syntactic signal: You use a small window (2), which likely enhances syntactic cues. How do your results vary with larger windows or CBOW vs. Skip-gram?

Additional comments
- Please switch to sentential-citation style \newcite{} where appropriate and clean up several reference entries (incomplete author/year formatting).
- Consider controlling for function-word context when assessing PROPN vs. NOUN similarity (e.g., remove articles/prepositions or weight contexts).
- Report macro vs. micro metrics per tag; clarify train/test splits to avoid leakage; include variance over random seeds.
