- Summary: Paper studies how skip-gram embeddings from the BNC encode PoS, using a logistic classifier to find systematic misalignments with gold tags, plus a context-free PoS predictor and dimension-wise feature analysis.

Contributions:
- Detailed error analysis showing embedding misclassifications reveal graded PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies.
- Empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, not isolated axes.
- A simple context-free baseline PoS predictor that attains nontrivial accuracy and aids corpus auditing.

Strengths:
- Strong, data-driven outlier analysis identifying plausible boundary cases and systematic BNC tagging issues.
- Cross-corpus checks (UD vocabulary and Stanford tagger contrasts) help triangulate confusions exposed by distributional predictors.
- Clear feature ablation (ANOVA ranking and k-feature curves) supporting distributed PoS cues.
- Practical utility for auditing PoS annotations and exploratory lexicon analysis, especially for low-resource settings.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: claims echo prior distributional PoS induction and embedding syntactic-regularity work, with Section 5 overlapping prior baselines and lacking comparative evaluation.
- Framing/claims inconsistent: stating embeddings are “mostly semantic” contradicts prior work and the paper’s own results; motivation should link to BNC’s original NLP purposes and evaluate downstream impact.
- Tagset and baseline choices questionable: switching to UPOS may hide fine-grained BNC distinctions, and there’s no comparison to Brown clusters or simple context/morphological baselines.
- Methodological blind spots: focus on frequent word–tag pairs ignores low-frequency tail and classifier failures; some reported noun differences may be confounded by function-word distributions and lack necessary controls.
- Minor issues: unclear footnote about failed clustering attempts and requested citation format change.

Questions for authors:
- Can you compare against prior distributional PoS induction methods and Brown clustering on the same data, reporting novelty and accuracy deltas?
- Does using embedding-induced classes instead of gold PoS improve downstream parsing or tagging?
- Why choose UPOS over original BNC tags, and can analyses be replicated with fine-grained tags?
- Can you analyze rare words using subword/morphological features beyond lemmatization to better handle low-frequency items?
- Please clarify Footnote 3: who attempted clustering, what were the failure criteria, and were Brown clusters tried?
