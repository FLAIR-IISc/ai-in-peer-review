**Paper Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional word representations (word embeddings) and Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier to predict the PoS tag of a word based solely on its pre-trained embedding. By analyzing the classifier's errors, they identify words whose distributional behavior diverges from the prototype of their assigned PoS class. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel methodology for analyzing PoS tagsets and corpus annotations. By examining the misclassifications of an embedding-based PoS classifier, the authors effectively identify both well-known areas of linguistic ambiguity (e.g., the noun-adjective continuum) and potential inconsistencies or systematic errors in the British National Corpus (BNC) annotation (e.g., the classification of currency amounts and years).
2.  An empirical demonstration that information related to a word's PoS is robustly encoded in its embedding, but is distributed across a large number of vector dimensions rather than being concentrated in a few. This is supported by an experiment showing that classifier performance increases steadily as more embedding dimensions are included.
3.  A proof-of-concept for a simple, context-independent PoS tagger. While not state-of-the-art, its performance is notable given that it relies only on a word's embedding and was trained on a small set of prototypical examples, suggesting potential utility in low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths**

1.  **Insightful Methodology for Corpus Analysis:** The core idea of using a classifier's prediction errors on embeddings as a lens to examine PoS boundaries is both clever and effective. It provides a principled, data-driven method for flagging "outlier" words and word classes, which can be a valuable tool for linguists studying grammatical categories and for annotators seeking to improve corpus consistency.
2.  **Excellent Qualitative Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is the highlight of the paper. The authors move beyond a simple confusion matrix to provide compelling linguistic explanations for the observed error patterns. The specific examples given—such as passive participles behaving like adjectives, currency amounts behaving like numerals, and years behaving like nouns—convincingly demonstrate the method's ability to uncover nuanced distributional distinctions and potential annotation inconsistencies.
3.  **Well-Designed and Complementary Experiments:** The experimental design is logical and supports the paper's claims well. The initial experiment using `lemma_POS` tokens is a creative way to isolate vectors for specific word senses/functions. The subsequent experiment in Section 5, which uses a standard embedding model, successfully shows that the core findings are not dependent on this specialized training setup. Furthermore, the comparison with the Stanford Tagger's errors effectively demonstrates that the proposed distributional method offers a different and complementary perspective on PoS ambiguity.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconventional Embedding Training:** The primary experiment in Sections 3 and 4 trains embeddings on a corpus where tokens are concatenated `lemma_POS` strings. This is a non-standard setup that conflates a word's tag with the tags of its neighbors in the context window. While this serves the purpose of creating distinct vectors for `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`, the implications of this choice are not fully discussed. It is unclear how these embeddings differ from those trained on a standard corpus of lemmas. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer motivation for this choice or a comparison to a more standard approach (e.g., training on lemmas and then averaging vectors based on the gold PoS tags).
2.  **Limited Evaluation of the PoS Predictor:** The PoS predictor in Section 5 is an interesting proof-of-concept, but its evaluation is minimal. It is difficult to assess the reported 0.84 accuracy without a simple, strong baseline. For instance, a baseline that assigns each lemma its most frequent tag from the training corpus would help contextualize the performance and clarify how much the distributional information is adding beyond simple lexical priors.
3.  **Apparent Contradiction in Feature Analysis:** In Section 3, the analysis of prototypical vectors for conjunctions (Figures 1 and 2) shows a very strong, isolated spike in a single vector dimension (component 94). However, the conclusion of Section 5 is that PoS information is broadly distributed across at least 100 dimensions. The paper notes that the conjunction case is "solitary," but does not reconcile these two seemingly contradictory findings. This could be clarified by explaining if this is a unique property of certain closed-class words, or perhaps an artifact of the `lemma_POS` training method used for the first experiment.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train the initial model on `lemma_POS` tokens? Have you considered an alternative where a standard model is trained on lemmas, and vectors are subsequently grouped and averaged according to their gold PoS tags in the corpus? Do you hypothesize that this would yield a similar set of "outlier" words?
2.  For the PoS predictor experiment in Section 5, could you provide results for a majority-class baseline (i.e., a tagger that assigns each word its most frequent tag from the training data)? This would help to better situate the 0.84 accuracy result and quantify the contribution of the embedding-based features.
3.  Regarding the analysis of vector components, could you offer more insight into the apparent discrepancy between the "killer feature" (component 94) for conjunctions and the conclusion that PoS information is widely distributed? Is this sharp feature specific to conjunctions, or do other closed-class words exhibit similar properties? Is this phenomenon also observed in the model trained on lemmas only, or is it an effect of the `lemma_POS` training scheme?
