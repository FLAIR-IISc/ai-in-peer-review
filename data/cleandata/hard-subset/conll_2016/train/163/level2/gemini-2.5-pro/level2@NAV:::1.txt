**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings capture information about Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). The core of the work is a detailed qualitative analysis of the classifier's errors, which are used to identify words that behave distributionally differently from their canonical PoS class.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A methodology for linguistic analysis using classification errors:** The paper compellingly demonstrates that analyzing the misclassifications of a PoS-from-embedding classifier is a valuable tool for linguistic discovery. This approach effectively identifies words on the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes (e.g., passive participles behaving as adjectives) and uncovers potential inconsistencies or systematic errors in gold-standard corpora.
2.  **Empirical support for graded PoS membership:** The work provides strong, data-driven evidence supporting the linguistic notion that PoS categories are not rigid but exist on a continuum. The identified "outliers" are shown to be linguistically motivated rather than random noise.

**Strengths**

1.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The primary strength of this paper is the thorough and well-reasoned analysis in Section 4. The authors provide concrete examples for each major error type (NOUN-ADJ, VERB-ADJ, NUM-NOUN, etc.), convincingly arguing that the distributional model captures meaningful linguistic nuances that challenge traditional PoS boundaries. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger's error patterns effectively highlights the unique value of this distributional approach.
2.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The methodology is clear and appropriate. The authors use separate training and test sets, weight instances by frequency to handle class imbalance, and validate their findings on a second dataset (Universal Dependencies Treebank), all of which lend credibility to the results. The second experiment, which builds a context-free tagger from scratch, further reinforces the core claim that PoS information is robustly encoded in the embeddings.
3.  **Clear Application:** The proposed method serves as a practical tool for computational linguists and corpus creators to probe annotations, identify systematic errors, and explore the syntactic properties of words in a data-driven manner.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of High-Level Findings:** While the analytical method is valuable, the core conclusions—that word embeddings contain syntactic information and that PoS boundaries are fuzzy—are well-established in the field. The paper confirms existing knowledge rather than presenting a fundamentally new discovery. The work would be stronger if it more clearly situated its specific analytical contribution against prior work that has correlated embedding dimensions with linguistic properties.
2.  **Unconventional Embedding Training:** The decision to train embeddings on concatenated `lemma_PoSTag` strings for the main analysis is unusual and not fully justified. This approach may conflate lexical and tag information in the context window, potentially influencing the results in non-obvious ways. A comparison with a more standard setup (training on lemmas, then labeling vectors for classification) would be necessary to isolate the effects.
3.  **Overstated Title:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is an overstatement. The paper provides a method to *probe* or *examine* PoS boundaries and inconsistencies, but it does not propose a new, formal PoS framework or tagset. The contribution is analytical and exploratory, not definitional.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for training embeddings on `lemma_PoSTag` strings? How might this approach affect the learned representations compared to training on raw lemmas and using the PoS tags purely as labels for the classifier?
