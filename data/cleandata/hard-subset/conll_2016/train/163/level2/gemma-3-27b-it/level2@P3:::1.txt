This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The core idea is that word embeddings, trained on a large corpus (BNC), implicitly encode information about PoS, which can be leveraged to identify inconsistencies in annotation schemes and support a more nuanced, ‘soft’ view of PoS boundaries. The primary contribution is demonstrating that PoS information is demonstrably present in word embeddings and can be extracted through classification and analysis of misclassifications, potentially revealing annotation errors or boundary cases. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of which vector components are most indicative of PoS affiliation, finding that it’s distributed across numerous dimensions rather than concentrated in a few. Finally, the work presents a baseline PoS tagger built solely on distributional information, without relying on contextual features typically used in traditional taggers.

Strengths:

1. **Novel Application of Distributional Semantics:** The work applies distributional semantic models to a classic NLP problem (PoS tagging) in a novel way – not to *improve* tagging performance directly, but to *re-examine* the underlying linguistic categories themselves. This is a valuable perspective shift that moves beyond purely performance-oriented evaluation. The exploration of what embeddings reveal about annotation inconsistencies is particularly insightful.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The thorough analysis of misclassified words is a significant strength. Going beyond simple accuracy metrics to examine *why* the classifier fails provides valuable linguistic insights. Identifying cases like currency amounts tagged as nouns, or the potential mis-tagging of adjectives as adverbs in the BNC, indicates the potential for the method to act as a corpus auditing tool.
3. **Demonstration of Distributed Representation:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector components supports the established theory of distributional semantics and provides empirical evidence against the idea that PoS can be captured by a small set of features. The experiments quantifying this distribution are well-designed and convincingly presented.
4. **Robustness Across Datasets:** Applying the trained classifier to the UD Treebank and achieving good performance further strengthens the claims of generalizability. This shows the learned representations aren’t overly specific to the BNC and exhibit a degree of transferability.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Scope of the PoS Tagger:** While the PoS tagger built solely from embeddings is a worthwhile experiment, its performance (84% accuracy) is considerably lower than state-of-the-art taggers that leverage contextual information. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential avenues for improvement (e.g., incorporating character-level information, or exploring different embedding training parameters) would be beneficial.
2. **Reliance on Universal Dependencies as Ground Truth:** The use of UD as a “gold standard” for evaluating the BNC annotation raises a potential issue of circularity. UD represents a specific linguistic perspective, and assuming its correctness when evaluating the BNC is debatable. While the inconsistencies identified are interesting, framing them solely as errors in the BNC, rather than potential differences in annotation philosophy, is a limitation.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods for Error Detection:** The paper demonstrates the ability to detect annotation errors, but doesn’t directly compare its approach to existing methods for corpus auditing, such as agreement studies or rule-based error detection systems. A comparative evaluation would strengthen the claim that this distributional semantic approach offers a unique or superior advantage.
4. **Superficial Discussion of Vector Component Analysis:** While the finding of distributed representation is valuable, the discussion of specific vector components (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions) is somewhat limited. A deeper dive into *what* these components capture linguistically, and *why* they are relevant to PoS, would enrich the analysis.

Questions to Authors:

1.  The paper mentions the possibility of using this approach for resource-poor languages. Could you elaborate on the practical steps involved and the expected performance gains in such a scenario? What quantity of manually tagged data would be required to achieve reasonable results?
2.  The error analysis identifies several interesting discrepancies between the BNC and UD annotations. Could you discuss the potential reasons for these differences in more detail, considering the differing annotation guidelines and linguistic philosophies behind each corpus?
3.  What specific efforts were made to ensure the quality of the manual mapping between BNC tags and Universal tags? Could you provide more detail on the decision-making process for resolving ambiguities?

Additional Comments:

*   The paper’s structure is logical and the writing is clear.
*   The references are relevant and up-to-date.
*   Providing the code and data used in the experiments would greatly enhance reproducibility.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key errors found and their potential implications for annotation guidelines.
