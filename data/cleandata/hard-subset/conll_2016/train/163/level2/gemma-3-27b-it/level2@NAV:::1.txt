## Review for Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors *do* contain PoS information. This approach allows them to identify words with distributional patterns deviating from others of the same PoS, potentially revealing annotation inconsistencies or supporting the idea of “soft” PoS affiliations. They also find that PoS information is spread across many vector components, not localized to just a few.

The primary contribution of this work is a compelling demonstration of the utility of distributional semantics in *revisiting* established linguistic categories. While the idea that embeddings capture syntactic information isn’t novel, the authors go beyond simply showing *that* it’s present, and leverage it to pinpoint potential issues within a well-established, manually annotated corpus (BNC). The secondary contribution is a detailed analysis of these “outlier” words, offering insights into the complexities of PoS boundaries (e.g., nouns acting as adjectives, currency amounts behaving like numerals). Finally, the finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components is a valuable observation regarding the nature of linguistic encoding in these models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Relevant Background:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of fuzzy PoS boundaries and positions the work within the context of distributional semantics and corpus linguistics.
*   **Empirical Rigor:** The authors employ a robust methodology, utilizing a large corpus (BNC), a well-established embedding technique (Skipgram), and thorough evaluation with both in-sample and out-of-sample testing, as well as comparison with a standard tagger.
*   **Interesting Findings:** The identification of potential annotation errors and the exploration of “soft” PoS boundaries are insightful. The analysis of misclassified words provides concrete examples and sparks further inquiry.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors mention releasing the mappings between BNC and Universal tags, increasing the potential for replication.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Technique:** While the application to PoS boundary analysis is valuable, the core technique of using embeddings for classification is well-established. The paper doesn’t present a novel embedding method or classifier architecture.
*   **Shallow Error Analysis:** While the error analysis is a strength, it could be more in-depth. For example, exploring the specific vector components that differentiate the misclassified words would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art PoS Taggers:** The comparison to the Stanford tagger is useful, but a more comprehensive comparison with recent, high-performing PoS taggers (especially those utilizing contextualized embeddings like BERT) would provide a better benchmark.
*   **Missing Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention hyperparameters but don’t discuss the sensitivity of their results to different parameter settings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the specific vector components that are most indicative of the identified errors? What linguistic properties might these components represent?
2.  How do the results change when using different embedding dimensions (e.g., 100, 600)?
3.  Did you consider using contextualized word embeddings (e.g., from BERT) in your analysis? If so, how did the results compare?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The code used to create the BNC/Universal tag mappings would be a valuable addition to the publicly available resources.
