- Core contribution: shows word embeddings encode significant PoS information and can reveal annotation errors or ambiguous cases.  
- Secondary contribution: demonstrates PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, indicating graded PoS affiliations.  
- Methodology: trains a PoS predictor on BNC embeddings and analyzes classifier misclassifications to identify atypical distributional patterns.  

Strengths:
- Well-motivated premise linking distributional semantics to grammatical understanding.  
- In-depth, linguistically insightful analysis of classifier misclassifications (e.g., proper vs. common nouns, participles as adjectives).  
- Robustness: experiments validated on a separate BNC test set and the UD Treebank, showing generalizability.  
- Notable finding that PoS cues are distributed across dimensions rather than concentrated in few features.  
- Clear, logical presentation and organization of the paper.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: approach (predicting PoS from embeddings) is not fundamentally new; main value is depth of analysis.  
- Classifier choice and baselines are weak: only logistic regression and majority baseline used; comparison to stronger/tagger baselines lacking.  
- Annotation dependency: reliance on BNC/UD annotations may bias findings; unclear impact of annotation quality on results.  
- Hyperparameter influence not explored: no experiments on embedding parameters (vector size, window, etc.).  
- Lack of quantitative breakdown of error types; qualitative analysis not supported by frequency statistics.  

Questions for authors:
- What is the Stanford POS tagger accuracy on the BNC, to benchmark error-detection capability?  
- Have you systematically compared BNC annotations to other corpora (e.g., Penn Treebank) to quantify annotation discrepancies?  
- Is classifier performance limited by the distributional model itself or by methodology (classifier choice, features)?  
- What computational resources were required to train the embeddings and classifier for reproducibility?  

Additional suggestions:
- Add a table summarizing most frequent misclassifications with frequencies and corresponding PoS tags.  
- Improve figure labels and annotations for clarity.  
- Expand discussion of study limitations and outline directions for future research.
