## Review for CONLL 2016: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze misclassifications to identify inconsistencies in annotation or support the idea of ‘soft’ PoS affiliations. They also explore the distributed nature of PoS information within the vector space.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Word Embeddings:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that distributional semantic models, specifically word embeddings, encode information about PoS tags. This is evidenced by the high accuracy achieved by a logistic regression classifier trained to predict PoS tags from embeddings, confirming the distributional hypothesis in the context of grammatical categories.
2. **Error Analysis for Linguistic Insight:** The analysis of misclassified words provides valuable insights into the complexities of PoS boundaries and potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Identifying cases like currency amounts misclassified as nouns, or adverbs mistaken for adjectives, highlights areas where annotation guidelines could be refined or where linguistic categories are inherently blurry. This contribution goes beyond simply showing *that* embeddings encode PoS information; it demonstrates how that information can be used to analyze and potentially improve linguistic resources.
3. **Confirmation of Distributed PoS Representation:** The study confirms that PoS information is not localized to a small number of vector components but is distributed throughout the embedding space. This supports the idea that semantic and syntactic information are intertwined within these models, and challenges the notion of easily identifiable ‘PoS features’.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The reported accuracy of the PoS classifiers (97.9% on training data, 91% on unseen data) is strong evidence supporting the core hypothesis. The use of 10-fold cross-validation and testing on a separate corpus (UD Treebank) strengthens the reliability of these results.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassifications is a significant strength. It moves beyond simply reporting accuracy numbers and provides concrete examples of linguistic phenomena that are challenging for both distributional models and traditional annotation schemes. The comparison to errors identified by the Stanford tagger is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the research question, and the sections are well-defined and easy to follow. The inclusion of examples enhances understanding.
4. **Use of Established Resources:** Leveraging the BNC and Universal Dependencies Treebank provides a solid foundation for the research and ensures reproducibility. The clear documentation of the mapping between BNC and Universal tags is commendable.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest avenues for future research, demonstrating a thoughtful understanding of the work's scope.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Finding:** While well-executed, the core finding—that word embeddings encode PoS information—is not entirely novel. Previous work (cited in the paper) has already established this connection. The novelty lies in the specific analysis of misclassifications and the detailed exploration of distributed representations within this context. However, the paper could benefit from more explicitly positioning its contribution relative to these prior studies, and highlighting *how* it extends or refines existing knowledge.
2. **Classifier Simplification & Baseline Comparisons:** The use of a simple logistic regression classifier, while effective, might obscure potential gains from more sophisticated models. Exploring more complex architectures (e.g., neural networks) could reveal additional insights. Furthermore, the comparison to a ‘majority class baseline’ is somewhat weak. Comparing against a strong, state-of-the-art PoS tagger on the same data would provide a more compelling benchmark.
3. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameters used for training the embeddings but doesn't explore their impact on performance. Investigating the sensitivity of the results to different window sizes, embedding dimensions, or negative sampling rates could strengthen the findings.
4. **Scalability Concerns:** The reliance on manually created mappings between BNC and Universal tags introduces a potential bottleneck for applying this approach to other languages or corpora. Exploring automated or semi-automated methods for tagset alignment would improve scalability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing a logistic regression classifier over other more complex models? Were any other models considered, and if so, why were they not pursued?
2.  The error analysis highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you considered quantifying the extent of these inconsistencies, and assessing their impact on the overall results?
3.  Regarding the distributed representation of PoS information: How do the top-ranked vector components identified relate to known linguistic features or semantic categories? Is there any interpretability to these components?
4.  You mention plans for extending the research to other languages. What challenges do you anticipate in adapting this approach to languages with different morphological or syntactic structures?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (1 and 2) are slightly difficult to interpret without more detailed explanations of the axes and their meaning.
*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential applications of this work, beyond simply refining PoS annotation. For example, could this approach be used to improve downstream NLP tasks like machine translation or information extraction?
