This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, exploring whether distributional semantic models can reveal insights into PoS boundaries and inconsistencies in annotation. The core idea is sound: if embeddings capture distributional information, they should reflect, and potentially refine, our understanding of PoS classes. The authors train a PoS predictor using embeddings from the BNC and analyze misclassifications to identify words with atypical distributional patterns.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that word embeddings *do* encode significant information about PoS, and that analyzing deviations from expected PoS assignments can highlight potential annotation errors or ambiguous cases. A second, related contribution is the empirical evidence suggesting that PoS information isn’t concentrated in a few vector dimensions, but is distributed across many, supporting the idea of ‘soft’ or ‘graded’ PoS affiliations. Finally, the detailed analysis of misclassifications provides a valuable resource for linguistic investigation, pinpointing specific word types and usage patterns that challenge conventional PoS boundaries.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Soundness of the Core Idea:** The premise – that distributional semantics can inform our understanding of grammar – is well-motivated and aligns with a growing body of work in computational linguistics. The paper builds on established theories (distributional hypothesis) and connects them to practical applications (PoS tagging, error detection).
2. **Comprehensive Analysis of Misclassifications:** The authors don’t simply report accuracy scores; they delve into the errors made by the classifier, providing insightful linguistic analysis of why certain words are misclassified. This is where the paper’s strength truly lies. The examples given (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, participles as adjectives) are compelling and illustrate the nuances of PoS boundaries.
3. **Robustness of Results:** The experiments aren’t limited to the training corpus. The classifier is tested on both a separate test set from the BNC and on the UD Treebank, demonstrating the generalizability of the findings. The good performance on the UD Treebank is particularly encouraging.
4. **Interesting Findings about Distributed Representation:** The observation that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions is notable and challenges the assumption that PoS can be readily identified by inspecting a few key features. This strengthens the argument for using distributional models to capture subtle linguistic variations.
5. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The organization is logical, progressing from background and related work to methodology, results, and discussion.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Overall Approach:** While the analysis of misclassifications is valuable, the core methodology – training a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags – isn't entirely novel. Previous work (cited in the paper) has already demonstrated this connection. The paper’s contribution is more in the *depth* of the analysis and the specific insights gained, rather than a groundbreaking new technique.
2. **Classifier Choice and Baselines:** The use of a simple logistic regression classifier is reasonable, but it would have been beneficial to compare the results against more sophisticated classifiers or state-of-the-art PoS taggers. The comparison to the majority class baseline is helpful, but a comparison to other methods could further contextualize the performance.
3. **Annotation Dependency:** The reliance on the BNC’s (and UD Treebank’s) annotation introduces a potential bias. If the annotation itself contains inconsistencies, the analysis of misclassifications may be skewed. While the authors acknowledge this issue, a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of annotation quality would be beneficial. The paper identifies errors in the BNC, but it's unclear to what extent these errors influence the overall findings.
4. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Influence:** The authors mention plans to explore the influence of hyperparameters, but the current paper doesn't present any results in this area. Investigating how different embedding parameters (e.g., vector size, window size) affect PoS discrimination could provide further insights.
5. **Lack of Quantitative Analysis of Error Types:** While the qualitative analysis of error types is insightful, a quantitative analysis (e.g., measuring the frequency of different error patterns) would strengthen the conclusions. For example, providing statistics on the percentage of each error type in the test set would be useful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the Stanford POS tagger was used for comparison in identifying annotation errors. Could you provide the accuracy of the Stanford tagger on the BNC corpus to provide a baseline for comparison to your method’s ability to detect errors?
2.  The paper highlights several cases where the BNC annotation might be inconsistent. Have you considered systematically comparing the BNC annotation to other corpora with different annotation schemes (e.g., Penn Treebank) to identify discrepancies and assess the prevalence of these issues?
3.  You note that the performance of the classifier isn’t spectacular compared to state-of-the-art taggers. Do you believe that the distributional model itself is a limiting factor, or are there other aspects of the methodology (e.g., classifier choice, feature selection) that could be improved?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to train the distributional model and the classifier? This information would be helpful for researchers interested in replicating the experiments.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the most frequent misclassifications, including their frequencies and the corresponding PoS tags.
*   The figures are helpful, but could be improved with clearer labels and annotations.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the study and potential areas for future research.
