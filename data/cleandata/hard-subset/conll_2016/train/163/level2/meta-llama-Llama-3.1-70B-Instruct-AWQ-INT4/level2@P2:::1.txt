This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and parts of speech (PoS) boundaries. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings and analyze the errors to identify words with distributional patterns different from other words of the same part of speech. The results show that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation, and the authors propose that this approach can be used to discover word groups with hidden inconsistencies in annotation or guidelines.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The authors demonstrate that word embeddings can be used to predict PoS tags with high accuracy, and that this approach can be used to identify words with distributional patterns different from other words of the same part of speech.
2. The authors show that the information about PoS affiliation is distributed among several components of the word embeddings, not limited to one or two specific features.
3. The authors propose that this approach can be used to discover word groups with hidden inconsistencies in annotation or guidelines, and to improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers.

Strengths:

1. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of PoS boundaries and the potential benefits of using word embeddings to address this issue.
2. The experimental setup is well-designed, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach using multiple datasets and metrics.
3. The analysis of errors is insightful, and the authors provide a detailed discussion of the types of errors that occur and their implications for PoS tagging.

Weaknesses:

1. The authors rely heavily on the BNC corpus, which may not be representative of other languages or genres.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, such as the potential for overfitting or the impact of hyperparameter tuning.
3. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach can be used to improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on how you plan to extend this work to other languages and genres?
2. How do you plan to address the potential limitations of your approach, such as overfitting or hyperparameter tuning?
3. Can you provide more details on how your approach can be used to improve the performance of existing automatic PoS taggers?

Additional Comments:

1. The authors may want to consider using more advanced techniques for analyzing the errors, such as clustering or dimensionality reduction.
2. The authors may want to consider using more robust metrics for evaluating the performance of their approach, such as precision and recall.
3. The authors may want to consider providing more details on the computational resources required to train and evaluate their approach.
