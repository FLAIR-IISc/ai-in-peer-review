**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) boundaries. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and analyze the results to identify patterns and inconsistencies in the annotation process. They also investigate the distribution of PoS-related information across vector components and propose a method for building a PoS tagger using distributional models.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. **Redefining PoS classes with distributional semantic models**: The paper demonstrates that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation and can be used to identify clusters of words that correspond to traditional PoS classes.
2. **Detecting inconsistencies in annotation**: The authors show that their approach can detect systematic errors or inconsistencies in PoS tags, whether they be automatic or manual.
3. **Distributional vectors as PoS predictors**: The paper proposes a method for building a PoS tagger using distributional models, which can predict PoS tags based solely on the proximity of a word's distributional vector to those of prototypical PoS examples.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel approach to PoS tagging**: The paper presents a new approach to PoS tagging that leverages distributional semantic models, which is a significant departure from traditional methods.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including an analysis of errors and inconsistencies in the annotation process.
3. **Insights into PoS boundaries**: The paper provides valuable insights into the nature of PoS boundaries and the challenges of annotating them accurately.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited scope**: The paper focuses primarily on English and may not be generalizable to other languages.
2. **Methodological limitations**: The authors acknowledge that their approach may not be suitable for all types of text data and that further research is needed to explore the effects of different hyperparameters on the performance of distributional models.
3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art PoS tagging methods to better understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other languages and what challenges do you anticipate?
2. Can you provide more details on the methodology used to train the distributional models and how you selected the hyperparameters?
3. How do you plan to address the limitations of your approach, such as its reliance on a large corpus of annotated text data?
