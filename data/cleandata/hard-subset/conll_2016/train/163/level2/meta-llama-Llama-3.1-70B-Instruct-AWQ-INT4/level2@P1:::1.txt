**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors use a distributional semantic model to train a classifier that predicts PoS tags for words based on their embeddings. They find that the model is able to accurately predict PoS tags for most words, but also identifies "outliers" that exhibit different distributional patterns. The authors analyze these outliers and discover interesting cases, such as words that are misclassified due to their context or words that exhibit characteristics of multiple PoS classes.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Demonstration of the effectiveness of distributional semantic models in predicting PoS tags**: The authors show that word embeddings contain information about PoS classes and can be used to predict PoS tags with high accuracy.
2. **Identification of "outliers" that exhibit different distributional patterns**: The authors analyze the errors made by the classifier and discover interesting cases that highlight the complexities of PoS classification.
3. **Insights into the nature of PoS boundaries**: The authors' analysis suggests that PoS boundaries are not strict and can be influenced by context and other factors.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to PoS classification**: The authors' use of distributional semantic models to predict PoS tags is a novel approach that offers new insights into the nature of PoS classification.
2. **Thorough analysis of errors**: The authors' analysis of the errors made by the classifier is thorough and provides interesting insights into the complexities of PoS classification.
3. **Use of multiple datasets**: The authors use multiple datasets, including the British National Corpus and the Universal Dependencies Treebank, to evaluate their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors' evaluation of their approach is limited to a few datasets and may not be generalizable to other datasets or languages.
2. **Simplistic classifier**: The authors use a simple logistic regression classifier, which may not be the most effective approach for PoS classification.
3. **Lack of comparison to other approaches**: The authors do not compare their approach to other approaches to PoS classification, which makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **How do you plan to extend your approach to other languages?**: The authors mention that they plan to conduct similar experiments with other languages, but do not provide details on how they plan to adapt their approach.
2. **How do you plan to improve the accuracy of your classifier?**: The authors' classifier achieves high accuracy, but there is still room for improvement. How do they plan to improve the accuracy of their classifier?
3. **How do you plan to address the issue of context in PoS classification?**: The authors' analysis highlights the importance of context in PoS classification. How do they plan to address this issue in their approach?
