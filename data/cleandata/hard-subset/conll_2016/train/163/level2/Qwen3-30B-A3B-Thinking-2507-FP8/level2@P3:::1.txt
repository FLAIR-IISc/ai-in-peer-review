

The paper investigates whether distributional semantic models encode part-of-speech (PoS) information, using classifiers trained on BNC-derived embeddings to identify lexical outliers and analyze PoS boundaries. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that PoS information is robustly embedded in word vectors (classifier F-score 0.979), (2) revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC through outlier analysis (e.g., proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, numerals mislabeled as nouns), and (3) establishing that PoS affiliation is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (requiring ~100 features for optimal performance).

Strengths include a rigorous analysis of annotation errors, where outliers (e.g., '1804' tagged as noun instead of numeral) expose systematic inconsistencies in the BNC, supported by cross-validation on Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99). The discovery that PoS knowledge spans numerous vector dimensions (Fig. 3), contradicting the assumption of "one or two key features," is a significant theoretical contribution. Additionally, the work provides a practical method for detecting corpus annotation errors without manual inspection, as evidenced by the identification of mislabeled adjectives (e.g., "plain" tagged as adverb).

Weaknesses include insufficient justification for the manual BNC-to-Universal tag mapping, which lacks validation metrics and could introduce errors in the outlier analysis (e.g., misaligned tags affecting error types in Tables 1–2). The classifier’s 0.84 accuracy on UD Treebank (Section 5) is underwhelming compared to state-of-the-art taggers (e.g., >0.97), and the paper does not adequately contextualize this gap—specifically, whether the "no-context" constraint is the sole reason or if embedding quality is a factor. Finally, the claim of "soft" PoS boundaries remains descriptive rather than theoretically grounded; the paper identifies distributional patterns but does not link them to established linguistic frameworks (e.g., Hanks’ graded categories or corpus-based typologies).

Questions to authors:  
1. How was the BNC-to-Universal tag mapping validated? Provide statistics on inter-annotator agreement or error rates in the mapping process.  
2. Given the classifier’s 0.84 accuracy (vs. >0.97 for modern taggers), what specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., morphological ambiguity, discourse context) prevent better performance, and how might this inform future embedding designs?
