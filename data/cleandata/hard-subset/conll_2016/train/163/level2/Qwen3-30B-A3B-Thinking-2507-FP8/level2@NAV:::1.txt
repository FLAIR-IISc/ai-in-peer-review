

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories, and whether this information can reveal inconsistencies in PoS annotation. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings from the British National Corpus, achieving 97.9% weighted F-score on training data and 91% on held-out test data. They then analyze misclassified words to identify cases where distributional patterns suggest annotation inconsistencies.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components rather than concentrated in a few features, (2) revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC through classifier errors (such as proper nouns misclassified as common nouns and verbs mistaken for adjectives), and (3) showing that distributional models can detect systematic annotation errors that standard PoS taggers might miss.

Strengths include the clear demonstration of PoS information distribution across vector components (Figure 3), the valuable linguistic insights gained from analyzing misclassifications (particularly the discovery of systematic annotation errors), and the robust evaluation across multiple datasets including the Universal Dependencies Treebank. The paper effectively shows how distributional models can uncover "soft" PoS boundaries that challenge traditional categorical approaches.

Weaknesses include insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers (which typically achieve >97% accuracy), limited discussion of limitations (particularly regarding words absent from the embedding vocabulary), and insufficient exploration of how different embedding hyperparameters affect PoS discrimination. The claim that the approach "reveals hidden inconsistencies" would be stronger with more detailed linguistic analysis of specific error cases.

The paper is well-executed methodologically, with appropriate statistical validation and clear presentation of results. However, the authors should clarify the practical significance of their method compared to existing PoS taggers, and address how their approach could be integrated into corpus annotation pipelines. The analysis of "outlier" cases provides valuable linguistic insights, but the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how these findings could inform future PoS tagging guidelines.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that bridges distributional semantics and linguistic theory, with clear potential for improving corpus annotation practices. With modest revisions to address the limitations noted above, this paper would be a strong fit for the conference.
