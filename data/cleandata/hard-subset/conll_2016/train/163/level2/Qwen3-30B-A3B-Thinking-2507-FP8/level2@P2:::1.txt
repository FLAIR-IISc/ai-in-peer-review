

Summary: This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, using the British National Corpus (BNC) and Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust information about PoS affiliation, enabling classification of PoS tags with high accuracy (F-score 0.979); (2) identifying "outlier" words that don't align with traditional PoS annotations, revealing inconsistencies in annotation processes; and (3) showing that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components (at least 100 out of 300) rather than concentrated in a few features.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a novel and valuable approach to analyzing PoS boundaries using distributional semantics, which offers concrete insights into linguistic inconsistencies that are difficult to detect through traditional linguistic analysis alone. The identification of specific annotation errors (e.g., adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs) is particularly compelling and directly useful for corpus linguists and NLP practitioners.

2. The methodology is sound and thoroughly documented, with careful attention to corpus preprocessing, model training, and evaluation. The authors' decision to weight training instances by word frequency to address class imbalance is particularly thoughtful and addresses a significant challenge in PoS classification.

3. The paper demonstrates the robustness of their approach through multiple evaluations (BNC test set, Universal Dependencies Treebank), showing consistent performance across different datasets. This strengthens the claim that their findings are not specific to a particular corpus or annotation scheme.

4. The analysis of vector components and their relationship to PoS classes provides valuable empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across the embedding space, which has theoretical implications for how we understand the relationship between semantics and syntax in distributional models.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper overstates its contribution by claiming to "redefine" PoS classes, when in fact it merely identifies inconsistencies in existing PoS annotations. The paper does not propose a new PoS system or provide evidence that the distributional patterns should replace traditional PoS boundaries. This overstatement weakens the significance of the contribution.

2. The comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers is insufficient. The authors acknowledge that their classifier's performance (F-score 0.84 on the test set) is inferior to modern taggers, but fail to adequately explain why this is the case or what the implications are for their main claim that distributional models contain robust PoS information. A more thorough comparison with contextual PoS taggers would strengthen the argument.

3. The paper's claim about information being "distributed among dozens of vector components" is not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. While they show that accuracy increases gradually with more features, they don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why this distribution is significant for PoS, nor do they demonstrate that this distribution is unique to PoS (as opposed to other linguistic features).

4. The paper misses an opportunity to compare their approach with existing work on using distributional models for PoS tagging. While they cite some relevant work (e.g., Tsuboi, 2014), they don't clearly position their contribution relative to prior methods, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach.

Questions to authors:
1. How do you reconcile the fact that your classifier achieves 97.9% accuracy on the training set but only 84% on the test set (using UD Treebank) with the claim that "information about PoS is distributed among dozens of vector components"? Does this performance gap suggest limitations in the distributional approach?

2. In the section on "Distributional vectors as part-of-speech predictors," you state that your classifier "knows absolutely nothing about a word's context in the current sentence." How might incorporating contextual information improve the classifier's performance, and what would this imply about the relationship between semantics and syntax?

3. The paper mentions that "distributional models trained in a nondeterministic and stochastic way on large amounts of word contexts learn knowledge about part of speech clusters." How do you account for the fact that this knowledge is learned without explicit linguistic supervision, and what does this say about the relationship between semantics and syntax?
