- Paper shows word embeddings contain robust PoS information, enabling high classification accuracy (F=0.979 on training).
- Identifies outlier words that conflict with traditional PoS annotations, exposing annotation inconsistencies.
- Demonstrates PoS information is distributed across many vector components (≥100 of 300), not localized to a few features.

- Novel approach using distributional semantics to analyze PoS boundaries, yielding concrete, corpus-useful error detections.
- Sound and well-documented methodology, including thoughtful frequency-weighting to address class imbalance.
- Robustness demonstrated via multiple evaluations (BNC and UD Treebank) with consistent performance across datasets.
- Empirical analysis supports that PoS signals are spread through embedding space, informing semantics–syntax debates.

- Overstates contribution by claiming to "redefine" PoS classes when it only highlights annotation inconsistencies without proposing a new PoS system.
- Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art contextual PoS taggers and inadequate explanation for lower test performance (F=0.84).
- Claim that information is "distributed among dozens of components" lacks strong theoretical justification and uniqueness to PoS.
- Fails to clearly position the work relative to prior distributional PoS studies, leaving novelty unclear.

- Why does training accuracy reach 97.9% while test accuracy on UD is only 84%, and does this gap limit claims about distributed PoS information?
- How would incorporating contextual sentence information affect classifier performance and the semantics–syntax relationship?
- How do nondeterministic, unsupervised distributional models learn PoS knowledge without explicit supervision, and what does this imply about semantics vs. syntax?
