- Summary: Tests whether word embeddings encode PoS information using BNC and UD, training classifiers to predict tags, analyzing misclassifications and how PoS is distributed across vector dimensions.

- Contribution 1: Shows embeddings robustly encode PoS (97.9% F1 on BNC) and can detect words whose distributional patterns conflict with manual PoS annotations.

- Contribution 2: Identifies systematic annotation inconsistencies in BNC (e.g., currency terms mislabeled as NOUN, adjectives tagged as ADVB), using distributional models to find linguistic outliers.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates PoS information is distributed across many vector components (at least ~100 of 300), not localized to a few dimensions.

- Strength 1: Novel repurposing of distributional models as diagnostic tools for PoS annotation quality, with concrete examples that can inform tagging guidelines.

- Strength 2: Methodologically rigorous—uses classifier errors for linguistic analysis, cross-validates on BNC and UD, and applies frequency-weighted training to handle class imbalance.

- Strength 3: Theoretical contribution showing graded, distributed PoS knowledge in embeddings, aligning with views of grammatical categories as continuous.

- Weakness 1: Limited comparison to SOTA PoS taggers—84% accuracy on unseen UD vs >97% SOTA—and insufficient explanation of the approach’s value despite lower accuracy.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of why specific misclassifications occur (e.g., rare vs. common proper nouns), leaving uncertainty about causes like corpus coverage or embedding training.

- Weakness 3: Underdeveloped discussion linking “soft” PoS boundaries to broader linguistic debates and existing literature on graded categories.

- Question 1: How would the method distinguish between numerals like "£70" (NUM) and nouns like "pound" in a single embedding space, and could this enable a more nuanced PoS schema?

- Question 2: Given accuracy plateaus around ~100 components and some highly specific dimensions (e.g., dim 94 for conjunction), why is PoS information distributed rather than concentrated—could training stochasticity explain this?
