

Summary: This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories, using the British National Corpus (BNC) and Universal Dependencies Treebank. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings, analyze misclassifications to uncover annotation inconsistencies, and explore how PoS information is distributed across vector dimensions.

Primary Contributions:
1. Demonstrating that word embeddings robustly encode PoS information (97.9% F1 on BNC), enabling detection of "outlier" words whose distributional patterns contradict manual PoS annotations.
2. Revealing systematic annotation inconsistencies in BNC (e.g., currency terms mislabeled as nouns, adjectives erroneously tagged as adverbs), which distributional models can identify as linguistic outliers.
3. Showing that PoS affiliation is distributed across numerous vector components (at least 100 in 300D models), not concentrated in a few features.

Strengths:
1. **Novel application for linguistic insight**: The paper effectively repurposes distributional models not just as semantic tools but as diagnostic instruments for PoS annotation quality. The discovery of specific annotation errors (e.g., "33%" as NOUN instead of NUM, "plain" tagged as ADV instead of ADJ) provides concrete evidence for revising tagging guidelines, which is highly valuable for corpus linguists.
2. **Rigorous methodology**: The use of classifier errors as a lens for linguistic analysis (rather than merely reporting accuracy) is methodologically sound. The cross-validation across BNC and UD Treebank datasets, along with frequency-weighted training to handle class imbalance, strengthens the findings’ reliability.
3. **Important theoretical contribution**: The demonstration that PoS knowledge is distributed across vector dimensions (not confined to a few features) challenges simplistic assumptions about embedding structure and aligns with the "graded" nature of grammatical categories, offering a data-driven perspective on linguistic continuity.

Weaknesses:
1. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art**: The classifier’s 84% accuracy on unseen UD data (vs. SOTA taggers >97%) is underemphasized. The authors note it “may not seem impressive,” but should explicitly discuss *why* this approach is valuable despite lower accuracy (e.g., zero-context dependency, applicability to low-resource languages), rather than implying it’s a direct tagger alternative.
2. **Insufficient analysis of error causality**: While misclassifications are cataloged (e.g., "proper nouns as nouns"), the paper doesn’t deeply explore *why* certain words (e.g., "Himalayas" vs. "John") are misclassified. Is it corpus coverage (rare words), semantic ambiguity, or a flaw in embedding training? This limits the actionable insights for annotation refinement.
3. **Underdeveloped discussion of distributional implications**: The claim that PoS boundaries are “soft” is compelling, but the paper doesn’t connect this to broader linguistic debates (e.g., Hanks’ work on "words as verbs" or Manning’s "graded categories"). Expanding this would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper identifies currency terms (e.g., "£70") as misclassified nouns. How would your method distinguish between "£70" (NUM) and "pound" (NOUN) in a single embedding space, given their contextual similarity? Could this inform a more nuanced PoS schema?
2. Figure 3 shows accuracy plateauing at ~100 components. Given that the "conjunction" feature (dim 94) is highly specific, why is PoS information distributed rather than concentrated in a few dimensions? Could this relate to the stochastic nature of embedding training?
