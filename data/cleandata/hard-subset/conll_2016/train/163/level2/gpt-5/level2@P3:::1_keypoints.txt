- Paper studies how distributional word embeddings encode PoS by training classifiers on BNC (lemma+PoS) and analyzing errors; also trains embeddings without PoS and a context-free PoS predictor from unambiguous lemmas, finding PoS information distributed across many dimensions.

- Contribution: empirical evidence that embeddings robustly encode PoS, with PoS signal distributed across many dimensions rather than localized.

- Contribution: methodology using classifier confusions to expose PoS boundary gradience and corpus annotation issues, with BNC case studies and UD checks.

- Contribution: simple context-free PoS predictor from a small unambiguous-lemma set, useful for preliminary tagging in low-resource settings.

- Strength: strong empirical signal — frequency-weighted logistic regression yields high F-scores (0.91 on BNC held-out, 0.99 on UD-present lemmas), outperforming simple baselines.

- Strength: linguistically meaningful error analysis surfaces interpretable boundaries and probable BNC inconsistencies that could inform tagset design and corpus cleanup.

- Strength: sensible handling of class imbalance via token-frequency reweighting, improving coverage of closed classes.

- Strength: approach complements context-dependent tagger analyses by revealing distinct lexical tendencies when starving models of context.

- Strength: feature analyses (ANOVA, accuracy-by-k) support claim that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (~100).

- Weakness: circularity risk because main embeddings are trained on lemma+PoS, so classification may reflect training setup rather than standard embedding properties; second experiment partially addresses this.

- Weakness: error-claim rigor is limited — needs manual audit, per-class metrics, normalized counts, and confidence estimates to substantiate annotation inconsistency claims.

- Weakness: UD evaluation omits 27% of types (10% tokens), risking upward bias; OOV distribution not characterized and subword/retrofitting alternatives not explored.

- Weakness: Stanford tagger comparison is methodologically mismatched because feeding isolated tokens to a context-dependent tagger unfairly deprives it of context.

- Weakness: feature-importance analysis reports training accuracy and uses model-specific component indices; held-out curves and stability across seeds are needed.

- Question: How do outlier findings change when replicating Section 3 analyses with embeddings trained on lemmas without PoS tags?

- Question: Can the authors provide per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices for lemma+tag and lemma-only predictors, showing which classes drive scores?

- Question: How sensitive are results to embedding hyperparameters, frequency weighting, and randomness (seeds)?

- Question: For the context-free predictor, how does performance vary with different thresholds for “unambiguous” lemmas and what is per-class coverage among the 1,564 lemmas?

- Question: Can the authors provide a small manual validation (counts and contexts) for claimed BNC inconsistencies and discuss guideline implications?

- Question: Can the exact BNC→Universal tag mapping tables be included as supplementary material for reproducibility in anonymized submission?

- Suggestion: Move the Stanford tagger comparison to an appendix, clarify its purpose, and add a fair context-aware comparison.

- Suggestion: Provide detailed per-class tables, frequency-normalized error rates, and a small manual audit to substantiate corpus-cleaning claims.

- Suggestion: Report held-out k-best feature curves and stability across random seeds to strengthen the distributed-PoS claim.

- Suggestion: Fix minor presentation issues (truncated tables, reference formatting) to improve readability.

- Suggestion: If possible, release code, trained vectors, and mapping resources to facilitate replication.
