Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on the BNC encode part-of-speech (PoS) information, training a multinomial classifier on word-type vectors to predict PoS and using misclassifications to probe “soft” category boundaries and annotation inconsistencies.
- Claimed contributions, in my reading:
  1) A practical audit methodology that surfaces boundary cases and systematic PoS annotation issues via distributional outliers, with concrete findings in BNC (e.g., ADJ/ADV confusions, NUM vs NOUN for years/currencies).
  2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized in a few.
  3) A contextless PoS predictor trained on unambiguous seed words achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD tokens, suggesting utility for low-resource bootstrapping.

Strengths
- Strong empirical signal: The classifier trained on BNC lemma-tag embeddings achieves 0.979 F-score (10-fold CV) and 0.91 on held-out types; the UD application reports 0.99 F-score on covered items. These results convincingly show PoS regularities in embeddings.
- Insightful error analysis: The outlier audit surfaces linguistically meaningful boundary phenomena (e.g., deverbal adjectives, noun modifiers) and compelling corpus issues, including a substantial ADJ/ADV tagging inconsistency (~600 cases with “plain/clear/sharp”), demonstrating practical usefulness for corpus QA.
- Methodological clarity: Simple models (skip-gram + logistic regression, frequency weighting) make the approach reproducible and the results interpretable, and the ANOVA-based feature study supports the “distributed” PoS signal claim (plateau ~k≈100).
- Potential applicability: The seed-based predictor trained on ∼1.5k unambiguous lemmas attaining 0.84 accuracy without context is promising for preliminary tagging in low-resource settings.

Weaknesses
- Possible circularity: Training embeddings on lemma_POS tokens and then predicting POS from those vectors risks conflating POS-split lexeme senses with learned contextual regularities; the UD “0.99” evaluation is unclear given the vocabulary mismatch (lemma vs lemma_POS).
- Baseline fairness: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens (no context), which is not its operating regime; conclusions comparing confusion structures may be confounded.
- Limited scope and robustness: Only English/BNC with a single embedding configuration (window=2); no ablations across corpora, hyperparameters, or embedding families (CBOW/GloVe/fastText), and no statistical significance or per-class breakdowns.
- Validation of flagged errors: While examples are persuasive, there is no systematic human validation or quantitative estimate of precision/recall for detected annotation errors.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was the UD “0.99” evaluation conducted when the embedding vocabulary was lemma_POS? Were UD lemmas concatenated with UD tags for lookup? If so, does that leak gold labels?
- Can you report per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices for all evaluations, and sensitivity to the frequency weighting scheme?
- How stable are the findings across embedding hyperparameters and alternative models (e.g., fastText with subword features)?
- Did you conduct any human adjudication to quantify the accuracy of the detected BNC annotation issues?
