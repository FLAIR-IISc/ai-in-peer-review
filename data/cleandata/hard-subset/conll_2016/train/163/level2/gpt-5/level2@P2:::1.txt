Summary and claimed contributions
This paper examines how distributional word embeddings interact with part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The authors (i) train a classifier on word vectors to predict PoS and use misclassifications to surface “outlier” words whose distributional behavior differs from their annotated PoS, arguing these reveal graded category membership and annotation inconsistencies; (ii) provide qualitative analyses of prominent confusions (e.g., PROPN↔NOUN, NOUN↔ADJ, VERB(participle)↔ADJ, NUM↔NOUN/PROPN), including a plausible detection of systematic tagging errors in BNC; (iii) show that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions; and (iv) present a simple, context-free PoS predictor using untagged embeddings and a small set of prototypical lemmas.

As I see it, the primary contribution is the diagnostic use of embedding-based PoS predictions to identify boundary cases and corpus annotation inconsistencies; the secondary contributions are the empirical characterization of how PoS signal is distributed in embeddings and the proof-of-concept context-free predictor.

Strengths
- Insightful diagnostic analysis: The “outlier” analysis is thoughtful and linguistically informative. Several confusions (participles behaving adjectivally; years/decades functioning nominally; currency tokens behaving like numerals) are convincing and illustrate how distributional information surfaces graded PoS membership and annotation decisions. The identification of likely systematic BNC errors (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as adverbs in “make it plain/clear...”) is particularly valuable for corpus curation.
- Cross-resource check: Applying the approach to UD English helps show some issues are corpus-specific (e.g., currency tokens segmented into NUM+SYM in UD), lending support to the claim that the method can diagnose annotation strategies, not just properties of a single corpus.
- Clear evidence that PoS signal is distributed: The ANOVA-based feature ablation suggests that robust PoS discrimination relies on many dimensions, countering the common misconception that a few axes suffice. This is a useful empirical observation for interpretability work.
- Simple, low-resource predictor: Training a context-free classifier with only ~1.5k “prototypical” lemmas yielding 0.84 accuracy, despite no sentence context or word-order information, is an interesting baseline for low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in cross-validation: In Section 3, the classifier is evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation on instances where each word vector is repeated proportional to frequency. This likely places identical vectors for the same word type in both training and test folds, inflating F=0.979. Proper CV should group by word type (no duplicate vectors across folds). The held-out test (F≈0.91) is more informative; please re-run CV with type-level splits.
- Using lemma+PoS tokens to study PoS: Training embeddings on tokens concatenated with gold PoS (love_VERB, love_NOUN) makes the representational space already partitioned by PoS-labeled types. While the goal is to analyze alignment between distributional behavior and tags, this setup may exaggerate PoS separability and complicate generalization. The cleaner analysis is Section 5 (embeddings without PoS in the vocabulary); it would strengthen the paper to repeat the outlier analysis with that setup and compare.
- Ambiguity in UD evaluation (Section 3): It is unclear how UD lemmas were embedded when the BNC model’s vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens. Did you use UD gold tags to retrieve lemma+PoS vectors? If so, the evaluation partially conditions on gold tags to get vectors, which undermines the claim of cross-corpus generalization. Please clarify and, if needed, revise.
- Missing baselines for the context-free predictor: A strong baseline for Section 5 is a lexicon assigning each lemma its majority PoS from the training portion (and “unknown” otherwise), or a character/morphology baseline. Without these, it’s hard to assess the 0.84 accuracy. Given many lemmas are highly skewed to a dominant tag, a majority-lemma baseline might be competitive.
- Stanford tagger comparison is not well controlled: Running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens (no sentence context) is not the intended usage and predictably harms performance. This makes the comparison less informative. A fairer comparison would either use sentence context or compare against a type-level tagger that uses aggregated contexts, or simply present the distributional confusion matrix without the Stanford side-by-side.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you ensure that all instances of a given word type (same vector) are in the same fold? If not, can you report type-level CV results?
- UD application in Section 3: How were vectors obtained for UD lemmas if the BNC embedding vocabulary is lemma+PoS? Were UD gold tags used to select vectors? Please detail this pipeline.
- Frequency effects: Can you stratify error rates by frequency to quantify how much of the PROPN→NOUN confusion is due to sparse contexts versus genuine distributional similarity?
- Systematic BNC errors: For the “plain/clear/sharp” issue, can you quantify precision/recall of your detector and provide a broader list beyond the three adjectives? Was any manual validation performed?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strengthen the core claim by re-running the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on untagged lemmas (as in Section 5). If similar boundary cases and annotation issues resurface, the argument becomes much stronger and avoids the lemma+PoS tokenization concern.
- Add baselines: (i) lemma-majority PoS from training; (ii) a nearest-prototype cosine classifier; (iii) simple orthographic/morphological features. Report macro- and micro-averaged scores.
- Report robustness: Include variance over multiple random seeds for embedding training; explore window sizes (smaller windows typically encode more syntactic information) and subsampling. These choices likely affect PoS signal.
- Evaluate feature importance on held-out data rather than training accuracy to avoid overfitting when increasing k. Consider L1-regularized models to study sparsity in feature usage.
- Clarify reproducibility: Provide enough details (or a link, post-acceptance) to mappings, preprocessing, and code. BNC license constraints are understood, but scripts and mapping tables are important.
- Presentation: Fix numerous reference formatting issues and typos; ensure tables/figures are complete and legible.

Overall assessment
The paper offers a useful and largely convincing qualitative contribution: embedding-based PoS prediction can surface linguistically meaningful boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies. However, key methodological issues (type-level CV leakage; reliance on lemma+PoS tokens; unclear UD evaluation) need to be addressed. With corrected evaluation, stronger baselines, and a replication of the outlier analysis using untagged embeddings, this would be a solid and impactful contribution.
