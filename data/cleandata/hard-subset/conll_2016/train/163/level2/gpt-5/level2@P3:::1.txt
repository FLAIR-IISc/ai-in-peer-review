Summary and contributions
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings interact with part-of-speech (PoS) categories. Using the BNC (lemmatized and mapped to Universal POS), a multinomial logistic regression classifier is trained to predict PoS from word vectors; errors are then analyzed to identify boundary cases and potential annotation inconsistencies. A second experiment trains embeddings without PoS tags and learns a context-free PoS predictor from a small set of unambiguous UD lemmas. An analysis of embedding dimensions suggests PoS information is distributed across many components.

Perceived main contributions:
1) Empirical evidence that distributional embeddings robustly encode PoS information, with analysis showing this information is distributed across many dimensions rather than localized.
2) A methodology for using classifier confusions as a lens on PoS boundary gradience and corpus annotation issues, with concrete case studies on BNC and cross-corpus checks on UD.
3) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained from a small set of unambiguous lemmas, suggesting a practical route for preliminary tagging in low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Clear empirical signal that embeddings capture PoS: The frequency-weighted logistic regression achieves high F-scores on BNC-held-out vocabulary (0.91) and near-perfect performance when applied to UD lemmas present in the model (0.99). Majority and single-dimension baselines are strong negatives, underscoring that signal is not trivial or confined to one feature.
- Insightful error analysis with linguistically meaningful findings: The paper surfaces interpretable boundary cases (e.g., adjectives vs nouns in attributive use; passive participles; numerals vs years/decades; adverbs like “ultra”/“kinda”) and diagnoses likely BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs). These examples illustrate how distributional signals can inform tagset design and corpus cleanup.
- Sensible handling of class imbalance: Reweighting by token frequency mitigates the type-level skew between closed and open classes and leads to reasonable coverage of closed classes, which is often overlooked.
- Complementarity to standard tagger-based analyses: The confusion patterns obtained by starving a standard tagger of context differ from the distributional classifier’s confusions, suggesting the proposed approach reveals distinct, useful information about lexical tendencies independent of sentence context.
- Distributed nature of PoS information empirically supported: The ANOVA-based feature ranking and accuracy-by-k study suggest at least ~100 dimensions contribute meaningfully, supporting claims about distributed encoding rather than axis-aligned “PoS features”.

Weaknesses
- Potential circularity in the primary setup: The main embedding model is trained on tokens concatenated with PoS tags (lemma+tag), making PoS-specific contexts a built-in separation. Classifying from these vectors back to PoS partially measures how well a model that distinguishes PoS at training time preserves that distinction, rather than how well standard embeddings encode PoS. The second experiment (embeddings without tags) addresses this, but the central results and outlier analysis lean heavily on the lemma+tag setup.
- Limited quantitative rigor on error claims: While many cases are persuasive, several conclusions about annotation inconsistencies would benefit from a small-scale manual audit and per-class precision/recall/confusion tables. Some findings could be reinforced with counts normalized by class and frequency, confidence estimates, and inter-annotator verification of alleged errors.
- OOV and selection biases in UD evaluation: The UD evaluation omits 27% of types (10% tokens), potentially biasing performance upward. The distribution of omitted items is not characterized; an analysis of OOVs’ PoS/types and an evaluation with subword or retrofitting would strengthen generalization claims.
- Comparison to the Stanford tagger is methodologically mismatched: Feeding isolated tokens to a context-dependent tagger is not a fair use case. The resulting confusion matrix differences may be attributed to deprivation of context rather than inherent model properties, limiting the strength of that comparison.
- Feature-importance analysis uses training accuracy: The k-best feature curve is reported on training data; a held-out curve would better support claims about how many dimensions are necessary. Additionally, component indices are model-specific and unstable across runs; discussing stability across random seeds would improve robustness.

Questions to authors
- How do the main “outlier” findings change when using embeddings trained on lemmas without PoS tags (i.e., the non-concatenated setting)? An ablation that replicates the Section 3 analysis in that setting would address the circularity concern.
- What are per-class precision/recall and confusion matrices for both the lemma+tag and lemma-only predictor settings? Which classes drive the overall scores?
- How sensitive are results to embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, dimension, negative samples) and to the frequency weighting scheme? Any evidence that findings are robust across seeds?
- For the context-free predictor, how does performance vary with different thresholds for “unambiguous” lemmas (e.g., 0.6/0.7 dominance)? What is the per-class coverage among the 1,564 lemmas?
- Can a small manual validation be provided for the claimed BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as adverbs), with counts and contexts, and a discussion of guideline implications?
- The BNC→Universal tag mapping is stated to be released online. For reproducibility in the anonymized submission, can the exact mapping tables be included as supplementary material?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider moving the Stanford tagger comparison to an appendix with a clearer statement of its purpose (to contrast token-level tendencies) and adding a fair-context comparison for completeness.
- Provide detailed tables (per-class metrics, coverage, and frequency-normalized error rates) and a small manual audit to substantiate corpus-cleaning claims.
- Report test-set accuracy for the k-best feature analysis and discuss stability across random seeds to support the “distributed” claim more robustly.
- Minor presentation issues: some tables appear truncated; references contain formatting glitches. Cleaning these will help readability.
- If feasible, release code, trained vectors, and mapping resources to facilitate replication.
