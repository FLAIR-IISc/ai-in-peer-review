Summary and contributions
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on BNC interact with part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The authors (i) train simple classifiers on embeddings to predict PoS and use misclassifications to surface boundary phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies, (ii) show that PoS-relevant information is broadly distributed across many embedding dimensions, and (iii) demonstrate a preliminary PoS “lexicon-level” predictor using lemma-only embeddings and a small set of prototypical words.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A systematic error-analysis framework that leverages a PoS-from-embeddings classifier to identify graded category membership and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adjectives vs adverbs; numerals vs nouns; proper vs common nouns).
2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions, not concentrated in a few, with a simple feature-ranking study (ANOVA F-values) indicating a plateau around ~100 dimensions.
3) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained on untagged embeddings and a small set of unambiguous prototypes, achieving 0.84 token-level accuracy on UD.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven analysis linking embeddings to graded PoS behavior: The paper goes beyond reporting overall accuracy to analyze specific misclassification patterns that align with long-standing linguistic observations (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally; noun–adjective interchange in attributive positions) and with practical annotation issues (e.g., numerals vs years and currency forms). This makes the work useful for both linguistic insight and corpus curation.
- Solid empirical support that embeddings encode PoS cues: The lemma+PoS embedding model plus a multinomial logistic regression reaches 0.979 weighted F on 10-fold CV (10k frequent words) and 0.91 F on a held-out set (17k types), and performs strongly on UD lemmas (F=0.99 for types present in the model). The lemma-only embedding predictor shows 0.84 accuracy without sentence context, underscoring that much PoS signal is captured distributionally.
- Practical utility for corpus diagnostics: The identification of likely systematic BNC tagging issues (e.g., clear/plain/sharp tagged as adverbs in “make it clear that...”) is concrete and actionable. The analysis of currency amounts and time-of-day tokens highlights tokenization and tagging conventions that can be harmonized with UD practices.
- Evidence for distributed encoding of PoS across dimensions: The feature ablation study indicates that performance increases smoothly with the number of top-ranked dimensions, with no single or small set of dimensions sufficient. This is a valuable, specific empirical finding for interpretability discussions.
- Reproducibility-oriented details: The paper reports core hyperparameters (Skip-gram, 300d, window=2, negatives=10, epochs=5), type selection criteria, and the frequency-weighting approach for class balancing. This enhances clarity and facilitates follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Potential circularity in the main outlier experiment: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens may inflate PoS signal because every context token is also annotated with PoS. The classifier then detects deviations with respect to an annotation-enriched distribution, not raw lexical distributions. While this design is appropriate for probing annotation consistency, it weakens claims about what generic embeddings (trained without PoS tags) encode. The authors partly address this with the lemma-only experiment, but the core error analysis relies on the lemma+PoS model.
- Limited validation of discovered “annotation errors”: Several insightful cases are proposed, but most are supported by anecdotal examples and small counts. The claim of ~600 BNC instances of adjectives tagged as adverbs is strong; however, a systematic manual evaluation (sampled instances, inter-annotator agreement, correction rate) would substantiate the corpus-diagnostic contribution.
- Comparison to a standard tagger is methodologically weak: Running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens is not representative of its intended use (sentence context is essential). The resulting confusion matrix is therefore not a fair baseline for insight extraction. A stronger comparison would be (i) a lexicon-level classifier using simple features (e.g., character n-grams, morphological features, Brown clusters) trained on the same supervision, or (ii) an embedding-based classifier trained on lemma-only vectors vs the lemma+PoS setting.
- Scope and generality claims: The usefulness for low-resource languages is speculative without evidence on other typologically diverse languages or on smaller corpora. Similarly, hyperparameter sensitivity (window size, vector size, subword modeling) is not explored; a window of 2 is known to emphasize syntactic signal, which may be central to the observed effects.
- Ambiguity and token-level performance: The lemma-based predictor trained on “unambiguous/dominant” UD lemmas is evaluated at token level, but many tokens are ambiguous and OOV tokens are dropped (860/21759). A breakdown by ambiguous vs unambiguous tokens and by frequency/OOV would clarify where the 0.84 accuracy comes from and how it would translate to a practical tagger.

Questions to authors
- How much do the key findings persist if embeddings are trained on lemma-only tokens for the outlier analysis (i.e., re-run Section 4 with lemma-only embeddings)? Do you recover similar boundary phenomena and corpus inconsistencies?
- Can you quantify the “annotation error” rate with a small manual study (e.g., sample 100 predicted errors across categories; report correction proportion and annotator agreement)?
- How sensitive are results to window size and model type (CBOW vs Skip-gram, subword models)? Does a larger window reduce PoS separability?
- For the distributed-dimension analysis, are the ranked dimensions stable across random seeds and retrainings? Is component “94” for conjunctions consistent?
- Could you compare against a lexicon-level baseline using simple features (orthographic, affixes, character n-grams) to disentangle how much PoS signal embeddings add beyond form-based cues?

Additional comments
- Please clarify capitalization handling for proper nouns (BNC lemma standardization) and whether orthographic cues leak into the embeddings.
- It would be helpful to release the mapping, models, and the list of flagged outliers with contexts (upon de-anonymization), to support adoption for corpus diagnostics.
- The observation that proper nouns are distributionally distinct from common nouns (cosine ~0.67) is interesting; a deeper analysis (e.g., typical contexts) would strengthen the claim.
- Consider an ablation that removes numerals and symbols during training to test whether “year-as-noun” effects persist, disentangling tokenization conventions from distributional behavior.
