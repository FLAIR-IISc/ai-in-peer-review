## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The core idea is that distributional semantics can reveal nuances and inconsistencies in traditional PoS annotation, and potentially support a more “soft” or graded view of PoS affiliation. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings and analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic patterns and potential annotation errors.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that word embeddings *do* encode significant information about PoS, even without explicit PoS information during training. The authors convincingly show this through a relatively high-performing PoS classifier and, more importantly, through a detailed error analysis that uncovers interesting linguistic phenomena – inconsistencies in BNC annotation (e.g., adverb/adjective confusion) and words straddling PoS boundaries (e.g., passive participles). A secondary contribution is the finding that PoS information is distributed across *many* vector components, challenging the notion of a few key features defining each PoS. Finally, the comparison with a standard PoS tagger (Stanford Tagger) highlights the unique insights gained from the distributional approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Relevance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in NLP – the nature of PoS categories – and connects it to the powerful paradigm of distributional semantics.
*   **Strong Empirical Support:** The authors provide robust experimental results, including classifier performance and detailed error analysis. The use of both the BNC and the Universal Dependencies Treebank adds credibility.
*   **Insightful Error Analysis:** The analysis of misclassified words is particularly valuable, revealing potential annotation errors and highlighting the inherent fuzziness of PoS boundaries.
*   **Demonstrated Practicality:** The successful training of a PoS predictor solely from distributional vectors suggests potential applications in low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The idea that distributional semantics captures syntactic information is not entirely new. The paper would benefit from more explicitly positioning itself within the existing literature and highlighting *how* its approach differs or extends previous work.
*   **Classifier Simplicity:** While the logistic regression classifier is sufficient for demonstrating the core idea, exploring more sophisticated models might yield better performance and potentially reveal more nuanced patterns.
*   **Lack of Qualitative Examples:** While the error analysis is detailed, including more concrete examples of misclassified words and their contexts would make the findings more accessible and impactful.
*   **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into how different embedding parameters (window size, vector dimensionality) affect the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific types of annotation inconsistencies identified in the BNC, and provide more concrete examples beyond those mentioned in the paper?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding parameters (e.g., vector size, window size)? Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the impact of these parameters?
3.  You mention the potential for applying this approach to low-resource languages. Could you discuss the challenges and limitations of doing so, particularly regarding the need for a seed vocabulary?



Overall, this is a solid paper with valuable insights. While the core idea isn’t entirely novel, the thorough experimental evaluation and insightful error analysis make a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
