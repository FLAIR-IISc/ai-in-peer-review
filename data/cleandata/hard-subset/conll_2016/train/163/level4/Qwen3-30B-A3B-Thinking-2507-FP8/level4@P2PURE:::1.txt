

The paper aims to demonstrate that distributional information in word vector models encodes part-of-speech (POS) label information. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and subsequently employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier for predicting word POS tags. Evaluations are conducted on the same corpus (via cross-validation) as well as on additional corpora. The results are clearly presented, thoroughly discussed, and extensively analyzed.

The paper is well-written and clear. The primary limitation is that it does not introduce any novel contributions to NLP or machine learning. It describes a series of straightforward experiments without incorporating new NLP or ML concepts or methodologies. While the results are empirically interesting and provide grounding for the notion of POS, the work lacks methodological or theoretical innovation. Consequently, it is certainly worthy of publication in a (quantitative/empirical) linguistic venue.

Additionally, the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings requires more extensive citation (e.g., Lin et al., 2015; Ling et al., 2015 [EMNLP]; Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg, 2016).
