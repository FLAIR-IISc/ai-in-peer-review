

Summary: This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings contain information about part-of-speech (POS) categories. The authors create a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal POS tags, train word embeddings on this corpus, and then use these embeddings to train a logistic regression classifier for POS prediction. The main contributions of this work are: (1) demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust information about POS categories, not just semantic content; (2) revealing hidden inconsistencies in POS annotation through analysis of misclassified words; and (3) showing that POS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in a few specific features.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear empirical demonstration that distributional models contain significant POS information, with a classifier achieving 97.9% F-score on the BNC test set. This robust result supports the notion that POS categories are not categorical but exist along a continuum, with words often exhibiting "soft" or graded affiliations to different parts of speech.

2. The analysis of misclassifications is insightful and reveals meaningful linguistic phenomena. The authors identify cases where BNC annotations contain systematic errors (e.g., adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs), as well as cases where words straddle POS boundaries (e.g., proper nouns that behave like common nouns, or participles that function as adjectives). This analysis provides valuable empirical grounding for linguistic discussions about POS boundaries.

3. The paper demonstrates that POS information is distributed across multiple vector components rather than concentrated in a few specific features. The authors show that while some components (like component 94 for conjunctions) are highly indicative of specific POS classes, overall POS information requires many vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), which aligns with theoretical expectations about embedding representations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks novelty in terms of NLP or machine learning methodology. The authors employ standard distributional modeling and classification techniques (continuous skipgram embeddings with logistic regression) without introducing any new algorithms, architectures, or methodological innovations. The approach is straightforward and replicable, but this does not constitute a significant contribution to the field.

2. The literature review of existing work on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings is insufficient. Key recent works such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016) should be cited and discussed, as they have already explored similar applications of distributional models for POS tasks. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with these prior studies.

3. The paper could better articulate the significance of its findings beyond linguistic insights. While the empirical evidence supporting "soft" POS categories is valuable for linguistics, the paper does not sufficiently address how these findings might impact NLP applications or whether the proposed approach offers practical advantages over existing POS taggers.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would you compare your approach to existing POS induction methods that use word embeddings (e.g., Lin et al., 2015; Ling et al., 2015)? What specific advantages does your distributional classifier offer over these methods?

2. The paper mentions that the classifier achieves 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank without contextual information. How does this compare to the performance of standard neural POS taggers on the same dataset? What would be the practical implications of using your approach for POS tagging in resource-poor languages?

3. The authors note that POS information is distributed across at least 100 vector dimensions. What are the implications of this finding for the design of future word embedding models and for the interpretation of embedding space geometry?
