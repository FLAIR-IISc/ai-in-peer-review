

General comments:  
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, drawing interesting (though largely predictable) conclusions about PoS tag consistency and the clear alignment between word vector representations and PoS categories. The detailed error analysis (examining classification outliers) constitutes a strong contribution.  

However, the paper omits a critical perspective: the original purpose of PoS-tagged corpora like the BNC. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories (e.g., under semantic prototype theory, Croft 1991), these corpora were created and tagged to support downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion could thus be reframed as assessing whether distributional vector distinctions offer greater utility for parsing than the original tags (or UPOS).  

Additionally, the paper lacks key related work on distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating recent non-DNN studies (e.g., Blunsom and Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012). In this context, the results in Section 5 are barely novel, as systems with stricter external knowledge constraints achieve comparable performance.  

Specific issues:  
The abstract claims that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is not novel for English: every distributional PoS induction system from the past 15 years reporting "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" metrics demonstrates this.  

The assertion in lines 79–80 ("relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic") is incorrect. Mikolov et al. (2013b) and Collobert et al. (2011) show significant syntactic information in these vectors, and the paper itself reverts this claim in lines 107–108.  

Why transition to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are inherently more informative.  

Footnote 3 is unclear: were these failed attempts conducted by the authors or cited works? By what criteria did they fail? What about Brown cluster vectors, which align nearly perfectly with UPOS tags?  

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) lacks novelty; the prevalence of "the" (the most frequent function word) likely explains this distinction.  

While the analysis of frequent word/tag pairs is practical, examining the tail (low-frequency words) would be valuable—both for vector behavior and classifier error patterns. This could inspire alternatives to pure distributional/morphological features for better generalization to low-frequency words.  

Minor issues:  
Replace all sentential citations with \newcite{} (e.g., replace "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" with "\newcite{Mikolov2013b} showed").
