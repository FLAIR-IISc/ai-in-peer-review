- POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (at least ~100 of 300), not concentrated in a few features.  
- Distributional semantic models can identify inconsistencies in POS annotations and reveal graded/soft POS boundaries.  
- Classifier misclassifications provide a methodology to detect linguistic outliers and systematic annotation errors.  
- Methodological contribution: using distributional models to uncover corpus annotation errors (e.g., adjectives mislabeled as adverbs).  
- Thorough error analysis uncovers distributional overlaps between categories (proper vs common nouns, verbs vs adjectives), supporting graded POS affiliation.  
- Robust cross-corpus performance (0.91 F on unseen BNC, 0.99 F on UD) indicates generalizability of observed POS patterns.  
- Analysis of embeddings offers a nuanced view of how POS information is encoded across vector dimensions.  
- Insufficient engagement with prior POS induction/tagging literature and inadequate positioning relative to related work.  
- Unclear practical applications: the paper does not explain how insights could be integrated to improve POS taggers or NLP pipelines.  
- Claim that POS info is distributed among dozens of components is somewhat overstated; the largest performance jump occurs between k=2 and k=3.  
- Limitations not fully addressed, e.g., 27% of word types omitted from UD experiments due to absence in the distributional model.  
- Question: How could this approach be integrated into corpus annotation pipelines to improve POS tagging quality?  
- Question: Which embedding dimensions are most informative for particular POS classes, and do they map to known linguistic features?  
- Question: How can the omission of many word types (27%) be mitigated, especially for resource-poor languages?  
- Question: How does the model distinguish numerals like "1804" in different contexts, and what are the implications for POS tagging conventions?
