

Summary: This paper investigates whether word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) contain information about part-of-speech (POS) categories. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags using distributional word vectors, then analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic patterns. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that POS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features, (2) revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC through outlier analysis, and (3) providing empirical evidence for the "soft" nature of POS boundaries.

Strengths: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence for the distributed nature of POS information in word embeddings, showing that at least 100 vector components contribute to POS classification rather than just one or two features. The analysis of misclassified words effectively identifies systematic annotation errors in the BNC, such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs and currency amounts misclassified as nouns, which demonstrates practical utility for corpus annotation quality control. The approach successfully detects linguistic boundary cases (e.g., passive participles that behave like adjectives) that align with established linguistic intuition about flexible POS boundaries.

Weaknesses: The paper does not introduce any novel NLP or machine learning methods, merely applying standard distributional semantic models to POS prediction in a straightforward manner without technical innovation. The results, while linguistically interesting, do not represent a significant advancement in NLP methodology or performance compared to existing POS taggers. The literature review fails to cite key works on POS induction using distributional models, including seminal papers by Ling et al. (2015), Plank et al. (2016), and Tsuboi (2014), which undermines the perceived novelty of the work.

Questions to Authors: How do the authors reconcile their finding that POS information is distributed across multiple vector components with the observation that specific components (like component 94 for conjunctions) show strong POS associations? What specific linguistic insights would the authors recommend for corpus annotators to address the annotation inconsistencies they identified?
