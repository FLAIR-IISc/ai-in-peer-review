

This paper investigates whether distributional word vector models encode part-of-speech (POS) information. The authors use a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and subsequently employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic regression classifier for POS prediction. Evaluations are conducted on both the same corpus (via cross-validation) and additional external corpora, with results presented clearly and analyzed thoroughly.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. However, its primary limitation is the lack of substantive novelty in natural language processing (NLP) or machine learning (ML). The work describes a series of straightforward experiments without introducing new NLP techniques, ML methods, or theoretical insights. While the results are empirically interesting—providing validation for the conceptual relationship between distributional semantics and POS—they do not advance the field methodologically or conceptually. This contribution alone makes the paper suitable for publication in a quantitative or empirical linguistics venue, rather than an NLP conference.

Additionally, the related work section requires significant expansion. The paper should more comprehensively cite prior research on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings, including key studies such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015) [EMNLP], and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016). Omitting these references weakens the paper’s contextualization within existing literature.
