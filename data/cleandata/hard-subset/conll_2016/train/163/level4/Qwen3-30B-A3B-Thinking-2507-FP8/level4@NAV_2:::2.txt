

Summary: The paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors contain meaningful information about PoS affiliation. The primary contributions are: (1) the discovery of linguistic inconsistencies in PoS annotation through classifier error analysis, (2) the identification of words exhibiting "soft" or graded PoS affiliations, and (3) the demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features.

Strengths: 
1. The detailed analysis of classifier outliers reveals genuine inconsistencies in PoS annotation that are not immediately apparent from standard corpus analysis, providing valuable insights into the fluid nature of part-of-speech boundaries.
2. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model) rather than being concentrated in specific features represents a significant technical contribution to understanding word embedding representations.
3. The robustness of their classifier (F-score 0.91 on unseen data, 0.99 on Universal Dependencies Treebank) demonstrates the practical utility of distributional models for PoS-related tasks, especially for resource-poor language settings where manual tagging is difficult.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to address the fundamental purpose of PoS tagging in corpora like BNC, which was primarily to facilitate NLP tasks such as parsing, rather than linguistic exploration. The discussion should have framed the results in terms of whether distributional vectors' PoS distinctions are more beneficial for parsing than the original tags.
2. The claim that "distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation" (abstract) is not novel, as this has been demonstrated in numerous distributional PoS induction systems over the past 15 years through cluster purity metrics.
3. The paper omits critical related work on distributional PoS induction, including Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom and Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012), making the results in Section 5 appear less novel than they are.

Questions to Authors:
1. Why was the Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset (UPOS) used instead of the original BNC tagset, which contains more fine-grained distinctions that would be more linguistically interesting?
2. Can you clarify the purpose and results of the failed "one-feature baseline classifier" mentioned in footnote 3, and why Brown cluster vectors (which align well with UPOS) were not considered?
3. How would the results change if the analysis included low-frequency words in the tail of the distribution, which might reveal different patterns of PoS behavior?
