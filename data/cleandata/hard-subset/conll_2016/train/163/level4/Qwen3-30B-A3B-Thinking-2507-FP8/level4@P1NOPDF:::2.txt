

General Comments:  
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, drawing straightforward yet interesting conclusions about the consistency of PoS tagging and the clear correspondence between word vector representations and PoS categories. The detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a notable strength.  

However, the paper overlooks a critical context: the original purpose of tagging corpora like the British National Corpus (BNC). These resources were not created for linguistic exploration under semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991) but to support downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion should therefore be reframed to assess whether distributional vectors offer parsing benefits compared to traditional PoS tags or Universal PoS (UPOS).  

Additionally, the paper lacks essential related work. It should cite Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) as a foundational review and incorporate recent non-DNN approaches (e.g., Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012). In this context, the results in Section 5 offer little novelty, as systems with stricter external knowledge constraints already achieve comparable performance.  

Specific Issues:  
The abstract claims that "distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation." This is not a new finding for English—every distributional PoS induction system from the past 15 years reporting "many-to-one" mappings or cluster purity scores demonstrates this.  

The assertion in lines 79–80 that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic" mischaracterizes the literature. Mikolov et al. (2013b) and Collobert et al. (2011) established significant syntactic information in these vectors, and the paper itself revises this claim in Section 2 (lines 107–108).  

The shift to UPOS lacks justification. The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more linguistically meaningful than UPOS.  

Footnote 3 is unclear: Were the failed attempts conducted by the authors or others? What criteria defined failure? Brown cluster vectors, for instance, align nearly perfectly with UPOS tags.  

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) seems unsurprising, as high-frequency function words like "the" largely explain this difference.  

While analyzing frequent word/tag pairs has practical value, extending the analysis to rare words (both in vector space and error patterns) would strengthen the work. This could explore alternatives to purely distributional/morphological features to improve PoS generalization for low-frequency items.
