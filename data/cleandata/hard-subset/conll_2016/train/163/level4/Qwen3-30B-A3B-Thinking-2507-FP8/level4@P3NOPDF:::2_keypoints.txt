- Strong error analysis of classification outliers, but overall work has significant conceptual gaps and lacks novelty.  
- Fails to justify the purpose of POS tagging; should evaluate whether distributional vectors give practical advantages for parsing/NLP, not just show they contain POS information.  
- Omits key related work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012), making results appear less novel than claimed.  
- Claims in Section 5 are not new—prior non-DNN systems with limited external knowledge achieved comparable POS induction performance.  
- Overstated and inconsistent claims: saying vectors are “mostly semantic” is incorrect and contradicted by prior work and the paper’s own later statements.  
- Unjustified shift to UPOS: discards the linguistically richer original fine-grained tagset (e.g., BNC’s 150+ tags) without rationale.  
- Footnote about “failed attempts” is ambiguous—unclear if these were the authors’ experiments and what failure criteria were used.  
- The claim that proper nouns differ from common nouns is trivial and largely explained by function-word co-occurrence, adding little insight.  
- Neglects the low-frequency tail: the paper should analyze rare words and explore morphological or hybrid methods to improve generalization.  
- Minor issues to fix: inconsistent citation formatting, grammatical errors, and ambiguous phrasing.  
- Overall recommendation: reframe motivation around NLP utility (e.g., parsing), integrate foundational literature, retract overstated semantic claims, and investigate low-frequency behavior to make the contribution meaningful.
