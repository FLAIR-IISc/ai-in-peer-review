- Strength: Detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a notable contribution.  
- Major contextual omission: Fails to acknowledge that PoS-tagged corpora (e.g., BNC) were created for downstream NLP (parsing) rather than linguistic theory.  
- Reframing needed: Core question should be whether distributional vectors improve parsing/NLP utility compared to traditional PoS/UPOS tags.  
- Literature gap: Does not cite or engage foundational work, especially Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom & Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012).  
- Novelty concern: The finding that embeddings encode PoS information is well-established and not novel for English.  
- Specific claim incorrect: Assertion that vector relations are “mostly semantic” contradicts prior work (Mikolov et al. 2013b, Collobert et al. 2011) and the paper’s own statements.  
- Method question: Rationale for using UPOS instead of the original fine-grained tagset is unclear and likely limits linguistic insight.  
- Ambiguity: Footnote about “failed attempts” lacks detail—who attempted them and what were the failure criteria?  
- Missing comparison: Omission of Brown cluster vectors, which align well with UPOS, is a noticeable gap.  
- Trivial observation: Claim that proper nouns differ from common nouns is expected and largely explained by high-frequency function words like “the.”  
- Scope limitation: Paper focuses on frequent word/tag pairs; it should analyze rare words (the tail) and error patterns for low-frequency items.  
- Suggestion: Investigate whether adding non-distributional/morphological features improves generalization for rare words.  
- Minor fixes: Replace sentential citations with \newcite format and correct author-year reference styles (e.g., Mikolov et al. (2013b)).  
- Language clarity: Rephrase ambiguous terms (e.g., use “strong alignment” instead of “clear connection”).  
- Overall assessment: Without reframing toward parsing utility, engaging prior work, and addressing UPOS and low-frequency analyses, the paper offers incremental rather than meaningful contribution.
