

Review:  
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, drawing conclusions about PoS consistency and the alignment between vector representations and PoS categories. The detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a notable strength.  

However, the paper overlooks a critical contextual point: the original purpose of PoS-tagged corpora like the BNC. These resources were not created for linguistic theory exploration (e.g., semantic prototype-based morphosyntactic categories, as in Croft 1991), but to support downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The core contribution could be reframed around whether distributional vector distinctions offer greater utility for parsing than traditional PoS or UPOS tags—a perspective absent here.  

The work also lacks engagement with key related literature. The review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) should be cited as foundational, alongside non-DNN advances like Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Given this body of work, the results in Section 5 lack sufficient novelty; existing systems with stricter external knowledge constraints achieve comparable performance.  

**Specific Issues:**  
- The abstract claims "distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation." This is not novel for English: every distributional PoS induction system from the past 15 years reporting "many-to-one" or cluster purity metrics demonstrates this.  
- The assertion (lines 79–80) that "relations between vectors are mostly semantic" is incorrect. Mikolov et al. (2013b) and Collobert et al. (2011) established significant syntactic information in word vectors, and the paper itself reverses this claim in Section 2 (lines 107–108).  
- The rationale for adopting UPOS over the original fine-grained tagset is unclear. The finer distinctions in the original tagset would likely yield more linguistically insightful analysis.  
- Footnote 3 is ambiguous: Were the "failed attempts" conducted by the authors or others? On what criteria did they fail? Additionally, Brown cluster vectors align nearly perfectly with UPOS tags—why omit this comparison?  
- The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) is trivial; the high frequency of "the" (a function word) largely explains this difference.  
- While analyzing frequent word/tag pairs is practical, examining low-frequency items (the "tail") would be valuable. How do vectors behave for rare words? What error patterns emerge, and could non-distributional/morphological features improve generalization?  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Replace all sentential citations with `\newcite` (e.g., "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" → "Mikolov et al. \newcite{mikolov2013b} showed").  
- Correct technical references: "Mikolov or Collobert" → "Mikolov et al. (2013b) or Collobert et al. (2011)."  
- Clarify ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "clear connection" → "strong alignment").  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper’s core insight—that word embeddings encode PoS information—is well-established and not sufficiently novel. The absence of critical context (corpora’s NLP purpose) and related work undermines its contribution. The error analysis is a redeeming feature, but the work does not advance the field beyond existing distributional PoS induction literature. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1) Reframe the discussion around utility for parsing/NLP tasks;  
2) Engage deeply with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and subsequent non-DNN work;  
3) Address the UPOS rationale and low-frequency analysis gaps.  
Without these revisions, the paper offers incremental progress rather than a meaningful contribution.
