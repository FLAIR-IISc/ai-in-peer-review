

**Summary**  
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (POS) categories. The authors preprocess the BNC by replacing words with lemmas and annotating them with Universal POS tags. They train word embeddings on this corpus, then use the resulting vectors to train a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags. The classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979) on the BNC and 0.99 on the Universal Dependencies Treebank. The authors analyze misclassified words to identify "outliers" that reveal potential inconsistencies in POS annotation or suggest "soft" POS boundaries.  

**Main Contributions**  
1. Demonstrating that POS information is robustly encoded in word embeddings, distributed across multiple vector components (at least 100 in a 300-dimensional model) rather than concentrated in a few features.  
2. Providing empirical evidence for "soft" POS boundaries by identifying words that cluster with a different POS class than their annotation (e.g., adjectives mislabeled as adverbs, proper nouns mistaken for common nouns).  
3. Revealing systematic annotation errors in the BNC (e.g., adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs) through distributional analysis, offering a tool for corpus quality assessment.  

**Strengths**  
1. The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that distributional models encode meaningful POS information, with the classifier achieving near-perfect accuracy (F-score 0.979) on the BNC. This supports the theoretical notion that POS boundaries are not categorical but graded—a significant contribution to linguistic theory.  
2. The analysis of misclassified words yields valuable linguistic insights, such as identifying 600+ instances where adjectives were incorrectly tagged as adverbs (e.g., "plain" in "makes it plain that..."). This demonstrates how distributional models can uncover annotation errors that might otherwise go unnoticed, directly improving corpus quality.  
3. The finding that POS information is distributed across many vector components (not limited to a few key features) aligns with the theoretical underpinnings of distributional models. The smooth accuracy increase with additional features (Fig. 3) offers a nuanced perspective on how linguistic information is encoded in embeddings.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper lacks novel NLP or ML contributions. The methodology—training embeddings followed by a standard logistic classifier—is straightforward and applies existing techniques without introducing new algorithms, models, or frameworks. This limits its relevance for a computational linguistics conference like CONLL.  
2. The literature review on POS tagging and induction using embeddings is insufficient. Key recent work (e.g., Lin et al. 2015, Ling et al. 2015, Plank et al. 2016) is omitted, weakening the paper's positioning within the field. These studies explore similar questions but with more sophisticated methods, making the current work appear incremental.  
3. The paper does not adequately address the practical utility of the approach. While the classifier achieves 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank test set, this is far below state-of-the-art POS taggers (typically >97% accuracy). The authors fail to explain why their method would be useful in practice despite this performance gap, especially since it relies solely on word-level distributional patterns without contextual information.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do you envision your approach being practically useful for POS tagging, given its lower accuracy (84%) compared to state-of-the-art taggers (>97%)? What specific applications (e.g., low-resource languages, corpus cleaning) might benefit from your method despite this gap?  
2. Your analysis reveals that 22% of subordinate conjunctions were misclassified as adverbs, whereas the Stanford POS tagger shows minimal errors of this type (Table 3). How do you explain this discrepancy, and what does it suggest about the distinct error patterns detected by distributional models versus traditional taggers?  
3. The paper states that POS information is distributed across "at least one hundred components" (Section 5). Given the smooth accuracy curve in Figure 3, what is the practical significance of this finding for NLP applications? Is there a point where adding more features provides diminishing returns?
