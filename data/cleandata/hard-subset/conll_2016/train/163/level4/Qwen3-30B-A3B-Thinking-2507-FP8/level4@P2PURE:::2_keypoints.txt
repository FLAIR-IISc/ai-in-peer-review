- Finds PoS information in embeddings and clear alignment between vectors and PoS categories; detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a strong contribution.  
- Omits the critical perspective that PoS-tagged corpora were created to support downstream NLP (e.g., parsing); paper should assess whether distributional distinctions are more useful for parsing than original tags/UPOS.  
- Lacks key related work on distributional PoS induction—should cite Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and non-DNN studies like Blunsom & Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012).  
- Results in Section 5 are scarcely novel, as prior systems with stricter external-knowledge constraints reach comparable performance.  
- Abstract claim that embeddings contain PoS information is not novel for English given 15 years of distributional PoS induction literature reporting cluster purity/many-to-one metrics.  
- Statement that vector relations are “mostly semantic” is incorrect; prior work (Mikolov et al. 2013b, Collobert et al. 2011) and the paper’s own later text show significant syntactic information.  
- Questionable choice to transition to UPOS—fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset may be more informative.  
- Footnote 3 is unclear: clarify whether reported failed attempts were by the authors or cited works and what failure criteria were used; consider discussing Brown clusters, which align well with UPOS.  
- Observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns lacks novelty; high-frequency function words like “the” likely drive this distinction.  
- Recommend analyzing tail (low-frequency) words to study vector behavior and classifier error patterns and to explore alternatives for better generalization to rare words.  
- Minor formatting issue: replace sentential citations with \newcite{} (e.g., use \newcite{Mikolov2013b}).
