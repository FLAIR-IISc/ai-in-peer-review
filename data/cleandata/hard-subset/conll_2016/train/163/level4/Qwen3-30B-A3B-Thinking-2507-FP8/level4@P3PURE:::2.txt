

General comments:  
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (POS) tags and word embeddings, drawing conclusions about the consistency of POS tags and the alignment between word vector representations and POS categories. The detailed error analysis of classification outliers constitutes a significant strength. However, the paper omits a critical foundational point: the original purpose of POS-tagged corpora like the BNC. Unlike purely linguistic explorations grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were created and tagged specifically to support downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. Consequently, the discussion could be reframed to evaluate whether distributional vector distinctions offer greater utility for parsing than the original tags (or UPOS).  

Additionally, the paper lacks substantial coverage of related work in distributional POS induction. I recommend incorporating Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) as a starting point, along with recent non-DNN studies such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Given this literature, the results in Section 5 offer minimal novelty, as systems with stricter constraints on external knowledge achieve comparable performance.  

Specific issues:  
The abstract claims that "distributional vectors contain information about POS affiliation." This assertion is not novel, particularly for English; every distributional POS induction system from the past 15 years reporting "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" metrics demonstrates the same finding.  

The statement in lines 79–80 ("relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic") is incorrect. Mikolov et al. (2013) and Collobert et al. (2011) establish that distributional vectors encode substantial syntactic information, contradicting this claim (the authors later acknowledge this in lines 107–108).  

Why adopt UPOS instead of the fine-grained original tagset, which offers more linguistically interesting distinctions?  

Footnote 3 is unclear: Were these failed attempts conducted by the authors or prior work? By what criteria did they fail? How do Brown cluster vectors (which nearly perfectly align with UPOS tags) fit into this analysis?  

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) lacks significance. The high frequency of words like "the" likely explains this difference, rendering the observation unsurprising.  

While the analysis of frequent word/tag pairs is pragmatically justified, exploring the tail distribution—both for vector representations and classifier error patterns—would yield more insightful results. This could motivate alternatives to pure distributional and morphological features for improving POS generalization to low-frequency words.  

Minor issues:  
Replace all sentential references (e.g., "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed") with `\newcite{}` formatting.
