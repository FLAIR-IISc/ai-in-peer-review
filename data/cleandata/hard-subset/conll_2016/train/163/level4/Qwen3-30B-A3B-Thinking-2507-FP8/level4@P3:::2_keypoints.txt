- Paper analyzes embeddings vs. PoS on BNC; main contributions claimed: outlier analysis of misclassified words, evidence PoS info is distributed across many embedding dimensions, and use of distributional models to detect annotation errors — but core finding (embeddings encode PoS) is not novel.  
- Strength: Insightful outlier analysis revealing real PoS annotation inconsistencies (e.g., passive participles showing adjectival patterns; adjectives mislabeled as adverbs).  
- Strength: Empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions, with classifier accuracy growing smoothly and requiring ~100 features for near-perfect performance.  
- Strength: Practical utility for corpus quality control — high classifier accuracy (F=0.99 on UD) and ability to flag systematic tagging errors (e.g., numerals mislabeled as nouns).  
- Weakness: Overstated novelty — prior work (e.g., Mikolov et al. 2013; Tsvetkov et al. 2015) already shows embeddings encode syntactic categories.  
- Weakness: Lacks critical contextualization of PoS tagging’s role in NLP (e.g., parsing) and does not compare distributional distinctions to NLP tagsets or tasks.  
- Weakness: Inadequate related work coverage — omits key distributional PoS induction literature (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) that achieves comparable results.  
- Weakness: Methodological choices are unjustified — switching to UPOS from BNC’s fine-grained tags is not defended, and references to “failed attempts” are unclear.  
- Weakness: Neglects low-frequency words by restricting analysis to frequent words (>500 occurrences) and does not explore remedies or different error patterns for rare items.  
- Question: How does outlier-based error detection compare to existing methods (e.g., confusion matrices from taggers) and what unique insights does it provide?  
- Question: Why adopt UPOS instead of the original BNC tagset, and how would fine-grained distinctions (e.g., proper vs. common noun) affect the outlier analysis?  
- Question: Can the method be extended to low-frequency words (e.g., via subword units or transfer learning), and how would that change error patterns or practical utility?  
- Additional comment: Correct the inaccurate claim that vector relations are “mostly semantic” — distributional vectors encode substantial syntactic information.  
- Additional comment: Fix citation formatting (replace Mikolov et al. (2013b) with the CONLL-style \newcite{mikolov2013b}).
