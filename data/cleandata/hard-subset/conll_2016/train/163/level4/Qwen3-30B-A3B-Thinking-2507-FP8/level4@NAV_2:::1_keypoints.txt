- Summary: Tests whether BNC-trained word embeddings encode POS by training a logistic regression on vectors and analyzing misclassifications for linguistic patterns.

- Contribution: Shows POS information is distributed across many vector dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.

- Contribution: Outlier/misclassification analysis reveals annotation inconsistencies in the BNC (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs, currency amounts mislabelled).

- Contribution: Provides empirical evidence for "soft" POS boundaries and boundary cases (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives).

- Strength: Strong empirical evidence that many (~100) embedding components contribute to POS classification, supporting distributed encoding.

- Strength: Misclassification analysis effectively identifies systematic corpus annotation errors, useful for corpus quality control.

- Strength: Method detects linguistically plausible boundary cases that align with established linguistic intuition.

- Weakness: No methodological novelty—uses standard distributional models and logistic regression without new techniques.

- Weakness: Limited NLP impact—findings are linguistically interesting but do not advance POS tagging performance or methodology.

- Weakness: Literature review omits key POS induction works (Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016; Tsuboi 2014), undermining novelty claims.

- Question: How do authors reconcile distributed POS encoding with the presence of strongly POS-associated individual components (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions)?

- Question: What specific, actionable recommendations should annotators use to address the annotation inconsistencies identified?
