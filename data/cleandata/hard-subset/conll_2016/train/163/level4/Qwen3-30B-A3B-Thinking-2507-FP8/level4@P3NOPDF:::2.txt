

This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (POS) tags and word embeddings, presenting an analysis of how distributional vector representations correlate with POS categories. While the detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a strong point, the paper suffers from significant conceptual gaps and lacks novelty in its core claims.

The primary weakness lies in the paper’s failure to address the *purpose* behind traditional POS tagging. Corpora like the BNC were not created for linguistic exploration under semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), but to support NLP tasks—primarily parsing. The discussion should therefore be reframed around whether distributional vectors offer *practical advantages* for parsing compared to original or UPOS tags, rather than merely confirming that vectors contain POS information. This critical context is missing, undermining the paper’s contribution.

Furthermore, the work omits essential related literature. The authors should begin with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), which reviews early distributional POS induction, and incorporate key non-DNN approaches like Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Given this body of work, the results in Section 5 lack novelty: systems with minimal external knowledge have achieved comparable or superior performance in POS induction, making the paper’s claims unsubstantiated.

Several specific claims require correction. The abstract asserts that "distributional vectors do contain information about POS affiliation"—a well-established finding for English since the mid-2000s. Every distributional POS induction system (e.g., those reporting "many-to-one" or cluster purity scores) demonstrates this. The claim in lines 79–80 that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic" is factually incorrect. Mikolov et al. (2013) and Collobert et al. (2011) explicitly show that word vectors encode substantial syntactic information, and the paper itself later retracts this in lines 107–108. This inconsistency weakens the argument.

The shift to UPOS (Universal POS) is unjustified. The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset (e.g., BNC’s 150+ tags) are linguistically more interesting than the simplified UPOS categories, yet the paper dismisses this without justification. Why prioritize UPOS over the tagset that motivated the corpus’s creation?

Footnote 3 requires clarification. It states "failed attempts" but does not specify whether these were the authors’ own experiments or prior work, nor the criteria for failure. For instance, Brown cluster vectors align closely with UPOS tags (e.g., by design), so this footnote’s relevance is unclear.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) is trivial. The stark difference is largely explained by frequent function words like "the," which consistently co-occur with common nouns but not proper nouns. This analysis adds little value.

While the focus on frequent word/tag pairs is practical, the paper neglects the *tail* of the frequency distribution. Analyzing low-frequency word types and classifier errors in this regime would be insightful. The authors should investigate whether morphological features (beyond lemmatization) or hybrid approaches could improve generalization to rare words—a direction hinted at but unexplored.

Minor issues require attention:  
- Replace "Mikolov et al. (2013b)" with `\newcite{mikolov2013b}` for consistent citation style.  
- Correct grammatical errors (e.g., "the paper seems to have missing" → "the paper is missing").  
- Clarify ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "the whole discussion could then be reframed" → "the discussion should be reframed").

In summary, this paper’s core contribution is not novel, its theoretical framing is flawed, and it overlooks foundational literature. The error analysis is a redeeming feature but insufficient to compensate for these gaps. To strengthen the work, the authors must: (1) reframe the motivation around NLP utility (e.g., parsing), (2) integrate key related work (Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom and Cohn, 2011), (3) retract overstated claims about vector semantics, and (4) explore low-frequency word behavior. Without these revisions, the paper offers little new insight to the field.
