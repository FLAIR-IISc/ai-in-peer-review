

Summary and Contributions:  
This paper investigates how word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. The primary contributions are: (1) Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (not concentrated in a few features), (2) Revealing annotation inconsistencies in the BNC through outlier analysis of a distributional PoS classifier, and (3) Providing evidence for "soft" PoS boundaries via misclassification patterns. The authors’ strongest contribution lies in using classifier outliers to systematically identify linguistic edge cases—such as verbs behaving as adjectives or currency terms mislabeled as nouns—which exposes annotation ambiguities not previously highlighted in corpus studies.

Strengths:  
1. **Insightful outlier analysis**: The paper’s most compelling contribution is its systematic examination of classifier errors. By analyzing misclassified words (e.g., "white-collar" tagged as adjective but classified as noun, or "1804" tagged as numeral but misclassified as noun), the authors uncover concrete annotation inconsistencies in the BNC. These cases directly challenge rigid PoS boundaries and align with known linguistic debates (e.g., participles straddling verb/adjective categories).  
2. **Robust classifier performance**: The logistic regression classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979) on BNC data and generalizes well to the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99). Crucially, the model’s success—despite relying *only* on distributional context—validates that PoS information is inherently embedded in word vectors, not artificially injected via tag concatenation.  
3. **Distributional evidence for "soft" PoS**: The paper convincingly shows PoS affiliation is distributed across vector dimensions (Fig. 3 confirms performance rises steadily with >100 features), countering the assumption that a single "PoS feature" exists. This aligns with linguistic theories of gradient categories (e.g., Houston 1985) and explains why PoS boundaries are fluid.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Lacks critical framing for PoS tagging purpose**: The paper misses a key context: PoS tags in corpora like BNC exist *for NLP tasks* (e.g., parsing), not purely for linguistic theory. The authors should address whether distributional vectors offer *practical advantages* over standard tags (e.g., UPOS) for downstream tasks. Without this, the work risks appearing disconnected from real-world NLP needs.  
2. **Overlooks foundational related work**: The paper fails to cite seminal PoS induction literature (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011), which already established that distributional models capture PoS information via cluster purity. Section 5’s results (F-score 0.84) are modest compared to prior work (e.g., Blunsom & Cohn 2011 achieved >90% accuracy with similar methods), making the novelty questionable.  
3. **Mischaracterizes embedding semantics**: The claim that "relations between vectors are mostly semantic" (p. 2) contradicts established work (Mikolov et al. 2013a/b; Collobert & Weston 2008), which shows embeddings encode *syntactic* patterns (e.g., "Brazil" → "Brazilian" mirrors "England" → "English"). This undermines the paper’s premise about embedding utility for PoS.  
4. **Unjustified UPOS shift**: The move to Universal PoS tags (UPOS) lacks justification. Fine-grained BNC tags (e.g., "NNS" vs. "NN") likely contain more linguistically relevant distinctions than UPOS’s coarse categories. The analysis of "proper nouns vs. common nouns" (p. 331) is trivial—this difference is largely explained by the high frequency of "the" (a determiner), not a deep linguistic insight.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Footnote 3 states "one-feature baseline classifier" achieved F-score 0.22. Was this a *failed attempt by the authors* or cited prior work? If the latter, clarify the source.  
2. Why replace BNC’s fine-grained tags with UPOS? Could the insights (e.g., "currency terms mislabeled as nouns") be replicated with original tags?  
3. How does this work compare to Brown clustering (Yatbaz et al. 2012), which aligns nearly perfectly with UPOS? If Brown clustering outperforms the classifier, why not cite it?  
4. Given the classifier’s reliance on distributional context alone, how would it handle low-frequency words (e.g., "luftwaffe") where context is sparse? Could morphological features improve robustness?
