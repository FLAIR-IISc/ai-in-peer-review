

This paper investigates whether distributional information in word vector models encodes part-of-speech (POS) label information. The authors use a lemma-based version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags. They train word embeddings on this corpus and subsequently employ these vectors to train a logistic regression classifier for POS prediction. Evaluation is conducted both on the training corpus (via cross-validation) and on external datasets, with results presented clearly and analyzed in depth.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. However, its primary limitation lies in the absence of novel contributions to NLP or machine learning methodology. The work describes a sequence of straightforward experiments—training embeddings on a lemma-processed corpus, using these embeddings for POS prediction via a standard logistic classifier—without introducing new algorithms, model architectures, or theoretical insights. While the results are empirically interesting and provide meaningful validation of POS as a linguistic construct (demonstrating that semantic distributional patterns correlate with grammatical function), the approach itself does not advance the field technically. The findings would be more appropriately situated for publication in a quantitative linguistics venue than in a computational linguistics conference like CONLL, where methodological innovation is expected.

A significant gap exists in the related work discussion. The paper fails to adequately contextualize its approach within the established literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings. Key prior work should be cited more thoroughly, including Lin et al. (2015) on POS induction from embeddings, Ling et al. (2015, EMNLP) on unsupervised POS tagging, and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) on leveraging word embeddings for linguistic analysis. Omitting these references weakens the paper’s positioning and overlooks relevant methodological precedents. For instance, Ling et al. (2015) already demonstrated effective POS induction using similar embedding-based classifiers, making the current work’s incremental nature even more apparent.

The evaluation strategy is sound, with cross-validation on the BNC and testing on external corpora providing robustness. The results confirm that word vectors retain sufficient information for POS prediction, aligning with broader evidence that distributional semantics intersects with grammatical categories. This is a valuable empirical contribution to linguistic theory, reinforcing the idea that POS is not merely a syntactic artifact but a dimension embedded in semantic space. Nevertheless, the paper’s methodological simplicity—relying on standard embedding training and a basic classifier—means it does not offer new tools or techniques for the NLP community. The contribution is primarily descriptive and confirmatory rather than innovative.

In summary, while the paper presents clear, well-analyzed results that contribute to linguistic understanding, it lacks the methodological novelty expected for CONLL. The work would be better suited for a venue focused on empirical linguistics. To strengthen it for NLP publication, the authors would need to either introduce a novel approach (e.g., a specialized embedding model or classifier architecture) or provide significantly deeper analysis of why the observed POS correlation occurs, potentially linking it to specific linguistic phenomena. As currently written, the paper serves as a useful baseline study but does not advance the state of the art in NLP.
