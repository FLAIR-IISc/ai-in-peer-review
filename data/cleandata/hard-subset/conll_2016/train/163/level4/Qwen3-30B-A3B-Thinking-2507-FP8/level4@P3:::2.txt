

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) categories using the British National Corpus. The primary contributions are: (1) a detailed analysis of "outlier" words misclassified by a distributional PoS predictor, revealing inconsistencies in PoS annotation; (2) evidence that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (not concentrated in a few features); and (3) a demonstration that distributional models can detect systematic annotation errors in corpora like BNC. These contributions are distinct from the authors' claims, as the core finding (embeddings contain PoS information) is well-established, while the outlier analysis and error-detection utility represent the paper's strongest contributions.

**Strengths**  
1. **Insightful outlier analysis**: The paper excels in identifying and analyzing misclassified words (e.g., proper nouns mislabeled as common nouns, verbs mistaken for adjectives), revealing genuine inconsistencies in PoS annotation. For example, it demonstrates that passive participles like "overgrown" exhibit strong adjectival patterns, highlighting a known linguistic ambiguity. Crucially, it shows that distributional models detect systematic errors (e.g., adjectives like "plain" erroneously tagged as adverbs in BNC), which the authors argue could improve corpus quality.  
2. **Evidence of distributed PoS information**: The analysis of vector components (Figures 1–2, Section 5) compellingly shows that PoS affiliation is not localized to a few dimensions. Training classifiers with increasing vector components (Figure 3) demonstrates smooth accuracy growth, reaching near-perfect performance only with ~100 features. This aligns with the theoretical expectation that linguistic features are distributed in embedding spaces.  
3. **Practical utility for corpus quality control**: The method provides a scalable way to audit PoS-tagged corpora. The classifier’s high accuracy (F-score 0.99 on Universal Dependencies Treebank) and ability to flag errors (e.g., "1804" mislabeled as a noun instead of numeral) suggest real-world value for corpus developers, complementing traditional error analysis via confusion matrices.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper’s core claim—that embeddings contain PoS information—is not novel. As early as 2013 (Mikolov et al.), it was established that distributional vectors encode syntactic categories. The abstract’s assertion (line 15) misrepresents this as a contribution, while the authors’ own Section 2 cites foundational work (Tsvetkov et al., 2015) showing similar results. The paper would benefit from acknowledging that this is a known phenomenon and focusing on its *new* insights (outlier analysis, error detection).  
2. **Lack of critical context**: The authors fail to address *why* PoS tagging exists in the first place: to support NLP tasks (e.g., parsing), not just linguistic theory. A meaningful contribution would compare distributional PoS distinctions to those used in NLP (e.g., UPOS vs. original tags). The paper’s failure to engage with this (e.g., Section 5’s UPOS shift lacks justification) weakens its relevance to the broader NLP community.  
3. **Inadequate related work**: The paper omits key literature on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011), making Section 2 superficial. Recent work (e.g., Yatbaz et al., 2012) already achieves comparable results with minimal external knowledge, rendering Section 5’s "novel" classifier less impressive. Without this context, the paper’s contribution appears overstated.  
4. **Unjustified methodological choices**: The shift to Universal POS (UPOS) is not defended, despite the original BNC using fine-grained tags (e.g., "proper noun" vs. "common noun"). The authors cite UPOS’s "standardization" but ignore that fine-grained distinctions are linguistically richer. Additionally, footnote 3’s reference to "failed attempts" (e.g., "one-feature baseline") lacks clarity—were these the authors’ experiments or prior work?  
5. **Neglect of low-frequency words**: The focus on frequent words (frequency >500) limits generalizability. The authors note (Section 4) that rare words like "luftwaffe" are misclassified, yet they do not explore whether low-frequency words exhibit different error patterns or how to address this. This omission weakens the practical utility of the method.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your approach to detecting PoS annotation errors (via outlier analysis) compare to existing methods (e.g., confusion matrices from Stanford Tagger)? Specifically, what unique insights does it provide that prior work missed?  
2. Why was UPOS adopted instead of the original BNC tagset? How might fine-grained distinctions (e.g., "proper noun" vs. "common noun") impact your outlier analysis?  
3. Could your method be extended to low-frequency words (e.g., using subword units or transfer learning)? If so, how would this affect error patterns and practical utility?
