- Summary: Tests whether BNC-trained lemmatized+UPOS embeddings encode POS information via logistic regression; achieves high F-scores and analyzes misclassifications to find outliers.  
- Contribution 1: Shows POS information is robustly encoded and distributed across many embedding components (at least ~100 of 300).  
- Contribution 2: Provides empirical evidence for "soft" POS boundaries by identifying words that cluster with different POS classes than their annotations.  
- Contribution 3: Uses distributional analysis to reveal systematic annotation errors in the BNC, offering a corpus-quality assessment tool.  
- Strength 1: Strong empirical support that embeddings encode POS (classifier F=0.979), supporting graded POS boundary theory.  
- Strength 2: Misclassification analysis yields linguistic insights and uncovers many annotation errors (e.g., 600+ adjectives tagged as adverbs).  
- Strength 3: Demonstrates POS information is distributed across many vector dimensions, with smooth accuracy gains as features increase.  
- Weakness 1: Limited methodological novelty—standard embedding training plus logistic regression without new algorithms.  
- Weakness 2: Incomplete literature review—omits relevant recent work (e.g., Lin et al. 2015, Ling et al. 2015, Plank et al. 2016).  
- Weakness 3: Practical utility unclear—84% accuracy on UD is far below SOTA (>97%), and method ignores contextual information.  
- Question 1: How is the approach practically useful given its lower accuracy—what applications (e.g., low-resource languages, corpus cleaning) justify it?  
- Question 2: Why do distributional models misclassify 22% of subordinate conjunctions as adverbs while the Stanford tagger shows minimal such errors?  
- Question 3: What is the practical significance of POS being spread over "at least 100 components," and where do additional features yield diminishing returns?
