

This paper investigates whether distributional word vectors encode part-of-speech (POS) information, using a lemma-based version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags. The authors train word embeddings on this corpus and employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic regression classifier for POS prediction, evaluating performance via cross-validation on the same corpus and additional external datasets. The results are clearly presented, thoroughly analyzed, and discussed in depth, demonstrating that word vectors reliably capture POS-related patterns.

The paper is well-written and methodologically sound, with transparent experimental design and robust evaluation. The findings provide empirical validation for the linguistic intuition that POS categories correlate with distributional similarity—a contribution of value to linguistic theory. However, the core limitation is the absence of novel NLP or machine learning contributions. The approach is straightforward: standard word embedding training followed by a simple classifier, with no new algorithms, model architectures, or theoretical insights. While the results are interesting as evidence for POS as a distributional construct, they do not advance the state of the art in NLP or computational linguistics. This is particularly notable given the extensive prior work on POS induction using embeddings, which the paper fails to adequately engage with.

For instance, the literature on leveraging word embeddings for POS tagging or induction—including seminal studies by Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015) at EMNLP, and Plank et al. (2016)—is not sufficiently cited or contextualized. These works already demonstrate that embeddings encode POS information, often through more sophisticated methods (e.g., contextualized embeddings, joint learning frameworks, or unsupervised induction). By omitting this critical discussion, the paper risks misrepresenting its contribution as novel when it largely replicates established practices with minor variations (e.g., lemma-based preprocessing). A more rigorous comparison to these prior efforts would have strengthened the analysis and clarified the paper’s unique value.

Given CONLL’s focus on cutting-edge NLP research, this work’s lack of methodological innovation makes it a poor fit for the conference. The empirical insights are compelling for linguistic audiences but do not address core NLP challenges like model generalizability, efficiency, or integration with downstream tasks. The paper would be more appropriately targeted at venues specializing in empirical linguistics or computational linguistics (e.g., *Computational Linguistics*, *Language Resources and Evaluation*), where the focus on validating linguistic constructs aligns with the venue’s scope.

In summary, while the study is methodologically rigorous and the results are well-justified, it offers no new NLP or ML techniques. The contribution lies in empirical confirmation of an existing linguistic hypothesis rather than in advancing the field. To improve, the authors should substantially revise the related work section to position their findings within the broader literature, explicitly address how their approach differs from or extends prior work, and clarify the theoretical significance of their results for linguistic theory. Without these revisions, the paper’s suitability for an NLP venue like CONLL remains limited.
