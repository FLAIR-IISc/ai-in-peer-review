

The paper investigates whether distributional information in word vector models encodes part-of-speech (POS) label information. Using a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags and lemmatized words, the authors train word embeddings and subsequently employ these vectors to train a logistic regression classifier for POS prediction. Evaluations are conducted on the same corpus (via cross-validation) and on additional external corpora, with results presented clearly and analyzed comprehensively.  

The paper is well-written and technically sound. However, its primary limitation is the lack of novel contributions to NLP or machine learning. The methodology involves straightforward experiments without introducing new NLP techniques, ML approaches, or theoretical insights. While the results are empirically significant—providing validation for the notion that word vectors inherently capture POS-related information—the work does not advance the field methodologically. Consequently, the paper would be more suitable for publication in a quantitative or empirical linguistic venue rather than a computational linguistics conference like CONLL.  

Additionally, the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings requires more extensive coverage. Key prior work, such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015, EMNLP), and Plank et al. (2016), should be cited and discussed to contextualize the contribution.
