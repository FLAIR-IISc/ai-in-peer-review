

General comments:  
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, drawing some interesting (though largely expected) conclusions about PoS consistency and the alignment between word vector representations and PoS categories. The detailed error analysis (examining classification outliers) constitutes a strong contribution.  

However, the paper misses a critical perspective: the original purpose of PoS-tagged corpora like the BNC. Unlike purely linguistic explorations grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these resources were created for downstream NLP tasks—primarily parsing. The discussion should therefore be reframed to evaluate whether distributional vector distinctions offer greater utility for parsing than the original tags (or UPOS).  

Additionally, the paper lacks essential related work on distributional PoS induction. It should begin with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporate key non-DNN studies (e.g., Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012). Given this literature, the results in Section 5 are not novel, as prior systems with stricter external knowledge constraints achieve comparable performance.  

Specific issues:  
The abstract claims "distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation," which is neither new nor surprising for English—every distributional PoS induction system from the past 15 years reporting "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" metrics demonstrates this.  

The assertion in lines 79–80 ("relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic") is incorrect. Mikolov et al. (2013b) and Collobert et al. (2011) established substantial syntactic information in these vectors (see also the cluster purity discussion). The paper later contradicts this in Section 2 (lines 107–108).  

The rationale for adopting UPOS over the original fine-grained tagset is unclear, as the latter’s distinctions are more linguistically meaningful.  

Footnote 3 is ambiguous: Were the cited "failed attempts" conducted by the authors or others? What criteria defined their failure? Brown cluster vectors, for instance, align nearly perfectly with UPOS tags.  

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331–332) lacks novelty; the prevalence of "the" (the most frequent function word) likely explains this difference.  

While analyzing frequent word/tag pairs has practical merit, extending the analysis to low-frequency items—both in vector representations and error patterns—would be valuable. This could motivate alternatives to pure distributional/morphological features for better generalization to rare words.  

Minor issues:  
Replace all sentential citations with `\newcite{}` (e.g., "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" → "Mikolov et al. \newcite{mikolov2013b} showed").
