

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (POS) tagging by training classifiers to predict POS tags from word embeddings. The authors demonstrate that word embeddings contain robust information about POS affiliation, which can be used to detect outliers and inconsistencies in existing POS annotations. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. Empirical demonstration that POS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings (at least 100 out of 300 dimensions), rather than concentrated in a few specific features.
2. Novel application of distributional semantic models to identify inconsistencies in POS annotations, revealing "soft" or "graded" POS boundaries rather than categorical distinctions.
3. Methodology for detecting linguistic outliers and systematic annotation errors through analysis of classifier misclassifications.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a valuable methodological contribution by demonstrating how distributional semantic models can be used to uncover inconsistencies in existing POS-annotated corpora. The authors show that their approach can identify systematic annotation errors (e.g., adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs in the BNC), which is particularly useful for corpus linguists and NLP practitioners working with POS-tagged data.

2. The thorough error analysis reveals important linguistic insights about the "soft" boundaries between parts of speech. For example, the paper identifies cases where proper nouns are misclassified as common nouns (suggesting contextual similarity between these categories), and where verbs are misclassified as adjectives (indicating a distributional overlap between passive participles and adjectives). These findings support the theoretical notion of graded POS affiliation rather than strict categorical boundaries.

3. The paper demonstrates robust performance across multiple datasets (BNC, Universal Dependencies Treebank) with a classifier achieving 0.91 F-score on unseen BNC data and 0.99 F-score on the UD Treebank. This cross-corpus validation strengthens the claim that the observed POS patterns are generalizable, not idiosyncratic to a single corpus.

4. The investigation of how POS information is distributed across vector dimensions provides a nuanced understanding of semantic representation. The finding that POS information is distributed across many dimensions (rather than concentrated in a few) is a significant contribution to our understanding of how linguistic information is encoded in word embeddings.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper lacks sufficient engagement with the existing literature on POS induction and tagging using distributional models. While the authors cite some relevant work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016), they fail to adequately position their work within the broader context of this research area. For instance, they should discuss how their approach differs from and advances upon methods like those proposed by Lin et al. (2015) or Plank et al. (2016).

2. The paper does not sufficiently articulate how the insights gained could be practically applied to improve existing POS taggers or other NLP systems. While the methodological contribution is valuable for corpus linguistics, the authors do not clearly explain how their approach could be integrated into the NLP pipeline to improve performance or address specific challenges in POS tagging.

3. The paper's claim that "information about PoS is distributed among dozens of vector components" is somewhat overstated. While Figure 3 shows a smooth increase in performance with more features, the authors acknowledge that the largest performance jump occurs between k=2 and k=3, suggesting that a small number of features might be most informative. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of which features are most informative and why.

4. The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of their approach. For example, the classifier's performance on the UD Treebank is 0.99, but the authors note that 27% of word types were omitted due to absence in the distributional model. This represents a significant portion of the vocabulary and should be discussed in terms of implications for practical application.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that the classifier can detect annotation errors in the BNC, such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs. Could you elaborate on how this approach could be integrated into corpus annotation pipelines to improve the quality of POS tagging?

2. The paper shows that POS information is distributed across many vector dimensions. Could you provide more insight into which specific dimensions are most informative for particular POS classes, and whether these dimensions correspond to known linguistic features?

3. The authors note that their approach achieved 0.99 F-score on the UD Treebank, but 27% of word types were omitted due to absence in the distributional model. How might this limitation be addressed for resource-poor languages where training data is scarce?

4. The paper identifies cases where words like "1804" (a year) are incorrectly tagged as numerals. How does the distributional model distinguish between numerals used as standalone tokens (e.g., "1804") versus numerals used in compound expressions (e.g., "the year 1804"), and what implications does this have for POS tagging conventions?
