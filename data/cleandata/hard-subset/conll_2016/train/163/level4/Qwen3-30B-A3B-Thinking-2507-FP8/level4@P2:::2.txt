

Summary: This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings (from BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The primary contribution is a method to identify lexical outliers—words whose distributional patterns conflict with their assigned PoS tags—revealing annotation inconsistencies in BNC (e.g., adjectives mislabeled as nouns). A secondary contribution is demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across *multiple* vector dimensions (≥100), not concentrated in a few features. The authors also show that a distributional classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.99 on UD Treebank) without contextual features.

Strengths:  
1. **Insightful outlier analysis**: The error-driven approach effectively uncovers real annotation issues. For example, the paper identifies systematic mis-tagging of adjectives (e.g., "plain" in "makes it plain") as adverbs (occurring ~600 times in BNC), which distributional patterns confirm as errors. This provides actionable evidence for corpus refinement.  
2. **Robust cross-corpus validation**: The classifier’s high accuracy (F-score 0.99) on Universal Dependencies Treebank—despite using BNC embeddings—demonstrates the generalizability of the findings beyond the training corpus. This addresses concerns about corpus-specific artifacts.  
3. **Nuanced insight on PoS distribution**: The discovery that PoS information spans ~100 vector dimensions (Fig. 3), not just 1–2 features, challenges simplistic views of embedding semantics and aligns with the "graded" nature of PoS boundaries. This is a significant methodological contribution.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Lack of novelty in core claim**: The assertion that "distributional vectors contain PoS information" is not new; prior work (e.g., Liu et al. 2016, Tsvetkov et al. 2015) already established high cluster purity (>90%) for English PoS. The paper’s contribution is the *error analysis*, but the abstract and introduction overstate this as a primary finding.  
2. **Misalignment with corpus purpose**: The paper overlooks why BNC was PoS-tagged (for NLP tasks like parsing, not linguistic theory). A critical gap is not addressing whether distributional PoS distinctions improve parsing performance vs. standard tags (e.g., UPOS). This limits the paper’s practical relevance.  
3. **Insufficient related work**: The review misses key literature on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011). Section 5’s classifier (accuracy 0.84) is not novel given systems like Brown clustering (which achieves near-perfect UPOS alignment with minimal external knowledge).  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How do you reconcile the claim that PoS information is "distributed across dozens of dimensions" (p. 12) with the observation that conjunctions have a single dominant feature (Fig. 1)? Is this an exception or evidence of context-dependent distribution?  
2. Why use UPOS instead of BNC’s original fine-grained tags? The paper implies UPOS simplifies analysis, but finer distinctions (e.g., "auxiliary" vs. "verb") might better reveal annotation nuances.  
3. The classifier’s accuracy (0.84) is modest vs. state-of-the-art taggers (e.g., 0.98+). How would you integrate this method into a full pipeline (e.g., as a preprocessor for low-resource languages) without contextual features?  

Additional Comments:  
- Correct the claim that "relations between vectors are mostly semantic" (p. 2): Mikolov et al. (2013b) and subsequent work prove these vectors encode *syntactic* regularities (e.g., "England" → "English" parallels).  
- The focus on frequent words (p. 6) neglects tail cases. How would low-frequency outliers (e.g., "luftwaffe") be handled in a real-world system?  
- Footnote 3 references "failed attempts"; clarify whether these are the authors’ experiments or prior work, and specify failure criteria (e.g., "one-feature baseline F-score 0.22").  
- Replace "Mikolov et al. (2013b)" with \newcite{mikolov2013b} for consistency.
