This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating a connection – though perhaps an expected one – between distributional vector representations and PoS categories. A key strength of the work lies in its detailed error analysis, providing valuable insights into classification outliers.

However, the paper overlooks a crucial context: the original motivation for PoS tagging in corpora like the BNC. These corpora were primarily created to support downstream NLP tasks, particularly parsing, rather than as purely linguistic investigations. Consequently, the discussion would benefit from being reframed to assess whether distinctions captured by distributional vectors offer advantages for parsing compared to existing tags (or UPOS). 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend including foundational work such as Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), alongside more recent non-DNN approaches like Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Considering this existing body of research, the results presented in Section 5 appear incremental, as comparable performance has been achieved by systems with more limited external knowledge.

Specifically, the claim in the abstract that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation” is not novel, particularly for English, given the consistent findings of prior distributional PoS induction systems. The assertion on lines 79-80 that relations between vectors are “mostly semantic” is also inaccurate; work such as Mikolov et al. (or Colobert et al.) demonstrates significant syntactic information within these vectors, a point seemingly contradicted later in the paper (lines 107-108). 

The motivation for transitioning to UPOS tags is unclear, as the finer-grained distinctions of the original tagset are potentially more informative. Footnote 3 requires clarification: were the referenced failed attempts conducted by the authors or reported in other work? What criteria defined these failures? The near-perfect alignment of Brown cluster vectors with UPOS tags also warrants discussion.

While the observation regarding the dissimilarity between proper and common nouns (lines 331-332) is noted, its significance is questionable, potentially explained by the prevalence of the definite article "the."  Analyzing the performance on less frequent words – both in terms of vector representations and classifier errors – would be insightful. Exploring alternative features beyond distributional and morphological cues could lead to improved generalization for these low-frequency cases.

Finally, please revise sentential references to use the \newcite{} command consistently (e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”).
