### General Comments

This paper presents an analysis of the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to draw several interesting conclusions regarding the consistency of PoS tags and the strong correspondence between vector representations and PoS categories. A particular strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis of the classification outliers.

However, the paper's central motivation could be strengthened by considering the primary purpose for which large corpora like the British National Corpus (BNC) were PoS-tagged. Beyond purely linguistic exploration of morphosyntactic categories, these resources were created to support downstream NLP tasks, most notably parsing. The entire discussion could be reframed into a more compelling research question: are the distinctions captured by distributional vectors more beneficial for a task like parsing than the original gold-standard tags (or the Universal POS tags)? Such a framing would provide a more practical motivation and a clearer metric for evaluating the utility of the vector-based distinctions.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from more thorough engagement with the extensive literature on unsupervised or distributionally-based PoS induction. The discussion is missing key related work that provides important context for the paper's findings. A good starting point would be the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), supplemented with more recent non-neural work such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). When situated within this body of work, the novelty of the results in Section 5 is somewhat diminished, as previous systems have achieved comparable results, often with more restricted access to external knowledge.

### Specific Issues

*   **Novelty of Claims:** The abstract states that a key contribution is showing that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This claim, while true, is a well-established finding in the field. For over 15 years, nearly every paper on distributionally-based PoS induction has demonstrated this, often quantitatively through "many-to-one" mapping evaluations or "cluster purity" scores.

*   **Nature of Information in Vectors:** The assertion in lines 79-80 that relations between word vectors are "mostly semantic" is inaccurate. Foundational work (e.g., Mikolov et al., Collobert et al.) and numerous subsequent studies have demonstrated that these vectors encode a significant amount of syntactic information. In fact, the paper contradicts its own assertion at the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108). This initial claim should be revised to reflect the current understanding of what information these vectors capture.

*   **Motivation for UPOS:** The motivation for mapping the original, fine-grained tagset to the coarser Universal POS (UPOS) tagset is unclear. An analysis of the fine-grained distinctions, which are often the most challenging and interesting, would seem more aligned with the paper's goals of exploring the nuances of PoS categories. The paper should justify why this loss of information was necessary or beneficial for the analysis.

*   **Clarity of Footnote 3:** Footnote 3 requires clarification. It is unclear whether the "failed attempts" refer to the authors' own experiments or to previous work. The criteria for what constituted a "failure" are also not specified. It would also be relevant to consider other vector representations, such as Brown clusters, which have been shown to align well with PoS tags and might have been a suitable alternative.

*   **Analysis of Noun Similarity:** The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be as insightful as suggested. This difference can likely be explained by a simple, high-frequency distributional cue: the presence or absence of determiners like "the," which co-occurs frequently with common nouns but rarely with proper nouns.

*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is currently focused on the most frequent word/tag pairs. A valuable extension would be to examine the long tail of low-frequency words. Investigating the vector representations and classification errors for these words could reveal the limitations of purely distributional features. Such an analysis could inspire alternative approaches (e.g., incorporating morphological features, since lemmatization is already used) to better generalize PoS tags for rare words.
