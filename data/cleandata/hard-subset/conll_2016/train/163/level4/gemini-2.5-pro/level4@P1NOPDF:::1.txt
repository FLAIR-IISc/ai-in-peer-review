This paper investigates the extent to which distributional information, as captured by word vector models, encodes Part-of-Speech (POS) information. The authors' methodology involves training word embeddings on a lemmatized version of the BNC corpus annotated with Universal Dependencies POS tags. These embeddings are then used to train a logistic regression classifier to predict the POS tag for a given word. The authors perform a thorough evaluation, using cross-validation on the training corpus as well as testing on several out-of-domain corpora. The paper presents its results clearly, offering extensive discussion and analysis.

The paper is clear and well-written. Its main weakness, however, is a lack of methodological novelty. The work describes a straightforward set of experiments that do not introduce new techniques or theoretical insights to the fields of NLP or machine learning. The findings are nonetheless interesting, as they provide strong empirical grounding for the distributional basis of Part-of-Speech categories. In that regard, the paper would be a good fit for a quantitative or empirical linguistics venue.

Finally, the discussion of related work on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings could be strengthened. The authors should consider citing several key relevant works in this area (e.g., Lin, Ammar, Duer and Levin, 2015; Ling et al., 2015; Plank, Søgaard and Goldberg, 2016).
