- Paper studies how pre-trained BNC word embeddings predict PoS tags via a classifier and analyzes misclassifications to argue for graded PoS membership and annotation inconsistencies.

- Main contribution: a qualitative error analysis that identifies words whose distributional profiles conflict with BNC PoS tags, revealing linguistic patterns and potential annotation errors.

- Strength: The error analysis yields clear, data-driven insights (e.g., adjective-like passive participles, noun-like years) useful for corpus exploration and annotation refinement.

- Strength: Methodology is simple and reproducible (Skip-gram embeddings + logistic regression) with justified design choices like frequency weighting.

- Weakness: No connection or evaluation showing whether the identified distributional distinctions help downstream NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, NER, information extraction).

- Weakness: Paper insufficiently engages prior work on unsupervised/semi-supervised PoS induction, overstating novelty of the findings.

- Weakness: Overstates novelty of the core premise that distributional vectors encode PoS information, which is well-established in prior literature.

- Question: Why map BNC tags to coarse-grained UPOS instead of analyzing the original fine-grained BNC tagset that might reveal subtler inconsistencies?

- Question: Which clustering methods were attempted for the reported “naive clustering” failure, and what criteria defined that failure?

- Question: How well do the findings generalize to low-frequency words with sparse embeddings; discuss limitations regarding the long tail of the vocabulary.

- Question: Is the proper-vs-common noun distinction driven by simple contextual cues (e.g., presence/absence of determiners), and which embedding components are most discriminative?

- Additional comment: Clarify the apparent contradiction between claiming word vectors are “mostly semantic” and later asserting they contain syntactic/PoS information.

- Additional comment: Standardize citation formatting (use “Author et al. (Year)” when the author is part of the sentence).
