### Summary and Contributions

This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors train a classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its embedding, and then use the classification errors—the 'outliers'—to analyze inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation and explore the notion of 'soft' or graded PoS membership. A secondary experiment attempts to build a standalone PoS tagger using only distributional information from unambiguous words.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  A detailed qualitative analysis of cases where distributional evidence from word embeddings contradicts the gold-standard PoS tags in the BNC. This analysis successfully identifies both annotation inconsistencies and linguistically interesting boundary cases between word classes.
2.  An empirical demonstration that PoS-related information in word embeddings is distributed across many vector dimensions, rather than being concentrated in a few.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The paper's primary strength is its detailed qualitative analysis of the classification outliers. This analysis provides compelling evidence for inconsistencies in the BNC annotation and offers data-driven support for the linguistic concept of graded PoS membership. By examining cases where the distributional evidence contradicts the gold-standard tag (e.g., nouns behaving like adjectives, passive participles behaving like adjectives), the authors identify interesting linguistic phenomena that are often smoothed over by standard tagsets.

2.  **Clear and Focused Methodology:** The methodology of training a classifier on embeddings to explicitly find and analyze outliers is simple yet effective. It provides a clear framework for identifying words whose distributional profiles do not match the prototype of their assigned class. This approach is more targeted than simply comparing confusion matrices from standard taggers, as it isolates discrepancies rooted purely in distributional patterns.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors base their experiments on publicly available resources (BNC, Universal Dependencies) and standard tools (word2vec, logistic regression). The description of the experimental setup is clear enough to allow for replication of the core results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Extrinsic Motivation and Evaluation:** The paper is framed as an intrinsic exploration of PoS categories but misses a crucial point: large-scale PoS annotation projects like the BNC were primarily created to support downstream NLP tasks, such as parsing. A more compelling argument could be made by reframing the discussion around this goal. For example, are the distinctions learned by the distributional model more useful for a parser than the original BNC tags? Without this connection to a downstream application, the practical impact of the findings is unclear.

2.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work:** The paper overlooks a significant body of literature on unsupervised and semi-supervised PoS induction from distributional information. The claim of novelty in Section 5 is particularly weakened by this omission. There are many systems that achieve comparable or better results with similar or even fewer resources. The authors should engage with this literature, starting with the survey by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and including more recent non-deep-learning work such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), to properly contextualize their results.

3.  **Overstated Novelty of Core Findings:** A central claim, stated in the abstract, is that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is a well-established fact, demonstrated implicitly by nearly every distributionally-based PoS induction system over the past 15 years through metrics like cluster purity or many-to-one accuracy. Similarly, the observation that word vectors contain syntactic information (contradicting the assertion in lines 79-80) is a foundational finding of modern distributional semantics. While this paper provides further evidence, these points should not be presented as primary contributions.

4.  **Focus on High-Frequency Words:** The analysis is restricted to the most frequent words in the corpus. While this is understandable for practical reasons, a discussion of how these findings might (or might not) apply to the long tail of low-frequency words would strengthen the paper. These low-frequency words are often where PoS taggers struggle most, and it would be interesting to consider how other features (e.g., morphology) could complement the distributional signals for these cases.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to map the BNC tags to the coarse-grained Universal PoS (UPOS) tagset? An analysis based on the original, more fine-grained BNC tagset might have revealed more subtle and interesting distinctions. What was the rationale for this simplification?

2.  Footnote 3 mentions that naive clustering attempts "inescapably failed." Could you clarify this? Were these your own experiments? What were the failure criteria? Given that methods like Brown clustering often produce clusters that align well with PoS tags, this result is surprising and warrants more detail.

3.  The analysis notes that proper nouns are distributionally dissimilar to common nouns (lines 331-332) and suggests this is an interesting finding. However, couldn't this largely be explained by the distribution of determiners like "the," which frequently precede common nouns but rarely precede proper nouns? How deep do you believe this distinction truly is?
