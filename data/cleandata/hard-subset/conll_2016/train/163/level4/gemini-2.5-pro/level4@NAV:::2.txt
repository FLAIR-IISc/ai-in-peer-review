**General Comments**

This work investigates the relationship between word embeddings and their corresponding part-of-speech (PoS) tags. Using word embeddings, the authors draw several conclusions regarding the consistency of PoS tags and the evident link between word vectors and PoS categories. While these findings are interesting, they are also somewhat expected. A key strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis, particularly the examination of classification outliers.

Nevertheless, the manuscript overlooks a fundamental point: the primary motivation for PoS-tagging corpora like the BNC was not purely for linguistic inquiry into morphosyntactic categories (which often rely on semantic prototype theories, e.g., Croft, 1991), but to support downstream NLP applications, most notably parsing. This context suggests a reframing of the central research question: are the distinctions captured by distributional vectors more advantageous for tasks like parsing than the original, manually annotated tags (including UPOS)?

Furthermore, the paper omits a substantial body of related work on distributional PoS induction. It is recommended that the authors consult the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporate more recent non-neural approaches, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Considering this established literature, the novelty of the results presented in Section 5 is diminished. Comparable performance has been achieved by systems that rely on even fewer external knowledge sources.

**Specific Issues**

*   The abstract claims as a contribution that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This finding is not novel; it has been a foundational assumption and demonstrated result in virtually every distributionally-based PoS induction system for English over the past 15 years, as evidenced by "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" evaluations.

*   The claim in lines 79-80 that relations between vectors are "mostly semantic" is inaccurate. Seminal work by Mikolov, Collobert, and others has demonstrated that these vectors encode significant syntactic information, a point reinforced by the high cluster purity scores mentioned previously. The authors even contradict this assertion in lines 107-108.

*   The motivation for mapping to the coarse-grained UPOS tagset is unclear. An analysis based on the original, more fine-grained tags would likely yield more interesting and nuanced insights.

*   Footnote 3 requires clarification. It is not clear whether the "failed attempts" mentioned were conducted by the authors or are from prior work. The criteria for what constituted a "failure" are also not specified. Additionally, it is surprising that Brown cluster vectors are not mentioned, as they are known to align well with UPOS tags.

*   The observation in lines 331-332 that proper nouns show low similarity to common nouns is not particularly insightful. This difference can be largely attributed to distributional context, such as the frequent co-occurrence of common nouns with determiners like "the," which is the most common function word in the corpus.

*   The analysis is limited to the most frequent word/tag pairs. While practical, this focus misses an opportunity to investigate the long tail of the distribution. An examination of low-frequency words—and the types of classification errors that occur for them—would be highly valuable. This could motivate a discussion on alternative features beyond purely distributional or morphological ones (given the use of lemmatization) that could improve generalization for these less frequent items.

**Minor Issues**

*   Bibliographic references within sentences should use the `\newcite{}` command (e.g., "As shown by \newcite{mikolov2013b}...") instead of parenthetical citations.
