**General comments**

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, using the latter to draw some interesting, albeit somewhat straightforward, conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the clear link between word vectors and PoS categories. A notable strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis, particularly the discussion of classification outliers.

However, the paper seems to miss a critical point: the primary motivation for creating large, PoS-tagged corpora like the BNC in the first place. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, which are often underpinned by semantic prototype theories (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were created and annotated to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, most notably parsing. The entire discussion could be powerfully reframed by asking whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more beneficial for a task like parsing than the original tagset (or the UPOS tagset).

Furthermore, the paper omits significant related work on distributional PoS induction. The authors should consult the survey by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporate more recent non-neural work, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this extensive body of research, the novelty of the results in Section 5 is limited; there are existing systems that achieve comparable results with even stricter limitations on external knowledge.

**Specific issues**

*   The abstract claims as a contribution that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is hardly a novel finding, especially for English. Nearly every distributionally-based PoS induction system from the last 15 years has demonstrated this, often by reporting "many-to-one" mapping accuracy or cluster purity scores.

*   The assertion in lines 79-80 that relations between word vectors are "mostly semantic" is inaccurate. Seminal work in this area (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013; Collobert et al., 2011) and subsequent research have repeatedly shown that these vectors encode a great deal of syntactic information, as evidenced by the high cluster purity scores mentioned above. In fact, the authors themselves seem to contradict this statement at the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

*   The motivation for mapping the original tags to the coarse-grained UPOS tagset is unclear. An analysis focusing on the fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset would arguably be more interesting and revealing.

*   Footnote 3 is confusing. It should be clarified whether the "failed attempts" refer to the authors' own experiments or to prior work. The criteria for what constituted a "failure" also need to be defined. Additionally, it is surprising that methods like Brown clustering are not discussed, as the resulting vectors are known to align very well with PoS tags.

*   The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) is unsurprising. This difference can be almost single-handedly explained by their distributional contexts; for instance, common nouns are frequently preceded by determiners like "the" (the most frequent word in English), whereas proper nouns typically are not.

*   While the practical reasons for analyzing only the most frequent word/tag pairs are understandable, it would be highly insightful to see an analysis of the long tail. Examining the vector representations and classification errors for low-frequency words could motivate the exploration of alternative features—beyond purely distributional and morphological ones (given the use of lemmatization)—that could enable better generalization of PoS tags to this part of the vocabulary.

**Minor issues**

*   Please use the appropriate command for textual citations (e.g., `\newcite{}` or `\citet{}`) where the reference is part of the sentence, as in "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed...".
