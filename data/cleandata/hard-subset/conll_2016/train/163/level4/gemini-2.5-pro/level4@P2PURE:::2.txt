### General Comments

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. Using word embeddings, the authors draw several interesting, albeit somewhat straightforward, conclusions regarding the consistency of PoS tags and the clear correspondence between word vectors and PoS categories. A key strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis, particularly the examination of classification outliers.

However, the paper seems to miss a critical point: the original motivation for PoS tagging corpora like the BNC. These corpora were not created for purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories (which often rely on semantic prototype theory, e.g., Croft, 1991), but rather to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. This context suggests that the discussion could be fruitfully reframed. A more compelling question would be whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more beneficial for parsing than the original BNC tags or the universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper overlooks a significant body of related work on distributional PoS induction. The authors should consider starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and including more recent non-DNN work from authors such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this existing literature, the results presented in Section 5 lack novelty. Previous systems have achieved comparable results, often with greater restrictions on the external knowledge they leverage.

### Specific Issues

*   The abstract claims as a contribution that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is a well-established finding, not a novel contribution. For at least 15 years, virtually every distributionally-based PoS induction system for English has demonstrated this through metrics like many-to-one accuracy or cluster purity.

*   The assertion in lines 79-80 that relations between word vectors are "mostly semantic" is inaccurate. Foundational work by Mikolov et al. and Collobert et al., as well as subsequent research, has clearly shown that these vectors encode significant syntactic information. This is also evidenced by the success of PoS induction systems, as mentioned above. The authors themselves seem to contradict this assertion at the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

*   The motivation for mapping the original tags to the UPOS tagset is unclear. An analysis of the fine-grained distinctions in the original tagset would arguably be more interesting and insightful.

*   Footnote 3 is unclear. It is not specified whether the "failed attempts" were conducted by the authors or are from other studies. The criteria for "failure" are also undefined. It would be helpful to clarify this and perhaps to consider other representations like Brown cluster vectors, which are known to align well with UPOS tags.

*   The observation in lines 331-332 that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" seems trivial. This difference can likely be explained almost entirely by context, specifically the co-occurrence patterns with determiners like "the," the most frequent function word in English.

*   While analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs is practical, an analysis of the long tail of the distribution would be more insightful. Examining the vectors and classification errors for low-frequency words could reveal limitations of purely distributional and morphological features (given the use of lemmatization) and motivate alternative approaches for generalizing PoS tags to rarer words.

### Minor Issues

*   Citation formatting should be corrected. Textual citations like "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" should use the appropriate LaTeX command (e.g., `\citet{}`).
