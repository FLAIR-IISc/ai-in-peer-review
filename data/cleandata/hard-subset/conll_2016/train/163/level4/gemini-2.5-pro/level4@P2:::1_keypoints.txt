- Trains Skip-gram on lemmatized PoS-tagged BNC and uses logistic regression on embeddings to predict PoS, focusing on qualitative error analysis.  
- Contribution: systematic identification and linguistic analysis of PoS outliers revealing gradience and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., passive participles, currency/year tagging).  
- Contribution: shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (≥100 of 300), not localized to few components.  
- Strength: deep, linguistically grounded error analysis that explains systematic misclassifications (e.g., nouns used as modifiers, passive participles).  
- Strength: clear, reproducible experimental setup with well-documented data preparation and hyperparameters.  
- Strength: compelling proof-of-concept: simple classifier on embeddings achieves high PoS prediction (F1 0.91 on BNC, 0.84 on UD) and enables low-resource tagging experiments.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—uses standard embeddings + classifier without new algorithms or evaluation paradigms.  
- Weakness: paper’s framing fits corpus/quantitative linguistics better than a top-tier NLP conference; insights are linguistic rather than advancing PoS tagging methods.  
- Weakness: incomplete engagement with related work on embedding-based PoS tagging/induction (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016).  
- Question: since embeddings were trained on tokens that include PoS tags (e.g., love_VERB vs love_NOUN), how would results change if embeddings were trained without PoS-tagged tokens and gold tags used only for classifier labels?  
- Question: beyond linguistic insight, what concrete takeaways or applications does this work offer to computational linguistics practitioners (e.g., improving models, error analysis, or data augmentation)?
