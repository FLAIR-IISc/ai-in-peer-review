- Paper studies how pre-trained skip-gram embeddings (on lemmatized BNC with PoS-tagged lemmas) encode Part-of-Speech by training a logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings and analyzing errors.

- Authors use classifier mispredictions to identify distributional outliers, arguing these reveal annotation inconsistencies, systematic corpus tagging errors, and graded PoS boundaries.

- Contribution: methodological demonstration of using a simple classifier as an analytical probe, with thorough error analysis uncovering linguistic edge cases (e.g., participles vs. adjectives, years vs. numerals).

- Contribution: empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, with classification improving as more dimensions are included.

- Strength: deep, insightful error analysis supported by concrete examples that highlight fuzzy PoS boundaries and annotation issues useful for linguists and corpus developers.

- Strength: paper is clear, well-written, and experimentally reproducible with straightforward setup and presentation.

- Strength: experimental design is appropriate for the analytical goal—simple logistic probe isolates information in static embeddings; evaluation on BNC and UD shows robustness.

- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—findings about embeddings encoding syntactic/PoS info are already well-established, so the work is primarily analytical rather than technical.

- Weakness: insufficient engagement with contemporary literature using embeddings in neural PoS taggers (e.g., LSTMs), leaving the work poorly contextualized with respect to state-of-the-art.

- Weakness: unfair baseline comparison—the Stanford PoS tagger was run on isolated tokens (no sentence context), undercutting its sequence-model strengths and skewing error comparisons.

- Question: clarify the paper’s main novel contribution for the computational linguistics community at CoNLL, which usually emphasizes models, algorithms, or resources.

- Question: justify running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens and discuss how error analysis would change if the tagger were run in full sentence context.
