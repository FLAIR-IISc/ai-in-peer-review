This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (POS) information is encoded within distributional word embeddings. The authors' methodology involves training word embeddings on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC), which has been annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags. Subsequently, they train a logistic regression classifier using these embeddings as features to predict the POS tag for a given lemma. The classifier's performance is evaluated through cross-validation on the BNC and is also tested on out-of-domain corpora. The results are presented clearly and supported by a thorough analysis.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The primary weakness of this work, however, lies in its limited methodological novelty. The experimental setup, while sound, follows a well-established paradigm of using pre-trained embeddings as features for a simple downstream classifier. The paper does not introduce new techniques or approaches in either natural language processing or machine learning.

Despite the lack of methodological innovation, the results are valuable from a linguistic standpoint. The work provides strong empirical evidence that distributional semantics effectively capture fundamental grammatical properties, offering a quantitative grounding for the notion of POS. In this regard, the paper makes a solid contribution to empirical linguistics and might be better suited for a venue focused on quantitative or computational linguistics, where this type of analysis is the primary contribution.

A further point of concern is the literature review, which could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly recent studies on POS tagging and POS induction that leverage word embeddings. For example, the authors should consider contextualizing their work with respect to key papers such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016), among others. A more thorough engagement with the existing literature would better position the paper's contribution within the field.
