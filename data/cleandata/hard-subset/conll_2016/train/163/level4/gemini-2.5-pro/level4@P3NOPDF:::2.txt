This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (POS) tags and distributional word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to analyze the consistency of POS tags and demonstrate the strong connection between a word's vector representation and its POS category.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its detailed error analysis. The examination of outliers from the classification task provides valuable insights and is a welcome contribution.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its interesting analysis, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution.

1.  **Framing and Motivation:** The paper seems to overlook the primary motivation for creating large-scale, POS-tagged corpora like the BNC. These resources were developed not just for linguistic exploration of morphosyntactic categories, but fundamentally to support downstream NLP applications, most notably parsing. The entire discussion could be significantly strengthened by reframing the core research question: are the distinctions captured by distributional vectors more beneficial for a task like parsing than the distinctions made by the original, manually annotated tags (or the Universal POS tags)? Without this connection to a downstream application, the analysis feels disconnected from a key area of NLP research.

2.  **Missing Related Work and Novelty:** The paper fails to engage with the extensive body of work on distributional POS induction. The literature review is notably missing key foundational and recent papers in this area. I strongly recommend the authors consult the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) as a starting point, and also incorporate more recent non-deep learning work, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this existing literature, the results presented in Section 5 are not particularly novel. Several previous systems, often using more constrained external knowledge, have achieved comparable or better results in unsupervised or semi-supervised POS induction.

**Specific Comments**

*   **Abstract:** The abstract claims as a contribution that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is a well-established finding and has been the foundational assumption for nearly every distributionally-based POS induction system for the past 15 years. This is routinely demonstrated through "many-to-one" evaluation metrics or cluster purity scores in that body of work.

*   **Lines 79-80:** The assertion that relations between word vectors "are mostly semantic" is inaccurate. Foundational work by Collobert and Weston, Mikolov et al., and others has clearly shown that these vectors encode a substantial amount of syntactic information. This is, in fact, the very reason distributional POS induction is successful. The authors themselves seem to contradict this initial assertion later in the paper (lines 107-108).

*   **Choice of UPOS:** The motivation for mapping the original, fine-grained BNC tags to the coarser Universal POS (UPOS) tagset is unclear. The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more interesting and present a greater challenge for distributional models. A justification for this choice is needed, as it simplifies the problem and may obscure important nuances.

*   **Footnote 3:** This footnote is ambiguous. It mentions "failed attempts" to use other vector types but provides no context. Were these attempts made by the authors or are they from other published works? What were the specific criteria for "failure"? Furthermore, the footnote omits Brown cluster vectors, which are known to align very well with POS tags and would be a natural point of comparison.

*   **Lines 331-332:** The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" is not a particularly insightful finding. This distributional difference is largely predictable; for instance, the presence or absence of determiners (e.g., "the," one of the most frequent words in English) is a powerful signal that almost single-handedly separates these two classes.

*   **Analysis of High-Frequency Words:** The analysis is limited by its focus on the most frequent word/tag pairs. A more compelling analysis would investigate what happens in the long tail of the distribution. Examining the vector representations and classification errors for low-frequency words could lead to a more interesting discussion. This could motivate the need for alternative features beyond the purely distributional (and morphological, via lemmatization) to allow for better generalization of POS tags to rare words.
