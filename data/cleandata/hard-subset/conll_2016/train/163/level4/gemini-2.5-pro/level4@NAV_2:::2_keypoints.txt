- Paper studies how pre-trained word embeddings reflect PoS classes by training a PoS predictor on vectors and analyzing misclassifications to find PoS boundaries and BNC annotation issues.
- Contribution: Qualitative error analysis identifies outlier words at graded PoS boundaries and uncovers potential systematic inconsistencies in BNC tagging.
- Contribution: Empirically shows PoS-related information is spread across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.
- Strength: In-depth analysis of misclassified tokens provides strong linguistic evidence for soft PoS boundaries and real tagging ambiguities (e.g., currency amounts, years).
- Strength: Well-written, clear experimental setup with effective visualizations (prototypical vectors, PoS vector similarity) supporting the claims.
- Weakness: Lacks motivation tying findings to downstream NLP utility (e.g., parsing), so practical relevance of redefining PoS classes is unclear.
- Weakness: Insufficient engagement with prior work on unsupervised/distributional PoS induction, making some experiments appear non-novel.
- Weakness: Overstates novelty—existence of PoS/syntactic information in embeddings is already well-established.
- Weakness: Analysis scope limited by mapping to coarse Universal PoS tags and restricting to high-frequency words, ignoring fine-grained tags and low-frequency vocabulary.
- Question: Why map BNC tags to the coarser Universal PoS set rather than analyze the original fine-grained distinctions?
- Question: Please detail the "inescapably failed" clustering attempts—algorithms used, failure criteria, and comparisons to methods like Brown clustering.
- Question: Could the proper/common noun distributional differences be driven by contexts with high-frequency function words (e.g., articles) rather than intrinsic class differences?
- Question: Have you considered task-based evaluation (e.g., whether a parser benefits from classifier-assigned PoS tags for outliers) to demonstrate practical impact?
