### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and Part-of-Speech (PoS) categories. The authors train a classifier to predict the PoS tag of a word based solely on its pre-trained word embedding from the British National Corpus (BNC). The primary focus is not on the classifier's performance itself, but on a detailed qualitative analysis of its misclassifications. The authors argue that these "outliers" reveal inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation, highlight words that exist on the fuzzy boundaries between traditional PoS classes, and support a "graded" view of PoS affiliation. Finally, the paper briefly explores the feasibility of a standalone PoS predictor built on this principle and analyzes the distribution of PoS-related information across the embedding dimensions.

### Contributions

1.  **A Detailed Qualitative Analysis of PoS Tagging Inconsistencies:** The paper's main contribution is the use of a simple classification method to systematically identify words whose distributional profiles conflict with their assigned PoS tags in the BNC. This error analysis provides data-driven evidence for well-known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the adjective-like nature of passive participles) and, more significantly, uncovers potential systematic annotation errors or inconsistencies within a major corpus (e.g., certain adjectives consistently mis-tagged as adverbs, or the inconsistent classification of years and currency amounts).

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable Error Analysis with Clear Insights:** The core strength of this work lies in the detailed analysis presented in Section 4. By examining the classifier's errors, the authors provide concrete, data-driven examples that support the linguistic concept of "graded" PoS membership. The methodology serves as an effective and intuitive tool for corpus exploration and quality control. Discovering patterns, such as the noun-like behavior of years (e.g., '1820s') or the adjectival contexts of certain BNC-tagged nouns (e.g., 'hypertext'), demonstrates the utility of this approach for refining annotation guidelines and understanding the distributional subtleties that standard tagsets may obscure.

2.  **Simple and Reproducible Methodology:** The experimental setup is straightforward and easy to understand. The use of standard tools (Skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression) makes the work highly reproducible. The authors clearly explain their reasoning for design choices, such as weighting instances by word frequency, which strengthens the credibility of their findings. This simplicity allows the focus to remain on the linguistic insights derived from the results, rather than on complex model architecture.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lacks Connection to Downstream NLP Applications:** The paper frames its investigation as a linguistic exploration but misses a critical motivation for PoS tagging in NLP. Corpora like the BNC are tagged primarily to serve as a foundation for downstream tasks such as parsing, named entity recognition, and information extraction. A crucial missing element is any discussion or evaluation of whether the distributional distinctions identified by the model are more or less beneficial for such tasks compared to the original BNC or Universal PoS tags. Without this connection, the practical impact of the proposed "redefinitions" on the broader NLP field remains unclear.

2.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work on PoS Induction:** The paper overlooks a significant body of literature on unsupervised and semi-supervised distributional PoS induction. A relevant survey like Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and more recent non-neural work (e.g., Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) have long explored the connection between distributional clusters and PoS tags. This omission leads to an overstatement of novelty, particularly regarding the claims in Section 5. The finding that a classifier can predict PoS tags from embeddings is well-established, and the performance of the proposed predictor is modest when viewed in the context of this extensive prior work.

3.  **Overstates the Novelty of the Core Premise:** The abstract and introduction present the finding that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation" as a key result. This is a foundational premise of distributional semantics and has been demonstrated for over a decade in virtually every paper on distributional PoS induction, often quantified with metrics like cluster purity or many-to-one accuracy. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this as a well-established fact and instead framed its contribution more precisely as a novel *application* of this principle for qualitative error analysis in an existing gold-standard corpus.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The rationale for mapping BNC tags to the coarse-grained Universal PoS (UPOS) tagset is not fully justified. Wouldn't an analysis based on the original, fine-grained BNC tagset potentially reveal more subtle and interesting inconsistencies that are lost in the mapping to UPOS?

2.  Footnote 3 states that naive clustering of word vectors failed. Could you please elaborate on this? Which clustering methods were attempted, and what were the specific criteria for "failure"? This is an important detail, as other vector representations (e.g., Brown clusters) are known to align well with PoS tags.

3.  The analysis focuses on high-frequency words (top 10,000 for training, 100-500 frequency for testing). How do you expect these findings to generalize to lower-frequency words, where embeddings are often sparser and less reliable? A discussion of the method's limitations with respect to the long tail of the vocabulary would be valuable.

4.  Regarding the finding that proper nouns are distributionally distinct from common nouns, could this difference be largely attributed to simple, high-frequency contextual cues, such as the presence or absence of determiners (e.g., "the")? Have you analyzed which vector components are most discriminative for these two classes?

### Additional Comments

*   The statement in the introduction that relations between word vectors are "mostly semantic" (lines 79-80) seems to contradict both subsequent work you cite and your own statement in Section 2 (lines 107-108) that they contain syntactic/PoS information. Clarifying this early on would improve the paper's consistency.
