- Detailed case study comparing distributional word vectors to annotated PoS tags, using classifier errors to find annotation inconsistencies and graded PoS membership.
- Empirical finding that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, with classifier performance improving up to ~100 features.
- Thorough, insightful error analysis that uncovers linguistic phenomena (e.g., passive participles as adjectives, years as nouns) and corpus issues.
- Comparison to the Stanford PoS Tagger shows the method exposes different boundary cases, offering a complementary perspective.
- Clear, well-documented experimental setup with released tag mappings, enhancing reproducibility.
- Lacks connection to downstream NLP utility (e.g., parsing); no extrinsic evaluation to demonstrate practical impact.
- Insufficient engagement with prior work on distributional/unsupervised PoS induction, making novelty claims—especially for the standalone PoS predictor—questionable.
- Questionable claims and design choices: overstating embeddings as “mostly semantic” and mapping BNC’s fine-grained tags to the coarse Universal PoS set without strong justification.
- Question: Would a parser trained on classifier-corrected tags yield better downstream performance (request for extrinsic evaluation)?
- Question: Clarify the novelty and comparative performance relative to established distributional PoS induction literature.
- Question: Explain what constituted “failure” in naive clustering attempts and the criteria used.
- Question: Provide rationale for converting the BNC fine-grained tagset to the coarser Universal PoS tagset for this analysis.
