**Summary of the paper and its main contributions**

This paper investigates the relationship between pre-trained word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word vectors and then analyze the classification errors to explore the nature of PoS boundaries and identify inconsistencies in the British National Corpus (BNC) annotation.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A detailed qualitative error analysis of a PoS classifier trained on word embeddings. This analysis successfully identifies "outlier" words whose distributional patterns deviate from their annotated PoS class, thereby highlighting words that exist on the graded boundaries between categories (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives) and revealing potential systematic inconsistencies in the source corpus annotation.
2.  An empirical demonstration that information related to PoS affiliation is not concentrated in a few dimensions but is distributed across a large number of components within the word embeddings.

**Strengths**

1.  The primary strength of the paper is its in-depth, qualitative analysis of the classification "outliers." This approach serves as an effective diagnostic method for corpus linguistics. By examining words that are misclassified, the authors provide compelling evidence for the 'soft' boundaries between PoS classes and successfully uncover potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation scheme. For instance, the discussion of currency amounts (`£70`) classified as numerals instead of nouns, or years (`1804`) behaving like nouns, points to genuine ambiguities in tagging guidelines. This type of detailed analysis provides valuable linguistic insights that go beyond simple accuracy metrics.
2.  The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and the experimental setup is straightforward and easy to follow. The visualizations of prototypical vectors for conjunctions and the analysis of vector similarity between PoS classes effectively support the authors' claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Context and Motivation:** The paper frames its investigation as a linguistic exploration but misses a critical motivation for PoS tagging in NLP: its utility for downstream tasks like parsing. The analysis would be significantly more impactful if it addressed whether the distributional distinctions it uncovers are more or less beneficial for such applications compared to the established BNC or Universal PoS tagsets. Without this connection, the practical relevance of "redefining" PoS classes remains unclear.
2.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work:** The submission overlooks a substantial body of prior work on unsupervised and distributionally-informed PoS induction. The paper would be strengthened by situating its findings within this literature (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012). In light of this previous work, the experiments in Section 5, which present a PoS predictor, lack novelty, as similar or better results have been achieved by systems with comparable or even more restrictive assumptions.
3.  **Overstated Novelty of Core Claims:** The central finding presented in the abstract, that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation," is a well-established fact in the community and not a novel contribution. This has been a foundational assumption and empirical result in virtually all work on distributional PoS induction for the past 15 years. Similarly, the presence of syntactic information in word embeddings is a widely known result from the seminal papers in the field.
4.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The decision to convert the BNC's detailed tagset to the much coarser Universal PoS tagset is not sufficiently justified. Analyzing the finer-grained distinctions of the original tagset could have yielded more nuanced and interesting insights. Furthermore, the analysis is restricted to the 10,000 most frequent words for training and other high-frequency words for testing, completely ignoring the long tail of the vocabulary. An analysis of how the method performs on low-frequency words, where distributional signals are sparse, would provide a more complete picture of its capabilities and limitations.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for mapping the original BNC tags to the coarser Universal PoS tagset? An analysis of the fine-grained distinctions might have revealed more subtle and interesting distributional patterns at the boundaries between classes.
2.  In Footnote 3, you mention that naive clustering attempts "inescapably failed." Could you provide more detail on these experiments? Specifically, what clustering algorithms were used and what were the criteria for failure? For example, how did these attempts compare to established methods like Brown clustering, which has been shown to produce clusters that align well with PoS tags?
3.  The finding that proper nouns are distributionally dissimilar from common nouns is interesting. However, could this difference be largely attributed to the distinct contexts of high-frequency function words? For instance, common nouns frequently follow articles like "the," a context in which proper nouns appear less often.
4.  While the paper provides a valuable linguistic analysis, have you considered a task-based evaluation? For example, would a parser perform better using PoS tags assigned by your classifier for the identified "outlier" words compared to using the original BNC tags? This would provide a more concrete motivation for the proposed approach.
