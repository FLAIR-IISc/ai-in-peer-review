### General Comments

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to draw some interesting, albeit somewhat straightforward, conclusions regarding the consistency of PoS tags and the clear link between word vectors and PoS categories. A key strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis of classification outliers.

However, the paper overlooks a critical point: the primary motivation for PoS-tagging corpora like the BNC. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories (which are often underpinned by theories like semantic prototypes, e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were created and tagged to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, most notably parsing. The entire discussion could thus be reframed around whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more beneficial for tasks like parsing compared to the original tags (or the Universal PoS tags).

Furthermore, the paper omits a substantial body of related work on distributional PoS induction. The authors should consult the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and more recent non-DNN work, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this prior work, the results presented in Section 5 lack novelty; systems with stricter constraints on external knowledge have already achieved comparable results.

### Specific Issues

*   The abstract claims as a contribution that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This is a well-established result, particularly for English. For the last 15 years, virtually every distributionally-based PoS induction system has demonstrated this through metrics like many-to-one accuracy and cluster purity.

*   The assertion in lines 79-80 that relations between word vectors are "mostly semantic" is incorrect. Seminal work on word embeddings (e.g., by Mikolov et al. or Collobert et al.) and subsequent research have demonstrated that these vectors encode a great deal of syntactic information. In fact, the authors contradict their own statement in lines 107-108.

*   The motivation for mapping to the coarse-grained Universal PoS (UPOS) tagset is unclear. An analysis of the fine-grained distinctions in the original tagset would likely be more insightful.

*   Footnote 3 is confusing. Does it refer to failed attempts by the authors or to failures reported in prior work? On what criteria were these attempts judged to have failed? Additionally, this claim should be qualified, as representations from methods like Brown clustering are known to align very well with PoS tags.

*   The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) is not particularly surprising. This phenomenon can largely be explained by the different distributional contexts; for instance, the presence or absence of determiners like "the" (the most frequent function word) is a strong signal that distinguishes common nouns from proper nouns.

*   While the practical reasons for analyzing only the most frequent word/tag pairs are understandable, it would be valuable to see an analysis of the long tail. Investigating the vector representations and classification errors for low-frequency words could motivate the development of alternative features—beyond purely distributional and morphological ones (since lemmatization is already used)—that would enable better PoS generalization.

### Minor Issues

*   Please use textual citations (e.g., `\newcite{}`) for sentential references. For instance, "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed..." should be used instead of a parenthetical citation at the beginning of a sentence.
