This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (POS) information is encoded within distributional word embeddings. The authors' methodology involves training word vectors on a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC), which has been annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags. These pre-trained vectors are then used as input features to train a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's POS tag. The evaluation is performed using cross-validation on the BNC, with additional experiments on out-of-domain corpora to test for generalization.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented transparently. A notable strength is the comprehensive analysis and discussion of the findings, which offers valuable insights into the relationship between distributional semantics and syntactic categories.

However, my primary concern is the paper's limited methodological novelty in the context of a top-tier computational linguistics conference like CONLL. The approach employed—training word embeddings and subsequently using them to train a simple classifier—is a standard and well-established pipeline. While the experiments are sound, the paper does not introduce any new techniques, models, or theoretical frameworks to the fields of Natural Language Processing or Machine Learning.

The contribution of this work appears to lie more in the domain of empirical or quantitative linguistics. The findings provide solid empirical validation for the long-held linguistic hypothesis that syntactic categories correlate strongly with distributional properties. While the results are indeed interesting from this perspective, for the CONLL community, which typically expects contributions that advance the state-of-the-art in NLP tasks or introduce novel computational methods, this work may be perceived as primarily confirmatory rather than pioneering. For this reason, it might be better suited for a linguistics-focused venue.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough engagement with the existing literature. The related work section is somewhat sparse and overlooks several highly relevant papers on POS tagging and POS induction, particularly those that leverage word embeddings. For instance, the authors should consider discussing and contextualizing their work with respect to key contributions such as Lin et al. (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank et al. (2016), among others. A stronger positioning within the current body of research would help to better delineate the paper's specific contribution.

In summary, while the paper presents a clear and interesting analysis, its lack of methodological novelty and limited engagement with recent NLP literature make it a challenging fit for the CONLL 2016 program.
