General comments:
This paper examines the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) categories and word embeddings trained on the BNC, using simple classifiers to predict PoS and then analyzing misclassifications to surface annotation inconsistencies and graded category membership. The outlier/error analysis is a clear strength and yields useful observations.
That said, the work seems to sidestep a central motivation for PoS annotation in resources like the BNC: enabling downstream NLP tasks, particularly parsing. Unlike a purely linguistic study of morphosyntactic categories (e.g., prototype-based views; see Croft, 1991), these tagsets were engineered with practical utility in mind. The discussion would be more compelling if framed around whether the distinctions induced by distributional vectors improve parsing (or other downstream tasks) compared to the original tags (or UPOS).
In addition, the paper omits a substantial body of related work on distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting from Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating subsequent non-neural approaches such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this literature, the results in Section 5 contribute limited novelty; there exist systems with fewer external assumptions that reach similar performance.

Specific issues
In the abstract, the claim that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation” is not novel (at least for English). Many distributional PoS induction papers over the past decade-plus report high many-to-one scores or cluster purity that already establish this.
The statement on lines 79–80 that relations between embedding vectors are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate. Work by Mikolov or Collobert (among others) has shown substantial syntactic regularities in embeddings; moreover, prior cluster-purity results echo this. The narrative then implicitly reverses this claim at the start of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
Why map to UPOS? The original, more fine-grained BNC/CLAWS distinctions are arguably more informative and would enable richer analyses.
Footnote 3 is unclear. Were the failed clustering attempts your own or from prior work? What were the failure criteria? How do Brown clusters compare here? They tend to align quite closely with UPOS.
The observation that proper nouns are not very similar to common nouns (lines 331–332) may be unsurprising. High-frequency function words like “the” could largely account for this separation.
While focusing on frequent word/tag pairs is practical, it would be valuable to probe the long tail: both how embeddings behave for rare types and what error patterns the classifier exhibits there. This could motivate augmentations beyond purely distributional (and morphological/lemmatized) features to improve generalization for low-frequency words.
