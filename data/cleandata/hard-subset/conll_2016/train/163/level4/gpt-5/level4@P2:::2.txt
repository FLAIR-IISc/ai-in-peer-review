Summary and contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings interact with part-of-speech (PoS) categories. Using BNC-based embeddings, the authors train a classifier to predict PoS from word vectors and then analyze misclassifications to identify “outlier” words that blur PoS boundaries or expose annotation inconsistencies. They further show that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions and build a simple tagger that predicts PoS from embeddings trained without PoS tags.

Contributions as I see them (in order of importance):
1) A careful, data-driven error analysis using embedding-based predictions to surface boundary cases and probable annotation issues in BNC (and to a lesser extent UD). This is the most valuable aspect.
2) Empirical evidence that PoS-relevant signal is spread across many embedding dimensions, not localized in one or two.
3) A simple, contextless PoS predictor based only on embeddings, demonstrating moderate accuracy.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis: The examination of misclassifications yields interpretable patterns (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally; currency amounts and years aligning with NUM vs NOUN; SCONJ/ADP/ADV confusions) and identifies likely systematic tagging issues in BNC (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs). This is useful both for corpus curation and for understanding graded PoS behavior.
- Clear empirical setup and robustness checks: Results are reported on frequent BNC items, a separate BNC-heldout set, and UD lemmas, with frequency-weighted training to counter open/closed class skew. The “prototypical vector” visualizations and inter-PoS similarities are informative.
- Demonstration that PoS signal is distributed: The ANOVA ranking and top-k feature curve support the claim that many dimensions contribute to PoS discrimination, aligning with the intuition behind dense embeddings.
- Practical angle: The minimal-supervision tagger trained on prototypical UD lemmas provides a reasonable baseline for resource-poor settings, despite lacking sentence-level context.

Weaknesses
- Framing and motivation: The paper downplays why corpora like BNC were tagged originally—primarily to support downstream tasks such as parsing. A stronger framing would evaluate whether the embedding-induced distinctions are more useful for downstream parsing or tagging than the original tagsets (CLAWS/UPOS). Without this, the impact beyond linguistic exploration remains unclear.
- Limited novelty relative to PoS induction literature: That embeddings carry PoS signal is well-established. Prior unsupervised/semi-supervised PoS induction work (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010 survey; Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012; Brown clustering) reports strong many-to-one/cluster purity results. Section 5’s predictor is therefore only modestly novel; comparable performance has been achieved with fewer assumptions or weaker supervision.
- Missing related work and inaccurate claims: The abstract’s claim that vectors “contain information about PoS affiliation” is not new. The introduction’s statement that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” conflicts with extensive evidence that embeddings encode syntactic regularities (e.g., Collobert and Weston; Mikolov et al.), a point the paper later implicitly concedes.
- Methodological confound in Sections 3–4: Training embeddings over lemma+PoS tokens means the vocabulary itself encodes PoS, which can bias both similarity structures and the classifier. This weakens the claim that embeddings “learn” PoS from distributional context alone. Section 5 partially addresses this by training embeddings without tags; that setup should be emphasized earlier and used consistently for the outlier analysis.
- Loss of granularity by moving to UPOS: Mapping CLAWS to UPOS simplifies analysis but discards distinctions likely to be informative. The paper should justify this choice and, ideally, show whether finer-grained tags yield richer or different outlier patterns.
- Incomplete analysis of the long tail: The study focuses on frequent items. Many interesting boundary cases arise in low-frequency words; examining tail behavior (and whether additional signals—morphology, orthography, subword embeddings—help) would strengthen conclusions.
- Some observations are unsurprising or under-analyzed: For example, proper nouns being dissimilar to common nouns may largely reflect determiners’ distributional patterns. This could be quantified (e.g., ablations excluding determiners or using dependency-based contexts).

Questions to authors
- Why prioritize UPOS over the original CLAWS tagset? What insights were lost by collapsing distinctions? Can you replicate the analysis at the finer granularity?
- In Sections 3–4, embeddings are trained on lemma+PoS tokens. How do you rule out leakage from this design? Can you repeat the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on plain lemmas to validate the conclusions?
- Footnote 3 states clustering “inescapably failed.” Were these your experiments? What algorithms, numbers of clusters, and evaluation criteria (e.g., V-measure, many-to-one) were used? How do these results compare to Brown clusters, which often align closely with UPOS?
- The introduction claims embedding relations are “mostly semantic.” Given prior work demonstrating syntactic content, how do you reconcile this? Could you report syntactic vs semantic probe evaluations?
- Can you quantify and manually verify the scale of systematic tagging errors you identified (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as ADV)? Will you release lists to support corpus maintenance?
- How does redefining PoS (or using embedding-induced categories) affect downstream parsing or tagging performance? A small parsing experiment would materially strengthen the paper’s impact.

Additional comments and minor issues
- Related work: Please add PoS induction and clustering literature (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010; Blunsom and Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) and early embedding/tagging work (e.g., Collobert and Weston; Tsuboi, 2014).
- Clarify evaluation baselines beyond majority and 1D-feature; e.g., Brown clustering + simple classifier.
- Consider dependency-based embeddings or larger windows to test robustness of syntactic vs semantic signals.
- Explore tail performance and OOV handling; discuss subword models (character or BPE) given you lemmatize and lose inflectional cues.
- Fix citation style (use newcite for sentential references). Replace placeholder references (e.g., “<MIKOLOV or COLOBERT>”) with proper citations.
- Provide details/links for the CLAWS→UPOS mapping and any released resources.
