General comments:
This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to draw some interesting—if somewhat straightforward—conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the strong connection between word vector representations and PoS. The detailed error analysis (i.e., analysis of classification outliers) is a clear strength.

However, the paper appears to be missing one critical main point: the original motivation for PoS-tagging corpora such as the BNC. Unlike a purely linguistic exploration of morphosyntactic categories (which are underpinned by a semantic prototype theory—e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were created and tagged to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion could therefore be reframed around whether the distinctions learned by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing than the original tagset (or UPOS, for that matter).

The paper is also missing substantial related work on distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and adding more recent non-DNN work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), and others. In light of this body of work, the results in Section 5 are barely novel; there are systems with stricter limitations on external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

Specific issues:
- In the abstract, one of the claimed contributions is that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” Unless I am misunderstanding, this is not a new result—especially for English. Every distributionally based PoS induction system in the past 15 years that reports “many-to-one” or “cluster purity” metrics demonstrates the same finding.
- The assertion in lines 79–80 (“relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic”) is not correct. The Mikolov et al. or Collobert et al. paper (and subsequent work) shows that these vectors encode substantial syntactic information. See also the previous comment about cluster purity results. In fact, you contradict that statement at the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
- Why switch to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more informative and interesting for analysis.
- I do not understand footnote 3. Were these failed attempts your own or from prior work? Under what criteria did they fail? What about Brown cluster vectors? They align almost perfectly with UPOS tags.
- Is the observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331–332) particularly interesting? Doesn’t the existence of “the” (the most frequent function word) almost single-handedly explain much of this difference?
- While I understand the practical reasons for focusing on the most frequent word/tag pairs, it would be valuable to analyze the tail, both in terms of vector behavior and the types of classifier errors. You could then explore alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological—since you are lemmatizing) features that might allow better generalization of PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues:
- Change sentential references to use \newcite{}, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”.
