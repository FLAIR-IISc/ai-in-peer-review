Summary and contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings trained on the BNC encode part-of-speech (POS) information. The authors lemmatize the BNC, map POS tags to Universal Dependencies, train Skip-gram embeddings, and then fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict a word’s POS from its vector. They evaluate via cross-validation on the BNC, on a held-out frequency band, and on the UD English Treebank, and analyze systematic misclassifications to probe graded POS boundaries and potential annotation inconsistencies.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
- Empirical evidence that standard word embeddings encode robust POS information, sufficient to train a simple classifier with high accuracy across datasets.
- A detailed error analysis showing how distributional signals reveal “soft” POS boundaries and can surface annotation inconsistencies (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, adjective/adverb confusions).
- The observation that POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized in a few, supported by a feature ablation analysis.

Strengths
- Clear experimental design and thorough analysis: The pipeline (lemmatization, UD mapping, Skip-gram training, logistic regression) is easy to follow, and the authors report results on in-domain (BNC via cross-validation), out-of-domain frequency bands, and the UD English Treebank. The consistent performance across settings supports the robustness of the central claim that embeddings encode POS information.
- Insightful error analysis with linguistic value: The misclassification study is carefully executed and leads to interesting findings about graded category membership (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally; nouns functioning as modifiers) and annotation issues (e.g., years/decades tagged as numerals vs. nouns; currency amounts; adjective/adverb confusions). This provides empirical grounding for long-discussed linguistic phenomena and suggests practical utility for corpus error detection.
- Useful comparison to a standard POS tagger’s confusion profile: By contrasting the distributional classifier’s confusions with those from the Stanford POS Tagger, the paper shows that the embedding-based approach surfaces different boundary cases, arguing for its complementary diagnostic value.
- Well-written and accessible: The paper is clearly presented, with results and observations explained in a way that bridges NLP and linguistic audiences. The discussion of how POS signals are distributed across many dimensions (via ANOVA ranking and incremental feature sets) adds a thoughtful methodological perspective.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in NLP/ML: The work applies standard tools (Skip-gram embeddings and multinomial logistic regression) to a familiar question. There are no new algorithms, modeling ideas, or learning techniques. While the analysis is careful and interesting, the methodological contribution to NLP/ML is minimal.
- Straightforward experimental setup: The core experiments amount to training embeddings and a linear classifier; the insights derive from analysis rather than from advancing models or training regimes. This reduces the impact for a venue emphasizing novel computational methods.
- Literature coverage on POS tagging/induction with embeddings is too narrow: Prior work leveraging embeddings for POS tagging/induction and related tasks should be discussed and compared in more depth. For example, Lin, Ammar, Dyer, and Levin (2015); Ling et al. (EMNLP 2015); and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) are directly relevant and should be cited and positioned against.
- Venue fit: The findings are valuable, especially for empirical/quantitative linguistics, but given the lack of algorithmic novelty, the contribution aligns more with linguistic analysis than with advancing the state of the art in computational modeling. This may limit its suitability for a conference prioritizing novel NLP methods.

Questions to authors
- Novelty and positioning: How does your approach and its findings differ from prior work that has already demonstrated POS information in embeddings and used it for tagging or induction (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016)? Please clarify what is new in your analysis or results beyond those studies.
- Related work: Could you expand the literature review to include and discuss the above works and any other relevant studies on embedding-driven POS tagging/induction, and explicitly compare methodologies, datasets, and findings?
- Scope of applicability: You briefly evaluate on the UD English Treebank. Could you comment on how you expect the approach to generalize to other languages or tagsets, and whether there are plans for multilingual validation?

Overall assessment
This is a clear, carefully executed study with solid empirical results and a valuable error analysis that sheds light on graded POS boundaries and annotation quality. However, the methodological contribution is limited, and the literature review does not sufficiently engage with prior work on embeddings for POS tagging/induction. The paper would be strong for a linguistic venue; for an NLP venue focused on methodological innovation, the lack of novelty is the main concern.
