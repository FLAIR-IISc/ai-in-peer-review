General comments:
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use embeddings to draw conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the strong link between vector representations and PoS. The detailed error analysis of misclassifications is a clear strength.
However, the paper overlooks a key motivation for PoS-tagging corpora like the BNC: they were tagged primarily to support downstream NLP tasks, especially parsing, rather than to conduct a purely linguistic study of morphosyntactic categories (which are grounded in a semantic prototype perspective; see Croft, 1991). The discussion would be stronger if reframed around whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more beneficial for parsing than those in the original tagset (or UPOS).
The paper also omits substantial related work on distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and adding more recent non-DNN approaches, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), and others. In light of this literature, the results in Section 5 offer limited novelty, as comparable performance has been achieved by systems with stricter constraints on external knowledge.

Specific issues:
- The abstract claims that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” Unless I am misunderstanding, this is not new—especially for English. Distributional PoS induction systems over the past 15 years reporting many-to-one or cluster purity results demonstrate the same point.
- The assertion in lines 79–80 that relations between vectors are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate. Work by Mikolov et al., Collobert et al., and subsequent research shows substantial syntactic information in these vectors. See also the earlier point about cluster purity. Indeed, you appear to reverse this claim at the start of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
- Why switch to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions in the original tagset are arguably more informative and interesting.
- Footnote 3 is unclear. Were these failed attempts yours or from prior work? By what criteria did they fail? What about Brown cluster vectors, which align almost perfectly with UPOS tags?
- Is the observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331–332) particularly noteworthy? The presence of “the” (the most frequent function word) may largely explain this difference.
- While focusing on the most frequent word–tag pairs is practical, it would be valuable to examine the tail—both the vectors and the classifier’s error types. This could suggest alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological, given lemmatization) features that better generalize PoS tags for low-frequency words.

Minor issues:
- Use sentential citations with \newcite{}, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed …”
