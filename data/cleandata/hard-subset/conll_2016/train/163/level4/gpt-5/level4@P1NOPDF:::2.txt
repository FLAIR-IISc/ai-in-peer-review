General comments:
This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. Specifically, the authors use embeddings to draw interesting—if somewhat straightforward—conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and their strong alignment with word vector representations. The detailed error analysis (classification outliers) is a clear strength.

However, the paper seems to miss one critical point: why corpora such as the BNC were PoS-tagged in the first place. Unlike a purely linguistic inquiry into morphosyntactic categories (often underpinned by semantic prototype theory; e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were tagged to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion would be stronger if reframed around whether the distinctions induced by distributional vectors are more beneficial for parsing than the original tagset (or UPOS).

The paper also omits substantial related work on distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and adding more recent non-neural work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), etc. In light of this body of work, the results in Section 5 offer limited novelty: there are systems with stricter limits on external knowledge that achieve comparable performance.

Specific issues:
- In the abstract, one stated contribution is that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” Unless I am misunderstanding, this is not new—especially for English. Virtually every distributional PoS induction system over the past 15 years reporting many-to-one or cluster-purity scores demonstrates the same point.
- The assertion in lines 79–80 (“relations between vectors are mostly semantic”) is not correct. Work by Mikolov et al. and by Collobert, among others, shows substantial syntactic content in these vectors. See also the earlier point about cluster-purity scores. In fact, you reverse this claim at the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
- Why move to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more informative and interesting, particularly if the goal is to inform downstream parsing.
- I do not understand footnote 3. Were these failed attempts yours or from prior work? By what criteria did they fail? Also, what about Brown-cluster-based vectors? They tend to align almost perfectly with UPOS tags and would be a strong baseline or point of comparison.
- Is the observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331–332) particularly informative? The presence of “the” (the most frequent function word) may almost single-handedly explain much of this difference.
- While I understand the practical reasons for analyzing the most frequent word–tag pairs, it would be valuable to examine the tail: both the behavior of the vectors and the classifier’s error types for low-frequency items. This could motivate alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological—since you lemmatize) features that support better generalization of PoS tags to rare words.

Minor issues:
- Change sentential references to use \newcite{}, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed …”
