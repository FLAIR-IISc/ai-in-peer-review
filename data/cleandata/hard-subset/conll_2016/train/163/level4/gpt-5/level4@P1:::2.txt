Summary and claimed contributions
This paper explores how word embeddings trained on the BNC relate to part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings, use misclassifications to surface “outlier” words that behave atypically for their tags, argue for graded PoS affiliations, and show that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions. They also present a preliminary PoS predictor that operates without sentence context.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A detailed error analysis showing that embedding-based PoS predictions can reveal boundary cases and potential annotation inconsistencies (notably in the BNC), and that PoS-relevant information is distributed across many components.
2) Empirical confirmation—across BNC and UD Treebank vocabularies—that embeddings encode PoS information sufficiently well to separate many classes, including some subtle distinctions.
3) A preliminary context-free PoS predictor trained on a small set of “prototypical” words, demonstrating reasonable accuracy given its limited information.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis: The analysis of outliers is the strongest aspect. It identifies linguistically meaningful boundary cases (e.g., participles, adjectival nouns, numerals vs. nouns, time expressions), potential systematic annotation issues (e.g., adverbs vs. adjectives in BNC), and cases where UPOS vs. BNC scheme differences matter.
- Methodological clarity and robustness: The setup is simple and well-documented (training details, frequency weighting), and generalizes plausibly, with consistent results on BNC and UD Treebank vocabularies. The observation that conjunctions exhibit a strong component and that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions is well supported.
- Practical value for corpus auditing: Using embeddings to flag likely annotation errors or scheme inconsistencies is compelling. The approach could be a useful tool for corpus maintenance and tagset refinement.
- Compact supervision: The preliminary tagger trained on ~1.5k prototypical words achieves reasonable accuracy without using sentence context, suggesting utility for low-resource settings as a weak tagger or bootstrap signal.

Weaknesses
- Missing central motivation with respect to downstream use: Corpora like the BNC were PoS-tagged chiefly to support downstream NLP (notably parsing), rather than to refine linguistic categories per se (contrast with prototype-theoretic views; e.g., Croft, 1991). The paper would be stronger if reframed around whether the distinctions learned by embeddings are more helpful for parsing than the original tags (or UPOS) and, ideally, if it provided evidence for downstream gains.
- Related work is incomplete for PoS induction: Important prior work on distributional PoS induction is missing, e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom and Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this literature, Section 5’s results are only weakly novel; comparable accuracies have been reported by systems with stricter constraints and less external knowledge. A careful positioning against this body of work is needed.
- Overstated/incorrect claim about embedding semantics vs. syntax: The statement that relations between vectors are “mostly semantic” is not accurate given prior work showing strong syntactic regularities (e.g., Mikolov et al.; Collobert and Weston) and is later contradicted in Section 2. This should be corrected and consistently framed.
- Tag granularity choice (UPOS): It is unclear why the paper maps to UPOS rather than retaining the original, more fine-grained BNC/CLAWS distinctions. The fine-grained distinctions are arguably more informative and could yield richer analyses of boundary cases.
- Methodological clarity around failed clustering and baselines: Footnote 3 is unclear—were the failed clustering attempts yours, and by what criteria did they “fail”? How do Brown clusters compare here? In many corpora, Brown cluster IDs align closely with UPOS, so a direct comparison seems necessary.

Questions to the authors
- Can you evaluate whether the embedding-induced PoS distinctions improve downstream parsing (or another structured task) compared to the original BNC tags or UPOS?
- How does your approach compare quantitatively to classical PoS induction methods (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., Blunsom and Cohn, Yatbaz et al.) under standard many-to-one or cluster purity evaluations?
- Please reconcile the “mostly semantic” characterization of embeddings with evidence of strong syntactic regularities (e.g., Mikolov et al.; Collobert and Weston), and the apparent inconsistency with Section 2.
- Why did you choose UPOS rather than the original BNC tagset? Would your analysis of outliers and boundary phenomena benefit from finer-grained tags?
- Clarify footnote 3: Which clustering algorithms, numbers of clusters, and evaluation metrics did you try? What exactly failed? How do Brown clusters perform as PoS predictors on your data?
- Could the observed dissimilarity between proper and common nouns be largely driven by the distribution of “the”? A simple ablation (e.g., removing articles) might clarify this.
- Please analyze the tail: What happens for low-frequency types (both vector quality and classifier errors)? Since lemmatization discards morphology, which additional features (e.g., character/subword, affixes) might help generalize to rare words?

Minor issues
- Revise sentential citations to use \newcite{}, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed …”.
- Replace placeholder citations like “<MIKOLOV or COLOBERT>” with proper references (e.g., Mikolov et al.; Collobert and Weston).
