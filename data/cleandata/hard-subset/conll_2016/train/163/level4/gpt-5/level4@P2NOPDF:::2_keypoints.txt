- Paper shows PoS information is present in distributional vectors but conclusions are unsurprising; error analysis of outliers is a strength.
- Paper fails to address that PoS-tagged corpora like the BNC are primarily designed to support downstream NLP (e.g., parsing), not purely linguistic category analysis.
- Recommend reframing evaluation around whether embedding-induced distinctions improve parser performance compared to the original tagset or UPOS.
- Substantial related work on distributional PoS induction is omitted; cite Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and non-neural methods like Blunsom & Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012).
- Given prior work, Section 5’s results offer limited novelty because similar performance has been achieved with fewer external resources.
- Abstract claim that “distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation” is not new for English.
- Statement that word-vector relationships are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate; embeddings capture substantial syntactic regularities.
- Unclear motivation for switching to UPOS; the original fine-grained tagset might reveal more nuanced embedding structure.
- Footnote 3 is unclear about whether failed attempts are the authors’ or prior work, what “failure” means, and omits Brown clusters as a strong baseline.
- Observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns is unsurprising and may be driven by distributional effects of high-frequency function words like “the.”
- Analysis focuses on frequent word/tag pairs; the paper should analyze rare words and low-frequency tags to assess embedding and classification behavior in the tail.
- Suggest empirically evaluating whether induced or embedding-informed PoS distinctions improve parsing metrics (UAS/LAS).
- Minor: use sentential citation format (e.g., \newcite{}) for citations like Mikolov et al. (2013b).
