Summary
The paper investigates whether distributional word representations encode part-of-speech (POS) information. Using a lemmatized, UD-annotated version of the BNC, the authors train word embeddings and then fit a logistic regression classifier to predict POS from the resulting vectors. They evaluate with cross-validation on the BNC and on additional corpora. The experiments are clearly presented, and the analysis is thorough.

Strengths
- Clear research question and a simple, reproducible setup that functions as a probing study of distributional representations.
- Careful, well-written presentation and an extended discussion of results.
- Empirical support for the long-standing hypothesis that POS categories are reflected in distributional patterns, which is of interest to quantitative/empirical linguistics.
- Cross-corpus evaluation adds some breadth beyond pure in-domain results.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: the methodology relies on standard embedding training and a straightforward linear classifier without introducing new models, learning signals, or analytical techniques. As such, the contribution is incremental for CoNLL.
- Potential circularity/inflation: embeddings and labels derived from the same corpus, even with cross-validation, can overstate generalization. It is important to more clearly separate representation learning, classifier training, and evaluation domains.
- Type-level vs. token-level ambiguity: predicting a single POS for a word type ignores syntactic ambiguity. Without an explicit treatment of ambiguous words, it is hard to interpret the linguistic implications. An analysis by ambiguity class would be valuable.
- Lemmatization removes morphological cues that are highly predictive of POS; while this makes the task “purer” distributionally, it also complicates interpretation. An ablation comparing lemma vs. surface-form embeddings (or subword/character-based embeddings) would clarify what signal is being used.
- Baselines are under-specified: it is unclear whether improvements over simpler count-based context vectors, Brown clusters, or even frequency-only heuristics are demonstrated. Without such baselines, the incremental value of dense embeddings is hard to assess.
- OOV and coverage issues for out-of-domain evaluation are not fully described: how are unseen types handled, what is the vocabulary overlap, and are the tagsets fully harmonized?
- Reproducibility details (embedding algorithm, hyperparameters, dimensionality, window size, negative sampling, regularization of the classifier, class balance handling, and evaluation metrics such as macro vs. micro F1) should be reported more systematically.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen related work. The literature on POS tagging/induction with embeddings and on probing representations should be cited more comprehensively (e.g., Lin, Ammar, Duer and Levin 2015; Ling et al. 2015 [EMNLP]; Plank, Søgaard and Goldberg 2016), along with earlier work on Brown clusters and more recent probing methodologies.
- Add competitive baselines and ablations:
  - Count-based bag-of-contexts vectors with logistic regression.
  - Brown clusters or other discrete distributional features.
  - Random or shuffled-context embeddings to test for artifact exploitation.
  - Frequency-only and majority-tag baselines.
  - Embedding variants (CBOW vs. skip-gram, window sizes, dimensionalities, subword/character n-grams).
  - Lemma vs. surface-form embeddings to quantify the role of morphology.
- Clarify evaluation design:
  - Clearly separate in-domain (BNC) and out-of-domain tests; consider training embeddings on BNC but training the classifier on a small labeled set from another corpus to test transfer.
  - Report coverage, OOV handling, and tagset mapping across corpora.
  - Provide confidence intervals and significance tests across folds.
- Analyze linguistic factors:
  - Break down performance by word frequency and by ambiguity (unambiguous vs. polyfunctional types).
  - Provide confusion matrices and per-class scores to understand which POS are best captured distributionally.
  - Explore token-level evaluation with a simple context-sensitive predictor (as a diagnostic), or at least discuss limitations of type-level prediction.
- Consider releasing code, trained embeddings, and the UD-annotated/lemmatized BNC splits to facilitate replication and follow-up work.

Overall assessment and recommendation
This paper is clear, careful, and empirically informative about the distributional grounding of POS categories. However, for CoNLL, the lack of methodological novelty and the reliance on standard tools without stronger baselines or deeper analytical innovations limit its contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form for CoNLL, with the suggestion that the authors either (a) substantially strengthen the experimental design and comparative baselines and add novel analytical or modeling components, or (b) consider a venue focused on quantitative/empirical linguistics, where the current framing and findings would be a good fit.
