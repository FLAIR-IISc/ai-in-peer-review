Summary and contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. The authors (i) train a multinomial logistic classifier on word vectors to predict PoS and use misclassifications to probe PoS boundary phenomena and annotation inconsistencies; (ii) show empirically that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions; and (iii) build a simple “context-free” PoS predictor from embeddings trained without PoS tags, achieving reasonable accuracy.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that static word embeddings encode robust PoS information, with a careful error analysis that surfaces linguistically meaningful “boundary” cases and potential corpus annotation inconsistencies.
2) A methodology for auditing PoS annotations via distributional outliers, with concrete examples (e.g., participles vs. adjectives, numerals vs. nouns, years and times) and corpus-specific issues (e.g., BNC adverb/adjective confusions).
3) Evidence that PoS-relevant signal is distributed over many embedding dimensions (rather than a single salient feature), supported by a feature-importance analysis.

Strengths
- Clear empirical setup and thorough analysis: The training on BNC lemmas with UD tags and a simple logistic classifier is described transparently. Results are reported on frequent and mid-frequency words, and on UD English lemmas, with clear metrics (e.g., F≈0.98 CV on frequent types, ≈0.91 on mid-frequency words, ≈0.99 on UD lemmas present). The work is easy to reproduce and interpret.

- Insightful error analysis with linguistic value: The confusion patterns (proper vs. common nouns; adjectives vs. nouns; participles vs. adjectives; numerals vs. nouns/proper nouns; adverbs tagged as adjectives) are well-motivated and grounded in examples. The analysis articulates graded PoS membership and points to concrete annotation inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” erroneously tagged as adverbs in BNC; treatment of years/times; currency tokens). This is useful for corpus maintenance and linguistic discussion of category boundaries.

- Annotation auditing utility beyond standard taggers: Comparing to a Stanford tagger applied to isolated tokens (to match the distributional predictor’s no-context setting) shows that the distributional approach highlights different—and arguably more linguistically informative—confusions. As a tool for corpus auditing, the proposed method appears complementary to standard taggers.

- Distributed signal across dimensions: The ANOVA-based ranking and k-feature ablation suggest PoS information is not concentrated in a few dimensions; accuracy keeps improving up to around 100 features. This supports claims about the distributed nature of embedding features and cautions against over-interpreting single salient dimensions.

- Practicality for low-resource settings: The “proper predictor” trained on ≈1.5k unambiguous UD lemmas achieves ≈0.84 accuracy without sentence context. While not a competitive tagger, it provides a practical baseline for lexicon induction or preliminary annotation in low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core components (skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression, confusion analysis) are standard. The main value is empirical and analytical rather than algorithmic. For a computational linguistics venue, the novelty lies in the analysis framework and findings, not in new modeling techniques.

- Cross-validation protocol risks leakage: The 10-fold CV on 177k “instances” repeats the same 10k vectors proportionally to frequency. If folds are created at the instance level, the identical vector for a given word type can appear in both train and test, inflating the reported F≈0.98. Grouped CV by word type (never seeing the same word vector in both train and test folds) would be more reliable. The mid-frequency unseen-type test (F≈0.91) partly alleviates this, but reporting grouped-CV results would strengthen claims.

- Generality and coverage limits: Embeddings are trained only on BNC. The UD evaluation excludes 27% of word types (10% tokens) absent from the embedding vocabulary, introducing selection bias and limiting external validity. Broader tests (other corpora or genres) and reporting performance on the full token set (with an OOV strategy) would better support generalization.

- Interpretation of component-level findings: Individual “salient” dimensions (e.g., conjunctions’ component 94) may be unstable across runs and hyperparameters. Without variability analysis (multiple seeds, model types, or hyperparameters), it is hard to know how robust these component-specific observations are. The paper partially addresses this by showing distributed signal, but robustness checks would improve confidence.

- Comparison baseline is artificial: Comparing the distributional approach to a standard tagger run on isolated tokens is informative but not fully convincing, since taggers are designed to use context. Alternative baselines (e.g., lexicon-lookup derived from training data, Brown clusters, or POS induction methods) would yield a fairer comparison for the “no-context” setting and better situate the contribution relative to prior unsupervised/weakly supervised PoS work.

Questions to the authors
- CV protocol: Did you ensure that the same word type (same vector) never appeared in both training and validation folds? If not, can you report grouped-CV by word type?

- Robustness: How sensitive are the findings (especially component-level salience and PoS similarity patterns) to random seeds, embedding algorithms (CBOW, GloVe, fastText), window sizes, or training corpora?

- Ambiguity handling: Beyond the “unambiguous/dominant” UD lemmas used for the predictor, how does the classifier behave on highly ambiguous lemmas? Can you quantify performance as a function of PoS ambiguity?

- Human validation: For claimed annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adverb/adjective cases, years/times), did you obtain any manual verification? Even a small adjudicated sample would substantiate the auditing claim.

- OOV strategy: For the UD evaluation where 27% of types were missing, can you provide results with a simple OOV handling (e.g., character/subword embeddings or a backoff lexicon) to assess end-to-end coverage?

Related work to include
- Prior uses of embeddings for PoS tagging and induction and related representation learning: Lin, Ammar, Dyer, and Levin (2015); Ling et al. (2015, EMNLP); Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016); Collobert et al. (2011); earlier clustering approaches (e.g., Brown clusters) as baselines for PoS-related distributional structure.

Overall recommendation
This is a clear, well-executed empirical study with solid analysis and interesting linguistic insights. While methodologically straightforward, the contribution as an auditing tool and the careful exploration of PoS boundary phenomena are valuable. With strengthened validation (grouped CV, robustness checks) and a more comprehensive positioning within the literature, the paper would make a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance, especially for a venue emphasizing empirical findings and linguistic analysis.
