Review- General comments:
This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech tags and word embeddings. In particular, the authors use embeddings to draw several interesting—though somewhat straightforward—conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the strong link between vector representations and PoS. The thorough error analysis (classification outliers) is certainly a strength of the work.
However, the paper appears to miss a crucial overarching point: the original motivation for PoS-tagging corpora like the BNC. Unlike a purely linguistic study of morphosyntactic categories (grounded in a semantic prototype framework—e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were annotated primarily to support downstream NLP tasks, especially parsing. The discussion could therefore be reframed around whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more useful for parsing than the original tags (or UPOS).
In addition, the paper omits significant related work on distributional PoS induction. I suggest starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-DNN approaches, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), etc. In light of this literature, the results in Section 5 have limited novelty (there are systems with tighter constraints on external knowledge that achieve comparable performance).

Specific issues
In the abstract, one stated contribution is that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” Unless I am misinterpreting, this is not novel, especially for English: virtually every distributional PoS induction system from the past 15 years reporting many-to-one or cluster purity results demonstrates this.
The claim in lines 79–80 (“relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic”) is incorrect: the Mikolov or Collobert papers (and subsequent work) show that these vectors encode substantial syntactic information. See also the earlier comment regarding cluster purity scores. In fact, you reverse this claim at the start of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
Why switch to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions in the original tagset seem more informative.
I do not follow footnote 3. Were these failed attempts yours or from prior work? By what criteria did they fail? What about Brown cluster vectors? They align almost perfectly with UPOS tags.
Is the observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331–332) particularly interesting? Doesn’t the presence of “the” (the most frequent function word) largely explain this difference?
While I understand the practical motivation for focusing on the most frequent word/tag pairs, it would be informative to examine the tail, both in terms of the vectors and the classifier’s error types. This could suggest alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological—since you lemmatize) features that enable better PoS generalization for low-frequency words.

Minor issues
Change sentential citations to \newcite{}, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”.
